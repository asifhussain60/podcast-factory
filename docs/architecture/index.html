The podcast-factory pipeline

A walk-through of how a scholarly Arabic book becomes a two-host podcast episode — the rooms the content moves through, the editing steps that polish it, the safety checks that bless it, and the team of AI helpers that runs the whole thing while you walk away from your desk.

01 — THE HOOK

What this is

One paragraph. No diagram. The shape of the whole thing.

This is a factory that turns scholarly Arabic books — mostly from the Ismaili tradition — into two-host podcast episodes for Google's NotebookLM (the tool that produces the actual audio). You hand the factory a PDF. The factory makes a private working copy so it can edit safely. It runs through a sequence of editing and review steps. At one point in the middle it pauses so you can review the chapter plan; otherwise it runs without you. When every quality check passes, the finished bundle moves from the private workshop into a public library where readers can see it.

There's also a small browser app that lets you read and comment on chapter drafts before they go to NotebookLM. That's the reader app. It's part of this same project. (The unrelated memoir project — "What I Wish Babu Taught Me" — lives in a separate place; this page is about the podcast factory only.) Everything described here is machine-agnostic since 2026-05-23: one project, one main working line, and a one-step human-approved promotion before anything goes live.

The factory's own code has gone through a six-wave rebuilding programme that finished on 2026-05-27 — all 32 steps complete. Sections 10 and 11 describe that rebuild and the seven content archetypes it produced.

02 — HOW CONTENT MOVES

From a PDF on your desk to a published episode

Three rooms. The content visits each one in order.

Every piece of content lives in one of three rooms at any given moment. The source room holds the original PDF (or document, or audio file) somebody gave us — it sits wherever it lives on disk and the factory never modifies it. The workshop is where the factory does its work: every draft, every chapter plan, every review note. Nothing in the workshop is ever shown to readers. The public library is what readers actually see — and the only way content reaches it is if every one of the seven safety checks in Section 6 says yes.

03 — THE ELEVEN STEPS

Eleven small steps, from raw PDF to finished episode

Click any step to see what it does, what it costs, and how it can go wrong.

The factory marches every book through eleven steps in order. The first six work on the whole book at once (translation, polishing, chapter design). The last several work one chapter at a time (writing the host conversation, reviewing it, publishing it). There is exactly one human pause, near the middle. At that pause you review the proposed chapter list — does this look like the right way to break the book into episodes? — and either approve it or fix it before the factory burns real money on the per-chapter work.

0a

Read the source

The factory scans every page of the Arabic PDF and produces a first-pass English draft. This is the only step that talks to an outside service (Microsoft's text-recognition and translation tools).

What it does
Reads the printed Arabic page-by-page, then converts that Arabic into rough English.
What it produces
A raw Arabic transcript and a first-draft English version, both stored in the working copy.
Cost
Low single-digit dollars for a typical 200-page book — the cheapest step that touches an outside service.
How it fails
Either the Microsoft credentials are missing, or the network glitches mid-translation. Both are fixed by re-running this one step.
▶
0b

Polish the English

An AI writer rewrites the rough machine translation into readable, voice-consistent English. It works in chunks so it never tries to read a 400-page document in one go.

What it does
Takes the rough English from the previous step and rewrites it as something a human would actually want to read.
Cost
This is the most expensive single step — budget five to twenty dollars per book depending on how long the book is.
How it fails
Occasionally a chunk break lands mid-sentence. It usually self-corrects on the next chunk.
▶
0c

Pronunciation guide

The factory spots Arabic names, technical terms, and quoted passages that will appear in the audio — and writes a pronunciation hint for each one so the two NotebookLM hosts say them correctly.

What it does
Pulls the names and terms that need careful pronunciation out of the polished English and pairs each one with a written hint.
Cost
Moderate — one careful pass over the book.
How it fails
The AI occasionally invents a name that isn't actually in the source. A later check catches this.
▶
0d

Decide the chapters

The factory decides how the book divides into chapters — each chapter becomes one NotebookLM episode. It writes a short plan describing what each chapter is about.

What it does
Looks at the polished English plus the pronunciation guide and decides where the natural breaks are.
What it produces
A short plan per chapter, plus the actual text of each chapter cut from the source.
Then what
A quick automated check follows immediately to spot duplicates or gaps before anyone reviews the plan.
How it fails
If any chapter plan is malformed, the whole factory halts until you fix it — there's no point continuing on a broken plan.
▶
0e

Add the supporting quotes

The factory weaves in citations and cross-references from a curated list of trusted sources. The two hosts of the eventual podcast will draw on these to add depth to the conversation.

What it does
For each chapter, finds approved supporting quotes from a tiered list of trusted sources and weaves them into the chapter plan.
Cost
Per-chapter AI cost — it scales with how many chapters the book has.
How it fails
Occasionally cites a source that isn't on the approved list. The editor (Section 4) catches this later as a serious block.
▶
0f

Stop and let you review

This is the one human pause. The factory writes a readable plan summary — chapter count, episode lengths, structure — and then stops. You read it. If you like it, you tell the factory to keep going. If not, you fix the plan first.

What it does
Assembles a single readable summary of the whole proposed series and then waits for you.
Your job
Spend ten minutes reading it. Decide whether the chapter plan makes sense. Then tell the factory to resume.
Why this pause exists
Everything after this pause costs real money. Catching a bad chapter plan now is a hundred times cheaper than fixing it after fifteen chapters have already been written.
▶
B1

Per chapter: prepare the upload

For each chapter, the factory builds the file that NotebookLM will actually ingest as the source material for the episode.

What it does
Cuts the right slice of the book for this chapter and packages it as a clean upload-ready file.
What it produces
One chapter file per chapter, ready to drop into NotebookLM.
▶
B2

Per chapter: write the steering notes

An AI writes "steering notes" for this chapter — the angle, who the two hosts should be, what beats they should hit. These notes go in the NotebookLM customize box, which is how you control the eventual conversation.

What it does
Decides the framing of the episode — the angle, the host dynamic, the conversational arc.
What it produces
A short framing document per chapter that becomes the steering prompt you paste into NotebookLM.
▶
B3

Per chapter: assemble the episode file

The factory assembles the full text that will steer NotebookLM's conversation. Before saving it, an automated check sweeps for forbidden phrases — most importantly the theological mistakes the next section is about.

What it does
Combines the chapter, the steering notes, and the standard rules into the single text bundle that drives NotebookLM.
Hard stop
If a forbidden theological phrase (see Section 7) is detected, the chapter loop halts immediately until you fix it. Nothing ships with that phrase still in.
▶
B4

Per chapter: editor review

An AI editor reviews the chapter against a long checklist and gives a verdict — ready to ship, ready with caveats, or blocked. If there are problems, an AI rewriter takes a swing. The two go back and forth up to three rounds. After three rounds it stops trying and tells you.

What it does
Drives the full quality-review loop described in detail in Section 4.
Hard cap
Fifty dollars per book and twenty-four hours of wall time — whichever comes first stops the loop.
Possible verdicts
SHIP-READY (no issues) · SHIP-WITH-CAUTION (minor things, ships anyway) · FAILED (the loop ran out of attempts on a serious issue).
▶
0g

Register the finished series

After all chapters ship, the factory adds the finished series to a master list of all books that have ever been produced. No AI cost — just bookkeeping.

Why this comes last
It needs every chapter's final state to compute the series-wide totals, so it can't run until convergence finishes.
▶
04 — QUALITY REVIEW

How a single chapter passes (or fails) its quality review

The editor reviews. The rewriter revises. Repeat up to three rounds. Then either ship or surface to you.

Every chapter goes through the same review dance. An AI editor reads the chapter, the framing, and the steering notes, then issues a verdict: SHIP-READY means no issues found, SHIP-WITH-CAUTION means minor stylistic concerns but nothing blocking, BLOCKED means at least one serious problem the chapter must not ship with. A SHIP-READY verdict ships immediately. A SHIP-WITH-CAUTION verdict ships once the rewriter has had one round to address the minor concerns. A BLOCKED verdict triggers the rewriter to attempt fixes, then the editor re-reviews. After three full rounds with anything still unresolved, the loop halts and tells you — it never quietly downgrades a BLOCKED verdict and ships anyway.

Read top-to-bottom · arrows are what happens next
SHIP-READY · ships immediately SHIP-WITH-CAUTION · ships on attempt 2 or later BLOCKED · rewriter tries, then editor re-reviews

The dashed arrows are the retry path. On the third attempt, anything still unresolved halts the whole factory and surfaces to you for a human call. An older version used to silently downgrade BLOCKED to a cautious ship — that backdoor was closed. Nothing slips through quietly anymore.

05 — WORKING COPIES

Every new piece of content gets its own isolated working copy

One book, one working copy. Nothing disturbs anything else.

Every new piece of content the factory takes in gets its own isolated working copy. Think of it as a private branch of the project where all the in-progress edits happen, so two books being worked on in parallel never step on each other. The working copy is created when intake starts and is merged back into the main project line only after publishing succeeds. The naming pattern tells you at a glance what kind of content it is — a book, a single document, a lecture, an article, a letter, an interview. If the category can't be figured out, it falls back to a generic "draft" label.

Working-copy prefix Content type What it looks like in practice
book/ books book/kitab-al-riyad
doc/ documents doc/imamat-overview-2026
lecture/ lectures lecture/kunooz-al-hikmah-session-04
article/ articles article/the-meaning-of-tariqat
letter/ letters letter/al-aziz-billah-to-the-jamat
interview/ interviews interview/qazi-noman-on-tawil
draft/ (unknown — fallback) draft/pending-classification

The "draft/" prefix is the fallback when nobody has told the factory what kind of thing this is yet — it keeps the work organized while signaling that the classification is still open. Working copies merge back into the main line only after publishing succeeds. And the final promotion to the truly-live line always needs your explicit green light — the project is set up to enforce that.

06 — SEVEN SAFETY CHECKS

Seven safety checks that must all say yes before publishing

Click any check to see what it verifies, what failure means, and how to recover.

Before any chapter reaches the public library, it has to clear seven safety checks in order. If any one of them says no, the publish stops — nothing reaches readers. The checks range from simple ("the chapter files actually exist") to subtle ("the editor signed off on this content"). The last check was added on 2026-05-24 specifically to close a loophole where some content was sneaking into the library without ever going through the editor review described in Section 4.

G1

Do the files actually exist?

Both the chapter text files and the matching episode files are present and non-empty.

Why: No point doing anything else if the actual content isn't on disk yet.

FAIL means one of the earlier steps didn't run or wrote nothing. Re-run the missing step.
▶
G2

Do the chapters and episodes line up?

Every episode file has a matching chapter file, and vice versa — no orphans on either side.

Why: Each NotebookLM upload needs both pieces. An orphan means a manual edit broke the pairing.

FAIL usually points to a manual edit gone wrong; the project state file shows which chapter is the odd one out.
▶
G3

Are the chapters numbered 1, 2, 3, …?

Chapter and episode numbers are a clean unbroken sequence — no "Chapter 14a", no "Chapter 3.5", no gaps.

Why: Both the reader app and the future catalog assume contiguous numbering — a gap or a suffix breaks the listing.

FAIL means a chapter-design rerun left a gap. Re-run that step.
▶
G4

Are there zero serious blockers?

Re-running the assembly step against every episode turns up zero serious blockers. Minor advisories are warnings only by default; a strict mode can elevate them to blocking.

Why: Even after the editor signed off, a serious theological error must not slip past the final assembly.

FAIL means a forbidden phrase (see Section 7) leaked back in somehow. The theology checks in the next section show you where to look.
▶
G5

Did the factory finish this book cleanly?

The project's bookkeeping says this book is either fully done, or in the per-chapter phase with a clean status — not mid-step.

Why: Don't publish a book the factory still thinks it's working on. The bookkeeping is the source of truth.

FAIL usually means a previous run was interrupted and left the status marked "in progress". A force-publish flag exists for when you've manually confirmed it's actually done.
▶
G6

Is the publishing destination safe to write to?

The destination folder either doesn't exist yet (the factory creates it) or is safe to overwrite — never a symlink, never outside the library tree.

Why: Don't accidentally blow away something that turns out to be a shortcut to somewhere else on disk.

FAIL when the destination is a symlink — investigate before continuing.
▶
G7

Did the editor sign off?

The book went through the full editor review described in Section 4, and the final verdict is either SHIP-READY or SHIP-WITH-CAUTION — not missing, not blocked.

Why: Closes the backdoor where some books were sneaking past the editor entirely. A bypass flag exists for the rare case where the editor was run manually outside the normal flow.

FAIL means either the book never went through the editor at all, or the editor report is missing. Use the bypass flag only after a real manual review.
▶
07 — THEOLOGY CHECKS

Five theology checks that catch attribution and lineage mistakes

Each chapter walks through T1 through T5; serious findings block, lesser ones flag.

Think of this as a spell-checker for theology. Five different checks (named T1 through T5) run against every chapter to catch errors specific to the Ismaili tradition — quoting someone the wrong way, putting the wrong name on a specific Imam in the lineage, using phrases that signal a different tradition than the one these books come from. Two of the five (T4 and T5) are placeholders for future data we haven't built up yet — they're wired in but currently inert, so when we add the data later nothing has to be renumbered.

One specific bit of background that matters: in this tradition, Ali ibn Abi Talib is the Father of the Imams, not the first Imam himself. The first Imam is Hassan. So the phrase "Imam Ali" is a serious red flag — it reflects a different tradition's convention, not the one these books are written in. Whenever it appears, the factory blocks the chapter from shipping until it's fixed.

A chapter enters at the top · clean chapters exit on the left · problems exit on the right

The chapter runs through T1 through T5 in sequence. A clean walk (no findings at all) exits on the left and ships. Any serious finding kicks the chapter into the editor's BLOCKED branch of the review loop in Section 4. The seven canonical Imams in this tradition are Hassan, Hussain, Ali Zayn al-Abidin, Muhammad al-Baqir, Ja'far al-Sadiq, Isma'il ibn Ja'far, and Muhammad ibn Isma'il.

08 — THE TEAM OF HELPERS

One conductor, eight specialists, and two outside helpers

The conductor sits in the middle and calls on each specialist when its turn comes.

The factory is run by a small team of AI helpers. One of them is the conductor — it sits in the middle of the diagram and drives the whole show. You hand the conductor a PDF and walk away; it works through the eleven steps in order, pauses once in the middle for your review, then resumes and runs every per-chapter helper at the right moment. The other helpers are specialists — each does one job and only one job. The two helpers in the top-left and (offstage) bottom of the diagram are tooling helpers, used by you directly when you need to refine a request or refresh this very page; the conductor doesn't call them.

The center is the conductor · arrows point to the specialist it calls

Eight specialists radiate out from the conductor. The prompt refiner in the top-left is a tooling helper you use directly when cleaning up a request before handing it off. The docs writer that generated this very page is another tooling helper — it reads the project and rewrites this page, but never changes any actual factory state.

09 — OUTSIDE SERVICES

Four Microsoft services do the work the factory can't do itself

All passwords live in macOS Keychain. Nothing sensitive ever lands in tracked files.

The factory leans on four Microsoft Azure services for the things AI models can't do well on their own: reading the text off a scanned page, translating Arabic to English, and spotting named entities (people, places, terms). They all live in one resource group in Microsoft's eastern-US region. The passwords for each one are stored in your Mac's built-in Keychain — never in any file that gets shared. There's a fifth service, Azure Speech, that's switched off by default and only turns on for the audio-lecture project; everything else uses the four core services.

Pipeline steps on the left call out to Microsoft services on the right

The "Read the source" step is the busy one — it calls both Document Intelligence (to read the text off the page) and Translator (to convert Arabic to English). The named-entity step is wired but not yet active in the pipeline. Speech only turns on for the Urdu-lecture project. Every connection is authenticated with passwords pulled out of your Mac's Keychain at runtime.

11 — CONTENT ARCHETYPES

Seven named content types, each with its own quality playbook

Adding a new content type means writing three files — no code changes required.

When the factory decides how to handle a book, the first question is: what kind of thing is this? A dense philosophical treatise needs a different framing strategy than a stage-play dialogue. A series of transcribed lectures needs different audience signals than an aphorism collection. The answer lives in a registry of seven named content types — what the factory calls archetypes. Each archetype has three files: a specification (the rules and required metadata fields), an exemplar (a model episode that shows what good looks like for this type), and an anti-patterns catalog (the specific mistakes the editor watches for). The factory reads these at runtime — no code changes needed to add a new type.

The three archetypes marked New in Wave F had their specifications and exemplars in place since Wave C, but their anti-patterns catalogs and exemplar episodes were completed in Wave F (2026-05-27).

Scholarly Deep-Dive

Established
scholarly-deep-dive

Dense philosophical or theological prose that builds its argument across many chapters. Needs a multi-tier capstone, deep citation support, and careful honorific handling throughout. The default type for most Ismaili scholarly texts.

Known titles: Kitab al-Riyad · Asaas al-Ta'weel · Rahat al-Aql (planned)

Aphorism Collection

Established
aphorism-collection

Short, self-contained sayings or letters — each one complete on its own. No preface needed. No multi-tier capstone. The host conversation in each episode leans into the resonance of a handful of sayings rather than building a long argument.

Known titles: Ayyuhal Walad (O Son)

Narrative Prose

Established
narrative-prose

Continuous fictional or literary narrative — the kind that unfolds as a story arc, not as an argument. Reserved for future Arabic literary novels entering the catalog. Augmentation is opt-in; pre-synthesis is required before the capstone.

Known titles: Future additions

Socratic Dialogue

Established
socratic-dialogue

A dialogue-structured text where the philosophical argument unfolds through question and answer between a teacher and student — distinct from stage-play drama in that both speakers are always "in the room." Configured as an alias of Play-Novel with dialogue-density overrides.

Known titles: Future additions

Play-Novel

New in Wave F
play-novel

Dramatic dialogue or theatrical fiction — named speakers with explicit turns, scene headings, a cast of characters. The two NotebookLM hosts mirror the in-text voice dynamic. Augmentation is never on for this type; the dramatic arc is the value. Anti-patterns catalog and exemplar episode completed in Wave F.

Known titles: The Master and the Disciple

Lecture-Series

New in Wave F
lecture-series

Transcribed or composed oral teaching — numbered sessions, second-person address to an implied audience, pedagogical recap patterns. Each episode covers one session. Augmentation is opt-in. Anti-patterns catalog and exemplar episode completed in Wave F.

Known titles: Kunooz Al-Hikmah

Encyclopedic-Epistolary

New in Wave F
encyclopedic-epistolary

Letter-form texts that combine encyclopedic depth with an epistolary frame — exhaustive enumeration addressed to a named recipient. The canonical example is the Rasāʾil Ikhwān al-Ṣafāʾ. Seven Rasāʾil-specific metadata fields, phased rollout support, and a diagram-density setting. Anti-patterns catalog, exemplar episode, and seven metadata fields all completed in Wave F.

Known titles: Rasāʾil Ikhwān al-Ṣafāʾ (planned)

Each archetype is three files in a shared folder — a spec, an exemplar, and an anti-patterns catalog. Adding an eighth archetype means writing three new files. No code changes required.
