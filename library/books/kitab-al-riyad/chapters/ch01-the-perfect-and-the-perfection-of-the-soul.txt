# The Perfect and the Perfection of the Soul

*This chapter covers the opening of* this book *— the author's preface and prayer for the Imam of his time, the statement of method that frames the entire book, the table of ten chapters, and the first ten sub-chapters of Chapter One: the foundational argument between the author of* the book *The Correction* *(Jonathan) and the author of* the book *The Defense* *(Samuel) over whether the Soul is perfect or imperfect — and over what "perfection" even means.*

## The author's preface

the author Ahmad the author opens:

> Praise be to Allah, Lord of fathers and mothers, Creator of animals and plants. To Him belongs all that is in the earth and the heavens, and He bestows favor upon His creation with blessings. Beings have no existence except by His command, and beings have no existence except by His decree. His destiny is firm, so that the minds do not deny it, and it endures, so that it does not suffer from it.

He continues: from God, His might, greatness, and grace have been extinguished, and by His command the blessings have overflowed. By His ear, the earth and the heavens were raised; the misguided and the doubtful were lost, and they went astray far away. *The author* praises Him, thanks Him, believes in Him, does not disbelieve in Him, and bears witness that there is no god but God — the true One who created all things with their different essences, who possesses majesty (*Jalal*) and dignity (*Nadhid*), the One like Whom there is nothing, who is exalted above all comparison.

He bears witness that the Most Merciful sent the chosen one, *Muhammad* (peace and blessings be upon him) — His servant, the chosen one, the pure one (*the pure*) — and shattered the idols and images that the people had been worshipping. Where an idol had stood in the sanctuary, the landmarks of the Hanifi religion had been threatened; the fire of disbelief had been burning, the edifices of polytheism had been raised, and the people had sunk into misguidance. So the Prophet rose up as a diligent striver and contended on their behalf with what was harder than clothing and darkness. He shattered the idols and called the people to the worship of the Most Merciful and to the rites of faith and Islam — replacing the houses of fire with the Sacred House of God, abrogating the rule of the crosses by turning toward the Corner (*the corner pillar*) and the Station (*the station*), guiding the servants of God onto the straight path, saving them from the claws of the seductive devil, and bringing them by his blessing to the trustworthy refuge. He led them to the courtyard of guidance and meeting, until the word of monotheism shone bright, and the word of *associating partners with God* was humiliated. The tongue pronounced clear verses, and the wind drove the ships of salvation across the seas of dispute.

The Prophet has a prayer that bears witness for his followers in general, and for the preachers in particular — that God may make easy for them a day on which neither wealth nor children will be of any use, except for the one who comes to God with a sound heart.

Then the author invokes the line of Imams. Upon *Qutb of the faith*, the axis of the religion, and the pillar of the believers — may his lights meet them — and upon the Guardians (*the friends of God*) by command of his followers; and upon *the seventh Imam* (the suppressor of anger in fulfillment of God's covenant), the patient one under the humiliation of usurpation for the sake of seeking the face of God — the Commander of the Believers, *the Commander of the Faithful* (peace be upon him) — whose exploits are innumerable and whose virtues are without count. And upon their descendants, the righteous Imams, whose lights immortalize souls, by whose obedience hope is realized, and by whose intercession perfection is attained — down to the ruling Imam of *the author's* own time, the Commander of the Faithful and his fathers, the Guardians of the Faithful, the family of *Taha* and *Yasin* (Quran 20:1, 36:1), peace be upon them all.

## Why this book was written

After this address: the most deserving matter for the monotheist, the author says, is to direct his attention and devote his efforts toward what leads to the Unity of God and the knowledge of His limits — toward a settled system of certain belief, and toward the calling that disperses the confusion and the darkness from the souls of the believers.

Now the situation that prompted the author to put down his pen is named directly:

> When I saw that Shaykh Jonathan (may God have mercy on him) had set out to correct, in the book the book *The Harvest* (*The Harvest*), what he claimed had been corrupted, and that Shaykh Samuel Samuel (Samuel, may God have mercy on him) had supported the author of the book *The Harvest* and judged his statement to be correct — I bore witness that what they disputed about was not among the branches in which disagreement is permissible while the soundness of the principles is preserved.

In some places, the author writes, he found *Samuel* sincere in the way he intended his rebuttal, yet prejudiced against *Jonathan* in others; and in some passages their words were issued in another order. And nevertheless, there was in the book *The Harvest* — from the words of its author in the chapter on monotheism, concerning the First Intellect in particular — what it was incumbent upon *Jonathan* to correct and to address. He did not. He left it and neglected it. All of that aggregated to make the people of the Guiding *The call* angry whenever they stood upon a point of difference in the paths of *monotheism* and the knowledge of the limits.

So the author lays out his method:

> I thought to quote the sayings of each one of them in the book the book *The Correction* — that is, the book *The Correction* (*The Reform*) — and the corresponding passage in the book the book *The Defense* (*The Support*), contrasting their objects, and speaking about what is necessary concerning them, what is meant by them, and the face by which it is correct to attribute their meanings to their authors — so that the truth of one may be distinguished from the other, and the prejudices of the rebellious among them may appear, and the meaning of what they differed about may be understood. Then I will speak about what was neglected, of correction in the book the book *The Harvest*, among the foundations in which disagreement is not permissible — and I will show the truth in pursuit of reward.

So he gathered all of it in a single book and called it *this book*. The title carries its own argument. The "Gardens" — because in what it collects of their sayings and what it quotes, it is at once a separation between them and a fulfillment of duty, drawing on the blessings of the *Awliya* of God upon them. It is, the author writes, like *Riyad*: a vegetable in the eye, a fruit that comes into the hands. God is our helper, and He will guide him in His land in bringing forth the thing from the effects of error, even when we are unaware of it.

The book gathers ten chapters, which together include one hundred and fifty-seven sub-chapters. And God is the Helper; He is the Protector and the Trustee.

The ten chapters announce the road ahead. Chapter One: what *Jonathan* and *Samuel* said in the chapter on the soul, which is the first emanator — thirty-eight sub-chapters. Chapter Two: on the First Intellect, the First Creator — nine sub-chapters. Chapter Three: on the soul and the spirit, and whether they resemble the former or not — six sub-chapters. Chapter Four: on the souls as parts and effects — eight sub-chapters. Chapter Five: on human beings as the fruit of the world — seven sub-chapters. Chapter Six: on motion, stillness, and image — nine sub-chapters. Chapter Seven: on the divisions of the world — seven sub-chapters. Chapter Eight: on *Qadr* and Destiny — twenty-four sub-chapters. Chapter Nine: on the law of *Adam* and *Nuh*, the Guardian over them, peace — thirty-three sub-chapters. Chapter Ten: what the author himself undertakes by way of correction from the book the book *The Harvest* in the chapter on monotheism and the First Creator — of what its author was first to reform — sixteen sub-chapters.

This is the scaffold. Each of the first nine chapters is a referee's report — *Jonathan* in the book *The Correction*, *Samuel* in the book *The Defense*, and *the author's* verdict between them. The tenth is the author speaking in his own voice. We now enter the first.

## Chapter One: on the soul, which is the first emanator

The first chapter is the longest of the ten — thirty-eight sub-chapters. It is also the most foundational, because everything that follows depends on what it settles about the Soul: is the Soul perfect like the Intellect, or deficient relative to it? And what does "perfect" mean in the first place?

### Section One — the opening claim

*Jonathan* sets out his position in the book *The Correction*: the Soul is perfect in itself, because it was emitted from the Intellect. The First Intellect is perfection itself, which is the emancipation of the perfect, because the Intellect is perfect. The argument is built on a chain of cause and effect: that which emanates from perfection cannot itself be other than perfect.

*Samuel* attacks this in the book *The Defense*. He says that the author of the book *The Correction* does not realize that perfection (*kamal*) is better and more complete than the perfect (*tamm*) — because perfection is an attribute, while the perfect is what is described; perfection is portable, while the perfect is the carrier. The described and the bearer are better and more complete than the attribute and the borne. The perfect is described by perfection, and bears it; and perfection is the attribute of the perfect, which is predicated upon it.

*The author* steps in. The author of the book *The Correction*, he says, did not mean what *Samuel* takes him to mean. When *Jonathan* called the Soul "perfect" because it was emitted, he was not arguing about whether perfection is an attribute or not, or whether perfection is described or not. He meant something else: that the perfect is like the cause for which the existence of the effect occurs. The perfect is the cause of perfection, and is superior to it in rank — for were it not for the perfect, perfection would not be found, just as if perfection is lifted in the imagination, the perfect is lifted with its lifting. The effect, if it is lifted, is lifted by the lifting of its cause. *Jonathan's* "perfect" was the First Intellect — the cause; his "perfection" was the Soul — the emanation. And if that is what he meant, then the attack misses entirely.

### Section Two — the contradiction inside the attack

*Samuel* continues: the perfect is better than perfection. *The author* now turns the screw. By *Samuel's* own logic, the perfect could only be perfect because of perfection; without perfection, it would not have been complete. If perfection is a thing, then that thing is its cause; and if perfection is the cause of the perfect, and the perfect is the effect of perfection, then by *Samuel's* own ordering, perfection is the cause and the perfect is the effect. From that, it would follow that what disables a thing is more honorable than what is disabled — and this is impossible.

The trap closes: *Samuel* cannot say both that the perfect is more honorable than perfection (because perfection is an attribute of the perfect) and that perfection has the higher rank (because perfection is what makes the perfect perfect). He has contradicted himself. So the author of the book *The Defense*, the author concludes, was prejudiced against the author of the book *The Correction*.

### Section Three — the attribute, the described, and the developing force

*Samuel* presses on. The perfect is an attribute, perfection is what is described; the perfect is a portrayal, perfection is a carrier. This indicates that the perfect is more honorable than perfection, and that the pregnant (the carrier) is more honorable than the carried.

*The author* says: this is not absolutely true in our existence. The Second is what is carried, and it is more honorable than the bearer. Consider the case of the powers in a living body. The power of the sting is the proximate cause of the existence of the developing force, and is its bearer; the developing force is borne in it, and is its complement — and the developing force is more honorable than that which carries it. The developing force in turn is the proximate cause of the existence of the sensual force, which is its bearer; sensuality is borne by the developing force, and is more honorable than it. The sensory power is the proximate cause of the existence of the speaking force; the speaking force is borne by the sensory, and is more honorable than the sensory. At every step of the chain, the bearer is the cause of the borne; and at every step, the borne is more honorable than the bearer.

So *Samuel's* general principle — that the carrier is more honorable than the carried — does not hold even in the natural world. And the analogy he is trying to apply to the supra-natural realm — to the First Intellect and the Soul as bearer and borne — is doubly broken, because in the world of origination and emanation, it is impossible that the First Intellect should be a carrier and the Second Intellect, which is emitted from it, be borne; or that the perfect (which is the First Intellect) should be an attribute of the perfect (which is the Second Intellect). The categories of attribute and described, bearer and borne, do not even apply at the level *Jonathan* is speaking about.

The author of *Samuel's* inference, then, was a prejudice. And how could the meaning of what he said be true — unless he is depending on the natural things, which are far from what the author of the book *The Correction* is speaking of, namely the creative and emulsive principles?

### Section Four — what "perfection" actually names

*The author* now pauses to lay out the terminology that the rest of the chapter will run on. "Perfection," he says, is a name applied to two things at once: to the First — which is the First Cause and the First Perfection — and also to the First Effect, which is completed by the addition of its existence to what is above it. The First Effect's existence and its completion come about by being added to that which is above it, as in the case of origination and the originated.

The originated is not something that lacks origination. It is that which has the originator as its very essence. It is the same thing twice over: if its existence is added to what is above it, it becomes a creation; and if it is added in respect of the character of the event itself, it is originative.

The First Intellect is Perfection itself, and it is perfect in truth — but only inasmuch as it deserves to be that which abstains from existing as derivative. The perfect, in this technical sense, is that which has nothing outside its own quality. The body is also called perfect — perfect in its three dimensions and having nothing of its kind outside itself; whatever resembles it derives from it, and such instances are few. The Sun, likewise, is complete: nothing exists outside it that is like it in quality or kind.

The claim that the existence of something subordinate to the First Intellect is imperfect holds — yet that deficiency is not in itself. In addition to what is above it, all things are perfect in themselves, because their existence comes from the Perfection that is the First Intellect, and there is no deficiency in them in themselves. If they were deficient in themselves, their existence would have been impossible by the proofs already given. It is also impossible that anything pertaining to the deficient should require the perfection of its own self — because the deficient is impossible — just as it is impossible that the existence of something not its own should be ascribed to it.

Deficiency is necessary only when it is added in relation to what is above it in rank, and not in itself. The self, insofar as it is the subject of a single thing — like numbers, which are perfect in themselves yet diminish only when added to others — exists from what is above it. The Intellect, like all the beings of the world, is complete and lacks nothing where it is the subject; it is only incomplete with respect to the ordering of forms, and the difference among forms when some are more noble than others — such as the human being, who is more noble than all the natural forms. In itself, the human being is as perfect as any other form; he is deficient only in relation to those things above him. And if this is the case in the physical realm, then the manner of contact applies likewise: a body shifts on this scheme in respect of rank, by way of analogy.

To anchor the move, the author cites the Quran:

> *We will show them Our signs in the horizons and in their own selves until it becomes clear to them that it is the truth.* — (Quran 41, the chapter explained in detail, verse 53, in the rendering the author gives.)

That is: God will establish for them the Imams and the limits, in the gross and the subtle, so that the state of the visible bodies and the souls whose actions exist through them may be apprehended — so that what they had failed to know, the limits of God which are the truth, becomes clear to them through the form.

The same vocabulary of "perfect in itself, deficient only in addition" is what the Quran's oath in *Surat the chapter on the sun* preserves at the level of the human soul. The verse names *taswiya*, "proportioning," as the Creator's own finishing act upon the Soul; whether the Soul then succeeds or fails is named separately, in time, as purification or corruption:

> *And by the soul and Him who proportioned it, who inspired it with discernment of its corruption and its righteousness — successful is the one who purifies it, and lost is the one who corrupts it.* — (Quran 91, the chapter of the sun, verses 7 through 10, Sahih International translation)

The Soul is *sawwa*, finished in its proportion, perfect in itself, at its origin. Whether it then succeeds (*aflaha*) or fails (*khaba*) is not a question about the Soul's essence but about what is added in time. The Quranic split is the same one the author is pressing on *Samuel* in philosophical vocabulary: identity is one register, rank in addition is another, and an honest reading does not collapse them into a single scale.

### Section Five — action, time, and what needs perfecting

The argument has been operating, so far, in the register of philosophy: emanation, cause and effect, attribute and described. Before *Jonathan* sharpens what the next move is about, it is worth marking how the Quran sets the same problem. When the people of Madina asked the Prophet about the Soul, the answer was not a definition but a reservation:

> *Wa yas'alunaka 'an the spirit. Qul the spiritu min the divine commandi rabbi, wa ma utitum min al-'ilmi illa qalila.*
> *And they ask you about the soul. Say: The soul is of the affair of my Lord, and mankind has not been given of knowledge except a little.* — (Quran 17, the chapter of the night journey, verse 85, Sahih International translation)

The verse closes the door on Soul-as-substance, there is nothing of essence to define, and points instead to *the divine command*, "the command," the act by which the Soul comes into being. What *Jonathan* is about to say turns on the same axis: perfection and deficiency are not statuses of the Soul's essence but characters of its action, which unfolds in time.

*Jonathan* opens a new front. What is in need of perfection, he says, is the activity of the soul, not the soul itself. The soul's activity can only occur in time, and the coming-to-life of the soul occurs in time — just as the First Intellect and time are one, since the First Originator is origination, and time and perfection are one. The Creator created all the originated principles at once; and the soul, even though it needs to derive benefit from the First, is therefore not in itself a diminution, and the First was not deficient — though the soul is in need of His command. Completion and deficiency appear in the action of the soul, not in itself.

*Samuel* counters: *Jonathan* did not realize that the Intellect does not stand in need of anything in the way that the soul needs to derive benefit from the Intellect. The relation of the Intellect to matter is not like the relation of the soul to the Intellect, since matter, from the standpoint of the Intellect, is nothing but the Intellect itself — their identity is one identity. It is impossible to say that a thing needs what it itself is; need for one thing in relation to another arises only when the other is something distinct.

*The author* gives the verdict. The author of the book *The Defense* is responding to what the author of the book *The Correction* maintained — that the Intellect needs matter only. And since this discourse rests on principles that emerge from the natural world, the author of the book *The Defense* in this matter is closer to the truth than the author of the book *The Correction*. What deserves to be said is that the soul needs perfection, and that this can be brought about only in time, because it is an emanation from the natural world and not from outside it.

There is a finer distinction: the souls that do not attain the degree of perfection — that is, those whose ascent to the second degree of perfection occurs in time — have an existence that is the existence of emanation. So if the statement of the author of the book *The Correction* is taken in this sense, it is correct. Not as a universal claim about every soul whatever, but as a claim about the souls of the natural world that pass from potency to act in time.

### Section Six — Intellect and time are NOT one

The next move is *the author's* sharpest correction of *Jonathan*. *Jonathan's* formulation — that "Intellect and time are one, because the First Originator is origination, and time and perfection are the same" — is not true. Time is the same as the number of motions of the celestial sphere: the imagined duration between the motion of the first degree from it and the motion of what follows from another degree to where the first had been. Origination, which is the Intellect, and its perfection precede the celestial sphere whose degrees' motion brings about the existence of time; and the existence of time is bound up with the existence of the First Intellect — but bound up downstream, not at the same rank.

*Jonathan's* belief contradicts what is established in the law of the guiding *The call*. *The author* points to his own treatment in *the author's other treatise* (*The Repose of the Intellect*) — known here under the title "Peace of Mind" — where he has shown from the account of the universe that time, and what precedes it in existence, are indicated also by the law of the Prophet. And that suffices for the seekers.

The reader should mark this: it is one of the moments where *the author's* loyalty to *Jonathan* does not bend the truth. When *Jonathan* is wrong, the author says he is wrong. The collapsing of Intellect and time into a single principle is a misstep, and it stays a misstep.

### Section Seven — the sun does not overtake the moon

*Samuel* opens a new line of attack: if the Intellect did not deny divinity of itself, the next being would not have moved its knowledge beyond the level of the Intellect to the Creator. What deficiency, he asks, is more evident than this — that the Soul knows the Originator only by way of the First?

*The author* refuses the inference. That belief is not permissible, because the power active in all the noble beings of the world of unity gives them the knowledge that they have a Creator. Without that power they would not come to be, nor persist, nor pass away. For this reason, every thing is called One; and if it is multiplied in itself, its share of that power remains hidden, while justice stands manifest. It is impossible to imagine that a single individual of the human kind does not know that it has a Creator, or that it fantasizes that it has none. As for the Greater Limit, the author says: he apprehends — that is, his very existence apprehends — that he is the reflection of the True, even if he is in himself and is his own master.

If we apply to the First Intellect what *Samuel* mentioned, and to the Next what he said of the Next, then it follows that the Next must be the basis for his establishment in the world of the body, being the next in the world of reason. For if it were not for the Speaker, one would not know that one has a God. That is not the case: each of them has a share from God, but the Speaker advances in rank, because he is close to the First and is later than him. And so God said:

> *La al-shamsu yanbaghi laha an tudrika al-qamara wa la al-laylu sabiqu al-nahari wa kullun fi falakin yasbahun.*
> *It is not for the sun to overtake the moon, nor does the night outstrip the day; and each is swimming in an orbit.* — (Quran 36, the chapter of Ya-Seen, verse 40, in the rendering the author gives.)

All are of one kind, and of differing ranks. The verse names the principle: difference in rank is not difference in kind. Sun and moon, day and night, swim in the same orbit; one outranks the other without dispossessing the other of its share. So Intellect and Soul, Speaker and Foundation: of one kind, of differing ranks.

The same insight survives in one of Imam the Commander of the Faithful's sayings preserved by *the compiler of the sermons*. The aphorism turns on the very word *Jonathan* and *Samuel* have been quarrelling over, *tamm*, "perfect":

> *Idha tamma al-'aqlu, naqasa al-kalam.*
> *When the intellect reaches its perfection, speech diminishes.* — (the book *The Path of Eloquence*, Aphorism 71, compiled by the compiler of the sermons c. 1009 CE)

The construction is exactly what the author is defending. *Al-'aqlu tamma*, the intellect is complete; *al-kalam naqasa*, speech is diminished. Speech is not made non-existent or imperfect in itself; it is *naaqis* only in relation to the *tamm* of the intellect. The aphorism predicates "perfect" and "diminished" of the same hierarchy without collapsing the lower term into a negation of the upper. The Soul, in *Jonathan's* reading, occupies the same grammatical place: complete in its own essence, "less" only as a relational measure against the First Intellect from which it issues.

### Section Eight — forms longing for matter, or matter longing for forms?

*Samuel* presses again. A further indication of the deficiency of the First Intellect, he says, is that all the first ones — the earlier philosophers — held themselves to the view that forms are always longing for matter, and matter has no subsistence apart from forms.

*The author's* immediate reply: *Samuel's* statement that the soul is the form of the True, and his statement that the Intellect is like body and matter — since the discourse was on the First Intellect — was a misguided objection against the author of the book *The Correction*.

Then the substantive correction. "Intellect," he says, is a shared name. What *Samuel* said is not true of what is supra-natural — namely, the first creative and emanating Intellects — but is true in the world of nature regarding the other intellects: intellect-in-potentia, the intellect moving from potentia to actuality, and the intellect-in-act. If the discourse was on the supra-natural, then *Samuel* has carried what was said of the natural onto what was supra-natural, and his contradiction was prejudicial.

He said: "Forms are always longing for matter, and matter has no subsistence apart from forms." This is incorrect, because forms do not exist as longing for matter for the sake of matter, nor are they that which matter acquires; rather, it is matter that yearns for forms in order to exist — for the sake of the forms — and matter is acquired in addition to forms of inferior nature and to the more noble form. And if that is so — and the soul has run the course of form, and the Intellect has run the course of matter, and it has been ruled that matter has no subsistence apart from forms — then it is as if *Samuel* has made it necessary that the existence of the Intellect be in the Soul. How then can the belief stand that the Intellect's existence was an originative existence? Or how does he imagine the Intellect to be the cause of the existence of the Intellect and the Soul, while the discourse is on the supra-natural, which comes out of the world of nature?

It is known that the Next has its existence after, not before; its existence is therefore posterior. This is a manifest fallacy, intended only to make *Samuel* believe what is said about the natural philosophers.

As for what is in the world of origination and the first emanations, the matter of the originated principles is not from a class of nature; it is from the Intellect, and from the Soul, which is like the second emanation. Except for what *Samuel* has mentioned, from this standpoint his point is correct (*sahih*) — but only when restricted to the natural realm. Carried into the supra-natural, it is false.

### Section Nine — the Soul is not a "mean"

The ninth sub-chapter takes up the doctrine of emanation directly. *Samuel* says: the Intellect is the first mean, the Soul is the second mean, and the first mean is closer to the Cause than the second mean. The proximity and rank of one thing to another can arise only from perfection and nobility, and distance can arise only from lack and absence of nobility.

*The author* says: this statement is not true with regard to the creative and emanative Intellects.

First, being a mean requires two ends; and it is not admissible to say that the First Intellect is a mean unless its existence be in such a state that on one side it has an end, and on the other side too it has an end, so that it stands in a similar state in existence on both sides. How then is it permissible to say of that which is the first of the firsts that it is a mean, when nothing precedes it in existence such that it might be intermediate between that and the two terms?

Even to suppose that the First Intellect is a mean is impossible, because its existence is a first existence, and what is a first existence is not called a mean. Even the mean deserves the name of mean only by virtue of having beyond it something of its own kind of existence, so that there is an affinity between the two; and likewise something later than it, suitable to it in some respects: an affinity with that one from one face, and with this one from another face — so that it has two faces and is intermediate between the two. As for the First Intellect, there is no affinity at all between it and its Creator, and it is not of the kind of His existence — so it is not a mean between Him and what is below it; nor is there occasion of its being a mean between it and its Creator.

As for that which is between the First Intellect and its Creator that caused it to exist, that something else IS origination itself — the Cause that is said to be the originating Cause — as the author has shown in *an earlier treatise the bringer-forth'a* (the Illuminating Epistle) and in his well-known book *the author's other treatise* (*Peace of Mind*).

If that is the case, then there is no sense in the inference of the author of the book *The Defense* in what he said here, for what he has already advanced concerning deficiency and nobility is sufficient. Deficiency is necessary for the First Intellect only in addition to its predecessor, not in itself: it is of a rank that is not deficient and that possesses perfection, while in relation to its predecessor it lacks rank — as with the Speaker, the Foundation, the Sun, and the Moon. Each of these — like the Foundation and the Moon — is perfect in itself, lacking nothing; yet when added to the other that is its counterpart form — the Speaker and the Sun — it is deficient in rank.

The author of the book *The Defense* is forced into saying that deficiency arises through the addition to what precedes; and the author of the book *The Correction* is forced into saying that perfection arises through itself, because it emanates from the Perfection that is its Cause. So saying that on *Jonathan's* side is correct, and saying this is also true on its own face; but the author of the book *The Defense* takes the author of the book *The Correction* from where he did not mean and not from his actual intent.

### Section Ten — the formula that closes the opening movement

The tenth sub-chapter wraps the opening movement of the argument. *Samuel* says: if *Jonathan* will not accept that the Soul is like the Intellect in the chapter on perfection, then if the Soul is better than the Intellect or less than it, *Jonathan* should not say the two are alike. By his own reasoning, *Jonathan* ought to say that the Soul is deficient.

*The author*: the author of the book *The Correction* does not say what the author of the book *The Defense* takes him to assert. *Jonathan* said: all things are perfect in themselves. There is a deficiency in a thing only in addition to that which is higher than it, so it has a degree of rank. What is not found in this additive relation — and if it is related to that which is above — is the aspect of prejudice against *Jonathan*.

The formula is the heart of the first ten sub-chapters. *The book *The Correction*'s* doctrine, rightly understood, is that every existent is perfect *in itself*; deficiency arises only *in addition* — that is, only when one existent is set in relation to another that stands above it. The Soul is perfect in itself. It is deficient only in relation to the First Intellect. To say the Soul is "complete" and to say the Soul is "below the First Intellect" are not contradictions; they describe the same Soul from two different vantage points. The error of *Samuel's* attack, across these ten sub-chapters, has been to treat "perfect" and "deficient" as ranks on a single scale, and then to demand that *Jonathan* place the Soul on one or the other. The Soul, in *Jonathan's* and *the author's* reading, is on both — perfect in itself, deficient in relation. There is no scale on which the Soul sits singly.

The Soul as a created proportion that knows itself from its source has been the contemplative ground of every Islamic tradition that ever meditated on the relation. *Jalal of the faith Rumi* opens the *Mathnawi* with the same instinct, naming the human soul as a reed cut from a reed-bed, complete in itself as an instrument, yet bearing in its sound the testimony of the source from which it was severed:

> *Bishnaw in nay chun shikayat mikunad,*
> *Az judaihaa hikayat mikunad.*
> *Listen to the reed, how it tells a tale, complaining of separations.* — (Rumi, Mathnawi, Book 1, Verses 1–2)

The contemplative register and the philosophical register are working the same axis. The reed is whole — without it there is no melody — yet its whole nature is to recall the bed it was cut from. *The author's* Soul, perfect in itself, is *Jonathan's* Soul that takes its existence from the First; the distinction *Samuel* has been collapsing is the distinction the reed insists on every time it sounds. Identity is not the same axis as rank.

This is the platform from which the next stretch of *Chapter One* will operate. The argument now turns to the celestial analogies — the sun and the moon, the male and the female, the body and the sperm, the Speaker and the Foundation — which carry the same formula into a series of pairings that the listener already half-recognizes from the world. The instinct that says the moon is the moon, even though it is less luminous than the sun, is the same instinct *Jonathan* is working from. The Soul is the Soul, even though it stands below the First Intellect. To call it "imperfect" because it stands below is to confuse rank with identity. That is what *Samuel* has been doing for ten sub-chapters; and that is what the author, sub-chapter after sub-chapter, has been refusing to let stand.
