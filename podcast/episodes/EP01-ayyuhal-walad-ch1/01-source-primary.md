# Imam Ghazali's Response to the Letter

*From* Ayyuhal Walad (My Dear Beloved Son or Daughter)
*Author:* Imam Abu Hamid Muhammad al-Ghazali (1058 to 1111 CE)
*Translator (English from Urdu, with original Arabic transliterations preserved):* Irfan Hasan
*Original work:* Ayyuhal Walad, from *Majmu'a Rasail Imam Ghazali* (Three-Volume Collection of Short Books)
*Source scope for this episode:* the chapter titled *Imam Ghazali's Response to the Letter*, the opening section of the epistle. Pages 3 through 14 of the printed translation.

This file is a refined and enriched presentation of the chapter, organized into its argumentative movements. The verbatim hadith and Quranic passages are preserved in blockquotes with the original transliteration. Ghazali's prose has been clarified for English readability and structured by beat. Nothing has been added that is not in the source. Anything Ghazali only implies is marked as such.

---

## The setting

The chapter is Ghazali's written reply to a long-time student. The student had studied under Ghazali for years, had read his major works including *Ihya Ulum al-Din* (Revival of the Religious Sciences), *Kimiya as-Sa'ada* (The Alchemy of Happiness), *Jawahir al-Quran* (Priceless Gems of the Quran), *Arba'in* (Forty Steps), and *Minhaj al-Abidin*. He wrote to Ghazali with a quiet, devastating question: of all the branches of knowledge he had acquired, which would actually illuminate his grave, which would benefit him on the Day of Judgment, and which were merely the decoration of a life. He asked also for a supplication he could recite daily and for some advice he could always act on.

The hadith that hangs over the entire chapter is the one the student cites in his own letter:

> A'auzu Billahi Min 'Ilm-il La Yanfa'au.
> I seek Allah's refuge from the knowledge which is of no benefit.
*Prophet Muhammad. Cited at the opening of the letter.*

The whole reply is Ghazali working out what this prayer means, and what it demands of the person who has prayed it.

---

## Movement 1: The opening warning: time, waste, and the scholar's danger

Ghazali begins with care. He writes that the constitution of all good counsel rests on the Prophet's *Ahadith* (traditions) and *Sunnah* (outward and inward way of life). If the student has already received that counsel, he needs nothing more. If he has not, then what knowledge has he acquired in all these years?

The first hadith Ghazali presses on him is a diagnostic:

> A servant of Allah's involvement in useless things is an indication that Allah has removed His special favors from him or her. And if the servant is spending even a moment from his life on things other than for which Allah has created him, then it is a matter of great sorrow.
*Prophet Muhammad, cited by al-Ghazali.*

The framing is sharp. Wasted time is not a neutral act in this text. It is a sign that grace has already withdrawn.

Ghazali then turns to a category of person he keeps returning to throughout the chapter: the one who has acquired wisdom, philosophy, and learning, and believes the knowledge alone will be his salvation. Ghazali calls this belief wrong. He cites the hadith that should haunt every scholar:

> On the Day of Judgment, the most severe torment will be given to the scholar to whom Allah had not benefited from his knowledge. That is, he did not act upon the acquired knowledge.
*Prophet Muhammad, cited by al-Ghazali.*

The educated man is in the most dangerous position of all. He has the proof against himself.

## Movement 2: Junaid's dream and the two analogies: only action saves

To press the point further, Ghazali tells a story from the Sufi tradition. A saint sees Shaykh Junaid Baghdadi (one of the most revered figures in early Sufism) in a dream after Junaid's death, and asks him about his state. Junaid replies:

> Worships and signs all turned out to be useless. But some cycles of prayers I did at the time of Tahajjud, the third part of the night, were of benefit.
*Shaykh Junaid Baghdadi, as narrated in a dream and cited by al-Ghazali.*

The most decorated worshipper in the tradition reports, from beyond death, that the bulk of his religious life amounted to nothing. Only the silent prayers he made in the last third of the night, when no one was watching, survived the crossing.

Ghazali then drives two analogies into the student. They are mirror images of one another and they share a structure: knowledge that is not acted on cannot save.

First, the lion in the jungle. A man crosses a wilderness carrying ten sharp swords, fine bows, and full knowledge of warfare. A lion confronts him. Can he be saved without drawing his weapons? Knowing the art of war is not the act of fighting.

Second, the sick man and the medicine. A man is ill and knows exactly which medicine will cure him. He does not take it. Will the knowledge of medicine cure him? Diagnosis is not treatment.

Ghazali pushes the analogies into a single line:

> Acquiring a lot of knowledge and continually browsing a lot of books but not acting upon that knowledge will not be of any benefit to you unless and until you make yourself deserving of Allah's mercy through deeds of righteousness.
*al-Ghazali.*

He underwrites the claim with a Quranic verse:

> Wa Anna Laeysa Lil Insaani illa Maa Sa'aa.
> Human beings will get only what they have strived for.
*Quran 53:39, cited by al-Ghazali.*

The student, anticipating a counterargument from the legal tradition, might note that this verse is held to have been abrogated. Ghazali concedes the technical point and then asks: what do you do with these verses?

> Famaey Ya'mal Mithqaala Zarratin Khairaey Yarahu Wa Maey Ya'mal Mithqaala Zarratin Sharraey Yarahu.
> And so, the one who shall have done an atom's weight of good shall see it, and the one who shall have done an atom's weight of evil shall see it.
*Quran 99:7 to 8.*

> Famun Kana Yurju Liqaa-a Rabbihi Fal Ya'mal 'Amalun Salihun.
> Whoever looks forward to meeting his Lord, let him do righteous deeds, and let him not ascribe unto anyone a share in the worship due to his Lord.
*Quran 18:110.*

> Innal Lazina Aamano Wa 'Aamaylus Sawlihaatey Kaanat Lahum Jannaatul Firdausee Nuzula Khalideena Feeha.
> Verily, those who believe in Allah and perform righteous deeds, for them the highest Paradise is for their hospitality in which they will live forever.
*Quran.*

The conjunction *and* in these verses is structural, not decorative. Faith *and* deeds. Ghazali piles the verses on each other so that no one verse can be argued away.

He then folds in the foundational hadith of the five pillars of Islam: testimony, prayer, zakat, fasting, pilgrimage. The first pillar is belief. The remaining four are actions. The architecture itself answers the question.

## Movement 3: The angel and the worshipper: the emotional pivot of the chapter

Ghazali anticipates the obvious worry. If a person reaches Paradise through deeds, are they not earning it? Are they not, in some way, putting Allah in their debt?

He cuts that worry off cleanly. Entry to Paradise is by Allah's grace alone. The deeds do not earn the grace. They prepare the person to receive it. He quotes:

> Inna Rahmata Allahi Qareebum Minal Muhsineen.
> Verily, the Mercy of Allah is close to the doers of good.
*Quran.*

Then he tells the story that is, in the architecture of this chapter, the emotional pivot.

A man among the Children of Israel was given to excessive worship. Allah sent an angel to test him. The angel said:

> Allah, the Holy, says that you put yourself in trouble for no use since your worship is not accepted by Him and you will go to Hell.
*The angel, as cited by al-Ghazali.*

The man replied:

> Allah, the Exalted, has created us for His worship and servitude and our job is to serve Him no matter what, and He is the Owner of each and everything and everyone including myself, and the Powerful One.
*The righteous man, as cited by al-Ghazali.*

The angel returned to Allah and reported the answer. Allah said:

> If this slave of Ours does not turn his face away from Our worship and servitude, then We will also not turn Our Face away from him. O My angels! All of you be My witnesses that I have forgiven him.
*Allah, as cited by al-Ghazali via the Prophet's narration.*

This is the line the chapter pivots on. The man who serves without any expectation of being accepted is the only one Allah accepts. Worship that demands its own reward is not worship. Worship that continues even when told it will be refused is the worship that is in fact received.

Ghazali then reinforces it with a Prophetic hadith:

> Haasibu Qabala Un Tuhasabu Wa Zinu Qabala Un Tuuzanu.
> Take account of yourselves before you are taken into account, and weigh your deeds before they are weighed.
*Prophet Muhammad.*

And he adds two more voices to keep the listener from settling into either complacency or burnout. Ali, on the two errors:

> A person who thinks that he will go to Paradise without righteous deeds is misguided, and the person who thinks that he will go to Paradise only through striving is merely busy with hard work.
*Ali ibn Abi Talib, cited by al-Ghazali.*

Hasan al-Basri, on what desiring Paradise without deeds amounts to:

> To desire Paradise without good deeds is a sin from among the sins.
*Shaykh Hasan al-Basri, cited by al-Ghazali.*

Both excessive confidence and excessive self-reliance fail. The path is between them, in the posture of the man who answered the angel.

## Movement 4: Intention, and the question at the grave

Ghazali then turns the lens directly on the student. The whole letter has been moving toward this moment.

The student has stayed awake at night, rehearsing knowledge, sacrificing sleep to study. Ghazali asks the question that decides everything:

> You stayed awake at nights to continually rehearse knowledge and sacrificed your sleep to study it. If your intention was to gain worldly benefits and worldly prestige and status, then *fawailul laka thumma wailul laka*, there is sadness and woe for you and again there is sadness and woe for you. However, if your intentions were to establish the Path to God brought by Prophet Muhammad, attaining moral uplift and eliminating evil from your self, then *fatuba laka thumma tuba laka*, for you is happiness and blessings, and again for you is happiness and blessings.
*al-Ghazali.*

Same body in the same chair. Same hours of study. Two opposite destinations. The discriminator is intention.

Ghazali then offers the most piercing line in the chapter, what he says is the first of forty questions Allah will ask the corpse between casket and grave. He reports having read this in the Gospel of Jesus:

> O My slave! You decorated yourself with knowledge of outward matters for many years to show off to the mortal creations of Mine. But for My sake only, did you ever purify your heart from evil and heedlessness even for a moment?
*Allah's question to the corpse, narrated by al-Ghazali from the Gospel of Jesus.*

The decoration of knowledge is the indictment. The hidden purification of the heart, even for a single moment, is the defense. Ghazali pairs this with a hadith of the Prophet that what Allah looks at is not face nor body but the heart.

The voice continues, Ghazali says, daily in the seeker's own chest:

> O My slave! Why do you worship Me for show off and ostentation, even though the good and the bad circumstances are both in My Hands? For this reason, you should worship Me alone with sincere intention and devotion.
*Allah's address to the heart, as narrated by al-Ghazali.*

This is where Ghazali lands the chapter's most quoted line, the one that is the spine of the entire epistle:

> Knowledge without actions is madness, and actions without knowledge are useless.
*al-Ghazali.*

The two halves are not symmetric. Knowledge without action is *madness*, a stronger word than *useless*. To know what to do and not do it is not merely a missed opportunity. It is a kind of derangement.

## Movement 5: The night, the dove, and the seeker's four conditions

Having pressed the intention test into the student's chest, Ghazali turns to what the student should actually do. The answer is the night.

He stacks the Quranic and Prophetic material on *Tahajjud*, the optional prayer in the last third of the night before dawn. The Quran:

> Kanu Qaleelum Minal Laili Ma Yahja'oon.
> There are only a few slaves of Allah who sleep little in the last part of the night.
*Quran.*

> Waminal Laili Fatahajjad Bihi Nafilatal Laka.
> And in the last part of the night perform the Tahajjud prayers, which will be very beneficial for you.
*Quran.*

> Wa Bil Ashaarihum Yustaghfiroon.
> And those true slaves of Allah at the time before dawn plead for Allah's forgiveness.
*Quran.*

The Prophet:

> Do not sleep too much at night, because the one who sleeps too much at night will be empty handed on the Day of Judgment.
*Prophet Muhammad.*

And the three sounds Allah loves: the rooster who wakes people for *Tahajjud*, the recitation of the Quran, and the voices of those who at the end of the night plead for forgiveness.

Ghazali quotes Shaykh Sufyan al-Thawri on a wind Allah created that blows in the last part of the night and carries the prayers of those engaged in remembrance up to the divine court. He quotes him a second time on a sequence of four angels who proclaim through the night, each in his hour, that the worshippers, then the obedient, then the seekers of forgiveness should rise. The fourth angel proclaims at dawn: *O you the heedless ones, get up.* Then these people rise from their beds, Ghazali writes, *like the dead will get up from their graves upon resurrection.*

He cites Luqman's will to his son:

> My son, do not let the rooster be more intelligent than you are. It shouldn't happen that he gets up at the last part of the night to make proclamations while you keep sleeping.
*Luqman, cited by al-Ghazali.*

And he closes the night-prayer section with verses of poetry that drive the indictment into the listener's chest:

> At night, the dove sitting on the branch of a tree is calling on to its Lord
> While I am in a deep sleep.
>
> By the Lord of the House of Allah, I am a liar. If I had been a true lover, the doves would not have won over me in weeping.
>
> Alas, I do not even wet my eyes with tears while claiming to love my Beloved Lord,
> While the animals keep weeping in their love for their Creator.
*Poetic verse cited by al-Ghazali.*

The dove weeps for its Lord. The man sleeps. The animals beat the man at love.

## Movement 6: Worship is obedience, and the pivot to the rest of the treatise

Ghazali turns from the call of the night to the question of what worship actually is. His definition is simple and severe: worship is obedience to the Shari'ah, the *Sunnah* of the Prophet. Whatever the seeker does or refrains from doing, says or refrains from saying, must be in accordance with the teachings of Muhammad. An act that looks like worship but is performed against the Prophet's commandment is not worship. Fasting on the day of Eid, when fasting is forbidden, is a sin. Prayer at a forbidden time is the act of a sinner. Conjugal relations within marriage, which look outwardly unrelated to worship, are an act of obedience because they are within the Prophet's sanction.

The point is structural. Worship is the second name of obedience. Without obedience there is no worship. Without the *Sunnah* the seeker is misguided, however much knowledge he has acquired. This, Ghazali writes, is why the Prophet abrogated all previous religious learning. The path is the path the Prophet walked, in its outward and inward dimensions both.

Then comes the chapter's hinge, the line that opens the rest of *Ayyuhal Walad*:

> You should not do anything without the guidance of Prophet Muhammad, and be certain that you cannot walk on the Path of Allah with merely the knowledge that you have acquired. However, this Path must be traveled through hard work and Mujahadah (intense inner spiritual struggle and exercises), and the pleasures of the self and desires from the lower self must be cut with the sword of Mujahadah.
*al-Ghazali.*

Knowledge is not the vehicle. *Mujahadah* is the vehicle. The inner struggle to subdue the lower self, performed under the discipline of the *Sunnah*, is what carries the seeker forward.

Ghazali warns against the imitation Sufis of his day, men who speak the language of the saints while their hearts remain hollow and devious. He writes:

> The tongue speaking the words of deep wisdom and beauty while the heart worshipping heedlessness and desires of the lower self, this is a sign of mighty misfortune.
*al-Ghazali.*

Until the desires of the lower self are cut with the sword of truth and *Mujahadah*, the light of *Ma'arifah* (the deep intimate knowledge of Allah) will not be born in the heart. The chapter ends pointing forward. The student asked his teacher to give him something he could always study and always act on. Ghazali has just told him the form of that thing: not more knowledge, but the four conditions of the seeker.

The four conditions, which open the next section of the epistle and which the rest of *Ayyuhal Walad* will unfold, are:

1. Belief and fear of Allah, complete enough that the seeker does not fall into *bid'a* (innovation outside the established principles of the Quran and *Sunnah*).
2. *Taubatun Nasuh*, true and complete repentance, of a kind that does not return to its sins.
3. Settling every obligation to created beings, including enemies, so that no one has a claim left on the seeker.
4. Acquiring exactly enough knowledge of the *Shari'ah* to fulfill what is obligatory, and no more than that, in support of obedience.

These are the doorway. The rest of the treatise walks through it.

---

## Where the chapter ends, and where the listener stands

The chapter ends with Shibli's report that he served four hundred spiritual masters and adopted, from the four thousand traditions they narrated, a single hadith that he found contained all knowledge:

> Work for the world as much as you live in it. Work for the life hereafter as much as you want to live there. Work for Allah as much as you are in need of Him. Work for the Hell as much as you can be patient with its pain and sufferings.
*Prophet Muhammad, as narrated by Shibli and cited by al-Ghazali.*

Ghazali closes the chapter by saying the student does not need much knowledge; acquiring more is *Fard Kifayah*, a communal obligation rather than an individual one. What the student needs is to act on the little he has.

The chapter has moved the reader, in a few thousand words, from the question *which knowledge will save me* to the answer *none, by itself*. The decoration of learning is the indictment. The hidden purification of the heart, even for a moment, is the defense. The night prayer is the one thing the corpse carries. Intention is the discriminator. *Mujahadah* is the vehicle. And the four conditions of the seeker are the doorway into the rest of the letter.
