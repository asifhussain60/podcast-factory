# The Master's Call and the Disciple's Covenant

*This chapter covers the opening of the book* The Master and the Disciple*, attributed to the author — a son of one of the great early missionaries of Yemen, and one of the founding voices of the Ismaili call. The book is the dialogue treatise that the tradition treats as its foundational narrative of how a teacher draws a seeker out of his world and binds him by oath into the Party of God. The author opens with a delegation of believers asking a Master among them what work answers the threefold blessing of being called, of being shown knowledge, and of being commanded to work. The Master answers them with the law of thanks, then turns to the doctrines of the righteous by way of a story — a Persian wanderer rescued from the calamity of ignorance who must now repay the debt by carrying the call forward. The wanderer travels through the lands until he reaches a city on the far edge of the Arabian peninsula, sits at the edge of an assembly of disputants, weighs them by three qualities, and finally rises and speaks. A youth from the group — the son of one of the great Arab elders — follows him home, and the long Master-Disciple dialogue begins: what is this matter; whose is it; on what authority does the Master speak; can a man secure it by acquisition, or only by favor; what are the limits and the conditions; and what shall the disciple do about his father? After a long absence, on a quiet night when the informers have grown forgetful, the disciple finds the Master again. The Master recites to him the covenant of God — the great key that distinguishes the lawful from the unlawful and admits the bearer into the company of God's friends. The disciple weeps until his tears pour down, and the chapter ends on the moment the binding is completed and the Master begins to expound.*

## Where this chapter picks up

This is the opening of the book. *The Master and the Disciple* is a dialogue treatise composed in the early Fatimid period and assigned by long tradition to the author — a son of one of the great Yemeni callers of the founding generation, and one of the principal voices of the early Ismaili mission. The book takes the form of a sustained conversation, in the manner of the early Greek dialogues but on Islamic doctrinal ground. A Master arrives in an Arabian town; a disciple comes to him with a question; the dialogue runs through fourteen sections that move from the law of thanks, through the architecture of the cosmos, into the moral world, into the question of the elder and the naming, and on into the climactic confrontation with the disciple's own father over the unity of God and the necessity of the messengers. The book ends with the testament of the dying Master and the disciple's assumption of the chair the Master leaves behind.

Within this larger architecture, the opening chapter does three things at once. First, it states the *law of thanks* that the rest of the book will fill in: the one who has been awakened owes the one who has not yet been awakened the labor of awakening, and the one who has received the call owes the call onward as he received it. Second, it tells the *biography of the caller* — the Master is himself a former wanderer who was rescued from ignorance, and the asymmetry between Master and disciple is therefore not an asymmetry of essence but a matter of which side of the awakening the two of them happen to stand on. Third, it stages the *first transmission* — the binding of the covenant, by which the disciple passes from being one of the people of his town to being one of the Party of God. Everything that follows in the book runs on rails laid down in this chapter: the seven principles, the two layers — the apparent outer and the inner — the figure of the speaker-prophet at the head of a cycle, the ranks of the summoners; all are taken up later because the covenant has first been struck here.

The chapter unfolds across three movements. The first is the *prologue and the law of thanks*. The second is the *Master's arrival* — the autobiographical story of the Persian seeker, his journey to the edge of the Arabian peninsula, the assembly in the town, his sermon, and the youth's pursuit. The third is the *long dialogue* — the disciple's first question, the Master's instruction in method, the test of the disciple's submission, the imposition of the five conditions, the long separation, the reunion, and the binding of the covenant.

## The preachers' question and the law of thanks

The book opens not with the Master speaking, but with a delegation of believers and preachers of the religion approaching him with a question. They speak as students who have understood what is incumbent on them but cannot yet see how to discharge it. *You have set us at liberty,* they say to the Master, *by reminding us of what you have made us aware of, which we are obliged to fulfill — and that on three counts: thanks for that to which you have called us, thanks for the knowledge you have shown us, and thanks for the work you have ordered us to do. So show us, from each of these, what one who wills may do to render that thanks.* The triple structure is precise. The believer owes three thanks for three blessings, and the question is what *act* answers each. They tell the Master that they have already understood, from the limits of religion, what is obligatory upon them — the student in his question and what is required in his answer — and they have already understood, from the doctrines of the righteous and the manners of the seekers, what is easy to say. What they want from him is the harder thing: not the easy formulation, but the work.

The Master replies in a single composed paragraph that becomes the rule of the book. *The matter to which I have called you is the one I honor. May God be with His servants, and may He complete it for them and ennoble those who answer Him.* Then he sets out the structural claim: God has made *for the first of every thing a last, and for the last of every thing an end, and for every end a further end — and these are the houses of the hearts.* The houses of the hearts are sustained by *remembrance*, and remembrance unfolds in three stages: *affliction* at the first, *guidance* in the middle, *piety* at the last. Whoever has sought to understand the structure of things, and stood upon the limits of obligation within his reach, has *intended guidance along the features of the request* and has walked in it in the best way.

Then comes the threefold answer that anchors the whole book:

> As for thanking the Master, it is to obey him; as for thanking the knowledge, it is to act upon it and call to it; and as for thanking the work, it is to be patient with it and call to it.

Three blessings, three thanks: obedience answers the Master; action-plus-call answers knowledge; patience-plus-call answers the work itself. The Master has not yet named what the *call* is, what the *knowledge* is, what the *work* is — the rest of the chapter will draw those out. But the rule has been laid down. The one who is called is obliged to call.

The Master's whole formula sits on top of the Quran's most foundational rule of thanks:

> If you give thanks, I will give you more.
> — Quran 14:7 (standard English rendering)

The verse is the engine the Master's threefold law runs on. Thanks, in the Quran's grammar, is not a sentiment about a blessing already received; it is the act that *increases* the blessing. The Master's three answers — obey, act-and-call, be-patient-and-call — are the three modes in which a believer keeps thanks active rather than letting it close. The increase the verse promises is not a second helping of the same blessing; it is the next stage of the same blessing's work in the world.

## The Persian wanderer and the calamity of ignorance

Having settled the rule, the Master shifts genres. He turns from formula to narrative. *As for the doctrines of the righteous and the manners of the seekers,* he says, *we have heard that a man from the people of Persia was struck by the calamity of ignorance.* The man, the Master continues, was no ordinary fool. He possessed a *heart of keen retention, a sound intellect, and a culture he had acquired* — the equipment of a serious mind. *Yet ignorance prevailed over him with regard to the knowledge we have mentioned, and he grew up in that state, wavering in the heat of thirst, reckoning the glittering mirage to be the whiteness of water — so that when he came to it, he found nothing of it, and he found God there waiting for him, and his reckoning was settled.*

The image is precise: the man was not lazy, not stupid, not corrupt. He was a thirsty man chasing a mirage of cold water in the desert, doing exactly what a thirsty intellect does in the absence of true knowledge — chasing the brightest signal. And the wonder of the story is that *God was waiting at the mirage*. The man arrives at what he thought was water, finds nothing of what he expected, and finds *God*. *Then He honored his resting-place and uncovered him from his cover, and found him in a light, and so guided him and found him out. He became his sustainer and enriched him with knowledge — so that he became one of the scholars of Sinai and one of the dwellers of the Frequented House — the heavenly archetype of the holy sanctuary, above which the angels offer their continual worship.* The Persian wanderer, the Master is telling his audience, is now one of those who stand on Sinai and dwell in the angels' sanctuary — that is, he has been admitted to the highest rank in the architecture the call will spell out across this book.

The image of the wanderer-revived is exactly the image the Quran uses for the convert:

> Is one who was dead and to whom We gave life, and made for him a light by which to walk among the people, like one in darkness, never to emerge therefrom?
> — Quran 6:122 (standard English rendering)

For the author, this is not metaphor. The man brought from heedlessness to the call has been *raised from death*; and the man who has been raised owes the same revival to those who are still in the dark. The verse fixes the asymmetry the Master will return to: a living man and a dead one are not in the same genus, even if they share the same town.

The narrator pauses here, but the Master continues into a longer reflection. When the man's guidance was completed and *he had reached the end of his hope, it became incumbent upon him to give thanks to his Maker and to toil for his Lord.* He remembered a saying of his father, *one that was with him as the soul is with the body*: *the best of good things is to raise the dead.* And here the man's interior monologue runs: *I was dead and He revived me, ignorant and He taught me. I was not the first to be ignorant — that I might be the one to precede people to knowledge — and I am not the last to be ignorant — that teaching might end with me. In truth, I must be one who gives thanks for this blessing and who conveys this trust to those after me, just as it was conveyed to me by those before me.*

The thought that fixes him is the doctrine of *transmission*. This matter is the first of God's commandments, *and it has reached me only through many causes — the first reaching the second, the second the third — even if those who liked it most were the highest of God's creatures in the world.* The chain that runs through the speakers of the call (the Master will name them later as Imams and speaker-prophets and proofs and chiefs and summoners) is what brought the call to *him*; *the veil was breached, and the doors stormed, until it reached me.* And then the man asks himself the question that decides the rest of the book: *So am I its endpoint and its end?* The answer is *no* — those who transmitted the call and were faithful to it had the prior claim in respect of gains and virtues; if their gains are not his, then he rests on the fact that he is *the one who gained from them through their work*; and if their bounty does not exempt him from the work, then *he remains as poor as he was poor when he first sought knowledge*.

The same rule sits at the center of the teaching of the Prophet (peace and blessings of Allah be upon him) about transmission, preserved in the canonical hadith collections:

> Whoever guides to good is like the one who does it.
> — The Prophet, in the canonical hadith collections (Book of Government)

The transmitter does not stand below the practitioner. The chain that carried the call to him places him alongside every prior bearer in the reward. The Persian wanderer's resolve is, in effect, to enter that chain on its own terms: not as a final station, but as one more link.

The poverty of the seeker, the Master is telling his audience, does not end with knowing. Knowing only changes the direction of the poverty: from the poverty of the ignorant who has nothing, into the poverty of the called who has been entrusted with everything and now owes the call onward.

The Father of Imams (may God be pleased with him) named the same paradox in his counsel to a trusted student, preserved in the book *The Peak of Eloquence*:

> Knowledge is better than wealth. Knowledge guards you, while you must guard wealth. Wealth decreases with spending; knowledge multiplies with use.
> — The Peak of Eloquence, saying 147

The Master's image of the seeker's poverty turns on exactly this axis. Ignorance is poverty that nothing can fill. Knowledge is wealth that increases as it is spent on others. The seeker is poor either way — but the second kind of poverty is the kind that owes onward, and that is the only kind that can be discharged.

## The Master arrives at the assembly

Having understood that his duty is the very thing that is obligatory upon him, and that his last command is *in judgment the same as his first*, the Persian seeker takes the practical step. *He left his family and his wealth for the sake of his Lord, answering Him and praying for the good, calling toward the obligation,* and passed *through the peoples of the lands and through the tribes of the Arabs, scanning faces and casting questions — yet he found no answer and met no fellow seeker — until he ended at the far reaches of the Arabian peninsula.* He has crossed the inhabited world looking for someone to whom the call can be given, and has found no one until he reaches the far edge.

He enters a city at a time when *its people were heedless*. The phrase is the technical term of the call — *heedlessness*, the state of a population from which the call has been withdrawn, where the assemblies of the religion have lost their direction and the people have fallen back upon their own opinions. He arrives at a moment of withdrawal. *Recalling the signs and verifying the marks, he saw a group of the people of that land quarreling aimlessly over religion and wading in the depths of caprice. So he sat down at a little distance, in seclusion from them, listening to their words and watching to see which of them had the best method.*

His judgment of the group is precise. *These are the foremost in three qualities in living debate,* he says to himself: *the first is the conveying of the argument; the second is that they are of the people of the Muslim community; and the third is that they are closer in their awareness of religion, in their observation of it together, in their searching for it together — and the seeker is on the verge of meeting what he seeks.* He has not picked them because they are correct. They are quarreling and wading in caprice. He has picked them because they are *arguing*, because they are *Muslims*, and because they are *searching*. The form of the seeker matters more than the content of the answer. A man who is searching can be turned. A man who is not searching cannot.

When the group has finished, they notice the stranger sitting at his distance and turn to him. *Who are you, and from where, young one?* He gives the first of the responses that will run through the book — answers calibrated not to information but to *direction*. *I am a servant of God, and I am one of the dwellers of His sanctuary.* The answer is precise: he names his condition (servant of God), and he names his location (the inviolable sanctuary of the holy city), and he gives neither a tribe nor a town. He is not concealing himself; he is locating himself in the only frame that matters.

They press: *What is your need, and what is your work?* He answers: *My need has passed me by, and I am at work in seeking it.* The need that *has passed by* is the need they would expect of any stranger — a place to sleep, a guide, a transaction. The work he is *at work in* is the call. They have asked the wrong question, and he has given them the answer to the right one.

They ask: *Did anything of what we said please you?* He answers: *All of it is strange — to one who marvels.* They counter, alert: *Wonder is in two senses — praiseworthy and reprehensible.* And he agrees and turns the distinction onto words themselves: *Yes, I have learned that. So too, words are praiseworthy and reprehensible.* What is the praiseworthy kind? *It is what is correct.* And what is the correct? *That whose origin was from God, and which calls to God.* And the reprehensible? *That whose origin was caprice, and which calls to something other than guidance.* The four-part exchange has, in eight short lines, set out a doctrine that the rest of the book will work in detail: *speech is judged at two joints — its* source *and its* target. *Speech from God and toward God is the praiseworthy kind. Speech from caprice toward anything other than guidance is the reprehensible kind.*

The group recognizes the move. *We believe you,* they say. *Have you nothing of your own words for us to hear?* He answers in the formula of a caller — *I have nothing to say — but I am a follower of the right saying.* They press: *Then let us hear from your words what you may show us of your doctrine. We have wearied you of your condition, and may your brotherhood be a blessing to us. We hope that you will be a door of mercy that God has opened to us.* He consents — *Yes* — and the chapter pivots from the interrogation into the sermon.

## The Master's first sermon to the assembly

The Master *listened to the people, and received them with his face*. The phrase is liturgical — the *receiving with the face* is the gesture of a teacher who has admitted the audience into the act of teaching. And then he speaks the longest single passage in the chapter — a sustained address to the gathering on the structure of creation, the hiddenness of God's secrets, the *deposit* of the call in the breasts of the righteous, and the architecture by which the religion is carried.

*He who created the creation by His power has made each thing equal to what He has decreed for it.* The Master begins with the principle of *decree*: the creation comes out *measured*, every thing in proportion to what God has decreed for it. God is *the One who has brought forth the highways of the pasture, and made his outward and his inward free of caprices.* God is not ambiguous and is *not reached by the study of mere traces* — even when *imaginings are bewildered by the demand of Him, and minds neglect the search for His essence*, God *does not appear in being so as to be possessed by His attributes or so that the intention of His path can be altered.* The negative theology is firm: God is not *in being* in such a way that His attributes can be made into a possession; the intention of His path cannot be redirected by what creatures do with His names.

Then the Master shifts from the description of God to the description of the *call*. *Rather, He is generous: His knowledge is hidden by its very grace, and His secrets are protected by their proper family.* The image is concrete: God's knowledge is not hidden *against* anyone; it is hidden by its own *grace* — the hiddenness is the very condition that makes it knowable to those who would treat it rightly. The secrets are *protected by their family* — by the lineage of those qualified to bear them. *His honor is the plumage of guidance; he is completed by the garment of piety, marked with the light of lights — a deposit in the breasts of the righteous, the keepers of the Word, the honor of the scribes, a trust which they discharge and a limit which they do not exceed — it is the rule of judgment and the bounties of the Most Merciful.*

The doctrine of the *deposit* is the doctrine of the call in seed-form. The call is a *trust*; it is borne by a *family* (the Prophet's family and those who inherit their work); it is *discharged* by them as a duty and is *limited* by them as a discipline. And it is shielded from those who would handle it without right. *He honored him and shielded him, protected him and concealed him, persuaded him of his rights and entrusted to him His covenants. So the thoughts of the thinkers do not reach him, and the schemes of the schemers do not arrive at him.* The Master is naming the *proof of God* without naming him — the argument of God, who stands between God and the worlds; who cannot be reached by *thinking* alone because the matter is not in the genus of what thinkers reach; who cannot be reached by *scheming* because the matter is not in the genus of what is held by force.

The Master then names the *two layers*. *His outward is a remembrance and an evidence for those who are guided, and his inward is a sure drink, whose sky is brought low to the beholders and whose land is paved for the seekers.* The *outward* — the apparent — is the remembrance and the evidence, the visible scripture and the visible practice. The *inward* — the hidden inner — is the *sure drink*, the inward meaning whose *sky* is *brought low* for those who behold and whose *land* is *paved* for those who walk on it. The two are joined: the *outer* is for the guided, the *inner* is for the seeker. Both are required. *He has a guide for those who walk to Him, and signs whose wonders follow one another — she points to Him by her parables. His path is preferred, his method laid out in detail, his rulings binding, his prophetic practice the enacted law, and truthfulness has ennobled their doctrines, and justice has refined their gains.* The *guide* is the speaker-prophet; the *signs* are the summoners; the *path* is the sacred law; the prophetic practice is its enacted form.

And then the Master closes on the human condition. *For every good there is a leader and a way, and He has made it a paradise and a guide to its inner chamber. Then minds were compelled, by ignorance and by want, to seek knowledge and capacity. Blessed is He who has made night and day a succession for those who would be reminded or would give thanks; and may God bless those whom He chose from among them to worship Him and made a warning to the worlds.*

He ends on the imperative: *Fear God, and fear those who fear Him, and fear Him with the fear of one who would please Him. Seek knowledge, learn, work, and have mercy.* The fivefold call — *fear, seek, learn, work, have mercy* — is the chapter's first set of injunctions to the audience.

His eyes overflowed with tears. He broke off, took leave, *rose, and set out on his journey*. The people wept at the warmth and the eloquence of the sermon and wished to rise with him; but their *reply, in order to preserve courtesy, was to celebrate with him in all this. They did not know what they had agreed to do, so they went off to their houses.* The narrator's wry observation is important: the audience has been moved by the sermon but has not yet been *bound*. They will go home and continue as before. The transmission of the call requires more than a moving sermon.

## The youth follows the Master home

But not the whole audience returns to its houses. *He was followed by a youth from the group — the youngest and the most thoughtful of them — whose discipline had perfected his intellect and whose reflection had filled his heart, and this came on top of what he had heard from him of the Master's words, and of what had been laid before him and granted to him.* The narrator adds the detail that will hang over the rest of the chapter: *the youth had a father who was a great elder of the Arabs — the most honorable and the most influential of them over the people of religion.* The transmission of the call into this particular disciple will run through a household that is itself one of the local thrones of the religion. The risk and the value are both higher because of who his father is.

The disciple *kept the company of the elder-Master, with his permission, until the Master reached the end of his journey*. The Master brings the disciple into his lodging. Food is called for. They eat, and converse, and settle in. And then the disciple speaks the long opening that will frame the dialogue for the rest of the book.

*O wise one!* he says. *I have spoken and I have heard and I have preached; I was informed, and I sweetened the religion with the finest ornament and desired it with the best desire many times over, and I called to it by the noblest of its trusts.* He has been a participant in the religion of his town — not a sleeper. *The minds yearned for your question, by the cracks your words made in the hearts.* Then he gives the formula that is itself the disciple's first move toward the inner: *This excellence could only have appeared from one in whom the inward has been integrated as a teacher, and the outward has expanded his dream — for he is the seventh fountain of wisdom from a firm origin, his blessings upon the worlds, and the branches of his branches are fruits for the seekers.* He has read the Master against the typology of the *seventh* — the figure whose inner has been completed and whose outer has expanded, who is the *seventh fountain* from a firm origin and whose branches yield fruits for the seekers. The disciple is recognizing a *rank*. *I hope you may be one of those blessed branches, for what I see of your noble opinion, sound core, and perfect intellect. You have said that minds are compelled by ignorance to seek knowledge and capacity, and my mind is among the most needy and the most destitute. So may I have life from the way?*

## The Master's first instruction: the law of bringing minds to knowledge

The Master's answer is the first systematic teaching of the book. He restates the principle that opened the sermon — *He who created the creatures by His power did not create them in vain, nor as scholars from the start, nor does He abandon them in the misguidance of ignorance, nor is He content for them with stupidity in culture, nor with the garments of ignorance* — and then he names the *way out*: *He has made for them, to the exit out of all this, the way by which He composed their constitution and completed their creation.* The constitution of the human being is itself the road. *He made hearing, the eyes, and the hearts full of lights, as a sign of His dignity to them and as an argument against them.*

Then comes the doctrinal centerpiece. *He made the treasures of His wisdom and the marks of His will reside in doctrines that satisfy Him in His elect from among His creation. He entrusted them with religion and called them to it, and imposed upon them the answering of Him, by the instrument we have mentioned, so that God might distinguish the evil from the good. So the seeker is able, by his demand, to see — and the one ignorant of his own backwardness is made known — for there is no argument left to anyone against God after His Messengers and no excuse for going astray.*

The disciple answers in the form that the dialogues of the call will return to many times. *The lack of any excuse has frightened me, and the existence of the evidence has made me desire it. I implore you for a proof, and I will be a servant to you.*

The Master replies with a more precise version of the rule. *God did not create men already scholars. Rather, He created them as little children and ignorant ones who knew nothing. There is no great except after small, and no scholar except after ignorance. Smallness is the prelude to greatness, and ignorance is a guide to knowledge.* Then he draws out the pedagogical consequence: *knowledge is not bearable except with the introduction of some knowledge prior to it — knowledge quicker than itself to the hearts and smoother to the understanding — so that he may be steadied in his first imaginings, the trials may be lightened for his obligations, the wings may shiver in him for it, and minds may bear its purpose.* The lesser knowledge precedes the greater knowledge; the practice of God in His servants is gradation. *Let us not deviate, in religion, by our own opinion, nor follow with us anything other than the prophetic way.*

## The disciple presses for proof; the Master offers method, not mercy

The disciple now pushes the dialogue into its first real test. *I am too small for what I want, so raise me; I am ignorant of what I have asked for, so teach me; and I am bound by your favor, so have mercy on me.* He recalls the Master's own story back to him: *You were once in this very situation, so God forbid that you should withhold the blessing — through my hands — of the greatness of His affliction upon you, for which He is to be thanked through you. I have learned the condition of the one who suffers the distress of ignorance and the need for the weakness of resourcefulness.*

The Master's reply refuses the appeal to mercy and turns the disciple back upon himself. *The state you have described is indeed as you say, but I will not show you mercy by doing for you what should be done with your own effort.* The rule that follows is one of the formative rules of the entire book:

> Every claim is accepted only when it is truthful; knowledge does not benefit unless it is acted upon, and action does not benefit unless it is accepted. The truthfulness of speech is its justice, and the proof of knowledge lies in the public ways and documented exemplars that have endured throughout the lands and through which night and day have differed.

Then the five-part method: *Do not pursue an argument carelessly, do not dismiss one carelessly, and do not accept one on imitation alone — lest the first doubt that rises against it tear it out of your heart. Do not reject the proof of an argument either, lest falsehood find an opening; and do not let your own opinion render inquiry and consensus pointless.* The discipline is calibrated: a claim received without scrutiny will be unmade by the first counter-claim; a proof rejected without examination opens the door to falsehood; a single mind that lets its own opinion override inquiry and consensus has cut itself off from the conditions under which truth can become firm. The Master closes the rule with the positive command: *Think well of me without reservation, strive in your questioning without suspicion, seek the religion, seek one like yourself, and seek God's help in your affair. God does not let the seeker's reward go to waste, and does not deprive of reward anyone He sees seeking the good.*

## The matter to which the Master calls

The disciple now turns to the question of *substance*. *I ask God's help, and I ask you about this matter you are calling me to: what is it, whose is it, and to whom does it belong?* Three questions in one: *what* is it, *whose* is it (who is the proprietor), and *to whom* does it belong (who is the audience).

The Master answers: *It is the religion of God that He has accepted, and from whom it can be accepted is no one but Him.* The religion belongs to God; its acceptance belongs to God; *He delivered it first to the elite among His Messengers, and then in general to all His creation.* The specially favored owes thanks for it, action upon it, and a call to others to take it up; the one deprived of it owes the seeking after it so that he may come to know it, and obedience to the one who guides him to it. The asymmetry between the *elite* and the *general* is fixed at the origin: God delivered first to the elite, then to the general; the response of the elite is thanks-plus-call, the response of the general is seeking-plus-obedience.

The disciple presses on. *This favor that God has singled out for His friends — are all such favors equal in their excellence, or do some surpass others?* The Master answers from the principle of the chain: *Have we not said that every form of knowledge has its predecessor who taught it, and that each predecessor carried his own portion, so that they stand at graded ranks before their Lord, each one charged with what he has acquired of knowledge? Then every one of them is set right in his creation by the merit of his bestowal, and it becomes a mercy upon His servants.* The favors are *graded*; the chain is *ranked*; each rank carries its own portion and is set right by the merit of its bestowal.

The disciple presses once more: *This thing you are calling me to — is it that favor itself, or is it something other than it?* The Master answers with the figure that runs through the rest of the book: *It is neither identical with it nor wholly other than it — it is from it, like a single chamber of water drawn from the breadth of a great river. I call you to what God has accepted for His servants and honored. It is the day on which I complete it — precious, secure, generous, hidden. Its honor with its reward is dignity for the one who lifts it up; its retribution is debasement for the one who loses it. Its outward form is figures, and its inner reality is knowledge. Its wisdom is a yoke, and its justice is plain. This is what I call you to.*

The figure of the *single chamber drawn from the breadth of a great river* is the standard way the early callers distinguished their teaching from the wider community: *this branch is of the same water as the great river, not a different water and not the whole river either*. It is the formula by which the Ismaili teaching distinguishes itself from the broader Muslim community: it is not *another* religion, and it is not *all* of the religion; it is *of* the religion, drawn from the same river, with its own *outward form* of parables and pointers and its own *inner reality* of knowledge.

## The argument from need: action separates, ignorance joins

The disciple now poses the hardest of the early questions. *What has made you more exclusively entitled to this matter than others? If you claim it is from God for creation in general, then everyone has the same claim as you. What has made you more deserving of the name of truth than they — has this matter come to you bypassing them?* The objection is the standing objection to the call: if the religion is for everyone, what makes your party more entitled to it than mine?

The Master's answer is one of the great formulations of the early Ismaili tradition on the relation between the *acknowledgers* and the *agents*. *There is no scholar who speaks any word of truth except that he has taken it from the very origins from which we ourselves have taken it; in this they agree with us, by acknowledging it from us and among themselves. What then separated us from them was their abandonment of those principles while we held fast to them: we obeyed where they disobeyed, we preserved what we were entrusted with where they squandered it, we did what we were commanded where they neglected. So they agree with us in acknowledgement and disagree with us in fact.*

The verdict turns on the difference between *acknowledgment* and *action*. *Their acknowledgement is a witness for us; their loss is the sign of their poverty toward us and of their need for what is in our hands.* And then the great parable:

> They are like brothers who inherited great wealth from their father and divided it between themselves: some squandered it until they fell into poverty, while others kept it, preserved it, traded with it, and the wealth in their hands multiplied. Then the poor among the brothers turned to the wealthier ones, to serve them and seek their bounty. So too those who have lost the way of their Messengers turn, in their ignorance, to seek knowledge from the people of the original sources, the generous ones.

The proof, the Master concludes, that *we are more deserving of the truth than they* is *their need of us and their dependence on what is in our hands — so that our knowledge has become the call that answers their ignorance, our garment covers their nakedness, and our abundance satisfies their hunger. So the truth is binding upon those who would be virtuous, the credit goes to the one who confers favor, and thanks is owed to the one who bestows kindness.*

The disciple accepts the verdict on the others, but presses on his own behalf: *As for your superiority over them, that has become clear; and as for the argument of truth, it has been established for every honest seeker. But what is your argument against me, when I already agree with what you have said, stand by what you have described, deny what you have denied, and recognize the truth and its excellence as well as falsehood and its people?* The objection is precise: if the difference between the Master and the others is *action* not *acknowledgment*, and the disciple already *acknowledges*, then where is the Master's argument *against the disciple*?

The Master refuses the easy answer. *My argument against you is the same as my argument against them — through my disagreement with them in description and my agreement with them in meaning.* The disciple presses: *How is that, when I acknowledge the truth to its owner, and they deny it?* And the Master draws the line: *If their denial is what keeps you back from joining them, they too have refrained from what is forbidden to them. So what is the benefit of acknowledgement without an agent to put it into effect?* The lesson is the formative one: *acknowledgment without an agent* is the same as *denial*. The disciple who acknowledges the truth without an agent to give it to him stands, in respect of *fact*, where the deniers stand. Both are *outside* the working transmission.

The disciple makes the move that the dialogue has been preparing him to make: *Then take me to the people of truth that I may know them — or to an agent who can prove it to them. Show me which of the two you can do.* The Master closes the trap. *Do you not see that you have been made to need the proof of the rightful owner of the truth, or of his agent, just as the public has been compelled to need a proof of the truth? Action has separated you from them, even as ignorance has joined you with them.*

The line — *action has separated you from them, even as ignorance has joined you with them* — is the chapter's pivot. The disciple is now in a different category from the assembly he left behind. He is no longer simply one of *the people of his town*. He has been *compelled to need a proof*. The disciple yields: *I have believed, and the matter has become clear to me.*

The Master tightens the argument. *Have I not firmly established my argument against you and against the public?* *Yes.* *Can anyone prove to others a truth he holds for himself?* *No one is able to do that.* *Then the argument has been established against you and against the public. Nothing remains but submission, and the appointment of the one whom you cannot do without and who can do without you.*

The asymmetry is now named: *the one whom you cannot do without and who can do without you* is the proof of God in the time, the one who stands as the argument. The argument cannot be carried from one mind to another by one mind on its own authority; it requires *appointment*. The disciple concedes: *As for my poverty, I acknowledge it. As for the One who is rich while I am in need — I ask of Him, and I ask Him to have mercy on me.*

## The disciple submits; work over flattery

The Master now offers the last of the framing rules. *Have you ever seen someone driven by poverty to seek aid from a man of bounty obtain anything from him except by acquisition or by favor?* The disciple: *Yes — nothing can be obtained except by acquisition or by favor.* And the Master: *And do the people of favor reach the people of grace by way of acquisition?* *No.*

> Then seek this matter by work, and you will be granted it as a merit. Do not seek it by flattery, or you will be deprived of it.

The verse-like formulation is the rule that closes the first half of the dialogue. The matter is granted as a *merit* to those who seek it by *work*; it is *withheld* from those who seek it by *flattery*. The same hand that grants by grace withholds from those who try to flatter their way in.

The disciple answers in the formula of the receiver. *You are the key to goodness and its guide. Show me the most meaningful of the two paths and the nearest to guidance. I am the most destitute of those who follow you.*

## The five conditions and the question of the father

The Master now closes off the framing and moves to the binding. *O my son, there is nothing more for you, and nothing more is expected of me. What you have hoped from me has reached you — but there are limits not to be transgressed and conditions not to be forgotten.* The disciple agrees in advance: *Set the limits you love, and lay down the conditions you approve. I hope you will find me patient under them, and grateful for your kindness.*

The Master gives the limit first. *The first of the limits is to uphold the outward meaning of the Book and its laws, and to act upon what you know of it. Whoever does for God what he knows, God will guide him to what he does not know.* The principle is the working principle of the outer-and-inner doctrine: *act on what you know of the outer, and the inner will be opened to you*. There is no inner without the outer; the road to the inward runs *through* the apparent, not around it.

The disciple answers in a way that drops the first wall. *There are many Books, and all of them are from God. For every Book there is a scholar from among creation who adheres to it, and they are all united in safeguarding it and in the religion it contains.* It is the courteous formulation of the Abrahamic ecumenism that any town might naturally hold — the books are all from God, every book has its scholar, and the scholars are united.

The Master refuses the formulation. *We have returned to our earlier words, and we now need to settle what should not have needed settling.* The disciple asks how. The Master: *By your saying that the Books are many, and that all of them are from God, and that each has a scholar of its own. Have they then issued the verdict that the words of God and His Books cancel one another out — that the first of them gives the lie to the last, and the last gives the lie to the first?*

The disciple concedes that he had not thought so. *But what is the proof of it?* The Master gives the *succession*-argument. *If created beings had been able to do what is in the first Book, then it would have pointed them to the second; and if they did what is in the second, it would have pointed them to the third — so that the work culminates in the last of them, because the last is what the earlier ones were pointing toward. Even though all of them are from God, the later is nearer to the present age and clearer in doctrine, since it copies and confirms what came before it, and nothing came after it to copy or supersede it.*

The disciple answers: *I have believed; the knowledge of the truth and the sign of truthfulness have been shown to me. I am holding to the limits you have ordered me to keep — so what are your conditions for me?*

The Master gives the five.

> My conditions for you are five: do not be absent if I summon you; do not conceal anything from me if I ask you; do not question me until I answer you; do not request anything until I begin with you; and do not mention what passes between us to your father.

The first four are conditions of *availability and rank*: be present when summoned, conceal nothing, do not interrupt with questions, do not demand. The fifth is the practical condition that everything else in the book will turn on: *do not mention what passes between us to your father*.

The disciple accepts all five — *I will do all that you have mentioned — it is easy for me set against what I have hoped from you* — but then asks the obvious practical question. *But how can I manage the matter of my father? He is one of the heaviest trials upon me, and one of the heaviest I have to bear — so will you permit me to remove myself from him?*

The Master refuses the move into open separation. *Withdrawing from him would be a sign to others.* The withdrawal itself would be a *sign* — would advertise that something has happened. *Rather, stand with him and his estate with some outward agreement, and keep all this your secret and conceal it. God will be enough for us against his evil and the evil of others.* The doctrine here is the discipline of *outward conformity and inward concealment* in its earliest and most concrete form: outward agreement with the father's household, the father's religion, the father's estate, and inward concealment of the bond just formed. The disciple is to remain in his father's house, in outward conformity to his father's order, and to keep the covenant secret.

## The long absence and the night of reunion

The narrator now takes the chapter to its third and final movement. *Then the scholar and the disciple took leave of each other, and they were apart for a long time.* The disciple is left *perplexed about his affair: he knew nothing but what the law had brought him to stop at, did not know where the scholar was settled, and was unable to seek him out under the condition imposed on him, even as the absence lengthened — while the scholar showed him his own patience and endurance for what it cost him.* The asymmetry of the bond is now under load: the Master can wait; the disciple cannot. The disciple bears the cost of the absence as the test that justifies the bond. The Master is *also* bearing the cost, but in *patience and endurance*, not in *perplexity*.

A great Persian poet of the spiritual path opened his long poem of spiritual instruction with the same hunger by another name:

> Listen to the reed, how it complains, telling the tale of separations: since they cut me from the reed-bed, my cry has caused men and women to weep.
> — The Spiritual Couplets, opening verses (Nicholson rendering)

The disciple's perplexity during the long absence is the cry of the reed cut from its bed. To be cut from the Master is to be cut from the source one has just begun to taste. The poet's image and the author's narrative converge on the same point: the seeker's pain is not a defect in the bond; it is the proof that the bond has taken hold.

The reunion is choreographed by the narrative voice. *At last, on a quiet night when creation was still and the informers had grown forgetful, when they had settled together in their assembly, the disciple chanced upon him as his need pressed close and he was able to be alone with his companion.* The phrase *the informers had grown forgetful* is the precise descriptor of the conditions under which a covenanted meeting can take place: when the informers of the town, who would have reported the meeting to the disciple's father, have lost interest. The disciple rejoices.

When the disciple raises his head, the Master speaks: *I saw you prostrating in thanksgiving. Thanksgiving before the work inherits the obligation; thanksgiving cut short after the work makes the works themselves vain.* The rule is the third statement of the law of thanks (the first was at the chapter's opening; the second was the law of the elite and the general). *Thanksgiving before the work* (the prostration the disciple has just made) *inherits the obligation* — it places the work to come under the order of obligation. *Thanksgiving cut short after the work* — the failure to follow through with continuing thanks once the work is done — *makes the works themselves vain*. The disciple answers in the formula of the receiver again: *All your words are truthful, and all your works are mastery. I thank you for your kindness, and I hope from you for yet more of it.*

The Master closes the formula: *Thanksgiving is owed because gratitude is what makes thanksgiving increase. Your right has been fulfilled — so come to me with your heart, open your understanding, and listen to what is presented to you.* The disciple responds in the same liturgical register: *I have brought the instrument of understanding and I have taken up the instrument of hearing. Say what seems good to you.* The two instruments — *understanding* and *hearing* — are the two faculties the Quran itself names as the doors of obligation; the disciple is naming them as he hands them over.

## The key, and the covenant of God

The Master begins the binding. *Religion has a key that opens it and closes it, like that which distinguishes between forbidden union and lawful marriage.* The simile is direct and shocking: the key to religion is what stands between an unlawful union and a lawful marriage. Two acts can look identical at the level of the bodies; what separates them is the *contract*. So too with religion: two religious lives can look identical at the level of the outer; what separates them is the *covenant*. The disciple answers, properly arrested: *This key you have mentioned is great in the sight of God, since it is what distinguishes between the lawful and the unlawful, between truth and falsehood. So what is it?*

The Master gives the answer in the language that the rest of the book will return to repeatedly.

> It is the covenant of God which establishes His rights, gathers His obligations, makes Paradise binding for His friends — the rope of God upon His earth, and the safe haven among His servants. I will recite it to you, and bind you to it.

The covenant is *His rights established*, *His obligations gathered*, *Paradise made binding* for those who hold to it. It is *the rope of God upon His earth*, the line that connects the earthly seeker to the heavenly source. It is *the safe haven* among His servants — the place where the bearer is sheltered from the storm. And it is given by *recitation* and *binding* — the Master *recites* the words; the disciple is *bound* by them.

The figure of the *rope* sits directly on a Quranic verse the Master is invoking without quoting:

> Hold fast, all of you together, to the rope of God, and do not separate.
> — Quran 3:103 (standard English rendering)

The verse is the warrant for everything the Master is about to do. The covenant is the rope; the rope is collective before it is individual; what the disciple is about to receive is a place in a line that is already running. The binding is not the invention of a new bond but the joining of one more hand to a rope already held by many.

The disciple answers in the only words a receiver can answer with: *Yes — take from me whatever you love. I do not refuse your opinion, and I do not transgress your command.*

The narrator then takes the chapter to its closing image. *Then the scholar set about reciting the covenant to the disciple, conveying it to him and binding him fast by it, while the disciple could not contain himself for awe, and his tears poured down from the intensity of the instruction, until he came to the end of the covenant. He praised God and glorified Him, and thanked Him for what He had granted him; and he understood that, by accepting their pledge, he had become part of the Party of God and of the company of His friends. Then he fell silent in his composure — and the scholar began to expound and explain.*

The chapter ends on the threshold. The *recitation* has been completed. The disciple has been *bound*. He has entered the Party of God and the company of God's friends. He has *fallen silent in his composure* — the silence of the receiver who has been placed under the order of the bond. And the Master *begins to expound and explain*. Everything that follows in the book — the architecture of will and command, the seven principles, the figure of the speaker-prophet and the proof and the summoners, the doctrine of the outer and the inner, the moral world, the confrontation with the father, and the closing testament — begins in the silence into which the disciple has just fallen.

The chapter is the door, and the covenant is the key. The Master has called, the disciple has answered, and the call of the book has now begun its long work.
