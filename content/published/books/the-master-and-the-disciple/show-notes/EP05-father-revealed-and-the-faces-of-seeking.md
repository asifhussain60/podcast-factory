# Show notes — EP05

**Title:** The Father Revealed and the Three Faces of Seeking

**Length:** 50 to 60 minutes (extended tier)

**NotebookLM format:** Debate. Length: Long.

## Blurb

The chapter where the book at last names them. Chapter four ended on the father's anger. The disciple — re-born into the chain on the seventh day by the greater elder, given the highest transmission the source itself veils, and walked back to the edge of his city by the scholar — returned home with his heart absent in fear, and his father came to him, angry. This chapter is the answer.

The father reproves; the disciple offers two strands of gratitude (outward courtesy with divided hearts, or open debate with confidence between them); the father rejects the protective-concealment strand ("how long can outward agreement stand when its underpinnings differ?") and chooses debate; the disciple lays his bind ("either you are a scholar who withheld the teaching, or you are ignorant and needed the stranger; either way, I will precede you"); the father weeps, concedes, "they debated at intervals and disclosed things to each other at length, until they were guided to the right path." Through them, God revived many of His creation. The disciple was sent out into the world to preach, with his father's blessing.

Then — for the first time in the book — the source names them: the disciple is known by a name meaning "the righteous one"; his father by a name marking his early reputation. The book's framing convention preserves them by role only. News of them reaches a group who held to the old creed; the community goes to the senior scholar of the old creed and asks him what to do about the blood and property of these crossers-over.

The senior scholar refuses to judge by anger. He gives the figure of the breach ("a breach in religion is gravely damaging, like a breach in medicine — its flaw cannot be repaired") and lays out three states the new community could be in: "either they are upon what we know of religion (only with additions of literature, narrations, poetry); or they have been given an increase in what they hold — and knowledge is a lost thing of the believer; or the people have given them guidance other than what they have, by the prophecy of a prophet who has been brought to them. This is not denied by the command of God… God is at work every day."

The senior scholar voices the three-states doctrine that includes prophecy after the books as a live possibility. He warns the community against becoming "like the tyrants of the previous nations, who said God would not send messengers after their messengers… and even killed the prophets of God." He humbles their hearts, teaches them the door of seeking ("the needy person must humble himself before what he needs"), the sound heart ("the heart that does not persist in denying what is said to it"), the reverence-before-the-statement doctrine, and the petitioner-before-the-king parable. He collapses the timetable from three days to two to "only this day, if you will do it; otherwise I go on alone." They all repent, pay debts, bathe, put on the purest garments, and go out to the scholar-father's gate.

The senior scholar addresses the father by the honorific "father of the righteous one"; the father plays the title back open — "today my father is good, and I am the son of the righteous one; and he is good with his Lord." The senior scholar names the transpositions (the child has become the father, the Lord is in the earth, the Lord has a servant come to Him from him, the one who created annihilation has become eternal) and asks for the righteous servant. The scholar-father grants it under chain-discipline. The young teacher clears the council and welcomes them.

The chapter's central exchange is the long young-teacher to senior-scholar dialogue. The young teacher's opening rebuke — addressing the senior scholar as the paradigm of imitation-without-direct-knowledge — "is it imitation, then?" The senior scholar's concession: "religion stands above imitation."

The prophet-warner doctrine: "the warner is a prophet, and the prophet is an argument from God, both for-them and against-them." The three faces of seeking: a seeker who knows, a seeker who does not know, a seeker who knows that he does not know — with the parallel between money-seeking and Hereafter-seeking (the divine scholar who seeks out the dead; the learned man seeking the full end; the ignorant who knows his ignorance). The senior scholar places herself in the third — the knowing seeker who knows nothing but knows that he is in need.

The young teacher probes: knowledge by report or knowledge by direct sight? — and lays out the poverty-distress-capacity doctrine: "ignorance is a need, poverty is the narrowness of need, distress is a great need that compels the creature to seek capacity; and capacity is the breadth of knowledge, because knowledge is wide." The senior scholar confesses dire need.

The young teacher: "how do you know me, without having seen me beside a scholar of prophecy?" The essence-traders parable: the old creed's essence, presented to the people of metals, was found to be glass in camouflage. "Knowledge is not knowledge until it is presented to those who know the science of heaven."

The senior scholar raises the question that frames the closing movement: "we know of no prophets and messengers in our time; nor have the people had any covenant with such men since the revelation was cut off by the books." The young teacher: "any religion that is established only on narration is not called a religion." The books themselves fail because they give only the general.

The divorce-case parable: the senior scholar, asked for a ruling, gave two opposed rulings and said "act on whichever of the two you wish" — the petitioner goes home not knowing whether the woman in his house is his wife or another man's. The senior scholar's confession: "the sacred law of my age has been lost, corrupted, and disrupted, and we hold on only to the name of religion."

The young teacher: "what is religion?" The senior scholar: "command, prohibition, lawful, forbidden, tradition, obligations." The young teacher: "so you have combined religion and sacred law; then religion has been disrupted by the suspension of the sacred law — what is left with you?" The senior scholar sits a long while thinking, raises her head: "I do not see that anything is left with me. So what religion do you have?"

The young teacher's positive doctrine: "religion is with me, and I am not veiled from it… the cause that connects heaven to earth, unbroken — the loop of the firm handhold, the rope of those who hold fast, the ark of tranquility and the ship of life and the light of life, generous in its content, safeguarded in its sciences, marked in its jewels, its virtue hidden from no one who looks upon it."

The chapter ends on the senior scholar's seam-question into chapter six: "if God has a religion by which He will judge on the Day… but now, since it has been lost, the attribute is preferable to him — so how is it described?"
