## The Nature of Pleasure and Suffering

The author of this treatise examines ⟪ar-quote:لَزَّۃ⟫ (*lazz*, pleasure) and ⟪ar-quote:اَلَم⟫ (*alam*, pain), establishing that pleasure and sorrow exist in two fundamental modes: spiritual and physical. Within each mode lie numerous subsidiary states—happiness, relief, tranquility on the one hand; anxiety, fatigue, and unease on the other. These varied affections, though distinct in nomenclature, operate according to a unified principle: all arise from deviations from the body's and soul's natural equilibrium.[^cite-1]

The text distinguishes pleasure as the physical sensation accompanying the soul's removal from pain, while ⟪ar:ھم و غم⟫ (*hamm wa gham*, worry and sorrow) constitute spiritual pain. Physical distress originates when the four humors—⟪ar:سودا⟫ (*sawdāʾ*, black bile), ⟪ar:سفرا⟫ (*safrāʾ*, yellow bile), ⟪ar:بلغم⟫ (*balgham*, phlegm), and ⟪ar:دم⟫ (*dam*, blood)—exceed or fall short of their proper temperament. Comfort is spiritual pleasure; pain is physical; sorrow is spiritual pain. Thus:

| Physical | Spiritual |
| --- | --- |
| Pleasure (*lazz*) | Comfort |
| Pain (*alam*) | Worry and sorrow |

### Food Enjoyment

The All-Merciful has fashioned within the human frame a stomach—a vessel resembling a bag—wherein the ingested material undergoes transformation through digestive heat. This heat consumes the food, generating vital energy for the entire body. When the stomach has processed all its contents, it begins, in its emptiness, to consume itself—much as a flame consumes the candle that has entirely burned away.[^cite-2] This self-consumption occasions suffering; the awakened soul then generates appetite and craving. When food reaches the stomach anew, its heat resumes the work of digestion, and the person emerges from suffering into relief—the state called *lazz* (pleasure).

Similarly, the liver's heat produces thirst. When the liver lacks moisture, it too begins to burn itself; water restores balance, and thirst is quenched in a state of repose called *lazz*.

Should a person consume food or drink in excess, however, the stomach or liver's capacity becomes overloaded; the digestive heat proves insufficient, leaving the body burdened and distressed. Both hunger and surfeit represent departures from moderation—the former causing pain through deficiency, the latter through excess. The descent from excess toward balance is termed relief or pleasure; the ascent from deficiency toward balance is also *lazz*. Hence the Prophet, upon him be peace, declared: "The best course in all matters is the middle way." The state between two extremes of suffering is pleasure itself; any rupture of this balance at once occasions pain.

The Divine Wisdom has made it incumbent upon all animals to consume food and drink perpetually. Should this cease, the body's organs would begin to devour themselves, producing unbearable distress. The stomach's function is both to digest and to empty itself, so that it may receive fresh nourishment. Thus every animal eats and excretes in unbroken succession: after each state of pain comes pleasure's restoration; after each pleasure, pain's cycle returns.[^cite-3]

Mawlānā ʿAlī observed that the entire cosmos mirrors the human form. As the human being possesses stomach, liver, and other organs subject to hunger and thirst, so too must the world—the macrocosm—obey the same principle of pleasure and pain. Just as bodily hunger arises from the appetite of the flesh, so the soul's hunger—called *nafs* (desire)—drives all seeking in the spiritual order.

### The Pleasure of Intercourse

Male and female animals obtain pleasure through sexual union. The relief issuing from food is called pleasure; the relief arising from the expulsion of accumulated seed is called lust. The Divine has lodged sexual potency in semen. When the quantity of blood within a person increases beyond the body's capacity to use it, that excess blood is transmuted into semen. At this point, heaviness develops in the ⟪ar:اخلاط اربع⟫ (four humors), and all four endeavor to expel this semen from the body—an expulsion that brings desire for sexual union, itself a state of discomfort.

The analogy is precise: when urine and feces accumulate in the body and remain unvoided, the person suffers acutely. So too with semen (*mani*). This arrangement, rooted in divine wisdom and grace, concentrates lustful desire in the human being so that sexual union might occur and the human race advance. When the animal expels semen through intercourse, the heaviness accumulated in the four humors returns to moderation, and the person experiences *lazz*—tranquility and peace. This ease, however, is achieved only when the animal unites with a suitable mate; copulation with an inappropriate partner yields neither comfort nor peace.

> Allah Almighty has concealed the form of the human being within the vessel of semen. Were this not so, humankind would not appear in this world. This process unfolds eternally in the realm of generation and corruption.

Should the animal engage in excessive intercourse, expending semen beyond what the body can replenish, the balance is again disturbed, and a different species of pain ensues.

### The Pleasure of Sleep

Repose comes also through rest, tranquility, silence, and sleep. When an animal exerts itself excessively, friction among the organs generates heat and constipation in the temperament. The organs and muscles thereby become desiccated, thick, and rigid. Sleep serves to moderate the temperament: through repose, the body's members cool, moisture is restored to them, and all the nerves recover their facility of movement.[^cite-4]

Thus excessive exertion creates fatigue; the removal of that burden brings relief. This cycle of pain and pleasure continues unceasing: excessive movement overheats the temperament, driving the humors toward imbalance; sleep and tranquility then restore equilibrium, and sleep becomes comfort and pleasure.

Yet when the organs grow too cold, the body acquires a need for movement; the person awakens. Should one sleep beyond necessity, the temperament becomes excessively cooled, and the humors again fall into disarray. Thus even sleep, like all states, must maintain the narrow path of moderation to preserve the soul's well-being.
