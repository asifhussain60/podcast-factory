## Ādāb and the Ten Foundations — Introduction

The Messenger of Allah, upon whom be peace and blessings, would recite a particular prayer each morning upon leaving his home to begin his day:

⟪ar-quote:⟪ar-quote:⟪ar-quote:⟪ar-quote:⟪ar-quote:⟪ar-quote:⟪ar-quote:⟪ar-quote:الّٰلھُمَّ انی اعوذبک أَن أَضَلَّ أَو أُضَل⟫⟫⟫⟫⟫⟫⟫⟫

"O Allah, I seek refuge in You that I should go astray or be led astray;

⟪ar-quote:⟪ar-quote:⟪ar-quote:⟪ar-quote:⟪ar-quote:⟪ar-quote:⟪ar-quote:⟪ar-quote:أَو أَزِلَّ أَو أُزَل؛⟫⟫⟫⟫⟫⟫⟫⟫

or that I should stumble or cause another to stumble;

⟪ar-quote:⟪ar-quote:⟪ar-quote:⟪ar-quote:⟪ar-quote:⟪ar-quote:⟪ar-quote:⟪ar-quote:أَو اَظْلِم او أُظلَمَ عَلَیَّ⟫⟫⟫⟫⟫⟫⟫⟫

or that I should commit injustice or suffer injustice;

⟪ar-quote:او أَجھَل او یُجھَلَ عَلَیَّ⟫

or that I should display ignorance or be treated with ignorance."

It is customary to begin the study of knowledge with the topic of *ādāb* (comportment and discipline). This is precisely why the very first subject mentioned in the *Chahal Sabāq* (the forty foundations) is *ādāb*. To understand why this is so vital requires careful consideration of its etymological and spiritual significance.

[diagram: Two green-bordered boxes — left: 'أدب' (manners/discipline), right: 'بدأ' (to begin) — connected by a green double-arrow labelled 'انقلاب' (inversion).]
  (see ../images/001.png)
  *Arabic labels in image:* ⟪ar:أدب · انقلاب · بدأ⟫
  *Note:* Pedagogical wordplay: 'adab' (manners) is the letter-inversion of 'bada' (to begin).

The Arabic word for "beginning" is ⟪ar:بَدَأَ⟫ (*badā*). One of the fundamental meanings of the word "heart" in Arabic, *qalb*, derives from the concept of *inqilāb* — literally, to reverse or invert something. When one reverses the letters of ⟪ar:بَدَأَ⟫ (the letters *bā*, *dāl*, and *alif*), the result is *ādāb*. Thus *ādāb* is understood as the heart of knowledge itself — the portal through which the purification of the heart commences, and the threshold through which every genuine spiritual journey must pass. This is why Islamic tradition is so firmly rooted in the principle of *ādāb*. [^cite-1]

*Ādāb* is a subtle term, difficult to render precisely into English. It is commonly translated as "etiquette" or "courtesy," yet these words fail to capture its full resonance. The derivative noun *adīb* denotes an erudite person—one who is learned, possessed of excellent manners, and endowed with refined courtesy. A more fitting translation would be "comportment," which derives from two Latin roots signifying "how one carries oneself." Just as a porter carries luggage, *comportment* describes the manner in which one carries oneself through the world. This is the essence of *ādāb*.

What is remarkable about *ādāb* is that it is not something prescribed by *sharīʿa* (sacred law) alone. Rather, *ādāb* represents the most natural expression of human conduct, rooted in sound common sense. Much of *ādāb* is simply the application of basic decency and consideration—principles that any person of conscience would recognize as self-evident.

A telling incident from Islamic history illustrates this principle. After the Battle of Khaybar, which was fought against the Jewish tribes, the Messenger of Allah instructed a companion to bring a notable woman from the Jewish community, Ṣafiyya bint Ḥuyay, to his tent. As the companion escorted her through the battleground of Khaybar, she witnessed the bodies of her slain husband lying upon the earth. Overcome with grief, she began to wail. Hearing her cries, the Prophet emerged from his tent and asked, "What is the matter?" The companion replied, "I have brought Sophia as you commanded, but upon the way she saw her husband among the fallen and began to weep." The Messenger of Allah looked at him and asked, ⟪ar:این رَحمَتُکَ؟⟫ "Where is your mercy?" [^cite-2]

With these words, the Prophet made clear that one requires neither revelation nor divine law to comprehend basic human decency. This is the heart of *ādāb*: the natural expression of compassion and consideration that should guide human conduct.

The Islamic tradition places profound emphasis upon the relationship between student and teacher, between the seeker and knowledge itself. This bond has always been characterized by reverence and compassion. The Prophet said:

⟪ar-quote:لیس مِنَّا مَن لا یَرحَم صَغِیرَنَا.....  ولا یُوَقِّر کَبِیرَنا⟫

"Whoever among us does not show mercy to the young, and does not honor the great among us, is not truly of us." [^cite-3]

Here, *kabīr* (the great) does not refer merely to age; rather, it encompasses anything of high rank or great value—including, pre-eminently, divine knowledge itself.

In our present age, *ādāb* has become an endangered species, nearly extinct. Children show disrespect to parents; students show negligence toward teachers and toward knowledge itself. We abandon the *ādāb* of speech, of eating, of sleep. It is therefore unsurprising to encounter people within our religious community who have acquired profound knowledge, yet whose hearts remain wretched and unmoved. When one traces the cause of this disconnection, it almost invariably leads to a deficiency in *ādāb* during the acquisition of knowledge. For this is not mere academic learning—this is divine knowledge, celestial knowledge that proceeds from Allah, the Most High. He bestows this gift only upon the chosen few, and for a singular purpose: that the recipient should internalize it, practice it, and through the synthesis of knowledge and deed, purify the heart.

The Prophet said:

⟪ar-quote:من عَمِلَ بِمَا عَلِمَ ..... وَرَّثَهُ اللهُ ما لَم يَعلَم⟫

"Whoever practices what he has learned, Allah will grant him as an inheritance knowledge of what he did not know." [^cite-4]

Those whose lives exemplify this principle are marked by a distinctive characteristic: as their knowledge increases, so too does their practice deepen and transform. The beauty of this formula lies in its transparency. By observing what a person does, one can discern what they have truly learned. For when knowledge is acquired and then applied, understanding deepens. Yet if one neglects proper *ādāb* while receiving this divine gift, the knowledge becomes reduced to mere academic information—something interesting to know but spiritually inert. It remains confined to the intellect, never reaching the heart. Many people within Islam, and indeed within our community, bear witness to this tragic reality.

Therefore, we must cultivate genuine courtesy and respect toward Allah, the Most High. We must conduct ourselves with proper reverence in the presence of His divinity if we wish to achieve the true purpose of this knowledge—the purification of our hearts. Do not mistake this assertion: there is a divine presence in these gatherings. When Allah is invoked, He always responds.

⟪ar-quote:وَإِذَا سَأَلَكَ عِبَادِى عَنِّى فَإِنِّى قَرِيبٌ⟫

"And when My servants ask you about Me—indeed I am near."

⟪ar-quote:أُجِيبُ دَعْوَةَ ٱلدَّاعِ إِذَا دَعَانِ⟫

"I respond to the call of the supplicant when he calls upon Me." [^cite-5]

What is remarkable in this verse is that Allah does not say:

⟪ar-quote:وَإِذَا سَأَلَكَ عِبَادِى عَنِّى**فَقُل** إِنِّى قَرِيبٌ⟫

—"When My servants ask you about Me, tell them I am near." No. Rather, Allah removes the Prophet from the conversation entirely and places Himself directly within it:

⟪ar:وَإِذَا سَأَلَكَ عِبَادِى عَنِّى⟫.....⟪ar:فَإِنِّى قَرِيبٌ⟫

"When My servants ask you about Me—I am near." This is the sublime rhetoric of the Qur'ān, wherein Allah displays His immediate presence directly within the verse. Exalted is He!

Thus when Allah is invoked, He is present in that very instant. We know that the angels gather in assemblies where the Name of Allah is mentioned. If only we possessed the sight to witness it, we would behold angels surrounding us in this moment—praying for us, seeking forgiveness for us, simply because we invoke the Name of their Beloved.

The first prerequisite for acquiring this knowledge is therefore to display proper *ādāb* toward this Most Majestic King of Kings. If possible, be in a state of *wuḍū* (ritual purity) when you listen to these sessions, for:

⟪ar-quote:حصول العلم من العبادۃ⟫

"The acquisition of knowledge is a form of worship, and there is no worship without *wuḍū*."

Be mindful of Whose presence we occupy—whether in these sessions or in any other gathering, any *majlis*, any study circle. Allah, the Most High, is bestowing upon us His greatest gift. Do not diminish this gift through distraction. Do not check your phones, do not send messages, do not occupy your attention with worldly matters. These distractions reduce the efficacy of this gift for you. This knowledge is genuinely transformative. May Allah open the doors of our hearts to receive this divine knowledge, and may He purify our hearts through it.

## The Ten Foundations of Knowledge

Knowledge begins with *ādāb*, and the ultimate purpose of all divine knowledge is the purification of the heart. An entire branch of Islamic philosophy, known as *taṣawwuf* (Sufism), addresses this science. All of its teachings derive directly from the sayings of Mawlānā ʿAlī (upon whom be peace) as recorded in the *Nahj al-Balāgha* and other authentic sources. [^cite-6] All Sufis unanimously recognize Mawlānā ʿAlī as their spiritual leader and guide. Yet like *ādāb*, this topic remains among the most neglected of all Islamic teachings.

The underlying principle is this: before one studies knowledge, one must purify the heart. For no one benefits from knowledge if the heart is impure. The heart is the most precious possession a believer possesses. This is why the Qur'ān teaches us to pray:

⟪ar-quote:رَبَّنَا لَا تُزِغْ قُلُوبَنَا بَعْدَ إِذْ هَدَيْتَنَا⟫

"Our Lord, do not let our hearts deviate after You have guided us." [^cite-7]

The Qur'ān employs many terms to express Allah's wrath toward the disbelievers, and that wrath almost invariably manifests through effects upon the heart. Allah states:

⟪ar:ختم اللہ علی قلوبھم⟫—"Allah seals their hearts."

⟪ar:فَقَسَتْ قُلُوبُهُمْ⟫—"Their hearts become hardened."

⟪ar:كَلَّا..... بَلْۜ رَانَ عَلَىٰ قُلُوبِهِم⟫—"Nay, but there has gathered upon their hearts that which they have earned."

⟪ar:فَلَمَّا زَاغُوٓا۟ أَزَاغَ ٱللَّهُ قُلُوبَهُمْ⟫—"When they deviated, Allah caused their hearts to deviate."

The Qur'ān employs many such terms—⟪ar:أَکِنَّ⟫ (coverings), ⟪ar:أقفَلَ⟫ (locks)—to convey that all spiritual destruction originates in the heart:

⟪ar:أَفَلَا يَتَدَبَّرُونَ ٱلْقُرْآنَ أَمْ عَلَىٰ قُلُوبٍ أَقْفَالُهَآ⟫

"Do they not reflect upon the Qur'ān? Or are there locks upon their hearts?"

For the believer, the heart is the most vital entity. What purifies and cleanses the heart is knowledge—yet the heart must be prepared to receive it. The preparation resembles the cultivation of soil before planting: just as one plows the earth to remove stones and weeds before sowing seed, so too must the heart be cultivated. The heart must be plowed to remove negative qualities before it can be adorned with the virtues.

⟪ar-quote:اول التَّواضَع التَّکَلُّف⟫

"The beginning of humility is to force oneself to be humble." [^cite-8]

The entire philosophy of *taṣawwuf* centers upon this cultivation of humility. The root of the word *taṣawwuf* is *ṣūf*, which means "wool." Thus *taṣawwuf* literally means "to wear wool"—a practice adopted by early ascetics as an outward declaration of their spiritual station in the world. Wool is rough and uncomfortable; it lacks the softness of fine garments. The essence of Sufism lies in engaging with this world by voluntarily relinquishing its fleeting pleasures and vain desires for a higher purpose. It does not mean abandoning family and retreating into exile; rather, it signifies living in the world yet free from attachment to it. If one studies the *sīra* (biography) of the Messenger of Allah and of Mawlānā ʿAlī, one finds precisely this: they willingly endured the hardships of this world in service of a greater cause. They exchanged the beautiful, silken garments of worldly comfort for the rough cloak of sacrifice. This is the essence of *taṣawwuf*.

Before embarking upon the journey of knowledge, one must understand ten foundational principles concerning that knowledge. This is what the philosophers of *taṣawwuf* call ⟪ar:مَبادِی العَشرۃ⟫ (*mabādiʾ al-ʿashara*, the ten foundations). As the scholars say:

⟪ar-quote:أِنَّ مبادئ العلوم العشرة؛ الحدُّ و المَوضُوعُ ثُمَّ ثَمرۃ⟫
⟪ar-quote:و فَضلُهُ ونِسبَةُ و الوَاضِعُ ؛ و الاسمُ و الِاستِمدَادُ و الشَّارِع و الحُکمُ⟫
⟪ar-quote:وَ مَسائِلُ و البَعضُ بِالبَعضُ اِكتفى ؛ و مَن دَرَى الجِميعُ حَازْ الشُّرَفَا⟫

"The ten foundations of every science are these: definition, subject matter, fruit, virtue, relation, originator, name, principles, ruling, and details. Some may be known through knowledge of others; but whoever comprehends all ten attains the highest rank in that science."

### الحد (Definition)

The first foundation is *al-ḥadd*—the definition. In logic, a definition is that which penetrates to the essence of a thing. A proper definition must be simultaneously inclusive and exclusive: it must include everything that the thing is, while excluding everything that the thing is not.

Consider the definition: "A human being is a rational animal." Human beings are animals—they belong to the animal kingdom rather than to the mineral or plant kingdoms. Yet they are distinguished from other animals by their rationality and capacity for wisdom. This definition captures what it means to be human: membership in the animal kingdom united with the faculty of reason. It thus excludes inanimate matter, plants, and irrational animals.

The first step in acquiring knowledge is therefore to understand clearly the definition of what one is about to learn.

### المَوضُوعُ (Subject Matter)

The second foundation is *al-mawḍūʿ*—the subject matter. This answers the question: What is this thing that I am learning about?

### الثَّمرَۃ (Fruit and Benefit)

The third foundation is *al-thamarah*—the fruit, the benefit of studying this thing. What is the ultimate goal? What is the purpose?

### الفَضْلُ (Virtue)

The fourth foundation is *al-faḍl*—the virtue of that thing. Why should it be learned? Not all knowledge merits pursuit. Some knowledge is superfluous; some is an outright waste of time, such as commercial jingles or trivial songs. One can acquire such knowledge, but it squanders the precious capacity of the mind. This is why believers recite in their daily invocations:

"O Allah, I ask You for beneficial knowledge"—

knowledge that will illuminate and purify the heart. There exists knowledge that is actively harmful to the heart, knowledge that corrupts it. Most of us carry our minds burdened with trivia; I confess I would gladly reclaim the portions of my memory consumed by such useless information. Yet what we are about to study is profoundly beneficial. This is extraordinarily fruitful knowledge, and we must understand its virtue and why it deserves our time, effort, and full consideration.

### النِّسْبَۃُ (Relation)

The fifth foundation is *al-nisba*—relation and association. What is this knowledge related to? What companion fields of knowledge link with it? What context does it occupy within the broader landscape of human understanding?

### الوَاضِعُ (The Originator)

The sixth foundation is *al-wāḍiʿ*—the originator, the one who first formulated this science. Who is the source of this knowledge? This is genuinely important, for we would not wish to invest our efforts in knowledge stemming from a dubious source, would we?

### الاسْمُ (Name)

The seventh foundation is *al-ism*—the name or names by which this knowledge is known. Every field of knowledge has one or several names.

### الاسْتِمْدَادُ (Foundational Principles)

The eighth foundation is *al-istimḍād*—the foundational principles upon which this knowledge rests and from which it is derived.

### الشَّارِعُ و الحُکْمُ (Authority and Ruling)

The ninth and tenth foundations are *al-shāriʿ* (the legislative authority) and *al-ḥukm* (the legal ruling). One must understand the legal status of this knowledge. Is it *farḍ ʿayn* (obligatory for every individual)? Is it *farḍ kifāya* (obligatory upon the community as a whole)? Is it recommended? Is it optional?

The scholars then conclude:

⟪ar-quote:و مَن دَرَى الجِميعُ حَازْ الشُّرَفَا⟫

"And whoever understands all ten of these foundations attains the most honorable rank within that science."

These are the *mabādiʾ al-ʿashara*—the ten foundations of knowledge.

## The Ten Foundations of *Tawḥīd*

### الحد (The Definition of *Tawḥīd*)

*Tawḥīd* is defined as the science that investigates the nature (*al-māhiyya*) of Allah, the Most High. It concerns itself with the nature of *rubūbiyya* (Divine Lordship), with the nature of *ilāhiyya* (Divine Godship), with the nature of *al-asmāʾ wa-l-ṣifāt* (the Divine Names and Attributes). These latter concepts are also termed *taḥrīd* (separation) and *tanzīh* (transcendence), which we shall examine in due course.

*Tawḥīd* is thus the study of the nature of Allah, the Most High, and subsequently the examination of how this nature manifests itself throughout the spiritual and physical orders of creation. It therefore encompasses the study of the nature of the prophets, the nature of *wilāya* (the sacred guardianship or authority), and the nature of *samʿiyyāt* (eschatology—the doctrines concerning death, the Day of Judgment, and the final destiny of the soul).

### المَوضُوعُ (The Subject Matter of *Tawḥīd*)

The subject matter of *tawḥīd* comprises the essence and attributes of Allah, the Most High; the attributes of His prophets; the attributes of His religion, which encompasses the Seven Pillars of Islam; the various articles of faith; the study of *qadar* (Divine decree and destiny); and finally the study of *ʿadl* (Divine justice).

### الثَّمرَۃ (The Fruit of *Tawḥīd*)

The fruit and benefit of this knowledge lie in its protective function: it safeguards us from error in our understanding of Allah, the Most High. There are those who imagine the universe to be eternal, yet the Qur'ān is abundantly clear:

⟪ar-quote:بَدِیعُ السموات و الارض⟫

"[He is the] Originator of the heavens and the earth" (*badīʿ*)—one who brings forth creation without any precedent pattern.

⟪ar-quote:علی غیر مثال المُسبَق⟫

"Upon no prior example."

He is also called ⟪ar:فاطر السموات و الارض⟫ (*al-fāṭir*, "The Bringer-Forth"), the One who explosively brought forth the heavens and earth. He is ⟪ar:خالق السموات و الارض⟫ (*al-khāliq*, "The Creator"). He is *al-Bāriʾ* (The Evolver) and *al-Muṣawwir* (The Fashioner, The Shaper).

When one contemplates merely the single act of creation, how many Names and Attributes emerge! He is *al-Badīʿ* because He employed no prior pattern in His design. He is *al-Khāliq* because He physically created the cosmos. He is *al-Fāṭir* because His creative act was explosive and immediate in nature. He is *al-Bāriʾ* because He conferred material substance upon all creation. He is *al-Muṣawwir* because He fashioned and bestowed upon all things their particular form and shape. From this single act alone, so many Divine Names unfold.

Thus one comprehends the profound importance of this science. We possess both theory and practice. When we grasp the true essence of the theory, our practice transforms. When we learn that God is just, and that injustice is inconceivable in Him, the mysteries of *qaḍāʾ* and *qadar* (Divine decree and destiny) become easier to accept. When we understand that He is *al-Malik* (The Sovereign) and we are *al-mamlūk* (the possessed, the enslaved), when we grasp that He is *al-Rabb* (The Lord) and we are *al-marbūb* (the nurtured, His servants)—our entire perspective upon trials and tribulations shifts.

Allah may grant us a life of beauty or a life of suffering. He may do whatsoever He wills. This is the transcendent God, the God who inspires awe, the *Dhū al-Jalāl* (Possessor of Majesty), the God of tsunamis and earthquakes, the God of holocausts and devastation, the God of fire, the God of cancer, the God who takes small children from their loving parents. These too are aspects of His transcendence. As His servants, we submit to these dimensions of His nature to the best of our capacity, knowing first that we possess no power to resist His will, and second that He would never wrong us. Therefore, if we find ourselves in an undesirable state, there lies within it divine wisdom that we must strive to perceive and understand.

Yet He is not merely *Dhū al-Jalāl*. He is *Dhū al-Jalāli wa-l-Ikrām*—Possessor of Majesty and Generosity. He is also the God of boundless blessing, the God of overflowing bounties, the God who bestows the gift of life, the gift of sustenance. He manifests Himself in the unconditional love of a mother, in the affection of a sister, in the comfort of a true friend. He is the God who eases the heart of the grieving mother who has lost her child. He is the God who cures cancer when all physicians have abandoned hope. He is the God who revives the barren earth. He is the God who conceals His blessings within His terrors, and hides His mercies within His tribulations.

The study of these realities enables us to navigate the vicissitudes of time, to encounter the trials and calamities of existence with steadfastness and wisdom. This is the fruit of this knowledge.

Moreover, the study of the nature of His prophets and His beloved ones yields extraordinary consolation. We learn, for instance, that the prophets endure the greatest trials of all. No parent who has lost a child can fail to find solace in learning that the Prophet, upon whom be peace, buried all of his sons. The very best of creation lost every male child, yet he never complained. His eyes wept—we know this from the authentic reports—but his tongue never uttered complaint. He said: "Allah does not hold you accountable for your tears, but rather for what you say in your state of grief."

Such knowledge transforms us. Religion is fundamentally an experience, not merely an intellectual exercise. There is no knowledge without practice, and there is no authentic practice without knowledge. We must first acquire this knowledge, then manifest it through action, through devotion to our Creator. This knowledge of our creed is not merely theoretical; it is intensely practical. As we come to know Him, we stand in prayer and invoke His beautiful Names—*Allāh, al-Raḥmān, al-Raḥīm*—and as we call upon Him, He draws near, and our souls are imprinted with the seal of His divine presence.

Thus the fruit and ultimate purpose of this knowledge is to understand the theory of divinity such that we do not fall into error and misconception regarding our Creator. This knowledge of *tawḥīd* serves us as a safeguard. They say that creed is like a chaperone—and though lovers do not welcome chaperones, for they seem only to obstruct, a chaperone serves an essential function: to prevent impropriety between lovers. Similarly, in our love affair with Allah, the Most High, this knowledge of *tawḥīd* becomes our chaperone, present to ensure that we neither say nor think anything incorrect or inappropriate regarding our Beloved.

### الفَضْلُ (The Virtue of *Tawḥīd*)

The virtue of this knowledge is that it is the highest of all knowledge. The scholars affirm:

⟪ar-quote:التوحید أجل العلم⟫

"*Tawḥīd* is the most esteemed of all knowledge."

Why? Because it concerns itself with the relationship between the creation and the Creator, with the understanding of the nature of the Divine. This is the greatest gift one can receive; therefore, it possesses the highest virtue.

### الْنِّسْبَۃُ (The Relation of *Tawḥīd*)

Its relation (*nisba*) extends to all the ⟪ar:علوم الشَّرَعِیَّۃ⟫ (*ʿulūm al-sharʿiyya*, the sciences of Islamic law and doctrine). All other branches of Islamic learning relate to and depend upon *tawḥīd*.

### الوَاضِعُ (The Originator of *Tawḥīd*)

The ⟪ar:وَاضِع⟫ (*wāḍiʿ*, the originator) of this knowledge is, first and foremost, Allah, the Most High, through the revelation of the Qur'ān. The Qur'ān is the revelation that discloses to us the nature of Allah. It instructs us:

⟪ar-quote:فعلم انہ لا الہ الا اللہ⟫

"Know that there is no god except Allah."

Allah tells us: *Qul huwa-l-Llāhu aḥad* ("Say: He is Allah, One"). He tells us that He is ⟪ar:بَدِیعُ السموات و الارض⟫ (the Originator of the heavens and earth). He tells us that He is ⟪ar:قاهِرُن فوقَ عِبادِهِ⟫ (*Qāhirun fawqa ibādihi*, the Overwhelming, Subduing Force above His servants). He proclaims that He is ⟪ar:الْحَيُّ الْقَيُّوم⟫ (*al-Ḥayy al-Qayyūm*, the Ever-Living, the Self-Subsisting).

Much of the Qur'ānic narrative concerns itself with the question: Who is Allah? Yet in essence, all of it revolves around Allah, the Most High. Even when the Qur'ān recounts the tribulations of the prophets, it does so as part of the call toward Allah. The originator of this science is therefore Allah Himself, as revealed through the Qur'ān.

Yet this knowledge also came to us through the Messenger of Allah, upon whom be peace, who transmitted to us this divine revelation. Allah granted us the Qur'ān through the medium of the Prophet. Subsequently, this divine message was absorbed, internalized, and then simplified by his *wasī* (executor, successor)—ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib—who transmitted it to the Imāms, who in turn transmitted it to the *duʿāt* (the Summoners), until it was refined to a form accessible to ordinary people like ourselves, manifesting in the forms of *sharīʿa*, *ta'wīl* (esoteric exegesis), and the science of *mabdāʾ wa-l-maʿād* (beginning and return).

### الاسْمُ (The Names of *Tawḥīd*)

This knowledge is called *Uṣūl al-Dīn* (the Roots of Religion). It is also called *ʿilm al-tawḥīd* (the science of monotheism), *dāʿim al-Islām* (the foundation of Islam), *īmān* (faith), and *iḥsān* (excellence). These are various names for this science, though it is most commonly termed *Uṣūl al-Dīn*, the Roots of Religion—for upon *tawḥīd* is this entire religion built. This is the science of *tawḥīd*.

### الْحُکْمُ الشَّرعِيُّ (The Legal Ruling on *Tawḥīd*)

The ruling on its acquisition is that it is *farḍ ʿayn* (*obligatory upon every individual*). Every Muslim is obligated to know their Creator. The Prophet said:

⟪ar-quote:طلب العلم فریضۃ علی کل مسلم و مسلمۃ⟫

"The acquisition of knowledge is *farḍ* (obligatory) upon every Muslim man and woman." [^cite-9]

Someone once asked ʿAbdullāh ibn Masʿūd whether the acquisition of knowledge held greater priority than performing supererogatory prayers. He quoted this very *ḥadīth* and responded that the Messenger of Allah designated the learning of knowledge as *farḍ*, and *farḍ* always takes precedence over *sunna* and *nāfila* (optional practices).

### المَسائِلُ (The Details of *Tawḥīd*)

The details of this science constitute the topics that, God willing, we shall examine throughout these sessions.

These are the *mabādiʾ al-ʿashara*—the ten foundations of *tawḥīd*.

## The Journey Ahead: Overwhelmed by Divine Knowledge

What I hope to accomplish in these sessions, God willing, is to share with you a deeper understanding of the fundamental principles of our religion and our creed. A creed (*ʿaqīda*) is defined as a set of beliefs that guide one's actions and conduct. It represents what one believes in most profoundly. The Arabic word *ʿaqīda* derives from the root *ʿaqada*, meaning to tie or bind. An *ʿaqīda* is therefore that which binds one, that to which one holds with conviction. Marriage itself is called *ʿaqd* (a contract) because two people are bound together in a shared journey through life.

In these sessions, my aim is to share with you the underlying core principles of our *ʿaqīda*—first as Muslims (*muslimūn*), and then as Believers (*mu'minūn*). [^cite-10] Our tradition employs a rich vocabulary in this domain: *Muslim*, *Mu'min*, *Islam*, *īmān*, *yaqīn* (certainty), *ikhlāṣ* (sincerity), *tawḥīd*. We also speak of the *daʿāʾim* (the foundations or pillars): the Seven Pillars of Islam, which are *wilāya* (guardianship), *ṭahāra* (purity), *salāt* (prayer), *zakāt* (almsgiving), *sawm* (fasting), *ḥajj* (pilgrimage), and *jihād* (striving). We employ all these terms within the context of creed, yet we often lack a clear and unified understanding of them, or of how they relate to one another beneath the encompassing principle of *tawḥīd*.

For *tawḥīd* is the most essential principle of all. It is the rope that binds together all existence in the heavens and upon the earth. Often, we become so preoccupied with the particulars of creed that we lose sight of the vision of Allah, the Most High. We become so absorbed in the letter of the law that we miss its spirit—which is to know Allah, the Most High. And that is *tawḥīd*: to perceive the One through all the multiplicity that surrounds us.

As for the level at which these sessions are pitched, I believe they may prove challenging for young children, given the complexity of the terminology and the conceptual density. There is a saying in our tradition:

⟪ar-quote:الکافر حائرٌ فی المخلوق..... والمؤمن حائرٌ فی الخالق⟫

"The disbeliever is bewildered by creation, whereas the believer is bewildered by the Creator." [^cite-11]

This knowledge is indeed bewildering. Yet we have been granted the perfect faculties to receive it. Allah has prepared us for this knowledge. He created us with a *fiṭra*—a primordial disposition and nature that is sound and inclined toward Him. This *fiṭra* knows Him directly. Allah has granted us His beautiful Names in the Qur'ān: *al-Raḥmān* (the Compassionate), *al-Raḥīm* (the Merciful), *al-Malik* (the Sovereign), *al-Quddūs* (the Holy), *al-Salām* (the Peace), *al-Mu'min* (the Bestower of Security), *al-Muhaymin* (the Overseer). He has given us these Names so that we might invoke Him through them, so that we might contemplate Him through them, that we might come to know Him.

If the goal of knowledge were not to know Him, there would be no purpose to His self-disclosure through His Names.

Yet you must understand: this knowledge of Allah is overwhelming. It will cause your mind to reel and spin. But hear this truth: when you begin to think about Allah, you *should* feel as though you are drowning. If you do not, then you are not truly grasping it. There is nothing deeper in existence than the subject of *tawḥīd*. There is nothing more profound, vast, or fathomless than the knowledge of Allah, the Most High. There is no better occupation for the mind than to be preoccupied with this knowledge. It is as simple as that.

Therefore, if you feel overwhelmed in these sessions, be glad! *Al-ḥamdulillāh* (praise be to Allah). It merely signifies that you are beginning to grasp it. I confess that I feel overwhelmed every time I study or contemplate this subject. So yes, this knowledge is overwhelming, but do not regard this as negative. Do not allow yourself to think that you cannot learn this, for that is untrue. We were all created for this knowledge.

I wished simply to set the stage before we commence, for often people withdraw from learning simply because they feel overwhelmed by what they encounter. I wish to reframe that feeling, to place it in proper perspective. Feeling overwhelmed is excellent! By the Grace of Allah, every person in this gathering possesses more than sufficient capacity to understand this knowledge. Aristotle observed that the intellect is distributed most justly among mankind. Allah, the Most High, has been most equitable in His distribution of intellect above all else.

## An Invitation to Dialogue

Let us engage in genuine *muzākira*—that is, discussion and mutual consideration. If you prefer not to be called upon, please let me know.

Our Prophet, upon whom be peace, distinguished between three states: *Islām*, *Īmān*, and *Iḥsān*. The word *Islām*, in its general sense, means "to submit." One who voluntarily submits himself to Allah, the Most High, is called a *Muslim*.

Then there is the term *Īmān*, which signifies faith or belief. It is the verbal noun derived from *āmana*, "to believe." *Īmān* represents faith in the metaphysical aspects of *Islām*. Yet *īmān* differs from faith in the colloquial sense; it is not a blind belief. Rather, it is faith grounded in intellectual reasoning and rational deliberation.

Then there is another term, less commonly employed but profoundly important: *Iḥsān*, which means perfection, beauty, excellence. Here lies a fundamental principle: *Islām* addresses the question of what one should do; *īmān* addresses the question of why one should do it; *iḥsān* concerns the manifestation of beauty and excellence in both deed and action.

*Janna* (the garden, paradise) symbolizes peace, tranquility, and *niʿma* (blessing). The *Islām* that is submission through the signs of creation (*ʿālam*) leads to the garden of *Islām*. The *īmān* that is belief in the unseen leads to the garden of *īmān*.

There is a *ḥadīth* that states:

"The one who teaches people good, and yet the reward of his students does not diminish his own reward—indeed, all the good that a Muslim does returns to the Prophet, upon whom be peace, because he is the one who taught us to do these things. Consider therefore the reward he receives from Allah, the Most High, for his efforts."

And another states:

"If you seek knowledge for the sake of Allah, paradise seeks you; and if you seek disobedience, the fire seeks you."

The Qur'ān instructs us to ask the people of knowledge if we do not know. There are various types of knowledge. One fundamental aspect is that not all knowledge is dialogical in nature; some is strictly didactic. If one is learning grammar, one must understand the noun, the verb, and how they function together. This is knowledge that must be taught. There is little room for extended discussion; you either comprehend it or you do not.

However, the approach I wish to take in these sessions is what is called *muzākira*—a method wherein one actually discusses and explores what one is learning together. *Muzākira* is the crucible in which genuine questions emerge. In my judgment, a dialogical approach to knowledge yields far richer results than a purely didactic method. When you discuss something, you extract far more from it. Therefore, I do not wish these sessions to feel like a mere "data dump," wherein you arrive as empty vessels to be filled with information.

Rather, these are gatherings in which you must actively engage in order to make the knowledge real for yourself—so that it transcends mere information and becomes lived understanding. It is essential to think deeply about these matters, not to accept them superficially or at face value.

Religion is not authentic if it is not grounded in genuine belief. Religion is, fundamentally, an experience based on knowledge. And the means by which we excavate and uncover that knowledge is through questions. Our religion has never shied away from questions; that is one of its beauties. No true scholar will forbid you from asking a question.

If you are genuinely seeking to understand something, if you approach your inquiry from a place of sincere seeking rather than arrogance, then usually there exist answers to your questions. Yet one must acknowledge that when one enters into the essence of Allah, real limitations emerge—limitations imposed by the very nature of language. All books are composed of words, and all these words attempt to speak of Allah, who is ineffable. Allah is an entity whom we truly cannot articulate fully, for language falls short when attempting to speak of the Divine.
