## The Five Foundations of Islam

From His infinite knowledge, Allah has provided humanity with a complete way of life in this world, which is called Islam. The divine revelation affirms: ⟪ar-quote:ان الدین عند اللہ الاسلام⟫

There is but one path that leads to genuine success. Of all other ways, Allah declares: ⟪ar-quote:وَمَن يَبْتَغِ غَيْرَ ٱلْإِسْلَمِ دِينًۭا فَلَن يُقْبَلَ مِنْهُ وَهُوَ فِى ٱلْآخِرَةِ مِنَ ٱلْخَسِرِينَ⟫ Whoever seeks a way other than Islam will find it rejected, and in the afterlife will be among the losers.

Islam comprises two inseparable dimensions: ⟪ar:عبادات⟫ (worship) and ⟪ar:معاملات⟫ (conduct and social dealings). The first connects us with our Creator; the second binds us to His creation. To falter in worship is to sever ourselves from Allah; to fail in conduct is to break covenant with His people. Both are essential pillars of the faith.[^cite-1]

All human beings exist between two states of predestination: birth into this world and departure from it—neither by our choosing. Yet between these two boundaries, we have been granted free will. Allah instructs us: ⟪ar-quote:وَهَدَيْنَهُ ٱلنَّجْدَيْنِ⟫ We have shown him both paths. And again: ⟪ar-quote:إِنَّا هَدَيْنَاهُ ٱلسَّبِيلَ إِمَّا شَاكِرًۭا وَإِمَّا كَفُورًا⟫ Indeed, We have guided him to the way—whether he be grateful or ungrateful.

He says further: ⟪ar-quote:وَقُلِ ٱلْحَقُّ مِن رَّبِّكُمْ ۖ فَمَن شَآءَ فَلْيُؤْمِن وَمَن شَآءَ فَلْيَكْفُرْ⟫ Say: the Truth has come from your Lord. Whoever wills may believe, and whoever wills may deny. This world is not the place of reckoning but of trial. In it, the obedient and disobedient alike may prosper materially, for Allah does not assume the posture of judge—rather, that of witness.[^cite-2]

But a Day approaches of which the Qur'an warns: ⟪ar-quote:وَلَا تَحْسَبَنَّ ٱللَّهَ غَفِلًا عَمَّا يَعْمَلُ ٱلظَّلِمُونَ⟫ Never suppose that Allah is heedless of what the wrongdoers do. ⟪ar-quote:إِنَّمَا يُؤَخِّرُهُمْ لِيَوْمٍۢ تَشْخَصُ فِيهِ ٱلْأَبْصَرُ⟫ He delays them only until a Day when eyes will stare in terror—the Day Allah called ⟪ar:يَوْمُ ٱلتَّغَابُنِ⟫, the Day of Deprivation. ⟪ar-quote:يَوْمَ يَجْمَعُكُمْ لِيَوْمِ ٱلْجَمْعِ.....  ذَلِكَ يَوْمُ ٱلتَّغَابُنِ⟫ That Day will come suddenly: ⟪ar-quote:لَا تَأْتِيكُمْ إِلَّا بَغْتَةًۭ⟫ It will not come upon you except unexpectedly.

In this interim state, only the One who created us can reveal what is truly beneficial. The Messenger of Allah declared: ⟪ar-quote:بُنِیَ الاسلامُ عَلَی خَمسٍ..... شَھَادَۃِ أَن لا الہ الا اللہ، و أَنَّ محمداً رَسُولُ اللہ..... وَأِقَامِ الصَّلاۃ، وَ أِیتَاءِ الزَّکاۃ، وَ صَومِ رَمَضان، وَ حَجِّ البَیت مَن اِستِطَاع اِلَیہِ سَبِیلا⟫

The foundation of Islam rests upon five pillars:

1. **Testimony** that there is no deity except Allah, and that Muhammad (upon him be peace and the peace be upon his family) is His Messenger.
2. **Establishment of prayer** (iqāmat al-ṣalāh).
3. **Giving of the purifying alms** (zakat).
4. **Fasting during the month of Ramadan**.
5. **Pilgrimage to the House** (hajj) for those who have the means.

According to the Sulemani tradition, Islam encompasses seven pillars: (1) *wilāyah* (spiritual guardianship), (2) *ṭahārah* (purification), (3) *salāt* (prayer), (4) *zakāt* (alms), (5) *sawm* (fasting), (6) *hajj* (pilgrimage), and (7) *jihād* (exertion in the path of Allah). Though the hadith of the Messenger does not explicitly name these three—wilāyah, ṭahārah, and jihād—they are implicit within the five mentioned, not contradictory to them.[^cite-3] Wilāyah is contained in the testimony of faith itself; purification and exertion are embedded within the remaining practices. Before elaborating on each of the five explicit foundations, I wish to share an interpretation that illuminates their deepest meaning.

---

### Islam as the Journey and Development of Love

Islam begins with the *shahādah* (testimony of faith), which comprises two distinct dimensions—for human existence unfolds in relationship with two realities: the Creator and the creation.

⟪ar:لا الہ الا اللہ⟫—There is no god but Allah—concerns ⟪ar:عبادات⟫ (worship and devotion), our relation with the Creator. ⟪ar:محمدا رسول اللہ⟫—Muhammad is the Messenger of Allah—concerns ⟪ar:معاملات⟫ (conduct and social dealings), our relation with creation. In the study of the divine law (*sharīʿah*), one observes that the demands of worship are finite and specific, while the demands of conduct are infinite and ever-expanding.

Why does Islam begin with the shahādah? Because it concerns *wilāyah* (walāyah; spiritual affinity and guardianship)—a concept requiring clarification. The word derives from the root wa-li-ya (⟪ar:وَلِیَ⟫), signifying that when two things are placed in such proximity that no separation exists between them—when "two bodies share one soul" (دو جسم ایک جان)—the root "wa-li-ya" is employed. In its general sense, it denotes spiritual affinity, intimacy, deep friendship. A derivative, *mawlā* (master, guardian), denotes the one who possesses authority and control.[^cite-4]

Wilāyah connects directly to the shahādah, which opens with negation: *Lā ilāha illā-llāh*—There is no god save Allah. This negation affirms the One by setting aside all else. This is precisely the nature of love: love is the negation of all other possibilities in order to isolate and affirm *the* beloved. From countless options, you choose *one* person to love. When that choice is made—when all others are negated to affirm that singular beloved—no one stands between lover and beloved. This is *wilāyah*: the initial stage of spiritual closeness.

⟪ar:محمدا رسول اللہ⟫—Muhammad is the Messenger of Allah—is the perfected stage of the shahādah, for the Messenger is the pinnacle and the perfect embodiment of the testimony itself. ⟪ar:لا الہ الا اللہ⟫ is the seed; ⟪ar:محمدا رسول اللہ⟫ is the tree that grows from it. If you truly believe there is no god but Allah, then shape yourself according to the example of the Messenger of Allah. For this reason, he is *mawlā*—the guardian and controller. By testifying to Muhammad's messengerhood, we transfer dominion over our souls and actions to him; we make him our *mawlā*.

According to Ismaili tradition, this is the verse revealed on the day of Ghadīr, after the Messenger anointed Mawlānā ʿAlī (upon him be peace) as his successor:

ٱلْيَوْمَ أَكْمَلْتُ لَكُمْ دِينَكُمْ

Today I have *perfected* your faith for you. The word *al-yawm* (today) indicates this perfection occurs on this very day, unprecedented for any nation. *Lakum* (for you) shows it is granted to this community alone.

وَأَتْمَمْتُ عَلَيْكُمْ نِعْمَتِى

And I have *completed* My blessings upon you.

وَرَضِيتُ لَكُمُ ٱلْإِسْلَٰمَ دِينًا

And I am *content* with Islam as your religion.

Allah thus speaks of three realities: the perfection of the faith, the completion of blessings, and divine contentment with Islam. Not only has the religion been perfected on this day—Allah's grace is now complete, and the Almighty is satisfied with the submission of this community.[^cite-5]

---

The spiritual path brought by the Messenger comprises two elements: the Qur'an (revelation) and the *Sunnah* (the prophetic tradition and practice). Yet *Sunnah* does not mean mere imitation of outward actions—a reduction to which later Muslims have unfortunately confined it. Rather, *Sunnah* denotes the very substance of prophetic perfection to which the Qur'an itself testifies: ⟪ar-quote:وَإِنَّكَ لَعَلَىٰ خُلُقٍ عَظِيمٍۢ⟫ Indeed, you are of tremendous moral character.

The goal of *Sunnah* for every believer is not formal mimicry of words and deeds but the inner assimilation of its essence. There exists a natural affinity between the soul of the believer and the prophetic nature of the Messenger—the *fitra* (primordial nature) of the believer naturally yearns for all that the Messenger's life manifested physically. The Qur'an says: ⟪ar-quote:ٱلنَّبِىُّ أَوْلَىٰ بِٱلْمُؤْمِنِينَ مِنْ أَنفُسِهِمْ⟫ The Prophet is nearer to the believers than their own selves—again employing *walāyah* (spiritual affinity). The Prophet outwardly embodies the perfection that a believer's very nature craves.

The one person who fully and truly assimilated that prophetic substance was *Amīr al-Mu'minīn*, ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib. This is affirmed by the Messenger's declaration: ⟪ar-quote:العَلِیُّ کَنَفسِی⟫ ʿAlī is as my own soul. He also said: ⟪ar-quote:أَنتَ مِنِّی وَ أنَا مِنکَ⟫ You are from me and I am from you—expressing the profound unity of being. Thus the Prophet's closeness to ʿAlī is illuminated through the lens of *Sunnah*.

The Messenger proclaimed: ⟪ar-quote:انا مدینۃ العلم و علی بابھا، فمن اراد المدینۃ فلیات الباب⟫ I am the city of knowledge, and ʿAlī is its gate. Whoever desires to enter the city must enter through the gate. The gate guards the city, preserves it. ʿAlī is thus the gateway to prophetic wisdom.[^cite-6]

The other foundation of the Messenger's life was the Qur'an itself. He declared: ⟪ar-quote:علي مع القرآن ، والقرآن مع علي ، لن يفترقا حتى يردا على الحوض⟫ ʿAlī is with the Qur'an, and the Qur'an is with ʿAlī; they shall not separate until they meet me at the Pool. Thus the essence of both the Qur'an and the *Sunnah* were fully internalized by Mawlānā ʿAlī.

In the Sulemani tradition, we hold that the Messenger transferred this guardianship (*wilāyah*) and authority to Mawlānā ʿAlī on the Day of Ghadīr. His words were these:

⟪ar-quote:مَنْ کُنتُ مَولاہُ فَھٰذا عَلِیٌّ مَولاہُ..... وَ⟪ar-quote:مَنْ کُنتُ⟫ وَلِیُّہُ فَعَلِیٌّ وَلِیُّہُ..... ⟪ar-quote:وَ⟪ar-quote:مَنْ کُنتُ⟫⟫ نَبِیُّہُ ⟪ar-quote:فَعَلِیٌّ⟫اَمِیرُہُ⟫

Whomsoever considered me his *mawlā*, from this moment onwards, ʿAlī is his *mawlā*. Whomsoever regarded me as his guardian, his intimate, the one in charge of his soul and in control of his actions—from this day forth, ʿAlī holds that station. Whomsoever counted me his Prophet shall count ʿAlī his leader. This was the explicit transfer of *mawlāyat*—of guardianship—on the Day of Ghadīr. This is why *wilāyah* is associated with Mawlānā ʿAlī in the Ismaili understanding. And this is why, in the call to prayer, we testify: ⟪ar-quote:اشھد ان محمدا رسول اللہ..... اشھد ان مولانا علی ولی اللہ⟫

Why do we not say, ⟪ar:اشھد ان مولانا محمدا رسول اللہ⟫? Because that authority has been transferred to ʿAlī. Not that the Messenger is no longer *mawlā*—assuredly, he remains so. Rather, he has united his closeness, his affinity, his friendship with that of Mawlānā ʿAlī. The only path to Allah's nearness lies through the *wilāyah* of the Messenger. The only path to the Messenger's nearness lies through the *wilāyah* of Mawlānā ʿAlī. This is why, after proclaiming ⟪ar:من کنت مولاہ فھذا علی مولاہ⟫, the Messenger invoked:

⟪ar-quote:اللّٰھُمَّ فَوَالِ مَن وَالاہُ، وَعَادِ مَن عَادَاہُ، وَانْصُر مَن نَصَرَہُ، وَاخْذُل مَن خَذَلَہُ، وَأَدِرِ الحَقَّ مَعَہُ حَیثُ دَارَ⟫

O Allah! Be near to those who are near to him; be an enemy to those who are his enemies; aid those who aid him; abandon those who abandon him. And turn truth in the direction that he turns![^cite-7]

The *duʿāt* (preachers of the faith) have explained that *al-ḥaqq* (truth/rightness) here refers to the Imāms who will come from the progeny of Mawlānā ʿAlī and Mawlatunā Fāṭimah, for believers follow *al-ḥaqq* (the Imāms), and *al-ḥaqq* follows Mawlānā ʿAlī.

Thus the seed of Islam is *wilāyah*: the negation of all creation so as to affirm the one true Beloved—Allah, the Most High. *Wilāyah* establishes the direction of the heart; it sets the *qiblah* for spiritual longing. It establishes a goal: the love of Allah.

Allah declares: ⟪ar-quote:يا ابن آدم .. أنا لَكَ مُحِبُّ..... فَبِحَقِّي عَليكَ كُنْ لِي مُحِبَّا⟫ O children of Adam! I love you. By My right as your Creator, love Me in return.

With the shahādah, we set ourselves toward transformation—to mold ourselves into the pattern of the one whom the Prophet loved. We testify that the Messenger is the true embodiment of *Lā ilāha illā-llāh*—that Mawlānā ʿAlī and the Imāms are the living, practical manifestation of the *kalimah* (sacred formula) after him. The poet Abū Nuwās expressed this longing:

⟪ar-quote:أشْبَهت أعدائي فَصِرتُ أُحِبُّهمْ… إذ صَارَ حُظّي مِنكَ حُظّي مِنهُمُ…

وأَهَنْتني فأَهَنْتُ نفسي صاغرًا … ما مَن يهونُ عليك مِمّن أُكرِمُ…⟫

You appeared to me in the form of my enemies, and so I fell in love with my foes—for whatever image you cast upon something becomes beautiful to my eye. You dishonored me, so I dishonored myself humbly—for none whom you belittle can be honored.

This is the true essence of the shahādah: that it penetrates the inward self to such a degree that the outward life transforms into the living *sīrah* (example) of the Messenger and Mawlānā ʿAlī.

### Salāt (Prayer)

Once this direction is set—once the Beloved is identified as the *qiblah* (direction) of the heart—the next necessity is to *connect* with the Beloved. This is *salāt* (prayer). The word derives from *sīlah*, meaning to join with, to link together. It also means to burn up, for when joined to Allah, one burns in His love; sins dissolve in His presence. 

Having fallen in love, the natural next step is constant connection with the Beloved. This is what *iqāmat al-ṣalāh*—establishing prayer—means: establishing and maintaining the bond. For this connection nourishes love and enables it to grow. The more one connects with the Beloved, the more the intensity of love increases. In ages past, lovers maintained connection through letters; later, through correspondence; now through messages—yet all are forms of communion with the cherished one. For the true lovers, *salāt* is an incomparable gift; they cannot bear to wait until the next prayer time, and once connected with Allah, they extend it as long as they can.[^cite-8]

---

### Zakāt (Purifying Alms)

The next dimension is to spend in the pleasure of the Beloved. When one loves someone and is constantly connected to that person, there naturally follows love for those whom the Beloved loves. If I love you and maintain constant connection with you, I will naturally develop affection for those you cherish. If I learn your son is visiting my town, I will ensure he is cared for with all my ability—not for his sake primarily, but because he is precious to you. Thus he becomes precious to me. This is the spirit of *zakāt*.

Throughout the Qur'an, these two are mentioned together: ⟪ar-quote:وَأَقِيمُوا۟ ٱلصَّلَوٰةَ وَآتُوا۟ ٱلزَّكَوٰةَ⟫ Establish prayer and give the purifying alms. Why? Because if you love Me, you will care for those whom I love. Indeed, immediately after the command to prayer, the hadith of the five pillars continues: ⟪ar-quote:وَأِقَامِ الصَّلاۃ، وَ أِیتَاءِ الزَّکاۃ⟫ Establish prayer and give the purifying alms.

---

### Sawm (Fasting)

Until this point, scholars say the believer stands at the stage of *ḥubb* (love)—a state of affection with the Beloved. But with the constant reinforcement of prayer and alms-giving, the believer now ascends to the stage of *ʿishq* (passionate, consuming love). This is a degree of love approaching obsession, wherein the lover is so engrossed with the Beloved that interest in food and drink falls away. This is the meaning of *sawm* (fasting): the lover begins to physically sacrifice his own desires for the sake of the Beloved's desires.

One meaning of *ʿishq* is "to search," "to strive for," "to seek." Another meaning is "a fool," for the *ʿāshiq* (passionate lover) appears foolish—having surrendered intellect to the heart. *ʿishq* is also likened to a fatal disease, a vine that strangles the life from the plant it entwines, for the plant yields all its nourishment to the vine and withers in the attempt.

This is *sawm*—fasting in passionate love of Allah. The Messenger declared: ⟪ar-quote:قال اللہ تعالی، کُلَّ عَمَلَ ابْنَ آدَمَ لَہُ اِلَّا الصِّیَام..... فَأِنَّہُ لی وَ أنَا أُجزِیَ بِہِ⟫ Allah, Most High, has said: All deeds of the children of Adam belong to them—except fasting. Fasting is mine alone, and I shall reward it.

If all acts of worship are for Allah, why single out fasting? Is not Allah the One who rewards all acts? The scholars explain it is because of fasting's honored station and because it demonstrates sincerity to Allah alone. Fasting is a secret between the believer and his Master that none can witness save Allah. Moreover, it is the most difficult form of worship, for the believer must abandon his desire for sustenance out of love for Allah.[^cite-9]

Additionally, other deeds are quantified—a good deed receives tenfold reward—but by saying "Fasting is mine, and I shall reward it," Allah indicates: I will reward it according to My discretion; I will multiply its reward as I wish. For fasting is an act of *sabr* (patience), and the Qur'an assures: ⟪ar-quote:إِنَّمَا يُوَفَّى ٱلصَّبِرُونَ أَجْرَهُم بِغَيْرِ حِسَابٍۢ⟫ Indeed, the patient will be given their reward without measure.

*Sawm* is thus a state of abandonment. Linguistically, the word means "to abandon," "to sacrifice for the Beloved." It elevates love (*ḥubb*) to passionate love (*ʿishq*). One will always find the *ʿāshiq* in destitution—so consumed by love of the Beloved that the world's pleasures hold no allure. He abandons all else. The world can no longer capture his interest. This is the state of *ʿishq*.

---

### Hajj (Pilgrimage)

Yet there remains one more stage. As the saying goes: ⟪ar:دواء العشق الاتصال⟫—The remedy for passionate love is meeting the Beloved. Having reached the stage where longing becomes unbearable, the lover will journey to the ends of the earth to meet the Beloved.

Consider Salmān al-Fārisī! Behold his love! He traveled the world seeking the Messenger of Allah. Hearing of any righteous person, he would journey to verify whether he was the Prophet. His journey to the Messenger was filled with hardships and tribulations, yet driven by love. And consider his love for the Prophet's family—his devotion to ʿAlī, al-Ḥasan, and al-Ḥusayn was such that he would wipe the floors of Fāṭimah's house with his own garment. He became so close to the Messenger that the Prophet said: ⟪ar:سلمان منا اھل البیت⟫ Salmān is from among my family. Such is the closeness that love brings![^cite-10]

Consider ʿUways al-Qaranī's love for the Messenger. Upon learning that one of the Prophet's teeth was broken in battle, ʿUways extracted his own teeth one by one, uncertain which matched his Beloved's pain, until he had removed them all. This is true love: my Beloved suffers, and I wish to suffer equally. This is *ʿishq*!

Look at the love of Malik al-Ashtar. Behold Maymūnah, one of the wives of the Messenger. Thirty years she was married to the Prophet. As death approached, where did she wish to go? To the place where she had spent her first night with the Messenger—that is where she is buried. This is *ʿishq*, and this is what these blessed ones possessed. In our time, we have removed love from Islam entirely, rendering it something base. Yet this faith was always founded on love.

Now the Beloved invites the lover to meet Him. *Hajj* is an invitation from the Beloved to the lover to come and be received. We hold it as a sacred distinction that when we undertake *ʿumrah* or *hajj*, it is because Allah, Most High, has invited us to His House. It would be improper to appear uninvited at someone's door. Thus *hajj* is ⟪ar:الاتصال⟫—the union, the meeting of lover and Beloved. All the practices of *ʿumrah* and *hajj* are arranged to manifest the appearance of one crazed with love. We do not dress in fine clothes; we do not perfume ourselves; we do not comb our hair. We wrap ourselves in two simple garments and journey toward the Beloved. This final progression of *ʿishq* shows the lover so frantic to meet that he rushes out the moment the invitation arrives, abandoning all else.

The word *ʿumrah* comes from the root a-m-r (⟪ar:عَمَرَ⟫), meaning "to inhabit," "to dwell in," "to cultivate," "to repair." *Hajj* comes from the root h-j-j (⟪ar:حَجَّ⟫), meaning "to aim at" or "to intend," for *hajj* is performed once yearly and is obligatory but once in a lifetime.

*ʿUmrah* is the beginning part of *hajj*, and one meaning of *ʿumrah* is to visit an inhabited house. Historically, Mecca lies in a barren, harsh landscape—scorched earth and sand with nothing to offer travelers but extreme hardship. One would expect that the House of the King of Kings would be the most beautiful place on earth, surrounded by verdant mountains, flowing waters, perfect climate. Yet instead it is desolate. Why?

To test the sincerity of the lover's claim.

*On these stones, if you can walk, then come. / On the path to my house, there is no Milky Way.*

When the lover is truly obsessed, he cares nothing for the hardships he endures to meet his Beloved. All he desires is that first glance. Thus *hajj* is the lover's arrival, calling out in passionate madness to the Beloved. When Allah called people to prayer, He said to call silently and humbly:

⟪ar-quote:ٱدْعُوا۟ رَبَّكُمْ تَضَرُّعًۭا وَخُفْيَةً.....  إِنَّهُۥ لَا يُحِبُّ ٱلْمُعْتَدِينَ⟫

Call upon your Lord in humility and in private; indeed, He does not love transgressors.

But when He called them to His House, He said:

⟪ar-quote:فَلعَجَّ وَ ثَج⟫

Come to me shouting and crying! For this is what identifies the passionate lover. The lover rushes toward the Beloved, crying out:

⟪ar-quote:لب بیک اللھم لبیک⟫

Here I am, O Allah, here I am!

⟪ar-quote:لبیک لا شریک لک لبیک⟫

Here I am; there is no partner with You; here I am!

⟪ar-quote:ان الحمد و النعمت لک.....  والملک لا شریک لہ⟫

Truly, all praise and all blessing belong to You, and sovereignty—You have no partner in it.[^cite-11]

When Junayd al-Baghdādī was circumambulating the Kaʿbah, he observed a young girl also performing *tawāf* (circumambulation), reciting verses:

⟪ar-quote:أَبىَ الحُبُّ أن يَّخْفىٰ وَكَمْ قَد كَتَمْتَهُ ..... فأَصْبَحْ عِندِى قَد أَنَاخَ وَطَنَّبَا⟫

Love refused to remain hidden; I tried so hard to conceal it, and now it has come and pitched its tent within me.

⟪ar-quote:إِذَا اشْتَدَّ شَوقِى هَامَ قَلبِى بِذِكرِهِ ..... فَإِنْ رُمتُ قُرباً مِن حَبِيبِى تَقَرَّبَا⟫

When my longing intensifies, my heart wanders in remembrance of Him; and whenever I desire to draw near my Beloved, He too draws near.

⟪ar-quote:يَبدُو فَأَفنٰى ثُمَّ أَحْيٰى لَهُ بِهِ ..... وَيُسْعِدُنِى حَتّٰى ألَذَّ وأطْرَبَا⟫

He appears and I am annihilated; then He revives me through Himself, and His love brings me such joy and delight!

Junayd, hearing these verses, erupted in anger. What are you saying? How dare you speak thus in this place! Do you not know where you stand? She replied: Do not misunderstand my words. These verses are for the very One for whom you circumambulate.

⟪ar-quote:لَولا التُّقىٰ لَم تَرِنى أَهجِر طِيبِ الوَسَنِ⟫

Were it not for the love of Allah, you would not find me abandoning the comfort of sleep.

⟪ar-quote:إِنَّ التُّقىٰ شَرَّدَنى كمَا تَرى عَن وَطَنى⟫

It is love of Allah that has scattered me, as you see, far from my home.

Then she asked Junayd: What are you circumambulating? He answered: The Kaʿbah. Upon hearing this, she laughed and said:

⟪ar-quote:يَطُوفُونَ بالأحجار يَبغُونَ قُربَةُ..... إِلَيكَ وَهمٌ أَقسٰى قُلُوباً مِنَ الصَّخَرِ⟫

They walk around stones, seeking closeness to You, yet their hearts are harder than stone!

⟪ar-quote:فَلَو أخْلَصُوا فِى الوُدَّ غَابَت صِفَاتِهِم..... وَقَامَتُ صِفَاتُ الوُدَّ لِلحَقِّ بِالذِّكَرِ⟫

Were they sincere in their love for You, they would lose themselves entirely—their attributes would disappear, and only the attributes of love for You would remain, recalled in each breath.

Upon hearing these words, Junayd lost consciousness. When he awoke, the girl had vanished.[^cite-12]

Thus unfolds the progression of love through the obligatory forms of worship. The *shahādah* introduces it; it is *taʿarruf* (recognition)—identifying the Beloved by negating all others and understanding the perfected fruit of that love, which is the Messenger of Allah. Then *salāt* and *zakāt* are *taʿalluwq* (attachment and bonding)—connecting the lover with the Beloved, which cultivates love. *Sawm* then ignites the fire of passion, elevating love (*ḥubb*) to passionate, consuming love (*ʿishq*). In this state, the lover willingly surrenders all else for the sake of the Beloved; the beauties and pleasures of this world no longer hold any interest. *ʿishq* then compels the lover to seek meeting with the Beloved, which brings him to the Beloved's door. At that moment, the Beloved extends an invitation to meet—and this is *hajj*. *Hajj* is ⟪ar:وِصَال، لِقَاءُ المَحبُوب⟫ (union, the meeting of lover and Beloved).[^cite-13]

*On this pretext, turn my days into nights for me; / Speak to this one sometimes, to that one at other times.*

The essence and spirit of *hajj* is to set out in search of Allah. It is the final *hijrah* (exodus, migration). *Hijrah* means to depart from one place to another permanently, leaving all behind. There was a *ẓāhirī hijrah* (outward migration) that the Messenger performed when he journeyed from Mecca to Medina. That physical *hijrah* stands as a sign and reminder that every believer must also make a spiritual *hijrah*—moving from sin to obedience, from disobedience to submission, from evil to good, from spiritual turbulence to peace (*salāmah*), to *islām* (submission).[^cite-14]

Someone asked the Prophet: O Messenger of Allah, what is Islam? He replied: ⟪ar-quote:أَن تُسَلَّمَ قَلبَکَ لِی اللہ⟫ That you surrender your heart to Allah!

This is the essence of *hajj*: a determination to transform the heart before returning home. It is a fundamental shift in consciousness—from living according to one's own will and desires to living according to Allah's will and commands, which is the *sīrah* (exemplary conduct) of the Messenger. To do what He commands and refrain from what He forbids. This is the meaning of ⟪ar:لب بیک اللھم لبیک⟫—Here I am, O Allah, here I am.

*Hajj* is the most encompassing and perfect form of worship, containing all the others within itself. In *hajj*, one moves toward the *qiblah*, which is the essence of the shahādah; one prays; one spends wealth; one fasts; and each action requires striving and exertion—which is *jihād* (the struggle in Allah's path). The word *jihād* comes from j-h-d, meaning "to strive," "to struggle," "to exert oneself." Every act of worship contains within it exertion and struggle. Thus the five pillars unfold as a unified journey of love—from recognition through union.
