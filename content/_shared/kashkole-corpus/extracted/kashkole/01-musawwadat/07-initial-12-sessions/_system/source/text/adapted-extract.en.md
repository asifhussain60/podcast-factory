## Allah's Questions to Us

In the Qur'an, Allah puts several searching questions to us:

⟪ar-quote:أَم خُلق من غیر شئی⟫ (Are you brought into being from nothing, without a Creator?)*

Have you come about by accident? An ancient theory held that everything arose of itself: that the sun shone of its own accord, the moon cooled by chance, the winds softened on their own initiative, the earth spread out unbidden, the mountains were set in place, the rivers and oceans began to flow, the flowers bloomed, the plants grew — that all this somehow happens automatically.

In Iran there once lived a great scholar, teacher, philosopher, and master of logic by the name of ⟪ar:جلال‌الدین محمد بلخى⟫ — Rūmī. He passed away in 1273. He composed a ghazal called "When I Die," to which we shall listen in the next session. Inscribed upon his tomb is his own verse: "When I die, do not search for my grave in the earth, but in the hearts of people."

His seminary stood beside a fountain. Upon a table his books were arranged with great care. Shams of Tabrīz was a Sufi of the same age, then passing through Rūmī's land. As he came by, he glanced at the books and asked, "What is this?" Rūmī, with a touch of arrogance, replied: "It is something you would not understand." Whereupon Shams cast the books into the pool. "My fleet is sinking!" Rūmī cried; "you have ruined forty years of my labour." Shams descended into the water and drew the volumes out — every one of them dry as bone. Astonished, Rūmī asked, "What is this?" "This," replied Shams, "is what you would not understand."

⟪ar-quote:أَم ھُمُ الخالقون⟫ (Are you yourselves the creators?)*

Did you paint the colour of your own eyes? Did you shape your own features, choose your own height, your complexion, your nation, family, region, country? If so, you remain truly free.

⟪ar-quote:أَم خُلِق السموات والارض⟫ ("Or did you create the heavens and the earth?")*

If all this is your handiwork, then do as you please — build a mosque, build a temple, brew and sell wine, erect an idol house. Do whatever you wish.

If, somehow, you can prove that you have created all of this, then no demand is made of you. Do as you please.

But if none of these three things is true, then before you pursue any worldly knowledge — to become a teacher, an engineer, a doctor — the first knowledge you must seek is this: who is your Creator? Who created the heavens and the earth, and to what end? Why has He created us, and what does He ask of us? Are we born only to eat and drink, gather wealth, build houses, marry, raise children, and die? If so, animals do the same. Has He, then, drawn no distinction between us and the animals? We have made life a little more comfortable: they sleep in the forest, we in beds; they eat raw, we cook our food; they amuse themselves, and so do we, though our amusements differ. They raise their young, and so do we. Yet — they are doing this work too.

God has granted us one gift He has withheld from the animals: intellect. To what end, then, was this intellect given? If we use it only to do what the animals do, what is the use of it?[^cite-1]

### Why has God created us, and what does He want from us?

What is the reason that a person's desires are never satisfied? Men and women spend the whole day between Facebook, the phone, email, work, and entertainment. Why does one feel lonely in spite of all these distractions? Why is there an unceasing disquiet in the heart? If wealth were the spring of happiness, why do millionaires take their own lives? Why do people drown themselves in drink and drugs? The poor chase after wealth — that we may understand — but why does the rich man also keep chasing? And what is this servitude?

An urban dog used to go walking in the forest. There he met a wild dog. The urban dog was fat and well-fed; the wild dog's ribs showed through his coat. The wild dog asked: "Friend, what do you eat?" "I?" said the city dog. "I eat meat, I eat eggs, I drink milk." The wild dog replied, "I am dying of hunger; I cannot even get dry bones. Take me with you, let me eat my fill. I shall grow fat like you and put on a little colour." "Very well," said the city dog. "We shall feed you too." The two dogs walked from the forest into the city. As they emerged into the light, the wild dog saw a leash around the urban dog's neck. He was startled. "What is that?" he asked. "It is the strap of servitude, the badge of bondage," said the city dog. "What is this servitude?" asked the wild dog — for he was a free spirit of the forest.

"I do my work in my master's house and watch over their home," said the city dog. "In return they feed me meat and milk. But they keep me tied. From time to time they let me loose for a little while, so that my legs may be stretched; otherwise they bind me always." Today we count meat and egg-parathas as freedom; the great cars of this world as freedom; high posts and titles as freedom; wealth, honour, fame, and applause as freedom. And yet around our necks the leash is fastened.

I once listened to an interview with Junaid Jamshed. At the peak of his fame he said: "The position and life that every young Pakistani dreams of — all of it is mine. And yet my heart is empty. I feel like a boat with no shore." Many of you know the rest of his story. He spent his childhood and youth carelessly. Arriving in the United States, he made his money, took his fill of pleasures, satisfied lawful and unlawful desires alike. Yet his heart found no rest: in every relationship, disappointment; in every friendship, betrayal; every joy a moment's joy, and then again the search for a new one.

Why is it, when every comfort lies within reach, that the heart still cannot rest? Why does it search ceaselessly for "and"? Why is it never satisfied? Why does the human being always crave "more"? The demand for "more" arises only when we have not found what we seek. If we lose our phone, we keep searching; nothing else, however delightful, gives us rest until we have it back. And when we find the phone, the search ends. The search ends, in short, when the object is attained. What, then, is this heart searching for? Whose finding will satisfy it?

The reason is that the comforts of this world give pleasure to the body, but the heart is a stranger to bodily pleasures, and so it finds no peace in worldly things. It cries out for "and" because it has not yet found what it is looking for. The heart is not material; it is ⟪ar:'روحانیات'⟫ — spiritual. Its quest is for God, and its destination is ⟪ar:'اللہ'⟫ — Allāh. Until it finds Him, it will find no rest. As a fish gasps without water, so do those hearts that have not found God.[^cite-2] ⟪ar:خواجہ غلام فرید الدین⟫ has these verses:

The question that God puts to us, then, is this: why have you come? where are you going? who has sent you?

What is not yet known is the unseen. What is unveiled and understood becomes knowledge.

The first knowledge is this: I know who He is.

Then: why has He created me, and what does He want from me?

Why did He create this world? And why did He place me within it?

What, then, is this death? Why do we die? And if we were to be taken back, why did He create us at all? Who would willingly leave so great an establishment to die? Who would step down from the throne of a kingdom to enter the grave?

Who, then, is the one who takes us? Where shall we go after death? And what shall befall us when we arrive?

Who are these Prophets? Why are they sent? Who has sent them? And to what end?

These are the questions whose answers we are gathering in these sessions to understand. We shall see, in time, by what method we may arrive at them.

- I know I am a creation, because I have not always existed.

Now that I accept that I am a created being, I must necessarily have a "creator," for I see no creation in this world that lacks a creator. Who, then, is mine?

Am I my own creator?

- I had no role, no desire, no will, no volition in my own creation. Up to the moment of my birth in this world, someone else had to be in control of my:

Conception,

Looks and features,

Sustenance,

Birth.

- Though today I carry the illusion of control, I had no control whatsoever at the beginning. Likewise, when I die, I shall again have no control. There must, then, be someone in control both before and after my existence.

Are my parents my creator?

- Like me, they were born into the world — they have a date of birth, and they passed through the same stages of development as I did. This shows that they too are creations.
- They too were once children, then young, and now old.

Since I too was once a child, then young, I am bound to grow old just as they did.

I witnessed my mother's involuntary death; this means that I, too, shall die one day.

Everywhere, then, I see a pattern of involuntary existence and involuntary death in all created things, which indicates that creation is constrained. A constraint indicates the presence of someone who has placed that constraint upon us all.

If I am not my creator, and my parents are merely the "means" of my creation, then how am I to identify my Creator?

Does there have to be a Creator?

- Suppose you have just arrived on Earth from another planet, and someone places a working analogue "CLOCK" in your hands and asks you to examine it. Would you say that this clock came into existence by itself?

The way its many mechanical wheels (garārī) work in perfect synchrony with one another;

The way its seconds command its minutes, and its minutes command its hours;

The way it does not miss a single tick;

The way, though it is composed of many objects, they all work in harmony toward a single unified purpose;

The way each item is dependent on another, and yet individually performs its own action.

- You would have no choice but to conclude that someone made this clock, and that he:

Put all these items together;

Gave each one its own function;

Set them to work together toward a single end.

- If a mechanical clock cannot come into existence on its own, could this universe have done so?

How each season has its own properties, and yet they follow each other in a fixed sequence;

How flowers bloom in spring, leaves fall in autumn, and then return in the next spring;

How night follows day, the sun rises and sets, and the moon follows the sun;

How gravity draws all things toward itself;

How minerals feed the plants, plants feed the animals, and animals feed the human being.

There has to be a Creator.

## Who is Allah?

⟪ar:ان الحکیم قال⟫:

| {{IMG_URL:http://localhost/KASHKOLE/images/quote_open.gif}} Al-Dunya Bahr al-Dhanub, wa al-Nafs Bahr al-Shahwat, wa al-Mawt Bahr al-Amal, and al-Qabr Bahr al-Nadamah {{IMG_URL:http://localhost/KASHKOLE/images/quote_closed.gif}} |
| --- |
| There are four types of oceans. The world is an ocean of sins, and the soul is a sea of lusts, and death is an ocean of deeds, and the grave is an ocean of regret.

Here "ocean" signifies abundance — the defining attribute proper to each thing named. The world is a dwelling in which no one escapes wholly free of sin; indeed, this world, ⟪ar-quote:دار السقط اللہ⟫ — the house of divine displeasure — affords countless occasions for transgression. The self (*nafs*), in like manner, casts up desires in successive waves; should one fail to govern them by intellect (*ʿaql*), the soul drags him to ruin. Once death arrives, nothing remains of a person save his deeds, fair or foul; let him therefore multiply the fair while opportunity endures. And when at last he is laid in the grave and tastes its chastisement, regret overtakes him so completely that he wishes either he had not committed his evil deeds in the world, or that he had multiplied his good ones.

Recalling the principal teaching of the previous session: **what is knowledge?** To know a person is to grasp the full measure of his station and reality. Knowledge is of two kinds — the outward (*ẓāhir*) and the inward (*bāṭin*). To recognize one's mother by her face and bearing, to know that she is the woman who bore one into the world, is outward knowledge. After such recognition, the child will never again mistake another woman for his mother; outward knowledge, in short, is the knowledge of bare acknowledgement.

But to know what it is to *be* a mother — what she endured in bearing him, how she keeps sleepless vigil over him, what rights she holds upon him — this is her inward knowledge. And when inward knowledge takes root, the child begins to love. Love is born of knowing; as knowledge grows, so love grows, and so too does reverent fear.

God, however, is so exalted and so majestic that of His essence neither outward nor inward knowledge is possible.

Concerning the takbīr, Imām Jaʿfar al-Ṣādiq said ⟪ar:’اللہ اکبر‘⟫ — meaning, it is not to be heard as "God is greater *than* something," for that would imply comparison, and nothing stands beside Him to be compared. He is greater than that He should be described at all.[^cite-3]

The existence of God can only be acknowledged — that He *is*; it cannot be encompassed by understanding. How then do we begin our journey toward Him? There is one path only: through His creation. It is by way of the creation that an argument (*ḥujja*) is raised concerning the Creator. We look first, therefore, at the order of creation in this small world (the microcosm) and then, by its light, we strive to comprehend the order of creation of the universe at large (the macrocosm).

---

When a company manufactures a product, its first concern is the market value: who will use it? The purpose of any object lies in its use. A company that builds a thing for which there are no buyers has struck a losing bargain. When Apple produced the iPhone, its aim was to make telephony easier for human beings. Every enterprise in this world, directly or indirectly, attends to human need. Humans have always stood at the centre of every act of making and every act of trade; no business has ever been founded with the convenience of a chair or a table in view. Companies built the automobile for the benefit of man; its accessories were designed to multiply that benefit. Even without a charger the car will run, but the charger was added for human comfort. Every creation in the world, then, exists for man's sake.

When Apple released its product, it placed its logo upon it. The logo is its seal, declaring to whoever sees it that the maker of the device is Apple. The seal furnishes the proof that Apple is its creator.

A user manual, too, is prepared at the time of manufacture. For what purpose? So that even a person of modest discernment may benefit from the product. Apple produced a manual for the iPhone explaining how to use it — what to do, what to avoid. How to place a call, how to send a message; do not drop it from a height; if it grows dirty, do not throw it in the washing machine. The purpose of every manual, in short, is to secure the proper use and preservation of a thing through negation and affirmation, so that the user may draw benefit from it.

Yet the manual alone does not suffice; merely to hold a book in the hand changes nothing. The language may be unfamiliar, the idiom obscure. For this reason Apple appoints representatives who demonstrate, before the eyes of all, every function the device performs. They translate the written instructions into living practice, so that buyers are not perplexed and may be assured that Apple has not produced a device impossible to use. These representatives display the same operations under varied conditions — what settings suit the home, what suit the office, what suit one travelling, what suit one who wishes not to be disturbed during sleep — so that humankind may learn.

Then there are those who, having mastered the outward use of the device, wish to penetrate its inner workings — to grasp how the Apple craftsmen built so wondrous a phone, that they themselves may design its accessories: headphones, Bluetooth devices, speakers, and the like. For such people, one must first understand the device's inner constitution: what manner of motherboard it carries, what circuits it employs, how its wiring runs, how its microphones and speakers are fashioned and joined to one another. For this purpose Apple produced a technical manual. But this technical manual is not for ordinary users. It is meant only for those who possess the requisite turn of mind, who understand electronics and mechanics. Handed to a layman — a doctor, a professor, a carpenter — it would be of no use; he could neither comprehend it nor profit from it, and might ruin the device and his pocket in the attempt.

In sum, then, five things are required: (1) the benefit of the human being, (2) the seal of the maker, (3) the user manual, (4) the appointed representative, and (5) the technical manual.

---

We return now to this world. God created this vast universe, and within it a smaller world. For whom did He fashion it? In this world there are three orders of creature beneath the human: minerals, plants, and animals. Minerals merely lie inert, drawing no benefit from the world. Plants grow that animals may feed upon them — otherwise they rot and perish. Did God then create this world for the animals? Worldly experience tells us that animals possess no rational intellect. They cannot organize themselves, nor can they shield themselves from one another. Were a thousand goats gathered together, not one could be shepherd to the rest, leading them to pasture in season or guarding them from the wolf. If the world had been made for animals, only beasts of prey would remain in it, for they would have torn the weaker to pieces. Nor would it befit God Most High to have set the entire machinery of the universe in motion for the sake of an irrational genus such as the animal.

There is, however, one order of animal upon whom God has bestowed the substance of intellect — a substance by which the creature discerns good from evil, and by which it establishes its dominion over every other creature. By means of intellect man has made minerals and plants his servants, drawing from both his ornaments, weapons, garments, dwellings, and daily provisions. He has subdued the animals as well: some he eats, some he rides, some he yokes to the plough. Man alone among the creatures of God understands speech and himself speaks. No worldly creature except man can give articulate utterance; the rest can only fashion sounds to convey their meaning.

Reason therefore declares that God created this world for man, since of all creation he alone is capable of profiting from its goods.

If God has created this world, then it must bear His emblem and His seal, that the proof of His being its Creator may stand established. That seal is the testimony of pure oneness — ⟪ar:’لا الہ الا اللہ‘⟫. Books have been composed showing how the imprint of "There is no god but God" is stamped upon every creature.[^cite-4]

Then, when God created this world, He must have provided a user manual which speaks of negation and affirmation concerning what profits man and what harms him — declaring that if you do this, you will gain, and if you do that, you will lose. That manual is the Holy Qurʾān.

But the book of the Qurʾān is mute; it does not speak of itself. There was need, therefore, of a representative alongside the book — one who could not merely read its commands aloud but enact them in practice, that the rest of humankind might learn how worship is performed and what its requirements are; what constitutes moral character (*akhlāq*), what patience, sincerity, courage; how a man is to act in poverty and how he is to act in affluence.

Then, alongside the user manual and the representative, God Most High furnished a technical manual: the Imāms, beginning with the Commander of the Faithful, ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib, and continuing through his descendants until the Day of Resurrection. They unveil the esoteric exegesis (*taʾwīl*) — the inner meanings concealed behind the outward commands of the Qurʾān — and disclose the mysteries hidden within them. They tell man whence he has come, why he has come, what awaits him after death, and where he will go. How are the wires of the Hereafter joined to those of this world? What is the reality of prayer, of fasting, of pilgrimage, of almsgiving? Why is the *maghrib* prayer of three *rakʿas*, the *ʿishāʾ* of four? If shortage of time were the reason, why then is *fajr* of only two *rakʿas* — when at dawn there is no shortage of time? Why one prostration in one place and two in another? Such questions continue without end.[^cite-5]

And just as the technical manual is not given to every hand, so these inner sciences are not bestowed upon all. They are granted only to those whose intellects have ripened and who have entered into the era of the Imām of the Age (*Imām al-Zamān*). Were such sciences offered to others, they would at first not be grasped; and were they pursued by such persons, they would be misconstrued, to the harm of both seeker and sought.

When a person cannot understand a thing, he refashions it and brings it down to a form he can apprehend. The book of every earlier prophet was altered in this way — people removed what displeased them and inserted what suited their tastes. Yet the Qurʾān, after fourteen centuries, remains preserved. The reason is that the Muslims have not penetrated to its inner realities — they have not discerned whom it addresses or of whom it speaks. Had they known, they would have remade it to their own measure.

---

So then: God created the universe. Its seal is "There is no god but God." Its user manual is the Qurʾān. Its representative is the Prophet (peace be upon him), and its technical manual is ʿAlī and the Imāms after him. We now examine the existence of God first in the light of His manual, His representative, and His technical manual; thereafter, intellect attempts, by its own light, to determine whether He is indeed God.

For whom did God fashion all this?

## Death

Bismillāh al-Raḥmān al-Raḥīm.

From the first hour of creation to the present, no soul has eluded death. The opening aphorism states the matter without ornament: ⟪ar-quote:الموت لیس منہ الفوت۔ ان أَقَمتُم أَخَذَکُم، وَ اِن فَرَرتُم مِنہُ اَدرَکَکُم⟫ — death admits of no escape; should you stand against it, it overcomes you, and should you flee, it overtakes you in the end.

The point is sharpened in a second saying: ⟪ar-quote:ھَیھاتَ أَن یَفُوتَ المَوتَ مَن طَلَبَ، أَو یَنجُو مِنہُ مَن ھَرَبَ⟫ — far is it from the one who seeks death that he should slip past it, or from the one who flees it that he should be delivered.

Our own daily speech contains the same verdict, if only we attend to it: ⟪ar-quote:أِنَّا قَولَناَ أِنَّا للہ أِقرارٌ علی أَنفُسِناَ بِالمِلْک، وقولنا أنا الیہ راجعون، أِقرارٌ علی أنفُسِنا بِالھُلْک⟫ — when we say "we belong to God," we acknowledge that He owns us; when we say "to Him we return," we acknowledge that we must perish.[^cite-6]

Death is described elsewhere with three figures: ⟪ar-quote:أن الموت لزائزٌ غیر محبوب، وواترٌ غیر مطلوب، وقِرنٌ غیر مغلوب⟫ — a guest no one loves to host, an adversary against whom no claim may be pressed, and a rival whom none has overcome.

The remedy, however, is also given. ⟪ar-quote:شَوِّقُوا أَنفُسِکُم الی نعیم الجَنَّۃ، تُحِبُّوا الموتَ وَتَمقُتُو الحیاۃَ⟫ — fix your longing upon the bliss of the next abode, and you will come to love death and to find this world bitter on the tongue.

The march of mortality is then drawn out in a chain of brief clauses: ⟪ar-quote:کُلَّ مُتَوَقِّعٌ آت۔ وَکُلَّ آتٍ قَرِیب۔ وَکُلَّ قَریبٌ دان۔ کُلَّ أَمرءِ لاقَ حِمَامُہُ۔⟫ — everything expected arrives; everything that arrives is close; whatever is close is near at hand; and every soul will meet its appointed hour. Hence the rhetorical pair: ⟪ar-quote:کیف یَسلمُ من الموت طالِبُہُ؟ کیف تَنسیٰ الموت و آثارُہُ تُذَکِّرُکَ⟫ — how should the one who hunts death himself be safe from it? And how should you forget death when its very traces never cease to remind you?

The same law governs both the heavens and the dust beneath one's feet: ⟪ar-quote:لِکُل نَاجَمَ أَفُولٌ وَ لِکُلِّ نَفسٍ حِمامٌ، وَلکل حَیٍّ مَوتٌ⟫ — every rising star has its setting, every soul has its term, and every living thing must die. Nor does wealth or want make any difference: ⟪ar-quote:لَن یَنجُو مِن الموتِ غَنِیٌّ لِکثرۃِ مالِہ، ولَن یَسلَمَ مِن الموت فَقیرٌ لأِقلالِہ۔ لَو أَنَّ الموت یُشتریٰ ، لأشتَراَہُ الأغنِیَاءُ⟫ — no rich man is saved by his abundance, no poor man by his lack; if death could be bought, the wealthy would have purchased the whole stock of it long ago.[^cite-7]

*Jama Masjid Panama*

A final figure brings the same theme home through the turning of the heavens themselves: ⟪ar-quote:أَفلاَ تَرَونَ اِلی اللیل و النھار، یَبلِیاَنِ کُلَّ جَدید وَ یَقِرَّبانِ کل بعید وَ یَأتِیاَنِ بِکُلِّ مَوعُود⟫ — do you not see that night and day are wearing thin every new thing, drawing every distant thing closer, and bringing every promise of God to its appointment?

## Creation through Love

Do you wish your wife to love you, your neighbours to love you, and the wider circle of your household to love you? Then attend first to the foundation upon which God has built both this world and the religion that interprets it.

That foundation is love (maḥabba — affectionate attachment, the binding force between souls), not enmity, and not the heat of anger. The world endures because love endures within it; let love fail, and the world fails with it. As one teacher of ours, Naṣrullāh Ṣāḥib, used to remark: on the Day of Judgment every soul will be wholly preoccupied with itself. The father will not enquire after the son; the mother will not enquire after the daughter; the brother will pass the brother by. From which it follows that love will not survive that Day — and from which it further follows that the disappearance of love just is what the Hour means. The day on which love is no more is the day of the doomsday.

Consider the story preserved in the tradition. Pharaoh kept a jester in his court who would dress in the likeness of Mūsā (peace be upon him) and mock him before the assembly. When the sea closed over Pharaoh, this jester stood upon the shore watching the spectacle, untouched by the calamity. Later, when Mūsā ascended to commune with his Lord, he raised a complaint: "O Lord, You drowned the one who counterfeited You and made sport of Your majesty, yet You left untouched the one who counterfeited me." The reply came back: "O Mūsā, you do not yet understand love. I left him untouched because at that hour he wore your likeness, and to drown the likeness of the beloved is contrary to the law of love itself."

Love is, at root, the name of consent — of one thing yielding itself to be taken up into another. The order of nutrition rests upon this very principle. Until the mineral consents inwardly to the plant, the plant does not draw it upward into the green of its body. Until the grass consents to the goat, the goat does not assimilate it into flesh. Until the goat consents beneath the knife of the human being, the human being does not receive it as nourishment. And until a human being himself consents — until he relinquishes the ⟪ar:’أنا‘⟫, the proud "I" — he cannot rise to become the food of the angel. This consent, moreover, is given only by love. Appetite cannot give it; force cannot extract it.[^cite-8]

### The Love of God

In his Ḥayāt al-Ḥayawān, the polymath al-Damīrī relates that one day ʿĪsā (peace be upon him) passed by a mountain where a worshipper sat alone at his devotions. His back was bent from long prostration, his body wasted to a thorn. ʿĪsā asked: "Noble one, how long have you been at this worship?" He answered: "O Prophet of God, for seventy years; but in all that time not a single need of mine has been answered." Then he begged the Messiah: "You are the chosen of God — speak for me." ʿĪsā asked what he sought. The man said: "I ask my Lord to grant me one particle of His pure love." ʿĪsā lifted his hands; the prayer of the Spirit of God was answered. A few days later, ʿĪsā passed by that place again. The cell of the worshipper had collapsed; the ground beneath it had split open; and in the pit stood the old worshipper, his mouth open, his eyes shut, his face turned toward the heavens.

> The closed eye finds Him within the veil — far beyond the widest field of sight, yet near to the heart.

ʿĪsā saluted him; no answer came. Astonished, he enquired: "O Lord, what is the state of this elder?" The answer was: "O ʿĪsā, this worshipper sought from Us a single particle of Our pure love. Knowing that he could not bear what he asked, We gave him one part of seventy thousand parts of Our love. See how it has overcome him. Now tell Us — what would have become of him had We given him even a little more?"[^cite-9]

The mark of true love is that it does not run in one direction only; it returns. Of His own initiative God declares: ⟪ar-quote:يَٓأَيُّهَا ٱلَّذِينَ آمَنُوا۟ مَن يَرْتَدَّ مِنكُمْ عَن دِينِهِۦ فَسَوْفَ يَأْتِى ٱللَّهُ بِقَوْمٍۢ يُحِبُّهُمْ وَيُحِبُّونَهُ⟫ — He will bring forth a people whom He loves and who, in turn, love Him. He accepts them as His friends, and they in turn know Him as theirs. The joy of love lies only in this reciprocity, in the kindling of fire on both sides at once. And the love of God is reserved, in its fullness, for those who follow His Messenger in deed and in inward bearing. Hence the locus classicus, upon which all the rest stands:

> ⟪ar:‏قُلْ إِن كُنتُمْ تُحِبُّونَ ٱللَّهَ فَٱتَّبِعُونِى يُحْبِبْكُمُ ٱللَّهُ وَيَغْفِرْ لَكُمْ ذُنُوبَكُمْ ۗ وَٱللَّهُ غَفُورٌۭ رَّحِيمٌۭ ‎⟫
> *Say (O Muḥammad, peace be upon him, to mankind): "If you (truly) love God, then follow me; God will love you and forgive you your sins. God is Oft-Forgiving, Most Merciful."*
