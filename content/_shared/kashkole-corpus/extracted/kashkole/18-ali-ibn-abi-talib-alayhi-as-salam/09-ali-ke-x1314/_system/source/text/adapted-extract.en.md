## Various Slanders Against ʿAlī and the Qurʾān

There is a faction among the partisans of the Commander of the Faithful who maintain that the Qurʾān was in truth revealed to Ḥaḍrat ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib rather than to Muḥammad ibn ʿAbd Allāh (peace be upon them both). In the original codex, they say, the name of ʿAlī and the names of his descendants stood in unambiguous terms; only later were these names struck out. By their reckoning, then, the Qurʾān has suffered the same fate as the Torah and the Gospel at the hands of those Jewish and Christian custodians who tampered with their heavenly Books.

This is contrary to the truth. God Himself has pledged to preserve the revelation:

> ⟪ar:‏إِنَّا نَحْنُ نَزَّلْنَا ٱلذِّكْرَ وَإِنَّا لَهُۥ لَحَفِظُونَ ‎⟫
>
> *Indeed, it is We who sent down the Reminder, and indeed, We are its Guardian.* — Qurʾān ⟪quran 15:9⟫

Before refuting this false belief outright, it is appropriate to clarify the difference in rank between Muḥammad and ʿAlī — between the speaking-prophet and his foundation. We shall first set out that distinction as it is given in the Sharīʿa, then turn to its rational demonstration, so that the true conviction of the people of truth may emerge in full light.

### The Messenger of God is the analogue of Adam

According to the Sharīʿa and the heavenly Books, Adam is the father of humankind, and Eve was drawn forth from one of his ribs. God says:

> ⟪ar:‏يَٓأَيُّهَا ٱلنَّاسُ ٱتَّقُوا۟ رَبَّكُمُ ٱلَّذِى خَلَقَكُم مِّن نَّفْسٍۢ وَحِدَةٍۢ وَخَلَقَ مِنْهَا زَوْجَهَا وَبَثَّ مِنْهُمَا رِجَالًۭا كَثِيرًۭا وَنِسَآءًۭ ۚ وَٱتَّقُوا۟ ٱللَّهَ ٱلَّذِى تَسَآءَلُونَ بِهِۦ وَٱلْأَرْحَامَ ۚ إِنَّ ٱللَّهَ كَانَ عَلَيْكُمْ رَقِيبًۭا ‎⟫
>
> *O mankind, fear your Lord, who created you from a single soul (nafs wāḥida) and from it created its mate, and from the two of them scattered many men and women.* — Qurʾān ⟪quran 4:1⟫

The common exegetes hold that "the single soul" refers to Adam, formed from clay, that "its mate" refers to Eve, and that the proliferation of men and women refers to the multiplication of human progeny in the plain physical sense. But the people of taʾwīl (esoteric exegesis) read the verse otherwise. They observe that every prophet is the "Adam" of his cycle, since each is the founder of an age and the era is named after him. Just as Adam was the first to appear in human form and the first to be taught the names of all things, so in the final cycle Ḥaḍrat Khātim al-Nabiyyīn Sayyid al-Mursalīn Muḥammad (peace be upon him) was the first to emerge from the ḥanīfiyya of his own age. The priority spoken of is not bodily but pertains to his subtle, luminous self, which was the first to be initiated into the sciences of the Hereafter.[^cite-1]

### ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib is the analogue of Eve

Just as God created Adam — the "single soul" — from dust, and from him brought forth his mate Eve, so He brought forth from the Prophet (peace be upon him), in a spiritual and subtle mode, his consort and successor, ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib. Mawlānā ʿAlī is the bearer of the Prophet's trust and the custodian of his mysteries, of revelation, and of the sciences of the Qurʾān — just as the woman is the custodian of her partner's seed and gives shape, within her womb, to that which is otherwise formless. It is on account of this profound correspondence that the Prophet (peace be upon him) said, *O ʿAlī, you and I are the parents of the believers (the Adam and Eve of this community)*. Were the saying not pointing to this inner, spiritual generation, the two could not, in any physical sense, be the parents of all humanity. That the Prophet is in fact the father of the believers is established by the divine word itself:

> ⟪ar:‏ٱلنَّبِىُّ أَوْلَىٰ بِٱلْمُؤْمِنِينَ مِنْ أَنفُسِهِمْ ۖ وَأَزْوَجُهُۥٓ أُمَّهَتُهُمْ ۗ وَأُو۟لُوا۟ ٱلْأَرْحَامِ بَعْضُهُمْ أَوْلَىٰ بِبَعْضٍۢ فِى كِتَبِ ٱللَّهِ مِنَ ٱلْمُؤْمِنِينَ وَٱلْمُهَجِرِينَ إِلَّآ أَن تَفْعَلُوٓا۟ إِلَىٰٓ أَوْلِيَآئِكُم مَّعْرُوفًۭا ۚ كَانَ ذَلِكَ فِى ٱلْكِتَبِ مَسْطُورًۭا ‎⟫
>
> *The Prophet is closer to the believers than they are to themselves, and his wives are their mothers.* — Qurʾān ⟪quran 33:6⟫

If the Prophet's wives are the mothers of the believers, then the Prophet himself must be their father; and this fatherhood, plainly, is religious rather than physical. The line that issued "from Adam and Eve, many men and women" is therefore — in the inner reading — the line of the Imāms of the Family, who flourished spiritually from Muḥammad and ʿAlī. From this it follows that the difference between Muḥammad and ʿAlī is no smaller than the difference between heaven and earth, between man and woman, between Adam and Eve. When ʿAlī (upon him be peace) was, in this hierarchical sense, brought forth on account of the Prophet, how could the Qurʾān have been revealed to him rather than to the Prophet?

### What is revelation?

Let us consider next what revelation is and what it means for it to descend upon the prophets. The largest difference between us and the prophets is that we are slaves of nature — our souls in thrall to appetite, absorbed in the pursuit of bodily pleasures and the ordering of our worldly affairs. The souls of the prophets are the inverse of ours: nature is their servant, and they devote themselves to the cultivation of the Hereafter by setting aside appetite and the body that craves it.

For this reason, we are dependent upon the five outward senses for everything we come to know. The prophets, on account of the intelligence and transparency of their souls, acquire the sciences of religion and the Hereafter without the mediation of any outward sense, and only thereafter convey them to us in speech, which our ears receive. By the austerity, worship, and purity of their souls, the prophets stand near to the angels and so receive these sciences from them with ease; thereafter they clothe what they have received in meaningful words composed of the letters of the alphabet, and deliver it to our hearing. Hence the verse:

> ⟪ar:‏نَزَلَ بِهِ ٱلرُّوحُ ٱلْأَمِينُ ‎⟫
>
> ⟪ar:‏عَلَىٰ قَلْبِكَ لِتَكُونَ مِنَ ٱلْمُنذِرِينَ ‎⟫
>
> ⟪ar:‏بِلِسَانٍ عَرَبِىٍّۢ مُّبِينٍۢ ‎⟫
>
> *The Trustworthy Spirit brought it down upon your heart, that you might be among the warners, in a clear Arabic tongue.* — Qurʾān ⟪quran 26:193⟫ ⟪quran 26:194⟫ ⟪quran 26:195⟫

Note that God said *upon your heart* — not *upon your ear*. What descends upon the heart has no need of words at all. He then added, *in a clear Arabic tongue*, indicating that the substance of the revelation, having reached the heart without words, was thereafter clothed in words for the sake of these worldly listeners and explained in their idiom.[^cite-2] It is well known that when revelation came upon the Prophet (peace be upon him), he felt a vast weight settle upon him: he would lose consciousness, sweat would pour from him, and so deep was the state of absorption that, had a piece of flesh been cut from his blessed body, he would have known nothing of it. It was on account of these states that the disbelievers and enemies called him deranged — a slander God Himself overturned:

> ⟪ar:‏لِلَّذِينَ لَا يُؤْمِنُونَ بِٱلْآخِرَةِ مَثَلُ ٱلسَّوْءِ ۖ وَلِلَّهِ ٱلْمَثَلُ ٱلْأَعْلَىٰ ۚ وَهُوَ ٱلْعَزِيزُ ٱلْحَكِيمُ ‎⟫
>
> *For those who do not believe in the Hereafter is the evil likeness; and to God belongs the highest likeness — and He is the Mighty, the Wise.* — Qurʾān ⟪quran 16:60⟫

The evil likeness is not for the Prophet but for those who slander him.

### The rational demonstration of the Prophet's preeminence

Let us now examine this proposition by reason. If we survey the genera of created things, we find within each genus a particular species, and within that species a particular individual, in which the nobility of the whole genus is gathered. Take the genus of stones: among them is the species of jewel, and among the jewels stands the red ruby (yāqūt aḥmar). It too is a stone, but on account of certain singular properties it surpasses all others in nobility. God set it apart and, by the action of the sun, conferred upon it a colour and a luminescence which no common stone shares. By the same principle we may argue, for the luminous nature of the prophets among mankind, that although the prophets are human as we are, they stand among us as the red rubies stand among stones — incomparable in their form and beyond price. God endowed them with the powers of prophethood and made them strong enough to bear the burden of His trust. So too within the other genera: among the seed-bearing plants, grain stands at the apex; among the trees, the date palm; among the animals, the human being takes precedence by virtue of reason, reflection, and intellect. The prophets, then, are the noblest of mankind by reason of the purity and nobility of their souls — and after them, their successors.

### On the alleged distortion (taḥrīf) of the Qurʾān

The mistaken hold further that the present Qurʾān is defective, that its wording has been tampered with, and that it once contained the names of ʿAlī and the Imāms of the Ahl al-Bayt (upon them be peace) — names which were later excised. Just as the Jews and Christians altered their heavenly Books, they say, so the Qurʾān too has been distorted (muḥarraf).

This belief is false. The truth is that there can be no distortion in the words of the Qurʾān. The codex we hold in our hands is precisely what was delivered by the Messenger of God. What the deniers of ʿAlī's walāya and the enemies of the Ahl al-Bayt have done, however, is to seize at will upon the meanings of its verses and to interpret them as they pleased, thus suppressing what the verses say in honour of the infallible Imāms. Consider the divine word:

> ⟪ar:‏وَكَذَلِكَ جَعَلْنَكُمْ أُمَّةًۭ وَسَطًۭا لِّتَكُونُوا۟ شُهَدَآءَ عَلَى ٱلنَّاسِ وَيَكُونَ ٱلرَّسُولُ عَلَيْكُمْ شَهِيدًۭا ۗ وَمَا جَعَلْنَا ٱلْقِبْلَةَ ٱلَّتِى كُنتَ عَلَيْهَآ إِلَّا لِنَعْلَمَ مَن يَتَّبِعُ ٱلرَّسُولَ مِمَّن يَنقَلِبُ عَلَىٰ عَقِبَيْهِ ۚ وَإِن كَانَتْ لَكَبِيرَةً إِلَّا عَلَى ٱلَّذِينَ هَدَى ٱللَّهُ ۗ وَمَا كَانَ ٱللَّهُ لِيُضِيعَ إِيمَنَكُمْ ۚ إِنَّ ٱللَّهَ بِٱلنَّاسِ لَرَءُوفٌۭ رَّحِيمٌۭ ‎⟫
>
> *And thus We have made you a middle community (umma wasaṭ), that you may be witnesses over the people, and that the Messenger may be a witness over you.* — Qurʾān ⟪quran 2:143⟫

The "middle community" here are the Imāms of the Ahl al-Bayt (upon them be peace), who stand as witnesses over the people: each Imām is witness over the people of his age, and the Prophet is witness over the Imāms in turn. But the enemies of the Imāmate and the walāya have interpreted the verse to mean that every Muslim is a member of the "middle community," and that all Muslims are witnesses over one another — a reading which is empty on its face. For if every Muslim is a witness, who then is the *mashhūd ʿalayhi*, the one testified against? Should it be said that the testimony stands against Jews, Christians, and unbelievers, the difficulty only deepens: the rulings of Islam bind Muslims, not non-Muslims, and the office of the witness in this verse is precisely to testify whether the followers of the Messenger have kept to his path or strayed from it. The verse can have no proper bearing upon Jews, Christians, or polytheists. The exegetes of the common people, in effect, have shifted the meaning — exactly as God describes:

> ⟪ar:‏فَبِمَا نَقْضِهِم مِّيثَقَهُمْ لَعَنَّهُمْ وَجَعَلْنَا قُلُوبَهُمْ قَسِيَةًۭ ۖ يُحَرِّفُونَ ٱلْكَلِمَ عَن مَّوَاضِعِهِۦ ۙ وَنَسُوا۟ حَظًّۭا مِّمَّا ذُكِّرُوا۟ بِهِۦ ۚ وَلَا تَزَالُ تَطَّلِعُ عَلَىٰ خَآئِنَةٍۢ مِّنْهُمْ إِلَّا قَلِيلًۭا مِّنْهُمْ ۖ فَٱعْفُ عَنْهُمْ وَٱصْفَحْ ۚ إِنَّ ٱللَّهَ يُحِبُّ ٱلْمُحْسِنِينَ ‎⟫
>
> *They displace the words from their places (yuḥarrifūna al-kalim ʿan mawāḍiʿihi) and have forgotten a portion of what they were reminded of.* — Qurʾān ⟪quran 5:13⟫

The "words" here, in the inner sense, are the Imāms (upon them be peace). In the same way, the verse of mubāhala, which was revealed in honour of the Panjtan-i Pāk (the Five Pure Ones) alone — Muḥammad, ʿAlī, Fāṭima, Ḥasan, and Ḥusayn — has been wrested by these exegetes to embrace all and sundry.

In other words, what is written between the lines of the Qurʾān they could not change. The change has come about, and grievously, in the realm of meaning. The Qurʾān itself describes a similar form of corruption when it reports the boast of Satan and pronounces it fulfilled. Satan vowed:

> ⟪ar:‏وَلَأُضِلَّنَّهُمْ وَلَأُمَنِّيَنَّهُمْ وَلَآمُرَنَّهُمْ فَلَيُبَتِّكُنَّ آذَانَ ٱلْأَنْعَمِ وَلَآمُرَنَّهُمْ فَلَيُغَيِّرُنَّ خَلْقَ ٱللَّهِ ۚ وَمَن يَتَّخِذِ ٱلشَّيْطَنَ وَلِيًّۭا مِّن دُونِ ٱللَّهِ فَقَدْ خَسِرَ خُسْرَانًۭا مُّبِينًۭا ‎⟫
>
> *I will surely command them, and they will change the creation of God (khalq Allāh).* — Qurʾān ⟪quran 4:119⟫

That is to say: *I will command all of them to alter God's creation.* Yet it has never been observed that Satan or his party has, in any apparent sense, changed the outward shape of man, nor are they able to do so. Were they so empowered, a vast visible distance would lie between the forms of the unbelievers and the forms of the believers; the bodies of unbelievers would be misshapen and depart from the human form God designed — and yet they do not. What Satan did distort, however, is the soul-form (the inward features) of his followers. He corrupted their convictions and ruined their spiritual shape. In just this way, the outward words of the Qurʾān remain preserved, while the meanings have been turned aside.[^cite-3]

(Kitāb al-Majālis al-Muʾayyadiyya, Sayyidnā al-Muʾayyad fī al-Dīn al-Shīrāzī)

## Was ʿAlī Incapable of Attaining the Caliphate?

It is our firm conviction that ʿAlī (peace be upon him) did not refrain from claiming the caliphate (khilāfa) out of humility or weakness. Rather, he chose silence so that the Muslim community would not slip into idolatry and so that Islam itself would not be brought to ruin. The Messenger of Allāh had forewarned him of the trials to come, saying: "O ʿAlī, if the people accept your authority after me without dispute, take up the affair; but if they differ and follow others, leave them to what they have chosen — Allāh will open a way for you."[^cite-4] In accordance with this testament, he held his peace during the rule of the three who preceded him. Yet when the community at last pressed him to accept the caliphate as the fourth in succession, he hesitated for no one and made concessions to none, confronting every rebel and dissenter. At the battles of Jamal, Ṣiffīn, and Nahrawān, the sword Dhū-l-Faqār (the cleaving blade) flashed in the hand of Ḥaydar and subdued every opponent — and so the Prophet's own prophecy was fulfilled: "O ʿAlī, you will wage jihād against the Nākithīn (the oath-breakers), the Qāsiṭīn (the deviators), and the Māriqīn (the renegades)."[^cite-5]

The Commander of the Faithful once delivered a sermon at Kūfa, summoning the people to march against Muʿāwiya and the army of Syria. Al-Ashʿath ibn Qays al-Kindī rose from the assembly and challenged him: "O ʿAlī, I see that you recount the story of your grievance again and again, recalling how you have been wronged since the passing of the Messenger of Allāh. What, then, prevented you from drawing your sword to claim your right? Why did you endure such injustice in silence?"

He replied: "O Ashʿath, what stayed my hand was the very thing that once stayed the hand of my brother Hārūn. When Mūsā departed for the appointed meeting with his Lord, he left Hārūn as his deputy over the Children of Israel. They disobeyed him and turned to worship the calf fashioned by the Sāmirī. Hārūn admonished them, but they refused to listen; and he kept his peace. When Mūsā returned and saw what had happened, he rebuked his brother: 'What is this? Why did you not punish them?' Hārūn answered, 'I feared lest you should say that I had divided the Children of Israel and had not heeded your word.' And the counsel Mūsā had earlier given him was this: 'Set right what can be set right, and do not follow the way of the corrupters.'

"In the same manner, my brother had placed me in the station of Hārūn and said to me: 'If the people accept your authority without dispute or strife, well and good. But if they differ and refuse obedience, then keep silent, preserve your blood, and remain in your house. Do not cause division in my Umma (the Muslim community).' I too feared lest my brother, the Messenger of Allāh, should one day say to me: 'You rent my community asunder and did not act upon my word.' For that reason I held back and said nothing."

On another occasion the hypocrites maligned him, claiming that ʿAlī had relinquished his right to the caliphate out of humility and inability. Addressing them directly, he declared: "It has reached me that some imagine I forfeited my right to the caliphate out of meekness and impotence. By Allāh, this is folly and falsehood. I followed only the testament of my brother, the Holy Prophet. By God, had I willed to seize it, I could have done so — my hand is neither feeble nor short. But we are the honored servants of Allāh; we do not transgress His command.[^cite-6] And even were one to grant, for argument's sake, that I yielded my right out of weakness, would there be any disgrace in my following the example of the six great prophets?

"Nūḥ cried, 'O my Lord, I am overcome — so help me.' Ibrāhīm declared, 'I withdraw from you and from all you call upon besides Allāh.' Mūsā confessed to his people, 'I fled from you when I feared you.' Hārūn pleaded, 'The people reckoned me weak and were on the point of killing me.' Lūṭ lamented, 'Would that I had strength against you, or could betake myself to some powerful support.' And last of all, my brother the Messenger of Allāh was himself compelled to migrate from Mecca. I have done no more than follow their example.

"Then, when the Muslims themselves at last came to me in their bewilderment, having no other recourse, and pressed me to ascend the seat of the caliphate, I accepted it as the need of the hour required — and thereafter I showed no softness. The people broke their oaths, perpetrated injustice, cast off religion, and the hatred of their hearts came forth. I fought them all, and I struck down none unjustly. So let no one mistake my conduct: we act in accordance with the demands of the moment."

## The Debate of al-Maʾmūn al-Rashīd with Forty Scholars

What follows is rendered from the famous *munāẓara* (formal disputation) held in the court of the Abbasid caliph al-Maʾmūn al-Rashīd between the caliph and Qāḍī Isḥāq together with forty of the most eminent Sunni jurists of the age, as preserved in al-ʿIqd al-Farīd of Ibn ʿAbd Rabbih. The caliph maintained that ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib (peace be upon him) was the most excellent of the Companions and the most deserving of the caliphate; the qāḍī and the forty scholars maintained the opposite. For the duration of the encounter, the veil of awe that ordinarily silences a caliph's interlocutors was lifted and every man spoke freely. The exchange ran from dawn until close to noon, and by the testimony of the chronicler the field never once slipped from al-Maʾmūn's grasp.

---

**al-Maʾmūn:** My creed is that after the Messenger of God (may God bless him and his Family) ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib is the most excellent of all the caliphs and the most worthy of the caliphate.

**Isḥāq:** We see no warrant for that judgement. Since you have summoned us to debate, set out the proof of your claim.

**al-Maʾmūn:** Tell me first, Isḥāq — by what do men exceed one another in nobility?

**Isḥāq:** By righteous deeds.

**al-Maʾmūn:** Just so. Then consider this. If a man stood foremost in the lifetime of the Prophet, but after the Prophet's passing others performed deeds greater than his, are those later men therefore more excellent than the one who had stood foremost in the Messenger's own company?

Isḥāq paused at the question and was perplexed.

**al-Maʾmūn:** You cannot answer "yes," Isḥāq — for then I will lead you to those who came after and had longer scope for *jihād*, the Pilgrimage, fasting, prayer, and almsgiving than the first generation, and you will be forced to grant them precedence over the foremost. So tell me plainly.

**Isḥāq:** My lord — none can ever be set on a level with him who was foremost in the time of the Messenger of God.

**al-Maʾmūn:** Then weigh, in their multitude, the reports your own Companions, Successors, traditionists, and the leading teachers of your school have transmitted in the praise and virtues of ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib. Set beside them the reports you have received in the virtues of Abū Bakr. If those of Abū Bakr equal what stands for ʿAlī, you may claim he was his superior. No — by God, they do not. Then add to Abū Bakr the virtues of ʿUmar and weigh the sum against ʿAlī's. If even the joined account merely equals his, the precedence still remains your case to argue. No — by God, it does not. Add ʿUthmān as a third, set the threefold reckoning against ʿAlī alone, and even now you will not reach his measure. Add to them the ten whom the Prophet himself testified were the people of Paradise; weigh the gathered virtues of the ten against the virtues of ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib, and they will not surpass his. Beyond that, abandon this false notion.

Let that pass. Tell me, Isḥāq — at the moment the Prophet was sent, what was the most excellent deed?

**Isḥāq:** To pronounce the testimony of faith in sincerity.

**al-Maʾmūn:** And is precedence (*sabq*) in entering Islam not a greater excellence than any that came later?

**Isḥāq:** Indeed it is.

**al-Maʾmūn:** And God Most High says: ⟪quran 56:10⟫ — "And the foremost are the foremost; those are the ones brought near." The "foremost" (al-sābiqūn) in this verse are none other than those who took the lead in entering Islam. Do you know of any man who became a Muslim before ʿAlī?

**Isḥāq:** My lord — ʿAlī entered Islam as a child, in years when the Prophet could lay no charge upon him; Abū Bakr came to Islam in maturity, when the obligation could weigh upon him fully.

**al-Maʾmūn:** Tell me first only who was first to embrace Islam. The question of immaturity or maturity we may take up after.

**Isḥāq:** There is no doubt that ʿAlī accepted Islam before Abū Bakr — though I must add: as a minor.

**al-Maʾmūn:** Granted: ʿAlī accepted Islam as a young man. Now tell me — did ʿAlī enter Islam through the Prophet's invitation, or by direct divine inspiration?

Isḥāq grew uneasy at the question and fell to thinking.

**al-Maʾmūn:** You cannot say he was inspired directly — for then you would set him above the Prophet himself, since the Prophet did not receive Islam by inspiration but by the descent of Jibrīl.

**Isḥāq:** Beyond doubt, ʿAlī was not inspired; rather, the Prophet invited him.

**al-Maʾmūn:** Then the matter narrows to two possibilities. Either the Prophet invited ʿAlī by God's command, or he did so of his own desire.

Isḥāq was confounded.

**al-Maʾmūn:** Isḥāq — you cannot ascribe to the Messenger of God an act issuing from mere personal desire, for God Himself testifies of him: ⟪quran 53:3⟫ — "Nor does he speak from desire; it is but a revelation revealed."

**Isḥāq:** Of course not. By God's command the Prophet invited ʿAlī.

**al-Maʾmūn:** And is it permissible for God Most High to charge His Messenger with inviting one upon whom no charge of obligation may rest?

**Isḥāq:** God forbid — never.

**al-Maʾmūn:** Then consider what your own words entail. You said ʿAlī accepted Islam at an age when religious responsibility could not yet rest upon him. By that you imply that the Prophet imposed obligation upon children for whom obligation was beyond their capacity. And were such children, having entered Islam, to apostatize, would there be no penalty? Would the Prophet truly have burdened them in their tender years? Could you say such a thing of the Messenger of God?

**Isḥāq:** God forbid — never.

**al-Maʾmūn:** Then it stands proven: the Prophet invited ʿAlī to Islam and gave him precedence over all creation, distinguishing him by that very precedence so that men might come to apprehend his excellence. Had God commanded that children be summoned without distinction, the Prophet would have summoned other children alongside ʿAlī.

**Isḥāq:** Indeed.

**al-Maʾmūn:** Do you know of any other child whom the Messenger of God invited to Islam from among his kin? I press the point lest you imagine ʿAlī was summoned merely because he was a cousin, with no quality particular to him.

**Isḥāq:** I do not know — I cannot say whether the Prophet summoned another.

**al-Maʾmūn:** Isḥāq — do you suppose you will be questioned on the Day of Resurrection concerning what you do not know? Then why argue from what God has spared you the burden of knowing?

Let us proceed. Tell me — what was the most excellent deed *after* the first entry into Islam?

**Isḥāq:** Striving in the way of God (*jihād fī sabīl Allāh*).

**al-Maʾmūn:** Just so. Do you find in any Companion of the Prophet a record of service in *jihād* equal to that of ʿAlī?

**Isḥāq:** In which battle, my lord?

**al-Maʾmūn:** In any.

**Isḥāq:** Take, then, the Battle of Badr.

**al-Maʾmūn:** Badr. Are not the services of others slight beside ʿAlī's? Tell me — how many of the idolaters were slain that day?

**Isḥāq:** Some sixty, of the polytheists.

**al-Maʾmūn:** And how many fell to ʿAlī alone?

**Isḥāq:** I do not know.

**al-Maʾmūn:** Then hear: twenty-three by his hand, and forty divided among the rest of the Muslim host.

**Isḥāq:** But the Prophet himself observed that Abū Bakr was present with him in the *ʿarīsh* (the command-shelter) on that day.

**al-Maʾmūn:** Yes — but what was he doing there?

**Isḥāq:** Counsels were being taken.

**al-Maʾmūn:** Woe to you. Did the Messenger of God need counsel apart from himself? Or counsel along with himself? If alongside himself, did he take Abū Bakr as a partner in the decision, or did he rest upon Abū Bakr's opinion? Of these three suppositions, which do you commend?

**Isḥāq:** God forbid — neither did Abū Bakr counsel apart from him, nor was he a partner with him, nor had the Messenger of God need of his opinion.

**al-Maʾmūn:** Then where, brother, lies the excellence in sitting in the *ʿarīsh*? Is the man fighting with the sword in defence of the Messenger of God not more excellent than one seated at ease beside him?

**Isḥāq:** My lord — the whole army was engaged in *jihād*. What then distinguishes ʿAlī?

**al-Maʾmūn:** True — all fought. But he who fights with the sword, who shields the Messenger of God, surely stands above the man who sits beside him at his ease. Have you not read in the Book of God: ⟪quran 4:95⟫ — "Not equal are those of the believers who remain seated — other than the incapacitated — and those who strive in the path of God with their wealth and their lives. God has preferred the strivers, with their wealth and their lives, over those who sit, by a degree" — and though God has promised good to all believers, He has set the strivers above the sedentary by an immense reward.

**Isḥāq:** But Abū Bakr and ʿUmar were themselves *mujāhidūn*.

**al-Maʾmūn:** And had Abū Bakr and ʿUmar a precedence over those who did not take part at all in that battle?

**Isḥāq:** Surely they did.

**al-Maʾmūn:** By the same measure, is not the one who hazarded his life and gave battle with the sword more excellent than Abū Bakr and ʿUmar, who sat secure in the *ʿarīsh*?

**Isḥāq:** Yes — that too is true.

**al-Maʾmūn:** Now, Isḥāq, since you recite the Qurʾān — recite for me Sūrat al-Insān.

Isḥāq began to recite, and when he reached the relevant verse al-Maʾmūn halted him: ⟪quran 76:9⟫ — "We feed you only for the sake of God; we desire from you neither reward nor thanks."

**al-Maʾmūn:** In whose honour was that verse revealed?

**Isḥāq:** In honour of ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib.

**al-Maʾmūn:** Then mark: when ʿAlī fed the needy, the orphan, and the captive, he said within himself, *"We feed you only for the sake of God."* Has God ever extolled any other Companion as He extolled ʿAlī by this saying?

**Isḥāq:** No other has said *"We feed you only for the sake of God,"* nor has any other been so praised in the Qurʾān.

**al-Maʾmūn:** Just so — for God Himself knew the inward bearing of ʿAlī. Now answer me this. Do you testify that the ten promised Paradise by the Prophet were indeed of Paradise?

**Isḥāq:** I so testify.

**al-Maʾmūn:** If a man should say, "I do not know whether that *ḥadīth* is sound, nor whether the Prophet said it," does that make him an unbeliever?

**Isḥāq:** God forbid — no.

**al-Maʾmūn:** And if that same man should say, "I do not know whether Sūrat *hal atā* is the word of God or not" — does he become an unbeliever?

**Isḥāq:** Yes.

**al-Maʾmūn:** Then explain the difference. — Now, Isḥāq, do you also transmit *ḥadīth*?

**Isḥāq:** I do.

**al-Maʾmūn:** Then narrate to me what you know.

Isḥāq narrated the *Ḥadīth al-Ṭayr* (the tradition of the roasted bird): that a cooked fowl was set before the Messenger of God, and he prayed, *"O God, send to me the dearest of Your creation to You, that he may share this meal with me."* And ʿAlī came in, and the two of them ate together.

**al-Maʾmūn:** Until this moment, Isḥāq, I had argued with you on the supposition that you were no enemy of the truth. Now your hostility to the truth is laid bare. Are you certain this *ḥadīth* is sound?

**Isḥāq:** Yes — it is transmitted by men whose reports cannot be rejected.

**al-Maʾmūn:** Then understand what follows. If a man holds this *ḥadīth* sound and still maintains that there was, in the Umma, one more excellent than ʿAlī, he must accept one of three propositions: either that God did not answer His Prophet's prayer (and so did not send the dearest of His creation); or that God sent the dearest, yet there was someone better than the dearest (and thus the most beloved is not the most excellent); or that God made no distinction between excellence and divine love, sending ʿAlī merely as one among equals. To which of these three are you prepared to consent?

Isḥāq sat unable to speak.

**al-Maʾmūn:** You can consent to none of the three, Isḥāq — or I shall call you to repentance. If there is a fourth construction, state it.

**Isḥāq:** I am humbled in this matter — yet Abū Bakr too has his merits.

**al-Maʾmūn:** Certainly: were there no merit in him, there would be nothing to say even in claiming him superior to ʿAlī, for "the more excellent" presupposes excellence. But which merit do you have in mind?

**Isḥāq:** The verse of the cave, in which God Most High says: ⟪quran 9:40⟫ — "The second of the two when they were in the cave, when he said to his companion (*ṣāḥib*), 'Do not grieve; indeed God is with us.'" Here God names Abū Bakr the Prophet's companion.

**al-Maʾmūn:** God does indeed name Abū Bakr the Prophet's companion — but God also names a disbeliever a companion of one whom He loves and who loves Him. He says: ⟪quran 18:37⟫ — "His companion (*ṣāḥibuhu*) said to him while disputing with him, 'Have you disbelieved in Him who created you from dust, then from a sperm-drop, then fashioned you as a man?'"

**Isḥāq:** But the companion of *that* verse was an unbeliever, whereas Abū Bakr was a believer.

**al-Maʾmūn:** Quite so. Yet the point stands: when God designates a disbeliever the *ṣāḥib* of a man pleasing to Him, He may equally designate a believer the *ṣāḥib* of His Prophet. The designation itself does not confer pre-eminence.

**Isḥāq:** Even so, my lord — this verse weighs heavily for Abū Bakr, for God says *"the second of the two,"* and the Messenger of God said to him in the cave, *"Do not grieve; indeed God is with us."*

**al-Maʾmūn:** Tell me, Isḥāq: what was the cause of Abū Bakr's grief? Was it pleasing to God, or contrary to His pleasure?

**Isḥāq:** It was on the Messenger's account — Abū Bakr feared lest some calamity befall the Prophet.

**al-Maʾmūn:** That is not yet an answer to my question. Was the grief itself something God loved, or something contrary to God's will?

**Isḥāq:** It was pleasing to God.

**al-Maʾmūn:** Then we are landed in this: that God sent us a Messenger who forbade men what is pleasing to God.

**Isḥāq:** God forbid — that cannot be.

**al-Maʾmūn:** Did you not just grant that Abū Bakr's grief was pleasing to God?

**Isḥāq:** I did.

**al-Maʾmūn:** And do you not see that in the verse the Prophet forbids that very grief — *"Do not grieve"* — and so, by your account, forbids what is pleasing to God?

**Isḥāq:** What an error I have made!

**al-Maʾmūn:** Then mark what God adds: *"So God sent down His tranquillity (*sakīna*) upon him."* Upon whom did that tranquillity descend — upon the Messenger of God, or upon Abū Bakr?

**Isḥāq:** Upon the Messenger of God.

**al-Maʾmūn:** And consider the verse of Ḥunayn: ⟪quran 9:26⟫ — "Then God sent down His tranquillity upon His Messenger and upon the believers." Whom does God mean by "the believers" in that verse?

**Isḥāq:** I do not know.

**al-Maʾmūn:** At Ḥunayn the whole army turned its back, and none remained with the Messenger of God save seven men of the Banū Hāshim. ʿAlī fought with his sword in defence of the Prophet; al-ʿAbbās held the bridle of his mule; the other five formed a guard about him so that no idolater might reach him. So they stood until God brought His Messenger relief. "The believers" upon whom God sent down His *sakīna* are therefore precisely ʿAlī and those of Banū Hāshim who held their ground. Now answer: were those who stood with the Messenger of God in that hour more excellent — or those, Abū Bakr and ʿUmar among them, who fled with the rest? — and upon those fleeing God found no station of trial worthy of His *sakīna*.

**Isḥāq:** No — the more excellent is he upon whom God sent down His tranquillity.

**al-Maʾmūn:** Tell me then: is the one who was in the cave with the Messenger of God more excellent — or the one who slept upon the Prophet's bed, hazarding his life for him in the very seat of death, by whose self-offering the Migration (*hijra*) was accomplished? When God commanded the Prophet to bid ʿAlī take his place upon the bed, the Prophet spoke to him, and ʿAlī wept. The Prophet asked, "Do you weep for fear of death, ʿAlī?" He answered, "By God, not for that — but for fear lest harm reach you. If I lie upon your bed, will your life be preserved?" The Prophet said, "Yes." Then ʿAlī rejoiced and said, "O Messenger of God, what cause then for concern? I shall sleep there in perfect contentment and gladly give my life to save yours." That night he wrapped himself in the Prophet's cloak and slept upon his bed in tranquillity. Soon the men of Quraysh gathered round, supposing him the Prophet, and resolved that one from each clan should strike a single sword-blow, lest the Banū Hāshim find a single quarter on which to exact blood-vengeance. ʿAlī, hearing every word of the conspiracy, seeing the swords made ready for his life, felt no fear and slept in peace, prepared to die.

In the morning, the Quraysh looked upon him and asked, "Where is Muḥammad?" He answered, "What do I know?" They said, "ʿAlī — it would seem you have been your own enemy this night." From that hour to the day the Messenger of God departed this world, ʿAlī was proven, in every event, more excellent than other men, never once inferior to any in any virtue.

Now, Isḥāq — narrate the *ḥadīth* of Ghadīr.

Isḥāq narrated the *ḥadīth* of Ghadīr.

**al-Maʾmūn:** Mark, then, what that *ḥadīth* lays upon Abū Bakr and ʿUmar concerning ʿAlī, that it does not lay upon ʿAlī concerning them: obedience to ʿAlī was made obligatory upon Abū Bakr and ʿUmar, while obedience to them was never made obligatory upon him.[^cite-7]

**Isḥāq:** It is said the occasion of that *ḥadīth* was Zayd ibn Ḥāritha, on account of some quarrel between him and ʿAlī in which he denied ʿAlī's authority, whereupon the Prophet spoke as he did.

**al-Maʾmūn:** And when did the Prophet utter that *ḥadīth*? Was it not at Ghadīr Khumm, on the return from the Farewell Pilgrimage?

**Isḥāq:** Yes — on that occasion.

**al-Maʾmūn:** And Zayd ibn Ḥāritha was martyred long before Ghadīr. How then can you set him as the occasion of a *ḥadīth* he did not live to hear? Tell me — if one of your own boys, perhaps five years old, were to stand and say to the people, *"Whosoever is my master, this child of mine is also his master,"* would you not find it absurd? Would you not be ashamed that your son should announce to the people something already known and undisputed?

**Isḥāq:** It would be unseemly indeed.

**al-Maʾmūn:** Then will you suffer for the Messenger of God what you would not suffer for your own son? Woe to you. — Have you heard the saying of the Messenger of God: *"O ʿAlī, you are to me as Hārūn was to Mūsā"*?

**Isḥāq:** I have heard it.

**al-Maʾmūn:** Could the Prophet have said this in jest?

**Isḥāq:** God forbid — never.

**al-Maʾmūn:** Then did he say something meaningless, or something incomprehensible?

**Isḥāq:** God forbid — neither.

**al-Maʾmūn:** Do you not know that Hārūn was the full brother of Mūsā?

**Isḥāq:** Yes.

**al-Maʾmūn:** And that Hārūn was a prophet, whereas ʿAlī was not a prophet?

**Isḥāq:** Yes.

**al-Maʾmūn:** Then since ʿAlī was neither the full brother nor a prophet of the Messenger of God — though Hārūn possessed both qualities relative to Mūsā — what was the substance of the Prophet's saying?

**Isḥāq:** The Prophet wished to console ʿAlī, because the hypocrites had said he was left in Medina on the road to Tabūk out of some grievance.

**al-Maʾmūn:** Then by your account, the Messenger of God consoled ʿAlī through a falsehood?

Isḥāq was greatly troubled and could not answer.

**al-Maʾmūn:** Isḥāq — the meaning of that comparison stands plain in the Book of God itself.

**Isḥāq:** What is it, my lord?

**al-Maʾmūn:** The words of Mūsā to Hārūn: ⟪quran 7:142⟫ — "And Mūsā said to his brother Hārūn: 'Take my place among my people; set them right, and do not follow the way of the corrupters.'"

**Isḥāq:** My lord — Mūsā set Hārūn as caliph over his people while he himself was yet alive, when he went, at God's command, to Mount Sinai. Just so, the Prophet, in his lifetime, set out to Tabūk.

**al-Maʾmūn:** Tell me — when Mūsā made Hārūn his caliph, did any of his companions or any of the Israelites go forth with him?

**Isḥāq:** None — he went up the mountain alone.

**al-Maʾmūn:** And was Hārūn caliph over a few individuals, or over the whole Umma?

**Isḥāq:** Over the whole Umma.

**al-Maʾmūn:** But when the Messenger of God set out for Tabūk, he left no one in Medina except the aged, the sick, the women and children — all the rest he took with him. How then can this be the parallel of Hārūn and Mūsā? The fact is, the parallel lies in another word of God altogether: ⟪quran 20:29⟫ — "And appoint for me a minister (*wazīr*) from my family, Hārūn my brother; strengthen through him my back, and let him share in my task, that we may glorify You much." Likewise the Messenger of God said: *"ʿAlī, you are to me as Hārūn was to Mūsā"* — *you* are my minister and my brother through whom God strengthens my back, whom He has made partner in my task, that we may both glorify Him abundantly.[^cite-8] Can any reading of the *ḥadīth* be offered that does not render the Prophet's word void, false, or absurd, save this one?[^cite-9]

---

The author of al-ʿIqd al-Farīd records that the debate continued long after, until the day was full and noon approached. At last Isḥāq said, "The Commander of the Faithful has made the truth clear to the seekers and has proved his claim with arguments no man can refute." Hearing this, al-Maʾmūn lifted his hands and prayed: *"O Lord, I have given them sincere counsel and have fulfilled the duty of enjoining the good. O Lord, I seek nearness to You through love and the *wilāya* (guardianship) of ʿAlī, and I am a follower of this religion."*

*(From Kitāb al-ʿIqd al-Farīd; the chronicler, Shihāb al-Dīn Aḥmad.)*

## Mawlānā Muḥammad al-Bāqir's Debate with the Ḥarūrī

The exchange recorded in this section dates to the lifetime of Imām Muḥammad al-Bāqir (ʿalayhi al-salām). A man remembered only by his affiliation — a Ḥarūrī, that is, one of the Khārijīs whose movement took its name from the village of Ḥarūrāʾ near Kūfa where they first broke from the Imām ʿAlī — came before him with a stated purpose. He had come, he announced, to argue that Abū Bakr was the rightful claimant to the caliphate. The Imām bid him speak openly and undertook to answer every question put to him.

The Ḥarūrī rested his case on four virtues attributed to Abū Bakr:

| 1 | He was the first to believe. |
| --- | --- |
| 2 | He was the Prophet's companion in the cave. |
| 3 | During the Prophet's own lifetime he led the prayer. |
| 4 | He is buried beside the Messenger of God. |

When the Imām had heard the list, his reply was measured. "O Ḥarūrī," he asked, "are you reciting his virtues, or his defects?" The visitor was startled. "Do you hold all four to be defects?" The Imām answered that he would prove precisely that — that what the Ḥarūrī had taken for proofs of merit were in truth the gravest of failings.

### On the claim of being first to believe

The Imām began with the first claim. Had not the Commander of the Faithful, Amīr al-Muʾminīn (ʿAlī), embraced Islam before Abū Bakr? The Ḥarūrī conceded the chronological fact at once but raised the familiar objection: ʿAlī was a minor at the time, and a child's faith does not count. The Imām's rejoinder was swift. Even granting that ʿAlī had been young, the Messenger of God was no child. If the Prophet himself had received ʿAlī's profession of faith and accepted it as valid Islam, on what later authority is that acceptance to be overturned?[^cite-10] The Ḥarūrī fell silent.

### On the companionship of the cave

The Imām turned next to the well-known episode of the cave. There was, he said, a hidden history behind that journey. When the Messenger of God was commanded to undertake the *hijra* (the emigration), he had need of a swift mount; Abū Bakr is reported to have sold him the camel at ten times its true price. Having struck the bargain, Abū Bakr then attached himself to the Prophet rather than being summoned by him. Through the long night the two hid together in the cave, and Abū Bakr wept in fear of the pursuing Quraysh — a fear so audible that it drew a divine admonition:

Observe, the Imām continued, that although two men were in the cave, when God speaks of sending down His *sakīna* (the divine tranquillity), the verse names only one recipient. The *sakīna* descended upon the Messenger alone, not upon his companion. The Imām pressed further: do you know the rule that governs upon whom God's *sakīna* descends?

God Himself declares that He sends down the *sakīna* only upon His Messenger or upon the believers. In the cave were the Messenger and Abū Bakr; the *sakīna* descended upon the Messenger alone. This withholding, the Imām argued, is proof that God excluded Abū Bakr from the circle of belief at that very hour. Had he stood within that circle, divine justice would have required the *sakīna* to alight upon him as well. And if God Himself had thus excluded him from the company of the believers, could He then have chosen him to succeed to the prophetic office?

### On leading the prayer

The Ḥarūrī advanced his third proof. Once, during the Prophet's terminal illness, ʿĀʾisha had bidden her father lead the maghrib prayer; Abū Bakr had stepped forward; and the Prophet, rising with great effort, came out, displaced him, and led the prayer himself.

The Imām posed a careful question. "Tell me, O Ḥarūrī — when the Prophet displaced Abū Bakr in the middle of the prayer, did that displacement vitiate the prayer or not?" The Ḥarūrī answered that it did not; the Prophet had simply taken over the imāmate, while Abū Bakr, he supposed, had then become an *imām* (prayer-leader) for the rows behind him, the Prophet acting as imām only over Abū Bakr. The Imām let the absurdity surface of itself. "Ḥarūrī, have you ever known a single prayer with two imāms? Your master ends up neither imām nor follower."

### On burial beside the Messenger of God

Last came the claim about the gravesite. The Imām invoked the Qurʾānic command on the privacy of dwellings:

  (image preserved at .. /images/011.png)

Enter not the houses of others save with their leave. By whose leave, then, was Abū Bakr laid to rest beside the grave of the Prophet? The Ḥarūrī replied that no leave was needed, since the chamber belonged to his daughter ʿĀʾisha. The Imām took up the calculation. How many wives had the Messenger of God left behind? Nine, the Ḥarūrī answered. And what portion does a widow inherit? An eighth, said the Ḥarūrī — divided, in this case, among the nine. The Imām then drew the line tight: even granting ʿĀʾisha her arithmetic share of the chamber, that share is too narrow to accommodate a body laid out at full length. By what right of inheritance, and with whose permission asked of the Prophet, does the first caliph occupy the two yards of earth that his successors have allotted him?

When the exchange ended, those present acknowledged what they had heard: against this Imām, no argument prevails.

⟪ar:‏إِنَّا نَحْنُ نَزَّلْنَا ٱلذِّكْرَ وَإِنَّا لَهُۥ لَحَفِظُونَ ‎⟫ ⟪ar:‏بِلِسَانٍ عَرَبِىٍّۢ مُّبِينٍۢ ‎⟫ ⟪ar:‏فَبِمَا نَقْضِهِم مِّيثَقَهُمْ لَعَنَّهُمْ وَجَعَلْنَا قُلُوبَهُمْ قَسِيَةًۭ ۖ يُحَرِّفُونَ ٱلْكَلِمَ عَن مَّوَاضِعِهِۦ ۙ وَنَسُوا۟ حَظًّۭا مِّمَّا ذُكِّرُوا۟ بِهِۦ ۚ وَلَا تَزَالُ تَطَّلِعُ عَلَىٰ خَآئِنَةٍۢ مِّنْهُمْ إِلَّا قَلِيلًۭا مِّنْهُمْ ۖ فَٱعْفُ عَنْهُمْ وَٱصْفَحْ ۚ إِنَّ ٱللَّهَ يُحِبُّ ٱلْمُحْسِنِينَ ‎⟫ ⟪ar:‏لِلَّذِينَ لَا يُؤْمِنُونَ بِٱلْآخِرَةِ مَثَلُ ٱلسَّوْءِ ۖ وَلِلَّهِ ٱلْمَثَلُ ٱلْأَعْلَىٰ ۚ وَهُوَ ٱلْعَزِيزُ ٱلْحَكِيمُ ‎⟫ ⟪ar:‏نَزَلَ بِهِ ٱلرُّوحُ ٱلْأَمِينُ ‎⟫ ⟪ar:‏وَكَذَلِكَ جَعَلْنَكُمْ أُمَّةًۭ وَسَطًۭا لِّتَكُونُوا۟ شُهَدَآءَ عَلَى ٱلنَّاسِ وَيَكُونَ ٱلرَّسُولُ عَلَيْكُمْ شَهِيدًۭا ۗ وَمَا جَعَلْنَا ٱلْقِبْلَةَ ٱلَّتِى كُنتَ عَلَيْهَآ إِلَّا لِنَعْلَمَ مَن يَتَّبِعُ ٱلرَّسُولَ مِمَّن يَنقَلِبُ عَلَىٰ عَقِبَيْهِ ۚ وَإِن كَانَتْ لَكَبِيرَةً إِلَّا عَلَى ٱلَّذِينَ هَدَى ٱللَّهُ ۗ وَمَا كَانَ ٱللَّهُ لِيُضِيعَ إِيمَنَكُمْ ۚ إِنَّ ٱللَّهَ بِٱلنَّاسِ لَرَءُوفٌۭ رَّحِيمٌۭ ‎⟫ ⟪ar:‏وَلَأُضِلَّنَّهُمْ وَلَأُمَنِّيَنَّهُمْ وَلَآمُرَنَّهُمْ فَلَيُبَتِّكُنَّ آذَانَ ٱلْأَنْعَمِ وَلَآمُرَنَّهُمْ فَلَيُغَيِّرُنَّ خَلْقَ ٱللَّهِ ۚ وَمَن يَتَّخِذِ ٱلشَّيْطَنَ وَلِيًّۭا مِّن دُونِ ٱللَّهِ فَقَدْ خَسِرَ خُسْرَانًۭا مُّبِينًۭا ‎⟫ ⟪ar:‏يَٓأَيُّهَا ٱلنَّاسُ ٱتَّقُوا۟ رَبَّكُمُ ٱلَّذِى خَلَقَكُم مِّن نَّفْسٍۢ وَحِدَةٍۢ وَخَلَقَ مِنْهَا زَوْجَهَا وَبَثَّ مِنْهُمَا رِجَالًۭا كَثِيرًۭا وَنِسَآءًۭ ۚ وَٱتَّقُوا۟ ٱللَّهَ ٱلَّذِى تَسَآءَلُونَ بِهِۦ وَٱلْأَرْحَامَ ۚ إِنَّ ٱللَّهَ كَانَ عَلَيْكُمْ رَقِيبًۭا ‎⟫ ⟪ar:‏ٱلنَّبِىُّ أَوْلَىٰ بِٱلْمُؤْمِنِينَ مِنْ أَنفُسِهِمْ ۖ وَأَزْوَجُهُۥٓ أُمَّهَتُهُمْ ۗ وَأُو۟لُوا۟ ٱلْأَرْحَامِ بَعْضُهُمْ أَوْلَىٰ بِبَعْضٍۢ فِى كِتَبِ ٱللَّهِ مِنَ ٱلْمُؤْمِنِينَ وَٱلْمُهَجِرِينَ إِلَّآ أَن تَفْعَلُوٓا۟ إِلَىٰٓ أَوْلِيَآئِكُم مَّعْرُوفًۭا ۚ كَانَ ذَلِكَ فِى ٱلْكِتَبِ مَسْطُورًۭا ‎⟫
