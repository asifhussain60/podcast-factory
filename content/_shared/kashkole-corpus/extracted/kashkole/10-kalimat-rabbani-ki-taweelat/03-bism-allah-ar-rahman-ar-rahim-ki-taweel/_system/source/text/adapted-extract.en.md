## The Excellence of Bismillāh al-Raḥmān al-Raḥīm

There is no nobler or more elevating formula with which to open a book or a work than ⟪ar:بسم اللہ الرحمن الرحیم⟫ (Bismillāh al-Raḥmān al-Raḥīm, "In the Name of God, the Most Gracious, the Most Merciful"). Even in the age of jāhiliyya the Arabs were accustomed to begin speech and undertaking with ⟪ar:’بسم اللہ‘⟫; yet the addition of ⟪ar:’الرحمن الرحیم‘⟫ bestowed upon the phrase a splendour without parallel. Other religious communities, too, employ comparable formulae: the Magi recited "Oka Nāma na Dard," and the people of India write or pronounce "Hari Om." But the eloquence and rhetorical perfection (balāgha wa-faṣāḥa) of the Qurʾānic ⟪ar:بسم اللہ الرحمن الرحیم⟫ is unmatched in any other tongue.

According to ⟪ar:ائمہ طاہرین صلی اللہ علیھم⟫ (the Pure Imams, peace be upon them), this phrase is the key to the Qurʾān: without it the Qurʾān cannot be opened and no sūra may stand whole. So great is the importance the Ahl al-Bayt have attached to it that they hold no sūra in prayer to be acceptable without ⟪ar:بسم اللہ الرحمن الرحیم⟫. They have ruled that in any prayer in which the recitation is uttered aloud, ⟪ar:بسم اللہ الرحمن الرحیم⟫ too must be pronounced aloud, and that here taqiyya (precautionary dissimulation) is not permitted under any circumstance.[^cite-1]

Within this formula three ⟪ar:اسم (اللہ، رحمن اور رحیم)⟫ — that is, the three Names Allāh, al-Raḥmān, and al-Raḥīm — are conjoined; and names themselves are of two kinds. The first are constituted of mortal letters; the second is ⟪ar:’حی‘⟫ (living) and ⟪ar:’ناطق‘⟫ (speaking, articulate). These living and speaking names are the ʿuqalāʾ wa-shurafāʾ — the Prophets, their Awṣiyāʾ (testamentary successors), and the Imams — who are themselves the Names of God, the standing signs of the Hereafter, the guides to tawḥīd (the affirmation of divine unity), and the way between the servant and his Lord. God says:

> ⟪quran 7:180⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَلِلَّهِ ٱلْأَسْمَآءُ ٱلْحُسْنَىٰ فَٱدْعُوهُ بِهَا ۖ وَذَرُوا۟ ٱلَّذِينَ يُلْحِدُونَ فِىٓ أَسْمَٓئِهِۦ ۚ سَيُجْزَوْنَ مَا كَانُوا۟ يَعْمَلُونَ ‎⟫
> *And (all) the Most Beautiful Names belong to Allah, so call on Him by them, and leave the company of those who belie or deny (or utter impious speech against) His Names. They will be requited for what they used to do.*
> And all the best names belong to Allah, so invoke Him by them, and leave those who blaspheme the names of Allah, lest they be recompensed for what they used to do.
> — **Quran 7:180 (inline widget)**

The verse commands the believer to attain the perfect and true unity of God by way of His Names and to call upon Him through them, while separating himself from the mulḥidūn (apostates): those who profess belief in the Messenger yet withhold themselves from those who carry his charge after him.

**Statement.** Those who confess the Prophet but reject his waṣiyya (testament), and who refuse the daʿwa (the summons of the Imams), are called mulḥidūn — atheists in the technical sense — for they have severed the very names by which God is to be invoked.

It is related that ʿAbd Allāh ibn ʿAbbās was once questioned about ⟪ar:بسم اللہ الرحمن الرحیم⟫ after ⟪ar:صلوٰۃ المغربین⟫ (the prayer of Maghrib). He took his seat and continued to expound its meanings until the time of Fajr — and even then he had not exhausted the discourse upon a single letter, ⟪ar:’ ب‘⟫, of ⟪ar:بسم اللہ الرحمن الرحیم⟫. Then he said: "O questioner, were you to ask me concerning ⟪ar:بسم اللہ⟫ entire, I would tell you only what pertains to the bāʾ — ⟪ar:’ ب‘⟫."

⟪ar:أمیر المؤمنین⟫ (the Commander of the Faithful, ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib) has said that the virtues of ⟪ar:بسم اللہ الرحمن الرحیم⟫ are so vast that, were they to be inscribed, seventy camels would be required to bear the volumes — and the camel is the most weight-bearing of beasts.

It is reported, too, that ⟪ar:’ بسم اللہ الرحمن الرحیم‘⟫ was inscribed upon the main gate of Pharaoh's palace. By virtue of this inscription Pharaoh remained protected so long as he stood within those walls, and the wrath of God did not descend upon him. But the moment he emerged from the palace, the river engulfed him.

⟪ar:بسم اللہ الرحمن الرحیم،⟫ is the summary of ⟪ar:سورۃ الفاتحۃ⟫ (Sūrat al-Fātiḥa); ⟪ar:سورۃ الفاتحۃ⟫ is the summary of the whole Qurʾān; and the Qurʾān is itself the gathering up of the divine essence of all the past ⟪ar:صحف و کتب⟫ (scrolls and books).

## The Meaning of Bismillāh al-Raḥmān al-Raḥīm

### Bismillāh — Beginning with the Name of God

This word stands as proof that the Creator brought the eternal beings forth from non-being into being, and the secret of that reality is concealed within the Name Allāh. Allāh is a name not assembled from any prior word: the noun ʿālim is derived from ʿilm, and ḥākim from ḥikma, but Allāh is unique in itself. The uncomposability of this Name signifies that God is alone in His Essence and has no partner; and that the Name Allāh combines with no antecedent word signifies that He has originated the spiritual beings ex nihilo — without any pre-existing matter (lā min shayʾ) — which is precisely what ⟪ar:الٰہیت⟫ (ulūhiyya, godhood) entails.[^cite-2]

### Raḥmān — The Disclosed

⟪ar:رحمٰن⟫ is built upon the root ⟪ar:رحم⟫ (raḥm, mercy) and indicates that God Most High has drawn us out of ruin and shown us compassion. ⟪ar:رحمٰن⟫ is a name peculiar to God, though its reality is common to all creatures: it cannot be applied to any other than Him, for it designates the One who provides for the worlds. God's ⟪ar:رحمانیت⟫ (raḥmāniyya, the quality of being al-Raḥmān) embraces obedient and disobedient alike, since He bestows sustenance upon both without distinction.

It is God as ⟪ar:رحمٰن⟫ who supplies us with food, drink, garments, and the whole order of outward sustenance. In another register, the Name refers to the nāṭiq (the Speaking Prophet), since the nāṭiq is the bearer of ⟪ar:تنزیل⟫ (tanzīl, sent-down revelation), which is the outward face of the Word.

### Al-Raḥīm — The Interior

⟪ar:رَحِیم⟫ is a name whose form is common but whose reality is reserved to specific creatures. It may be applied either to God or to a creature distinguished by extreme forgiveness. God's ⟪ar:رَحِیمِیَت⟫ (raḥīmiyya, the quality of being al-Raḥīm) is granted only to those who obey Him, not to the disobedient, for through the Prophets and their successors He has nourished us in another mode: He fulfilled our bodily wants as al-Raḥmān, and bestowed mercy upon our souls through inner, spiritual nurture as al-Raḥīm.

In a further sense, al-Raḥīm refers to the asās (Foundation), who interprets and unveils the inward meaning behind the outward speech of the nāṭiq. The name al-Raḥīm is itself derived from raḥma, and points to the person of the Messenger of God, whose teachings are a mercy to our souls, by which we cultivate them and raise them from the corporeal world into the spiritual.

|  | Raḥmān | al-Raḥīm |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Origin | Raḥm (Mercy) | Raḥm (Mercy) |
| Bearer | God | The Messenger of God |
| Indication | Outward (utterance) | Inward (meaning) |
| Limit (ḥadd) | Nāṭiq | Asās |

⟪ar:رحمٰن و رحیم⟫ are both attributes of God; yet ⟪ar:رحمٰن⟫ contains more than ⟪ar:رحیم⟫, for ⟪ar:رحمٰن⟫ is exclusive to God. The very existence of the attributes ⟪ar:رحمٰن و رحیم⟫ is itself a token of the existence of God. ⟪ar:رحمن⟫ denotes ⟪ar:باب باطن⟫ (the inner gate) and ⟪ar:رحیم⟫ denotes ⟪ar:باب ظاہر⟫ (the outer gate). Both are attributes of God, which in their reality belong to the essence of the Imam.

## The Order of Letters in Bismillāh

That the three letters of ⟪ar:بسم⟫ in ⟪ar:بسم اﷲ⟫ precede the four letters of the Name Allāh is evidence that the four principles of religion — ⟪ar:عقل کل، نفس کل، ناطق، اساس⟫ (al-ʿAql al-Kull, the Universal Intellect; al-Nafs al-Kull, the Universal Soul; the nāṭiq; and the asās) — can be recognised only by way of the three lower limits: the dāʿī (Summoner), the ḥujja (Proof), and the imam.

A like correspondence appears in the ritual of purification: three sunan (use of the tooth-stick, rinsing the mouth, drawing water into the nose) precede the four farāʾiḍ (washing of the face, washing of the hands, wiping of the head, wiping of the feet). The lesser indices stand before the principal acts.

| ب | س | م | ا | ل | ل | ہ |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Dāʿī | Ḥujja | Imam | Intellect | Soul | Nāṭiq | Asās |
| Tooth-stick | Rinsing | Nasal cleansing | Washing of the face | Washing of the hands | Wiping of the head | Wiping of the feet |

The seven letters of ⟪ar:بسم اﷲ⟫ are followed by the twelve letters of ⟪ar:الرحمٰن الرحیم⟫, signifying the twelve ḥujjas who come after the seven Imams, receive spiritual support (taʾyīd) from these Imams, and convey it to the faithful.

## What is Meant by the Name of God?

Bismillāh signifies literally "in the name of God." A question arises: since the word Allāh is itself the name of the Self-Subsisting Creator, what purpose is served by saying separately, "in the name of Allāh"? If both expressions designate the same Reality, "Bi-llāh" would have sufficed; or it would have been more fitting to open with another of God's ninety-nine Names.

The reality is that a name is that by which one is identified. Our own identity is by means of our name. In the same way, the proper name of God — that by which He may be known — is the blessed person of Muḥammad Muṣṭafā in his time, peace be upon him. After him, his waṣī ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib was the Name of God in his time; and in every age the Imam of the Age is the true Name of God. Confirming this truth, God says:

> ⟪quran 6:121⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَلَا تَأْكُلُوا۟ مِمَّا لَمْ يُذْكَرِ ٱسْمُ ٱللَّهِ عَلَيْهِ وَإِنَّهُۥ لَفِسْقٌۭ ۗ وَإِنَّ ٱلشَّيَطِينَ لَيُوحُونَ إِلَىٰٓ أَوْلِيَآئِهِمْ لِيُجَدِلُوكُمْ ۖ وَإِنْ أَطَعْتُمُوهُمْ إِنَّكُمْ لَمُشْرِكُونَ ‎⟫
> *Eat not (O believers) of that (meat) on which Allah's Name has not been pronounced (at the time of the slaughtering of the animal), for sure it is Fisq (a sin and disobedience of Allah).&nbsp;And certainly, the Shayatin (devils) do inspire their friends (from mankind) to dispute with you, and if you obey them [by making Al&shy;Maytatah (a dead animal) legal by eating it],  then you would indeed be Mushrikun (polytheists) [because they (devils and their friends) made lawful to you to eat that which Allah has made unlawful to eat and you obeyed them by considering it lawful to eat, and by doing so you worshipped them, and to worship others besides Allah is polytheism].*
> And do not eat of that over which the name of Allah has not been mentioned, for indeed it is a disobedience. And indeed the devils put it in the hearts of their friends, that they may argue with you. And if you obey them, you will become polytheists.
> — **Quran 6:121 (inline widget)**

In the exegesis of this verse, whatever the Prophet has forbidden cannot become permissible even if the Name of God be pronounced over it; for the greatest Name of God is the Messenger himself, and what he has prohibited cannot be rendered lawful by invoking any other outward divine name. By the same principle, the sons of the Messenger — the Imams who have succeeded him in his command — are themselves the Names of God: what they declare lawful is lawful, and what they declare unlawful is unlawful.[^cite-3]

The taʾwīl (esoteric exegesis) of slaughter is the giving of the oath of allegiance (taking the ʿahd), and the taʾwīl of food is the acquisition of knowledge. The verse therefore says, in its inward sense: do not partake of any knowledge from a person who has not himself taken the covenant of the Imam of the Age; and do not divulge to such a one your own knowledge, taʾwīl, or realised truths.

## The Four Gates of Bismillāh

**Four words: Bismillāh, Allāh, al-Raḥmān, al-Raḥīm.**

These four words denote the four principles of religion. Two are spiritual — ⟪ar:بسم اللہ⟫ standing for the spiritual persons — and two are corporeal — ⟪ar:الرحمن الرحیم⟫ standing for the corporeal persons. The spiritual limits (al-ḥudūd al-rūḥāniyya) are ⟪ar:عقل کل اور نفس کل⟫; the corporeal limits, present in every age, are the ⟪ar:ناطق⟫ and his ⟪ar:اساس⟫.

**Ten Essences: the essence of the cosmos.**

The phrase is composed of ten discrete letters, each a jawhar (essence). Five of these letters indicate the spiritual limits — ⟪ar:عقل، نفس، جد، فتح، خیال⟫ (ʿAql, Nafs, Jadd, Fatḥ, Khayāl) — and five indicate the corporeal limits — ⟪ar:ناطق، اساس، امام، داعی، حجت⟫ (nāṭiq, asās, imam, dāʿī, ḥujja).

**Nine articulated units: Bismillāh al-Raḥmān al-Raḥīm.**

In the cipher of ⟪ar:بسم اللہ الرحمن الرحیم⟫ are nine articulated units. Two of these denote the corporeal limits of ⟪ar:ناطق⟫ and ⟪ar:اساس⟫; the remaining seven denote the ⟪ar:صاحبان اسبوع⟫ — the Lords of the Heptad, that is, the seventh Imam who gathers the seven Imams of his cycle. These Imams occupy the seat of imamate because, with their close, a cycle is sealed and a new cycle inaugurated, as the pen rises from the page only to descend upon it again. This pattern of the daʿwa mirrors the spiritual world, which possesses two prior Intellects (⟪ar:عقل اول اور عقل ثانی⟫, al-ʿAql al-Awwal and al-ʿAql al-Thānī), with seven further intellects issuing forth from their summons.

**Nineteen letters in total: B-S-M-A-L-L-H-A-L-R-Ḥ-M-N-A-L-R-Ḥ-Y-M.**

These are the seven of the daʿwa (the Imams) together with their twelve ḥujjas. To these nineteen Beings God refers in ⟪ar:سورۃ المدثر⟫, where He says ⟪ar-quote:عَلَیْھَا تِسْعَۃ عَشَرٍ⟫ — "Over it are nineteen" (Q 74:30) — a verse the Ismāʿīlī tradition reads as the cosmic correspondence of the nineteen-letter Bismillāh.

## The Taʾwīl of the Four Words of Bismillāh

⟪ar:بسم اﷲ الرحمٰن الرحیم⟫ contains four words pointing to the ⟪ar:أسمآء الحسنی⟫ (al-Asmāʾ al-Ḥusnā, the Most Beautiful Names of God). The first two, ⟪ar:بسم⟫ and ⟪ar:اللہ⟫, refer to ⟪ar:عقل کل،نفس کل⟫ — equivalent to ⟪ar:لوح و قلم⟫ (Tablet and Pen), or ⟪ar:عقل اول اور مبنعث اول⟫ (al-ʿAql al-Awwal, the First Intellect, and al-Munbaʿith al-Awwal, the First Emanator from among al-Munbaʿithūn). The remaining two, ⟪ar:الرحمن اور الرحیم⟫, refer to the two corporeal limits — ⟪ar:ناطق واساس⟫ — the Prophet and his asās, or the Imam and the Bāb.

A kalima (a "word" in the technical sense) is the conjoining of meaningful letters into a meaningful composite. These four spiritual and corporeal limits are termed kalimāt because each is complete in its own essence and within its own sphere; each requires only the taʾyīd of the limit above it in order to act. They are more complete than those below them and stand in no need of them. There is here a subtle point: every lower limit, by comparison with the limit above it — the ḥadd ʿālī (the upper limit) — is a ⟪ar:مُحمِل⟫ (muḥmil, a mere bearer or vessel) and so without meaning in itself; yet by comparison with what stands below it — the ḥadd dānī (the lower limit) — that same limit is itself a bearer of meaning. Thus ⟪ar:نفس کل، عقل کل⟫ is "meaningless" before the Intellect, but pregnant with meaning before the ⟪ar:ناطق⟫. This principle holds throughout the spiritual and corporeal daʿwa.[^cite-4]

In ⟪ar:بسم اﷲ الرحمٰن الرحیم⟫, the word Allāh is the essence and the soul of the whole: were Allāh absent, ⟪ar:الرحمٰن الرحیم⟫ would be left orphaned of meaning and Bismillāh would mean nothing. In the same way, ⟪ar:عقل اول⟫ is the essence that stands opposite Allāh in every world, spiritual and corporeal, without which no existence of anything else would be possible. Were the ⟪ar:عقل اول توحید مبدع تعالی⟫ — that is, the First Intellect's act of declaring the unity of Mubdiʿ Taʿālā (the Originator, Most High) — not to generate meaning within itself, all beings would remain devoid of meaning.

| Universal Soul | Universal Intellect | Nāṭiq | Asās |

## The Essence of Nineteen in Bismillāh

⟪ar:بسم اﷲ الرحمٰن الرحیم⟫ consists of nineteen letters, divided into two parts: seven letters belong to ⟪ar:بسم اللہ⟫ and twelve to ⟪ar:الرحمن الرحیم⟫.

Beyond this, if one counts within ⟪ar:بسم اللہ الرحمن الرحیم⟫ the diacritical marks — zer, zabar, pesh, madd, tashdīd — together with the intervals between letters, the total again arrives at nineteen.

### The Seven Letters of Bismillāh

The seven letters of ⟪ar:بسم اللہ⟫ refer to the seven Intellects of the spiritual world and their circles. These are beings that are ⟪ar:مُجَرِّدُ الاَجسَام⟫ (mujarrad al-ajsām, abstracted from bodies); they are not corporeal. Within the world of bodies, these same seven points signify a sequence of personal mediations.

### The Twelve Letters of al-Raḥmān al-Raḥīm

These twelve letters refer to the twelve ḥujjas of each Imam in the twelve "islands" (jazāʾir) of the ⟪ar:عالم جسمانی⟫ (the corporeal world). In the spiritual world likewise, twelve nobles encircle each ⟪ar:عقل⟫, in whose orbit the remaining intellects are arrayed. In another reading, they refer to the twelve emendators bearing the letters of ⟪ar:آدم ابدائیۃ⟫ (Ādam Ibdāʾiyya, the primordial Adam of origination).

## The Means of the Panjtan in Bismillāh

Among the most exalted virtues of ⟪ar:بسم اﷲ الرحمن الرحیم⟫ is that it is the wasīla (the means of approach) of the Five Blessed Ones — the Panjtan — concerning whom God says:

The subtlety of this verse is that God commands the believer to *seek* the wasīla, not to fabricate it as the unrighteous do; the means has already been appointed by God Himself, and our task is only to find it. In this world, every effect is brought about through means — this is the very meaning of ⟪ar:دار الأسباب⟫ (Dār al-Asbāb, the Abode of Causes). To come into being one needs the means of parents; to acquire knowledge one needs the means of a teacher. In every affair of this universe, religious and worldly alike, God has appointed instrumental causes.[^cite-5]

So too with ⟪ar:بسم اللہ الرحمن الرحیم⟫: there are five luminous beings through whom one may reach God — the Panjtan, ⟪ar:محمد، علی، فاطمۃ، حسن، حسین⟫ (Muḥammad, ʿAlī, Fāṭima, Ḥasan, Ḥusayn). We require this wasīla because we lack the capacity to reach God by ourselves. To be conjoined to God we need beings who resemble us in the corporeal world and who are also conjoined to Him in the spiritual order — beings who are like us yet not exactly like us, and who are not exactly like Him. For this reason ⟪ar:بسم اللہ الرحمن الرحیم⟫ is enjoined at the beginning of every act: for without the wasīla of these beings, whatever we do is in some measure done for other than God.

Thus it becomes clear that ⟪ar:بسم اللہ الرحمٰن الرحیم⟫ is blessed at its very inception because within it stands the light of the Beings who illumine the Word. Wherever and whenever ⟪ar:بسم اللہ⟫ is recited, its luminous effect is felt. This formula was inscribed upon the door of Pharaoh's palace: as long as he remained within those walls, the punishment did not descend; the moment he stepped out, the river closed over him.

⟪ar:بسم اللہ الرحمن الرحیم⟫ is itself the light of the Panjtan, the Purified: the formula ⟪ar:بسم اللہ الرحمن الرحیم⟫ contains nineteen letters, and the names of the Panjtan together also amount to nineteen letters.

## Wiping the Face with the Right Hand while Reciting the Bismillāh

The phrase ⟪ar:بسم اﷲ الرحمن الرحیم⟫ is composed of ten individual jewels — its letters. Of these ten, five are ⟪ar:مکرر⟫ (mukarrar, repeating) and five are ⟪ar:غیر مکرر⟫ (ghayr mukarrar, non-repeating), each occurring only once.

These mukarrar limits are such that when one of them passes, another succeeds him as caliph and heir: thus they keep arriving in succession, like the fingers of the right hand and the fingers of the left. Set against them are the five non-repeating limits of the spiritual world — ⟪ar:خیال، فتح، جد، نفس، عقل⟫ (al-khayāl, al-fatḥ, al-jadd, al-nafs, al-ʿaql) — whose stations remain forever fixed and undergo no alteration with the turning of the ages.

Against them stand the ⟪ar:محتجبین⟫ (al-muḥtajibūn, the concealed ones), who are non-recurring; they do not descend into this corporeal world but continue to render their decisions from within the world of life.

## The Tradition of Reciting the Bismillāh over Water and Blowing upon It

It is narrated in a tradition that on the Day of Resurrection, Hell will roar like a lion and bellow like a dragon. Then ⟪ar:رسول اﷲ (ﷺ)⟫ will come bearing a cup of water; he will recite ⟪ar:بسم اﷲ الرحمن الرحیم⟫ over that water and breathe upon it, then pour it into Hell, whereupon its fire will at once be extinguished and its voice silenced. A summons will issue from God Most High: "O Fire, what has befallen you, that you have grown silent and meek?" And she will reply, "O Lord, I have no power against Your Name."

The taʾwīl (esoteric exegesis) of this hadith is that the household of falsehood — the enemies of the Ahl al-Bayt — are themselves the very pattern of Hell, for they burn their followers in the fire of doubt and uncertainty; and when a question is put to them for which they possess no answer, that "Hell" growls before its interlocutor, threatening him with the outward force of its tongue or with the threat of divine punishment, until he is silenced.[^cite-7]

⟪ar:رسول اﷲ (ﷺ)⟫ — or the Imam of the age — is the true ⟪ar:أسم اﷲ⟫ (Name of God), and the cup of water upon which the ⟪ar:بسم اﷲ⟫ has been recited is the believer who has taken the covenant of the Imam of his age. This mustajīb is the humble disciple of the Imam, like a cup of water set beside an ocean of knowledge. Yet when this mustajīb contends with that outward scholar — that "Hell" — and asks of him but a single plain question while pressing for evidence, the outward scholar is at once reduced to silence, unable to answer; his voice falters, and the whole force of his rhetoric is undone.

## The Exemplars of the Bismillāh: Adam and Sulaymān

We recite ⟪ar:بسم اللہ الرحمن الرحیم⟫ at the opening of every undertaking. In the same way, the initiator of every age is himself the ⟪ar:بسم اللہ الرحمن الرحیم⟫ of his cycle. ⟪ar:حضرت آدم (عبد اﷲ بن تنجوم) علیہ السلام، دور ستر⟫ stands as the initiator of the Qurʾanic dispensation, and for this reason ⟪ar:آدم علیہ السلام⟫ is regarded as the very embodiment of ⟪ar:بسم اللہ الرحمن الرحیم⟫. The textual proof of this correspondence lies in the letters themselves. Just as ⟪ar:بسم اللہ الرحمن الرحیم⟫ contains both recurring and non-recurring letters in equal count of five, so too do the names ⟪ar:سلیمان اور عبد اﷲ⟫ contain recurring and non-recurring letters of ⟪ar:بسم اﷲ الرحمن الرحیم⟫ in precisely the same enumeration.

## The Bismillāh and the Walāya of ʿAlī

⟪ar:حضرت علی ابن ابی طالب بسم اﷲ الرحمن الرحیم⟫ — speaking of the universe — declares that all the treasures of Islamic wisdom and knowledge are summarized in the revealed scriptures, ⟪ar:صحف ابراھیم، توریت، زبور، انجیل⟫; the summary of these heavenly books is gathered into the Holy Qurʾan; the summary of the Qurʾan into ⟪ar:سورۃ الفاتحۃ⟫; the summary of ⟪ar:سورۃ الفاتحۃ⟫ into ⟪ar:بسم اﷲ الرحمن الرحیم⟫; the summary of ⟪ar:بسم اﷲ الرحمن الرحیم⟫ into ⟪ar:بسم اﷲ⟫; the summary of ⟪ar:بسم اﷲ⟫ into the ⟪ar:بسم⟫'s opening letter (bāʾ); and the summary of that bāʾ into its single point — and that point, he affirms, is none other than himself: ⟪ar:’ أنا نقطۃ التحت البآء‘⟫ ("I am the point beneath the Bāʾ").[^cite-9]

This means that the entire Qurʾan is encompassed within ⟪ar:سورۃ الفاتحۃ⟫. Within these seven verses Allāh has compressed the whole scripture. ⟪ar:اَلحَمْدُ لِلّٰہِ رَبِّ اَلعَالَمِیْنَ⟫ states the principle (⟪ar:مبدا⟫) of faith; ⟪ar:الرَّحْمٰنِ الرَّحِیْم⟫ articulates the divine attributes; ⟪ar:مَالِکِ یَوْمِ الدِّیْن⟫ articulates belief in the Resurrection; ⟪ar:اِیَّاکَ نَعبُدُوَاِیَّاکَ نَسْتَعِیْنَ⟫ confesses ⟪ar:عبودیت⟫ (servanthood, worship); ⟪ar:اِھدِنَا الصِّرَاطَ المُسْتَقِیْمَ⟫ is the petition for guidance upon the straight way; and ⟪ar:صِرَاطَ الَّذِیْنَ اَنْعَمْتَ عَلَیْھِمْ⟫ together with ⟪ar:غَیْرِ الْمَغْضُوْبِ عَلَیْھِمْ وَلاَ الضَّالِّیْنَ⟫ secures the principles of divine justice and accountability.

In commentary upon ⟪ar:سورۃ الفاتحۃ⟫ it is said: ⟪ar-quote:لاَ صَلٰوۃَ اِلَّا بِفَاتِحَۃِ الکِتَابْ⟫ — "There is no prayer save by al-Fātiḥa." The further compression into ⟪ar:بسم اللہ⟫ stands in equipoise with ⟪ar:سورۃ الفاتحۃ⟫: the Fātiḥa's seven verses correspond to the seven letters of the Kāf-form (kalima).

### The Walāya of ʿAlī

⟪ar:بسم اللہ الرحمن الرحیم⟫ remains readable as ⟪ar:بسم اللہ⟫ even if the dotted letters (⟪ar:ب ن ی⟫) are stripped away. But if the bāʾ (⟪ar:ب⟫) — which is the leading letter — is itself removed, the word collapses; it can no longer be read; it becomes void.

Every undertaking begins with ⟪ar:بسم اللہ الرحمن الرحیم⟫; without that opening, the act loses its weight. By the same logic, when ʿAlī calls himself the point beneath the bāʾ, he is teaching that every act of the Sharīʿa is rendered hollow without his walāya — just as the bāʾ of ⟪ar:بسم اللہ الرحمن الرحیم⟫ loses its meaning the moment its point is taken from it. And just as every action begins under the letter bāʾ of ⟪ar:بسم اللہ الرحمن الرحیم⟫ — and the meaning of that bāʾ subsists in its point — so the beginning of religion itself rests upon the walāya of ⟪ar:امیر المؤمنین⟫, in whose absence every other deed is forfeit.

To put it otherwise: ⟪ar:حجاب علی ابن ابی طالب⟫ — to deny ʿAlī's walāya is to suspend the whole Qurʾan. One cannot enter the meanings of the Qurʾan until one has cultivated the recognition of him, for he is the inward essence of all the Qurʾan's senses — just as the whole of the Qurʾan is gathered into him.

Our entire doctrine rests upon the walāya of Mawlānā ʿAlī. Without that walāya, no other devotion will avail us on the Day of Judgment among the ⟪ar:أعمال تکلیفیۃ⟫ (the obligatory works).

### The Black Stone

By the same pattern, God Most High made the whole earth into the essence of His sacred sites; then He gathered the summary of all sacred places into ⟪ar:مکۃ المعظمۃ⟫; the summary of ⟪ar:مکۃ المعظمۃ⟫ He placed in ⟪ar:مسجد الحرام⟫; the summary of ⟪ar:مسجد الحرام⟫ He placed in ⟪ar:کعبۃ اللہ⟫; the summary of ⟪ar:کعبۃ اللہ⟫ He placed in ⟪ar:رکن اسعد⟫; and the summary of ⟪ar:رکن اسعد⟫ He gathered into ⟪ar:حجر اسود⟫, which is the counterpart of ⟪ar:نقطۃ التحت البآء⟫. ⟪ar:کعبۃ اللہ⟫ is therefore the lock, and ⟪ar:حجر اسود⟫ the key, of every divine act and grace.

### The Believer

Another reading of the same statement: God Most High created man as ⟪ar:اشرف المخلوقات⟫ (the noblest of creatures) among the ⟪ar:موالید ثلاثۃ⟫ (the three kingdoms). Then He drew the summary of mankind into ⟪ar:أمت آدم⟫; the summary of ⟪ar:أمت آدم⟫ into ⟪ar:أمت نوح⟫; that of ⟪ar:أمت نوح⟫ into ⟪ar:أمت أبراھیمؑ⟫; that into ⟪ar:أمت موسیٰ⟫; that into ⟪ar:أمت عیسیٰ⟫; that into ⟪ar:أمت مسلمۃ⟫; and the essence of the Muslims is the ⟪ar:مومنین (سلیمانی)⟫ — the one sect of seventy-three that attains deliverance, the counterpart in the human realm to ⟪ar:نقطۃ التحت البآء⟫, for salvation is possible only by becoming a ⟪ar:مؤمن⟫; otherwise the whole purpose of creation is undone. ⟪ar:ب⟫ ⟪ar:بسم اللہ الرحمن الرحیم⟫

## The Bāʾ of the Bismillāh as Lāsiq

The whole Qurʾan is built from 28 ⟪ar:حروف معجم⟫ (lettered characters). Its summary, ⟪ar:سورۃ الفاتحۃ⟫, uses 21 of these. The opening formulae (al-fawātiḥ) of the Qurʾan use only 14. And ⟪ar:بسم اللہ الرحمن الرحیم⟫ uses 19. The distinction between the muqaṭṭaʿāt (the disjoined opening letters) and ⟪ar:بسم اللہ الرحمن الرحیم⟫ is that the phrase ⟪ar:بسم اللہ الرحمن الرحیم⟫ itself does not enter the body of the sūras, whereas the muqaṭṭaʿāt enter the sūras of the Qurʾan as their preface on account of their special grace.

Yet although the bāʾ belongs to the dark letters, it is the chief of them and the one most adjacent to the luminous letters.[^cite-10]

The station of ⟪ar:باءِ بسم اللہ⟫ is that of a ⟪ar:لاسق⟫ — a binder, a clasp — set between the ⟪ar:عالم حیات⟫ (the world of life) and the ⟪ar:فلک محیط⟫ (the encompassing sphere): one of its ends is fused with the spiritual world, the other with the corporeal. The bāʾ stands as a door between the ⟪ar:نورانی ثقیلۃ⟫ (the luminous "heavy" letters) and the ⟪ar:ظلمانی خفیفۃ⟫ (the dark "light" letters).

## Ascent is Possible Only through ʿAlī

When ⟪ar:امیر المؤمنین حضرت علی ابن ابی طالب علیہ السلام⟫ called himself the point beneath the bāʾ of the universe, he was indicating that he is, in truth, the summary of all spirituality. The letter bāʾ corresponds to ⟪ar:محمد مصطفی صلی اللّٰہ علیہ وسلم⟫, and the point of that bāʾ to ⟪ar:علی ابن ابی طالب⟫. God could not guide mankind through the angels: for the guidance of human beings it was necessary that there come among them one of their own, who would eat as they eat, drink as they drink, marry as they marry, and die as they die.

In the spiritual world, those letters that hesitated in answering the summons (daʿwa) of al-ʿAql al-Awwal (the First Intellect) were marked with points. The progression of a dotted letter is that it sheds its point and becomes pointless. The dotted letters number fifteen; the pointless letters number thirteen.

The bāʾ is the loftiest of the ⟪ar:ظلمانیۃ خفیفۃ⟫ (the dark "light" letters), adjacent to the ⟪ar:نورانی ثقیلۃ⟫ (the luminous "heavy" letters); but it could not yet be enrolled among them on account of its point. Once it sheds its point, it advances and joins the ranks of the luminous letters — that is, the bāʾ ascends from the dark letters into the luminous. Thus ⟪ar:حجاب علی⟫ declared: ⟪ar:أنا نقطۃ التحت الباء⟫ — "I am he who shall bring the assembly of my Imams out of the world of darkness into the world of light." In other words: if ascent is possible at all, it is possible only through ʿAlī.

When the bāʾ has thus advanced, the count of ⟪ar:نورانی و ظلمانی⟫ letters will at last be equal: fourteen and fourteen, the scales perfectly balanced.

## Bāʾ: The Imam's Upper and Lower Veils

The opening formula ⟪ar:بسم اللہ الرحمن الرحیم⟫ contains ten gems—ten letters—of which only one belongs to the order of ⟪ar:حرف ظلمانی⟫ (a "letter of darkness"), namely ⟪ar:ب⟫. The remaining nine are luminous. Yet Bāʾ, though counted among the dark letters, presides over them as their chief: its head is bound to the foot of the illuminated letters, so that what stands lowest among the luminous already reaches down to what stands highest among the dark.

In every era the Imam occupies the rank of ⟪ar:حجاب اعلی⟫ (the upper veil), corresponding to ⟪ar:اسم اللہ⟫ (the Name of God). To utter ⟪ar:بسم اللہ⟫ is therefore, in one register, to invoke the Imam of the Age. The ⟪ar:ب⟫ is the wellspring of tawassul (the act of seeking mediation): it is the Imam's ⟪ar:حجاب ادنی⟫ (lower veil), through which the seeker is drawn upward toward his upper veil.[^cite-11]

This ⟪ar:ادنی حجاب⟫ is the mode by which the Imam manifests and presents himself to the community. Were the ⟪ar:اعلی حجاب⟫ to appear undisguised, it could not be borne by sight. The ⟪ar:ادنی حجاب أنوار تائید⟫ stands adjacent to that higher rank, and it is to this lower veil that the formula ⟪ar:علیہ السلام⟫ is properly addressed. It is in the ⟪ar:صورت لحمی و دمی⟫ (the form of flesh and blood) that this ⟪ar:حجاب ادنی⟫ is beheld by the faithful.

### Mufaḍḍal's Plea to Imam Jaʿfar

⟪ar:جناب مفضل بن عمرو، امام جعفر الصادق⟫, who held the rank of ⟪ar:باب ظاہر⟫ (the outer gate), once addressed the Imam in supplication:

> "O my Lord! O my Lord! O my Lord! Fulfil thy promise to me, and do not overwhelm me."

The sense of the plea is plain: "Thou hast promised to deal with me through Thy ⟪ar:حجب دانیۃ⟫—through the ⟪ar:حجاب الادنی⟫—and not through Thyself in the rank of ⟪ar:حجاب اعلی⟫. Wert Thou to disclose Thyself through the ⟪ar:اعلی حجاب⟫, my very sight would all but perish." The implication is unmistakable: even one as elevated as the ⟪ar:باب الابواب⟫ (the Gate of Gates) lacks the capacity to behold the Imam directly in his upper veil.[^cite-12]

The rank-correspondence here is that ⟪ar:امام جعفر الصادق علیہ السلام⟫ stood in relation to ⟪ar:باب باطن مولانا زید بن جعفری⟫, the inner gate of his summons.

Just as one ascends from the ⟪ar:حروف ظلمانی خفیفۃ⟫ (the lighter letters of darkness) into the letters of pure light, so too is the rank of the ⟪ar:حجاب ادنی⟫ of the Imam: it has only just emerged from the ḥudūd of the daʿwa (the summons) and crossed the threshold into the veil itself.

⟪ar:مولانا علی علیہ السلام⟫ declared: "I am the dot beneath the Bāʾ. The letter Bāʾ is ⟪ar:محمد مصطفی صلی اﷲ علیہ و آلہ وسلم⟫—the very essence of the essence—and the dot beneath it is ⟪ar:علی ابن ابی طالب علیہ السلام⟫." In other words, ʿAlī testifies: "I am the ⟪ar:عین عظیمۃ⟫ (the great essence) who has manifested in the form of the ⟪ar:ادنی حجاب⟫, while the Messenger of God is that same ⟪ar:عین عظیمۃ⟫ in the rank of the ⟪ar:اعلی حجاب⟫."

### ʿAlī's Submission to the Prophet

ʿAlī's saying that he is the dot beneath the ⟪ar:ب⟫ is, in truth, a declaration of his submission to the Prophet. The lower veil of ʿAlī thereby teaches that, however his rank may be elevated within the world of generation, he remains everywhere beneath the Prophet.

ʿAlī himself says, ⟪ar-quote:أنا عَبدٌ مِن عَبِیدِ مُحَمَّد⟫—"I am but one slave among the slaves of Muḥammad"—while the Messenger of God in turn declares of him, "ʿAlī is from me," and again, ⟪ar-quote:اَناَ وِ عَلِی مِن نُورٍ وَاحِد⟫—"ʿAlī and I are of a single light."[^cite-13] ʿAlī's meaning is that, although he is master of the ⟪ar:دعوت باطنۃ⟫ (the inner summons) within the ⟪ar:عالم کون و فساد⟫ (the world of generation and corruption), he yet observes every principle set down by the Prophet, and it is precisely by this obedience that he has been graced.

The letters of the ⟪ar:ظلمانیۃ خفیفۃ⟫ class—those that articulate the rank of ⟪ar:نطقا⟫ within the ⟪ar:حجب دانیۃ⟫ and the ⟪ar:نواسیت⟫—are dark in name yet luminous in kind. For this reason the Messenger of God said, ⟪ar-quote:أنا وعلی من نور واحد⟫—"ʿAlī and I are of one light"—using precisely the word ⟪ar:نور⟫ (light). For though the Prophet's outer form belongs to the ⟪ar:ظلمانی خفیفۃ⟫, this rank carries with it its own confirmations from on high—⟪ar:عالی علیہ⟫—and on that account the veil itself can rightly name itself a light.

None of the letters of the ⟪ar:ظلمانی ثقیلۃ⟫ (the heavy letters of darkness) appear within ⟪ar:سورۃ الفاتحۃ⟫, since God has made it, together with ⟪ar:بسم اللہ الرحمن الرحیم⟫, a remedy for ailments both bodily and spiritual. These two stand apart from every other sūra in this regard: in every other sūra of the Qurʾān, the letters of the ⟪ar:ظلمانی ثقیلۃ⟫ class do appear.
