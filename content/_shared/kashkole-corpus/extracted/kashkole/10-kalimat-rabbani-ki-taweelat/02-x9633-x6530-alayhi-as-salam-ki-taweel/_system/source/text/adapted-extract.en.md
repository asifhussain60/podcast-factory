## The Love of ʿAlī Is a Virtue That No Evil Deed Can Mar

A hadith of the Holy Prophet (peace and blessings of God be upon him) declares: "The love and walāya (guardianship) of ʿAlī is a virtue that no evil can harm." Read outwardly, the saying appears to drain religious practice of all moral seriousness. If the lover of ʿAlī remains righteous before God after every sin and so escapes punishment, then good works lose their purpose and divine justice itself is compromised. Such a doctrine could only belong to unbelievers, not to the faithful. Clarification is sought, and gratefully received.

In the idiom of taʾwīl (esoteric exegesis), "good deeds" stand for the Imams of truth, since they alone summon humanity to Paradise. By the same logic, "evil deeds" stand for the imams of misguidance, since they lead their followers, step by step, toward the Fire.

What the Prophet conveys, then, is this: whoever loves Mawlānā ʿAlī (peace be upon him) in such a way that he recognizes his rank in the chain of caliphate, holds firm to belief in his Imamate, and obeys him — for the claim of love without obedience is empty and meaningless — such a person has been severed from the grip of the false imam and shielded from every evil of misguidance. Thereafter, no utterance and no insinuation from a false imam will turn him aside from the walāya and obedience of the true Imam.[^cite-1]

This is the sound meaning of the Prophet's hadith, and not the false doctrine that the ghulāt (extremists) advanced when they elevated Mawlānā Amīr al-Muʾminīn ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib (peace be upon him) to the rank of God. On the one hand, they had been warned and consumed by the fire of misguidance; on the other, they threw open the door to every sin for themselves. They were, in truth, ʿAlī's worst enemies. By that corrupt belief, they invalidated the sharīʿa (sacred law) of the Holy Prophet (peace and blessings be upon him). And to invalidate the sharīʿa is to invalidate his prophethood and his rank as Prophet — to topple oneself from the very heights one claimed to honor. Once prophethood and sharīʿa have been rejected through such a doctrine, how can the love of ʿAlī endure in the heart?

The Messenger of God (peace and blessings be upon him) said: "O ʿAlī, your friend is my friend and your enemy is my enemy." Those who turn their backs on the sharīʿa and reject its rulings have become hostile to ʿAlī (peace be upon him); and ʿAlī's enemy is the enemy of the Messenger (peace be upon him) and the enemy of God. Such a person cannot be a lover of ʿAlī.

(Risāla 20, *Qawl wa-Jawāb bi-l-Taʾwīl*, Sayyidnā ʿAlī b. Sayyidinā Sulaymān, may God be pleased with him)

## I Am the First, I Am the Last

⟪ar:### شرعی استدلال:

امیر المومنین علی ابن ابی طالب علیہ السلام⟫

### Scriptural Argument: Amīr al-Muʾminīn ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib (peace be upon him)

The saying ⟪ar:’أنا الاول و أنا الآخر‘⟫ ("I am the First and I am the Last") is in no sense a claim to divinity; it is a word spoken from the station of ⟪ar:’عبدیت‘⟫ — ʿubūdiyya (servanthood) before God. ʿAlī's words are, for the believers, a source of guidance and healing, just as God Most High has said:

> ⟪quran 41:44⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَلَوْ جَعَلْنَهُ قُرْآنًا أَعْجَمِيًّۭا لَّقَالُوا۟ لَوْلَا فُصِّلَتْ آيَتُهُۥٓ ۖ آ۬عْجَمِىٌّۭ وَعَرَبِىٌّۭ ۗ قُلْ هُوَ لِلَّذِينَ آمَنُوا۟ هُدًۭى وَشِفَآءٌۭ ۖ وَٱلَّذِينَ لَا يُؤْمِنُونَ فِىٓ آذَانِهِمْ وَقْرٌۭ وَهُوَ عَلَيْهِمْ عَمًى ۚ أُو۟لَٓئِكَ يُنَادَوْنَ مِن مَّكَانٍۭ بَعِيدٍۢ ‎⟫
> *And if We had sent this as a Quran in a foreign language other than Arabic, they would have said: "Why are not its Verses explained in detail (in our language)? What! (A Book) not in Arabic and (the Messenger) an Arab?" Say: "It is for those who believe, a guide and a healing. And as for those who disbelieve, there is heaviness (deafness) in their ears, and it (the Quran) is blindness for them. They are those who are called from a place far away (so they neither listen nor understand).*
> And if We had made it a Qur'an in a foreign tongue, they would have said: Why are not its verses expounded? What a foreign scripture and an Arabic Messenger? Say: It is a guidance and a healing for those who believe; and those who do not believe are deaf in their ears, and it is blindness for them; these are they who are called from a place far away.
> — **Quran 41:44 (inline widget)**

The "First" to which he points is ⟪ar:کلمۃ اللہ (لا الہ الا اللہ)⟫ — the Word of God, "There is no god but God": the light that was the very first to be brought into being, from which every subsequent creation issued. It is the Word before which nothing existed, and the "End" after which no other end will come. Its light is undying — first in shining and last in shining. As God Most High has said:

> ⟪quran 39:69⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَأَشْرَقَتِ ٱلْأَرْضُ بِنُورِ رَبِّهَا وَوُضِعَ ٱلْكِتَبُ وَجِآءَ بِٱلنَّبِيِّنَ وَٱلشُّهَدَآءِ وَقُضِىَ بَيْنَهُم بِٱلْحَقِّ وَهُمْ لَا يُظْلَمُونَ ‎⟫
> *And the earth will shine with the light of its Lord (Allah, when He will come to judge among men) and the Book will be placed (open) and the Prophets and the witnesses will be brought forward, and it will be judged between them with truth, and they will not be wronged.*
> And the earth will shine with the light of its Lord, and the Book will be set up, and prophets and witnesses will be brought, and it will be judged between them with justice, and they will not be wronged.
> — **Quran 39:69 (inline widget)**

It is certain that without uttering the word of sincere monotheism — ⟪ar:’لا الہ الا اللہ محمد رسول اللہ‘⟫ — the felicity of the hereafter cannot be attained, for this Word has become the foundation upon which Islam rests. It is the first formula of Islam, with no precedent before it, and on this point none of the sects of Islam disagrees. Now there are two manners of reciting this Word: one is to recite without understanding and without inward conviction; the other is to recite with knowledge and belief. The Messenger of God (peace and blessings be upon him) said: ⟪ar-quote:أمرت ان اقاتل الناس حتی یقولوا لا الہ الا الہ محمد رسول اللہ۔ فأذا قالوھا عصموا منی دمائھم و اموالھم الابحقھا و حسابھم علی اللہ⟫ — "I have been commanded by God to fight people until they pronounce the Kalima. When they utter ⟪ar:لا الہ الا اللہ محمد رسول اللہ⟫, they have placed their lives and their wealth out of my reach — except for what is rightfully owed, which must be discharged in any case — and their reckoning is with God."

By this hadith the Messenger of God (peace and blessings be upon him) guaranteed only the protection of life and property for those who recite the Word without inward belief — not their salvation in the hereafter. But on another occasion, in speaking of the greatness of the same Word, he said: ⟪ar-quote:من قال لا الہ الا اللہ مخلصا دخل الجنۃ⟫ — "Whoever utters ⟪ar:کلمہ لا الہ الا اللہ⟫ in sincerity enters Paradise." When he was asked, "What is this sincerity?", he answered: ⟪ar-quote:معرفت حدودھا و ادا حقوقھا⟫ — "Recognition of its spiritual and physical limits, and the discharge of their rightful claims."

So noble and exalted is this Word that it stands in an intimate relation to the Waṣī (the Designated Heir, peace be upon him): correct knowledge of ⟪ar:توحید⟫ (tawḥīd, the affirmation of divine unity), ⟪ar:تنزیہ⟫ (tanzīh, the negation of likeness), and ⟪ar:تجرید⟫ (tajrīd, the stripping away of attributes) cannot be acquired except from the Gate of the Prophet's knowledge. For it is through this Gate alone that one discerns the ⟪ar:’فاروق اعظم‘⟫ — the great criterion — between the friends of God, who are the ⟪ar:’حدود اللہ‘⟫ (the limits of God), and the ⟪ar:’اولیا شیطان‘⟫ (the friends of Satan). Whoever attempts to grasp the truth of sincerity from anyone else, abandoning the Gate of the Prophet's knowledge, will go astray and find no guidance; he will fall into shirk (associationism) and remain in doubt. He will be cut off from both ends of this Word and unbound from the causes of salvation. Amīr al-Muʾminīn is "the First and the Last" in precisely this sense: the very beginning of the knowledge of the Word of Sincerity is from his own self and through his understanding, and the ultimate salvation is bound to his very essence. ⟪ar:أنا الاول و أنا الآخر⟫ — that is, the knowledge of the Word of Sincerity, upon which Islam and faith depend, is to be attained from me, and once attained it can only be consummated through love of me and through my walāya. The origin and the end of the believer's soul, as it were, are bound to me. We say simply: this is ʿAlī — ⟪ar:’ھوالاول ھو الآخر‘⟫.[^cite-2]

There is a well-known hadith of the Prophet (peace be upon him) to the effect that when Ādam (peace be upon him) was in Paradise, he saw inscribed upon the Throne: ⟪ar:’لا الہ الا اللہ محمد رسول اللہ ایدتہ بعلی‘⟫. By this hadith, then, they are "the First" in the sight of God in rank and station, and "the Last" in respect of the close of prophethood and waṣiyya (legateeship).

It is well established that the close of prophethood and the close of the line of waṣīs were spoken of from the very beginning. Ādam himself, and the messengers who came after him, went on telling of the ⟪ar:’پیغمبر آخرالزمان‘⟫ — the Prophet of the End of Days — and of the ⟪ar:’نبأ عظیم‘⟫, the great tidings. So they are first and last in respect of outward manifestation.

It is also narrated in a hadith of the Prophet that God created souls six thousand years before bodies. Since souls precede bodies in creation, they are likewise higher than bodies in rank. And in the matter of prophethood and waṣiyya, no two beings stand higher than these two. He therefore said ⟪ar:أنا الاول و أنا الآخر⟫ in respect of nobility.

### Rational Demonstration from Human Stature

Man is "the First" in respect of his self, the soul, and "the Last" in respect of his outward appearance, the body. The explanation is this. The human ⟪ar:نطفہ⟫ (drop of seed), once it has become ⟪ar:علقہ⟫ (clinging clot), resembles a plant in degree; for at that moment nothing more than ⟪ar:قوت نامیۃ⟫ (the vegetative power of growth) is present in it. As it develops, it acquires ⟪ar:قوت حس⟫ (the sensitive power) and so comes to resemble an animal, producing the substrate that feels pleasure and pain. Then, after birth, man becomes rational and is set apart from the animal by reasoning and intellect — and these graduated stages are the means by which his origins are made intelligible, since they cannot be grasped by any other route. They show that the plant precedes the animal, the animal form precedes the human form, and the human form is the perfecting form: the most complete of the prior forms, and so capable of receiving the influx of reasoning and intellect from the spiritual powers. Yet these powers themselves have no part in ⟪ar:مزاجات طبیعیۃ⟫ (natural temperaments). Hence man receives his portion of intellect and intelligence from the world of the soul; and by his union with that spiritual world, through these powers, he is ⟪ar:’اول‘⟫ ("First"). And in his own person, as ⟪ar:قامت الفیۃ⟫ (a stature in the form of *alif*, the full upright frame), he is the locus capable of receiving the imprints of this lower world: he is ⟪ar:’آخر‘⟫ ("Last"). Man, that is, is first spiritually and last bodily.

Thus man is like a circle whose two ends meet, encompassing the heavens and the earth and all that lies between. Through his knowledge he rules over all of them; he carries his judgment over all of them. He puts the animals of every kind into his service and bends them to his purposes. And if such rational demonstration applies to every human being — that he is at once the First and the Last — how much more must it apply to the Absolute Man and the True Man, in whom prophethood and the line of waṣīs reach their culmination. He is, in the truest sense, the First and the Last.

### Rational Demonstration from the Science of Number

All of God's creation — the world of intellect and the world of nature — depends upon reckoning, and reckoning belongs in truth to all of it, as God Most High has said:

> ⟪quran 10:5⟫
> ⟪ar:‏هُوَ ٱلَّذِى جَعَلَ ٱلشَّمْسَ ضِيَآءًۭ وَٱلْقَمَرَ نُورًۭا وَقَدَّرَهُۥ مَنَازِلَ لِتَعْلَمُوا۟ عَدَدَ ٱلسِّنِينَ وَٱلْحِسَابَ ۚ مَا خَلَقَ ٱللَّهُ ذَلِكَ إِلَّا بِٱلْحَقِّ ۚ يُفَصِّلُ ٱلْآيَتِ لِقَوْمٍۢ يَعْلَمُونَ ‎⟫
> *It is He Who made the sun a shining thing and the moon as a light and measured out its (their) stages, that you might know the number of years and the reckoning. Allah did not create this but in truth. He explains the Ayat (proofs, evidences, verses, lessons, signs, revelations, etc.) in detail for people who have knowledge.*
> It is He who made the sun a light, and the moon a light, and determined it in phases, that you may know the number of years and the reckoning. God has created all this with deliberation. He distinguishes His verses for a people who understand.
> — **Quran 10:5 (inline widget)**

There are four orders of number:

| 1 | 10 | 100 | 1000 |

These four orders point to the ⟪ar:حدود اربعہ⟫ — the four limits — and to the hierarchy that orders the world of origination and the world of permanence. The science of arithmetic begins with "one" and ends with "thousand"; every number beyond is merely a repetition of these. So calculation has two extremes — a beginning and an end. The first extreme is "one," from which numbers proceed and through which all the others are unfolded. The second extreme is "thousand," beyond which calculation does not extend. No number other than one is worthy to be called ⟪ar:’علت مطلق‘⟫ (absolute cause), for it is the first of all. By the same token, no number other than a thousand is worthy to be called ⟪ar:’معلول مطلق‘⟫ (absolute effect), for it is the last of all. Every number that lies between these two is at once cause and effect within itself, and the relation between such causes and effects is, in a sense, the relation of father and son: two stands to one as son and to three as father; three stands to two as father and to four as son; and so on. But "one" is the father of all and has no father, and "thousand" is the son of all and has no son. This condition indicates that the further thousand is the limit and terminus of the world; "one" is the First before which there is no first, and "thousand" is the Last after which there is no further end. And both these extremes are in the hand of ʿAlī (peace be upon him) — just as we said of the Word of Sincerity, that both its ends are ʿAlī (peace be upon him). Whoever has been deprived of the witness of this Word in this world has been deprived of the reward attached to it.

### "The First in Thought, the Last in Deed"

There is a celebrated saying of wisdom — ⟪ar-quote:أول الفکر و آخر العمل⟫ — "first in thought and intention, last in execution and outward appearance." What is meant is this: when a man sets out to build a house, he first conceives it in his mind; then he draws it out on paper; then, stage by stage, he completes it according to plan. What was first in thought arrives last in manifestation. Between the initial thought and the finished work, many forms and accidents come and go. So it is with ⟪ar:امیر المومنین علی علیہ السلام⟫: such is his rank before God that, in the divine knowledge, the form of ʿAlī (whom God Most High brought into being) was the first to emerge in the world of manifestation. He is, in this sense too, ⟪ar:امیر المومنین اول الفکر و آخر العمل⟫. And so what he says — ⟪ar:’أنا الاول و أنا الآخر‘⟫ — is, in every sense, true.

The dāʿīs who followed treated love of the Imams as inseparable from obedience to them — never as an antinomian licence.

## I Am the Manifest, I Am the Hidden

God is at once outward (al-Ẓāhir) and inward (al-Bāṭin) — and yet these terms do not name attributes of the divine Essence itself, nor are they among the Beautiful Names rightly applied to ⟪ar:باری البرایا رب العالمین⟫ (the Originator of creatures, Lord of the worlds). They belong, rather, to His creation: to that which He has originated by His power and which subsists wholly within His dominion. The prelude to this praise of ours is the recognition that outwardness and inwardness pertain not to ⟪ar:باری سبحانہ⟫ (the Originator, glory be to Him) but to what He has fashioned. Consider the heavens: plainly God's creation, they are manifest in their motions and effects yet veiled in their essences from sight. The functions of our souls likewise disclose themselves through action while the substance of the soul remains hidden. Our movements testify to a power within the body that produces them, yet what that power is, and where it resides, escapes us. The soul, then, is at once outward and inward. And once we discover such properties in what God has made, we are compelled to separate Him from the qualities of His creatures; for true tawḥīd (the affirmation of divine unity) is to purify the Originator from every quality, corporeal or spiritual, that belongs to what He has originated.

⟪ar:أمیر المؤمنین علی ابن ابی طالب⟫ (Amīr al-Muʾminīn, ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib, the Commander of the Faithful) is himself among God's creation, and since outwardness and inwardness are properties of creatures, his utterance ⟪ar:’أنا الظاھر و أنا الباطن‘⟫ ("I am the outward and I am the inward") cannot be read as a claim to lordship. He is articulating a particular state and station of his being. He is manifest among the people of the hereafter who recognise his rank and apprehend his true station; he is hidden, like a stranger, among the people of this world who know him not. Amīr al-Muʾminīn described this same condition of the believer when he said: ⟪ar-quote:المؤمن مجھول فی الدنیا و معروف فی الملاء الأعلی⟫ — "the believer is unknown in this world and known in the highest assembly." The believer too is inward to the heedless and outward to those of the supernal realm.

The people of truth recognise that the station of ʿAlī (peace be upon him) bears the same relation as the ⟪ar:’لوح‘⟫ (Lawḥ, the Preserved Tablet) bears to the ⟪ar:’قلم‘⟫ (Qalam, the Pen).[^cite-3] In this world only those particulars and forms come to appearance which the Pen has already inscribed upon the Tablet; what is not inscribed there finds no manifestation here. By the same principle, those of ʿAlī's followers whose names are not written upon this Tablet have no portion of salvation in the hereafter. The station of ʿAlī was apprehended only by the people of truth — to whom he was manifest — while it remained hidden from the eyes of his adversaries, for they neither knew nor recognised him. The Prophet (peace be upon him) said: ⟪ar-quote:طوبیٰ لمن رأنی ؛ و طوبی لمن رای من رأنی ؛ و طوبی لمن رای من رأی من رأنی⟫ — "Blessed is the one who has seen me; blessed is the one who has seen the one who has seen me; and blessed is the one who has seen the one who has seen the one who has seen me." Were the Messenger's intent here merely sight through the bodily eye, then friend and enemy, Muslim and idolater alike, would be encompassed by this blessing. The disbelievers and polytheists of Mecca — ⟪ar:ابو لھب⟫ (Abū Lahab) and ⟪ar:ابو جہل⟫ (Abū Jahl) foremost among them — saw the Prophet plainly, from his childhood through his prophetic mission and beyond. Are we, then, to suppose that ⟪ar:ابولھب⟫ is more fortunate than the sincere believers who came after and knew the Prophet only through his name and remembrance? Plainly the seeing intended is ⟪ar:’رویت نفسانی‘⟫ (ruʾyat nafsānī, vision of the soul — that is, inward seeing). This is what God means when He says:

> ⟪quran 7:198⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَإِن تَدْعُوهُمْ إِلَى ٱلْهُدَىٰ لَا يَسْمَعُوا۟ ۖ وَتَرَىٰهُمْ يَنظُرُونَ إِلَيْكَ وَهُمْ لَا يُبْصِرُونَ ‎⟫
> *And if you call them to guidance, they hear not; and you see them looking at you, yet they see not.*
> — **Quran 7:198 (inline widget)**

The three verbs ⟪ar:’ترا‘⟫ (tarā), ⟪ar:’نظر‘⟫ (naẓar), and ⟪ar:’بصر‘⟫ (baṣar) all render in English as "to see," yet their meanings diverge. The verse establishes that the seeing in question is the seeing of the soul — vision through the eye of inward insight — namely, the recognition of the rank of the Messenger of God. Such true recognition was attained only by ʿAlī, as the Messenger himself bore witness: ⟪ar-quote:ما عرفنی حق المعرفۃ الاَّ اللہ و أنت یا علی⟫ — "None has known me with the rightful knowledge of me save God and you, O ʿAlī." ʿAlī is the custodian of the Messenger's secrets and the bearer of his trust. Accordingly ⟪ar:’طوبی لمن رأنی‘⟫ refers to ʿAlī himself; ⟪ar:طوبی لمن رای من رأنی⟫ refers to the imam, who is the waṣī (legatee) and God's deputy upon the earth; and the third category encompasses all the believers who acquire the sciences of sharīʿa (the revealed law) and ṭarīqa (the inner path), and who, recognising the ⟪ar:ائمہ علیھم السلام⟫ (the imams, peace be upon them) of their time as their guides, follow them. So God says:

> ⟪quran 13:7⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَيَقُولُ ٱلَّذِينَ كَفَرُوا۟ لَوْلَآ أُنزِلَ عَلَيْهِ آيَةٌۭ مِّن رَّبِّهِۦٓ ۗ إِنَّمَآ أَنتَ مُنذِرٌۭ ۖ وَلِكُلِّ قَوْمٍ هَادٍ ‎⟫
> *And those who disbelieve say: "Why has no sign been sent down upon him from his Lord?" You are only a warner, and for every people there is a guide.*
> — **Quran 13:7 (inline widget)**

This establishes that the prophet and the imam are outward in respect of the body, which every enemy and friend has beheld, and inward in respect of station and majesty, which none but the few have recognised.

### Second argument

Among the ignorant, the scholar holds no value. The wise man among them is as a sane man among children and the deranged. Those who possess understanding sit by him in reverence; those without it pay him no heed. He is, therefore, ⟪ar:’ظاہر‘⟫ (ẓāhir, manifest) among them in his outward form, and ⟪ar:’باطن‘⟫ (bāṭin, hidden) among them in his grace and his knowledge.

### Third argument

God said to His Messenger (peace be upon him):

> ⟪quran 93:7⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَوَجَدَكَ ضَآلًّۭا فَهَدَىٰ ‎⟫
> *And He found you unaware, and guided.*
> — **Quran 93:7 (inline widget)**

The exegetes have offered several readings of ⟪ar:’ضال‘⟫ (ḍāll) according to their opinions and conjectures: some have rendered ⟪ar:ضال⟫ as one gone astray, others as misguided, still others as one yearning in love of God. The infallible imams, however, hold that ⟪ar:’ضال‘⟫ here carries the same sense as the maxim of the wise — ⟪ar-quote:الحکمۃ ضالۃ المؤمن⟫ — "wisdom is the lost property of the believer." The ignorant Arabs to whom the Prophet was sent were as beasts among whom a precious essence lies unrecognised: not one of them perceived his worth. He was, in this sense, "lost" (⟪ar:ضال⟫) among them. Thus God commanded those ignorant Arabs to grasp his worth. For the Messenger of God was himself guided from the very origin of his being, as he declared: ⟪ar-quote:کنت نبی کان آدم بین المآء و التین⟫ — "I was already a prophet while Adam was yet between water and clay." The pronoun in ⟪ar:’فھدی‘⟫ ("and guided") does not, then, refer to the Messenger himself but to the people: it was the people whom God Almighty guided to the greatness of His Messenger. So the distinction between outward and inward becomes clear: his greatness was hidden among them, and God brought it to manifestation.

## I Am the First, I Am the Last, I Am the Manifest, I Am the Hidden

The reference to ⟪ar:عین عظیمہ⟫ (ʿAyn ʿAẓīma, the Great Source) belongs to this lofty state and rank — a station reached by none other. In the four-hundredth kawr (cycle), ⟪ar:جثہ ابدائیہ⟫ (jaththa ibdāʾiyya, the originated body) emerged from Sarandīb, after which the first round of the kawr commenced and disclosure (kashf) began. The jaththa ibdāʾiyya holds the supreme point — ⟪ar:’عین عظیمۃ‘⟫. Throughout this entire era no one of comparable rank has been born save the ⟪ar:قائم القیامۃ (قائم دور)⟫ (Qāʾim al-Qiyāma, the Lord of the Resurrection of the cycle) who is yet to come. Since the Qāʾim al-Qiyāma has not yet been raised up, the point of ʿAyn ʿAẓīma remains the highest. Her conjunction with ⟪ar:عقل عاشر (جثہ ابدائیہ)⟫ (the Tenth Intellect, the originated body) was complete: from the Tenth Intellect she received her substance directly, without intermediary — a substance that is never to be exhausted. From the appearance of jaththa ibdāʾiyya to the manifestation of the point of ʿAyn al-ʿAẓīma — leaving aside the coming Qāʾim al-Qiyāma — no support has been disclosed to any soul as it was disclosed to ʿAyn al-ʿAẓīma. The support of the Qāʾim shall be greater still, for ʿAyn ʿAẓīma is a part of that which flows from al-Qāʾim, and the whole of ʿAyn al-ʿAẓīma stands as her part in comparison to him. In the same way, all the imams are partial in relation to ʿAyn al-ʿAẓīma, while ʿAyn al-ʿAẓīma is the universal in relation to them; and the station of the Qāʾim al-Qiyāma is itself a part of the station of jaththa ibdāʾiyya. On account of this state many miracles were revealed from behind the veil of ʿAyn ʿAẓīma. Beholding these miracles, many of the Shia called him ⟪ar:’الہ‘⟫ ("god"). In ⟪ar:شموس الزاہرۃ⟫ (Shumūs al-Zāhira, the Resplendent Suns), our master ⟪ar:سیدنا ابراھیم بن حسین الحامدی⟫ (Sayyidnā Ibrāhīm ibn al-Ḥusayn al-Ḥāmidī) has compiled the great sermons of the Lord of the universe — sermons that have not reached the masses and remain only within our daʿwa (the summons) books.[^cite-4] In one such sermon Mawlānā ʿAlī said: ⟪ar-quote:اَناَ اَلاَوَّل وَاَناَ الآخر اَناَ الباَطِن وَاَناَ الظَّاہِر⟫ — "I am the First and I am the Last; I am the Inward and I am the Outward."

### I am the First and I am the Last

Those who utter such a saying are in truth the great ones, speaking through one of their lofty veils. ʿAlī is the first of all the cycles — first in the procession of every era that has unfolded across the rounds; and in this same respect he is also the last, for none above him in rank shall come until the Resurrection. The meaning of his statement is that the first human born of God bore the name ⟪ar:’علی‘⟫ (ʿAlī, the Exalted), and the last to appear shall also bear the name ⟪ar:’علی‘⟫. By the same principle, all the eternal parts of the temples at the inception of the akwār (cycles) and the adwār (rounds) shall bear the name ⟪ar:’علی‘⟫; and all the Qāʾims of the cycles, the Qāʾims of the eras, and the Qāʾims of the rounds at their close — every one of them — ⟪ar:’علی‘⟫.

### I am the Manifest and I am the Hidden

His statement that he is inward signifies that his temple is constituted from the totality of all the ⟪ar:صورات لطیفۃ⟫ (ṣūrāt laṭīfa, the subtle forms) and ⟪ar:نفوس جوہریۃ⟫ (nufūs jawhariyya, the essential souls) of the cycles. The temple may not be seen, but its effects are seen: hence it is inward. At the bāb al-abwāb (the Gate of Gates) of an imam an assembly of all the forms is gathered. ʿAyn ʿAẓīma made ⟪ar:’میم‘⟫ (Mīm) her chapter, so all the forms gathered around Mīm, and from them ʿAyn ʿAẓīma received their collected substance.

And his saying that he is manifest signifies that ⟪ar:معجزات الہیۃ⟫ (muʿjizāt ilāhiyya, divine miracles) have been made manifest through him, and that the deeds of all the saints (awliyāʾ) — works performed outwardly together with the sciences of taʾwīl (esoteric exegesis) and ⟪ar:حکمت الہیۃ⟫ (ḥikma ilāhiyya, divine wisdom) — have been linked, each in turn, to its proper imam, and have finally been gathered into him.

He said: "I caused the death of Adam, Noah, Abraham, Moses, and Jesus, and I raised up prophets and sent messengers." The veil of ʿAyn al-ʿAẓīma who pronounced these words is in truth one of the forms which he encompasses. In another sense, this is a declaration spanning nine thousand years — three thousand years of the prophetic ages and six thousand years of the seventy — which ʿAyn ʿAẓīma had dispatched. All these figures are persons of the highest rank.

In yet another sense, all the imams are part of ʿAlī, while ʿAlī himself is the universal of them. All the imams who have appeared, or who shall yet appear, are ʿAlī in his partial mode (inward); and when ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib himself appears or shall appear, they shall manifest in their universal mode (outward). From the beginning to the end, ʿAlī is ʿAlī — or rather, ʿAlī is the seal pressed upon all the cycles and the rounds.

## I Am the Knower of All Things

Imam ʿAlī (peace be upon him) said: ⟪ar-quote:أنا بکل شئی علیم⟫ — "I am the Knower of all things." To common perception no creature has the right to a claim so vast. In reply ⟪ar:سیدنا مؤید⟫ (Sayyidnā al-Muʾayyad) advances several arguments.

His first argument: ⟪ar:’علم‘⟫ (ʿilm, knowledge) is the quality that grants one being precedence over others. All human beings are physically equal — whether small or great, lettered or unlettered. But when a person becomes acquainted with knowledge and skill, recites the Qurʾan, masters tawḥīd, learns to write, and acquires a craft useful for his religion and his life, he inevitably attains an honour and superiority over all other men like him, because he has come to know these things. Knowledge, that is, was a grace and a marvel bestowed upon him.

Now, although in itself impossible, suppose hypothetically that we conceive of God as an Essence: then by our supposition He must be a perfect Essence without parallel. If He is already perfect, it serves nothing to ascribe knowledge to Him, for He stands in no need of any virtue we might add to enlarge His majesty. And if we suppose His Essence to be imperfect, requiring by degrees the attribute of being All-Knowing, All-Powerful, and the like, then we have introduced into the divine Essence the gravest of defects. For on this supposition His Essence would attain perfection only by knowing things outside Himself — things bearing no relation to His Essence and which, indeed, would not yet exist. Knowledge, then, is not a property reserved exclusively to God.

Secondly: our souls are reckoned most noble when we acquire the knowledge of tawḥīd, for the knowledge of unity is the highest and the finest of sciences. How then could we say of God that He became Knower by acquiring knowledge of His creatures, or by knowing their states and deeds? Creatures are inferior and base; it is no honour for the most exalted to acquire the most lowly knowledge — on the contrary, it is a diminution.

Thirdly: the very definition of knowledge is that the true form of the known comes to be in the soul of the knower. This admits of two modes. Either the knower acquires knowledge of the known by acquisition, or the form of the known subsists in the knower alongside the knower's own subsistence. If we suppose that the imprint of the known is impressed upon the divine Essence by acquisition, then the divine Essence stands as defective until it is completed by acquired knowledge — an attribute that cannot be applied to God, for it implies He was first defective and then knowing, or first ignorant and then learned. And if it be supposed instead that the form of the known abides in His knowledge from eternity, then this world and all that it contains becomes co-eternal with God — which we know to be impossible, for the world is contingent (it did not always exist; it was originated).

Fourthly: knowledge encompasses the trifling occupations and crafts of the world — agriculture, dyeing, tailoring. Such humble crafts are included in the verse ⟪ar-quote:وھو بکل شئی علیم⟫ ("And He is Knower of all things"). Are we then to say that God is conversant with dyeing, with sewing, with tilling? If we say yes, no glory accrues to Him thereby; if we say no, we have invalidated the verse.

Fifthly: among the sciences and crafts there are some whose ignorance is preferable to their knowledge — deceit, treachery, fraud, and the practitioners thereof, universally condemned; and the knowledge of how to brew wine or to cut a pocket. Does God know these too? Does such knowledge add to His glory? Do human beings ever esteem and applaud these things? God has indeed declared Himself ignorant of many such matters. He says:

> ⟪quran 10:18⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَيَعْبُدُونَ مِن دُونِ ٱللَّهِ مَا لَا يَضُرُّهُمْ وَلَا يَنفَعُهُمْ وَيَقُولُونَ هَٓؤُلَآءِ شُفَعَٓؤُنَا عِندَ ٱللَّهِ ۚ قُلْ أَتُنَبِّـُٔونَ ٱللَّهَ بِمَا لَا يَعْلَمُ فِى ٱلسَّمَوَتِ وَلَا فِى ٱلْأَرْضِ ۚ سُبْحَنَهُۥ وَتَعَلَىٰ عَمَّا يُشْرِكُونَ ‎⟫
> *And they worship besides God things that neither harm them nor profit them, and they say: "These are our intercessors with God." Say: "Do you inform God of that which He knows not, in the heavens or on the earth?" Glorified and exalted is He above what they associate with Him.*

From all these evidences it is clear that the verse ⟪ar:ھو بکل شئی علیم⟫ refers not to God but to His chosen servants. The Prophet (peace be upon him) praised the Qurʾan, saying: ⟪ar-quote:ان القرآن ظاھره انيق و باطنہ عميق لا تفني عجائبہ و لا تنقضي غرائبہ و لا تکشفُ الظلمات الا بہ⟫ — "Its outward is exquisite and its inward profound; its wonders shall never wear out, nor its marvels come to an end; nor are the darknesses dispelled save through it."

⟪ar-quote:أنا بکل شئی علیم⟫ — as if the earth, were speech granted to her, were to say: "I am the knower of all that subsists upon me, in valley and mountain, in mineral and animal. I am intimate with every instrument of generation and corruption." Would such a saying from the earth be false? Now Amīr al-Muʾminīn is himself the earth of all that is generated for the hereafter; none knows its districts and chapters as he does. Nothing there is hidden from him; no secret is sealed against him. For this reason he said: ⟪ar-quote:واللہ لو کشف الغطاء ما ازددت یقینا⟫ — "By God, were the veil to be lifted, my certainty would not increase."[^cite-5] The claim of Amīr al-Muʾminīn, ⟪ar-quote:انا بکل شئی علیم⟫, refers to this very knowledge — a claim in which there is not the slightest pretension to lordship.

## I Am the One Who Raised Up Her Heaven and Spread Out Her Earth

When we consider the faculties and powers of the human being, we find that God has granted him certain capacities of this lower frame, and has also bestowed upon him certain mighty powers that resemble those of God Himself. By his psychic and intellectual powers — themselves only an effect of ⟪ar:نفس کلیہ⟫ (nafs kulliyya, the Universal Soul) — man brings forth springs of water from the earth and pours them across its surface; he makes his fields lush and verdant; he draws water from beneath the ground and revives man and beast; from this water grain grows and sustenance is provided. In our day he draws oil from the earth and travels by land, by river, and through the air. If these are the powers of man, what is to be said of the powers of Amīr al-Muʾminīn, which stand to each particular as the universal stands to its parts?[^cite-6]

We may now corroborate this rational argument with a Sharia argument drawn from the words of the Prophet (peace be upon him). The Prophet said: "God moved the Pen upon the Tablet, and inscribed thereon all that is created and made manifest in the heavens and the earth and what lies between them." The "Tablet" of which the Lawgiver spoke is what the philosopher calls ⟪ar:’نفس کلیۃ‘⟫ (nafs kulliyya). The inscription became fixed upon the Tablet and shone forth, appearing to the people of the world. The correspondence of the Pen to the Tablet is thereby plainly drawn.

The second Sharia argument: the place of birth of the Prophet and the imam, for the hereafter, stands in the same relation as the Tablet and the Pen for this worldly birth. As what the Pen has written upon the Tablet alone appears in this world — neither more nor less — so in the hereafter only those forms shall come to manifestation who are bound to the Prophet and have taken refuge in the domain of ʿAlī. None besides them shall appear. And just as the corporeal heavens, earth, and mountains were already engraved upon the Tablet, so the heavens, the earth, and the mountains of the hereafter — embodied in the rank of ⟪ar:’وصی‘⟫ (waṣī, the legatee) — were likewise inscribed.

His heavens — ⟪ar:’ائمۃ الھداہ‘⟫ (aʾimmat al-hudāt, the rightly-guiding imams) — are higher and more elevated than all human beings through the strength of divine support. From them the human soul receives the sustenance of knowledge, just as bodies receive their sustenance from the visible heaven. The ⟪ar:ائمہ ھداۃ⟫ are, on the other hand, the stars and constellations of the spiritual world, who illuminate the human soul with their light and brilliance and guide it through the labyrinths of error, just as the stars of the outer heavens guide our bodies through the darkness.

So too are these heavens — the earth of the imams — wedded to their counterparts: their ḥujjat (proofs), their nuqabāʾ (deputies), and their duʿāt (the Summoners), who, by drawing knowledge from them, yield every species of brilliant flower and beneficial fruit, as the earth absorbs and assimilates the celestial substance and produces all manner of flowers and fruits. The mountains of this spiritual earth are the steadfast members of their daʿwa whose convictions never waver, just as the outward mountains stand fast in their places. And finally, the tillage of this spiritual earth is the believer who answers the summons — those who, like the outward plants and trees, live by degrees of differing knowledge and works.

## Every Stone and Clod Bears Witness to My Prophethood

Every body — whether stone, clod, person, or thing — is bounded by the six dimensions of extension; nothing escapes them, and nothing is exempted from them. Within these six dimensions, every body is enclosed. And the six dimensions, taken as a sign, point to the sixth Nāṭiq — that is, to the time of the Prophet Muḥammad (peace be upon him). The very structure of things, which neither is nor can be outside the six limits, is itself a silent witness to his prophethood. If religion is mapped onto the body of the world, it likewise unfolds across six periods, six Nāṭiqs.

## Whoever Has Three of His Offspring Die, Reckon Them His Shield from the Fire

The Prophet (peace and blessings be upon him) is reported to have said: "Whoever has three of his offspring die and bears it patiently, awaiting his reward — those departed will shield him from the Fire." When asked whether the father of two such children would receive the same benefit, he answered, "Yes, why not?"

Taken in its plain sense, the report yields a strange result. If interpretation stops at the surface, then any father among the seventy-three sects of the community whose children die would be saved from the Fire. Yet another ḥadīth declares that of those seventy-three sects, only one is saved and the remaining seventy-two are in the Fire. The Prophet said: "My community will divide into seventy-three sects; one alone is saved, and seventy-two are people of the Fire." If the words are read literally, the obedient and the disobedient would be levelled and would receive an equal reward — an unacceptable conclusion.

In truth, "children" here designates religious progeny, not bodily offspring. In taʾwīl bāṭinī, "death" denotes the believer's passage from a lower rank to a higher one — the elevation of one's own degree, as Syedna Qāḍī al-Nuʿmān has explained in his Taʾwīl al-Daʿāʾim. "Fire," in turn, signifies the doubts and uncertainties that kindle in the heart, burn faith to ashes, and consume the soul.

The sense of the ḥadīth, then, is this: when the dāʿī al-muṭlaq (the absolute Summoner) — who is in reality the spiritual father of the believers — elevates one of his children (the believers) to the rank of mukāsir (the breaker of objections), then promotes the second (formerly maʾdhūn, the authorized agent) to the rank of maʾdhūn muṭlaq, and finally raises the third (formerly the assistant) to the rank of dāʿī (Summoner) in his stead, the Summoner himself is set free.

In his place he installs these three men to answer the objections of the enemies — objections that are, at root, a tangle of doubts — and so retires from the active conduct of his office. The task of refuting the partisans of falsehood is no longer the burden of the absolute Summoner. The responsibility of quenching and cooling the fire that the enemies of religion kindle in believers' hearts now passes to the dāʿī, the maʾdhūn, and the mukāsir.

The partisans of falsehood are called *al-Nār* (the Fire) because they constitute the hell of this world, the summons that draws souls toward the real Hell. By the same token, the daʿwa (the summons) of guidance is the paradise-in-potential, the summons that draws souls toward the real Paradise. Should the dāʿī fail to raise three men to these three ranks, and succeed only with two — making one a dāʿī and the other a mukāsir — even these two are sufficient to confront the people of falsehood. The two between them can return a fitting reply to the feeble objections their adversaries raise, and the absolute Summoner need have no anxiety.

(Risāla 20, Qawl wa-l-Jawāb bi-l-Taʾwīl) Syedna ʿAlī ibn Sayyidunā Sulaymān (may God be pleased with him)

## The Believer Eats with One Intestine, the Disbeliever with Seven Intestines

The Prophet (peace and blessings be upon him) is reported to have said: "The believer eats with one intestine, and the disbeliever eats with seven intestines." How could this be? The viscera of all human beings are alike in number — no more, no less. If the saying refers to the quantity of food, we plainly observe that many believers eat more than many disbelievers.

Here too, the apparent meaning is not what is intended. Rather, the believer who accepts the summons of the true Imam (ʿalayhi al-salām) and enters the precinct of faith under covenant and pledge receives the meanings and the sublime senses of the wisdom-words of the Holy Qurʾān; and although they are few, he is fully satisfied by them. He has no need to wander through other sciences in search of nourishment. His heart and mind, grasping the true meaning, attain repose, and his hunger is stilled.

The disbeliever and the hypocrite, by contrast, when they wish to satisfy their spiritual hunger by understanding the Qurʾān, turn to the self-appointed scholars and to the caliphs of their own choosing. These furnish them with false interpretations that do not nourish but only inflame their appetite; and so they remain perpetually hungry. This is the disbeliever's eating — much, without satisfaction — and the believer's eating: little, with contentment.

Note: Translated by Maulvi Amiruddin.

Outwardly too, the believer eats less, and the disbeliever and the hypocrite more. That is to say, the believer keeps within the bounds of the pure Sharīʿa, while the disbeliever fills his stomach with whatever comes to hand, drawing no line between the lawful and the unlawful. Second, the believer eats at the appointed time; he abstains from food and drink outside it, so that his stomach remains clean, and in truth he eats in order to live and to worship. The disbeliever and the hypocrite, by contrast, live in order to eat.

## The Intention of the Believer Is Better Than His Deed

A pure ḥadīth runs: "The intention (niyya) of the believer is better than his deed, and the intention of the disbeliever is worse than his deed." What is meant by this?

Even when a believer intends a righteous religious act and is then prevented by some constraint from performing it, his reward is not forfeit. He will surely receive the recompense of his intention; indeed, according to one report, the believer's intention will prove more beneficial than his action. Qāḍī al-Nuʿmān, in Daʿāʾim al-Islām, opens his treatment of worship by underscoring that every act takes its value from the intention behind it, so that the worth of an act lies first in the heart that conceives it.[^cite-8]

By the same measure, if a disbeliever intends some evil — especially harm to the friends of God — and is then prevented by some constraint from carrying it out, his evil intention is, even so, worse than his evil deed. Punishment is meted out according to intention.

Note: Translated by Maulvi Amiruddin.

To slap, harm, or abuse another in a fit of anger or excitement is not as serious as to do the same thing through deliberate planning. He who is thwarted today will act tomorrow, when the next occasion presents itself — as the hypocrites in the Prophet's own time did.

(Risāla 20, Qawl wa-l-Jawāb bi-l-Taʾwīl) Syedna ʿAlī ibn Sayyidunā Sulaymān (may God be pleased with him)

## The Believer Is Like the Bee — He Eats What Is Pure and Brings Forth What Is Pure

There is a pure ḥadīth: "The believer is like the bee — he eats what is pure and brings forth what is pure." On its face it seems only half true, for though the believer may eat what is pure, what leaves his body is waste — a foul and noisome thing. What, then, does this saying mean?

The Prophet (peace and blessings be upon him) is not pointing here to outward food, but to certain inner matters. He likens the believer to the bee, which draws nectar from flowers and discharges from its own body the sweetness of honey. By "pure food," then, the Prophet (peace be upon him) means the acquisition of sound and righteous knowledge. The believer takes such knowledge from the true bearers of truth; this knowledge brings repose and stillness to the heart and mind; he then puts it into practice across the whole span of his life; and at the last he brings it forth in the form of a noble form, luminous and full of majesty. At his death this form parts from him and ascends to its proper limit. Such is the meaning of "eating what is pure and bringing forth what is pure."

In a second sense, the believer who acquires righteous knowledge then displays it to the people of the world as a pure and exemplary character. He learns beneficial sciences from his elders and teaches the same to others, so that they too may be spiritually shaped — as bees confer benefit on others through the honey they produce.

## Whoever Multiplies His Prayers by Night, His Face Will Be Beautified by Day

A ḥadīth of the Prophet (peace and blessings be upon him) reads: "Whoever multiplies his prayers by night, his face will be beautified by day." Yet experience and observation seem to argue the contrary. He who keeps vigil through the night appears worn the next morning; his face shows fatigue, and he has little appetite for work. The real and inward ṣalāt (ritual prayer) for those summoned to the inward call is to strive in the daʿwa of the true Imam (ʿalayhi al-salām). To be joined to the Imam is, in truth, to be at prayer. Even outwardly, the comeliness of his face will be granted him as a further bounty for his service. Nāṣir-i Khusraw, in Wajh-i Dīn, sets out at length the taʾwīl of ṣalāt as the soul's standing in service to the Imam — a standing in which the outward bowing is the visible sign of the inward bond.[^cite-10]

## Whoever Builds a Mosque, Even the Size of a Sandgrouse's Nest, Allah Builds for Him a House in Paradise

The Messenger of Allah (peace be upon him) conveyed the glad tidings: "Whoever builds a mosque, even one as small as the nest of a sandgrouse, Allah will build for him a house in Paradise." Taken at face value, the saying is bewildering. Of what use is so diminutive a structure? How are the prayers — their bowings, prostrations, recitations — to be performed within it? And if no prayer can be performed there, in what sense is it a mosque at all? The literal reading collapses into absurdity, and that very collapse is the door through which taʾwīl (esoteric exegesis) must enter.

In the interpretive register, ṭayr (bird), with its plural ṭuyūr, signifies the prayers themselves. The prayers of the higher ranks resemble the great birds; the prayers of the lower ranks resemble the lesser ones. The mosque, in turn, denotes the hierarchy and station within which prayer is performed.

Read in this light, the purport of the Prophet's words becomes clear. Whoever prepares a person fit for any degree of religious service — even the humblest station within the daʿwa (the summons) — and equips him to undertake the scholarly work of the daʿwa al-hādiya (the guiding mission), Allah grants him an exalted station in Paradise: the Lāhūt-i Imāmī, the Paradise of the imam, which is the true Paradise. The daʿwa al-hādiya is the Paradise of truth, and the divine register is the Paradise of the imam.[^cite-11]

## Wipe Yourselves with the Earth, For It Is Dutiful to You

The Prophet (peace be upon him) said: "Wipe yourselves with the earth (for tayammum, for purification), for surely the earth is dutiful to you." What does this hadith mean? The Prophet's command is given in general terms, drawing no distinction between pure and impure ground — yet tayammum is not lawfully performed with unclean soil. And what is signified by the phrase "for it is dutiful to you" (fa-innahā bikum birra)? What kind of goodness is being ascribed to the earth?

The reference here is to the exemplary summons of the earth — namely, the daʿwa of the Imam of the Age, which is the call of truth. To "wipe oneself" with it is to maintain contact with it, and to take the oath of allegiance (bayʿa, hand in hand), just as one who performs tayammum places his palm upon the ground and is touched by its clay, which then bestows purity upon him. What greater virtue could there be than this — that the earth purifies the impure, makes him fit and worthy of prayer, and renders him eligible for entry into Paradise? And entry into the Paradise of this Imam is not possible save by entry into his summons.[^cite-14]

In a further sense, "the earth" denotes the ḥujja (proof, the imam's chief witness) of the Imam of the Age (peace be upon him). To wipe oneself with the ḥujja is to develop a spiritual bond with him and to draw from him the blessings of knowledge and sanctity — a goodness greater than any other. Thus both the daʿwa al-hādiya and the ḥujja of the Imam of the Age are "the earth." The question of purity and impurity does not arise: both are pure.

## The Fewest of the Dwellers of Paradise Are Women

The Prophet (peace be upon him) said: "The fewest of the dwellers of Paradise are women." What is meant?

Here, "Paradise" (al-janna) refers to the daʿwa al-hādiya, which is the Paradise of the spiritual powers (Jannat al-Quwā). "Women" (al-nisāʾ) refers to the body of the mustafīdūn — those who only receive benefit, the disciples — and correspondingly "men" (al-rijāl) refers to the mufīdūn, those who impart benefit, the teachers.

The meaning is this: the ḥudūd al-ʿulwiyya — the upper limits of the daʿwa, the mufīdūn — discharge the duties of the summons. They stand fast in teaching and learning, and they meet the enemies of the call with scholarly argument. Within the daʿwa of the Imam of the Age (which is the Paradise), those who serve the cause of guidance are the more numerous; while in the lower ranks the women — those who cannot impart benefit to others but only draw it for themselves — are the fewer.[^cite-15] In sum, the Prophet has said: "In the sanctuary of the Imam there shall be fewer mustafīdūn (women) and more mufīdūn (men)."

## Truly, the Punishment of This Ummah Has Been Placed in Its World

The Messenger of Allah (peace be upon him) said: "Truly, the punishment of this Ummah has been placed in its world." Taken at face value, the hadith appears to nullify the punishment of the Hereafter — and yet the Qurʾān is filled with the promises and warnings of Allah concerning the world to come. Outwardly, then, the hadith seems to contradict the Qurʾānic verse. How is it to be reconciled? The Prophet (peace be upon him) himself forbade the unconditional acceptance of hadith in such cases, saying: "Whatever command or report is conveyed to you in my name, weigh it against the verse of the Qurʾān. If it accords with the Qurʾān, accept it; if it disagrees, reject it — for I cannot say anything against the Qurʾān." The question, then, is by what means the harmony of this hadith with the Qurʾān may be established and confirmed.

In this hadith, the word "Ummah" refers to the true Ummah that walks in the Prophet's footsteps — the chain of imams from his descendants. This Ummah is itself upon guidance and guides others, in the sense of standing as the middle term within the wider community. Its well-known personages are Mawlānā Amīr al-Muʾminīn ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib, the successor (waṣī) of the Prophet, and then the infallible imams of his line.[^cite-16]

The meaning, now, is clear. The true Ummah of the Prophet (peace be upon him) has endured grievous tribulations at the hands of its enemies. Their rights were stripped from them, they were slain, their women widowed, their children orphaned. From Badr and Ḥunayn to Khaybar and Khandaq, from Jamal and Ṣiffīn to Nahrawān and Karbalāʾ, the hatred long concealed in the breasts of those who had embraced Islam in hypocrisy was made manifest in the martyrdom and imprisonment of the Prophet's descendants — his true Ummah — by which those breasts cooled their rancour.[^cite-17] In this sense, the Messenger of Allah (peace be upon him) said: "Whatever has befallen my Ummah has befallen it here. My Ummah will not have to suffer the punishment of the Hereafter."

## He Who Knows His Own Soul Has Known His Lord

This tradition stands among those of which the Prophet ⟪ar:رسول اللہ صلی اللہ علیہ و آلہ و سلم⟫ said, ⟪ar-quote:بَعَثتُ بِجَوَاِمعُ الکَلاَم⟫ — "I have been sent with the comprehensive sayings, the gems that gather all meaning." A questioner once asked him to make God known, and he replied, ⟪ar-quote:مَن عَرَفَ نَفسُہُ فَقَد عَرَفَ رَبُّہُ⟫ — "He who knows his own soul has thereby known his Lord." On another occasion he said, ⟪ar-quote:اَعرَفکُم بِنَفسِہِ اَعرَفکُم بِرَبِّہِ⟫ — "The most knowledgeable of you concerning his own soul is the most knowledgeable of you concerning his Lord."

By this the Messenger of God made plain that the knowledge of the Lord is accessible only by way of the knowledge of the soul. For how shall the creature comprehend, with the very intellect bestowed upon him, the Essence that fashioned both his soul and that intellect? If a human being cannot exhaustively grasp his own self, how shall he grasp the Lord who brought that self into being?

It is for this reason impossible to know Mubdiʿ Taʿālā ⟪ar:مبدع تعالی⟫ (the Originator, Most High) as He is in Himself; one can only confess that He is. Cognition and recognition are always directed toward what stands higher in rank than the knower.[^cite-19] Three terms emerge in this hadith:

| ʿAql (Intellect) | Nafs (Soul) | Rabb (Lord) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Intellect | Soul | Lord |

The pronoun ⟪ar:مَن⟫ ("who") refers to the one who, through his ⟪ar:عقل⟫ (intellect), strives to recognise the inner capacities of his ⟪ar:نفس⟫ (soul), and who through these capacities moves toward the knowledge and recognition of his Lord.

For the people of the outward sense (ahl al-ẓāhir, the exoterists), the hadith bears this meaning: when a person reflects upon his own coming into being, he sees how God fashioned for him, from clay, the nutritive components ⟪ar:اجزاء غذائیۃ⟫ and the extract ⟪ar:سلالۃ⟫, then the drop of seed ⟪ar:نطفۃ⟫. This drop in turn became a clinging clot ⟪ar:عَلَقَۃ⟫, then a morsel of flesh ⟪ar:مُضغَۃ⟫, then bones ⟪ar:عظام⟫ clothed with flesh ⟪ar:لحم⟫. Into this a spirit was breathed; the being emerged upon the surface of the earth, was made sovereign over all that is upon it, and was sustained with wholesome food and drink. Since he himself had no part in any of this fashioning, he is brought to confess that there is Another who has summoned him from non-being into being. Reflecting further still, he observes that he was born weak, gradually gained strength, and then declined again into weakness and grey hair — as God says:

### Direct and Indirect Creatures

The people of the inner sense (ahl al-bāṭin) ponder this saying of the Messenger and observe that the human being is not, in the strict sense, a direct creation of God. For God is bound by neither time nor place in any of His acts, nor is He dependent upon the four elements ⟪ar:عناصر اربع⟫ — fire, air, water, and earth. It is the human being who requires instruments and ingredients to bring anything into being; the Creator of the human being has no such need. He is free and pure of every such dependence. To attribute these requirements to God in the fashioning of man would be to assimilate Him to a creature — weak, incapable, and finite. Just as a person needs paper, ink, pen, and hand to set down a single line, must God then need clay, soil, womb, and nine months of gestation to bring forth a man? Such a conception cannot stand alongside a sound monotheism (tawḥīd).[^cite-20]

For this reason the human being, who emerges gradually from clay and seminal fluid, is not a direct but an indirect creature. The argument is plain. Man is brought forth in this world, takes his sustenance from it, and depends upon it for his subsistence. The world, however, is not in turn dependent upon any of the conditions that man requires at his birth. This very asymmetry separates the human being from the cosmos. The world itself stands in need of no tools, no time, no place at its own origination; it is directly God's creation. Man, fashioned from the four elements ⟪ar:عناصر اربع⟫ of this world, is creation at one remove. Of the world's own coming into being God says:

> ⟪quran 16:40⟫
> ⟪ar:‏إِنَّمَا قَوْلُنَا لِشَىْءٍ إِذَآ أَرَدْنَهُ أَن نَّقُولَ لَهُۥ كُن فَيَكُونُ ‎⟫
> *Verily! Our Word unto a thing when We intend it, is only that We say unto it: "Be!" and it is.*
>Whatever We intend to do, Our only saying is that We say to it, 'Be,' and it is.
> — **Quran 16:40 (inline widget)**

### Part or Effect

Expounding the hadith ⟪ar-quote:مَن عَرَفَ نَفسُہُ فَقَد عَرَفَ رَبُّہُ⟫ of the Messenger of God ⟪ar:رسول اللہ (ﷺ)⟫, Sayyidnā al-Muʾayyad ⟪ar:سیدنا مؤید اعلی اﷲ قدسہ⟫ explains that the human being is divided into two: a body and a soul. The body is composed of the four elements (fire, air, water, and earth). From the terrestrial sphere ⟪ar:کرۃ الأرض⟫ man draws his earthen part; from the air he draws his aerial part; from the heat of bodily life he draws his fiery part; and from water he draws his aqueous part.

Yet alongside this dense composition there is in the human being a subtle reality by which he encompasses the heavens and the earth within the centre of his reflection — without bodily displacement, without exertion, without strain. This faculty is commonly known as the rational soul (nafs nāṭiqa) ⟪ar:نفس ناطقۃ⟫. It is by virtue of this nafs nāṭiqa ⟪ar:نفس ناطقۃ⟫ that the human being is designated the noblest of created beings (ashraf al-makhlūqāt) ⟪ar:اشرف المخلوقات⟫ and is distinguished from the animal soul (nafs ḥayawāniyya) ⟪ar:نفس حیوانیۃ⟫.

Beyond this there is yet another subtle reality within the self, finer than the soul itself, which is called intellect (ʿaql), and by which a person discriminates good from evil. Justice demands that, just as the body has its source, so too the soul and the intellect must have a centre from which they draw their being and to which they stand in relation. We call this centre the Universal Soul (al-nafs al-kulliyya) ⟪ar:نفس کلیۃ⟫ and the Universal Intellect (al-ʿaql al-kulliyya) ⟪ar:عقل کلیۃ⟫, for all the souls and intellects of this physical world are partial, and they draw their powers from their centre.

In this way man stands divided: in respect of body and ⟪ar:نفس⟫ and ⟪ar:عقل⟫, he is the child of this natural world; and in respect of the Universal Soul ⟪ar:نفس کلیۃ⟫ and Universal Intellect ⟪ar:عقل کلیۃ⟫, he is the child of another. Thus is it established that the human being is the offspring of two worlds.

| Jism (Body) | Nafs (Soul) | ʿAql (Intellect) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| The Four Elements (Fire, Air, Water, Soil) | Nafs Kulliyya | Universal Intellect |

Yet we observe that the portion of earth in the human body is so minute that no real proportion can be drawn between man's share of soil and the soil of the whole earth. No ratio can be established between them. So too with the aerial sphere ⟪ar:کرۃ ہوائیۃ⟫ — what proportion does the air in the human body bear to it? What proportion to the sphere of fire ⟪ar:کرۃ النار⟫ does the heat within the body bear, or to the watery sphere ⟪ar:کرۃ آبی⟫ does the water within him bear? No comparison whatsoever can be drawn. **The four natures within the human body are only a small portion of those elements ⟪ar:عناصر اربع⟫, not the effect of them as such!**

Turning then to the world of the Universal Soul ⟪ar:عالم نفس کلی⟫ and the Universal Intellect ⟪ar:عقل کلی⟫, we see that the human ⟪ar:نفس اور عقل⟫ are likewise partial; they are merely an effect within the universal world ⟪ar:عالم کل⟫, not a part of it. From this it follows that, despite our utmost efforts, no proportional relation can be established between the human ⟪ar:نفس و عقل⟫ and that higher world.

When the human being is born of these two orders — the world of body ⟪ar:عالم جسم⟫ and the world of soul and intellect ⟪ar:عالم نفس و عقل⟫ — and is unable to establish a true proportional relation with either, despite being derivative of both, from where then shall he draw the power to comprehend the reality of tawḥīd, where no relation at all can be drawn, namely the reality of Mubdiʿ Taʿālā ⟪ar:مبدِع تعالی⟫?

In this way the human being comes to acknowledge that what he had taken to be the comprehension of monotheism is itself a mirage. He then grasps the truth of the saying of Mawlānā ʿAlī ⟪ar:مولانا علی⟫ — ⟪ar-quote:اَلعِجزُ عَن دَرَکِ اِلأدرَاکِہِ اِدراک⟫ — "incapacity to attain the apprehension of the Originator ⟪ar:مبد تعالی⟫ is itself an apprehension."[^cite-21] Thereafter he confirms the saying of the Prophet: when a person cannot know his own soul, how shall he know his Lord?

## He Who Knows His Soul Has Known His Lord — The Centres of Body, Soul, and Intellect

Continuing his exposition of the Prophetic hadith ⟪ar-quote:مَن عَرَفَ نَفسُہُ فَقَد عَرَفَ رَبُّہُ⟫ of the Messenger of God ⟪ar:رسول اللہ (ﷺ)⟫, Sayyidnā al-Muʾayyad ⟪ar:سیدنا مؤید اعلی اﷲ قدسہ⟫ returns to the division of the human composite. Man, he insists, is two: body and soul. The body is fashioned of four elements — fire, air, water, and earth. From the terrestrial sphere ⟪ar:کرۃ الأرض⟫ he draws his earthen portion; from the air he draws his aerial portion; from the heat resident in living bodies he draws his fiery portion; from water he draws his aqueous portion.

But within this dense body there is a subtle reality by which the human being encompasses the heavens and the earth within the very centre of his reflection, without bodily exertion and without displacement. This faculty is the rational soul, the nafs nāṭiqa ⟪ar:نفس ناطقۃ⟫. By virtue of this nafs nāṭiqa ⟪ar:نفس ناطقۃ⟫ he is named the noblest of created beings ⟪ar:اشرف المخلوقات⟫ and is distinguished from the animal soul ⟪ar:نفس حیوانیۃ⟫.

Beyond this still, there is in the self another reality, finer than the soul itself, which is called intellect, and by which a person discriminates good from evil. Justice requires that, just as the body has its source, so too the soul and the intellect must have a centre from which their relation is drawn. We name this centre the Universal Soul ⟪ar:نفس کلیۃ⟫ and the Universal Intellect ⟪ar:عقل کلیۃ⟫, for all the souls and intellects of this physical world are partial and derive their powers from this centre.[^cite-22]

In this manner is the human being divided: in respect of body, of ⟪ar:نفس⟫, and of ⟪ar:عقل⟫, he is the child of the natural world; and in respect of the Universal Soul ⟪ar:نفس کلیۃ⟫ and the Universal Intellect ⟪ar:عقل کلیۃ⟫, he is the child of another. Thus it stands proven that man is the offspring of two worlds.

| Jism (Body) | Nafs (Soul) | ʿAql (Intellect) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| The Four Elements (Fire, Air, Water, Soil) | Nafs Kulliyya | Universal Intellect |

The portion which man draws from the earth is so vanishingly small that no proportional relation can be drawn between his share of soil and the soil of the whole world. Likewise with the aerial sphere ⟪ar:کرۃ ہوائیۃ⟫ — what proportion does the air within his body bear to it? What proportion does the heat within him bear to the sphere of fire ⟪ar:کرۃ النار⟫, or the water within him to the watery sphere ⟪ar:کرۃ آبی⟫? No comparison whatsoever stands between them. **The four natures present in the human body are only a small portion of those elements ⟪ar:عناصر اربع⟫, not the effect of them as such!**

Turning then to the other side — the world of the Universal Soul ⟪ar:عالم نفس کلی⟫ and the Universal Intellect ⟪ar:عقل کلی⟫ — we see that the human ⟪ar:نفس اور عقل⟫ are themselves partial: they are mere effects within the universal world ⟪ar:عالم کل⟫, not parts of it. The conclusion is unavoidable: despite a thousand thousand attempts, we shall not succeed in establishing any proper relation of the human ⟪ar:نفس و عقل⟫ to that world.

When the human being is born of two orders, the world of body ⟪ar:عالم جسم⟫ and the world of soul and intellect ⟪ar:عالم نفس و عقل⟫, and yet remains unable to establish his proportional relation with either despite his derivation from them, from where shall he then draw the power to comprehend the reality of tawḥīd — where no relation at all can be drawn — that is, the reality of Mubdiʿ Taʿālā ⟪ar:مبدِع تعالی⟫?

In this manner the human being acknowledges that what he had taken to be his comprehension of monotheism is itself a mirage. He then grasps the truth of the saying of Mawlānā ʿAlī ⟪ar:مولانا علی⟫ — ⟪ar-quote:اَلعِجزُ عَن دَرَکِ اِلأدرَاکِہِ اِدراک⟫ — "incapacity to attain the apprehension of the Originator ⟪ar:مبد تعالی⟫ is itself an apprehension." Thereafter he confirms the saying of the Prophet: when a person cannot know his own soul, how shall he know his Lord?

## The Grave Is Either a Garden Among the Gardens of Paradise

The Prophet (peace and blessings of God be upon him) said: "The grave is either a garden among the gardens of Paradise or a pit among the pits of Hell." In a second tradition he said: "After death there is no further seeking of God's pleasure, and after this world there is no abode except Paradise or Hell." In a third he said: "Whoever dies, his resurrection has already been established."

From these verses it is understood that recompense and reward do not befall the human being immediately upon death, but only after a very long interval. How then, one must ask, can the apparent contradiction between these Prophetic sayings and the verses of the Qurʾān be reconciled?

The words of the Messenger of God are true; they do not stand against the Qurʾān — provided that the People of Remembrance (ahl al-dhikr) and the Gate of Knowledge are consulted, and the meaning is drawn from them.

### The Garden of Paradise and the Pit of Hell

When a believer dies and his subtle form departs from the dead body, that form is at once united and merged with its upper limit (ḥadd ʿālī, the higher rank to which it is connected). The two become so closely joined that no separation remains between them. In the language of taʾwīl (esoteric exegesis), the true grave of the believer is this very upper limit (ḥadd ʿālī) into which he has merged and with which he has become one — just as the outward body, when buried, becomes one with the earth of its grave. The soul of that upper figure, which is the spiritual grave of the believer, is itself a garden among the gardens of Paradise. For upon arriving there the believer's soul receives the fragrance of repose, tranquillity, contentment, and knowledge. This is the first stair, the first step, toward the Gardens of Eden (the Lāhūt of the Imam).[^cite-23]

By the same principle, the grave can also be a pit among the pits of Hell. When the enemy of the Ahl al-Bayt (peace be upon them), or a non-believer, dies, the evil, impure, dark figure which he has fashioned through his enmity and through the commission of forbidden and unlawful deeds separates from his body at the moment of death. For a certain time it wanders, and then it comes to rest in foul, dark, terrifying places. This is the first of the gates of Hell to which he is directed, and he suffers in these wretched places. As for his sensuous self, it disperses into his corrupted body, and the parts of that body decay and scatter in places where fires forever burn. Such a painful place and abode of torment becomes his grave — a pit among the pits of Hell.

These two stations — the believer's attainment of every comfort and ease, and the disbeliever's suffering of the punishment proper to his form and soul in unclean places after the dispersal of his bodily parts — constitute their barzakh until the Day of Resurrection, in which they abide until the day of the Qāʾim of Remembrance, peace be upon him.

### Death and Resurrection

This is also the meaning of the second and third traditions. That the resurrection has been established for the one who dies means that every individual death is a partial qiyāma (resurrection) for that person. Some of what is to befall on the Day of the Great Resurrection befalls him at once, while much else is dissolved into the composition of the body, and accompanies it as a lesser reckoning.

The believer beholds the goodly and beautiful form of his righteous deeds, and after death he rejoices to see his own life. The disbeliever, by contrast, beholds the terror of his evil deeds. So Amīr al-Muʾminīn said to al-Ḥārith al-Hamdānī: "O Ḥārith of Hamdān, every man who dies shall surely see me — to the believer, I shall be bearer of glad tidings; to the disbeliever, I shall stand against him."[^cite-24] Both alike behold their hour, and wonder what shall come to pass on the Day of Reckoning.

The disbeliever further perceives that the gate of repentance has now closed upon him. God can no longer be sought after, no contentment of His can be won; he must now bear whatever punishment is decreed. The Prophet (peace be upon him) thus made plain that after death there is no third abode beyond Paradise and Hell.

## Between My Grave and My Pulpit Is a Garden Among the Gardens of Paradise

There is also the noble hadith: ⟪ar-quote:بَینَ قَبرِی وَ مِنبَرِی رَوضَۃٌ مِن رِیاَض اَلجَنَّۃ⟫ — "Between my grave and my pulpit is a garden among the gardens of Paradise." What does this signify?

In their exegesis of this hadith, the duʿāt (Summoners) explain: the grave is the place that conceals the whole body of a person. The pulpit, by contrast, is the elevated place within the mosque from which the Imam delivers the sermon openly. It is built in a raised and visible spot so that, when the Imam ascends it, all those who listen may see him plainly. In short, the function of the grave is concealment; the function of the pulpit is manifestation.

In this sense the grave of Muḥammad Muṣṭafā (peace be upon him) is in truth Mawlānā ʿAlī (peace be upon him), for it was into him that all the knowledge and wisdom of the Prophet were buried and hidden. ʿAlī concealed within himself the entirety of the Prophet's inner sciences. In like manner the pulpit of the Prophet (peace be upon him) refers to the blessed personage of the Qāʾim ʿAlī Dhikrahu al-Salām: when he appears, he will openly broadcast the sciences which ʿAlī had stored away within his breast.[^cite-25]

The conditions of the time of the Qāʾim (peace be upon him) shall be such that knowledge and truth will be proclaimed openly from the pulpits. And the Paradise that lies between the Prophet's grave and his pulpit is the chain of Imams (peace be upon them) between ʿAlī and the Qāʾim. They are the very Paradise of the believer's form and soul; they are the gardens whose trees are laden with the fruits of wisdom, in which the fragrance of the flowers of knowledge is ever present.

### Realities

The heart of Madinat al-Munawwara is the grave of the Holy Prophet.

The space between the grave and the pulpit is a place of divine presence.

In the Prophet's Mosque, the Pillar of ʿAlī (Isṭiwānat ʿAlī) is a station at which every act of repentance is accepted. The Prophet (peace be upon him) said: "There is a spot in my mosque of such glory and honour that, were its excellence to be made known to the people, they would contend for it in war."

## Hold Fast to the Qurʾān, for It is an Intercessor Whose Intercession is Accepted

Concerning the excellence of the Qurʾān, the Prophet (peace be upon him) declared: hold fast to the Qurʾān, for it is the intercessor (shāfiʿ) whose intercession is accepted and the witness whose testimony is confirmed. Whoever sets it before him — that is, takes it as his guide — it will lead him to Paradise; whoever casts it behind his back, it will drive him into the Fire. The Qurʾān is the surest guide to the noblest path of God. Whoever speaks by it speaks truly; whoever acts upon it is rewarded; whoever judges by it judges with justice.

Does this prophetic saying refer to the written Qurʾān alone, or does it also designate ʿAlī (peace be upon him)?

It does indeed refer to the Qurʾān — and this in no way diminishes the worth, the dignity, the nobility, or the majesty of the Book. That Book remains in its place. Yet at the Battle of Ṣiffīn, when the partisans of Muʿāwiya raised the leaves of the Qurʾān upon their spears to halt the fighting, the Commander of the Faithful, ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib, addressed the manuscript itself:

Then, declaring his own station, he said: "I am the Speaking Book of God (al-Qurʾān al-Nāṭiq), and this is the Silent Book of God (al-Qurʾān al-Ṣāmit)." When the bound parchment returned no answer, he made plain that he himself was the articulate Word, and the written leaves its silent counterpart. This pairing of the inspired text with the living articulator who unfolds its meaning is a recurring axis of Ismaili thought; it stands at the foundation of the doctrine that revelation reaches the community only through one who can speak it.[^cite-26]

The prophetic saying thus refers, by its inner sense (taʾwīl, esoteric exegesis), to the Imam of every age, who is the heir to the Book of the Prophet. These Imams are themselves the Speaking Books of their time. They are the pure, the immaculate, noble in lineage and in conduct — the ones whose intercession will be welcomed on the Day of Resurrection, and whose testimony has been confirmed as true. Whoever takes them as his Imam will be conducted by the Imamate to Paradise; whoever rejects them is cast into the Fire. Whoever acknowledges their authority speaks truly, and whoever orders his deeds by their command earns a beautiful recompense. Whoever judges by the Sharīʿa of their grandfather, the Messenger of God (peace and blessings be upon him), has judged with justice; whoever does otherwise has done wrong.

## The Likeness of My Household is the Likeness of the Ark of Noah

The Prophet (peace and blessings be upon him) likened his Ahl al-Bayt to the Ark of Noah and said: "The likeness of my Household is the likeness of the Ark of Noah — whoever boards it is saved, and whoever holds back from it is drowned and perishes."

This image is itself a confession that the Prophet anticipated a storm. To compare his Family to an ark while expecting no flood would be to compare them to nothing at all. The episodes the Qurʾān recounts of past prophets are not narrated for the sake of mere remembrance; their purpose is to disclose a recurring pattern — what once befell those communities will return, in altered form, to the community that comes after. And so, after the Messenger of God, every catastrophe described in the earlier stories reappeared, though clothed in different garments.

Take the story of Noah's Ark. After the Messenger of God, a flood of innovations rose against the religion of Islam, and the souls of the Muslim community were submerged in waves of doubt and bewilderment. ## The Hadith on Bringing Images and Statues to Life

The Prophet (peace and blessings be upon him) said: "On the Day of Resurrection, those who fashion images and statues will be brought before God, and they will be commanded: breathe a spirit into these creatures of yours."

In their inner sense (taʾwīl), these statues and effigies are the imams of falsehood and the unjust caliphs — those who set themselves against the rightful Imams, the vicegerents of God upon the earth, and deceive the people by adorning themselves in the costume of scholars while their substance is satanic. Such caliphs are images without souls, lifeless effigies into which the spirit of true and abiding life has never been breathed; that is to say, the Messenger of God (peace and blessings be upon him) never installed them in his caliphate by a binding designation upon them.

The Messenger of God (peace and blessings be upon him) was, in his own age, the first into whom the True Spirit was breathed.

## The Tradition of Reciting the Basmala over Water and Blowing upon It

A tradition relates that on the Day of Resurrection, Hell will roar like a lion and bellow like a dragon. Then ⟪ar:رسول اﷲ (ﷺ)⟫ will be brought a cup of water; he will recite ⟪ar:بسم اﷲ الرحمن الرحیم⟫ over it and breathe upon it. The water will be poured into Hell, and its fire will at once be extinguished and its voice silenced. By the command of God, Most High, a voice will call out to it: "O Fire, what has befallen you, that you have fallen silent and grown subdued?" And it will answer: "O Lord, I have no power against Your Name."

The inner meaning of this hadith is as follows. The family of falsehood and the enemies of the Ahl al-Bayt are themselves a likeness of Hell, for they burn their followers in the fire of doubt and suspicion. When a question is put to them to which they possess no answer, they roar before the questioner and seek to silence him through the outward force of their tongues — invoking, by way of threat, the worldly authority they command or the punishment of God.

The ⟪ar:رسول اﷲ (ﷺ)⟫ — or, in his time, the Imam of the Age — is the true Name of God (⟪ar:أسم اﷲ⟫); and the cup of water over which the Name of God is recited (⟪ar:بسم اﷲ⟫) is the believer who has taken the covenant of the Imam of the Age. This responder (mustajīb) is a humble disciple of the Imam, like a single cup of water set beside the ocean of his master's knowledge. Yet when this disciple debates with the outward scholar — who is the very figure of Hell in this image — and puts to him one plain question in pursuit of evidence, that ostensible scholar is humbled and unable to answer. His voice falters; his rhetorical strength dissolves.

قَوْمٍ هَادٍ ‎⟫ ⟪ar:‏يَعْمَلُونَ لَهُۥ مَا يَشَآءُ مِن مَّحَرِيبَ وَتَمَثِيلَ وَجِفَانٍۢ كَٱلْجَوَابِ وَقُدُورٍۢ رَّاسِيَتٍۚ ٱعْمَلُوٓا۟ آلَ دَاوُۥدَ شُكْرًۭاۚ وَقَلِيلٌۭ مِّنْ عِبَادِىَ ٱلشَّكُورُ ‎⟫ ⟪ar:‏يَوْمَ نَدْعُوا۟ كُلَّ أُنَاسٍۭ بِإِمَمِهِمْ ۖ فَمَنْ أُوتِىَ كِتَبَهُۥ بِيَمِينِهِۦ فَأُو۟لَٓئِكَ يَقْرَءُونَ كِتَبَهُمْ وَلَا يُظْلَمُونَ فَتِيلًۭا ‎⟫ ⟪ar:‏يَٓأَيُّهَا ٱلَّذِينَ آمَنُوٓا۟ إِذَا نُودِىَ لِلصَّلَوٰةِ مِن يَوْمِ ٱلْجُمُعَةِ فَٱسْعَوْا۟ إِلَىٰ ذِكْرِ ٱللَّهِ وَذَرُوا۟ ٱلْبَيْعَ ۚ ذَلِكُمْ خَيْرٌۭ لَّكُمْ إِن كُنتُمْ تَعْلَمُونَ ‎⟫
