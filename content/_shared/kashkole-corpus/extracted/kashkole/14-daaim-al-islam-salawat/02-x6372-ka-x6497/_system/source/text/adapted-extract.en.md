## The Four Obligatory Pillars of Prayer

⟪ar:رسول اللہ (ﷺ)⟫ The Messenger of Allah has established that prayer rests upon four obligatory pillars (arkan). Should even one be omitted or left incomplete, the prayer is rendered null and unaccepted.

| 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Time | Direction of Prayer | Intention | Ritual Ablution |

This structure parallels the office of the Summoner (nāṭiq, the speaker of divine law). Just as the Summoner must perform four functions in sequence—to bring forth the Sharīʿah (divine law), to enforce it, to disseminate it, and to embody it personally—so too must each of these four pillars be present in prayer. Whether the tenure of a given Summoner be brief or extended, the absence of any single element renders his office incomplete. He must accomplish all four within his own epoch.

| Time | Direction of Prayer | Intention | Ritual Ablution |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Bringing the Sharīʿah | Disseminating the Sharīʿah | Enforcing the Sharīʿah | Embodying the Sharīʿah |

Similarly, the establishment of the daʿwa (summons, the invitation to faith) requires four persons: ⟪ar:ناطق⟫ the Summoner, ⟪ar:اساس⟫ the Foundation, ⟪ar:امام⟫ the Imam, and ⟪ar:حجت⟫ the Proof. To deny even one renders membership in the covenant impossible. Since ⟪ar:'ناطق '⟫ prayer signifies the summons of God Himself, it becomes an allegory of Time, which encompasses and dominates all creation. The daʿwa cannot commence until the Summoner occupies his station. The Foundation (asās) corresponds to the direction of prayer (qibla), toward which the Summoner gestures. We possess an inner Qibla: ⟪ar:امیر المومنین علی ابن ابی طالب علیہ السلام⟫ Amīr al-Muʾminīn ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib, to whom our spiritual affiliation (wilāya) is oriented. The Imam stands for the intention (niyya) with which we covenant. The Proof (ḥujja) corresponds to ablution, that which purges doubt and obscurity from our knowledge.[^cite-1]

### The Prayer of the Sulaymānīs Alone Is Accepted

⟪ar:ناطق اعظم محمد مصطفی صلی اللہ علیہ و آلہ و سلم⟫ The entire Muslim Umma acknowledges the summons of the Prophet Muḥammad (upon him be peace and blessings), and all recite his testimony of faith. None dispute his prophethood or the seal of prophecy upon him. This is why all humanity observes the times of prayer with such diligence. Yet the Ahl al-Sunna (the People of Sunnah), having rejected the bequest (waṣiyya) and spiritual guardianship (wilāya) of Maulānā ʿAlī—which constitutes the inner Qibla—their prayers are rendered impermissible. The Shīʿa have accepted Maulānā ʿAlī, but they failed to recognize the true succession of Imams from his descendants, who embody the intention of prayer. Therefore their prayers too remain unaccepted. The Dāʾūdī Bohorā, though they profess belief in Maulānā ʿAlī and his Imamic descendants, have denied the Proof of this age, ⟪ar:سیدنا سلیمان بن حسن اعلی اللہ قدسہ⟫ our master Sulaymān ibn Ḥasan. In this regard they perform prayer lacking ablution, which is forbidden. Only the Sulaymānī community possesses all four obligatory pillars in their integrity; therefore only our prayers are lawful and acceptable.[^cite-2]

## What Is Worship?

Every action that pleases and delights the Almighty is reckoned as worship. Thus on the night of the Hijra, when Maulānā ʿAlī lay upon the Prophet's bed to divert the assassins, his sleep constituted an act of worship—more meritorious than the night vigils of others—because it was performed to please Allah and His Messenger. Conversely, the person who stands in prayer all night in the mosque yet harbors resentment toward ʿAlī in his heart performs no worship, for such prayer cannot please Allah. The essence of worship is the quest for and attainment of divine pleasure.

**The Worship of Zayd**

A man named Zayd came to the Messenger of Allah shortly before a battle and declared, "O Messenger of God, I am a disbeliever, yet Allah has granted me the ability to embrace Islam at your hands." The Messenger taught him the testimony of faith. Zayd then said, "O Messenger of Allah, pray that I enter Paradise!" The Messenger of Allah prayed for his admission. When the time for battle came, Zayd said, "O Messenger of Allah, now that I have embraced Islam and received your grace, permit me to join the struggle." The Messenger granted permission. Zayd fought and was martyred. Upon receiving news of his death, the Prophet declared, "Zayd never performed a single ritual prayer, yet he has entered Paradise straightaway."

True worship is indeed the pleasure of Allah. An act that pleases Him is worship; if prayer does not please Him, it is not worship. The Prophet has said that many who pray never have their prayers reach Allah. The angels hurl those prayers back into the faces of such worshippers. This is the condition of those who merely recite the Qurʾān without understanding.[^cite-3]

How then could it be permissible to treat lightly or abandon a pillar of the Sharīʿah that possesses such power and produces such salutary effect upon the human soul five times daily?

Sayyidunā al-Muʾayyad fī al-Dīn (may Allah sanctify his soul) has declared in his luminous discourses: "I bear witness that no self or being deserves worship save that which cannot be known except through His limits (ḥudūd, the divine boundaries)." True monotheism avoids two errors: the false deification of creation, or the delimitation of the Infinite.

Allah has prescribed five prayers across the twenty-four hours, making them obligatory at all seasons and all times, so that the believer becomes ever-ready to answer the call and prayer becomes a habit of the soul. This habit imparts understanding of prayer's magnitude to the intellect and cultivates in the heart an eager anticipation for prayer itself.[^cite-4]

The believer should also strive to comprehend the inner reasons for prayer—why the number of prostrations (rakʿāt) varies, what significance attaches to standing, bowing, prostration, and the closing testimony (tashahhud). Once these realities are grasped, the believer becomes reconciled with the outward obligatory works, and the light of knowledge diffuses through the soul. Spiritual forms are generated; the soul connects with the divine boundaries, attaches to their causal origins, unites with them in a bond never to be severed. This applies equally to all the pillars of the faith. Hold fast to these things and preserve them; through them you shall ascend to the ranks of the angels and become a neighbor of honor and might.

The limits (ḥudūd) have said on occasion: "We shall find a dwelling-place near to God, the One whose Names are utterly sanctified and pure, and there shall we dwell in peace." In the Names of God dwell luminous beings who inhabit the realm of the kingdom, wherein is neither flesh nor blood.[^cite-5]

## What Is the Purpose of All Obligatory Acts?

The outward prayer engages all the external limbs. Prayer stands as the essence of all the modes of worship in previous religions. In the practices of former communities, some involved standing alone, others recitation, others bowing, still others prostration only, and yet others sitting in meditation. When the Prophet possessed the knowledge of these preceding forms, he incorporated them all into his mode of worship—prayer—gathering them into a single, magnificent, and ordered practice. He arranged each element such that the spirit of all prior worship was realized within prayer.

**The Meaning of Obligatory Acts**

The word "prayer" itself derives from reconciliation and harmony. The purpose of prayer and all obligatory acts is the transmutation of coarseness into subtlety. The Messenger of Allah declared, "Humility and submissiveness are the soul of prayer." Just as the human body without a soul is a putrid and decaying corpse, so prayer without humility and submissiveness is a hollow and worthless act. The perfection of prayer lies in the movement of all limbs in a prescribed manner and order. The Prophet fixed specific limits and a precise form for each gesture, thereby creating a comprehensive glorification and a correct understanding of the true divine reality.

All forms of worship, and especially prayer, constitute a rigorous and necessarily toilsome process. Allah states in His Book:

Allah has not prescribed prayer and the other obligatory duties to afflict His servants; rather, He has ordained them out of mercy and compassion. He clothed the outward prayer as a parable and rendered the physical tangible as an indication of the intelligible realm. His aim was to guide His elect to the divine boundaries through prayer, to purify dense bodies through obedience to them, and through love of and spiritual affiliation (wilāya) with these boundaries, to rectify the afterlife and to escape sin and denial of Allah through their recognition and knowledge. Inner prayer—that is, humility and submissiveness—relates to attachment to the chosen servants of Allah, which is the love of Amīr al-Muʾminīn and the People of the House. The union of both constitutes the perfection of the believer, who thereby truly becomes the servant of Allah (ʿAbd Allāh).[^cite-6]

## The Means for Worship

Allah has established means for all things. When a believer supplicates, he does not do so directly to the Creator; rather, he appeals through the divine boundaries (ḥudūd) whom the Creator has appointed. Some object that such prayer constitutes associating partners with God (shirk).

Consider the example of a small child hungry for milk, who asks his mother. The mother does not say, "Your Creator is Allah—ask Him alone, lest you commit shirk!" Rather, she fulfills the child's need, recognizing that Allah has created her and made her the child's nourishment. Asking the mother does not divert the child from spirituality. Prayer truly expounds the system of the summons (daʿwa). Its examples are the divine boundaries through which we attain knowledge of Allah. When we descended from the spiritual world into the ranks of material existence, we were separated from image into selfhood, from soul into body. Now, when our soul desires to worship, it depends upon our hands and feet, upon our prostration and rising. These became the means of worship; we cannot worship without them. Similarly, though our worship is directed toward the Creator, to reach Him we require intermediaries and exemplars between ourselves and the Divine, just as a distance exists between the second and first source of emanation. We cannot attain truth without knowing the people of truth. In the same manner, we cannot know Allah without knowing the people of Allah. This is the import of the saying: ⟪ar-quote:مَن عَرَفَ نَفسُہُ فَقَد عَرَفَ رَبُّہُ⟫ "Whosoever knows his self has known his Lord." Man's journey toward knowledge of self is endless; thus his knowledge of the Lord knows no limit. For this reason, the self is known through knowledge of the outward body.

The body is the outward form in which virtue arises from the soul. Prayer likewise is an outward act that corresponds to the noble summons (daʿwa sharīf). Its essence is understanding the system of that summons. In the outward aspects of prayer, all the divine boundaries are arrayed in order. Just as we recognize the body through its various organs and understand the body through knowing which organ surpasses which, so too in understanding the order of the boundaries of an Imam shall we gain knowledge of that Imam.

Since we are confined within this gross and material realm, we cannot apprehend the spiritual world by dwelling here, for our intellect cannot function in both worlds simultaneously. We can only comprehend the realm of spirit—the noble summons—by means of the physical world.[^cite-7]

## Prayer: The Confluence of the Custodian and the Established

In Arabic, the verb *salat* signifies meeting or joining together. The name of prayer thus denotes the joining of our spirits or the connection between them. The obligatory prayers correspond to the Imams; the supererogatory prayers correspond to the Prophets. In this sense, the true confluence of the custodian (mustawdaʿ) and the established (mustaqarr) in reality is termed prayer. The Qurʾān records Abraham's words to his son:

The submission (islām) of the Prophets is that they bow before their Imam. The true name of all this is al-islām (submission). *Salāt yasalat min al-dānī ilā al-ʿālī*—"Prayer is a link that joins the lower to the higher." Pointing to this meaning, the Prophet declared, "Cursed is he who cuts the kinship bond."

**The Final Testament of the Messenger of Allah**

The Prophet's final bequest was prayer. He said:

> Prayer, prayer, and upon you be peace.
> Prayer, prayer, prayer—it is obligatory upon you, prayer is binding, prayer is imperative.

That is, he charged all the trustees to attach themselves to the mosque. Or rather, it was a bequest for every boundary to attach itself to its higher boundary.

Beyond prayer, the Prophet also enjoined in his final testament kind treatment of wives. Outwardly addressing the community, he conveyed an inward message: guard the outward prayer and remain inwardly connected to the noble summons; outwardly treat wives with kindness, and inwardly, you who are boundaries, guard those beneath you. Do not impart knowledge to them that might terrify them.

## Three Kinds of Prayer

Qāḍī al-Nuʿmān (may Allah sanctify him) states in his work *Mafātīḥ al-Niʿam* that the Messenger of Allah established prayer in three categories: the obligatory (farḍ), the customary (sunna), and the supererogatory (nāfila).

**The Obligatory**

There is no remission from obligation, nor can it be set aside for any reason. Whoever performs the obligatory prayers is called a servant (ʿabd)—one who has been enclosed within the Imamic sanctuary. Whoever stands outside the covenant of servitude is a disbeliever. In this station there exist only two stations: servant and disbeliever.

**The Customary**

The second category is the Sunna, which is subordinate to the obligatory and holds the rank after it. Whoever performs the Sunna is called the pious one (muttaqī). Should he neglect the Sunna, he is called a transgressor, yet not a disbeliever, for he has discharged his obligation.

**The Supererogatory**

Whoever does not perform the supererogatory after the obligatory and the Sunna shall have his faith perfected; he is called a believer, yet his station remains below that of the pious one and he does not attain the highest ranks.

|  | Obligatory | Customary | Supererogatory |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Practitioner | Servant | The Pious | The Believer |
| Non-practitioner | Disbeliever | Transgressor | Below the Pious |

The pious one is a believer who ascends to the Garden upon the two wings of knowledge and deed. The pious one is he who acquires knowledge and acts upon it. The Prophet declared, "Prayer is the sacrifice of every pious person, and I am the grandfather of the pious." The word *qurbān* (sacrifice) derives from *qurb* (nearness). The Prophet said, "Prayer is the sacrifice of every pious person, and I am the ancestor of every pious person." His statement that he is the ancestor of the pious indicates that through these stations of piety, one ascends from the station of manifest leadership to the station of guidance.[^cite-8]

## Prayer and the Number Nineteen

The obligatory prayers of the day consist of seventeen prostrations:

| Dawn | Noon | Afternoon | Sunset | Night |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 2 | 4 | 4 | 3 | 4 |

This total of twenty-four rak'ahs divides evenly by nineteen (24434 ÷ 19 = 1286). Moreover, if we combine the ordinal position of each prayer with its number of rak'ahs—for instance, the Dawn prayer as the first prayer containing two rak'ahs yields (2, 1)—and apply this method to all five prayers, the following emerges:

| Dawn | Noon | Afternoon | Sunset | Night |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 2 (1) | 4 (2) | 4 (3) | 3 (4) | 4 (5) |

The resulting number (2142433445) divides evenly by nineteen: 2142433445 ÷ 19 = 112759655. Furthermore, when the two rak'ahs of the Congregational (Friday) prayer are added to these, the total becomes nineteen rak'ahs:

| Dawn | Noon | Afternoon | Sunset | Night | Friday | Total |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 2 | 4 | 4 | 3 | 4 | 2 | 19 |

In this verse, heedlessness of prayer signifies the denial of Maulānā ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib. Those possessed of worldly inclination and burdened by the observance of the Sharīʿah began to mislead others through distorted interpretation of the divine ordinances. They claim that once the esoteric meaning of a Sharīʿah ruling is comprehended, the outward practice of that ruling becomes unnecessary. They say: "If the inner meaning of prayer is understood, then the outward prayer is no longer obligatory." Such people have fallen into the abyss of ruin and perdition by rendering the forbidden lawful. Though they call themselves Muslims or believers, they inflict greater harm upon the faith and the faithful than do the polytheists.

The esoteric meaning (taʾwīl) of prayer exists and is real. This meaning was veiled in the garment of the outward form to facilitate the ascent to inner understanding. Yet once the meaning is grasped, the outward act does not become null. These people fail to recognize that the outward prayer dwells in the palace of the body, while its meaning resides in the palace of the soul. Can one discover the soul by annihilating the body? Certainly not! Rather, it is through the union of the outward and the inward that one is called a person; without it, one is but a corpse.

In the same manner, the outward prayer exemplifies this world, while its inner meaning exemplifies the Hereafter. Should we come to know the Hereafter, does the present world—on which we walk, from which we draw sustenance, and by whose air we live—thereby cease to exist? Is it possible to abandon worldly affairs upon acquiring knowledge of the next life? Can one quench physical thirst merely by imagining water?

**Pure Spiritual Worship**

Pure worship in its essence is reserved for the angels near to Allah, for they are purified of materiality and its grossness and are purely spiritual. For them, worship alone suffices. But since we journey with the spirit while bound to a dense body, we must practice both the outward worship and the inward meaning.[^cite-9]
