# 001 - 023

This book... ⟪ar:کتاب العالم و الغلام⟫-  The Master and the Disciple is an old and timeless story of a seeker’s quest for true knowledge, and the process through which he gradually achieved it. What's interesting about this book is that it has been presented as a story - which is very different from our other religious books. It's written by ⟪ar:سیدنا جعفر بن منصور الیمن قدس اللہ روحہ⟫who was a very high ranking ⟪ar:داعی⟫. He was a ⟪ar:باب الابواب⟫ for those of you who know what that means. He was considered a master of ⟪ar:تاویل⟫.

Through this book, he guides us to the **path**of the friends of Allah - these special, chosen people who possess the spiritual knowledge that makes them spiritually eternal. He explains how these friends of Allah receive this divine trust from their Lord; and how they fulfill their responsibility of transmitting this divine knowledge to their fellow human beings. He obviously uses the Quran and HADEES as the source for all his arguments and statements that he uses in this story. But what I love about this book is that it acts as a map; a guide that each and everyone of us will follow, and interpret in our own way. We will interpret this story in the light of our own individual experiences and insight. So what I get out of this material will be very different from what you will get out of it.

This book is basically about a series of dramatic dialogues that take place between the ‘Master’ ⟪ar:العالم⟫and his young disciple (⟪ar:غلام⟫). The word ⟪ar:غلام⟫in Arabic refers to a young boy. It's not the same as the Urdu ⟪ar:غلام⟫which means a slave. The word for slave in Arabic is ⟪ar:عبد⟫. The word ⟪ar:غلام⟫refers more to a young boy who is under the guardianship or supervision of an adult. So let's begin...

(١) بَلَغَنَا أَنَّ طَوَائِفَ مِنَ الْمُؤْمِنِينَ وَنَفَرًا مِنْ دُعَاةِ الدِّينِ قَالُوا لِعَالِمٍ لَهُمْ: قَدْ أَعْتَقْتَنَا بِأَنَّكَ قَدْ عَرَّفْتَنَا أَمْرًا وَجَبَ لَكَ عَلَيْنَا بِهِ الشُّكْرُ لِثَلَاثَةِ وُجُوهٍ: شُكْرُكَ فِيمَا دَعَوْتَنَا إِلَيْهِ، وَشُكْرُ الْعِلْمِ الَّذِي دَلَلْتَنَا عَلَيْهِ، وَشُكْرُ الْعَمَلِ الَّذِي أَمَرْتَنَا بِهِ، فَبَيِّنْ لَنَا مِنْ ذَلِكَ مَا يَقُومُ بِهِ مَنْ أَرَادَ شُكْرًا... ثُمَّ عَرَّفَنَا مِنْ حُدُودِ الدِّينِ الْوَاجِبِ فِيمَا لَنَا وَعَلَيْنَا، وَمَا يَجِبُ عَلَى الطَّالِبِ فِي مَسْأَلَتِهِ وَعَلَى الْمَطْلُوبِ فِي جَوَابِهِ، وَعَرَّفَنَا مِنْ مَذَاهِبِ الصَّالِحِينَ وَآدَابِ الطَّالِبِينَ مَا تَيَسَّرَ مِنَ الْقَوْلِ. 

(1) It has reached us that groups of believers and a number of religious callers said to a scholar of theirs, "You have truly emancipated us by making us aware of a matter for which gratitude is due to you in three ways: gratitude for what you have called us to, gratitude for the knowledge you have guided us to, and gratitude for the actions you have commanded us to. So explain to us from that what is incumbent upon the one who desires to show gratitude... Then teach us the obligatory limits of religion in what is for us and upon us, and what is incumbent upon the seeker in his inquiry and upon the one asked in his response. And teach us from the paths of the righteous and the etiquette of the seekers whatever is possible of speech."

**(٢) قَالَ لَهُمُ الْعَالِمُ: إِنَّ الْأَمْرَ الَّذِي دَعَوْتُكُمْ إِلَيْهِ هُوَ الَّذِي أَكْرَمَهُ اللهُ لِعِبَادِهِ وَأَكْمَلَهُ لَهُمْ وَأَكْرَمَ بِهِ مَنْ أَجَابَهُ، فَجَعَلَ لِكُلِّ أَوَّلٍ مِنْهُ آخِرًا وَلِكُلِّ آخِرٍ مِنْهُ غَايَةً وَلِكُلِّ غَايَةٍ نِهَايَةً لَا يَبْلُغُ أَمَدَهَا، وَتِلْكَ مَنَازِلُ ذَوِي الْأَلْبَابِ مَفَاتِيحُهَا ذِكْرَى، وَأَوَّلُهَا - بَلْوَى، وَأَوْسَطُهَا هُدًى، وَآخِرُهَا تَقْوَى. فَمَنْ كَانَ طَالِبًا لِعِلْمٍ مَا عَلَيْهِ بِنِيَّةِ الْأُمُورِ، وَوَقَفَ عَلَى حُدُودِ الدِّينِ الْمَقْدُورِ، قَصَدَ الْهُدَى بِمَعَالِمِ الطَّلَبِ، وَسَارَ فِيهِ بِأَحْسَنِ مَذْهَبٍ. فَأَمَّا شُكْرُ الْعَالِمِ فَطَاعَتُهُ...**

[1] Now it has been narrated to us that a number of the truly faithful (⟪ar:مؤمنین⟫) and a group among those who call people to the true religion (⟪ar:دعاۃ الدین⟫) said to one of their scholars: ‘You have liberated us by helping us learn about an affair of great importance; and for that we feel obligated to express our gratitude for three reasons:
1. ⟪ar:شکرک فیما دعوتنا الیہ⟫- Our gratitude for your having called us to the true religion (⟪ar:دین⟫)
2. ⟪ar:وشکر العِلْم الذی دللتنا علیہ⟫ - Our gratitude for the knowledge (⟪ar:علم⟫) that you pointed us towards
3. ⟪ar:وشکر العَمَل الذی أمرتنا بہ⟫- Our gratitude for the religious practices (⟪ar:عمل⟫) that you commanded us to perform

So they are asking this scholar, how do we express our gratitude for the call to religion; for the knowledge you provided; and for the actions you ordered us to perform.

{{IMG_URL:Resources/IMAGES/157/3ee10131-01b4-4655-a9da-c3593ddabc86.jpg}}

So let us know how we should express our gratitude for these three blessings. In addition:
- ⟪ar:ثم عَرَّفنا من حدود الدین⟫ - inform us about the rights and duties that are obligatory on us as directed by the ordinances of religion (⟪ar:حدود دین⟫);
- ⟪ar:وما یجب علی الطالب فی مسألتہ⟫ - tell us about the obligations of a seeker in his mode of questioning. In other words, what are the proper etiquette for seeking knowledge?
- ⟪ar:وعلی المطلوب فی جوابہ⟫ - tell us about the obligations upon the scholar in his response to our questions - how should one impart his knowledge to the one who seeks it.

So you can see it's a very Aristotelian form of thinking. This is how Aristotle would talk, and break logic down into smaller bits and then address each bit individually. So ⟪ar:سیدنا جعفر⟫ is using a similar approach in this book. So this group is asking about the obligations of religion, the student and the teacher.
- ⟪ar:وعرَّفنا من مذاھب الصالحین و آداب الطالبین⟫ - Also inform us about the ways of the righteous, and the proper behavior (⟪ar:آداب⟫) of the seekers.

So notice that right at the offset, the author is highlighting two extremely important concepts. The concepts of ⟪ar:شکر⟫ and ⟪ar:ادب⟫ which is gratitude and comportment. What is comportment? It is the way in which you carry yourself in relation to someone or something. The force that opposes these two principles, is desire.

Today we live in a time where desires have become extremely customized. Starbucks is a really good example of that. I remember there was a time when I would go to a cafe and order coffee - just coffee, and that's it. Today we go into Starbucks and, can I have a cappuccino with soy milk, whipped cream and a dash of salt and cinnamon with caramel sauce and nutmeg - Oh low fat milk please, right? It represents a diversification of the ⟪ar:نفس⟫. Every one of us has become extremely customized about our individual desires. Rasul Allah(Salallahu alayhi wa aalihee wa sallam) described the end of time as every soul will have their own specific and customized desires. That's one of the reason why we're so fragmented as people. We're all withdrawing into our own autistic type of life experiences. This is why the world today is in this state of stress and anxiety, because we're so used to getting what we want "right now"; and exactly the way I want it. Internet is not fast enough, cars are not fast enough, the color isn't right. Amazon deliveries are not fast enough. We used to be so happy about their 3 days delivery a few years ago. Then it became prime, and 2 day delivery, then same day delivery; now 1 hour delivery; and even that is not fast enough for me. I want "mind delivery". I think of something, and I want Amazon to deliver that thing to me - Actually, I and amazon to deliver it to me before I even think what I want. Our expectations have gone absolutely crazy. It's just an influx of never ending expectations, which keeps adding misery in our lives. A companion once advised his followers:
أِخشَوشَنوا فأنَّ نِعمۃ لا تَدُوم

Be tough on yourself for blessing do not last forever. It's an amazing statement. In other words, if you're always enslaving yourselves to comforts, then when life suddenly throws you out of that comfort zone, you won't be able to take it. Look at what's happening with these quarantines because of the Corona virus. People are going absolutely crazy. Not for necessities, for extravagant stuff. They're hoarding beer, coke, cookies - Toilet Paper!

People at my work are bored out of their mind, because they're stuck in their house. I believe the word used was "imprisoned". In their luxurious, comfortable houses, with heating and water supply; and food stacked up to the roof. With their libraries of books they never read; with high speed internet - they feel imprisoned. They have all these luxuries and yet they can't stop complaining. Why? Because every thing, every desire, every hobby, every meal is so customized - cause they can't go to Starbucks; cause they can't go their favorite restaurant, and order their food with the exact toppings and condiments they want - right? And look at how everyone is to his own self right now - ⟪ar:نفسی نفسی⟫.

This is why our religion is so focused on ⟪ar:ایثار⟫, helping others, because it gets people out of the traps of their egocentricity. Because when you're less egocentric, not only is there a benefit for yourself,you make life easy for others as well - when you move out of the constraints of the self. Egocentricity makes life miserable for everybody. This is why servitude, or serving others, rather than a constant desire of self-service, is what makes life pleasurable. Just being grateful for what we have; and sharing what we have with others as an expression of our gratitude, right?

They did an experiment where they hooked people up to wires and asked them to do charity work, and what they found, was that all the pleasures zones in their brain fired up as they performed those acts of charity. Because that's the way we are designed. We are designed as ⟪ar:عبد⟫for the task of ⟪ar:عبودیۃ⟫- servants created for the task of service. Servitude is what makes life more enjoyable as an experience - servitude to Allah being the greatest of all delights. It is not the satisfaction of the self. That's the illusion of happiness. *If I can just satisfy myself I will be happy*- that's an illusion. Real happiness is in serving others. In philosophy, they call it the "hedonistic principle"; because its a paradox. Why do people feel happy when they serve others?! It's counter-intuitive. I'm giving away, I'm losing my possessions, so why is it making me happy?

This is where ⟪ar:ادب⟫comes into play. ⟪ar:ادب⟫is basically prioritizing someone else over the self. The greatest crisis of our time is this complete disregard for ⟪ar:ادب⟫. But that's what ⟪ar:ادب⟫is - prioritizing, respecting someone or something over the self. So if you think about the ⟪ar:ادآب⟫of eating, you'll see that the process has been designed to respect and prioritize that sustenance over the self. The process is designed to humble the self in relation to the blessing of food. Because I'm paying them, right?

# Gratitude

This is why I love this book so much; because it's such a great reminder of humility. Look how the very first concern this group has is about ⟪ar:اداب⟫ and ⟪ar:شکر⟫- how do we express our gratitude towards you as a teacher; how do we express our gratitude for the knowledge you have given us; and how do we express our gratitude for the actions deriving from that knowledge.

[2] The scholar replied: ‘Now the affair to which I invite you, is the religion that Allah has bestowed upon his servants as an honor. A religion, that he has perfected for them and through which he honors whoever responds to Him.

We have absolutely no idea, what an honor this religion is. How blessed we are to be born as Muslims; how blessed we are to be born ⟪ar:مؤمن⟫. Really! We just take it for granted. When I say "we" I include myself in it first. Really! I'm not being humble - I really mean it. All these deviations that I'm talking about, I'm thinking about myself. So this is as much a reminder for me as it is for you. But we don't really appreciate the fact that Allah considered us worthy of Islam; He considered us worthy of submission and servitude to Him. He who does not need worship, submission or servitude. But we look at Islam as a curse. Oh man! prayers again! ⟪ar:شیطان⟫has completely distorted our perspective of how beautiful this religion is. So the scholar says, Allah bestowed this religion as an honor upon his slaves.

⟪ar:فجعل لکل أول منہ آخر ؛ و لکل آخر منہ غایۃ ؛ و لکل غایۃٍ نھایۃ ؛ لا یبلغ أِمْدَھا⟫

So for every beginning (⟪ar:أول⟫), he has placed within it an end (⟪ar:آخر⟫); and for every end he designed a goal (⟪ar:نہایۃ⟫). Each goal has a limit (⟪ar:غایۃ⟫) whose full extent cannot be attained.

So for instance we're reading this book. This story has a beginning and it has an end. The purpose of this story, as we move from the beginning to end is in achieving its goal - grasping the moral of the story. But none of us will be able to get to the furthest depth of its moral. Cause we can dive only as deep as we can hold our breadth, right? So this is a very profound statement!

⟪ar:فجعل لکل أول منہ آخر ؛ و لکل آخر منہ غایۃ ؛ و لکل غایۃٍ نھایۃ ؛ لا یبلغ أِمْدَھا⟫

Every beginning has an end; every end has a goal; every goal has a limit whose full extent cannot be reached.

Same is true for Allah's religion. It has a beginning (life), it has an end (death); for this short span of time there is a goal - the goal of ⟪ar:توحید⟫recognizing our master and lord. That recognition has a limit a , ⟪ar:نھایۃ⟫whose furthest limits can never be attained. The word ⟪ar:نھایۃ⟫means the nth degree. In Urdu we say نہایت ہی شریف آدمی ہے؛ نہایت کی بد تمیز آدمی ہے . What does that mean? This man cannot be anymore decent; or he cannot be anymore بد تمیز. He's reached the final stage of being a بد تمیز. He is basically بد تمیزیin the flesh. That's ⟪ar:نھایۃ⟫.

These are (the ⟪ar:منازل⟫) the way-stations of the people of true understanding. That goal is their destination - that's where they're headed; that's what they're trying to achieve. The key to success is a constant remembrance Allah (⟪ar:ذکر اللہ⟫).

⟪ar:وأولھا بلوی، وأوسطھا ھدی و آخرھا تقوی⟫

Their initial levels are trials (⟪ar:اولھا بلوی⟫); their middle state is that of right guidance (⟪ar:اوسطھا ھدی⟫) and their final stage is piety (⟪ar:آخرھا تقوی⟫).

Now he's describing what to expect when we walk on this path of religion. He says the initial stage is going to be ⟪ar:بلوی⟫trials. Because this very customized ⟪ar:نفس⟫is going to rebel. It's going to get in the way, like a wild horse refusing to be tamed. It'll experience a loss of desire, and it will push you towards doing what its used to; towards what you want, rather than what Allah want. That is the stage of trial. But then, once you've succeed in taming this wild horse of the self, the next stage will be ⟪ar:ھدی⟫- you will find guidance. You will begin experiencing the benefits of that guidance. The final stage he says, is going to be ⟪ar:تقوی⟫- a constant and a deep rooted awareness of the presence of Allah in your life.

Now the **tool** - that will help you persevere in this journey, and get you through these stages of trial, guidance and piety is ⟪ar:ذکر اللہ⟫- the remembrance of Allah. That is the key to success.

{{IMG_URL:Resources/IMAGES/157/132e78ee-93dd-4ae8-822e-2e9ca61e4dff.jpg}}

So whoever is seeking to learn the basics of Allah's affairs with the truest of intentions, and then discovers the ranks of the divinely determined (⟪ar:حدود الدین المقدور⟫), he has sought guidance from the correct sources and has set upon it in the best possible manner.

So now he's talking about seeking guidance from the right sources; and sincere intentions. You can't be guided if you follow just anyone and everyone. You first have to determine that your guide himself knows the way. If your guide is lost, and doesn't know the way; or he simply thinks he knows the way; he's going to end up misguiding you with him. But then also sincerity. Even if you find the right source but your intentions are not pure - you're not seeking knowledge to improve yourself, then finding the right source is not going to help either. The scholar continues:

- As for showing gratitude to the **scholar**(the teacher who guided you), that is through his **obedience**;
- As for the gratitude for the **knowledge**(that he gave you), is (⟪ar:عمل⟫) **turning it into action** and calling others to it.
- and the gratitude for the **right practice** (he taught you), is (⟪ar:صبر⟫)**striving patiently** in it and calling others to it. To be consistent with the practice.

So what he's saying is that if you wish to truly express your gratitude to your teacher, its by obeying him. Not just a verbal thank you, or flowery speeches about how you've changed my life - No! To really show gratitude to your teacher, you must obey him. The gratitude for knowledge is turning it into action; and the gratitude for action is being consistent with it; persevering patiently in it. Not giving up when the going gets tough. Cause the spiritual guide is like the doctor who prescribes a medication for our illness. Now I can praise the doctor and his skills all I want - that's not going to cure me. What's going to cure me is doing what he tells me to do; refraining from doing what he has prohibited me to do. Taking the prescribed medication in the right amount at the right times, for the prescribed duration - being consistent with it, otherwise without consistency, all effort will be wasted.

[3] As for teaching you the ways of the righteous and the proper behavior of the seekers (⟪ar:آداب الطالبین⟫), the following story has been narrated to us about a man from Persia who was among those, subject to the trials of spiritual ignorance. # In search of disciple

So now the author is narrating this story to these seekers of knowledge (and us of course). It's a story about an academically well educated man; who realizes the weight of his spiritual ignorance, and sets out to quench his thirst. He finds many people claiming to be true scholars, but none are able to satisfy him (and that's why he refers to them as mirages - having the illusion of water; the illusion of knowledge). But he does not give up his struggles, until he finds the one who has the answers to his questions - here the implication is for the true Imam of that time.

{{IMG_URL:Resources/IMAGES/157/223c072a-4708-4c75-bde6-f35c5ca0c7d5.jpg}}

Then Allah honored his abode and removed the veil from his intellect (through this teacher). He found him astray, so he guided him; He found him in need, so He satisfied him with knowledge. Thus by his teacher's grace, this man eventually became one of the great scholars of Sinai. He became an angelic dwellers in the well-populated temple. (⟪ar:بیت المعمور⟫)

{{IMG_URL:Resources/IMAGES/157/6e4371d5-082b-4b79-bd45-233a8d2064bd.jpg}}

[4] Finally when his guidance had been completed and he reached the goals of his aspirations, he felt duty-bound to show gratitude to his Master and to exert himself for his Lord. He used to hear his spiritual father repeat a proverb, which held the rank of soul to his body.

In other words, that proverb served as the essence and the ultimate reason for all his efforts.
ان افضل الحسنات أحیا الأموات

The most excellent of deeds is giving life to the dead. What this means is that the most excellent of all deeds is imparting knowledge to the ignorant. Because the ignorant is like a corpse. It cannot move itself, it's moved by others. Without the soul, it rots away, right?

{{IMG_URL:Resources/IMAGES/157/dd86bfa9-be04-4065-a1c0-ea2117c9bcb2.jpg}}

So he said to himself: ‘I used to be dead, and my master gave me life; I was ignorant, and he gave me knowledge. I am not the first person to ever be ignorant, such that I attained knowledge before everyone else; nor am I the last one to be ignorant, such that the process of spiritual teaching has come to an end on me. Therefore out of gratitude for this blessing, it is only appropriate for me to pass on this divine trust to those who come after me; just as those who preceded me have handed it down to me.

Look how he's defying his ego. I'm not special. I wasn't the first to receive this knowledge; nor am I the last. I have been given this blessing, so now I must serve others; I must help others who are seeking this same knowledge. Not, I must lead others. Not, I must hoard it so I can be superior to others, right?

{{IMG_URL:Resources/IMAGES/157/c921adb7-5d64-413c-8be3-b0f3f7d3bb18.jpg}}

For the origin of this affair is from Allah, and it only reached me through its many intermediaries (⟪ar:اسباب⟫), the first passing it on to the second, the second passing it on to the third,

⟪ar:حتی اذا ھوی من الملاء الاعلی الی الخلائق من اھل الدنیا⟫

until it descended from the heavenly host to the creatures of this lower world.

⟪ar:ثم خرق الحجاب وتدافعتہ الابواب الی ان وصل الیَّ، فأکون أنا غایتہ ومُنتھاہُ؟⟫

After that, did all this knowledge rip through the veil between Allah and his creations, and did the ‘intermediaries’ pass it onward until it reached me, only so that I could be its final goal and ultimate end?

This is so amazing how he's getting his own ⟪ar:نفس⟫out of the way. In other words, he's asking himself, Am I really that special that this entire flow of knowledge was sent through all these means and channels so that it could end on me?

{{IMG_URL:Resources/IMAGES/157/271ef0b5-00ee-458c-a493-d79037c8abc7.jpg}}

No! not at all! For those who have transmitted it and handed on this trust before me are more deserving of precedence and spiritual gains! What they have earned does not belong to me, so that I could rely on it. No! in fact I (myself) am part of what they have earned through their acts of teaching me

In other words, their teaching me, and my learning from them is part of their effort - not mine.
