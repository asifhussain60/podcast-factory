# Anti-Patterns — Socratic Dialogue (Question-and-Answer)

This document catalogs authoring failures specific to the socratic-dialogue archetype.
Each anti-pattern is flagged by the challenger and blocks episode approval.

---

## Dialogue register

**Anti-pattern: Hosts summarising the Q&A instead of inhabiting the inquiry.**
"In this exchange, the questioner asks about [topic] and the respondent explains…"
is not an episode — it is a paraphrase. The hosts must be present inside the inquiry
mode: asking genuine questions, engaging the answers, naming what each response does
and doesn't resolve.

**Anti-pattern: Treating the questioner as naive.**
In Socratic dialogue within the Islamic philosophical tradition, the questioner is not
a student asking for basic information — the questioner is a skilled interlocutor whose
questions are designed to force the respondent to reveal their full position. Episodes
must treat the questioner as an active intellectual agent, not a convenient foil.

**Anti-pattern: Conflating questioner and respondent roles mid-episode.**
The inquiry register depends on clarity about who is asking and who is answering. When
hosts alternate between both roles without distinguishing them, the dialogue structure
collapses into a two-person essay.

---

## Arc structure

**Anti-pattern: Episode ending before the doctrinal answer is delivered.**
Socratic dialogues in this tradition are engineered to arrive at a doctrinal position.
An episode that ends at a complication — before [respondent]'s final answer — leaves
the listener in the wrong place. If the exchange is too long for one episode, the split
must happen at a natural inquiry pause, not mid-answer.

**Anti-pattern: Omitting the inquiry pivot.**
Every substantial dialogue has a moment where the original question is reframed. Missing
this pivot — treating all exchanges as equally weighted — flattens the architecture
[Author] built into the dialogue.

---

## Citation handling

**Anti-pattern: Doctrinal claims left unattributed.**
Socratic dialogue in the Ismaili and classical Islamic philosophical tradition is
citation-dense. When [respondent] makes a doctrinal claim, the hosts must name the
source the claim rests on — Quran, hadith, prior philosophical authority — or flag that
[Author] is making an independent argument.

**Anti-pattern: Arabic speaker labels dropped raw into TTS.**
Every named figure in the dialogue must be given a stable English role label on first
occurrence: "The seeker, [Arabic name], asks…" — thereafter "the seeker" in the TTS
layer. Unsubstituted Arabic names in TTS produce pronunciation failures that break the
inquiry register.
