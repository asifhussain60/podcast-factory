# Anti-Patterns — Narrative Prose (Literary Immersion)

This document catalogs authoring failures specific to the narrative-prose archetype.
Each anti-pattern is flagged by the challenger and blocks episode approval.

---

## Narrative register

**Anti-pattern: Summarising scenes instead of inhabiting them.**
"In this scene, [character] does X and then Y" is a plot summary, not an episode.
The hosts must be present in the narrative moment — speaking from inside the scene's
tension, not reporting on it from the outside.

**Anti-pattern: Breaking the immersive register with biographical asides.**
"[Author], who was born in [year] and died in [year]…" is not enrichment for a narrative
episode. It is a register break. Biographical context belongs in the series introduction
episode, not scattered through the narrative arc.

**Anti-pattern: Condensing descriptive passages to bullet points.**
[Author]'s descriptive prose is not padding — it is how the story works. Summarising
"the long description of [place]" strips the content that carries meaning. Engage the
description analytically: what is it doing in the narrative?

---

## Arc structure

**Anti-pattern: Ending before a narrative arc closes.**
If an episode starts a scene, it must land the scene's resolution (or deliberate
irresolution if the author intends a cliff-hanger). Episodes that stop mid-scene
to hit a runtime target break the listener's immersion and signal poor episode planning.

**Anti-pattern: Using episode boundaries as chapter boundaries.**
The narrative's chapter breaks are not necessarily the right episode breaks. Episodes
should follow dramatic arcs — scenes, movements, turning points — not the author's
typographic divisions.

---

## Character handling

**Anti-pattern: Arabic character names dropped raw into TTS.**
"Ḥāmid and Zaynab then…" creates pronunciation stumbles that break immersion. Every
character with a non-English name must be given a stable English label on first
occurrence: "The master, Ḥāmid, and his student, Zaynab…" — thereafter "the master"
and "the student" in the TTS layer.

**Anti-pattern: Characters described rather than encountered.**
Telling the listener that a character is "wise" or "conflicted" is a summary. Show the
character's wisdom or conflict through what they do and say in the text — let the
listener arrive at the characterisation themselves.
