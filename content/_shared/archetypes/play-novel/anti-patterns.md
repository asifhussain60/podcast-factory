# Anti-Patterns — Play-Novel (Dramatic Dialogue)

This document catalogs authoring failures specific to the play-novel archetype.
Each anti-pattern is flagged by the challenger and blocks episode approval.

---

## Host behavior

**Anti-pattern: Narrating the drama instead of inhabiting it.**
Hosts that summarize a scene in third-person voice ("and then the character said")
flatten the dramatic register into a book-report. The episode must stay inside
the encounter — hosts respond to the argument, not to its stage directions.

**Anti-pattern: Breaking character mid-scene.**
One host speaking as an omniscient commentator while the other inhabits a
character creates tonal whiplash. Once the dramatic mode opens, both hosts stay
in the analytical-encounter register until the scene closes.

**Anti-pattern: Ending before dramatic resolution.**
Cutting an episode at an act break rather than a dramatic resolution leaves
listeners without the arc payoff. Each episode must reach a settled stance,
even if partial, before closing.

---

## Citation

**Anti-pattern: Footnote-style citation inside a character's mouth.**
Dramatic characters do not cite sources mid-sentence. Scholarly attribution
belongs in the host framing (before or after the encounter), never in dialogue
assigned to a named character.

**Anti-pattern: Tier-4 hadith citations in dramatic scenes.**
Dramatic texts rarely require tier-4 hadith; inserting them signals genre
confusion. If a citation is essential, it must be voiced by the host narrator
before or after the scene, not woven into the dialogue.

---

## Register

**Anti-pattern: Arabic character names dropped raw into TTS.**
An Arabic name uttered with English TTS phonemes breaks dramatic immersion.
Every character must receive a stable English label established in the first
episode and used consistently thereafter.

**Anti-pattern: Anachronistic idiom inside historical dialogue.**
Phrases like "let's unpack this" or "moving forward" spoken by a 10th-century
character destroy historical credibility. The character's diction must remain
consistent with the source tradition's register.

**Anti-pattern: Scene-break treated as episode boundary.**
Episodes follow dramatic arcs, not the physical scene divisions of the source
text. Splitting an episode at a scene heading without completing the arc
produces an unsatisfying listening experience.

**Anti-pattern: Hosts re-narrating scenes already dramatized.**
If a dramatic encounter has been performed, hosts must not then summarize what
just happened. The scene speaks for itself; the host's role is analytical
framing before and synthesis after.
