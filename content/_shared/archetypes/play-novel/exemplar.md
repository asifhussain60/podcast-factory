# Exemplar Episode — Play-Novel (Dramatic Dialogue)

This document shows a well-formed episode structure for the play-novel archetype.
It is illustrative, not prescriptive — adapt framing language to the specific text.

---

## Opening

Host A frames the dramatic encounter in analytical terms:
> "In the third act of *[Source Title]*, [Character A] and [Character B] meet
> at the exact moment the argument reaches its breaking point. Their exchange
> over [core doctrine/question] is not a debate — it's a collision. Today we
> sit inside that collision and watch what it reveals."

Host B grounds the dramatic stakes:
> "What makes this encounter worth two thousand years of commentary is that
> neither character is wrong in the way we'd expect. [Character A] is not the
> villain. The disagreement is architectural — two different accounts of what
> [concept] even is."

---

## Discussion

The hosts move through the encounter in scene-arc order:

**Scene 1 — Setup:** Frame the characters' positions before the argument begins.
Neither host summarizes the scene in hindsight. They speak from inside it.

**Scene 2 — Collision:** The central disagreement surfaces. Hosts give each
position its strongest form, citing the source text directly (in translation).
No Tier-4 hadith here; dramatic texts carry their own authority.

**Scene 3 — Consequence:** One position yields — or the encounter closes without
resolution. Hosts name what shifted and why it matters for the tradition's arc.

*Transition example between scenes:*
> Host A: "But that's where [Character B] refuses to follow the logic."
> Host B: "Because the premise has already been conceded — and [Character A]
> knows it."

---

## Closing

Host synthesis returns to the analytical register:
> "What this scene does is expose [central tension in the tradition]. It doesn't
> resolve it — the text doesn't want to resolve it. The point is the exposure."

Host B offers the listener a next-step orientation:
> "In the next episode we enter [next dramatic unit]. The cast is the same; the
> stakes are higher. The argument [Character A] lost here becomes the argument
> [Character B] has to defend."

*No premature closure.* Do not end with "so ultimately the answer is…" —
the dramatic register forbids it. If the encounter closes without resolution,
say so explicitly and let the tension stand.
