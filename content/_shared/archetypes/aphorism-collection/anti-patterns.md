# Anti-Patterns — Aphorism Collection (Wisdom Sayings)

This document catalogs authoring failures specific to the aphorism-collection archetype.
Each anti-pattern is flagged by the challenger and blocks episode approval.

---

## Register

**Anti-pattern: Flattening the aphorism into a self-help lesson.**
"This saying teaches us to live more mindfully" is not an explication — it is a
category error. Classical wisdom literature is doctrinal, not therapeutic. The episode
must locate the aphorism in its theological or ethical tradition, not convert it into
an action item.

**Anti-pattern: Paraphrasing instead of explicating.**
Restating the aphorism in plainer words adds nothing. The hosts' contribution is to
locate the aphorism's doctrinal resonance — what Quran verse it echoes, what classical
debate it enters, what practice it counsels and why that practice matters in this
tradition.

---

## Structure

**Anti-pattern: Reading aphorism numbers aloud.**
"Saying number 47 states…" is not how wisdom literature is heard. Group aphorisms
thematically; introduce each cluster by the question it circles, not by its position
in the collection.

**Anti-pattern: Sequential episode structure mirroring the book's order.**
A collection of 200 sayings should not produce 200 episodes in book order. Episodes
follow thematic clusters — groups of sayings that triangulate the same question. The
episode design must do the editorial work of finding those clusters.

**Anti-pattern: Episode ending without a forward question.**
Wisdom collections are designed to be pondered, not consumed. Every episode must end
with a question that invites the listener to sit with the theme — and that the next
episode will enter from a different angle.

---

## Citation handling

**Anti-pattern: Missing the Quran or hadith echo.**
Many aphorisms in this tradition are implicit commentaries on canonical texts. If the
hosts don't name the echo, the listener has no way to hear the depth of the saying.
Every aphorism that echoes a Quran verse or hadith must have that echo named and briefly
explained.

**Anti-pattern: Biographical padding as enrichment.**
"This saying was composed during [Author]'s exile in [place]" is not doctrinal context.
Biographical facts are decoration here. The enrichment the listener needs is doctrinal:
what theological position does this saying take, and who in the tradition disagrees?
