# The Concept of Tawheed

## Pre-Islamic Conditions

When the seventh century began, merciful conceptions of the divine gradually deteriorated into satanic ones. The monotheistic concept of God fragmented into polytheism. Since the Arabs believed that God controlled all benefit and harm, they came to worship whatever seemed to profit or injure them. In this way, a form of "Hindu" religion took root among the Meccans, and they fashioned countless idols—tradition records 360,000 in number. Over time, they recognized the impracticality of such abundance and reduced their pantheon to 365 idols, one for each day of the solar year, worshipping a different deity daily. Of these, three held special prominence: ⟪ar:لات⟫, ⟪ar:منات⟫, and ⟪ar:عزۃ⟫ (the goddess). Some authorities report that a temple was erected in Ṭāʾif wherein ⟪ar:'عزۃ'⟫ was venerated, while idols—notably that of ⟪ar:ھُبُل⟫—stood upon the roof of the Kaʿba. Contemporary Hindu devotion preserves this triad in altered form: ⟪ar:برھما⟫ as the Creator, ⟪ar:وشنو⟫ as the Sustainer, and ⟪ar:شوا⟫ as the Destroyer.[^cite-1]

To remedy this spiritual disease, the Almighty dispatched a Messenger to each age. Adam, Noah, Abraham, Moses, and Jesus all came with the summons of divine unity. When Jesus proclaimed monotheism, he communicated the doctrine of God's oneness to his community in three modes; yet his disciples went astray, reverting to polytheism and fragmenting the Godhead into three parts—Father, Son, and ⟪ar:روح القدس⟫ (the Holy Spirit). In truth, Jesus addressed his Imām as "Father" because the Imām dwells in the spiritual realm of causation, and Jesus occupied the station of effect; his disciples, however, misunderstood. They assumed that if Jesus called someone "Father," then Jesus himself must be a "Son." And when Jesus declared, "A revelation comes down upon me," his followers mistakenly accepted the angel-messenger as divine. Thus was established ⟪ar:'تثلیث'⟫—the doctrine of trinitarian godhead.

After Jesus's era came a period when this trinity seemed untenable. The world appeared divided into only two principles: good and evil. This dualism crystallized, and the concept of two gods emerged: ⟪ar:'اھرمن'⟫ (⟪ar:اھرمن⟫), the deity of malevolence, and ⟪ar:'یزداں'⟫ (⟪ar:یزداں⟫), the God of all goodness.[^cite-2]

⟪ar:پیغمبر علیہ السلام⟫ Before the Prophet's advent, the Arabian peninsula had sunk into profound moral darkness. There existed no vice, however grave, that the Arabs did not perpetrate. Their ignorance was such that in years of abundant dates, they would fashion an idol from the surplus, worship it, and prostrate before it throughout the year. When the harvest failed, they would smash the same idol and distribute and consume it. The Arab nation had descended into utter barbarism. They devoured crocodile and elephant flesh, roasted goat-skin with goat meat, burned enemies alive or slew them, flayed their corpses, fractured skulls, and drank wine from them. Women stained their garments with the blood of the slain. The enemy's camel was tethered to his grave and left to starve until death. They murdered pregnant women and buried alive the daughters of their foes, and used enemy children as targets for sport. The Arabs had become so brutal that their very prayers expressed this savagery:

> ⟪ar-quote:یا ربِّ فلا  تجعل حیاتی مَذِلَّۃ ولا موتَتِی بَینَ النِّسآء نوائِحِ⟫
>
> "O Lord, do not render my life shameful, nor let my death be lamented by women around my bed."

> ⟪ar-quote:ولاکن قَتیلاً  تَمزقُ الطیر حولَہُ و تَشرُبُ غِربانُ الدَّم من جوارح⟫
>
> Rather, grant me death in battle—that birds tear at my corpse and ravens drink blood from my limbs.

Women held no place in society, treated as livestock. Enslaved persons were wholly subject to their masters' dominion, their very lives hanging by a thread. Adultery, gambling, and carnal pleasure had reached their zenith; brothers married sisters, sons their mothers. Poetry recounted depravities and indecencies without shame.

In religion, the Arabs were unequivocal polytheists. Goddesses and divine powers were venerated in every city, hamlet, and tribe. The Kaʿba stood adorned with 360 idols, each representing the god of a different clan. For instance, ⟪ar:'لات'⟫ belonged to the tribes of Ṭāʾif; the goddess of ʿUzza, Quraysh, and Kinānah; and Manāt to the tribes of ʿUthmān and Khazraj. All these idols bore a relic—called Hubul—that was positioned atop the Kaʿba. The Kaʿba's walls displayed images of Abraham and Ishmael, Mary and Jesus. The people of Ḥimyar (Yemen) worshipped the sun; the people of Canaan, the moon. Human destiny was deemed determined by the motions and influences of the stars.[^cite-3]

## Three Methods of Knowledge

Scholars of wisdom affirm that the intellect apprehends reality through three pathways:

**The Five Senses**

The first method—⟪ar:حواس خمسۃ⟫ (the five senses)—comprehends the principles of things through the powers of sight, hearing, smell, taste, and tactile sensation. All objects possessing corporeal extension are perceived through ⟪ar:حواس خمسۃ⟫.

**Intellect**

The second pathway is ⟪ar:عقل⟫ (intellect), which operates through the faculties mentioned above. Through intellect, we ascend from ⟪ar:محسوسات⟫ (sensible particulars) to ⟪ar:معقولات⟫ (intelligible universals); we infer absent things from the attributes of present ones. For example, observing smoke, we deduce the existence of fire somewhere nearby.

**Logical Reasoning and Argumentation**

The third method apprehends an object through rational arguments and proofs, wherein both ⟪ar:عقل⟫ and ⟪ar:حواس خمسۃ⟫ collaborate. Here we endeavor to comprehend realities transcending the domain of particulars and their attributes through logical and philosophical demonstration—the method of the sage.

**Proving Axioms through Sensible Evidence and Inference**

⟪ar:مبدع تعالی⟫ (the Originator, Most High) belongs to a category that ⟪ar:حواس خمسۃ⟫ cannot perceive, and since no attribute adheres to His essence, it is impossible to say ⟪ar:تشبیح⟫ (that He resembles created things). For this reason, comprehension of Him through intellect alone proves impossible. His existence can only be established through logical arguments and demonstrative proofs wherein both ⟪ar:عقل⟫ and ⟪ar:حواس خمسۃ⟫ are engaged.[^cite-4] In this manner, we may acknowledge the existence of the Originator through rational demonstration and philosophy, yet we cannot truly know Him.

## What Is Knowledge of the Originator, Most High?

To know ⟪ar:مبدع تعالی⟫ is to understand His ⟪ar:توحید⟫ (unity); His monotheism is the appellation for His absolute distinction from creation. Such knowledge is knowing that it is impossible to know Him. Whatever enters the imagination contradicts His reality. There exists no equivalent, no parallel. Tongues fall mute before His attributes; intellects become helpless in His comprehension. Whoever presumes to articulate His essence speaks in ignorance; whoever remains silent appears foolish. Yet there is only Allāh—inescapable and unknowable. Whoever imagined he had grasped Him made a baseless claim. Whatever we imagine of Him, no matter how apparently true to our minds, shall be negated by His reality, and in our conceptualizations there appears only a creature fashioned in our own image.

The sound principle of knowing and perceiving an object is that a correlation must subsist between the object and the perceiver. Perception is impossible without such correspondence. There is, for example, a correlation between the visible and the eye; between what is heard and the ear; between scent and ⟪ar:قوت ریحیہ⟫ (the olfactory power); between what is smelt and the faculty of smell; between taste and ⟪ar:قوت ذائقہ⟫ (the gustatory power); between what is tasted and taste; and between touch and ⟪ar:قوت حسیہ⟫ (the tactile power).

If we were to assume that Allāh could fall within our senses and imaginations, and that we might know Him, this would necessitate establishing a correspondence between Allāh and the intellect through which knowledge might arise. Accepting this premise, we would be forced to believe in divine influence flowing between Allāh and every human being—and thus compelled to believe that ⟪ar:(نعوذ باللہ) اللہ⟫ (God forbid, that God) dwells in every soul.

⟪ar:غیب الغیوب⟫ (the Hidden of Hiddens) transcends even this. Our hearts dare not conceive of it; our rational faculty ⟪ar:ناطقۃ⟫ (the speaking soul) is bereft of its comprehension. ⟪ar:مولانا علی⟫ (Our Master ʿAlī) therefore declared:[^cite-5]

| ⟪ar-quote:اَلعِجزُ عَن دَرَکِ اِلأدرَاکِہِ اِدراکٌ⟫ | ⟪ar-quote:وَالبَحثُ عَن سِرِّ ذاَتِ السِّرِّ اِشرَاکٌ⟫ |
| --- | --- |
| The incapacity to perceive Him is itself a form of perception. | And to inquire after the secret of the Essence-that-is-Secret is to commit association with Him (shirk). |

All jinn and mankind, after submitting in their knowledge of Him, remain devoted to the knowledge of His creations. The servant of Allāh is he whom the Almighty has granted courage to understand the creation of the heavens and earth, and to whom He has guided toward this knowledge. Neither our thoughts can encompass Him, nor can any name or attribute be ascribed to Him. We possess no avenue of comprehension save to affirm that there exists an Originator of all these worlds, and to acknowledge that all intellects stand weak before His creation—that is, neither can we comprehend Him, nor can any intellect above us.

The Messenger of Allāh declared: ⟪ar-quote:وَاِذاَ بَلَّغتَ اِلیَ اللّٰہِ تَعاَلیٰ فَسکُتُو⟫ ("When you attain knowledge of Allāh the Most High, be silent"), for to the degree that you approach understanding the Almighty, your Allāh becomes truly Allah. ⟪ar:سیدنا مؤید الشیرازی⟫ (Our Master al-Muʾayyad al-Shīrāzī) taught: "Monotheism is not knowledge of Allāh Himself, but knowledge of His limits."

Allāh proclaims in the Holy Qurʾān:

> ⟪quran 3:18⟫
> ⟪ar:‏شَهِدَ ٱللَّهُ أَنَّهُۥ لَآ إِلَهَ إِلَّا هُوَ وَٱلْمَلَٓئِكَةُ وَأُو۟لُوا۟ ٱلْعِلْمِ قَآئِمًۢا بِٱلْقِسْطِ ۚ لَآ إِلَهَ إِلَّا هُوَ ٱلْعَزِيزُ ٱلْحَكِيمُ ‎⟫
>
> *Allah bears witness that La ilaha illa Huwa (none has the right to be worshipped but He), and the angels, and those possessing knowledge, bearing witness with justice. La ilaha illa Huwa (none has the right to be worshipped but He), the All-Mighty, the All-Wise.*

This verse reveals that ⟪ar:اللہ⟫ comprises one category and ⟪ar:ھو⟫ comprises another. ⟪ar:ھو⟫ is a word of reference pointing to a creature of ours, and therefore it is improper to attribute it to ⟪ar:مبدع تعالی⟫. Yet due to our humility and emptiness of essence, we were constrained to employ it—⟪ar:ھو⟫. Both ⟪ar:سیدنا مؤید (ھبۃ اللہ بن موسیٰ الشیرازی⟫ (Our Master al-Muʾayyad, Hibat Allāh ibn Mūsā al-Shīrāzī) and ⟪ar:سیدنا حمید الدین کرمانی⟫ (Our Master al-Ḥamīd al-Dīn al-Kirmānī) stated: "We stand ensnared in bondage and confined by the straits of our contingency. When we attempt to recall You without that which You transcend, we point to You perforce—a sin, yet connected to forgiveness."[^cite-6]

Thus ⟪ar:سیدنا مؤید⟫ (Our Master al-Muʾayyad) in ⟪ar:کتاب کنز الولد⟫ (the *Book of the Treasure of the Child*) supplicates Allāh for forgiveness, saying:

O Allāh, I beseech Your pardon for that which is bound in ⟪ar:عبودیت⟫ (servitude) and whose scope is narrow. When I made mention of You with words unworthy of You—for I was constrained to remember You, though obligated to invoke You—this is a sin joined only to my forgiveness.

## Knowledge of the Originator, Most High, through Logical Proofs

### That Verb Is Absolute

Everything in this cosmos divides into ⟪ar:'فاعل'⟫ (the agent, the doer) and ⟪ar:'مفعول'⟫ (the patient, that upon which action is performed); that is, all reality operates according to causality. Rain is the effect of cloud-roar; the cloud results from vapor-ascent; vapor arises from rivers and waters. In this chain, every entity becomes the agent in one relation and the patient in another. If we trace this causal sequence backward, we arrive necessarily at the First Agent—that which has never been the patient of another cause. This is ⟪ar:'مبدع تعالی'⟫ (the Originator, Most High). The masses recognize this First ⟪ar:'فعل'⟫ (action) as ⟪ar:'اللہ'⟫ (Allāh).

Three requirements obtain for anything to exercise or receive effect:

| Agent | Action | Patient |
| --- | --- |--- |
| ⟪ar:'فاعل'⟫ | ⟪ar:'فعل'⟫ | ⟪ar:'مفعول'⟫ |

⟪ar:'فاعل'⟫ is that which produces effect and becomes a cause; ⟪ar:'مفعول'⟫ is that which receives effect; ⟪ar:'فعل'⟫ is the action itself. Thus, if we consider the Creator of the Almighty to be merely a knower, He manifests three stations: the knower, who possesses knowledge; the knowledge itself, of which He is cognizant; and the known, which He possesses. If Allāh is ⟪ar:'عالم مطلق'⟫ (an absolute knower), then the universe must first exist such that He may know it. This would mean He became dependent upon the universe for His station as knower of the universe—as though the universe preceded Him in being, upon which His knowledge depends. These three stations apply to all other divine attributes:

| Agent | Action | Patient |
| --- | --- | --- |
| ʿAlim (Knower) | ʿIlm (Knowledge) | Maʿlūm (Known) |
| Qādir (Powerful) | Qudra (Power) | Maqdūr (Empowered) |
| Raḥīm (Merciful) | Raḥma (Mercy) | Marḥūm (Object of Mercy) |

There is a distinction between ⟪ar:'عالم'⟫ (a knower, one among knowers) and ⟪ar:'عالم مطلق'⟫ (the Absolute Knower). To understand the Creator alone as ⟪ar:'عالم'⟫ constitutes shirk (association), for if He were merely a knower, we too are knowers—though our knowledge is inferior to His. ⟪ar:'عالم مطلق'⟫ denotes one whose knowledge depends upon no cause or effect.[^cite-7]

### None of Its Partners

⟪ar:مبدع تعالی⟫ has no partner. Should we hypothetically assume a partner in Allāh, by the method of proof by contradiction, four possibilities arise regarding their respective powers:

**Both Flawed in Their Nature**

If both were defective by nature, existence itself would be impossible—hence this condition cannot obtain.

**Both Perfect in Their Nature**

If both were perfect and equal in nature with no difference or opposition, the existence of one would be superfluous and redundant. Since all power manifests completely from one God alone, this condition is impossible.

**One Perfect, One Flawed**

If one is defective and its partner fully powerful, the defective one is unnecessary. Therefore this situation cannot subsist.

**Both Deficient in Their Natures, Each Completing the Other**

If either of the two is defective by nature, both stand proven defective. For if both attain perfection only through mutual aid, then each individually is imperfect. It is impossible for Allāh to be imperfect.

All these proofs establish that Allāh is One, perfect in power, and has no partner.[^cite-8]

### He Is One, But Not in Terms of Count

⟪ar:مبدع تعالی⟫ is one, yet not in numerical terms. That is, when viewed through counting, a distinction arises: there exist ⟪ar:عاد⟫ (counters) and ⟪ar:معدود⟫ (the counted). ⟪ar:مفعول⟫ (the patient, the effect). If ⟪ar:مبدع تعالی⟫ were taken as numerical one, it would become a ⟪ar:مفعول⟫—a condition that cannot apply to Allāh. This is why the ⟪ar:سورۃ اخلاص⟫ (*Chapter of Sincerity*) addresses Him not as ⟪ar:'واحد'⟫ (one in number) but as ⟪ar:'أحد'⟫ (absolutely unique):

> ⟪quran 112:1⟫
> ⟪ar:‏قُلْ هُوَ ٱللَّهُ أَحَدٌ ‎⟫
>
> *Say (O Muhammad, Peace be upon him): "He is Allāh, (the) Absolutely One."*

Moreover, nothing can be counted unless it is confined within a boundary. Therefore, if we call ⟪ar:مبدع تعالی⟫ one, it is as though we imprison Him within limitation—an impossibility, for He is the Creator of limitation itself. Furthermore, the number one necessarily implies a relation to multiplicity: one book is one among many books; one chair is one among many chairs; one fan is one among many fans. Similarly, one god suggests a god among many gods.

### He Is Disembodied and Unconfined

Everything that possesses a body is encompassed within six dimensions—up, down, forward, backward, right, left—and is confined within what is called its place. What exists in one location cannot exist in another. Every created thing in this cosmos, even the heavens themselves, is confined to its proper place and cannot transcend it. Every body is dependent upon its Creator for continued existence—a dependency that stands opposed to ⟪ar:صمدیت⟫ (complete self-sufficiency) that characterizes ⟪ar:مبدع تعالی⟫.[^cite-9] The Essence of Allāh is not limited; it cannot be said that He is here and not there, in the east and not the west, in the north and not the south. All things of this world are circumscribed within place, which is the hallmark of physicality. Allāh is the Creator of both space and time.

Every entity is governed by the limits of its place. Were we to confine ⟪ar:مبدع تعالی⟫ within place, His dominion and power would likewise become circumscribed—as though no kingdom or dominion existed beyond that particular boundary. But His sovereignty is absolute.

### He Is Timeless

Time is the measure of celestial motion by which we discern whether an object first came into existence and afterward ceased. Scholars teach that ⟪ar:معلول⟫ (time is the effect) and celestial motion is ⟪ar:علت⟫ (the cause) thereof—that is, the motion of the heavens brought time into being. Every motion, too, has its opposite, called rest or tranquility. The interval between motion and stillness is termed time. Both motion and repose apply only to physical beings. There is no motion preceding ⟪ar:مبدع تعالی⟫ and what antecedes it, nor is there time. Words prove helpless to describe it. One must remain silent, knowing only that He has always been.

⟪ar:امام جعفر الصادق ؑ⟫ (Imām Jaʿfar al-Ṣādiq, may peace be upon him) is reported to have declared in an assembly: ⟪ar:اللہ اکبر!⟫ He then asked his audience what ⟪ar:اللہ اکبر⟫ signifies. One person replied, "I understand it thus: ⟪ar:اللہ اکبر مِن کُلِّ اَکبَر⟫ (Allāh is greater than all greatness)." The Imām responded: "⟪ar:اِذاً حَدَّتَّہُ⟫ (Then you have limited Him). If you say the Mighty is greater than the mightier, then the Mighty itself stands in relation to a Greater Might—much as a drop relates to the ocean." He then explained: "The true meaning of ⟪ar:اللہ اکبر⟫ is: ⟪ar-quote:اَللّٰہُ اَکبَر مِن اَن یُّحَدَّ وَ یُحسَر⟫ (*Allāh is greater than to be confined to any limit or measure*)."[^cite-10]

### He Is Ancient (Without Beginning)

All things have been brought into ⟪ar:وجود (الیس)⟫ (existence from non-being). They possess many parts and components mutually dependent, each contingent upon another for existence. Allāh is ancient—not in temporal terms, but in the sense that no one knows the origin of His existence. ⟪ar:عدم (لیس)⟫ (*nothingness*) never obtained for Him. He created time itself; therefore, it is impossible for His existence to fall under time.

(From the *Kitāb Tāj al-ʿAqāʾid*, attributed to Sayyidnā ʿAlī ibn Muḥammad al-Walīd)

### He Is Eternal in His Perfection

He created all beings from nothingness, without matter or substance. Had He created from something already existent, His action would be flawed, for it would imply He could not act without that pre-existent thing. Had He created in a place, He would require that place to sustain His creation and would thereby become contingent—hence defective. But Allāh cannot be defective. Thus it is proven that He created all beings from absolute nothingness, without matter, without substance, and without any medium.[^cite-11]

(From the *Kitāb Tāj al-ʿAqāʾid*, attributed to Sayyidnā ʿAlī ibn Muḥammad al-Walīd)

### He Is Neither Form Nor Substance

Allāh is not a form, for every form depends upon its Creator; and everything contingent upon another for existence is a creature, not a Creator. Every creature confesses: "My Creator is not like me"—the form itself testifying that its Creator is not form. Similarly, every substance is contingent upon its Creator and ⟪ar:مبدع تعالی⟫ cannot be contingent. In other words, ⟪ar:مبدع تعالی⟫ is neither form nor matter, but the Creator of both. To conceive otherwise would divide Him into two parts, each dependent upon a third entity superior to both. This is impossible.

(From the *Kitāb Tāj al-ʿAqāʾid*, attributed to Sayyidnā ʿAlī ibn Muḥammad al-Walīd)

### He Has No Name

A name is a symbol by which creatures of different kinds are distinguished from one another, so that each gender, type, and form may be recognized without repeated elaboration. Since Allāh possesses no form—neither intellectual, nor intelligible, nor physical, nor artificial—His Essence transcends the very concept of naming. The names of creatures cannot adhere to Him. Yet when He brought the world forth from nothingness, He bestowed upon creation names, enabling distinction among different forms. Thus Allāh says in the Qurʾān:

> ⟪quran 2:31⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَعَلَّمَ آدَمَ ٱلْأَسْمَآءَ كُلَّهَا ثُمَّ عَرَضَهُمْ عَلَى ٱلْمَلَٓئِكَةِ فَقَالَ أَنۢبِـُٔونِى بِأَسْمَآءِ هَٓؤُلَآءِ إِن كُنتُمْ صَدِقِينَ ‎⟫
>
> *And He taught Adam all the names (of everything), then He presented them to the angels and said, "Tell Me the names of these, if you are truthful."*

Yet He Himself stands free from all names and attributes:

> ⟪quran 37:159⟫
> ⟪ar:‏سُبْحَنَ ٱللَّهِ عَمَّا يَصِفُونَ ‎⟫
>
> *Glorified is Allāh! (He is) Free from what they attribute unto Him!*

Constrained by necessity, the righteous point to Him by the pronoun ⟪ar:(ھو)⟫. It is erroneous to call Allāh "the Mighty," or to use the appellative ⟪ar:مُبدِع⟫ (Creator) or even ⟪ar:ھو⟫ (He) to refer to Him. He possesses neither noun nor adjective. We venture to express the Inexpressible with our mortal tongue. Letters are themselves mute; we are their authors, for no letter sounds unless its speaker is present.[^cite-12] Thus we attempt to interpret our Creator through His creation—letters—an undertaking doomed to failure.

(From the *Kitāb Tāj al-ʿAqāʾid*, attributed to Sayyidnā ʿAlī ibn Muḥammad al-Walīd)

---

**Principle:**

Allāh is not a name but *the* Name. This Name is not the denomination of the Essence, but the name of the ⟪ar:عقل اول⟫ (the First Intellect). The ⟪ar:عقل اول⟫ possesses ninety-nine attributes, which are designated by names. These ninety-nine attributes together with the Essence constitute one hundred.[^cite-13]

### He Has No Attributes

All attributes pertain to entities—whether physical or psychological. Bodies are characterized by their forms: height, color, and the like. Souls possess qualities such as knowledge and ignorance, virtue and vice. ⟪ar:اسمآء⟫ (Names) and ⟪ar:صفات⟫ (Attributes) are inseparable. When we observe ⟪ar:اسم (رحیم )⟫ (the name "Merciful"), its corresponding ⟪ar:صفت (رحم )⟫ (the attribute "Mercy") is necessarily implicated. We behold the merciful and possess a concept, standard, and example of mercy, such that we can observe a merciful person and say: ⟪ar:رحیم⟫ (He is merciful).

⟪ar:مبدع تعالی، اسم⟫ is neither body nor soul. It is impossible for a ⟪ar:صفت⟫ (attribute) to exist when its ⟪ar:اسم⟫ (name) does not. He is not a thing among things but the Creator of all things. It is therefore necessary that no attribute applicable to creation can adhere to its Creator. We are obliged solely to negate and deny every attribute pertaining to Him.

(From the *Kitāb Tāj al-ʿAqāʾid*, attributed to Sayyidnā ʿAlī ibn Muḥammad al-Walīd)

### He Has No Opposite

Allāh has no opposite. Let us not imagine Satan standing arrayed against Allāh. None can rival God. Consider Adam's story: Satan himself believed in Allāh. He denied only Adam, and swore by Allāh, "I shall lead Your servants astray," and besought respite until the Day of Resurrection. The Devil is not ignorant; he knows all things, yet he rebels. ⟪ar:معلم الملکوت⟫ (The Teacher of the Celestial Kingdom), he was—experienced and learned. Yet his knowledge and service availed him naught, for he disobeyed Allāh, and therefore was he called ⟪ar:رجیم⟫ (the Accursed), and Hell became his abode. Whoever follows him shall likewise enter Hell.

Allāh proclaims in the Qurʾān: ⟪ar-quote:لا اکراہ فی الدین⟫ (*There is no compulsion in religion*). Whether one turns toward Allāh or toward Satan, it is by one's own choice. Therefore do we pray in every prayer: ⟪ar-quote:اھدنا الصراط المستقیم⟫ *(Guide us upon the Straight Path)*, for both roads lie open before us.[^cite-14]

### Can the Almighty Commit Injustice?

Allāh cannot revoke the existence He has granted. A debate once arose between believers and skeptical sects in which they asked: "Is Allāh capable of all things?" The believers answered: "Surely, Allāh can do all things, yet He cannot commit wrongdoing." The skeptics objected: "Why do you limit Allāh's power by saying He cannot commit injustice?"

The believers replied: "Can you eat all things?" The skeptic boasted: "Of course I can!" They then presented filth before him and said: "Eat this." The conclusion is clear: Allāh possesses all power but does not commit injustice. He refrains not because He is unable to wrong, but because wrongdoing contradicts His glory and majesty.[^cite-15]

## Monotheism and Sincerity

Monotheism (tawhīd, التوحید) becomes real only through sincerity. ⟪ar:مولانا علی⟫ (Our Master ʿAlī) expressed this truth:

⟪ar-quote:اَلأِخْلاَصْ یَکُونُ بِہِ اَلْخَلاَصْ وَ اَلأِخْلاَصْ ثَمَرَۃُ الیَقِین وَاَلأِخْلاَصْ ثَمَرَۃُ العِباَدَۃْ واَلأِخْلاَصْ ھِیَ خَیرُ العَمَل وَلاَ یَکُونُ خَیرُ العَمَل اِلاَّ بِالعِلم وَاَجَلُّ اَلْعِلْم، اَلعِلْمُ التَّوحِید⟫

*Sincerity is that through which deliverance comes. Sincerity is the fruit of unwavering certainty (yaqīn). Sincerity is the fruit of worship—the more our certainty and worship increase, the deeper our sincerity becomes. Sincerity is the noblest of deeds; and no deed can be noblest save through knowledge. The most exalted knowledge is the knowledge of monotheism (tawḥīd).*[^cite-16]

The Originator is One (Aḥad), the Self-Sufficient (⟪ar:صمد⟫, Samad)—eternally enduring, everlasting. He is the First, before whom nothing existed, and the Last, who shall remain when all else perishes. He transcends all place and is unbound by the progression of days, months, and years. He brought all things into being from absolute nothingness—effortlessly, without pain or movement or repose, not after the example of another thing, not from any substance, not from need or purpose, and not from fear of solitude.

He is not anxious lest something slip from His control, and therefore He does not hasten. He does not act from need of anything. He is the All-Knowing, in whose knowledge there is no uncertainty. He is the Just Ruler who does not oppress. He is the Forbearing One who does not rush in judgment. He does not doze nor sleep. He is free from all error, possessing wisdom that is beyond reproach. He is exalted above all things. He is All-Powerful, the Guardian, and there is nothing like unto Him. He hears and sees. Even when there was nothing to hear or see, He possessed the faculties of hearing and sight.

The foundation of religion is knowledge of Him—acknowledgment that He is the Creator. The reality of such knowledge is His *tawhīd*. The system of *tawhīd* is for the sound intellect to bear witness that He is free from all attributes, for every attribute and the one described by it testify to their created nature. Every creature confesses: "I am not eternal; therefore I am not the Creator."[^cite-17]

He initiated all beings, which is proof that He has no beginning, for the initiator of every beginning is something other than itself. He bestowed upon humans organs and faculties—eyes, ears, hands, feet—which bear witness that He is without such limbs, for those possessing limbs are dependent upon the Creator of their limbs. His Names are mere expressions, not realities. Whoever describes His attributes is ignorant. Whoever named Him has transgressed. Whoever described His condition established an analogy of God. Whoever mentioned place has confined Allāh—and that which is confined falls within counting. He is ⟪ar:أحد⟫ (absolutely One), yet He transcends all enumeration. He is ⟪ar:صمد⟫ (the Self-Sufficient), yet independent of body. He is the Inner (hidden), yet His existence is manifest. He is the Outer (apparent), yet not physically present. He shines forth brilliantly, yet is invisible to eyes. He is near, yet not through physical proximity. He is far, yet not through spatial distance. He is ⟪ar:بصیر⟫ (the All-Seeing), yet without eyes. He is not bound by time nor confined to any place. He acts, yet not under compulsion. He is Self-Sufficient, yet not through the possession of anything existent or non-existent.

Our consciousness and senses have taught us that He has no boundary. The essence of substance informed us that He has no maker of His essence. The creation of the created declared that He has no creator. The opposition of things revealed that He has no opposite. The pairing of things testified that He has no partner. Time and space, by their limitation, proclaimed that He is neither subject to time nor to space. He was the Creator even before He created creation, and He was the Originator even before He brought forth the originated.

He transformed darkness into light, gloom into brightness, softness into hardness, heat into cold, and created connection among opposing things. Unity manifest despite diversity, and diversity despite unity, both testify to His ⟪ar:ربوبیت⟫ (Lordship). All these varied and opposing states are witnesses to His power and evidence of His hiddenness.

This is affirmed through intellect alone, and the intellect bears witness that there is no faith without sincerity, no sincerity without knowledge, no knowledge without affirmation, no affirmation without *tawhīd*, no *tawhīd* without sincerity, and sincerity cannot coexist with ⟪ar:تشبیہ⟫ (anthropomorphism, likening Him to creation), for anthropomorphism entails attributing characteristics to Him.[^cite-18]

*Tawhīd* cannot exist without the complete negation of attributes. When the essence of someone is described through an attribute, it denotes limitation. What is understood by example is perceived by the intellect. What enters the imagination takes on form. What is encompassed by directions is revealed by causes. What exists in one space is absent from another. What has a body has weight. What is still can move. What adheres to something depends upon that attachment. The intellect that learns through memory is likewise subject to forgetfulness. One who speaks through letters is constrained. One who plans through thought is occupied. One who creates through consultation is incomplete in essence. What enters imagination becomes likened to something, and what is encompassed by imagination loses transcendence. ⟪ar:مبدع تعالی⟫ is exalted and sanctified beyond all such restrictions and limitations.[^cite-19]

The praise of Him is the garment of others—that is, the words of praise that emanate from creation are only fitting for creation itself, for whatever one owns circulates within one's ownership. Absence of attributes is His attribute. His glory knows no limit. His action has no external cause. His existence has no temporal span. None of His creation can attain Him, and none can pierce the veils of His hiddenness.

Allāh alone is pure and exalted beyond all the attributes of His creation. Attributes indicate essence and describe something akin to themselves. No creature created the Essence of the Originator, and no dominion made Him sovereign; rather, He has eternally been the Sovereign. Attributes did not bring Him into existence; rather, He is the Originator of every existent, the Creator of every attribute and the attributed, the All-Knowing and the known. He is the Agent of every action, yet His action occurs without ⟪ar:مباشرت⟫ (direct physical contact). His guidance comes without sign. His word is without articulation. His Face is wherever you turn. His path is ⟪ar:استقامت دین⟫ (the Uprightness of Religion). He explains us to ourselves and teaches us through ourselves.[^cite-20]

## He Who Knows His Own Soul Has Known His Lord

This sacred hadith exemplifies those specific utterances for which the Messenger declared: ⟪ar-quote:بَعَثتُ بِجَوَاِمعُ الکَلاَم⟫ "I have been sent with comprehensive words and gems of wisdom." When someone approached ⟪ar:رسول اللہ صلی اللہ علیہ و آلہ و سلم⟫ (the Messenger of Allah, may God's blessings and peace be upon him and his family) asking him to reveal the knowledge of the Almighty, he replied: ⟪ar-quote:مَن عَرَفَ نَفسُہُ فَقَد عَرَفَ رَبُّہُ⟫ "He who knows his own soul has thereby known his Lord." Upon another occasion, he affirmed: ⟪ar-quote:اَعرَفکُم بِنَفسِہِ اَعرَفکُم بِرَبِّہِ⟫ "The one among you most knowledgeable of his own soul is most knowledgeable of his Lord." In this way, the Messenger of Allah (peace and blessings be upon him) made manifest a profound principle: the knowledge of the Almighty cannot be approached directly through the knowledge of the human soul alone. Rather, he posed a fundamental question: if a human being lacks the capacity to fully comprehend his own self—a self that he inhabits and experiences—how then can he claim to know his Lord, who created the soul and granted it intellect?

⟪ar:مبدع تعالی⟫ (the Originator, Most High) cannot be known in His essence. Knowledge is attainable only of that which is subordinate in rank. The hadith presents three reciprocal realities:

| From (Intellect) | Through (Soul) | To (Lord) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Intelligence | Soul | Lord |

Within this sacred utterance, the term ⟪ar:مَن⟫ (he who) refers to the one whose ⟪ar:عقل⟫ (intellect) and ⟪ar:نفس⟫ (soul) are engaged in recognizing their inner capacities, and through these capacities, moving toward knowledge and recognition of the Lord.[^cite-21]

For those who follow the exoteric path, this hadith unfolds through contemplation of one's birth and genesis. When a person reflects upon how the Almighty created him from ⟪ar:اجزاء غذائیۃ⟫ (nutritive elements) and ⟪ar:سلالۃ⟫ (extract of clay), beginning with ⟪ar:نطفۃ⟫ (a sperm-drop), then transforming it into ⟪ar:عَلَقَۃ⟫ (a clot), then ⟪ar:مُضغَۃ⟫ (a lump of flesh), then into ⟪ar:عظام⟫ (bones), upon which ⟪ar:لحم⟫ (flesh) was drawn—when he perceives how the Spirit was breathed into this form, how he was brought forth upon the earth's surface, and made the regent over all earthly things, clothed with sustenance and drink—he recognizes that none of these gifts arose from his own effort.[^cite-22] He comprehends that a Creator other than himself brought him from non-existence into being. Further reflection reveals the cycles of his own strength: born in ⟪quran 30:54⟫ profound weakness, gradually advancing in strength, then declining into old age and infirmity, as the Qur'an testifies:

> ⟪quran 30:54⟫
> ⟪ar:‏ ٱللَّهُ ٱلَّذِى خَلَقَكُم مِّن ضَعْفٍۢ ثُمَّ جَعَلَ مِنۢ بَعْدِ ضَعْفٍۢ قُوَّةًۭ ثُمَّ جَعَلَ مِنۢ بَعْدِ قُوَّةٍۢ ضَعْفًۭا وَشَيْبَةًۭ ۚ يَخْلُقُ مَا يَشَآءُ ۖ وَهُوَ ٱلْعَلِيمُ ٱلْقَدِيرُ ‎⟫
> *Allah is He Who created you in (a state of) weakness, then gave you strength after weakness, then after strength gave (you) weakness and grey hair. He creates what He wills. And it is He Who is the All-Knowing, the All-Powerful.*

In another place, the Qur'an records a conversation illustrating this principle:

> ⟪quran 18:37⟫
> ⟪ar:‏قَالَ لَهُۥ صَاحِبُهُۥ وَهُوَ يُحَاوِرُهُۥٓ أَكَفَرْتَ بِٱلَّذِى خَلَقَكَ مِن تُرَابٍۢ ثُمَّ مِن نُّطْفَةٍۢ ثُمَّ سَوَّىٰكَ رَجُلًۭا ‎⟫
> *His companion said to him, during the talk with him: "Do you disbelieve in Him Who created you out of dust (i.e. your father Adam), then out of a sperm-drop, then fashioned you into a man?"*

### Direct and Indirect Creation

The people of the inner dimension consider this teaching of the Messenger (peace and blessings be upon him) and arrive at a profound distinction. They recognize that man is not a direct creation of the Almighty—for the Creator transcends all dependence upon time, place, and the ⟪ar:عناصر اربع⟫ (four elements: fire, air, water, and earth). It is the human being who requires tools, preparation, and conditions to fashion and create. Not so the Creator! He is free and purified from all such limitations. To attribute these dependencies to the Almighty in creating man would be to measure the Infinite by the finite, to describe the Creator by the constraints of the creature. Does not the human need paper, ink, pen, and hand to write? Does the Almighty require soil, womb, and nine months of time to create man? Such thinking contradicts the true understanding of Divine Unity (tawheed).

Therefore, it is said that the human being, emerging gradually from clay and elemental matter, is not a direct creation but an indirect one. The proof lies evident: man is created within this world where he dwells and earns his sustenance; he is dependent upon this world for his survival. Yet this world depends not upon the conditions and tools that man requires at birth. A clear distinction thus emerges: the world itself needs neither instruments nor time in its own origination—it is a direct creation of the Almighty. Man, fashioned from the ⟪ar:عناصر اربع⟫ of this world, is therefore an indirect creation.[^cite-23] The Qur'an speaks of the world's direct creation:

> ⟪quran 16:40⟫
> ⟪ar:‏إِنَّمَا قَوْلُنَا لِشَىْءٍ إِذَآ أَرَدْنَهُ أَن نَّقُولَ لَهُۥ كُن فَيَكُونُ ‎⟫
> *Verily! Our Word unto a thing when We intend it, is only that We say unto it: "Be!" and it is.*

And concerning the creation of humanity and the cosmos together:

> ⟪quran 10:3⟫
> ⟪ar:‏إِنَّ رَبَّكُمُ ٱللَّهُ ٱلَّذِى خَلَقَ ٱلسَّمَوَتِ وَٱلْأَرْضَ فِى سِتَّةِ أَيَّامٍۢ ثُمَّ ٱسْتَوَىٰ عَلَى ٱلْعَرْشِ ۖ يُدَبِّرُ ٱلْأَمْرَ ۖ مَا مِن شَفِيعٍ إِلَّا مِنۢ بَعْدِ إِذْنِهِۦ ۚ ذَلِكُمُ ٱللَّهُ رَبُّكُمْ فَٱعْبُدُوهُ ۚ أَفَلَا تَذَكَّرُونَ ‎⟫
> *Surely, your Lord is Allah Who created the heavens and the earth in six Days and then Istawa (rose over) the Throne (really in a manner that suits His Majesty), disposing the affair of all things. No intercessor (can plead with Him) except after His Leave. That is Allah, your Lord; so worship Him (Alone). Then, will you not remember?*

### Parts and Wholes: A Crucial Distinction

⟪ar:رسول اللہ (ﷺ)⟫ In his exposition of the hadith "He who knows his soul knows his Lord," ⟪ar:سیدنا مؤید اعلی اﷲ قدسہ⟫ (our master al-Muʾayyad, may God sanctify him) clarifies that man comprises two distinct dimensions. One is the body, the other the soul. Corporeal existence is constituted of four elements—fire, air, water, and earth—⟪ar:کرۃ الأرض⟫ (the terrestrial sphere). From this realm, man derives the earthen component through the soil, the aerial through the air, the igneous through the body's heat, and the aqueous through water itself.

Yet within this dense corporeal frame dwells something subtle and wondrous: a power by which man encompasses the heavens and earth within the center of his thought and imagination, without discomfort or physical movement. This subtle force is termed ⟪ar:نفس ناطقۃ⟫ (the rational soul). It is by virtue of this ⟪ar:نفس ناطقۃ⟫ that he is called a human being, ⟪ar:اشرف المخلوقات⟫ (the most noble of creatures), distinguished from ⟪ar:نفس حیوانیۃ⟫ (the animal soul) and all lower beings.

Beyond even this, there exists another dimension, still more subtle: the intellect itself, by which a person discerns good from evil, justice from injustice. Just as the body requires a central seat and governing principle, so too must the soul and intellect have their center. This universal center we call ⟪ar:نفس کلیۃ⟫ (the universal soul) and ⟪ar:عقل کلیۃ⟫ (the universal intellect), for all particular souls and intellects of the physical world are partial, deriving their powers from this supreme center.[^cite-24]

By this distinction, we have proven that man is twofold in origin: through his body and ⟪ar:نفس⟫ and ⟪ar:عقل⟫, he belongs to the natural world and to another dimension beyond. He is thereby the child of two worlds. The constitution of this human compound may be represented thus:

| Body | Soul | Intellect |
| --- | --- | --- |
| The Four Elements (Fire, Air, Water, Earth) | Universal Soul | Universal Intellect |

Consider now: the earthly matter man receives is so infinitesimal that no meaningful ratio can be established between his portion and the whole terrestrial sphere. The same applies to his aerial component relative to ⟪ar:کرۃ ہوائیۃ⟫ (the sphere of air), to his heat relative to ⟪ar:کرۃ النار⟫ (the sphere of fire), and to his water relative to ⟪ar:کرۃ آبی⟫ (the sphere of water). Not a single proportion can be drawn. **Thus, the four natures within the human body are parts of those wholes, not effects of them.**

When we turn to the other dimension—⟪ar:عالم نفس کلی⟫ (the realm of the universal soul) and ⟪ar:عقل کلی⟫ (the universal intellect)—and observe that man's ⟪ar:نفس اور عقل⟫ are partial, we conclude that they are not mere parts of some ⟪ar:عالم کل⟫ (whole realm), but rather effects emanating from it. Here lies the critical insight: despite our most strenuous efforts, we cannot establish any meaningful relation of the human ⟪ar:نفس و عقل⟫ to the universal order ⟪ar:عالم جسم⟫ and ⟪ar:عالم نفس و عقل⟫ from which they proceed.

When a human being is born of these higher worlds and cannot establish their relationship to them despite being connected to them, how then can he possess the power to know and comprehend the reality of Divine Unity—wherein lies no relation whatsoever? Man admits at last that what he imagined to be tawheed was but a mirage. Then he understands the truth of ⟪ar:مولانا علی⟫'s declaration: ⟪ar-quote:اَلعِجزُ عَن دَرَکِ اِلأدرَاکِہِ اِدراک⟫ "To fall short in comprehending [the Divine] is itself a form of comprehension." He confesses that the Prophet's saying is the supreme truth: when one cannot know one's own soul, how can one know the ⟪ar:مبد تعالی⟫ (the Most High Originator)?

## The Journey from the Soul to the Knowledge of the Lord

⟪ar:مولانا امیر المؤمنین سید العارفین، علی ابن ابی طالب علیہ وعلیٰ آلہٖ افضل السلام⟫ (our master the Commander of the Faithful, the Chief of the Knowers, Ali son of Abu Talib, upon him and upon his progeny be the finest peace) was asked: "O Ali! The Prophet said, ⟪ar-quote:مَن عَرَفَ نَفسُہُ فَقَد عَرَفَ رَبُّہُ⟫ 'He who knows his own soul knows his Lord.' What is the way to this knowledge?"

Ali replied with profound wisdom: "It is imperative for a human being to know himself and his soul before attaining knowledge of the Almighty. Without this, there can be no true knowing of God. Before recognizing one's soul, understand that it is composed of four contradictory natures: fire, water, earth, and air. These four ⟪ar:عناصر⟫ are also called the ⟪ar:اُمہات⟫ (mothers), for they are the fundamental principles.

These four warring elements were unified by nature to form a dense body—what is perceived by the five senses and grasped within ⟪ar:ثلاثہ ابعاد⟫ (three dimensions: length, width, and depth). This body is limited in space and destined to dissolve, returning to its elemental components, becoming dust and ash.

Next, man must recognize that this dense body, like all bodies, cannot move of itself. Something subtle and non-material must move it—the soul itself. From the soul comes life; from its departure, death and dissolution. When he perceives the soul's superiority over the body, he recognizes that the subtle soul, being infinite in nature, does not require the instruments and sensory apparatus of physicality. It transcends ⟪ar:ثلاثہ ابعاد⟫. At that moment, he understands that the attributes of the soul are of an order higher and more subtle than the body. They are invisible because they belong to the spiritual realm. Contemplating the soul necessarily leads him to contemplate the spiritual world itself. Through continued reflection, he comes to understand that by treading the path of truth, by holding fast to knowledge and practice, and by cultivating piety, the soul will eventually depart the body and return to its source—for ⟪ar-quote:کُلَّ شَئی یَرجِعُ اِلاَ اَصلِہِ⟫ "all things return to their origin."[^cite-25]

As he contemplates the spiritual world, he must acknowledge that it is utterly free from the attributes and conditions of the physical realm. Nothing material exists within it. Eating and drinking, standing and sitting, sleep and waking—all these material concerns have no place in the spiritual world. If he perseveres in this contemplation, he will arrive at a profound realization: the ⟪ar:مبدع تعالی⟫ (the Creator of these two worlds) must surely be free from the attributes of both worlds. He is exalted, holy, and glorified, standing above the names and praises by which we endeavor to describe Him. We can only refer to Him as ⟪ar:ھو⟫ (That One), and even then with the acknowledgment that His ⟪ar:ھویت⟫ (His Essential Reality) transcends even this designation. ⟪ar:اللہ اکبر!!⟫ Our words, sentences, and texts cannot reach Him; how much less can our thoughts, imaginings, and suppositions? In summary, whoever enters the field of true knowledge must ultimately confess: ⟪ar-quote:اَلعِجزُ عَن اِدرَاکُہُ اِدرَاک⟫ "The inability to perceive Him is itself true perception." He will admit his own inadequacy, and this humility is true knowledge.[^cite-26]

### Knowledge of Allah by the Abrahamic Path

There exists another approach to knowledge: the way of Abraham himself, ⟪ar:حضرت ابراھیم علیہ السلام⟫. It proceeds through gradual advancement, through the achievement of discipleship, through understanding the virtues that dwell within each hierarchy and the boundaries that separate them. By maintaining these distinctions and acknowledging the ⟪ar:مبدع المبدعات⟫ (the Originator of all originators), one arrives at the proclamation: ⟪ar:لاَ اِلٰہَ اِلاَّ ھُو⟫ "There is no god save Him." The Qur'an describes this Abrahamic path:

> ⟪quran 6:76-79⟫
> ⟪ar:‏فَلَمَّا جَنَّ عَلَيْهِ ٱلَّيْلُ رَآ كَوْكَبًۭا ۖ قَالَ هَذَا رَبِّى ۖ فَلَمَّآ أَفَلَ قَالَ لَآ أُحِبُّ ٱلْآفِلِينَ ‎ ‏فَلَمَّا رَآ ٱلْقَمَرَ بَازِغًۭا قَالَ هَذَا رَبِّى ۖ فَلَمَّآ أَفَلَ قَالَ لَىِٕن لَّمْ يَهْدِنِى رَبِّى لَأَكُونَنَّ مِنَ ٱلْقَوْمِ ٱلضَّآلِّينَ ‎ ‏فَلَمَّا رَآ ٱلشَّمْسَ بَازِغَةًۭ قَالَ هَذَا رَبِّى هَذَآ أَكْبَرُ ۖ فَلَمَّآ أَفَلَتْ قَالَ يَقَوْمِ إِنِّى بَرِىٓءٌۭ مِّمَّا تُشْرِكُونَ ‎ ‏إِنِّى وَجَّهْتُ وَجْهِىَ لِلَّذِى فَطَرَ ٱلسَّمَوَتِ وَٱلْأَرْضَ حَنِيفًۭا ۖ وَمَآ أَنَا۠ مِنَ ٱلْمُشْرِكِينَ ‎⟫
> *When the night covered him over with darkness he saw a star. He said: "This is my lord." But when it set, he said: "I like not those that set." When he saw the moon rising up, he said: "This is my lord." But when it set, he said: "Unless my Lord guides me, I shall surely be among the erring people." When he saw the sun rising up, he said: "This is my lord. This is greater." But when it set, he said: "O my people! I am indeed free from all that you join as partners in worship with Allah. Verily, I have turned my face towards Him Who has created the heavens and the earth Hanifa (Islamic Monotheism, i.e. worshipping none but Allah Alone) and I am not of Al-Mushrikun."*

In such a way, each ⟪ar:مؤمن مستفید⟫ (believer who benefits) is in truth a reflection of his own ⟪ar:مفید⟫ (benefactor), his own limit. The believer's spiritual life flows from Him; his Lord and the Lord of all his predecessors is the highest of the benefactors—He who is the ⟪ar:مُوجد⟫ (Giver of Existence), the Intellect, and the Guide. Though all Muslims accept the five pillars of faith, each emphasizes them differently: some give primacy to prayer, others to fasting, still others to pilgrimage. This is the concept of Tawheed that the ⟪ar:'یکتا'⟫ Creator has established all beings in their own uniqueness and singularity—each entity is one, a ⟪ar:'حد' (Boundary)⟫ in its own place.

## The Upper Limit and the Lower Limit

⟪ar:صاحب رسائل اخوان الصفاء⟫ (the author of the Epistles of the Brethren of Purity) explains that all things in this universe—the heavens, the celestial spheres, and human beings—are bound together in a single chain. The analogy is that of chain links: the upper portion of each link connects with the link above it, and its lower portion with the link below it. Thus, everything in the universe occupies its place within this hierarchy based on its proximity or distance from the material. This series of limits begins with the intellect and ascends to the heavens (which in one sense are angels), proceeds to the four natures, and culminates in humanity—the supreme purpose of divine creation. Humans occupy the middle of this chain, connected above to the angels and below to the beasts.

In all classes, each belongs to both an upper and a lower order. Through development, minerals connect to plants; plants to animals; and animals to humans. The lowest mineral is base metal, the highest is the red ruby. The lowest plant is moss upon water, the highest the palm tree. The highest of minerals mingles with the lowest of plants; the highest plants adjoins the lowest animals; the highest animals adjoins the lowest humans. Thus, the ⟪ar:موالید ثلاثہ⟫ (three births) form a continuum: minerals feed plants, plants feed animals, and animals feed humans. In this sense, the highest human becomes sustenance for angels. Each finite being nourishes the next higher; thus does development proceed. In the language of the divine summons, it is said: every greater fish swallows a smaller fish.[^cite-31]

## What Is Meant by "Boundary" or "Limit"?

In the terminology of the divine summons, a "limit" (ḥadd) refers to the boundary within which each entity, in its own measure of subtlety, exercises its individual jurisdiction. Should an entity wish to increase its powers, it must first elevate its rank. No two limits can exist simultaneously in the same position. A limit cannot vacate its station until it has prepared a successor to assume that rank. This is why ⟪ar:سیدنا مؤید⟫ says: ⟪ar-quote:اَلمُؤمِنُ لاَ یَمُوتُ اِلاَّ یَخلُقُ المُؤمِنُ مِثلُہُ⟫ "A believer does not depart this life except that he begets another believer like unto himself."[^cite-32] ⟪ar:سیدنا مؤید⟫ also declared: ⟪ar-quote:اَلتَّوحِیدُ لاَ فِی اللّٰہ بَل فِی الحُدوُد⟫ "Tawheed is not [solely] in the Creator but in His limits [as well]."[^cite-33] The Qur'an confirms:

> ⟪quran 51:56⟫

This humility and reverence have been made the ladder of ascent through the ranks. The true purpose of the Supreme Creator is to elevate His limits and establish them in their rightful stations.

## The Makasir: The First Boundary in the Dominical Rank

The order of boundaries begins with ⟪ar:مکاسر⟫ (the Breakers, or those who break idols), which is ⟪ar:رتبہ ملکوتیۃ⟫ (the dominical rank), the first limit within the hierarchy. A believer is not yet included in the order of limits; he has not yet stepped into the ⟪ar:رتبہ ملکوتیہ⟫.

A **believer** (momen) is characterized by: the word of the heart, the affirmation of the tongue, the acknowledgment of the heart, and the action of the body. A **makasir** (breaker) is one who shatters false beliefs and establishes true belief in their place. First, he purifies himself, breaking his own false convictions. For if he cannot break his own idols, how can he break another's? This same process continues throughout all the higher limits: each who is a guide (maʿdhun) is both a guided one and a guide in their own station.

Outwardly, all humans stand on the same ground, similar in flesh, bone, blood, veins, skin, and muscle. Yet the makasir differs greatly from others. His essence has been refined; his nature has been clarified and elevated through the ranks. He alone progresses from this platform, ascending ever higher, while all others descend. The first makasir is ⟪ar:شخص ملکوتیۃ⟫ (a person of dominical form) because he ascends toward the angel realm; the second is ⟪ar:اشخاص حیوانیۃ⟫ (animal-form persons) because they are falling into animal mold.[^cite-34]

Consider an example: among halal animals (goats, cattle, and camels) and haram animals (lions, dogs, and cats), all share identical composition—meat, bone, blood, veins, skin, and muscle. Yet a halal animal becomes food for humans and thus attains humanity; it is slaughtered and thrives, while the haram animal dies its natural death and falls into a lower mold. The halal animal loves humans and remains near them, while the haram animal flees or harbors hostility. Similarly, one makasir stands closest to the Imam and loves him; all others flee or oppose him. The Imam slaughters the makasir symbolically, attaching his form to the Imam's and raising him to a higher ⟪ar:(رتبہ ملکوتیۃ)⟫.[^cite-35]

The body of the summons (daʿwa) corresponds to the feet, for the makasir bears all the burden of education and training, just as the feet bear all the body's weight. No matter how heavy the burden, the feet do not protest. The preachers and higher boundaries lack time to teach because they perform other duties of the summons; therefore, the entire pedagogical burden falls upon the makasir. Their recompense is great: their dust is exceedingly protected; no creature in the grave disturbs them. From their bones are fashioned jewels for the courts of kings. Their bones become salt that cures seventy diseases. The pebbles cast at the pillars during the Hajj are made from their bone matter. The pigeons of the Kaaba itself are composed of makasir elements.[^cite-36]

In the progression of development, the supreme believer—the most elevated human type—stands at the summit of humanity. This highest human form borders the lowest angelic form, which is the makasir. As development continues through the chain of transcendence, one reaches ⟪ar:باب الابواب⟫ (the Gate of Gates), which is adjacent to the ⟪ar:امام مستودع⟫ (the Preserved Imam). Both ⟪ar:امام مستودع، امام مستقر⟫ (the Preserved Imam and the Established Imam) connect through the intellect by means of which ⟪ar:قوائم، جثوں⟫ (the standing ones and the bodies) reach toward the ⟪ar:عقل اول⟫ (First Intellect), which is the highest and foremost link in the entire chain.[^cite-37]

## The Definition of Abstraction

The abstraction (⟪ar:تجرید⟫) of the Messenger of Allah from all created beings and their attributes, and the negation of all innovations—this abstraction entails the removal of all ⟪ar:الہیت، أبداع الذوات⟫ (divinity, the origination of essences) from all ⟪ar:مُبدَعات⟫ (originated beings) and all ⟪ar:مخلوقات⟫ (created things), and adherence only to ⟪ar:مبدع تعالی⟫ (the Most High Creator).

When one wrings a wet cloth and removes all water from it completely, this is ⟪ar:(جَرَدَ)⟫ (to strip, to divest). Similarly, when one pulls the skin from a goat's body and separates it entirely—this too is ⟪ar:'تجرید'⟫ (abstraction, stripping away).[^cite-38] In theological terms, abstraction means to posit that among all the boundaries—spiritual and material—there is no limit that possesses the power of ⟪ar:الہیت⟫ (divinity: the capacity to give existence from non-existence). Though there are very lofty beings whose powers are magnificent—some are called ⟪ar:'قادر'⟫ (Mighty), some ⟪ar:'رازق'⟫ (Sustainer), some ⟪ar:'قھار'⟫ (Overpowering), some ⟪ar:'جبار'⟫ (Irresistible)—yet despite these tremendous attributes and powers, none of them is ⟪ar:'الہ'⟫ (a god, a creator). That is, none possesses the power to create from ⟪ar:'ہست'⟫ being, or to bring forth ⟪ar:'نیست'⟫ (non-being). Only the Creator possesses this power.[^cite-39]

## The Definition of Transcendence

It is written: "Transcendence (⟪ar:تنزیہ⟫) is the negation of all attributes from the ⟪ar:غیب الغیوب⟫ (the Hidden of the Hidden) and the acknowledgment that every attribute of nobility and majesty—every definition of ⟪ar:الٰھیت⟫ (divinity), every interpretation (the separation of one attribute from another)—whether expressed in any language or gesture, resides solely in the ⟪ar:عقل اول⟫ (First Intellect)."[^cite-40]

The word ⟪ar:تنزیہ⟫ is derived from ⟪ar:نزح⟫ (purity, cleansing). Transcendence is the name for the negation of all attributes from ⟪ar:مبدع تعالی⟫ (the Most High Creator)—the condition of being ⟪ar:مُنَزَّح عَنِ الصِّفاَت⟫ (freed from attributes). The highest of all limits—⟪ar:عقل اول⟫ (the First Intellect)—was fashioned for all intellects below it. To negate from it all the magnificent attributes by which it has been praised—whether expressed in any language or gesture—and to ascribe them solely to ⟪ar:الہیت⟫ (Divinity) is, in reality, transcendence.

⟪ar:صفات⟫ (Attributes) are those that make a noun known. Allah is infinite, yet ninety-nine attributes are known to us. If these attributes are fully grasped, knowledge of the Essence is attained. But since ⟪ar:ذات (أسم)⟫ (the Essential Self, as Name) cannot be known, neither can ⟪ar:صفات⟫ be fully apprehended. Before all ⟪ar:أسم⟫ (Names), there is the highest of Creators—⟪ar:عقل اول⟫ (the First Intellect). These ninety-nine attributes appeared for knowledge of this Being. Transcendence is precisely the removal of all these attributes [from all else and their ascription to the First Intellect].[^cite-41]

The ninety-nine Names of Allah are in reality boundaries, divided into thirty-three each. When we articulate an attribute, we cannot determine its precise measure. For instance, we cannot determine what rank ⟪ar:(سَتَّار)⟫ (the Concealer) occupies. Therefore, we ascribe all attributes to the Imam of this entire glorification—⟪ar:عقل اول⟫ (the First Intellect)—for it has been established that what exists in the part surely exists in the whole. ⟪ar:عالم روحانی، نفسانی اور جسمانی⟫ These boundaries are distributed among the spiritual, psychic, and physical realms.

**In summary:** Abstraction means to separate the attributes of the one above from oneself and from those below. Transcendence means to purify oneself ⟪ar:من فوق⟫ (from the above) and from those below oneself. This is why the Qur'an proclaims:

> *Glorified is Allah! (He is Free) from what they attribute unto Him!*

## The Difference Between "Ilah" and "Allah"

⟪ar:عُلُوٌّ⟫ (Elevation) is derived from ⟪ar:تعالی⟫ (the Most High), from which the words ⟪ar:متعال⟫ (the Transcendent) are formed. ⟪ar:الٰھیت⟫ (Divinity) has two dimensions, both related to wonder. The first is wonder at the origination—how the ⟪ar:عقل اول⟫ (First Intellect) and ⟪ar:مبدع تعالی⟫ (the Most High Creator) came into being from non-being. This wonder is called ⟪ar:ھُوِیَتِ مُتَعاَلِیَۃ⟫ (exalted identity)—wonder at the attribute, at the quality ⟪ar:ھو⟫ (of Presence).[^cite-42] ⟪ar:الہیت⟫ 

Secondly, the precedence of each upper limit over its lower counterpart is a wonder—how my upper limit preceded me in tawheed, though we were created alike. This wonder is the sign that all ⟪ar:مُبدَعَات⟫ (originated beings) wonder at themselves, and ⟪ar:عقل اول⟫ (the First Intellect) wonders at ⟪ar:مبدع تعالی (الہ)⟫ (the Creator Most High).[^cite-43]

| Ilah (God) | Allah (The God) |
| --- | --- |
| Wonder at Origination | Wonder at Precedence |
| *(The attribute of being divine)* | *(The unique Divine Reality)* |

That at which our intellect marvels and whose greatness our intellect cannot encompass is ⟪ar:الہ⟫ (an ilah, a god worthy of wonder). The kalimah (testimony of faith) is composed of three letters: Alif, Lam, and Ha. When we negate "god" (⟪ar:الہ⟫), we negate all ⟪ar:معبود (اللہ)⟫ (false deities), proclaiming that only the True Worshipped One exists. This is why the true measure of ⟪ar:مبدع تعالی⟫ (the Most High Creator) is unknowable to us—as He says: ⟪ar:وَماَ قَدرُ اﷲِ حَقَّ قَدرِہِ۔⟫[^cite-44]

In Arabic grammar, the article ⟪ar:علامت معرفہ (ال)⟫ (the definite article) makes a noun specific. When we say "book," we mean any book; when we say "the book," we mean a specific one. Allah is in fact ⟪ar:ال + الہ⟫ (the god)—that is, when the article ⟪ar:معرفہ (ال)⟫ is attached to ⟪ar:الہ⟫, it becomes ⟪ar:(اللہ)⟫. In Arabic, ⟪ar:(الہ)⟫ means ⟪ar:مالک⟫ (Lord, Master), and the true master of a thing is he who created it. In this sense, ⟪ar:الہیت⟫ (divinity) is the attribute of our Creator. Since the Almighty is the Creator of our form in the Hereafter, He is for us the ⟪ar:الہ⟫ (Divine Master). When our limit stands in the position of the highest ⟪ar:الہ⟫, all the limits above him are also in the position of the highest ⟪ar:الہ⟫ for us.[^cite-45]

We advance in the field of tawheed by knowing the boundaries and crossing them one by one until we reach a point where our thoughts cannot encompass them because of their transcendent greatness. In the attempt to know them, our intellect becomes humble, bewildered, and disoriented. At that moment, that boundary becomes for us a ⟪ar:(الہ)⟫ (a deity), and when ⟪ar:علامت معرفہ (ال)⟫ (the article of specification) is applied to it, it becomes ⟪ar:ال + الہ = اللہ⟫ (Allah) for us. According to this analogy, each person's Allah is particular to that person; thus all the Imams, the proofs, and their followers become Allah to us—because we cannot comprehend the elevation and majesty of their station, and our capacity relative to theirs is ⟪ar:وَلِھَا یَعلُہ⟫.[^cite-46]

**Statement:** We regard the Imam (upon him be peace) as divine because we are not merely astonished at his exalted station but confess that we shall never attain to his greatness. They can inscribe knowledge; we cannot. They can make theologians; we cannot.

## The Unity, Abstraction, and Transcendence of the Most High Creator

From the moment the ⟪ar:مومن مستجیب⟫ (believing respondent) speaks, he marvels at the hierarchy of the ⟪ar:مکاسر⟫ (the Breaker), and in his amazement, the makasir becomes his ⟪ar:الہ⟫ (his divine master). Then he sees that the makasir himself wonders at the one ⟪ar:ماذون⟫ (the Authorized Delegate) above him. Witnessing this, he thinks: "It is not the makasir who is ultimate, but rather the one above him." Then he sees that the authorized delegate also wonders at the one above him. He perceives this same wonder in every circle until he carries his amazement to the point of the master of physical limits—⟪ar:امام علیہ السلام⟫ (the Imam, upon him be peace).

Upon reaching this station, he sees that the Imam himself is astonished at the spiritual limits above him. At this point, he begins to explore the spiritual dimensions and reaches the realm of his own astonishment—⟪ar:عقل اول⟫ (the First Intellect), chief of the spiritual boundaries. Upon arriving there, he sees that ⟪ar:عقل اول⟫ itself wonders at ⟪ar:مبدع تعالی⟫ (the Most High Creator) and has its own ⟪ar:الہ⟫ (divine master). He recognizes that access to the highest point is impossible. He comes to understand that none of these boundaries—neither spiritual nor physical—possesses the attribute of ⟪ar:الہیت⟫ (divinity): none can originate being from non-being.[^cite-47] When the believer has attained this tawheed through knowledge of all boundaries, he is told:

> ⟪quran 89:27-28⟫
> ⟪ar:‏يَٓأَيَّتُهَا ٱلنَّفْسُ ٱلْمُطْمَئِنَّةُ ‎ ‏ٱرْجِعِىٓ إِلَىٰ رَبِّكِ رَاضِيَةًۭ مَّرْضِيَّةًۭ ‎⟫
> *(It will be said to the pious): "O (you) the one in (complete) rest and satisfaction! Come back to your Lord, Well-pleased (yourself) and well-pleasing unto Him!"*

O thou Soul of Contentment—for thou hast attained contentment through tawheed—return to thy Lord, well-pleased with Him and He well-pleased with thee.[^cite-48]

After this divine address, he begins the process of negation (⟪ar:نفی الہیت⟫). Confused about the ⟪ar:عقل اول⟫ (First Intellect), he declares it to be not-a-god (⟪ar:عقل اول الہ⟫), bringing it back to the ⟪ar:صفت الٰہیت⟫ (attribute of divinity) and passing through all the spiritual and physical boundaries, confessing that none of these limits is his true ⟪ar:الہ⟫ (Creator). He acknowledges that all the attributes that dwell in these limits are not attached to them but only to ⟪ar:مبدع تعالی⟫ (the Most High Creator) and ⟪ar:عقل اول⟫ (the First Intellect). In this way, after ⟪ar:تجرید⟫ (abstraction) and ⟪ar:تنزیہ⟫ (transcendence), he attains ⟪ar:توحید⟫ (unity).[^cite-49]

That is, the believer accepts each boundary as divine (because of the majesty of its rank) and carries his wonder to the upper realms until he reaches the ⟪ar:عقل اول⟫ (First Intellect). Upon arrival, he begins the process of ⟪ar:نفی الہیت⟫ (negating divinity) and brings it downward through each circle until he returns to himself (the ⟪ar:مکاسر⟫). Upon reaching this point, he is convinced that ⟪ar:مبدع تعالی⟫ (the Most High Creator) cannot be characterized by the attributes of creation, and he purifies himself of all ⟪ar:اسمآء⟫ (divine names) other than those belonging to transcendence.

Thus he attains true ⟪ar:توحید⟫ (unity)—through knowledge of all boundaries—⟪ar:تنزیخ (نفی الہیۃ)⟫ (transcendence: negation of false divinity) and ⟪ar:تجرید (صلب الصفات الہیۃ)⟫ (abstraction: stripping of divine attributes).[^cite-50]

## The Unity, Abstraction, and Transcendence of the Highest Limit

### The Unity of the Highest Limit

⟪ar:توحید⟫ (Unity) of the exalted one means recognizing the majesty of his rank and acknowledging that my exaltation is higher in station than I am and that I am inferior to him. It means acknowledging that he is unique in his rank and has no partner in his degree.

### The Abstraction of the Limit

To perform ⟪ar:تجرید⟫ (abstraction) of the highest is to admit that I do not possess the attribute of ⟪ar:الہیت⟫ (divinity)—that I lack the capacity to originate my own form and to elevate myself to the station of the highest, even though the Almighty created us both in an equivalent ⟪ar:(کمال اول)⟫ (primary perfection). Only my exaltation can elevate me to my rank. I cannot do so myself. The Creator fashions the form of my Lord; I cannot fashion the form of the Creator. To strip ⟪ar:صفت الہیت⟫ (the attribute of divinity) from oneself and from all lower boundaries—this is true abstraction.

### The Transcendence of the Limit

⟪ar:تنزیہ⟫ (Transcendence) means acknowledging that all the excellent qualities I possess—knowledge, beauty, kindness, generosity, miracles, and signs—are lesser versions of those that reside in my superior. If I am ignorant, my superior is supremely knowledgeable. If I am a worshipper, my superior is the object of worship. If I am learned, my superior is incomparably wiser, and I am ignorant before him. Whatever good qualities I possess, these exist in my superior in infinite measure. To remove from one's own exalted one all the attributes of one's own being—this is true ⟪ar:تنزیہ⟫.

To establish the conviction that my Almighty grants me knowledge; I do not grant knowledge to him. He provides for me; I do not provide for him. He sustains me; I cannot sustain him. It is the negation of one's own exalted one from one's own attributes that constitutes his ⟪ar:تنزیہ⟫.
