# The Marriage of the Outer and the Inner

<!-- section 1 (id=1058, raw_sort=1): ہر ظاہر کے پیچھے باطن ہے -->

## Every Manifest Form Conceals an Inner Reality

The word "Zahir" (ظَہَرَ⟫) derives from the Arabic root denoting the back or exterior, while "Batin" (⟪ar:بَطَنْ⟫) signifies the belly or interior. The vital organs (⟪ar:اعضاء رئیسۃ⟫) of the human body reside not upon the back but within the belly; what is truly real and substantial (⟪ar:بَطَنْ⟫) lies concealed, while the outer form (⟪ar:ظَہَرَ⟫) serves merely as an indicator pointing toward that hidden truth. So too with the outward commandments of the Sharīʿa (sacred law): each external injunction points inward to its inner meaning.

Behind every manifest appearance exists an inner dimension in which the inward precedes the outward. The outer garment wraps around the inner, much as a peel encloses fruit. The spiritual realm (⟪ar:عالم روحانی⟫) thus precedes the material realm (⟪ar:عالم جسمانی⟫); the essence of the self precedes the body. The outward always gestures toward the inner self. Similarly, the parable (⟪ar:مثل ممثول⟫), words, and intellect (⟪ar:عقل معقول⟫) all imply their meanings through correspondence. For this reason intention (niyyah) is placed before action: man's deed reflects his inner intent. Intention constitutes the inward dimension; action is the outer expression of that intention.[^cite-1]

We observe in this world that every tangible thing possesses both an outer and an inner aspect. Everything manifests itself through one of the five senses (⟪ar:حواس خمسۃ⟫). Yet everything that pertains to knowledge and reasoning—properties of an object, its benefits, its defects—cannot be perceived through the outer senses (⟪ar:حواس ظاہرہ⟫) alone. The outward form dominates its inner counterpart; the outer is visible while the inner remains hidden, just as the body is visible and the soul is concealed. Words exist openly, yet their meanings lie hidden within them. For this reason, the outward and inward are called spouses and partners of one another. As Allāh declares:

> ⟪quran 51:49⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَمِن كُلِّ شَىْءٍ خَلَقْنَا زَوْجَيْنِ لَعَلَّكُمْ تَذَكَّرُونَ ‎⟫
> *And of everything We have created pairs, that you may remember (the Grace of Allah).*

If whatever is mentioned in the Qur'ān and Hadīth is a "thing" (⟪ar:شئی⟫), then it must necessarily be paired according to this verse, so that by this pairing Allāh is distinguished by oneness (⟪ar:وحدانیت⟫). Allāh alone (⟪ar:أحد⟫) has no partner or correspondence; all beside Him must exist in pairs.

### The Correspondence of Outer and Inner: Proof of Divine Oneness

Man appears outwardly as a single individual, yet he is a composite of body and soul—the body manifest and the soul concealed. When we examine both, each reveals itself to be divided into two principles. The body is a mixture of coldness (⟪ar:برودت⟫) and dryness (⟪ar:یبوست⟫), while the soul is a mixture of heat (⟪ar:حرارت⟫) and moisture (⟪ar:رطوبت⟫). When the soul departs the body, the body becomes cold and dry—evidence of this fundamental duality.

| Body | Spirit |
| --- | --- |
| Cold + Dryness | Heat + Moisture |

Examining all things in creation, we discover that everything possesses a spouse, for the material world divides into three states of substance:

| Solid | Liquid | Gas |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Solid | Liquid | Vapors |

Every substance further divides into two aspects: matter (⟪ar:مادہ⟫) and form (⟪ar:صورت⟫), or essence (⟪ar:ذات⟫) and attributes (⟪ar:صفات⟫).[^cite-2] Nothing in existence is devoid of this duality. All things are either hot or cold, dry or wet, present or absent, large or small, solid or fluid, alive or dead, dense or subtle, heavy or light. The Almighty alone transcends every such attribute and quality. He cannot be called existent or non-existent, many or few—His existence is proven by His signs and is utterly distinct from all creation. Allāh says:

> ⟪quran 42:11⟫
> ⟪ar:‏فَاطِرُ ٱلسَّمَوَتِ وَٱلْأَرْضِ ۚ جَعَلَ لَكُم مِّنْ أَنفُسِكُمْ أَزْوَجًۭا وَمِنَ ٱلْأَنْعَمِ أَزْوَجًۭا ۖ يَذْرَؤُكُمْ فِيهِ ۚ لَيْسَ كَمِثْلِهِۦ شَىْءٌۭ ۖ وَهُوَ ٱلسَّمِيعُ ٱلْبَصِيرُ ‎⟫
> *The Creator of the heavens and the earth. He has made for you mates from yourselves, and for the cattle (also) mates. By this means He creates you (in the wombs). There is nothing like unto Him, and He is the All-Hearer, the All-Seer.*

In the first verse Allāh declares, "Everything (⟪ar:'شئی'⟫) has a spouse," while in this verse He proclaims, "Nothing (⟪ar:'شئی'⟫) is like unto Him." By this paradox, the Divine oneness (tawḥīd) is proven: creation exists in pairs, yet the Creator alone stands without equal or correspondence.

## The Significance of Parable and Reality

Whosoever follows the Sharīʿa by understanding both the parable and its reality will be counted among the foremost (⟪ar:'سابقین'⟫)—the elect who grasped the truth from the very beginning. Those among the people of truth who, having not discerned the parable from its reality, merely stand by the outer law, shall be among the followers and those who come late (⟪ar:'تابعین و لاحقین'⟫).[^cite-3] The outward is established upon the inner, from which the outward becomes beneficial; the inner is confirmed by the outward, from which the inner becomes beneficial. Allāh commands:

> ⟪quran 6:120⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَذَرُوا۟ ظَهِرَ ٱلْإِثْمِ وَبَاطِنَهُۥٓ ۚ إِنَّ ٱلَّذِينَ يَكْسِبُونَ ٱلْإِثْمَ سَيُجْزَوْنَ بِمَا كَانُوا۟ يَقْتَرِفُونَ ‎⟫
> *Leave (O mankind, all kinds of) sin, open and secret. Verily, those who commit sin will get due recompense for that which they used to commit.*

Because the two dimensions of worship are inseparably bound:

| Pillar of Faith | Inner Significance in the Spiritual Realm |
| --- | --- |
| Ritual Purity (ṭahāra) | Wash away the filth created by inclining one's ear toward Satan, through knowledge of the Imams |
| Prayer (ṣalāt) | Remaining steadfastly at the threshold of the Imams' summons (daʿwa) |
| Alms-giving (zakāt) | Bestowing the forty portions of knowledge upon the deserving |
| Fasting (ṣawm) | Concealing knowledge from the undeserving |
| Pilgrimage to the House of Allāh (Hajj) | The House of Allāh is the inner sanctuary; preparing one's form to enter the congregation of the inner community |
| Striving (jihād) | Endeavoring to remove the evils born of heeding the self and its impositions; acting with thought, word, and deed in accordance with truth |

## The Relationship Between Parable and Reality

There exists always a special correspondence between parable and reality: the same qualities present in the reality must also inhere in the parable. This correspondence between parable and reality is termed taʾwīl (⟪ar:'تاویل'⟫), or esoteric exegesis. For this reason, all the speakers of divine law (nuṭaqāʾ) set forth a parable for their Sharīʿa, so that ascent from parable to reality becomes possible. Noah (⟪ar:نوح علیہ السلام⟫), for instance, made the ark his parable; Abraham (⟪ar:ابراھیم علیہ السلام⟫) made the Ka'ba (⟪ar:کعبہ اللہ⟫); Moses (⟪ar:موسیٰ علیہ السلام⟫) and Jesus (⟪ar:عیسی علیہ السلام⟫) employed their own parables; and Muhammad al-Muṣṭafā (⟪ar:محمد مصطفی صلی ﷲ علیہ و آلہ وسلم⟫) made the declaration "There is no god but Allāh" (⟪ar:'کلمہ لا الہ الا اﷲ'⟫) the parable for his law. The parable is the outward, the exoteric is its inward; the manifest always points to its concealed reality. Water serves as a parable; knowledge (⟪ar:'علم'⟫), its reality. In the worldly sense, all physical properties of water are mirrored in its counterpart knowledge.

| Water (Parable) | Knowledge (ilm) |
| --- | --- |
| Water flows eternally from higher to lower | Knowledge descends always from the higher to the lower; what arrives from one's own level is mere information, not knowledge |
| Water purifies the body | Knowledge purifies the soul of ignorance and darkness |
| Water takes the form of the vessel into which it pours | Knowledge becomes like the vessel that receives it; if received by the righteous, it yields benefit; if by the wicked, harm |
| Water is the life of all creatures | Knowledge is the life of all souls |
| Water remains pure so long as it flows; if stalled, it corrupts | Knowledge remains pure when transferred from preacher to preacher; if blocked, doubt and confusion enter it |
| Water assumes different states—ice, liquid, vapor—according to heat and cold | Knowledge too has different degrees and refinements (subtle knowledge vs. dense knowledge); Sharīʿa and Knowledge of Interpretation (taʾwīl) |

Water comprises two elements (hydrogen and oxygen): hydrogen with two atoms and oxygen with one. Knowledge likewise comprises two things—the sayings of the Prophet (peace be upon him) and the wisdom of Maulānā ʿAlī (⟪ar:'علم'⟫). In inheritance law, the male heir receives two shares and the female one share; thus hydrogen corresponds to the male realm and oxygen to the female. In religion, man embodies the essence of the Messenger of Allāh, for his knowledge is double; ʿAlī embodies the example of woman, whose knowledge is half that of the Messenger.

| H₂ | O |
| --- | --- |
| Sayings of the Messenger of Allāh | The Wisdom of Maulānā ʿAlī |
| Man | Woman |

In the same manner, behind every outer parable of the world there must exist an inner parable within religion. All commandments of the Sharīʿa bind the body; knowledge of the inner meanings of these commandments binds the soul.

## The Highest Parable

Allāh declares in the Qur'ān:

> ⟪quran 16:60⟫
> ⟪ar:‏لِلَّذِينَ لَا يُؤْمِنُونَ بِٱلْآخِرَةِ مَثَلُ ٱلسَّوْءِ ۖ وَلِلَّهِ ٱلْمَثَلُ ٱلْأَعْلَىٰ ۚ وَهُوَ ٱلْعَزِيزُ ٱلْحَكِيمُ ‎⟫
> *For those who believe not in the Hereafter is an evil description, and for Allah is the highest description. And He is the All-Mighty, the All-Wise.*

Allāh (⟪ar:'اللہ'⟫) is a self (dhāt) that is invisible and possesses no material form. To envision Allāh as something concrete is to commit association (shirk). Since Allāh Himself is a parable, it becomes necessary to set forth a parable for Him. Every circle and station is called the "chief" (imām) and "chief of the chief," for we cannot fully grasp His majesty; we lack the capacity to comprehend His greatness utterly. We only acknowledge the existence of this chief (whether Imam or guide) and approach his knowledge through his boundaries. Whatever we ponder, whatever exceeds our understanding, we call "Allāh" (⟪ar:'اللہ'⟫). In this verse, "the highest parable" (⟪ar:' اَلمَثَلُ الأَعلَی'⟫) refers to the chief of each circle (⟪ar:مثل الأعلی⟫). This is so because, though they are not Allāh, they possess all the powers of Allāh.

In the circles of believers, the Imam (⟪ar:امام علیہ السلام⟫) is the highest parable (⟪ar:مثل الأعلی⟫); in the circles of Imamate itself, Maulānā ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib (⟪ar:مولانا علی ابن ابی طالب علیہ السلام⟫) is the highest parable. If we desire to understand ʿAlī, we must first understand the Imam, for ʿAlī is to the Imam as the parable is to its reality—ʿAlī himself is called "the Great Eye" (ʿayn ʿaẓīma).

| Level of Understanding | Parable | Reality | Chief |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Among Believers | Imam | ʿAlī | Great Eye |
| Among the Appointed | Dāʿī (Summoner) | Imam | — |
| Among the Unbelievers | Believer | Dāʿī | — |

## The Outer and Inner Meanings in the Qur'ān

Allāh the Most High mentions the outward and inward repeatedly in the Qur'ān, declaring: "Allāh has bestowed upon you His blessings both manifest and hidden."

> ⟪quran 31:20⟫
> ⟪ar:‏أَلَمْ تَرَوْا۟ أَنَّ ٱللَّهَ سَخَّرَ لَكُم مَّا فِى ٱلسَّمَوَتِ وَمَا فِى ٱلْأَرْضِ وَأَسْبَغَ عَلَيْكُمْ نِعَمَهُۥ ظَهِرَةًۭ وَبَاطِنَةًۭ ۗ وَمِنَ ٱلنَّاسِ مَن يُجَدِلُ فِى ٱللَّهِ بِغَيْرِ عِلْمٍۢ وَلَا هُدًۭى وَلَا كِتَبٍۢ مُّنِيرٍۢ ‎⟫
> *See you not (O men) that Allah has subjected for you whatsoever is in the heavens and whatsoever is in the earth, and has completed and perfected His Graces upon you, (both) apparent and hidden? Yet of mankind is he who disputes about Allah without knowledge or guidance or a Book giving light!*

Then He says: "You shall be questioned on that Day about these blessings."

> ⟪quran 102:8⟫
> ⟪ar:‏ثُمَّ لَتُسْـَٔلُنَّ يَوْمَئِذٍ عَنِ ٱلنَّعِيمِ ‎⟫
> *Then, on that Day, you shall be asked about the delight (you indulged in, in this world)!*

This verse establishes that inner blessings exist in contrast to outer blessings. These inner blessings—the Imams (⟪ar:ائمہ علیہم السلام⟫)—are those concerning which one will be questioned on the Day of Judgment: why did you deny their guardianship (wilāya)?

The appointed Summoner (dāʿī), Maulānā Muḥammad Shākir (⟪ar:حضرت المنصوب مولانا محمد شاکر صاحب⟫), taught that after a thousand years, one of the greatest blessings that humanity denied is the guardianship of Sayyidnā Sulaymān ibn Ḥasan (⟪ar:سیدنا سلیمان بن حسن اعلی اﷲ قدسہ⟫). On the Day of Judgment, the question will be whether this Sulaymānī (⟪ar:سلیمانی⟫) blessing was recognized or denied. Proof of this exists in the science of numerology (ʿilm al-jafar): the numerical value of "the Bliss" (⟪ar:'النعیم'⟫) equals that of "Sulaymānī" (⟪ar:'سلیمانی'⟫), just as the value of "Lord" (⟪ar:'رب'⟫) equals that of "Muḥammad and ʿAlī" (⟪ar:'محمد + علی'⟫).[^cite-4]

| al-Naʿīm (The Bliss) | Sulaymānī |
| --- | --- |
| 201 | 201 |
| al-Rabb (The Lord) | Muḥammad + ʿAlī |
| 202 | 202 |

The Messenger of Allāh (⟪ar:رسول اکرم (ﷺ)⟫) declared: "No verse of the Qur'ān has been revealed to me that does not possess an outer and an inner meaning." Elsewhere Allāh says: "Abandon both the manifest and the hidden sins!"

> ⟪quran 6:120⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَذَرُوا۟ ظَهِرَ ٱلْإِثْمِ وَبَاطِنَهُۥٓ ۚ إِنَّ ٱلَّذِينَ يَكْسِبُونَ ٱلْإِثْمَ سَيُجْزَوْنَ بِمَا كَانُوا۟ يَقْتَرِفُونَ ‎⟫
> *Leave (O mankind, all kinds of) sin, open and secret. Verily, those who commit sin will get due recompense for that which they used to commit.*

It is of utmost importance to know the inner sin alongside the outer, for he who knows not the inner sin shall surely fall into it and merit punishment in the Hereafter. If the outer sin is denial of the outer blessings, then the inner sin is denial of the inner blessing—the Imam. Then Allāh declares:

> ⟪quran 3:7⟫
> ⟪ar:‏هُوَ ٱلَّذِىٓ أَنزَلَ عَلَيْكَ ٱلْكِتَبَ مِنْهُ آيَتٌۭ مُّحْكَمَتٌ هُنَّ أُمُّ ٱلْكِتَبِ وَأُخَرُ مُتَشَبِهَتٌۭ ۖ فَأَمَّا ٱلَّذِينَ فِى قُلُوبِهِمْ زَيْغٌۭ فَيَتَّبِعُونَ مَا تَشَبَهَ مِنْهُ ٱبْتِغَآءَ ٱلْفِتْنَةِ وَٱبْتِغَآءَ تَأْوِيلِهِۦ ۗ وَمَا يَعْلَمُ تَأْوِيلَهُۥٓ إِلَّا ٱللَّهُ وَٱلرَّسِخُونَ فِى ٱلْعِلْمِ ۗ يَقُولُونَ آمَنَّا بِهِۦ كُلٌّۭ مِّنْ عِندِ رَبِّنَا ۗ وَمَا يَذَّكَّرُ إِلَّآ أُو۟لُوا۟ ٱلْأَلْبَبِ ‎⟫
> *It is He Who has sent down to you (Muhammad PBUH) the Book (this Quran). In it are Verses that are entirely clear, they are the foundations of the Book; and others not entirely clear. So as for those in whose hearts there is a deviation (from the truth) they follow that which is not entirely clear thereof, seeking Al-Fitnah (polytheism and trials, etc.), and seeking for its hidden meanings, but none knows its hidden meanings save Allah. And those who are firmly grounded in knowledge say: "We believe in it; the whole of it (clear and unclear Verses) are from our Lord." And none receive admonition except men of understanding.*

Some verses of the Qur'ān are clear and unambiguous—the definitive verses (⟪ar:محکمات⟫)—which form the foundation of the Book. Others are allegorical (⟪ar:متشابھات⟫), their meanings not immediately apparent to all. The true significations of these verses lie concealed within the fabric of their words. Regarding the allegorical verses, Allāh declares: "Only Allāh knows their true meaning, and those firmly grounded in knowledge (⟪ar:راسخون فی العلم⟫)"—these are the Imams of the Ahl al-Bayt (upon them be peace).[^cite-5] 

This stands as one of the Qur'ān's great miracles: though it speaks with a single tongue outwardly, it contains within itself countless hidden meanings. Just as none save Muḥammad al-Muṣṭafā (peace be upon him) could explain the outward meaning of the Qur'ān, so too none save the Imams of the Ahl al-Bayt possesses the authority to expound its inner meanings. They reveal these meanings to those who are worthy according to their capacity, while concealing them from the undeserving—forbidding them to impart such knowledge, for Allāh has granted this authority to them alone:

> ⟪quran 38:39⟫
> ⟪ar:‏هَذَا عَطَآؤُنَا فَٱمْنُنْ أَوْ أَمْسِكْ بِغَيْرِ حِسَابٍۢ ‎⟫
> *[Saying of Allah to Sulaiman (Solomon)]: "This is Our gift, so spend you or withhold, no account will be asked."*

## Auspicious and Inauspicious Days

Among the 354 days of the lunar year, certain days and nights hold special significance for the believers; these are exalted occasions. These outer days correspond to the rotation of the celestial spheres and are measured by sunrise and sunset. Yet these manifest days possess no innate knowledge of themselves; they have no awareness of their own comings and goings, for they move under compulsion, not by choice. If the days themselves do not recognize their own fortune or misfortune, whence could they derive the power to bestow blessing upon others? How could such virtue arise in them that some would be called the Days of Allāh and be revered, while others are termed ill-omened and deemed heavy and evil?

The greatness and virtue of these outer days are proven only when they are united with their inner counterparts—when it is understood that Allāh's chosen ones (the Prophets and the Imams) are in truth the Days of Allāh: they are living, sovereign, and self-subsisting. They themselves recognize their own fortune and greatness; the outer auspicious days are auspicious precisely because they point to these inner, spiritually auspicious beings. The outward days resemble parables; the inner beings are their realities. In the same way, inauspicious days signify inauspicious and unfortunate souls among men.[^cite-6]

## The Pairing of the Months of the Year

Just as all things in the universe are created in pairs, so too Allāh created the months of the year in pairs. Sayyidnā Sulaymān (⟪ar:سیدنا سلیمان صاحب⟫) teaches that Muḥarram al-Ḥarām (⟪ar:محرم الحرام⟫) and Shawwāl (⟪ar:شوال⟫) share the same day of the week on their first of the month, for they are spouses to one another. Muḥarram (⟪ar:محرم⟫) has thirty days while Shawwāl (⟪ar:شوال⟫) has twenty-nine; Muḥarram is the first month of the year and Shawwāl the tenth, and one corresponds to ten. Similarly, among the remaining months, wedlock is established between every two: the perfect months (30 days) occupy the station of husband or spirit, while the imperfect months (29 days) occupy the station of wife or body.

| Month | Days | First Day | Spouse Month | Days | First Day |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Muḥarram (1) | 30 | Sunday | Shawwāl (10) | 29 | Sunday |
| Ṣafar (2) | 29 | Tuesday | Rajab al-Ḥarām (7) | 30 | Tuesday |
| Rabīʿ al-Awwal (3) | 30 | Wednesday | Dhū al-Ḥijja (12) | 29 | Wednesday |
| Rabīʿ al-Ākhir (4) | 29 | Friday | Ramaḍān al-Ḥarām (9) | 30 | Friday |
| Jumādā al-Awwal (5) | 30 | Thursday | Shaʿbān al-Karīm (8) | 29 | Thursday |
| Jumādā al-Ākhir (6) | 29 | Monday | Dhū al-Qaʿda (11) | 30 | Monday |

### Jumādā al-Awwal and Shaʿbān al-Karīm

Among all the months, there are two—Jumādā al-Awwal (⟪ar:جمادی الاول⟫) and Shaʿbān al-Karīm (⟪ar:شعبان کریم⟫)—which, though paired with one another, do not align on the day of their first date. This is because these two months are incomparable and singular in their nature: the first day of neither falls on the same day of the week as the other, nor as the first day of any other month. They are paired yet misaligned, indicating a unique and incomparable relationship. Shaʿbān refers to the month of the Messenger of Allāh (⟪ar:رسول اللہ (ﷺ)⟫), and Jumādā al-Awwal to the month of Maulātnā Fāṭima (⟪ar:مولاتنا فاطمۃ⟫), whose martyrdom occurred in that month. The fact that the first days of these two months do not coincide is a sign that these two luminaries—Muḥammad (⟪ar:محمد صلعم⟫) and Fāṭima (⟪ar:مولاتنا فاطمۃ⟫)—are not to be found together in any moment of time throughout the year.[^cite-7]

| Shaʿbān al-Karīm | Jumādā al-Awwal |
| --- | --- |
| The Messenger of Allāh (ṣallallāhu ʿalayhi wa ālihi) | Maulātnā Fāṭima |

### Jumādā al-Awwal, Jumādā al-Ākhir, and Dhū al-Qaʿda

The fifth month is Jumādā al-Awwal (⟪ar:جمادی الاول⟫) and the sixth is Jumādā al-Ākhir (⟪ar:جمادی الاخر⟫). When five and six are added, the result is eleven, and the eleventh month is Dhū al-Qaʿda (⟪ar:ذوالقعدۃ⟫). The number eleven equals the numerical value of "He" (⟪ar:' ھو'⟫), concerning which all intellects are perplexed and unable to comprehend His majesty.

Muḥammad al-Muṣṭafā (⟪ar:محمد مصطفی (ﷺ) جمادی الآخر⟫) is the eleventh Imam, and Maulānā al-Mahdī Billāh (⟪ar:مولانا مہدی باللہ صلوٰۃ اللہ علیہ⟫) is the final Imam; both are associated with the letter "ʾalif" (⟪ar:ذوقعدۃ⟫). The fact that the first day of these two months aligns is proof of the special bond between them. Just as the Messenger of Allāh (peace be upon him) was he who revealed Islam after the age of ignorance, so too when the era of Imam Jaʿfar al-Ṣādiq (⟪ar:امام جعفر الصادق ؑ⟫) reached seventy, the Imam al-Mahdī (⟪ar:امام مہدی علیہ السلام⟫) was the first to establish the Sharīʿa of Islam anew.[^cite-8]

## The Two Divisions of the Sacred Law

Amīr al-Muʾminīn ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib (⟪ar:أمیر المؤمنین علی ابن ابی طالب علیہ السلام⟫) declared: "Acquire understanding of the religion first, then engage in commerce." The world and religion are not separate realities but two forms of a single essence—the inner and the outer. The spiritual dimension of religion can only be realized through physical actions in the world, and religion's final destination is attained only by traversing the world's path. For this reason, both must be approached together to fulfill the needs of humanity (⟪ar:قامت الفیۃ⟫).

Most people imagine that only the ritual acts of the Sharīʿa (prayer, fasting, pilgrimage) constitute religious practice. This is untrue. Everything done by command of Allāh and His Messenger is an act of religion—whether eating, drinking, sleeping, or spending time with family and friends. Even the etiquette of using the toilet and the observance of marital relations are part of the religion. The sole condition is that worldly actions be performed with a religious intent and aim toward the pleasure of Allāh.

Man's soul and body are two forms of a single essence. The subtle form is called soul (⟪ar:'عقل'⟫, intellect; ⟪ar:'نفس'⟫, self), and the gross form is called body (⟪ar:'جسم'⟫); the subtle self (⟪ar:نفس⟫) mirrors the gross body. Similarly, the subtle form of religion is called knowledge, and the gross form is called the world. The Almighty divided His Sharīʿa into two parts: one concerning the outward actions of the Sharīʿa, which He entrusted to the Messenger of Allāh; the other concerning knowledge of the esoteric interpretation (taʾwīl) of that Sharīʿa, which He entrusted to ʿAlī. Yet in reality, both are two forms of a single light, just as Muḥammad and ʿAlī are two aspects of a single radiance. He made knowledge (⟪ar:'علم'⟫) obligatory upon the subtle self because it is the refined element of the human being, and action (⟪ar:'عمل'⟫) obligatory upon the body because it is the dense element.

| | Subtle | Gross |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Religion | Knowledge | Action |
| Human Being | Self | Body |

Therefore, in prayer we recite: "I approach the Subtle with the subtle, and the Gross with the gross"—that is, I direct my subtle self toward the spiritual Ka'ba and my gross body toward the physical House of Allāh.

The Messenger of Allāh (⟪ar:رسول اللہ (ﷺ)⟫) declared in a ḥadīth: "If you seek success, take religion and the world together and establish the balance (⟪ar:'میزان'⟫) between them!" This is what is meant by piety (taqwā) (⟪ar:'تقوی'⟫)—joining knowledge and action in equilibrium.[^cite-10]

## The Speakers of Divine Law and the Difference in Their Sharīʿas

Sayyidnā Idrīs ibn Ḥasan (⟪ar:سیدنا ادریس بن حسن اعلی اللہ قدسہ⟫), expounding this philosophy, states: "From Adam (⟪ar:رسول اللہ (ﷺ)⟫) there have been six speakers of the law (nuṭaqāʾ). The common understanding differs fundamentally from the truth. The masses believe that each prophet abolished the law preceding him and promulgated his own; the reality is that each speaker preserved the Sharīʿa of his predecessor and added to it his own ordinances, thereby adapting them to the changing circumstances and the exigencies that arose in his era.

Adam (⟪ar:آدم علیہ السلام⟫) brought guardianship (⟪ar:'ولایت'⟫). After him came Noah, who preserved Adam's law and added purity (⟪ar:'طہارت'⟫). In truth, Noah's Sharīʿa is the esoteric interpretation of Adam's guardianship (⟪ar:ولایت⟫). As the ages progressed, the problems multiplied, and the Sharīʿa of Noah (⟪ar:نوح علیہ السلام⟫) became the outer teaching, while its interpretation (⟪ar:'تاویل'⟫) was the guardianship of Adam (⟪ar:حضرت نوح علیہ السلام⟫). After Noah came Abraham (⟪ar:حضرت ابراھیم علیہ السلام⟫), who preserved Noah's law and added prayer (⟪ar:' صلوٰۃ '⟫). Abraham's Sharīʿa is in truth the interpretation of Adam's guardianship, Noah's purity, and Abraham's prayer combined.

After Abraham came Moses (⟪ar:حضرت موسی علیہ السلام⟫), who retained the Sharīʿa of his predecessor and added alms-giving (⟪ar:' زکوٰۃ '⟫). The Sharīʿa of Moses becomes the interpretation of guardianship, purity, prayer, and the alms-giving added by Moses.

After Moses came Jesus (⟪ar:حضرت عیسیٰ علیہ السلام⟫), who kept the law of Moses and added fasting (⟪ar:'صوم'⟫). The Sharīʿa of Jesus becomes the interpretation of all previous principles plus fasting.

Finally came Muḥammad al-Muṣṭafā (⟪ar:حضرت محمد مصطفی صلی اﷲ علیہ و آلہ و سلم⟫), the Seal of the Prophets and Messengers (⟪ar:خاتم الانبیاء و المرسلین⟫), who retained the law of Jesus and added pilgrimage (⟪ar:'حج'⟫). The Sharīʿa of the Prophet (⟪ar:رسول اللہ (ﷺ)⟫) is the interpretation of Adam's guardianship, Noah's purity, Abraham's prayer, Moses' alms-giving, and Jesus' fasting.

The Greatest Speaker—the sixth (⟪ar:ناطق اعظم سادس⟫)—studied the Sharīʿa of the previous five speakers through five teachers (⟪ar:آدم، نوح، ابراھیم، موسی، عیسی⟫). When he had mastered both the outer and inner aspects of their laws, he compiled his own Sharīʿa incorporating the exoteric and esoteric foundations of all preceding Sharīʿas. As the Seventh Speaker, Muḥammad ibn Ismāʿīl (⟪ar:ناطق سابع، مولانا محمد بن اسماعیل علیہ السلام⟫), when he came, did not establish a new Sharīʿa but rather preserved and carried forward the law of the Messenger of Allāh according to its inner and outer dimensions (⟪ar:من وعن⟫). And when the Qāʾim (⟪ar:قائم علی ذکرہ السلام⟫) shall come, he will not alter the Sharīʿa of the Messenger of Allāh but will take it toward the ultimate unveiling.

When the Qāʾim comes, he will make it obligatory upon the believers to engage in striving (jihād) (⟪ar:'جہاد'⟫), which shall be the interpretation of all previous speakers' Sharīʿas. At that time, such a comprehensive law will be established that the two dimensions—action and knowledge—will become unified, and the distinction between outer and inner will vanish. Similarly, upon the death of our physical forms, religion and world will become one, and no separation between them will remain.

Each speaker was obliged to practice the commandments of his own Sharīʿa. For this reason ʿAlī (⟪ar:أمیر المؤمنین علی ابن ابی طالب علیہ السلام⟫) is called a "partial qāʾim" (⟪ar:'قائم جزوی'⟫) because he fulfilled the obligation of jihād in practice through the battles of the Camel (⟪ar:جنگ جمل⟫), Ṣiffīn (⟪ar:صفین⟫), and Nahrawān (⟪ar:نحروان⟫). He not only acted but made it incumbent upon the believers of his time.[^cite-11]

## The Speaker and the Divine Law: Their Codification

One of the pillars of faith, and the most robust of its foundations, is adherence to the Sharīʿa (⟪ar:'شریعت'⟫). Through the divine law (nāmūs) (⟪ar:'ناموس'⟫), the speaker commands and forbids. All such ordinances rest upon the divine law (⟪ar:أوامر و نواحی⟫). The nāmūs is the straight path whose destination is Paradise, for those who follow it remain perpetually distant from sin.

The most blessed and noble deed in the sight of Allāh, and the most exalted of human acts (⟪ar:اعمال بشریۃ⟫), is that which can only be performed by persons of particular station—a station approaching the rank of the angels. This is the station of the lawgiver (⟪ar:' نطقا '⟫). In other words, in the sight of Allāh, the establishment of the Sharīʿa represents the highest and most excellent deed.

The relationship of all those who establish the Sharīʿa, those who follow them, and those who follow the Sunnah after receiving the divine law's ordinances from those guides is like sky, rain, earth, and vegetation. The speaker is like the sky (⟪ar:'بارش'⟫), those who hear and memorize the word are like the earth (⟪ar:'زمین'⟫) that absorbs the rain, and the result—the fruits arising from hearing and acting upon the word—is like vegetation (⟪ar:'نبات'⟫). This benefit issues from devotion and righteous deeds. Allāh declares:

> ⟪quran 13:17⟫
> ⟪ar:‏أَنزَلَ مِنَ ٱلسَّمَآءِ مَآءًۭ فَسَالَتْ أَوْدِيَةٌۢ بِقَدَرِهَا فَٱحْتَمَلَ ٱلسَّيْلُ زَبَدًۭا رَّابِيًۭا ۚ وَمِمَّا يُوقِدُونَ عَلَيْهِ فِى ٱلنَّارِ ٱبْتِغَآءَ حِلْيَةٍ أَوْ مَتَعٍۢ زَبَدٌۭ مِّثْلُهُۥ ۚ كَذَلِكَ يَضْرِبُ ٱللَّهُ ٱلْحَقَّ وَٱلْبَاطِلَ ۚ فَأَمَّا ٱلزَّبَدُ فَيَذْهَبُ جُفَآءًۭ ۖ وَأَمَّا مَا يَنفَعُ ٱلنَّاسَ فَيَمْكُثُ فِى ٱلْأَرْضِ ۚ كَذَلِكَ يَضْرِبُ ٱللَّهُ ٱلْأَمْثَالَ ‎⟫
> *He sends down water (rain) from the sky, and the valleys flow according to their measure, but the flood bears away the foam that mounts up to the surface, and (also) from that (ore) which they heat in the fire in order to make ornaments or utensils, rises a foam like unto it, thus does Allah (by parables) show forth truth and falsehood. Then, as for the foam it passes away as scum upon the banks, while that which is for the good of mankind remains in the earth. Thus Allah sets forth parables (for the truth and falsehood, i.e. Belief and disbelief).*

The sky (⟪ar:پیغمبر علیہ السلام⟫) represents the prophet, and water represents the Qur'ān—the word of Allāh. The valleys, filled with water, represent the knowledge of the Qur'ān (⟪ar:علم القرآن⟫) reaching all Muslims. When the valleys overflow, foam rises to the surface—referring to those who seize only the outer words of the Qur'ān while abandoning their meaning. These are like foam, separated from the believers through hypocrisy, doubt, and false wonder in their hearts. Then Allāh mentions another foam produced when ores are heated in fire—representing those who, following the Sharīʿa's ordinances, burn their bodies in the fire of obedience, thereby purifying their souls of foam and dross. Thus Allāh distinguishes between truth and falsehood: the foam rises to the surface, dies, and collects at the edges, separating from the water. This knowledge laden with doubts is like a corpse—the dead create nothing. Similarly, such shallow knowledge cannot generate spiritual form. Yet the beneficial things remain embedded in the earth—the original and inner meanings of the Qur'ān dwell in the hearts of the believers and the righteous, from whom springs forth wisdom. Therefore Allāh declares:

> ⟪quran 14:24⟫
> ⟪ar:‏أَلَمْ تَرَ كَيْفَ ضَرَبَ ٱللَّهُ مَثَلًۭا كَلِمَةًۭ طَيِّبَةًۭ كَشَجَرَةٍۢ طَيِّبَةٍ أَصْلُهَا ثَابِتٌۭ وَفَرْعُهَا فِى ٱلسَّمَآءِ ‎⟫
> *See you not how Allah sets forth a parable? - A goodly word as a goodly tree, whose root is firmly fixed, and its branches (reach) to the sky (i.e. very high).*

The parable of this good word of wisdom is a virtuous tree whose roots are firmly planted in the earth and whose branches reach toward heaven—a metaphor for knowledge that is both grounded in tradition and ever-ascending toward truth.[^cite-12]

## Esoteric Interpretation and the Circles of Reality

The Messenger of Allāh (⟪ar:رسول اللہ (ﷺ)⟫) declared: "Allāh has founded His religion upon the parable of His creation, so that through the religion you may establish proof of the world, and through the world proof of the religion." That is, Allāh created the world as a parable (⟪ar:'مثال'⟫) and religion as its reality (⟪ar:'ممثول'⟫). The name given to understanding the meeting of the parable (⟪ar:مثل⟫) and its reality (⟪ar:'تاویل'⟫, esoteric exegesis) is taʾwīl. In other words, seeking the correspondence between the material (⟪ar:مادیت (جسمانیت)⟫) and the non-material (⟪ar:غیر مادیت (روحانیت)⟫) is called interpretation.[^cite-13]

Allāh created the religion first and formed the world according to its pattern. That is, He first created the reality and then created its parable to point toward it. The parable is visible; the reality is unseen—yet the reality becomes known through its parable. Just as the body is visible and the self invisible, yet the self is recognized through the body's movements. Had the reality not existed, its parable would not have been created. For instance: wood exists, therefore chairs can be made from it; without wood, nothing could be fashioned.

### The Origin of the Circles of Parables

This is why there have arisen circles of parables and problems. Since we cannot access the Creator directly in any way, we approach His reality (⟪ar:'توحید'⟫, divine oneness) through His essences (⟪ar:'ذوات'⟫), from which the circle begins. These parables are signs of our humility. We cannot comprehend the Creator, so we attempt to understand Muḥammad and ʿAlī (⟪ar:محمد و علی⟫). When you understand them, you will attempt to understand Muḥammad and ʿAlī (⟪ar:محمد اور علی⟫) further. And this ascent continues.

When a stone falls into water, concentric circles form outward from the point of impact. Truth or reality dwells uniquely at the stone's location—the innermost point. The circles around it testify to the existence of this single truth, and they are called parables of that truth, serving as means to reach it. The outermost circle possesses the least force and the largest diameter, just as the outermost levels of understanding are the most numerous and least powerful. As we move inward from the outer circles, the manifestations of truth grow weightier and fewer in number until we finally reach this single truth at the center. Once this truth is attained, progression ceases.

> [Image preserved: concentric circles diagram representing layers of esoteric interpretation]

The circle upon which our sight is fixed and our knowledge stops becomes for us the truth (⟪ar:'حق'⟫), and all circles beneath it become its parables (⟪ar:'ممثولات'⟫). If our gaze is fixed upon the seventh circle, it becomes our truth, and the sixth circle and all below it become parables of this seventh reality for us. Interpretation is the endeavor to ascend toward the realities (⟪ar:'حق'⟫).

Sayyidnā Ghulām Ḥusayn (⟪ar:سیدنا غلام حسین صاحب⟫) said: "Interpretation is a bridge to the realities." Through taʾwīl we can cross toward the truths, which are the ultimate destination. Once the bridge is crossed, it no longer serves us. Similarly, once the reality of something is known, the need for interpretive language vanishes.

> [Image preserved: diagram of bridge concept linking parable and reality]

Just as these circles make us acquainted with the place where the stone fell, so parables acquaint us with their realities. Were there no reality, the parable would not exist—just as without a stone falling into water, no circles would form. Parables are the names of the circles of interpretation. Taʾwīl is knowledge of the beings and limits established by the Creator of all existence (Subḥānahu wa Taʿālā)—to know their ranks and stations, to make them matters of guardianship (wilāya), and in them to perceive the Creator's oneness (tawḥīd). The more we transcend the circles of interpretation, the nearer we approach the Creator—or rather, approach the Truth.

What were realities in the Ṭāʾib era (⟪ar:دور طیبی⟫)—in the time of Sayyidnā Sulaymān (⟪ar:سیدنا سلیمان صاحب⟫)—have the status of interpretations in the Qāʾim era (⟪ar:دور قائمی⟫). What is understood becomes interpretation; what is not yet understood and is the object of seeking becomes reality. The truest worship lies in humility before the divine. There will come a moment when, compelled by our humility, we resort to silence (⟪ar:'سکوت'⟫). Where silence begins, the parables commence. We cannot reach Allāh, so we resort to His signs and His names. Though worship is directed toward Allāh alone, we can only worship Him through these parables.
