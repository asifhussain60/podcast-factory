The word for "beginning" in Arabic is called بَدَأَ; From the same root you get the word ابتدا, which means the 'start' or the 'beginning'. And we'll soon see in one of the future sessions, that one of the several meanings for the word "heart" (QALB) is "to reverse something". For example you have the word INQILAAB that comes from the same root QLB. What does INQILAAB mean? It means revolution. And what does revolution do? It reverses the order of things. So of the meanings of the word "heart" or "qlb" is to reverse something.

Now if you reverse the letters in the word بَدَأَ (which are BA DAL and ALIF), what do you get? You get ADAB! And so ADAB is the heart of knowledge. ADAB is a portal to the purification of the heart. It is the portal through which every spiritual journey begins. And for this reason, our Islamic tradition is so firmly rooted in this idea of ADAB.

ADAB is a difficult word to translate in English. It is generally translated as étiquettes or courtesy. The word ADEEB (which is a derivative of ADAB), means a "erudite person; someone who is learned, and has the best manners; someone who is very courteous". But in my opinion, a really good translation for the word ADAB is "comportment". 

I did not even know what that word meant when I first read it.  I thought it was that thing in a car in which you put your stuff (the glove comportment).

Comportment comes from two Latin words which means "how you carry yourself". The word "porter" is derived from it, because a porter is someone who carries your luggage. So comportment is "the way in which you carry yourself". And that's pretty much what ADAB is.

Now interestingly, ADAB is not something that is dictated by the SHAREEAH. The idea behind ADAB is that it is the most natural thing for a person. Because most of ADAB is simply common sense. 

Right after the battle of KHAYBER, which was faught against the Jews; a companion was asked to bring a notable lady from the Jewish community (صفية بنت حيي) to Rasul Allah (Salallahu alayhi wa aalihee wa sallam). Rasul Allah said, "Couldn't you find another route to bring her to me?". And he said, "I did not think that you would mind". 

In other words, "what's the big deal?". Isn't that response interesting? Because this is our response to pretty much everything in life these days, right? What's the big deal. So what if I don't conver my head when I sit for dinner? What's the big deal if I don't say BISMILLAH.....

10 Foundational Principles of knowledge

The ancient arabs used 10 criteria; or 10 measuring sticks; before they would attempt to learn something. They called it مَبادِی العَشرۃ (the 10 fundamental principle of knowledge). They believed that before one embarks on a journey of knowledge, one must know 10 things about that knowledge. In our terms today, this would translate to, "Do your homework!". So they had 10 fundamental criteria

أِنَّ مبادئ العلوم العشرة؛ الحدُّ و المَوضُوعُ ثُمَّ ثَمرۃ

و فَضلُهُ ونِسبَةُ و الوَاضِعُ و الاسمُ ؛ و الِاستِمدَادُ و الشَّارِع و الحُکمُ

وَ مَسائِلُ و البَعضُ بِالبَعضُ اِكتفى ؛ و مَن دَرَى الجِميعُ حَازْ الشُّرَفَا

Now let's first understand these criteria before we apply them to what we're about to study.

## الحد

The first one is AL-HADD, which in the English language we call a "definition". It is important to know the definition of what we are studying. In the science of logic, a definition is something that gets to the essence of a thing. In other words when you define something, you have to define it "essentially". A proper definition should always be inclusive, as well as exclusive. It should include everything that that thing is; and it should exclude everything that the thing isn't. 

To give you an example of this, logicians say that a human being is a "rational animal". Human beings are animals, but they are rational animals. This is a definition that gets to the "essence" of what a human being is. To be human is to be specifically from the animal kingdom (and not from any other kingdom, such as the mineral or plant kingdom); but in addition to being an animal, it also has to be rational; it has to be sapiential; it must have intellect and the ability to reason; whereas other animals don't have that ability. So this definition excludes all the other kingdoms, and it also excludes the irrational animals that operate based on instinct. So the first thing we need to know is the definition of what you are about to learn.

## و المَوضُوعُ

Then you have the MAWDOOAH, which is the subject matter. What is this thing that you are learning about? 

## ثَمرَۃ

Then there is the SAMRAH, which is the fruit. What is the benefit of studying this thing? What is the end goal?

## فضلہ

Then you have the FADAL; the virtue of that thing. Why should that thing be learned? Because not all knowledge should we learned. There are certain types of knowledge that are superfluous. There are others that are a complete waste of time, like commercial jingles, or songs. You can learn this stuff but it is a waste of time. And this is why in one of our prayers that we recite after FARD we say:

But what we are planning to study is not. This is an incredibly fruitful knowledge! So we must know its virtue; and we must understand why it is worthy our time, our effort and our consideration. 

## نسبۃُ

And then knowing what it relates to. In other words, what other types of knowledge are associated with it? What are the companion fields of knowledge that link in with this one.

## الواضِعُ

Who is the first one who made this science. Who is the source of this knowledge. And this is really important, because we don't want to invest time acquiring a certain knowledge, if we're not source about its source. AQL (Intellect)

The ⟪ar:عقل⟫(intellect) is essential for processing information that a person receives from a myriad of sources. This information is then used to develop a person's knowledge, understanding and wisdom. 

Especially important in this process is the opportunity to know about the Divine through indirect knowledge from the universe, and direct knowledge from revelation and Prophets.

Ghazali defines ⟪ar:عقل⟫as the "force of knowledge ('ilm) of the real nature of things," as well as "that which perceives knowledge." The ‘aql  is therefore the seat of perception.

Nasr (1979, p. 1) adds that according to Islamic terminology the single term, ⟪ar:العقل⟫is used to denote both reason and intellect. He explains that although the two are distinct and inter-related, reason is however dependent upon intellect. He further elaborates that the Arabic term ⟪ar:العقل⟫stems from the root ‘ql  which means to bind. He asserts that in Islamic theology it is the ⟪ar:عقل⟫(intellect) which binds a person to God. The `aql  is thus critical to maintaining connectivity to the Divine.

Depending on whether it is used positively or negatively, the intellect can cause a person to rise above animality or stoop below stupidity. Akhir (2012, p. 718) explains that the 'aql  (intellect)that has been bestowed on humans provide them with unique abilities such as reflection and memory recall. The ⟪ar:عقل⟫is inherently pure and dignified, and distinguishes humans from other creatures. He asserts that if used appropriately it can ennoble the person; but if misused indiscordance with Divine tenets, will eventually cause the individual to be disgraced.

Nasr (1979, p. 2) asserts that in the religious sciences, a person must exercise his/her"intelligence in order to understand God’s revelation." Revelation then enables the intellect to be illuminated and to function properly especially in the acquisition of the Divine truth. He argues that the amalgamation of the intellect and revelation makes it possible for the mind to engage intuition and faith in order know the truth i.e., “the meaning of religion and more particularly God’s word as contained in the Holy Quran.” (Nasr, 1979, p. 3)

The nafs (ego), an equally essential complement to the intellect, deals with innate human desires ranging from the carnal to the spiritual.
