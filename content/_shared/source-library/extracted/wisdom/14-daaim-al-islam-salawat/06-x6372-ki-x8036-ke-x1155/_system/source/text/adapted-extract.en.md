## Esoteric Interpretation of Prayer Categories

Prayer is fundamentally an act of drawing near to one's superior—a movement of the soul toward higher stations of knowledge and being. Every Imam, in his station, embodies four divine realities that correspond precisely to the four words of the Basmala: "In the name of God, the Merciful, the Compassionate" (⟪ar:بِسْمِ اللَّهِ الرَّحْمَٰنِ الرَّحِيمِ⟫). These four realities are arranged in a sacred hierarchy: the Self (al-dhāt), the noble Face (al-wajh al-sharīf), the Divinity (al-Lāhūt), and the Humanity (al-Nāsūt).[^cite-1]

| BSM | Allah | Al-Rahman | Al-Rahim |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Self | Noble Face | Divinity | Humanity |
| First Obligation | Second Obligation | Third Obligation | Fourth Obligation |
| Imam's Essential Nature | Things Derived from Believers | Supreme Degree | Inferior Degree |

The lowest station for the Imam is his displeasure or withdrawal; above this stands his essential self; and above that, his noble form. Thus does the Divine Law establish that no obligatory prayer shall exceed four rak'ahs (cycles), just as it ordains that no obligatory prayer shall fall below two rak'ahs—for these two rak'ahs manifest the outward appearance and the inward personality of the Imam (al-Imām).[^cite-2]

In the obligatory prayers, the believer recites the small testimony (tashahhud) after two rak'ahs. This pause serves a crucial function: it distinguishes between the first two cycles and the final two, acknowledging that the Imam's essential attributes differ from the blessings he bestows upon the community of believers. When one travels and thus shortens the prayer to two rak'ahs, one indicates thereby the Imam's supreme and transcendent nature.

The prayer of greeting the mosque (Tahiyyat al-Masjid) connects the worshipper to the highest limits of being. The five daily prayers correspond to the invitation (daʿwa) extended by five great Speakers (Nutaqāʾ), who are themselves divided into three categories. The obligatory prayer points to the Imam (al-Imām), the recommended prayer indicates his Proof (ḥujja), and the supererogatory prayer points to his Summoner (dāʿī).

| Obligatory | Recommended | Supererogatory | Volunteer |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Speaker (Nāṭiq) | Basis (Asās) | Imam | Proof |
| Imam | Proof | Summoner | Appointed |

The Speaker is called "obligatory Imam" because God made him a Prophet. The proponent of the Recommended is appointed by the Messenger of God. The Imam of the supererogatory was appointed by the Guardian at God's command, and the example of voluntary prayer rests upon the Imam's appointment by divine order. God attests to this principle in His revelation.[^cite-3]

The night and day form the basis for the five prescribed times of prayer. In total, there are fifty-one rak'ahs of prayer across the day and night: seventeen are obligatory, and these seventeen cycles indicate seventeen spiritual and physical limits (ḥudūd).

| Tablet | Pen | Speaker | Basis | Imam | Summons | Total |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | 1 | 1 | 1 | 1 | 12 | 17 |

Whosoever abandons these seventeen limits falls into unbelief. As the Prophet declared, "Whoever forsakes the obligatory is a disbeliever (kāfir), and whoever abandons my Sunnah is accursed." The seventeen obligatory rak'ahs and the thirty-four recommended and supererogatory rak'ahs together comprise the full fifty-one cycles. The supererogatory prayers are reckoned among the Sunnahs because the obligatory prayers indicate the Imams, while the Sunnah prayers point to the supplications through which the Imams manifest divine grace. The Sunnah of the preceding Imam progressively becomes incorporated into the duties of the age that follows.

## Examples of Supererogatory Prayers

There are six supererogatory prayers, each of which embodies a profound spiritual principle. The supererogatory (tatauwwuʿ) is also called the voluntary (nāfila), signifying "descendant of the descendant"—a term the Maʿzūn uses to denote the successor of the Speaker, just as the Speaker succeeds his spiritual father, the Proof. Within this category fall nine prayers, associated with the seven Imams and two supreme rational limits.[^cite-4]

These nine prayers are: the Prayer of the Forenoon (Ṣalāt al-Ḍuḥā), the Prayer of the Standing (Ṣalāt al-Qāʾim al-Qiyāma), the Prayer of the Breaking of the Fast (Ṣalāt al-ʿĪd al-Fiṭr), the Prayer at the elevation of the Mustajīb through the ranks, the Prayer for Rain (Ṣalāt al-Istisqāʾ), the Prayer of the Successor of the Qa'im—one who will inherit the Qa'im's station on earth or on a new plane and continue the Imamate, bringing relief from the famine of knowledge—and the Prayer at the Eclipse of sun or moon (Ṣalāt al-Kusūf). Each of these prayers expresses a moment when the Imams, through their intimate knowledge, illuminate the obscurity that eclipses ordinary understanding.

| Friday Prayer | Prayer of Eid al-Fitr | Prayer of Eid al-Adha |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Peace be upon him | The Mahdi of the Last Age | The Standing (Qa'im) |
| Funeral Prayer | Prayer for Rain | Prayer at Eclipse |
| Elevation of the Accepted | Successor of the Qa'im | The Hidden Imam |

**Prayer in Fear (Ṣalāt al-Khawf)**, Prayer in Travel, and Prayer in Security represent three conditions of the soul's readiness.

### Prayer in Fear (One Rak'ah)

Besides these nine prayers, the Prayer in Fear is also prescribed, yet it differs fundamentally from prayer offered in peace. The Prayer in Fear consists of but one cycle; the Prayer in Travel contains two cycles; and the Prayer in Security comprises four cycles. The inner meaning is this: when a believer finds himself overwhelmed by outward appearances, he must stand firm from the single perspective of the manifest reality, obeying only the manifest Imam, just as the manifest people themselves obey in the external dimension.

### Prayer in Travel (Two Rak'ahs)

The traveler who prays only two rak'ahs resembles a believer who has transcended the outward and begun the inward struggle. It is not obligatory for this traveler to recite the remaining two cycles, which exemplify the Intellect and the Soul of tomorrow, for he has not yet grasped the spiritual limits.

### Prayer in Security (Four Rak'ahs)

The Prayer in Security exemplifies the believer who dwells in the inner and esoteric teachings of the true summons, having attained knowledge of both spiritual and physical limits. Such a one confesses the two inner limits—the First Intellect and the universal Soul—and acknowledges and obeys the two outer limits (the particular intellect and the essence).

## The Descending Order of Prayer Rak'ahs

The divine prescriptions for prayer were revealed in five stages. In Mecca, thirty-two rak'ahs were prescribed in four periods; in Medina, thirty-one additional cycles were revealed, bringing the total to fifty-one.

### Thirty-Two Rak'ahs of Mecca

The Meccan prayers divide into thirty-two rak'ahs, distributed as follows:

| Obligatory | Recommended | Sunnah | Intercession of Succession |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 10 | 7 | 12 | 3 |

The five obligatory daily prayers, offered at their appointed times, consist of seventeen rak'ahs—ten rak'ahs before the testimony (tashahhud), which descend directly from God, and seven rak'ahs added by the Prophet after the testimony. These seven are based on the intellect and the soul.

**The Ten Obligatory Rak'ahs:**

The Prophet first prayed two cycles of the Noon Prayer (Ṣalāt al-Ẓuhr). After six hours, he prayed two cycles of the Afternoon Prayer (Ṣalāt al-ʿAsr). In the first hour of the night, he prayed two cycles of the Evening Prayer (Ṣalāt al-Maghrib), followed two hours later by two cycles of the Night Prayer (Ṣalāt al-ʿIshāʾ). Between day and night, he prayed two cycles of the Dawn Prayer (Ṣalāt al-Fajr), saying, "The Dawn Prayer is witnessed by the angels of day and night."

| Prayer | Noon | Afternoon | Evening | Night | Dawn |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Rak'ahs | 2 | 2 | 2 | 2 | 2 |

The two rak'ahs of Dawn refer to the two supreme limits of the spiritual realm: the First Intellect (al-ʿAql al-Awwal) and the universal Soul (al-Nafs al-Kulliyya). The next seven rak'ahs point to the seven Intellects established by accepting the call. The final cycle signifies the Tenth Intellect, who was entrusted with the governance of creation and extends the summons to the nine preceding limits.[^cite-5]

**The Seven Additional Rak'ahs:**

The Messenger of God then added seven rak'ahs to these ten, explaining their reward to his followers. Those who accepted his words became obedient; those who rejected the inner truth grew distressed. Thus the Prophet called these seven cycles "near to acceptance" (maqrūna), and divided them among the five prayers out of obedience to God's intellect and soul.

He joined two rak'ahs to the Noon Prayer, making it four obligatory cycles. He combined two with the Afternoon Prayer, likewise making four obligatory. He added one to the Evening Prayer, making three obligatory. He added two to the Night Prayer, making four obligatory. These seven correspond to the seven physical limits—the seven Imams of each period—which contrast with the seven Intellects of the spiritual world.

The five prayers thus contained seventeen obligatory rak'ahs across five times, named obligatory and "near to acceptance" (maqrūna):

| | Noon | Afternoon | Evening | Night | Dawn |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Obligatory Rak'ahs (10) | 2 | 2 | 2 | 2 | 2 |
| Additional Rak'ahs (7) | 2 | 2 | 1 | 2 | — |

**Twelve Rak'ahs of the Sunnah:**

Seven days later, God commanded the Prophet to establish twelve recommended rak'ahs, which the Messenger distributed across the five obligatory times as a teaching of the intellect and soul.

The Prophet included six rak'ahs in the Noon Prayer: four before the obligatory prayer and two after. Then two after the Evening Prayer, two after the Night Prayer, and two before the Dawn Prayer. In this way, the twelve recommended cycles are distributed across five times.

The Basmala (⟪ar:بِسْمِ اللَّهِ الرَّحْمَٰنِ الرَّحِيمِ⟫) contains nineteen letters, arranged as seven, two, and twelve, corresponding to the seven Imams and the twelve Proofs—in contrast to the seven rak'ahs and twelve recommended cycles of prayer.[^cite-6]

| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Obligatory Rak'ahs (10) | 2 | 2 | 2 | 2 | 2 |
| Additional Rak'ahs (7) | 2 | 2 | 1 | 2 | — |
| Recommended Rak'ahs (12) | 6 | 2 | — | 2 | 2 |

| Obligatory | Recommended | Sunnah |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 10 Intellects | 7 Imams | 12 Proofs |

**Three Rak'ahs of the Even and Odd Prayers:**

Thereafter, three cycles were revealed. The Messenger of God retained them after the Night Prayer and called them the "Even and Odd Prayer" (Ṣalāt al-Shafʿ wa-l-Witr), or the Prayer of Conclusion (Ṣalāt al-Khitām).

During the Meccan period, the Prophet held his hands before his face, anointed his socks, performed the call to prayer (iqāma) once, recited four takbīrs at the funeral rite, and created graves without smoothing their surface. When the command came to migrate to Medina, he opened his hands in prayer, anointed his bare feet, performed the call twice, recited five takbīrs at the funeral rite, built graves with four edges, and said: "I was sent at the beginning with a word without deed, then commanded to establish deeds based on word—and that is sincerity."

### Thirty-One Rak'ahs of Medina

After this, thirty-one additional rak'ahs were prescribed in Medina. The Prophet divided nineteen of these among the five prayers and named them supererogatory (nāfila) cycles, bringing the total to fifty-one. The remaining twelve rak'ahs he called the "Night Vigil Prayer" (Ṣalāt al-Tahajjud), which he prayed at night.

| Supererogatory | Night Vigil |
| --- | --- |
| 19 | 12 |

**The Nineteen Supererogatory Rak'ahs:**

Of these nineteen rak'ahs, he placed four in the Noon Prayer—two before the obligatory prayer and two after—bringing the total of Noon Prayer rak'ahs to fourteen. He added four before the Afternoon Prayer, making eight total. He placed four before the Evening Prayer, making nine total. He assigned six to the Night Prayer: four before the obligatory prayer and two after. After the Even and Odd Prayers, he reserved one cycle for the "Sitting Prayer" (Ṣalāt al-Jalūs), changing its recitation method; two rak'ahs recited without sitting count as one cycle.

These supererogatory rak'ahs do not include the four additional Evening Prayer cycles. When these were added later, the total reached fifty-one rak'ahs per day. These fifty-one cycles symbolize the complete human lifespan, which reaches its appointed measure at fifty-one years.

## The Limits of Prayer

The Messenger of God said: "The limits of prayer are just as there are limits to a house." Whoever recognizes these limits and performs prayer according to their true nature and proper conditions has completed the prayer and has not broken it. The limits (ḥudūd) of prayer are seven, equal to the number of the seven Imams and seven Proofs. Of these, four are obligatory duties and three are recommended; four are indispensable for the completion of prayer, and three are necessary for the validity of prayer's inner reality. The obligatory limits point inwardly to the station of the Imam, while the recommended limits point to the station of the Proof.[^cite-7]

**The Four Obligatory Limits:**

That which was revealed in God's Book and the Sunnah—which the Messenger established by God's command—constitutes obligation. Thus does God establish the Speaker and establish the Testifier by divine order. There are four obligatory limits from God without which prayer cannot be established:

| Qibla | Time | Intention | Purification |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |

Time is the Proof (ḥujja) for the Soul that created the world; the Qibla is the Proof for the Intellect, for all creatures move toward it as their ultimate end. The other two obligatory duties of the Prophet contain proofs: intention proves the Basis (asās), and purification proves the foundational reality. Prayer cannot be performed without the outer and the inner—both established by the Intellect and the Soul, supported by both the Universal Soul and the individual soul. The Speaker fixed the outer form of prayer through the power of the Soul and the esoteric interpretation (taʾwīl) of prayer with the support of the Universal Intellect.

**The Three Recommended Limits:**

There are three Sunnahs (recommended practices) of prayer:

| Call to Prayer | Iqāma | Congregation |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Speaker | Proof | Summoner |

The Call to Prayer (adhān) and the Iqāma (the second call immediately before prayer) point to the Speaker and to the author of the week (the seventh Imam who concludes a short cycle), whom we call the "author" (muʿallim). The seventh Imam after six Imams is called the "author" because at his station the religion reaches its completion—the Sabbath of religious time. The Call to Prayer thus exemplifies the prayer that summons people to the manifest; the Iqāma exemplifies the Proof who sustains the inner dimension; and the Congregation exemplifies the Speaker, for the gathering of all believers is established in his gathering.

## Examples of the Noon Prayer

In the religion of Islam, the Holy Prophet was the first to establish the Noon Prayer (Ṣalāt al-Ẓuhr) as the "apparent" (ẓāhir) prayer. Its time falls after the sun passes its zenith, for prayer is not offered at the moment of zenith. Prayer exemplifies the summons (daʿwa), and the zenith indicates the rulings which the Speaker removes from previous revealed laws. Prayer is not performed at the time of zenith because at that moment the influences of Saturn and Mars are dominant.[^cite-8]

The Messenger of God is like the Noon Prayer itself—luminous and manifest—and the one who prays is about to enter the true summons. No one can enter the summons without taking the covenant; the example of the covenant is ablution (wuḍūʾ), without which prayer is not accepted.

The Noon Prayer comprises fourteen rak'ahs in total: six recommended cycles (also known as the Prayer of Early Afternoon, the First Prayer, or the Prayer of the Penitent), then four obligatory rak'ahs, then four supererogatory rak'ahs.

### Illuminations

The Noon Prayer, the chief of prophets and messengers, exemplifies Muhammad Mustafa, peace be upon him. The light of the Noon Prayer first abundantly illuminates the realm of bodies, then gradually diminishes. For this reason, it is virtuous to pray at the best time. These illuminations affect material things and draw them upward, which is why the light decreases over time.

### Six Recommended Rak'ahs of Noon

The recommended Noon Prayer is called "the Prayer of the Penitent" (⟪ar:صلوٰۃ التوابین⟫), the prayer of those who turn in repentance. These six cycles point to the six great Imams during the time of Jesus, whose reign drew to a close and who heralded the coming of Muhammad Mustafa, peace be upon him. These Imams were overseers of the assemblies of earlier ages.

## Examples of the Afternoon Prayer

The four rak'ahs of the Afternoon Prayer (Ṣalāt al-ʿAsr) rest upon the Master of the Standing (Sāḥib al-Qiyāma). Prayer exemplifies the summons; since there is no summons after the Qa'im (al-Qāʾim), there is no other prayer after the Afternoon Prayer. Prayers must therefore be shortened. With the appearance of the Qa'im, one epoch closes and a new account begins with his successor, who will establish the feasts anew.

The word "Afternoon" (ʿAsr) means to squeeze all the juice from fruit. This indicates that the Qa'im will squeeze out all sects and spread the pure religion of Islam throughout the world—for which God says: "To God alone belongs sovereignty on that day, the One, the Irresistible."[^cite-9]

In another sense, the Qa'im, in the remembrance of Islam, is the essence of all previous religions; all previous congregations will come to you and merge into your temple.

In another sense, the Afternoon Prayer exemplifies our Sovereign Muhammad ibn Ismaʿīl, the Seventh Seal, and the four recommended rak'ahs rest upon his two deputies and two veils.

### Illuminations

At the time of Afternoon, the mercy of the lights first decreases, then gradually increases; yet even when high, it slows. Since the Afternoon Prayer rests upon the Qa'im, there is no prostration in it, for his period has not yet commenced. The meaning of Afternoon is "squeezed," and this prayer rests upon the Qa'im in the remembrance of Islam, for he will squeeze all sects and bring them to Islam.

## The Dawn Prayer Is Reckoned in Both Day and Night

The Dawn Prayer exemplifies the Imam of every age. This prayer is offered between night and day, so it is counted in both periods.

**As a Night Prayer:**

It occurs at night. Recitation is not performed aloud in day prayers, but it is recited aloud in night prayers. Therefore, the Dawn Prayer is a night prayer because recitation is aloud.

**As a Day Prayer:**

Fasting is observed during the day, and we pray the Dawn Prayer while fasting. In this sense, the Dawn Prayer is a day prayer.

Every morning at dawn, an angel calls from heaven: "Is there any servant who seeks God's forgiveness to whom I should grant repentance?" In fact, the angel of heaven is the chapter of the Imam who calls out and accepts the forgiveness of those who seek it from God.
