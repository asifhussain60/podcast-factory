## Spiritual Invitation and the Seven Intellects

When both the al-ʿAql al-Awwal (the First Intellect) and the first emanator attained kamāl thānī (secondary perfection), they resolved that they should unite and summon all ⟪ar:اشباح نورانیہ⟫the luminous forms so that these beings, like themselves, might succeed in the tawhīd (monotheistic devotion) of the Creator and attain secondary perfection. For this purpose, the First Intellect appointed the first emanator as his wife and hijāb (veil), commanding him to invite all the remaining souls to the tawhīd of the Creator. Thus began the daʿwa (the summons), the spiritual invitation. Prior to this, no summons had been established in the realm of spirit.

The supplications allude subtly to this moment, saying, "When the two met, the applause began." Just as both hands clap and their sound resonates outward to affect others, so too did the First Intellect and the first source meet, issuing forth the daʿwa to all beings toward tawhīd.[^cite-1]

The sole purpose of every invitation is the submission of nature—the transformation from kamāl awwal (primary perfection) to kamāl thānī (secondary perfection), from jismāniyyat (corporeality) to rūḥāniyyat (spirituality). The first source established his daʿwa in the spiritual realm by command of the First Intellect, calling all toward the tawhīd of the Creator (may He be glorified and exalted). No obstacle or materiality existed in the spiritual world to prevent these luminous forms from accepting the invitation, for they were masters of all knowledge, life, and power, requiring no external summons. Yet because they did not exercise their authority spontaneously, the caller had to invoke them. All these unnumbered beings received the same feast of invitation equally—none was offered less, none more.

Although the daʿwa extended equally to all beings, its primary purpose was to remove the waywardness of the second source ⟪ar:'وحم مساوات'⟫. An analogy illuminates this: when one part of cloth becomes soiled, though the whole cloth is washed uniformly, the cleansing action concentrates at the point of dirt. Similarly, though the invitation reached all souls equally, the main aim concerned the correction of the second source. [^cite-2]

Responding to ⟪ar:أجابت⟫ the daʿwa of the first munbaʿith (the Emanator), each successive intellect began to answer and advance toward the tawhīd of the Creator of the Almighty, until seven intellects and their circles were established in correspondence to the seven letters of Sūrat al-Ikhlās. Each acknowledged the tawhīd of the Creator, recognized the majesty and prestige of the first and second intellects, and acknowledged the precedence of the intellect that preceded it within its horizon. This act bound these intellects through tawhīd to ⟪ar:مواد الٰہی⟫the divine substance, and through the First Intellect—itself the ⟪ar:ھیولی⟫primordial matter and ⟪ar:صورت⟫form—all attained perfection.[^cite-3]

## Circle and Its Center

The chiefs and members of the circle were all called ⟪ar:عقول⟫intellects, and among them a hierarchy of highest to lowest was established. Yet the last member of the sixth circle of intellect surpassed the chief of the seventh, though the former held membership while the latter held leadership. The First Intellect, having made no invitation, formed no circle; only the one who summons establishes a circle. For this reason, the circular form ranks as the noblest among all forms.

In the spiritual realm, all seven intellects arose because of the kalimah lā ilāha illā-Allāh ⟪ar:کلمہ لا الہ الا اللہ⟫—the utterance "There is no god but God." The first letter corresponds to the First Intellect, and the seven letters that follow correspond to the seven intellects. Just as these letters combine to form the word, so too shall these seven intellects be integrated into the realm of the first source on the Day of Resurrection. The verses shall join as the word; the First Source is the whole, and the seven intellects are its parts. This assemblage is called the 8 Aʿquls ⟪ar:'**ملائکۃ حملۃ العرش**'⟫—the angels who carry the Throne and have lifted up the First Intellect. These eight angels are, in truth, the eight gates of Paradise, of which the circle of the first angel is the highest heaven.

Each of these seven intellects came into being through the intellect of its predecessor. The First Intellect was commanded to invoke until the Second Intellect was established. Thereafter, the Second Intellect's last member was ordered to issue the daʿwa, thus forming the Third Intellect, and this process continued until all seven intellects and their circles were constituted. Each intellect was established through the daʿwa of its predecessor's final member.

In the word of tawhīd, seven stanzas arise when the pen lifts from the tablet and then descends again upon it. The pen's rise signals the completion of an intellect's feast; its fall signifies that the last member of that circle now issues the daʿwa. That is, when the feast of lā ⟪ar:' لا '⟫ was completed, the last member received command to feast. At his daʿwa, the circle began forming from those who ⟪ar:لب بیک⟫answered the call. Then, at the invitation of that last member, one was born at ⟪ar:' لہ'⟫. This process continued until the word of tawhīd was complete and the seven intellects established.

Each circle pointed toward its center, for without center, no circle could exist. Each circle stood amazed at the greatness of its center, circumambulating it in wonder and seeking knowledge, much as one circles the Kaʿba. The First Intellect marvels at the Creator of the Almighty, since the Creator is the center of the First Intellect, which revolves around Him in wonder and knowledge. In like manner, the First Intellect is the center of the Second Intellect, and the Second revolves around it in amazement at its majesty. God fashions each center for its own sphere. Whosoever is astonished at His greatness is called to Him; thus we call Him Allāh ⟪ar:'اللہ'⟫, because the First Intellect was the first to be astonished at Him, and having declared tawhīd, pointed toward Him. So too did all intellects marvel at the greatness and knowledge of the First Intellect, leading to the appellation ⟪ar:'اللہ'⟫"First Intellect."

In another sense, the center signifies quiescence and the circle signifies motion. Tranquility surpasses motion, for the center's stillness derives from its constancy, while the circle's motion arises from its wonder. The final tranquility of every motion is the essence of the First Intellect. Motion within the realm of the First Intellect is exceedingly subtle. Were it not for this slightest gesture, it would rest in the peace of the Almighty. This motion is a gesture of praise to His Creator, which ⟪ar:سورۃ الفاتحۃ⟫the opening sūra of the Qurʾān ⟪ar:'الحمد'⟫—"The Praise"—signifies.

When this motion and quiescence entered the temporal world, the imprint reversed itself. Tranquility became a symbol of death; motion became a symbol of life. Since we have not heeded the invitation of the intellect in the spiritual world, we must fulfill this action through the daʿwa of the Imāms, so that we may attain the peace of the Hereafter. All our deeds are for the sake of God's tranquility.

## The Circles of the Intellects

When the people of tawhīd advanced toward the Creator of tawhīd, they were joined by their circles, whom they called the Raʾīs (chief). When the first emanator moved toward the tawhīd of the Creator, a number of souls came forward, met his gaze, and became monotheistic in his monotheism. For this state he prays: ⟪ar-quote:النَّظَرُ بِنَظَرِہِ و اَلبَصَرُ بِبَصَرِہِ⟫"Seeing by his seeing and perceiving by his perceiving." These souls became the realm of the Second Intellect, and he became their chief or leader. When the circle's number reached twenty-seven—and some texts say one hundred—the last member of the circle was commanded to issue the daʿwa. An analogy: when the Imām steps forward to lead prayer, the people rise and join him upon seeing him. He is called their chief or imām, and all others become his followers. The entire congregation prays, yet the precedence of the one who stood first is established.[^cite-4]

The master Sayyidnā ʿAlī ibn Sulaymān (may God sanctify his spirit) ⟪ar:سیدنا علی بن سلیمان اعلی اﷲ قدسہ⟫ mentions twelve chiefs in the circle of the first source. One hundred years later, Sayyidnā Ḥasan ibn Hibat Allāh (may God sanctify his spirit) ⟪ar:سیدنا حسن بن ھبۃ اللہ صاحب⟫ mentioned twenty-eight chiefs in the same circle. When Sayyidnā ʿAlī ibn ʿAlī (may God raise his rank) ⟪ar:سیدنا علی بن علی اعلی اللہ قدسہ⟫ inquired of this discrepancy, he explained that the difference arose because Sayyidnā ʿAlī ibn Sulaymān ⟪ar:سیدنا علی بن سلیمان⟫ spoke of the parts, while Sayyidnā Ḥasan ⟪ar:سیدنا حسن⟫ spoke of the whole. A comparison: two persons viewing the same book—one looking from a distance sees only pages, while one looking closely sees both pages and words. Both accounts are thus correct. The first source encompasses a grouping of twenty-eight, a group of one hundred, and a group of twelve.

When the last member of the circle of the first source called all to tawhīd, at his daʿwa an intellect advanced beyond itself. With the awakening of this intellect, a great multitude stepped forth—the al-Munbaʿithūn ⟪ar:'انبعاثی عقول'⟫(the Emanators). It took the precise form of that of the first source. Then the last member of this circle of Emanators issued the daʿwa, at which another vast group came forward, called the Third Circle of Wisdom.

The one who advanced beyond himself at the daʿwa of the last member of the first source's circle became chief of all by virtue of precedence. The nobles became the center and chief of the entire circle, and all the rest were called his circle or followers. All intellects of the circle equaled the Third Intellect in intensity, but by precedence, order was established among them. This occurred in all intellects and circles alike.

We also name the noble and the last member of his circle the "first," for every member of the circle equals his noble in perfection. Only the order of precedence distinguishes them.

To form a circle, the compass is rotated by placing it at the center, and the circle closes where it began. When the circle's last point meets its first point, the circle is complete. Thus the Third Intellect served as the center upon which the circle was placed. Its first point became its chapter; then successive degrees were established until the last point. It was then commanded: "Now you must issue the daʿwa!" From this point's invitation the next intellect advanced and formed its circle.

### Setting of Boundaries in Rationale

The seven intellects and all souls in their circles were so interconnected that the foot of the one above was joined to the head of the one below. The foot of the Third Intellect was attached to the head of the first member of its circle, becoming thereby his ḥadd ʿālī (upper limit). Similarly, the foot of the last member of the Third Intellect's circle was connected to the head of the chief of the Fourth Intellect. This chain of upper and lower limits was established according to their precedence. The current of the Creator's bounty that began from the First Intellect reached its limits through all exalted means.

## The Essence of the Word in Monotheism

The actions (movements) that occurred in the spiritual world correspond to the letters—alif, lām, and hāʾ—in the word "God" ⟪ar:'الہ'⟫. First, the intellect advanced from its own self toward the self without any external summons. This is the state of alif ⟪ar:' ا '⟫. Observing this, and without any daʿwa, another proceeded toward it—this is the state of lām ⟪ar:'ل'⟫. Then, by command of the First Intellect, the first source began the daʿwa, upon which seven intellects advanced. This is the state of hāʾ ⟪ar:' ھ '⟫. Thus three states were established in the spiritual realm, corresponding to three essences within the Word ⟪ar:لا الہ الا اللہ⟫.[^cite-5]

The same pattern arose in all intellective realms. When the first member began the daʿwa, an intellect advanced beyond itself at his daʿwa and accepted the invitation—this is the state of alif ⟪ar:' ا '⟫. This being became noble and chief of its circle. Witnessing this act, a vast multitude revolted and joined the noble, coming to understanding. The multitude required no daʿwa; they accepted the invitation simply by seeing their chief. If the example be drawn from the circle, then the noble became the center of the multitude, for without his advance, the multitude would not have moved forward seeing him. This is the state of lām ⟪ar:' ل '⟫. Then the last member of the assembly (lām) was commanded by his chief to issue the daʿwa. At his daʿwa, a vast multitude joined him, called the members of the intellect's circle. As soon as the last member received the command to summon, he became the center for the next, for without his daʿwa, the congregation accepting the invitation would not have advanced. This is the state of hāʾ ⟪ar:' ھ '⟫.

The noble of the Third Intellect and all people within his constituents, the al-Munbaʿithūn ⟪ar:'انبعاثی عقول'⟫(the Emanators), dwelled all in the same heaven and attained perfection equally. Through knowledge of the Second Intellect and tawhīd, they all achieved the same perfection. Only the distinction of sābiqiyyat ⟪ar:سباقیت⟫(precedence) remained among them.

Alif moved from itself. Witnessing alif, lām stepped forward. At the daʿwa of lām, hāʾ proceeded forward.

The Raʾīs al-ʿAql (chief of intellect) is the Imām for his group, and the members of his group are his followers. He became the intellect ⟪ar:حَد⟫(the limit) and his group became the muḥaddidīn ⟪ar:مُحَدِّدِین⟫(those who define the limit). He became the wise ⟪ar:رئیس⟫(chief) and all others became his marʾūsūn ⟪ar:مرؤسین⟫(those under him). The circles are attributed to their nobles, just as the circle is connected to its center. Therefore, when we speak of the Third Intellect, we mean the Third noble and all the people within his circle.

All those ⟪ar:عین عظیمہ⟫who advanced toward knowledge of the Divine Beings became Imāms. Those who advanced after witnessing the Imāms became the bāb al-inbiʿāth ⟪ar:باب انبعاثی⟫(the gate of emanation), and those who advanced at the daʿwa became the bāb al-bāṭin ⟪ar:باب باطن⟫(the inner gate).

## "God Bears Witness That There Is No God But He"

When the seven intellects were established, the First Intellect manifested as ⟪ar:نور شعشعانی⟫radiant luminescence. Witnessing this ⟪ar:نور شعشعانی⟫radiant light, all the intellects were astonished and astounded, seeing His greatness and exaltation. At that moment, all these intellects acknowledged the First Intellect and conferred upon Him the Name ⟪ar:أسم اللہ⟫"Allah." For this reason, Allāh says in the Holy Qurʾān:

> ⟪quran 3:18⟫
> ⟪ar:‏شَهِدَ ٱللَّهُ أَنَّهُۥ لَآ إِلَهَ إِلَّا هُوَ وَٱلْمَلَٓئِكَةُ وَأُو۟لُوا۟ ٱلْعِلْمِ قَآئِمًۢا بِٱلْقِسْطِ ۚ لَآ إِلَهَ إِلَّا هُوَ ٱلْعَزِيزُ ٱلْحَكِيمُ ‎⟫
> *Allah bears witness that none has the right to be worshipped but He, and the angels, and those endowed with knowledge (also bear witness to this). He is always maintaining His creation in Justice. There is no god but He, the All-Mighty, the All-Wise.*

That is, the First ⟪ar:اللہ⟫Intellect testified that there is no god but the Creator ⟪ar:ھو⟫, as did the First Source and the seven intellects ⟪ar:الملائکۃ⟫(the Angels), and ⟪ar:جثہ ابدائیۃ⟫the primordial community (ūlū al-ʿilm, those endowed with knowledge) testified in the same manner and were thereby justified.[^cite-6]

### Allah

The First Intellect says that lā ilāha ⟪ar:'لا الہ'⟫ is in fact a negation of one's own self and of the attribute of divinity ⟪ar:صفت الہیۃ⟫. And to affirm illā Huwa ⟪ar:'الا ھو'⟫ is to confess that only the Originator, Most High (Mubdiʿ Taʿālī) ⟪ar:صفت الہیۃ⟫⟪ar:مبدع تعالی⟫(Huwa) possesses the attribute of divinity.

Though it is improper to refer to the Creator, Most High, yet had the intellect not proclaimed ⟪ar:'ھو'⟫Huwa in this testimony, how could it have confessed to the Creator?

### The Eight Letters of Al-Malaika

The letter alif ⟪ar:الملائکۃ⟫differs from the other letters. It refers to the first source, which stands apart from enumeration, having not advanced at anyone's daʿwa but through witnessing the First Intellect, advancing beyond its own self. The seven intellects, founded upon the daʿwa of the first source, are in fact the angels.

### Those Endowed with Knowledge

⟪ar:أولو العلم⟫Ūlū al-ʿilm (those endowed with knowledge) refers to ⟪ar:جثہ ابدائیۃ⟫the primordial community, those who have been made to possess knowledge.
