## Draft (Musawwada)

The proper Arabic definition of ⟪ar-quote:لزمہ ولم یفارقہ⟫ in the term ⟪ar:'عبد'⟫ is "one who cleaves at all times and never departs." It is in this sense that Maulana ʿAlī rendered complete servitude to Muḥammad al-Muṣṭafā, and Ḥaḍrat ʿAbbās rendered complete servitude to Imām Ḥusayn.

The soul departs the human body on the day of ʿĀshūrāʾ, if it be prepared and pure. The emergence of the Qāʾim shall be from Qahira; the appearance of the Mahdī, from Cairo.

The commands of the Prophet are received in two registers. The first is verbal — to affirm in the heart that what he says is true; this is ⟪ar:'طاعت'⟫ (ṭāʿa, obedience). The second is practical — to act upon what he commands; this is ⟪ar:'اتباع'⟫ (ittibāʿ, following). The "standing one" (qāʾim) signifies one who stands upon two legs: the first leg is Sayyidnā Sulaymān, the second is the Qāʾim himself.

Three fasts mark three saints: the first Thursday for Maryam, the middle Wednesday for ⟪ar:حضرت یحیا⟫, and the last Thursday for ʿĪsā. To taste salt at the beginning and end of a meal is to invoke ʿAlī at the beginning and the end. Murder is the separation of two things, as soul from body; combat is to dispute. The "elephant," in the symbolic register, is Abū Bakr.

Adam erred in eating the wheat raw: he ought to have winnowed it, ground it into flour, kneaded it, baked bread, and only then partaken. People hold that the place where Adam met Ḥawwāʾ was named ʿArafāt, but in truth the Muhammadan station bears the name ʿArafa.

A deceased pīr (elder) does not carry with him all the forms (ṣuwar) he had gathered. He hands over the forms of the three Orders to his successor for further training. Likewise, certain souls are deposited upon the graves of the pīrs in cemeteries, where the elder continues to train them. Every soul in the grave has its attention turned upon its own tomb — that is, upon the gathering of souls who attend it. For this reason, prayer is forbidden between the graves: the daʿwa (the summons / the invitation) of the occupant of the grave is already underway there, and it is impermissible to set up one's own invitation upon his.[^cite-1]

The universe is manifested by gradations within the human world. First is the form of the soul, conforming to the universal Soul. Then the form of the subtle organ (laṭīfa), manifested in the physical realm and corresponding to the soul of the spiritual world. Then the form of the brain, corresponding to the form of body. Then the forms of the organs, corresponding to the bodies of the greater world — our universe, from the heavens down to the centre of Saqar.

From the Manifest Book (al-Kitāb al-Mubīn) man has been granted ⟪ar:تشبیح⟫. The Book is the whole; its pages are the parts of which it is composed. Man, in respect of his partial soul and abstracted intellect, is the rational book named Umm al-Kitāb. As preserved Book (al-Kitāb al-Maḥfūẓ), he is the heart, which is the Manifest Book. In this world of nature, his birth corresponds to the Book of Revelation and Affirmation, and his body to the Book of Revelation. Thus the creation of man is the partial book by which the Book of Tomorrow may be contemplated.[^cite-3]

It is no wonder that the entire world is folded within the creation of man. He is a part, yet in reality a compendium of the morrow. The wonder is rather this: that the morrow was created for his sake, that the morrow is his servant and he the morrow's servant. The morrow prostrates and man is its place of prostration. Man is the locus of manifestation for the Holy Essence and the perfections grounded in it, while the universe is the locus of manifestation for the heavens, the Attributes, and the actions grounded therein. Man is perfect because Allāh willed that he become the axis (quṭb) of the world and its caliph.

Al-Raḥīm is named thirty times in the Qurʾān, corresponding to thirty Imāms. Ibn Muljam is the one who was reined in. The one who oversees the table-spread (māʾida) of the Nāṭiq is called the Waṣī. Shahrbānū is the mother of Imām ʿAlī Zayn al-ʿĀbidīn.

The numerical values of *lām* and *yāʾ* together yield forty (30 + 10). The number of *ʿayn* is seventy. The sources yield: A(n) L(ā) — 5, 228, 7. The full witness is *Lā ilāha illā Allāh*. In Sayyidnā Ḥātim's seventieth Majlis it is recorded that ʿAlī possessed seventy qualities which could be united in no other person. No companion was able to gather more than ten of those attributes. Likewise it is reported that Umar uttered "*law-lā ʿAliyyun la-halaka ʿUmar*" — and when counted it was found he uttered the saying seventy times.

Allāh says in the Qurʾān: "the poor, the orphan, and the captive." When the Five — ʿAlī, Fāṭima, Ḥasan, Ḥusayn, and Fiḍḍa — observed their fast, the poor man, the orphan, and the captive came to their door, and all five gave them their bread. In the inner sense, the teachings of the Imam are spread throughout the universe. The "poor" was a dāʿī (Summoner) who attended the feast of the Khamsat al-Aṭhār — called poor because he gave others sustenance and was himself absorbed and constrained from imparting benefit; the Five released him so that he might transmit. The "orphan" denotes one of high station whose benefactor has passed away, prevented for some reason from delivering benefit; the Five released him too. The "captive" denotes one held where adversary scholars dominate — the inverse of imprisonment — neither able to receive nor give benefit; the Five freed such persons from this bondage. The disclosure here is that the feast was the Five's own, in which all of them surrendered the solemn provisions of their own banquet to liberate others.

The command to invoke the Imāmate proceeds from ʿAyn al-ʿAẓīma. There are diverse modes of revelation: not only through Jibrāʾīl, but from behind the veil, or through a person's appearance, or by direct address.

The knowledge that made Adam the qibla of the angels was, in truth, the knowledge of the Ahl al-Bayt.

The chain of Imāms has been established from the very first day, from one end to the next. The son of *Jathā Abdaʾiyya* is the first Imām; the Jathā are elevated from the rank of Imāmate. The process of Imāmate begins with this son, who is the first Imām of the Kashf period, and from him a sequence proceeds from father to son until the final establishment of the *kawr*. The last Imām of the kawr will possess a special distinction: he will be the foundation of the entire kawr, growing with the whole kawr, the heart of the body that is to come.

A woman's *jihād* is to live a good life with her husband. *Jihād al-marʾa ḥasan al-tabaʿʿul*.

Most invocations are made through the name of Allāh — for instance, "Allāh is the Creator of all things." The pronominal reference of Allāh, the *baṣmaka*, moves toward ʿAyn al-ʿAẓīma. The Mīm through which we address the exalted is the veil by which we know them; just as we are known by our name, the means by which we may know the highest is the Mīm — their veil — which is Muḥammad the Arabian.

**The death of the Nāṭiq through the veil of the Awṣiyāʾ.** Sayyidnā Ismāʿīl ibn Hiba states in *al-Kawākib al-Durriyya* that on a certain night the veil of the prophets dies and the Nāṭiq dies through that veil. The transition of the prophets occurs first, and then the Prophet's. Just as ʿAlī died before the Prophet — though he lived thirty years after the Prophet's apparent death — in reality the veil of the Prophet was active throughout that entire period. For this reason the Prophet said to ʿAlī, "I grant you the right to divorce ʿĀʾisha after my death."

When the Messenger of Allāh was bewitched, Jibrāʾīl said to him, "You are the Muʿawwidhatān (the two refuges)." The recitation of Sūrat al-Falaq dispelled the effect of that magic.

### The Qurʾān and its inner order

*Bismillāh al-Raḥmān al-Raḥīm* is repeated fourteen times in the Qurʾān, for many reasons. Everything that does not begin with the Basmala is held to be deficient.

Concerning the Qurʾān, the Master of the Universe said: "This is the Furqān which is not separate from the Burhān. This is the light that the slumberers cannot recognise. This is a river whose depth cannot be sounded. This is the fountain that never runs dry."

Imām al-Ḥasan said: "There are four orders of knowledge in this Qurʾān — *ʿibārāt* (the expressions, for the common people), *ishārāt* (the signs, for the elect), *laṭāʾif* (the subtleties, for the saints of Allāh), and *ḥaqāʾiq* (the realities, for the prophets)."

All other scriptures and books were revealed from the lowest level of the spiritual world, but the Qurʾān was revealed from the highest — that is, from al-ʿAql al-Awwal (the First Intellect).

Had the people of the seventies stood at the elevation required to receive it, only Sūrat al-Fātiḥa would have sufficed, for it contains the essence of the entire Qurʾān. In the period of *kashf*, more than this will be unveiled.

At one time, the most verses in any single revelation (fifty) were sent down in Sūrat al-Mursalāt, indicating fifty Imāms from Imām al-Ḥasan onwards before the Day of Resurrection. In that Sūra a single phrase is repeated ten times, signalling: ten persons shall be brought down at Ṣakhra.

Imām Jaʿfar al-Ṣādiq said, "I can articulate every word of the Qurʾān in seven aspects." Then he said: "Nay — in seventy."

The phrase *Bismillāh al-Raḥmān al-Raḥīm* contains nineteen letters; the occurrences of *Bismillāh*, *al-Raḥmān*, and *al-Raḥīm* in the Qurʾān are each divisible by nineteen:

### Ḥadīth and Qurʾān: a single light

The Qurʾān and ḥadīth bear a single meaning, issuing from one light. That to which the Messenger said, "This is a verse," became Qurʾān; that to which he said, "This is my word," became ḥadīth. Both emerge from the same source, yet when we read the Qurʾān we never doubt that it is not a ḥadīth, nor when we read a ḥadīth do we doubt that it is not Qurʾān. The wonder is that he set their cadences so distinct.

### The change of veils

When the Prophet said *Man kuntu mawlāhu fa-hādhā ʿAlī mawlāhu*, the veils of both were exchanged — ʿAlī assumed the veil of the Prophet and the Prophet assumed the veil of ʿAlī. Nāṭiqs and Awṣiyāʾ die through their veils: ʿAlī thus "died" within the Prophet's lifetime. The veil of the Prophet's death is in truth the veil of ʿAyn — that is, ʿAyn died before Mīm. All prophets die within the lifetime of the Prophet, since he is *Sayyid al-Nāṭiqīn*, and their affairs are continuous with his.

The Nāṭiq dies through his veils, meaning he employs them for his death and burial. Because the veil had been exchanged, the Prophet died through the veil. The one who shrouded him was ʿAbd Allāh ibn Rawāḥa; the one who buried him, ʿAbd Allāh ibn Masʿūd; and the one who remained at the grave, Ubayy ibn Kaʿb.

All the reports of Karbalāʾ have come down to us through Isḥāq ibn Nabāta. Imām Ḥusayn took him along for precisely this purpose. Imām Ḥusayn was deprived of water because the entire water there had become a confluence of corrupt residues; the efforts of Ḥaḍrat Qāsim and Ḥaḍrat ʿAbbās to bring water were aimed at displacing some of those corrupt residues and yielding up evil.

The five who are the image of God are mentioned in Sūrat al-Ikhlāṣ: *Qul huwa Allāhu aḥad* (Muḥammad), *Allāhu al-Ṣamad* (Fāṭima), *Lam yalid* (Ḥasan), *wa-lam yūlad* (Ḥusayn), *aḥad* (ʿAlī).

Imām Jaʿfar al-Ṣādiq said that the believers stand within a circle. If anyone steps outside, nature returns him to it; if anyone seeks entry from without, nature stops him. The Qurʾān describes the same matter: the circle of the Covenant within which lies mercy, and outside which lies chastisement.[^cite-4]

ʿAlī said in the *Four Hundred Counsels*: "If a person falls asleep after reciting *Qul huwa Allāh*, seventy thousand angels guard him until he wakes." Reciting it three times is equivalent to reciting the whole Qurʾān.

The Messenger of Allāh made Madīna his stronghold. Even after the conquest of Makka, he did not make Madīna a fortress — for "*lā hijrata baʿd al-fatḥ*" (no migration after the conquest): after the conquest, the blessings of hijra come to an end.

Why did the Prophet say, "Seek knowledge even unto China"? Because China at that time was an island. The point is that even if you must leave your kinsfolk and travel to distant isles, you should go.

Ubayy ibn Kaʿb attained the heart of Imām al-Ḥasan's temple. If there were no prophets, we would not have been born — they are the origin of our birth: *law-lāka la-mā khalaqtu al-aflāk*.

Maulana ʿAlī says: "Two of us shall emerge — the first will descend from the second's descendants. The first is named Mahdī, the second Walī."

The Prophet said: "I have received four things from the Throne of Mahdi which none has received but me: Sūrat al-Fātiḥa, Āyat al-Kursī, the closing verses of Sūrat al-Baqara which we recite when laying the deceased in the grave, and Sūrat al-Kawthar."

If in doubt, recite Āyat al-Kursī. To sharpen memory, recite Sūrat al-Muzammil — it will sharpen the memory so keenly that people will call you a sorcerer. Reciting Sūrat al-Wāqiʿa wards off destitution and prevents the stretching out of the hand. Sūrat Yāsīn — every word brings glad tidings of Paradise.

After the obligatory prayer one should recite *Astaghfir Allāh* seven times. Two things of peace have come from Allāh: one has risen and one remains among us. What has risen is the essence of the Messenger; what remains is the word *Astaghfir Allāh* recited at the close of Āyat al-Kursī. Those forgivenesses are in truth the virtues of the Mahdī.

When Maulana ʿAlī travelled to Yemen on the Prophet's command, he conquered the whole of Yemen by sermon, and the whole of Yemen entered Islam. When the Messenger appointed him judge, ʿAlī feared the weight of the responsibility. The Prophet gave him his saliva, then said: "Close your mouth — let no word issue until you have prayed two *rakʿahs*. In the first recite Āyat al-Kursī; in the second recite Sūrat al-Aʿrāf." ʿAlī did so, and thereafter became the most learned of the umma and its finest judge. Such is the miracle of the Prophet's saliva — what then of the rest of his virtues?

In Sūrat al-ʿAṣr, truth is linked with patience: with patience for the hardships that beset the recognition of truth. The destination of truth is one, but there are diverse milestones along the way. Truth here denotes the essence of the Imām, and patience in their gnosis is incumbent. Drinking water is the knowledge of taʾwīl (esoteric exegesis); food is the knowledge of realities.

*Samʿ* (hearing) is the instrument granted for the perception of what has passed, what has disappeared, what has died — for learning the past. *Fikr* (thought) is the instrument by which we acquire knowledge of what is to come: we give a form within our souls to that which is about to occur and fix it within our own configuration. Allāh presents such things to His servants in dreams — disclosing the past to give intimation of the future, granting glad tidings or warning of evil.

### The *waṣiyya*

A *waṣiyya* (testament) means obedience to Allāh and His Messenger and to the one to whom command has been entrusted. Matters of inheritance are determined by Allāh's command, whether one makes a testament or not; the estate is divided into six shares (*sitta sihām*) — self, father, mother, son, daughter, wife.

The testament is the declaration *Lā ilāha illā Allāh, Muḥammad rasūl Allāh*, obedience to the Prophet, and the conduct of religious affairs accordingly. A testament is made to the soul that is about to depart; it is an elective act. Those who remain behind do not make a testament, for after death the person has no further power over the dispositions of inheritance. The name of supplicating Allāh is *waṣiyya*.

Marriage and divorce are, in reality, matters of formation. Maulana ʿAlī said, "I would take up the sword, but I would not divorce." The excision of the form once it has come to the temple is divorce. Each Imām has a particular portfolio in which he labours. Imām al-Ḥasan married fifty-three times and divorced — for there were states in which it was his charge to exclude. Imām ʿAlī Zayn al-ʿĀbidīn brought a portfolio of supplication, and the Messenger brought the affairs of ʿAlī Amīr al-Muʾminīn.

The Prophet said: "Whoever neglects the *witr* or delays the dawn prayer is not of us and our Shīʿa. Whoever commits adultery with a married woman, who uses intoxicants, is not of us. Whoever does not know the truth of me and my Ahl al-Bayt is also not of me."

### The Prophet's instructions

The Prophet said: "Haste is error, and to act with patience is among the attributes of the Almighty." Yet in three matters there is a command to hasten: 1. the guardianship of the orphan; 2. the marriage of the widow; 3. the burial of the deceased.

The Prophet loved three things especially: the *miswāk*; the use of perfume; and the love of women. The miswāk works upon the teeth (an emblem of limits) — clearing doubts about the limits and conveying their teachings simultaneously, so the miswāk is drawn across the breadth, not the length. Perfume immediately becomes nourishment for the soul — passion of worship and ardour arise from it. The Messenger loved his wives deeply: he had twelve wives, nine at one time, and though preoccupied with his ministry, none ever complained of being deprived of his time. He said, "Whoever does not marry is not of my umma," and "the adulterer marries only an adulteress, and the chaste does not marry an adulteress."

### Three categories within the soul

Three matters concern the self: that which is yours and belongs to Allāh — your soul, which is yours but ultimately His; that which is yours and shall be yours — your action, by which the soul is shaped; and that which is yours and from Allāh — your supplication answered. The last is signified by the supplication at the close of wuḍūʾ.

### The planetary correspondences

Zuḥal (Saturn) and Bahrām (Mars) are two of the seven planets. Sunday's first hour belongs to the Sun; its second hour to Venus. Monday's first hour belongs to the Moon; the first hour of the week belongs to Saturn. Saturn in Persian is *Kayvān*, set under Jupiter (Jaʿfar), then Mars (ʿAmmār), then the Sun (Ubayy ibn Kaʿb). In the first hour of the week the lights of Saturn from every sphere descend upon this world; the office of the first hour is entrusted to Salmān al-Fārsī. The planets are essentially fixed in their stations, and their gnostics labour in concert with them.

Kayvān's role is, by the antithetical invitation, to deepen the hostility of those he draws into his influence — that he may consummate their deeds and that the believers may be purified. Kayvān and Bahrām are good in themselves, though their effects appear harsh and inauspicious — like a father's scolding, painful for the good but rightly placed. On Saturdays, the people of the antagonistic order hunt and slaughter; the inner parable of their hunting is the offering of the noble sacrifice on the day of Ghadīr Khumm. The Imām preys upon our souls and casts the trap of truth that our souls may be caught therein. The Qāʾim ʿalā Dhikrihi al-Salām will be the greatest hunter; the prey of *nās* and *nās* in daʿwa are those familiar with the daʿwa, the Imāms themselves. The Qāʾim will migrate to Maʾrib and invite the whole world to Islam. Marriage is contracted at the hour of the *Saʿdayn*. The Sun casts its effects upon the Kaʿba so that one who is awakened by it may awaken — busying himself in preparation for the appearance of the Qāʾim.

Millions of tons of substance fall daily upon the world — cosmic dust. These are the residues of our world and other realms, descending from the heavens; all these planets administer their dispatch. Maulana Aḥmad ibn ʿAbd Allāh, the author of *al-Mursalīn*, said: "The stars of the heavens are countless; their total is 1,029 — 1,022 fixed stars and 7 planets. In the same measure, 1,029 *ḥudūd* (limits) are summoned to the Imām's call to truth — 7 in the Palace of the Planets and 1,022 in the Palace of the Stars. All revolve about the Kaʿba, which is the essence of the Qāʾim. For this reason Maulana ʿAlī says, *Anā Kaʿbat Allāh, anā Bayt Allāh*."

A slab has three dimensions — length, width, depth. ʿĪsā was upon the cross three days, signifying that he engaged in instruction for three days after his apparent death. A believer has three signs: prayer, fasting, and almsgiving. A dāʿī raises or lowers the seat of his limit by the measure of his perseverance in knowledge and action.

There is a distance of rank between the limit (ḥadd) and the Imām. When the Imām wishes to bring a limit near, he elevates its rank, narrowing the distance between himself and that limit. Should there be deficiency in knowledge or action, the Imām lowers it, widening the distance. Every man's scales of knowledge and action have been set; either he lightens them or weighs them. You will see no change in the *Sunna* of Allāh, nor any alteration. He who does good is exalted; he who does evil falls. The soul that ascends to the heavens ascends; the soul that descends descends.

Within our body lie the four humours (*arbāʿ*) — sanguine, choleric, phlegmatic, melancholic. These ummahs raise or precipitate things from the *arbāʿ*, the molecules of our body. Fire consumes the messengers, twice over. When the humours diverge in temperament, the body sickens. The soul follows these dispositions and ascends with those who accept its invitation; those who refuse, it casts down into the body. Every subtle thing grows with time, and every impure thing falls with time. If one limit was placed above another, it is because the intellect perceived a change in its disposition. This system operates in every sphere: had the fatigue of the umma and its dispositions overcome the soul, neither he who advances nor he who is being advanced would have gone forward.

### Suction at the lowest soul

The lowest soul is that by which the outward life is sustained; the highest soul is the rational soul. Many intermediate souls lie between. Around the Kaʿba there is a special water called *Māʾ Saʿīd*. It has become the conduit for the conveyance of residues to the heavens and the zodiac. When the Imām's *bint* is divided into five kinds, one of them becomes the Mother of Saʿīd, pouring water into residues. The complete soul of one who commits suicide is dispersed throughout the body, and for this reason the funeral prayer is not offered over him.

### Islām and faith

The phrase *Lā ilāha illā Allāh* is repeated in the Qurʾān in six dialects and recited thirty-nine times. ### The Prophet's poisoning

The Prophet was poisoned by the Shamrat al-Khaybriyya. As soon as she placed the herb in her mouth, it cried out, "O Messenger of Allāh, poison has been mingled with me!" The Messenger spat the morsel forth at once. In truth the morsel was a composition of herb, shock, and poison: the poison was compounded of the elements of Miṣr and the arrogant. ### On the soul

Someone asked Amīr al-Muʾminīn: "What is the sense of the self?" He said: "It is an astronomical power whose origin is the sun and whose birth is upon the physical side. Its seat is the heart, and its function is sensation, motion, advance, and dominion. The cause of its separation from the body is the divergence of substances — the four humours. When the soul leaves the body, it returns to its origin in the heavens."

### The lawful and the slaughtered

The goat is a lawful form among animals, yet its consumption is not permitted unless it is ritually slaughtered. Likewise, it is not enough that a believer be born in a believer's house, though the believer's child has been born in lawful form. He must also die a lawful death, attained only through the *slaughter* of the Holy Age. Just as we do not eat a goat that has not died by slaughter, so too, if a believer dies without the Covenant, his exaltation will not partake of his form — for it will not be lawful to him.

Within are the *Laṭāʾif*, parts of which fall at the close of the cover. Within these is the *Rūḥāniyya*, which falls at the close of the peacock.

### Lessons of Mulla Ghulam Hussain (*Dār al-Hijra*)

The Qurʾān we hold in our hands is a collection of letters, verses, sūras, and signs. Set over against it is the essence of the Imām, a collection of forms. Every incoming Imām is in truth a continuation of the Qurʾān. The Qurʾān refers to the Imām or the Imāmate, for which the Prophet said: "*The Book of Allāh and my Ahl al-Bayt*: they shall never part until they return to me at the Pool of Kawthar." That is, what the Qurʾān says shall be the work of the Ahl al-Bayt, and what the Ahl al-Bayt say, the verses of the Qurʾān shall witness and evidence.

The Imām cannot die without a *naṣṣ*. Imām Jaʿfar al-Ṣādiq wrote a designation upon Maulana Ismāʿīl and then passed away; thereafter the Imām showed the people that Ismāʿīl had indeed passed away. Yet those who know the realities know that Imām Jaʿfar died there in the Ismāʿīlī form (as the apparent corpse), and Maulana Ismāʿīl was present in the Jaʿfarī form (in life). All this took place through veils.

The Imām designates for his son for four days, forty days, or four years, yet does not entrust to him the temple he has built through his chapters. He entrusts the Temple-Imām to his son at the final moment. *Wilāya* is real, and its state lies hidden within all other devotions. The self desires to make the sensible and the noble like itself, and this is most readily accomplished in the city of Allāh. The heat generated in a person's soul by fasting melts the sensual and irascible forces of the soul and converts it into relationship.

The people of former days had little knowledge, yet upon that scant ground they raised a lofty edifice of faith. In our time, we have much knowledge, yet upon it our faith stands small and slight.

Jābir ibn Fahd was the elder brother of Sayyidnā Muḥammad ibn Fahd, whose biography is treated in *ʿIbrat al-Labīb*. In his early days he could neither read nor write, yet later he advanced until he reached the rank of *duʿāʾ* (prayer / Summoner). In that early state he had a wife named Zaynab, herself a scholar who studied under Sayyidnā Dāʾūd ibn ʿAjab Shāh in Yemen. She had completed several books and was reading *Janl al-Ḥaqāʾiq* (the first book of realities composed as verse) when Sayyidnā Dāʾūd ibn ʿAjab Shāh resolved to journey to India. She petitioned: "O Sayyidnā, you are travelling — entrust me to a teacher that my study may continue in your absence." Sayyidnā Dāʾūd said, "You should read with your husband." Just as Qanbar, while living in the house, could not perceive the atmosphere, Zaynab too did not perceive Jābir. She said, "O Lord, you know they cannot read or write." He replied: "You shall read the text aloud, and they shall explain it to you."

### On certitude

Certitude in the best (*ẓannuhum akbar min imānikum*): the rank of certitude that you would reach by utmost effort and toil — *they* will be taken to it by their conviction alone.

When the cow is released into the field, she takes full advantage of the opportunity. She picks and consumes green grass, storing it in her stomach; then she sits beneath a shady tree, brings everything back from her stomach, chews it thoroughly, and only then eats. The beneficiary, likewise, should reflect upon the knowledge he has gained from assemblies, gatherings, or lessons during his free time, considering how to improve his life through those sciences. In the same way, what we ought to do in eleven months is concentrated into the city of Allāh.

Just as we have three veils over our hearts, the Kaʿba also has three veils — and the Kaʿba bears three meanings. The cleansing of the soul takes place in the heavens; nature uses temperament to elevate the souls and uses temperament to unleash them. Temperament has become the spiritual stepping-stone of these lives; they rise as vapours and descend in the form of dew.

The private is compassion with God. *Nāsūt* is connected to the particular divinity, just as the heavens are connected to the world of life. This city is an exemplar of Allāh's wisdom, within which Laylat al-Qadr lies. At the Imām's bidding, the Supreme Limit draws the form of its boundaries. The pre-burial life of the shroud is high (*al-saqqūn al-saqyūn*), and the post-burial life is low, upon which the grave is requited. Those who enter the world (itself a grave) are the best, while those who emerge after being buried in the earth are inferior. High-ranking veils are included among the veils that are grown in the elect; and from the lowest, the veils become those through which the Imām is visible.

The prophets are the days of Rajab and the Imāms are its nights — for we know the Imāms through the prophets, just as we know the night by the day. The prophets are manifest and the Imāms are concealed, as days are visible and nights are concealed. Fasting is observed during the day. The thirty days of the city of Rajab do not signify Imāms in continuous succession, but a special selection of thirty Imāms. From Maulana Ibrāhīm to Maulana Abū Ṭālib there were seventy Imāms, of whom only thirty were elected. For this reason the fasts of Rajab are voluntary — for fasting is observed in the day. The fasts of the city of Allāh are by contrast obligatory: the days of the city of Allāh signify the Imāms, and its nights the proofs of these Imāms. During the period of *atma*, the Imāms manifested and their proofs were hidden.

Maulana ʿAlī was asked: "What two friends, once separated, never meet again?" He said: "The soul of a believer and his body. Once the soul departs, it never re-enters the body." And what friends never get along? He said: "Night and day." When night becomes day, it is no longer called night, but day; and when day becomes night, it is no longer called day, but becomes night.

### "Something" and the verses

An object is something that is not a mirage. The mirage is a *lasha*, and so the dead body is called a *lasha* — that is, the original person (the soul) has departed, and the object that is visible is not, in truth, an object, though it appears so. In the realities, *Shayʾ* is called a believer and *Lā-shayʾ* a disbeliever. "We have encompassed everything within the Manifest Imām" — that is, the Imām has not encompassed the door, the threshold, the house — rather, the meaning of this verse is that every believer has been encompassed by the Imām. Every Imām shall conjoin Nāsūt and Lāhūt on the day of the month and attain a station. Allāh — that is, the Imām — has assured power over the souls of all the believers and their affairs. In Allāh there is something ambiguous: that Allāh is the All-Knowing. That is, where are all the matters of the deceased believer, his soul, his virtues, his life, and where do they develop? How much waste has been cleansed, and how much remains? When will all the waste be cleansed? For He is the Knower of all these things. There are three verses in the Qurʾān concerning "something."

Synagogues come together to be separated, and separate to come together. The heart is the leader of the entire body; in the same way, *Bāb al-Abwāb* takes the place of the heart in the Imām's temple, and the heart becomes the temple, for its leadership is established over the whole temple.

### Husband and wife as inner cipher

The Prophet said, "The pursuit of knowledge is the duty of every Muslim man and woman." In the marital relationship, it is the wife's task to express her desire for the marital process, but she cannot solicit it secretly or present herself uninvited; it is the husband's right to summon his wife to that office whenever he wishes. It is the wife's task always to prepare herself, that she may present herself when her husband asks. In the inner interpretation, it is incumbent on the beneficiary (wife) to express her desire for knowledge to her benefactor (husband), but she cannot seek that knowledge; calling her to receive knowledge is grounded in his discernment of her readiness. When the time is ripe, it is incumbent on the beneficiary to offer herself to receive. The beneficiary should always be in preparation.

### ʿAlī's "highest garment"

There is an incident concerning Maulana ʿAlī. In an assembly he counselled that one should put on one's best garment for the Friday prayer. One Friday he himself arrived in a patched garment, and someone objected that he had taught one ought to come dressed in one's best. He answered: "I have two garments. One has two patches, and one has only one — and so I have come dressed in the highest of the two." In truth, garments are one's veils. He was saying: "I have come in the highest of my two lowest veils." That ʿAlī who came on Friday was, in fact, the highest of his two lowest veils.

### Lesson 5

Just as you permit the slaughter to cool, let food cool before eating. The heat of food is the share of the devil and the elements of shock. Hot food is forbidden: there is no blessing in it, you cannot satisfy your stomach with it, you shall not be safe from hellfire, and you shall die a bad death. The sins of the believer come in the form of an animal for slaughter to cleanse him, upon which the wicked form rides. With the slaughter, the wicked form is sundered from him; the flesh becomes pure, and its connection to the wicked form is severed. The believer is the instrument by which this connection is broken; the meat of the slaughtered animal becomes part of the believer's body. But just as residues remain in our body even after death and emerge in the course of life, the effect of the wicked form remains in the meat. When we cook it, those residual effects are dispelled by the heat — this is what it means to seek refuge from the fire of hell. If these evil effects enter the body, the body becomes a haven for them. The Holy Prophet thrice told Maulana ʿAlī during his life to give him charity.

### Lesson 11: What is taʾwīl?

Maulana ʿAlī says: "There are four things which every intelligent person of my umma shall observe: *istimāʿ al-ʿilm* (hearing knowledge), memorising it, acting upon it, and broadcasting it." Four kinds of people renew their concerns: the patient when healed; the polytheist when he embraces Islam; one who emerges from the Friday prayer (an increase in the power of accountability); the pilgrim who completes his Ḥajj.

Then knowledge: it has four conditions — silence (of one's present thoughts), attentive listening, action upon it, and transmission. Be silent when there are articles of taʾwīl and reality. When knowledge is attained, connect with your highest limit (prayer). Affirmations are in truth knowledge. Prayer is the name of connection to one's highest limit; we gain our knowledge in the state of prayer. There are four limits of prayer, just as there are four walls of the house: *al-waqt* (time), the *qibla*, *nāṭiq*, ablution. The future of the Kaʿba refers to the exalted. The support received in prayer is faith, which has four pillars: patience, justice, jihad (the effort of improvement).

Four branches of patience: *al-ṣabr ʿalā al-shawq* — patience over longing (whoever desires Paradise must be patient and abstain from lust); *al-ṣabr ʿalā al-khawf* — patience in fear; the *zāhid* — piety: leave the forbidden so resolutely that you even begin to relinquish the lawful (the one who leaves the forbidden is called a believer; the one who leaves the lawful is called *zāhid*; the *zāhid* never enters into trouble, that is, he feels no hardship); and *al-khayrāt*. Certitude: wisdom is produced by commentary upon reason; *taʾwīl al-ḥikma* (the taʾwīl of wisdom, which is the rank of supplication); and lesson: by attaining it, one becomes first in all things.

He prays: when length, breadth, and depth were created, had we stopped at length, there would have been no need to come into this world — we would have become *Anwār* and *Laṭīf*.

### Lesson 16

Numerically, *al-Qadr* — 1 + 30 + 100 + 4 + 500 = 635. In a sense, Maulana ʿAlī has 635 special forms that illuminate the day and night of Laylat al-Qadr.

"*Innā anzalnāhu fī Laylat al-Qadr. Wa-mā Laylat al-Qadr?*" There is a difference between *mā* and *man*. *Mā* is used for that which we do not know; *man* (mind) for that which we know. *Man* refers to the essence; *mā* comes to indicate the attributes. On this night, the Qurʾān is revealed to the hearts of the believers, just as it was revealed to the pure heart of the Messenger.

ʿĀʾisha narrates that the Messenger said something to Fāṭima and she began to weep; then he said something more, and she laughed. *Tabassum* refers to the acquiring of waste; *zaḥaqa*, a fuller smile showing some teeth, refers to the discharge of impurity; laughter refers to the attainment of life and fortune by means of the waste. She asked, "Why are you weeping?" She said: "The Messenger told me, 'Jibrāʾīl used to present the Qurʾān to me once a year — but this year the Qurʾān was presented to me twice. I knew that my death was imminent.' On hearing this I wept. Then he said, 'You shall be the first of my Ahl al-Bayt to die and meet me' — and I laughed when I heard that." Maulatnā Fāṭima died seventy days after the death of the Prophet. Then the Messenger said, "You are the best of children to me. Are you content that all of you are *Sayyidat al-Nisāʾ al-Muʾminīn*?" At that, she laughed.

The Qurʾān's twofold presentation signifies that Maulatnā Fāṭima was declared as the Qurʾān. Previously ʿAlī was the *mumthal* (signified), but he became the plateau, and now Fāṭima has become the *mumthal* of the Qurʾān. All prophets, Imāms, and offspring of the Awṣiyāʾ derive from Maulana Fāṭima — this is the gathering you met in Laylat al-Qadr.

### Lesson 17

Outward purification is by water; inner purification is by knowledge. As long as we are in the world, purity is incumbent upon us. Purification is incumbent upon every part of the body. It is the movement from evil to evil, or from disbelief to faith. *Yā* purifies the heart; ablution purifies the organs upon which faith has been made obligatory.

There are five *rukūʿs* in Sūrat Yāsīn, and in the same way five things are obligatory upon the heart: confession, knowledge, contract, willingness, and acknowledgement that Allāh is one and has no partner. The Messenger of Allāh is His servant — what has come from Allāh is a Messenger and a Book; the Prophet must be the means to know Allāh. The Prophet came as pure and purifier. The one who passes beyond his usefulness is called *Yā*. The office of *Yā* is to impart knowledge and to purify the heart. *Yā* is the heart of the Qurʾān, revealed to the heart of the world. The heavens and the earth were created only for the Messenger, that the Prophet might come.

There is great virtue in feeding people: our sins that are not washed away by ordinary worship are washed away by giving food. The Prophet asked, "What is Ḥajj called?" — and replied, "Ḥajj is the name of pilgrimage."

### Lesson 2: When prayers are answered

There are five places where supplications are answered: at the recitation of the Qurʾān; at the call to prayer; when it rains (rain is a gathering of beauties scarcely returned to the world — and so when rain ceases we are commanded to seek forgiveness, for rain is held back by sins); when two ranks meet in battle; and when the oppressor commits oppression, the prayer of the oppressed is answered. If Sūrat al-Inshirāḥ is recited twenty-one times, a special light is generated in the heart.

### The etiquette of food as a figure of acquiring knowledge

How should knowledge be acquired? The answer is found in the etiquette of eating. Food is knowledge, and to obtain food is to acquire knowledge. As there are many kinds of food, so there are many kinds of knowledge — some sciences are drunk as water, some are hard and solid as meat that must be chewed. The purpose of food is to nourish the body and accomplish the daily work of the world; likewise, the purpose of knowledge is to establish a spiritual conception by which we may meet our spiritual needs and equip the soul to depart this physical world.

The first condition of diet is that it be *ḥalāl*. The first principle of acquiring knowledge is to affirm that we have obtained that knowledge from the knowledge of the Ahl al-Bayt. If we acquire knowledge from people of dishonest pedigree, that knowledge, however interesting and wonderful, shall not be permissible — just as forbidden meat, however delicious and beautifully served, remains forbidden because the name of Allāh was not pronounced over its slaughter. So first one must know that the knowledge being acquired is in truth *ḥalāl*.

Salt is to be tasted before and after the meal, which cures seventy maladies — that is, before and after the acquisition of knowledge one should seek the supplication by whose knowledge seventy doubts are dispelled. Likewise, *Bismillāh al-Raḥmān al-Raḥīm* is to be recited before eating: its nineteen letters signify the angels appointed as guardians of Hell — that is, one asks these angels to safeguard us against the hell of doubt through this knowledge.

For food it is said that the morsel should be small, well chewed, and that one should not extend the hand for the second until the first morsel is complete. This points to reading an article thoroughly and thoughtfully — examining how one might act upon it, whether one's life may be improved in light of this new thing. If a philosophy of origins or the like is being taught, every claim should be examined and weighed, presented against prior conceptions, the Qurʾān, and the ḥadīth, until the truth of the matter is confirmed within the heart (the first morsel swallowed). Then one reads the next portion.

We consume food three times a day to maintain the body's energy. Otherwise the body weakens. Likewise, if the whole day's food were consumed at once, health would become a burden and daily tasks sluggish. The etiquette of eating prescribes withdrawing one's hand while still a little hungry and eating only enough to provide comfortable energy until the next meal. So with knowledge: if a book is being read, one should not attempt to read it all at once or to acquire all its knowledge in a single sitting. Just as it is impossible to eat the whole day's meal at once, knowledge must be acquired little by little, with intervals — taking a break to reflect and act upon it, as we give a meal time to digest. Like diet, the acquisition of knowledge is a continuous process to be sustained throughout life. It is not that one studies for a time and then for a time studies nothing; rather, learning must be made a part of our daily routine, that spiritual powers may be constantly maintained.

When a person eats too much at once and does not expend this energy through activity, his nature becomes burdened and weight increases; the more weight, the slower one becomes in daily affairs. Similarly, by reading too much and not acting upon the knowledge, doubts arise, the soul becomes heavy with the burden of those doubts, and grows sluggish in good deeds. As with physical health, spiritual health endures only when knowledge is acquired in measured portions, translated into action, and prayers are followed. It is important to maintain this balance throughout life. If knowledge is not acquired, then the soul becomes weak like the body, and the unruly soul gains strength and incites to sins. Conversely, if much knowledge is acquired at once and not practised, the soul becomes burdened with it and grows sluggish in its actions. If wheat is eaten directly, the stomach pains. One must grind it, cook it, chew it, and then eat. Similarly, higher learning cannot be received directly.

The duration of the world is ten peacocks — though Hazrat Ḍiyāʾ ʿAlī Ṣāḥib has mentioned three peacocks also. All the Nāṭiqs have entered the ranks of Divine Revelation (one of the levels of Resurrection), and among them the Prophet Noah holds a very special station — for he entered Constantinople.

### *Ramaḍān* as the rank of the foundation

The month of Ramadan is the noblest of the twelve months. Its taʾwīl is that every prophet and every Imām has twelve proofs. One of the Nāṭiq's twelve proofs is his successor — the foundation of the Nāṭiq's life — and after the death of the Nāṭiq he is called his Waṣī and is established in the Nāṭiq's place. The eleven stars refer to the eleven proofs of Ḥaḍrat Yaʿqūb, of whom Yūsuf was the twelfth. The sun refers to Yaʿqūb himself, and the moon to his chapters. Their prostration signifies that all shall obey Prophet Yūsuf, and that Yūsuf shall be elevated in rank above all of them.

The city of Ramadan is the noblest and best of the twelve months, signifying the foundation of the Prophet — the bearer of the knowledge and wisdom of the Messenger, in whom is summed the entirety of the Sharīʿa and the truths of the Book and the Sunna which the Messenger brought.

He appointed a chapter for his son (the *Ibn Tām*) which would gather the verses of his daʿwa according to the principle, and the heart of the *Ibn Tām* became a temple. ### The final hour of the Messenger

Abū Dharr said: "I was with the Messenger before he died. At that moment the Messenger said to me, 'O Abū Dharr, come near.' I kept his head upon my chest until Maulana ʿAlī came in. Then the Messenger said, 'Rise, Abū Dharr — for ʿAlī deserves more than you to keep my head upon his chest.' Then ʿAlī sat in Abū Dharr's place and laid the Prophet's head upon his chest. Then the Messenger said, 'O Abū Dharr, sit before me.' Abū Dharr sat before him. The Prophet took Abū Dharr's hand into his own, spoke to him, and gave him instructions."

Abū Dharr is in truth the highest figure in the chain of the Prophet's *Bāb* (Maulana Salmān al-Fārsī) after Jaʿfar al-Ṭayyār and Ḥamza. His caliphate has also stood in the Salmānī ranks. The illness of the Messenger is in reality a veil upon the eyes of those who behold it; the patient sees the patient — that is, the messengers who gathered before the Messenger are the ones who saw the patient. The Messenger disclosed his illness because the intention was to explain to the people that after him, truth itself would fall ill — that thereafter his Ahl al-Bayt would be afflicted by the persecution of the Mughalbib. He disclosed his illness through the low veil of his successor's veil.

His leaning upon the chest of Abū Dharr — the Prophet's veil — was a token of trust and confidence in Abū Dharr for certain matters to be accomplished by his hand. The Prophet's laying his head upon ʿAlī's chest when ʿAlī came signalled that the Prophet would return in the form of ʿAlī. The Messenger said to Abū Dharr, "Come and sit before me." The two hands of the Prophet are Imām al-Ḥasan and Imām al-Ḥusayn — that is, the station of Abū Dharr and his merger lie with Imām al-Ḥasan and Imām al-Ḥusayn, for al-Ḥasan is in the hands of al-Ḥusayn. ### *Shafʿ*, *Witr*, and the procession prayer

*Ṣalāt al-Istiftāḥ* (the opening prayer) corresponds to the call to prayer and the *iqāma*: the first *rakʿa* corresponds to the *adhān*, and the second to the *iqāma*. For this reason the *adhān* and *iqāma* are not pronounced for the *Shafʿ* and *Witr*.

*Ṣalāt al-Shafʿ wa-l-Witr*: it is reported from Imām Jaʿfar al-Ṣādiq that *Witr* means "to give a pause," and the *Witr* includes the procession prayer. *Shafʿ* is two *rakʿa*s; *Witr* is one *rakʿa* in which the *qunūt* is recited. Other matters may be taken up after the *Shafʿ*, but there is no interval between *Witr* and the procession prayer, for there is continuity between the two. The minimum number of *rakʿa*s in a prayer is always two; the single *rakʿa* of *Witr* is joined to one *rakʿa* of the procession (since two *rakʿa*s prayed sitting equal one *rakʿa*).

## Paradise and Its Gate

The metaphor at the heart of this section is architectural. Every dwelling has a single door through which alone one may enter, and Paradise — properly understood — is no exception. In the esoteric reading preserved by the Ismaili tradition, Paradise (al-janna, the abode of bliss) is none other than al-ʿAql al-Awwal (the First Intellect), and its gate in every age is the Messenger of that age, succeeded after him by his awṣiyāʾ (the executors of his testament). The key to this gate is the kalimat al-shahāda — the testimony of faith ⟪ar:لا الہ الا اللہ محمد الرسول اللہ⟫ — and only the one who utters it sincerely receives that key and enters.[^cite-6]

That the Intellect is the Paradise of the hereafter may be demonstrated as follows. Every measure of comfort, ease, and peace that a human being enjoys flows from intellect; the creatures who have not received their portion of intellect endure pain, hardship, and dread. The wise cannot be wounded by the world; the ignorant is undone by grief, error, sorrow, and worldly greed. If the partial intellect granted to humankind suffices to turn this world into a kind of paradise, it follows a fortiori that the true Paradise is the Intellect itself, since it contains the totality of intellect's portions. From this it follows further that the one who is nearest of all creatures to the Intellect must be the gate of Paradise — for a door stands adjacent to the house it admits one into. ⟪ar:رسول اللہ صلی اللہ علیہ و سلم⟫ was the nearest of all creatures to the Intellect; therefore he is the gate of Paradise.

The one who obeys the Messenger — accepting his commands and grasping the truth of his words and deeds — enters Paradise. The Qurʾān states:

> ⟪quran 4:80⟫
> ⟪ar:‏مَّن يُطِعِ ٱلرَّسُولَ فَقَدْ أَطَاعَ ٱللَّهَ ۖ وَمَن تَوَلَّىٰ فَمَآ أَرْسَلْنَكَ عَلَيْهِمْ حَفِيظًۭا ‎⟫
> *Whoever obeys the Messenger has obeyed Allāh; and whoever turns away — We have not sent you over them as a guardian.*
> — Qurʾān 4:80

In the same manner, every Messenger was the gate of Paradise in his own age, and one reaches Paradise by following his ordinances together with knowledge of their taʾwīl (esoteric exegesis). Whoever accepts the Messenger's law without taʾwīl finds the gate closed.[^cite-7] The Qurʾān states further:

> ⟪quran 39:71⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَسِيقَ ٱلَّذِينَ كَفَرُوٓا۟ إِلَىٰ جَهَنَّمَ زُمَرًا ۖ حَتَّىٰٓ إِذَا جَآءُوهَا فُتِحَتْ أَبْوَبُهَا وَقَالَ لَهُمْ خَزَنَتُهَآ أَلَمْ يَأْتِكُمْ رُسُلٌۭ مِّنكُمْ يَتْلُونَ عَلَيْكُمْ آيَتِ رَبِّكُمْ وَيُنذِرُونَكُمْ لِقَآءَ يَوْمِكُمْ هَذَا ۚ قَالُوا۟ بَلَىٰ وَلَكِنْ حَقَّتْ كَلِمَةُ ٱلْعَذَابِ عَلَى ٱلْكَفِرِينَ ‎⟫
> *And those who disbelieved will be driven to Hell in throngs, until, when they reach it, its gates are opened and its keepers ask them: "Did there not come to you messengers from among yourselves, reciting the signs of your Lord and warning you of this Day of yours?" They will say: "Yes — but the word of punishment has come due against the disbelievers."*
> — Qurʾān 39:71

That the verse describes the gates as being *opened* proves that they had previously been closed. The laws delivered by the ⟪ar:انبیاء علیہم السلام⟫ are bound up in signs and parables; the salvation of their followers lies in the unsealing of those signs. The interpreters of the ⟪ar:انبیا⟫ have always been their ⟪ar:اوصیاء علیہم السلام⟫. When the awṣiyāʾ unveil the realities behind the symbols through taʾwīl, the door opens and the soul finds rest. So too the gates of Paradise are opened in every age, for every believer, by the ⟪ar:امام الزمان⟫.

Every door requires a key, and no one approaches a door without one. The key to the gate of Paradise is the kalima al-ṭayyiba — the wholesome word — and no one has come before ⟪ar:رسول اللہ صلی اللہ علیہ وسلم⟫ without first accepting the testimony of witness. The Messenger himself promised Paradise to whoever uttered the Word with sincerity:

⟪ar-quote:مَنْ قاَلَ لاَ اِلہَ اِلاَّ اَللّٰہ خَالِصاً مُخْلِصاً دَخَلَ الْجَنَّۃَ⟫ — *whoever recites the word of sincerity with a pure heart has entered Paradise.*

The kalimat al-shahāda is thus the key to the gate; whoever bears it to the door enters in.

## The Formation of the Science of the Stars

Ḥaḍrat Idrīs (peace be upon him) was the first to discover ʿilm al-nujūm (the science of the stars), and he devoted thirty years of his life to perfecting it. The formation of this science was his prophetic miracle, which he taught to his contemporaries. He was established between Ādam and Nūḥ, in the cycle of the seventy. The Imam of his time — also called Idrīs — designated him for the imamate before his death, but his community refused to recognise him.[^cite-8]

He put to those whom he summoned a single question: is it possible, in the sight of Allāh, that His earth should be void of His ḥujja (proof)? All replied that it was not. He said: then seek out the proof of Allāh on this earth. They asked: by what means? At this he devised astrology and taught it to them.

When they had mastered it, he commanded them to divide the earth into four quarters and to determine in which quarter the ḥujja was to be found. They did so and saw that in the quarter where they themselves stood the proof of Allāh also lay. He then bade them divide that quarter again into four, and again the ḥujja was in the quarter containing them. He bade them divide a third time, and a fourth, and so on, until the operation closed upon themselves and they recognised: "We find that you are the proof of Allāh, whom He has made a witness against us."

## The Reality of Knowledge

ʿIlm (knowledge) is a single point about which the ignorant have multiplied many words. There are two routes of knowledge. The first proceeds from unity to plurality — from the source (al-manbaʿ) to its outflow (al-muntahā) — and this is the way of revelation (tanzīl) and outward manifestation. The second moves from plurality back to unity — from the outflow to the source — and this is the way of al-asfār al-bāṭiniyya, interior progress. The first route is the grander; through it one understands what has been disclosed concerning soul and body, member and frame, the heavens and the elements, and the orders of generation. The second is the simpler and more openly attested; by it one seeks what lies beneath the point of being, what is folded within the truths and the mysteries — for instance, the recesses of the heavenly books, or the mysteries of the Miʿrāj of our Prophet (may God's prayers and peace be upon him).[^cite-9]

"I have been given the knowledge of the first things and the last things," declared the Commander of the Faithful; "Allāh has made me know the realities of all things, for I am the base of the enclosure. Ask me of all that lies beneath the Throne, for I am the point that the people call *ʿibādan* — a point before which there is no place. I am the point toward which the centre of being is oriented, around which the lines of its circle are drawn."[^cite-10]

Every circle has two corresponding points — a beginning and an end — and it is by virtue of this that a circle is round. So Allāh says: *Just as He began you, so shall you return.* The divine names operate in this same duality: al-Awwal wa-l-Ākhir (the First and the Last), al-Ẓāhir wa-l-Bāṭin (the Manifest and the Hidden); the categories of *azal* (eternity without beginning) and *abad* (eternity without end) likewise refer to these two points. The arc participates in the same orientation, for the arc marks the discontinuity of the realm of being — the gap between the bound and the absolute, between the possible (mumkin) and the necessary (wājib). It points to the proximity of the divine names. By this means, creation and return, descent and ascent, qualification and capacity are united with the Truth despite their apparent duality. Above this position there is nothing except the station of ⟪ar:' أو ادنی'⟫ — "or nearer" — the station of Aḥadiyya (absolute oneness). That is the place where intellect is extinguished and ceremony falls silent. It is the point from which the point of prophethood (nubuwwa) and the point of light (walāya) are themselves interpreted; and these two applications belong specifically to Muḥammad and ʿAlī, since al-nubuwwa al-muṭlaqa and al-wilāya al-muṭlaqa belong to them alone.

## Illusion versus Delusion

**Illusions.** An illusion is, simply put, a misleading perception. It is the seeing — most commonly — or the hearing, tasting, feeling, or smelling of something that is in fact present, but is misinterpreted by the perceiver. Illusions concern actually existing stimuli that are mistaken for something else, or that prove hard to interpret. They are also what enchant audiences at conjuring shows and what equip pavement con-artists with their craft. A practical example: you drape a spare uniform over the door-jamb to air it out; you arrive home late; walking down the unlit hall you glimpse a shadow and take it for an intruder; your pulse quickens — until you realise the intruder is only a coat-hanger wearing shirt and trousers. Another instance: hearing one's name called while the radio plays. Illusions happen to anyone. They are not, in themselves, a sign of mental illness, except when they become persistent and disruptive.

**Delusions.** Delusions are deeply fixed beliefs — either false or fanciful — that an individual maintains despite contradictory evidence. The deluded person cannot reliably distinguish what is real from what is not. Delusions are classed as either bizarre or non-bizarre. A bizarre delusion is one that is strange and wholly implausible: for instance, that aliens have removed all of a person's organs and replaced them with another's. A non-bizarre delusion is one whose content is mistaken but at least possible: for instance, that one is under constant police surveillance.

## The Counterfeit Note

Brothers and sisters of the Jamāʿat — the Lord of the Worlds has bestowed upon us blessings beyond enumeration. Some are woven into our own selves; many more surround us at every step. Gratitude toward Allāh is therefore obligatory in every situation, at every moment. Gratitude not only preserves blessings but multiplies them — *la-in shakartum la-azīdannakum* ("If you give thanks, I will surely increase you"). Wealth is among His foremost blessings; the proof of its character is that it travels and enables one to travel. If money were locked away in pockets or in vaults it would lose its benefit, just as a man confined to his house gains neither wealth nor breadth of intellect nor experience.

When money and wealth travel — that is, are put to productive use in commerce — they grow in value and quantity. Now that you have travelled here for the ʿUrs Sharīf, no doubt each of you brought something to cover your expenses; pardon me — I have no intention of probing pockets — but this much is certain, that every pocket carries some currency. Outward wealth is undoubtedly useful for our worldly affairs; just so, the bāṭinī (inner, esoteric) knowledge is critical and indispensable for our journey beyond death. Whether worldly wealth or wealth in the bāṭinī sciences, money provides our way-fare; indeed, it eases the journey and elevates both worldly and spiritual rank.

The point I wish to bring forward is this: during travel, the money we carry takes mostly the form of paper currency or traveller's cheques. What is a currency? A piece of paper — small or large. Its value is determined not by the weight of the paper nor by its quantity, but by the print upon it. Not every paper counts as currency: it must bear the governor's signature and the watermark approved by the government. A genuine note holds value everywhere. Brothers and sisters of the Jamāʿat — the lesson is this: every human being in their earthly form is a paper note. The raising of one's own value lies in one's own hands; with effort, one may render oneself a note of a thousand, of ten thousand, even of a lakh. Ḥaḍrat ʿAlī (upon him be peace) said: "A human shall attain the very rank he prepares himself for; strive, therefore, for the higher ranks." It is not character, morals, and conduct alone that determine one's worth; we must also bear the Sulaymānī certification — the watermark of their love and devotion. As a counterfeit note is turned away and consigned to the rubbish heap, so our aʿmāl (deeds) must not be rejected as counterfeit. Genuine currency circulates and continues its journey; the fake is bound for a fake destination.

Alḥamdulillāh, every brother and sister, young and old, gathered here for the ʿUrs Sharīf bears the Sulaymānī insignia stamped upon the heart; and in the presence of the Ḥujjat al-Imām, and ultimately in the presence of Allāh, their value and worth — God willing — shall be such that their wealth keeps multiplying and their condition continually improves. Their journey, and the journey of their wealth, shall end in success and triumph and shall be a shield against the Fire and against Saqar. Āmīn.

⟪ar:امیر المومنین  علی ابن ابی طالب⟫ ⟪ar:‏فَإِن لَّمْ تَفْعَلُوا۟ وَلَن تَفْعَلُوا۟ فَٱتَّقُوا۟ ٱلنَّارَ ٱلَّتِى وَقُودُهَا ٱلنَّاسُ وَٱلْحِجَارَةُ ۖ أُعِدَّتْ لِلْكَفِرِينَ ‎ ‏وَبَشِّرِ ٱلَّذِينَ آمَنُوا۟ وَعَمِلُوا۟ ٱلصَّلِحَتِ أَنَّ لَهُمْ جَنَّتٍۢ تَجْرِى مِن تَحْتِهَا ٱلْأَنْهَرُ ۖ كُلَّمَا رُزِقُوا۟ مِنْهَا مِن ثَمَرَةٍۢ رِّزْقًۭا ۙ قَالُوا۟ هَذَا ٱلَّذِى رُزِقْنَا مِن قَبْلُ ۖ وَأُتُوا۟ بِهِۦ مُتَشَبِهًۭا ۖ وَلَهُمْ فِيهَآ أَزْوَجٌۭ مُّطَهَّرَةٌۭ ۖ وَهُمْ فِيهَا خَلِدُونَ ‎ ‏ إِنَّ ٱللَّهَ لَا يَسْتَحْىِۦٓ أَن يَضْرِبَ مَثَلًۭا مَّا بَعُوضَةًۭ فَمَا فَوْقَهَا ۚ فَأَمَّا ٱلَّذِينَ آمَنُوا۟ فَيَعْلَمُونَ أَنَّهُ ٱلْحَقُّ مِن رَّبِّهِمْ ۖ وَأَمَّا ٱلَّذِينَ كَفَرُوا۟ فَيَقُولُونَ مَاذَآ أَرَادَ ٱللَّهُ بِهَذَا مَثَلًۭا ۘ يُضِلُّ بِهِۦ كَثِيرًۭا وَيَهْدِى بِهِۦ كَثِيرًۭا ۚ وَمَا يُضِلُّ بِهِۦٓ إِلَّا ٱلْفَسِقِينَ ‎⟫ ⟪ar:‏مَّن يُطِعِ ٱلرَّسُولَ فَقَدْ أَطَاعَ ٱللَّهَ ۖ وَمَن تَوَلَّىٰ فَمَآ أَرْسَلْنَكَ عَلَيْهِمْ حَفِيظًۭا ‎⟫ ⟪ar:‏وَسِيقَ ٱلَّذِينَ كَفَرُوٓا۟ إِلَىٰ جَهَنَّمَ زُمَرًا ۖ حَتَّىٰٓ إِذَا جَآءُوهَا فُتِحَتْ أَبْوَبُهَا وَقَالَ لَهُمْ خَزَنَتُهَآ أَلَمْ يَأْتِكُمْ رُسُلٌۭ مِّنكُمْ يَتْلُونَ عَلَيْكُمْ آيَتِ رَبِّكُمْ وَيُنذِرُونَكُمْ لِقَآءَ يَوْمِكُمْ هَذَا ۚ قَالُوا۟ بَلَىٰ وَلَكِنْ حَقَّتْ كَلِمَةُ ٱلْعَذَابِ عَلَى ٱلْكَفِرِينَ ‎⟫ ⟪ar:’ أو ادنی‘⟫ ⟪ar:’اتباع‘⟫ ⟪ar:’طاعت‘⟫ ⟪ar:’عبد‘⟫
