## ʿIbāda — On Worship

The world presents itself as a place of strangeness and flux. Every living being possesses an afterlife; for a moment the distinctions between day and night dissolve, yet He alone who does not perish remains—the One Being eternal and unchanging. We sit shadowed by death's proximity, and we carry our deeds toward the world's end. Should those deeds be corrupted, their consequence is corruption: torment of manifold kinds, punishments of every description, and above all the reproach of the Creator of the Universe (Mubdiʿ Taʿālā, the Originator, Most High). When one comes to ask, what answer shall be given?

Whoever abhors the truth deserves abandonment; if the Master is displeased, then the slave lies under curse. May God grant us blessing. Let Him inscribe counsel within our hearts and furnish us with upright character. The ornaments of Direct Behavior—Hope and Fear balanced within Faith—these sustain the soul. We do not dispute concerning punishment and reward; rather, we seek His pleasure from the Master alone. For God's pleasure bears the name of ʿibāda (worship), and Paradise itself is but another name for the pleasure of God.[^cite-1]

## Ghalib's Verses in Praise of ʿAlī

**Manifestation of the Goodness of God, O Soul and Heart of the Final Prophet**

⟪ar: مظهر فيض خدا جان و ديل خاتم الرسول‎‎‎‎‎‎‎ قبلة آل نبي كعبة إيجاد يقين ⟫

*Center of the Family of the Prophet and Ka'aba of articulated faith—*

The Prophet's footprints shine wherever they fall. That very soil becomes the guarantor of the honour of both worlds. Through the relation of his name alone such rank was granted that the vault of heaven bent eternally in service to the earth. For your noble form, the shoulder of the Prophet served as pulpit; for your esteemed name, the stretches of the Throne became gems adorning the divine ring.[^cite-2]

Who can glorify him who is himself the object of God's glorification? Who can beautify the already resplendent garden of the highest heaven? The commodity in the market of transgressions—Asadullah the Lion—has no buyer save you alone. For my nature, immersed in love of Duldúl (the faithful mount of ʿAlī), and for my passion's longing: as far as his feet advance, so shall my forehead follow in prostration at each footprint.[^cite-3]

Spent upon the enemies of the Prophet are the raging fires and bellowing smoke of hell-fire; reserved for the beloved are the flowers and sweet-scented plants of the garden of heaven above.

[^cite-1]: This understanding of ʿibāda as the pursuit of divine pleasure rather than reward-seeking reflects the Ismaili emphasis on spiritual station over external practice. See Kirmani, *al-Masabih fi Ithbat al-Imama*, on the distinction between exoteric observance and esoteric intention.

[^cite-2]: The metaphor of the Prophet's form as pulpit and his name as gem derives from the poetic tradition celebrating the spiritual preeminence of ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib. Cf. al-Muʾayyad's *al-Majalis al-Muʾayyadiyya*, which develops similar imagery of cosmic rank attendant upon the Imam.

[^cite-3]: The image of Duldúl as symbol of devotion and the prostrating forehead as measure of submission appears in classical Ismaili devotional literature celebrating the Imams' authority and the believer's surrender. See Nasir-i Khusraw, *Khwan al-Ikhwan*, on the hierarchy of spiritual attachment.
