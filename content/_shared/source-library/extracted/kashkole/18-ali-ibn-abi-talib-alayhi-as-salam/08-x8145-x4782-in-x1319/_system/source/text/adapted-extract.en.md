## Various Questions Put to ʿAlī

A man once asked ʿAlī, "O Amīr al-Muʾminīn, how far extends the sky from us?" He replied, "There is a limit to the reach of the gaze." That is to say, the extent to which sight returns becomes, for that person, the boundary of the sky.[^cite-1]

---

A Syrian posed another question: "What is the size of the sun, O Amīr al-Muʾminīn?" He answered, "Nine hundred and nine farsaq, while one farsaq equals three sharʿī miles, and one sharʿī mile equals approximately two thousand yards."

---

A Bedouin then inquired, "What is the distance from the earth to the sun?" ʿAlī replied, "If you mount your horse and ride toward the sun, you will arrive there in five hundred years."—adapting his answer, as was his custom, to the understanding of his questioner.[^cite-2]

---

Someone asked the Master of the Universe when the pyramids of Egypt were constructed. He first asked whether any pattern or design was visible upon them. The man replied, "Certainly, my lord! There is the figure of a bird—Nasr (the Eagle)—holding a scorpion in its mouth. Nasr is the name of a star, and Sarṭān is a constellation." ʿAlī said, "These pyramids were built in Egypt when Nasr was in the constellation of Sarṭān. This star travels to a constellation in two thousand years and is currently in Capricorn. Accordingly, it has been six thousand years since their construction."

---

A Jewish scholar posed several questions to Amīr al-Muʾminīn. He asked, "What creatures did not dwell in the womb of a mother nor were born from her, yet walked upon the earth?" ʿAlī answered, "They are Adam and Eve, the staff of Moses, and the ram (Kabsh) of Ishmael."

The scholar then asked, "What creature warns the people, yet is neither of the jinn nor of humankind?" ʿAlī replied, "It is the horn of Solomon, which warned his people."

He asked further, "What is it that possesses no soul yet breathes?" ʿAlī said, "Allāh says in the Qurʾān: ⟪quran 15:29⟫, 'And the morning that breathes.'"

He asked, "Tell me—what is the lock of the seven heavens, and what is the key to it?" ʿAlī answered, "The lock is polytheism (shirk), and the key to it is the word of divine unity (Tawhīd): 'There is no god but Allāh.'"

He asked, "What is the grave that bore a body?" ʿAlī replied, "It is the fish that swallowed Hazrat Yūnus."

He asked finally, "What are the things of which every whole is composed—the whole mouth, the whole eye, the whole foot, and the whole arm?" ʿAlī answered immediately, "The all-consuming mouth is fire, the all-encompassing foot is water, the all-seeing eye is the sun, and the all-grasping arm is air."

---

A man came to ʿAlī and said, "O ʿAlī, I had a chicken that died today. When I squeezed it, an egg came out. May I eat that egg?" He replied, "No, you cannot eat that egg. But if from the egg there emerges a living chick, then it becomes lawful for you." When the man asked the reason, ʿAlī explained: "The egg is forbidden because it has emerged from the dead, whereas the chick is permissible because it comes forth alive from the dead."[^cite-3]

---

During the caliphate of ʿUmar, a man came forward with a complaint: another man had attacked him, severing part of his tongue, which made communication difficult. ʿUmar was uncertain whether compensation (diyya) should be assessed for the loss of the entire speech faculty or merely a portion. When he could not decide, he brought the case to Hazrat ʿAlī.

ʿAlī said, "From this person, count the letters of the *Muʿjam* (the alphabetic letters that cannot be pronounced from his tongue). He should be paid compensation according to the number of letters he can no longer articulate."

## The Test of Hudhayfa ibn al-Yamān

One day, the second caliph met Hudhayfa ibn al-Yamān. According to the custom of Arabia, ʿUmar greeted him, saying, "How is your morning?" Hudhayfa replied, "O Caliph, my morning has been thus: I hate the truth, I love temptation, I bear witness to what I have not seen, I remember that which was never created, I recite prayers without ablution, I possess that which is not in heaven for Allāh on earth, and I believe what the Jews and Christians say."

Hearing this, Hazrat ʿUmar became enraged and was about to strike him when Amīr al-Muʾminīn arrived. ʿAlī looked upon them with displeasure and asked, "What is the matter?" When ʿUmar recounted the entire exchange, Amīr al-Muʾminīn said, "O ʿUmar! He has not spoken words of disbelief, but rather truth:

The truth he abhors is ⟪ar:الحق⟫—the truth itself. Allāh has called the truth abhorrent because truth is bitter, and Allāh has likewise decreed death as a right (ḥaqq), which no human being willingly embraces.

The temptation he loves is his wealth and his children. Allāh says in the Qurʾān: ⟪quran 3:185⟫ "Your wealth and your children are a trial (fitnah)."

What he bears witness to—without having seen—is the Essence of Allāh, death, resurrection, Paradise, Hell, the Bridge of Sirāṭ, the Throne, and the Chair. These are matters of faith apprehended by the heart, not by the eye.

The non-creation he memorizes is the Book of Allāh, the Qurʾān—words that have always existed in divine speech and were never created.

He recites blessings and prayers upon the Prophet (peace be upon him) without ritual ablution—which is lawful in the Sharīʿa.

Those things that are neither for Allāh nor from Allāh are His family, His children, and His tribe—for these are created beings, contingent upon Allāh's will, and therefore cannot be eternal with Him.

Regarding the words of the Jews and Christians: the Qurʾān itself confirms the truth in both their testimonies. The Jews say the Christians are not upon the truth, and the Christians say the Jews are not upon the truth. This statement is true of both communities, for each has strayed from the straight path."[^cite-4]

Hearing this exposition, ʿUmar said, "Had it not been for ʿAlī, ʿUmar would surely have been destroyed."

## Animals That Bear Young or Lay Eggs

⟪ar:أبن کوا⟫ A hypocrite, pretending piety, learned that ⟪ar:امیر المومنین، مولانا علی ابن ابی طالب⟫ was accustomed to praying at the outset of the call to prayer. He resolved to pose a question at the time of the *Maghrib* call that ʿAlī would either be unable to answer or, if he attempted, would spend so long in reply that the prayer time would pass.

Therefore, as soon as ⟪ar:امیر المؤمنین⟫ the call to *Maghrib* prayer sounded, he asked, "O ʿAlī, which animals lay eggs and which animals bear living young?" ʿAlī replied, "Go! Confirm my word for a year, then return and meet with me." Saying this, he departed for prayer.

The man returned after one year and told ʿAlī, "⟪ar:یا امیر المومنین!⟫ I have investigated thoroughly, and your statement has proven absolutely true!" ʿAlī then sent him back for another year.

One year later, the man returned and again confirmed ⟪ar:امیر المومنین⟫ his findings. At that time, ʿAlī said, "Go and reflect further: animals that suckle their young also lay eggs, and those that suckle give birth to living young." The man was astonished and said, "O Lord! How much longer will you continue to be identified? You say that as long as you keep asking, I will keep answering, and with each answer I will give you a new understanding!"

## The Question of "Lāshī" (That Which Is Not)

Once, Muʿāwiya wrote to the King of Rome, "I am the Caliph of the Prophet, and therefore your tribute should come to me in Syria." The King consulted his minister and concluded that first he should test whether this man truly was the Prophet's Caliph. The King of Rome therefore sent a messenger to Muʿāwiya with the question, "What is a *lāshī*?"

Muʿāwiya was perplexed. He consulted his minister ʿAmr ibn al-ʿĀs, who said, "By Allāh! No one else will be able to answer this save ʿAlī!" Muʿāwiya asked, "O Ibn al-ʿĀs! You are exceedingly cunning. Tell me—how shall I obtain the answer from ʿAlī?" ʿAmr replied, "The remedy is this: nowadays ʿAlī is in need of horses for war. Send him a magnificent Arabian horse. When he learns its value, tell him it is worth just that. Whatever answer he gives, demand from him proof from the Qurʾān, and send that same answer to the King of Rome."

Muʿāwiya did precisely this. He sent an excellent Arabian horse to Kufa with a trusted envoy. Upon arriving, the messenger found Amīr al-Muʾminīn with his son al-Ḥasan. ʿAlī said to al-Ḥasan, "Go and ask the man if he will sell his horse." When al-Ḥasan inquired, the man replied, "No, al-Ḥasan! I will not sell this horse to you. I will sell it only to ʿAlī!" Imām al-Ḥasan smiled and brought the man before his father. When Mawlānā ʿAlī asked about the price, the man said it was priced (at a great value). ʿAlī smiled, instructed Imām al-Ḥasan to place a handful of sand in his palm, and gave it to the man. The messenger was astounded and said, "The price of such a precious horse is merely a handful of sand?"

ʿAlī replied, "Indeed! What is the proof, O ʿAlī?" And he cited the Qurʾān:

> The likeness of those who disbelieve is as the likeness of one who shouteth unto that which heareth not, save a call and cry. ⟪quran 2:171⟫ (The example of the deeds of the disbelievers is that of a mirage—that is, sand—which they think is water, but when they reach it, they find no water.)[^cite-5]

The messenger was stunned by this answer, handed over the horse to ʿAlī, and returned to Muʿāwiya. When Muʿāwiya asked, "What answer did you bring from ʿAlī?" he said, "A handful of sand, O Muʿāwiya!" Muʿāwiya was seized with consternation and said, "If I were to strike the minister in the face or strike the rich man in the face, I would say I should meet the King of Rome in person. If he strikes me, I will strike him!" Muʿāwiya sent the same reply to the King of Rome through his messenger.

When the King of Rome asked why it had taken so long to obtain an answer, the messenger was compelled to reveal the truth—that this answer came from ʿAlī. At that moment, the reality of Muʿāwiya's station was disclosed to the King of Rome. He declared, "Our tribute shall go to Kufa, not to Syria!"[^cite-6]

## The Offspring of Dog and Goat

There was a shepherd who kept dogs to guard his flock. A dog and a goat mated and produced offspring. The shepherd asked Amīr al-Muʾminīn whether this creature, born of dog and goat, was lawful or unlawful to consume. His intention was to test ʿAlī's knowledge. He presumed that ʿAlī, though the Prophet's successor, had not tended flocks as the Prophet Muḥammad had, and would therefore be unfamiliar with such beasts, given the ritual impurity associated with dogs.

Hearing the question, ʿAlī replied immediately: "When the creature drinks water, observe whether it laps the water or sips it. If it sips, it is a goat; if it laps with its tongue, it is a dog!" The man departed and returned, saying, "O ʿAlī, I saw that the creature drank water in both manners."

ʿAlī said, "Place meat and grass before it. If it eats grass, it is a goat; if it eats meat, it is a dog." The man returned and reported, "O ʿAlī! It eats both meat and grass."

ʿAlī instructed, "Listen carefully to its voice. If it bleats, it is a goat; if it raises its head and barks, it is a dog." The man replied, "O ʿAlī! I heard both sounds."

ʿAlī said, "Observe its sitting posture. If it bends its hind legs while sitting, it is a goat; if it bends its back, it is a dog." The man returned and said, "O ʿAlī! It sits in both ways."

ʿAlī then said, "Take it to the pasture and let it roam free. If it walks in the middle of the field, it is a goat; if it walks along the edge, it is a dog." The man said, "O ʿAlī! It walks both in the middle and along the edge."

When the man continued to return with objections, ʿAlī finally said to him, "Recite *Bismillāh* (In the name of Allāh) and slaughter it. If only one intestine emerges, it is the offspring of a dog; if multiple intestines appear, it is the offspring of a goat!" At this answer, the man fell at ʿAlī's feet in astonishment and shame.[^cite-7]

## Questions Concerning Allāh

In his work *Anas al-Afida al-Mubīna*, it is recorded concerning the virtues of Mawlānā ʿAlī: "The King of Rome sent a messenger to Medina bearing wealth, with instructions to present it only to Muḥammad. If the Prophet was not present, the messenger was to inquire about his successor and pose three questions. If they were answered correctly, the wealth was to be given to him." When the messenger arrived in Medina, the Prophet had already departed this life. The messenger therefore asked who the Prophet's successor was, and was told that Abū Bakr was the successor of the Messenger of Allāh.

The messenger approached Abū Bakr and posed these three questions:

| *What is that which is not for Allāh?* | *What is Allāh's Word?* | *What is Allāh's Knowledge?* |
| --- | --- | --- |
| (What is it that Allāh does not have?) | (What is it that Allāh does not possess?) | (What is it that Allāh does not know?) |

When Abū Bakr heard these questions, he became angry and said, "May Allāh destroy you! You have increased in disbelief with these questions!" The King of Rome's messenger departed from him without obtaining an answer. He then went to ʿUmar. When no response came from there either, the messenger was deeply distressed. Hazrat ʿAbdullāh ibn ʿAbbās said to both Abū Bakr and ʿUmar, "You have not dealt justly with this man. Your obligation was either to answer his questions or to say, 'We do not know.'"

Both asked ʿAbdullāh, "Do you know the answers to these questions?" He replied, "I do not know, but I do know the person who possesses the answers—my master and your master, Hazrat ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib." The three brought the Roman messenger to Hazrat ʿAlī. Upon reaching his door, they knocked. ʿAlī emerged. A pen was affixed to his ear, and his fingers were drenched in ink (having recently been engaged in writing). ʿAbdullāh ibn ʿAbbās briefly explained the purpose of their visit.

Amīr al-Muʾminīn said to the messenger, "Ask what you wish to ask, and I shall answer you." The Roman messenger repeated his questions. Amīr al-Muʾminīn said:

> The answer to *What is that which is not for Allāh?* is: Allāh has no partner. The answer to *What is Allāh's Word?* is: There is no injustice in the sight of Allāh. The answer to *What is Allāh's Knowledge?* is: Allāh does not know that Jesus Christ is His son.

Upon hearing these answers, the Roman messenger, bearing witness to the word of monotheism, declared, "I bear witness that there is no god but Allāh, and that Muḥammad is the Messenger of Allāh, and that the will of Allāh is supreme." He then presented the wealth to ʿAlī. Amīr al-Muʾminīn gave it to his two sons, al-Ḥasan and al-Ḥusayn, saying, "Take this wealth and distribute it among the Muslims." This account signifies not only your knowledge, but also your intuition and spiritual perception.[^cite-8]

## Questions from Muʿāwiya

Abū Jārūd and al-Ḥārith al-Aʿwar have narrated: One day, ʿAlī was seated at *Maqām Ḥabrā* (the Station of Ḥabrā). People would approach with various questions, receive satisfactory answers, and depart. Some came merely to dispute; yet they too would find their answers. Suddenly, a man arose and exclaimed loudly, "As-salāmu ʿalayka, O Amīr al-Muʾminīn, wa raḥmatu-llāhi wa barakātuhu" (Peace be upon you, O Commander of the Faithful, and the mercy and blessing of Allāh). Amīr al-Muʾminīn turned to him, examined him carefully, and replied, "Wa ʿalayka as-salām, wa raḥmatu-llāhi wa barakātuhu" (And upon you be peace, and the mercy and blessing of Allāh).

He asked, "Who are you?" The man replied, "I am one of your people and a resident of this city." ʿAlī said, "That is a lie! Had you met me even once or greeted me, I would have recognized you." He then addressed the gathering: "Does anyone here recognize this man?" All refused. He said, "Look—they are all inhabitants of this land, yet none knows you. My claim stands: had I met you even once, I would have recognized you." The man became frightened and said, "O Amīr al-Muʾminīn, grant me your pardon."

ʿAlī asked, "Have you come to my city and murdered someone, or been guilty of theft?" The man did not answer. ʿAlī continued, "Perhaps you killed someone during the war?" He replied, "Yes." ʿAlī said, "Well, the war is over and weapons have been laid down—so let that matter be closed." The man then disclosed: "Muʿāwiya has sent me to you. He has commanded me to obtain answers to questions that Ibn al-Asghar has posed to him, without revealing this to you. He said, 'Muʿāwiya, if you claim the caliphate of Muḥammad, answer these questions of mine. If you answer correctly, I will obey you and pay you the *jizya* (poll tax).' ## Three Questions from ʿUmar

One day, the second caliph came to Imām ʿAlī and said, "O Abū al-Ḥasan, I wished to ask the Messenger of Allāh (peace be upon him) certain matters. Can you provide their answers?" Mullā ʿAlī replied, "Ask." ʿUmar posed these questions:

| 1 | What is the reason that a person sees very good things and very interesting things in dreams, yet when he opens his eyes, nothing comes to pass? |
| --- | --- |
| 2 | What is the reason that when a person meets a stranger at their very first meeting, without any apparent cause, he either immediately falls in love with him or hates him? |
| 3 | What is the reason that a person hears or sees something, and though he often mentions it, he forgets it when he needs it most? And then, when it is not needed, it comes to mind. |

Amīr al-Muʾminīn said:

**(1)** The reason why dreams are true and false is that Allāh causes people to die while they sleep. Whoever does not die in his sleep—Allāh restrains his soul (upon which a decree has been made) and sends it forth in exchange for another appointed term. A sleeping person bears some resemblance to one who is dead. Therefore, the dreams he sees at the time of the soul's dissolution are divine and true, whereas the dreams he sees after the soul's return are false and whispers from the devil.[^cite-9]

**(2)** The reason why a person loves or hates a stranger at the very first meeting is that Allāh created the souls two thousand years before the bodies and caused them to dwell in the air. Those who will love one another in this world loved one another there, and those who will be enemies here were enemies there.

**(3)** The reason for untimely forgetting and remembering is that upon every human heart there is a black spot, like the black spot upon the moon. When that spot disturbs the heart, a person forgets what he has seen and heard. When that spot is removed, all things are remembered.[^cite-10]

## The Ten Things, Each Harder Than the Other

Mawlānā ʿAlī was asked, "O ʿAlī! What are the ten things that are harder than one another?" He said:

| 1 | **Stone** | Stone is the hardest thing that Allāh has created. |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 2 | **Iron** | Iron is harder than stone—it is struck by stone and breaks. |
| 3 | **Fire** | Fire is harder than iron—it melts the iron. |
| 4 | **Water** | Water is harder than fire—it extinguishes fire. |
| 5 | **Clouds** | Clouds are harder than water—they hold water and rain it down. |
| 6 | **Wind** | Wind is harder than clouds—it scatters clouds. |
| 7 | **The Angel Who Drives the Wind** (*Mālik al-Rīḥ*) | The angel who drives the wind is harder than the wind itself. |
| 8 | **The Angel of Death** (*Mālik al-Mawt*) | The Angel of Death is harder than the angel of the wind. |
| 9 | **Death** | Death is harder than the Angel of Death—it is certain and inevitable. |
| 10 | **The Command of the Lord of the Worlds** | The Command of the Lord of the Worlds is harder than death—and I am born of it![^cite-11] |

## The Division of the Affairs of the World

There came a time when a Jewish priest, Jaflīq, approached the Messenger of Allāh at his pulpit and asked, "O Messenger of Allāh, if you are the true Prophet, tell me into how many sections the affairs of the world have been divided, so that I may be convinced you are the True Successor." The Prophet said, "Hear! All the affairs of the world have been divided into twenty-five sections as follows:

- Five from Fate and Destiny (*Qaḍāʾ wa-Qadar*)
- Five from Effort and Striving (*Ijtihād*)
- Five from Heredity (*Wirātha*)
- Five from Habit (*ʿĀda*)
- Five from the Essence of the Self (*Dhāt al-Nafs*)

Jaflīq said, "O Lord, tell me more and increase my knowledge!" The Master of the Universe said, "Listen! This knowledge is of such weight that if it were placed upon a mountain, that mountain would be shattered beneath its burden. Yet only a Prophet and an Imām, after attaining the stage of faith, can comprehend this knowledge."[^cite-12]

**Fate and Destiny:**

Remember the five matters placed within Fate and Destiny—matters neither in your hands nor in mine: (1) the choice of your wife, (2) the begetting of children, (3) the acquisition of wealth and property, (4) the attainment of country and dominion, and (5) the span of life, which you may increase through your virtues or diminish through your sins.

**Effort and Striving:**

The five things that depend upon striving and exertion are: (1) the desire for Paradise and its attainment through knowledge and deed, (2) aversion from Hell and withdrawal from its causes through one's deeds, (3) the learning of jurisprudence (knowledge of the Sharīʿa) and the cultivation of refinement through one's own efforts, (4) the mastery of a craft or skill, and (5) proficiency in writing, which a person may definitely achieve through struggle.

**Heredity:**

The five things that are inherited are: (1) piety and godliness, which you inherit from your parents, (2) *zakat* and discernment—fineness and keenness of sight, which come from the parents, (3) beauty, glory, and radiance, which do not proceed from the person's own nature but from the parents, (4) respect and dignity, which come from the parents, and (5) the last thing inherited as legacy: refined speech.

**Habit:**

The five things acquired through habit are: (1) the manner of eating and drinking—a person may alter his way of eating through daily repetition, (2) the coming and going of sleep depend upon habit, (3) walking and running in the sun is also formed by habit, (4) the act of marriage too follows the desire and habit of the person, and (5) the last thing that depends upon human habit is *tahāra* (ritual purification)—the removal of impurities from the body.

Jaflīq said, "O Master, you have told me five things regarding Qaḍāʾ wa-Qadar, five regarding effort and ijtihād, five regarding inheritance, five regarding habit. Now tell me the last five things that depend upon the Essence of the Self." He said, "Hear, and be grateful for what I grant you, and worship your Lord until certainty comes to you."

**Essence of the Self:**

He said, "The five qualities that are praiseworthy—which are not based on judgment or valuation, which are not born in us through ijtihād, nor are they inherited or acquired through habit, but which depend upon our repentance and eternal faith, upon the point of belief born in the spiritual world—these Allāh bestows upon us as five things: (1) First is patience and submission, bowing oneself in prostration, yielding oneself to martyrdom. (2) Trustworthiness—being merciful to the elders and the young, drawing near to relatives, being kind to youth, and respecting the elders. (3) Third is *salāḥ* (reform)—brotherhood among believers themselves, for a believer outweighs millions of disbelievers. (4) Fourth is *karām* (generosity). (5) And the last is *ṣidq* (truth and truthfulness)—and truth is to magnify Allāh, to bow one's head for the sake of Allāh, and to speak words from one's tongue for His sake alone."

*From the Sessions of Mullā Ghulām Ḥusayn ibn Hazrat Naṣrullāh*
