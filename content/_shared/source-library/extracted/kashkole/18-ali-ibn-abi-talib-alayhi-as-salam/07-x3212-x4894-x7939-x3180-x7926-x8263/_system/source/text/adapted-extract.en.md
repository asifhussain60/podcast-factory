## The Three Degrees of Certainty

Certainty (yaqīn) in Islamic epistemology manifests across three ascending degrees, each representing a deeper penetration into the reality of faith and divine knowledge. The first and most elementary is ʿilm al-yaqīn (the knowledge of certainty), wherein one acquires intellectual knowledge of something and assents to it through cognitive conviction alone. Beyond this lies ʿayn al-yaqīn (the eye of certainty), which arises when, having gained knowledge, one witnesses the reality of that knowledge directly through immediate perception and sensory verification. The highest and most perfected state is ḥaqq al-yaqīn (the truth of certainty)—a condition wherein one becomes so wholly absorbed in all levels of faith, through absolute sincerity, steadfast determination, and unwavering perseverance, that one's entire being is transmuted into the living substance of certainty itself.

The Qurʾan provides an luminous illustration of these three degrees through the narrative of ⟪ar:حضرت ابراھیم علیہ السلام⟫ (the Prophet Abraham, peace be upon him) in ⟪ar:سورۃ البقرۃ⟫ (the Chapter of the Cow). There, Abraham appeals to the Most High:

> ⟪quran 2:260⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَإِذْ قَالَ إِبْرَهِیمُ رَبِّ أَرِنِى كَيْفَ تُحْىِ ٱلْمَوْتَىٰ ۖ قَالَ أَوَلَمْ تُؤْمِن ۖ قَالَ بَلَىٰ وَلَكِن لِّيَطْمَئِنَّ قَلْبِى ۖ قَالَ فَخُذْ أَرْبَعَةًۭ مِّنَ ٱلطَّيْرِ فَصُرْهُنَّ إِلَيْكَ ثُمَّ ٱجْعَلْ عَلَىٰ كُلِّ جَبَلٍۢ مِّنْهُنَّ جُزْءًۭا ثُمَّ ٱدْعُهُنَّ يَأْتِينَكَ سَعْيًۭا ۚ وَٱعْلَمْ أَنَّ ٱللَّهَ عَزِيزٌ حَكِيمٌۭ ‎⟫
> *And (remember) when Ibrahim (Abraham) said, "My Lord! Show me how You give life to the dead." He (Allah) said: "Do you not believe?" He [Ibrahim (Abraham)] said: "Yes (I believe), but to be stronger in Faith." He said: "Take four birds, then cause them to incline towards you (then slaughter them, cut them into pieces), and then put a portion of them on every hill, and call them, they will come to you in haste. And know that Allah is All-Mighty, All-Wise."*

Abraham entreated his Lord: "Show me, O my Lord, how Thou givest life unto the dead." The Lord replied: "O Abraham, dost thou not already believe?" Abraham answered: "Yea, I possess ⟪ar:علم الیقین⟫ (knowledge of certainty), yet I desire ⟪ar:عین الیقین⟫ (the eye of certainty)—the peace and reassurance of my heart." Thereupon Allah commanded him: "Take four birds; slaughter them, dismember their parts, and mingle them so thoroughly that none can distinguish which portion belongs to which bird. Then scatter them upon separate mountains and call unto them—they shall come running to thee in haste." Abraham executed this command with perfect obedience, and the birds were restored to life before his eyes. Thus did he attain ⟪ar:'عین الیقین'⟫ (the eye of certainty). Yet even beyond this vivid witnessing, Abraham ascended to the highest station: he attained ⟪ar:'حقُّ الیقین'⟫ (the truth of certainty), becoming so utterly unified with the reality of divine power that his entire existence became a mirror of God's absolute knowledge and will.[^cite-1] It is by virtue of this consummation of certainty that ⟪ar:علی ابن ابی طالب علیہ السلام⟫ (ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib, peace be upon him) surpassed countless prophets in rank and spiritual station.

The higher one ascends upon this ladder of certainty, the nearer one draws to the very source of divine knowledge itself. Consider the testimony preserved in the Gospel regarding Jesus: he walked upon the waters of the sea. Yet the Messenger of God, Muhammad (may the blessings and peace of God be upon him), declared: "Had Jesus possessed a yet greater measure of perfected certainty, he would have walked upon the air itself."[^cite-2] From this saying, one may fathom—if human intellect is equal to such comprehension—the incomparable station of the Master of the Universe, ⟪ar:علی ابن ابی طالب علیہ السلام⟫. The faculties of human understanding are too narrow, too constrained, to encompass the fullness of ʿAlī's teachings and his spiritual realities within the bounds of ordinary thought.

When one asks the Commander of the Faithful, "O Mawlā, what measure of certainty hast thou attained?" he responds with words that shatter all bounds of human comprehension: "I have ascended so far into the knowledge of God that ⟪ar-quote:لَوکُشِفَ الغِطاَءُ ماَ اَزدَتُّ یَقِیناَ⟫ (even if all the veils and curtains of existence were stripped away from before my eyes, my certainty would not increase even by a single degree)."[^cite-3] His state transcends the distinction between knowing and seeing; knowledge and reality have become one.

## The Prayer for the Living Man

Word of ʿAlī's claim spread among certain people, and some conspired to discredit him. They resolved to test whether his certainty was truly absolute, or whether it might falter when confronted with apparent contradiction. To this end, they placed a living man in the courtyard of the mosque, concealing his life and presenting him as a corpse. Then they approached ʿAlī and requested that he lead the funeral prayer over this supposed deceased.

Without hesitation, the Commander of the Faithful proceeded to perform the funeral prayer with full rites and invocations. When he had completed the salutation of peace, those who had conspired rose up and challenged him: "O ʿAlī! Thou claimest that thy certainty is absolute—that even were the veil removed, thy faith would not increase. Yet this man before us is not dead but living. How couldst thou lead his funeral prayer? Dost thy claims of certainty not ring hollow when thy vision fails to discern that this person yet draws breath?"

ʿAlī responded calmly: "If thou sayest he is alive, then show me proof of his life." They removed the shroud—and the man was found to be dead. In that instant, the conspiracy dissolved, and the truth of ʿAlī's words stood revealed.

*—As transmitted by Mullā Ansar Ahmad ibn Sayyid Amīr al-Dīn*

## The Swift Justice of ʿAlī

On one occasion, the Commander of the Faithful resolved a question of extraordinary complexity—a matter that had confounded ʿUmar ibn al-Khaṭṭāb and remained beyond the reach of his judgment. Witnessing this manifestation of ʿAlī's knowledge and penetrating wisdom, ʿUmar grew deeply troubled. He approached him and said: "O Abū al-Ḥasan! Dost thou not move too hastily in thy judgments? Might not error sometimes overtake thee?"

The Commander of the Faithful turned to ʿUmar and asked: "O ʿUmar, how many fingers dost thou possess upon one hand?"

Without pause, ʿUmar replied: "Five fingers."

ʿAlī asked: "How didst thou answer so swiftly? Didst thou require time for reflection?"

ʿUmar answered: "Is there need to deliberate upon such a thing? The five fingers stand perpetually before my eyes. When they are visible to me at every moment, why should I delay in my response?"

Then the Commander of the Faithful spoke: "Thus it is with me, O ʿUmar. The Qurʾān, all its rulings and injunctions, and the entirety of the manifest and hidden knowledge of this cosmos stand eternally before my eyes, as clearly as thy five fingers stand before thine. Therefore do I judge swiftly and without hesitation." He then added with finality: "⟪ar-quote:لَوکُشِفَ الغِطاَءُ ماَ اَزدَتُّ یَقِیناَ⟫ Even were the veil to be lifted, my certainty would increase not in the slightest."

*—Transmitted from the Sessions of Ḥaḍrat al-Manṣūr ʿAbdullāh ibn Hibatullāh*

ʿUmar ibn al-Khaṭṭāb himself once testified with profound recognition: "Verily, God hath divided all knowledge of the universe into six portions. Of these six, only ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib hath received the whole—he alone, with none beside him as his equal. The remainder of mankind shares in only one of these six portions; yet even within that single sixth, there dwell more learned ones than all the peoples of the earth combined. Exalted is God! I am he who speaks this truth, though I grieve at my own recognition of it."

*—Transmitted from the Sessions of Ḥaḍrat al-Manṣūr ʿAbdullāh ibn Hibatullāh*

## The Parable of the Pigeon and the Dove

One day, as ʿAmmar Yāsir sat reciting in the mosque of Kūfa, Mawlānā ʿAlī gazed upward toward the dome and spoke aloud with tenderness: "Sadqiyya, sadqiyya—O Truthful One, be true to thy beloved." ʿAmmar, observing no person present to whom these words might be addressed, returned to his recitation, thinking the Commander had spoken to himself. A second time, ʿAlī repeated the words with the same gentle insistence. When he spoke them a third time, ʿAmmar could contain his curiosity no longer and asked: "O Lord of the Universe, to whom dost thou speak?"

ʿAlī replied: "Upon the dome of this mosque sit a pigeon and a dove. The dove has lost his way homeward, and by reason of his delay, the pigeon grows suspicious, believing him unfaithful and gone astray to another. The dove protests his innocence, saying only that the path eludes him—yet the pigeon will not listen. In his anguish, the dove hath sworn by the arch of heaven, which is under my dominion. I have therefore spoken thrice: the first two times, his heart was closed; but now, at the third calling, he perceives the truth, and together they take flight."

When ʿAmmar marveled at this knowledge, he asked: "O Master, dost thou truly comprehend the speech of birds?"

ʿAlī answered with profound simplicity: "The Prophet Solomon was my student, and I myself taught him the languages of all creatures—jinn and beast, bird and dragon. If a student is granted such power that he can understand and speak with all these forms of life, how can it be difficult for his teacher? What wonder is there in this?"[^cite-4]

## Gabriel the Archangel as Student

One day, the Archangel Gabriel descended to visit the Prophet Muḥammad (may the peace of God be upon him), and the Commander of the Faithful ʿAlī was present in that gathering. When Gabriel beheld ʿAlī, he smiled in recognition and offered his salutation with evident reverence. The Prophet observed this and asked: "O Gabriel, dost thou know this man?"

Gabriel replied with unmistakable clarity: "He is my teacher."

When the Messenger of God inquired into the particulars of this relationship, Gabriel disclosed a mystery veiled from common knowledge: "O Messenger of God! When the Lord created me, He addressed me with a question whose depth I could not fathom. He asked me: 'Who art thou, and what is thy name? Who am I, and what is My name?' In that moment of bewilderment, I possessed no answer that could satisfy the divine inquiry. It was then that this righteous servant—who had existed in the realm of lights (*ʿālam al-anwār*) before my own creation—came to me and instructed me, saying: 'Answer thy Lord thus:

Thou art the Lord of Majesty, and Thy name is the Majestic One. I am Thy servant, the lowly one, and my name is Gabriel.'"

Thus did Gabriel receive his very name—his essential identity—from ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib. And it is a truth universally acknowledged that the bestower of names holds dominion over the named, for the name is the very essence of being, bestowed by one who possesses knowledge of all things.

## Where Is Gabriel Now?

When the Muslims finally granted the caliphate to ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib—after the Prophet's death and the succession of others—he ascended the pulpit of Kūfa and spoke words of gratitude and joy: "Praise be to God, the Sustainer of all worlds. I am grateful to the Almighty that truth has reached its rightful place at last."

In that sermon, the Commander of the Faithful proclaimed: "Ask me what you desire before I am taken from you. Ask me concerning the mysteries of heaven, for I know the pathways of the heavens better than I know the earth itself!"

A man rose from the assembly and posed a question directly: "O Commander of the Faithful, where is Gabriel (*Jibrīl*) at this present moment?"

ʿAlī paused, raised his gaze to the heavens, then lowered it to the earth. He turned his sight toward the east, then toward the west. Having surveyed all directions, he addressed the questioner with piercing clarity: "Thou art Gabriel."

At ʿAlī's command, the man rose from among the gathered people. As he rose, his human form dissolved; his wings burst forth and shattered the roof of the mosque, and Gabriel ascended into the celestial realm, leaving the people astounded, crying out takbīr—"Allāhu Akbar, God is Most Great!"

The assembly then asked the Commander of the Faithful: "O Master, how didst thou know that this was Gabriel?"

He replied with sublime authority: "When I raised my gaze toward the heaven, my sight penetrated through all the veils of the upper throne and beyond. When I directed my sight earthward, my vision tore through all the layers of the earth itself. And when I gazed upon the east and the west, I scanned every corner and place. Since nowhere in all creation could I perceive Gabriel, I understood that he must be present in hidden form among those gathered before me."[^cite-5]

## The Wall of Gold

On one occasion, ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib came to the House of Revelation—the dwelling of the Prophet and his daughter Fāṭima al-Zahrāʾ—and asked his wife: "O Daughter of the Messenger of God, dost thou have food within this house?"

Fāṭima responded with tender sorrow: "What shall I bring to thee, O Abū al-Ḥasan? The condition of our household is such that I have been lulling Ḥasan and Ḥusayn to sleep for two days now with nothing but water to fill their bellies, and they sleep hungry upon the cold ground."

With a heart full of compassion, ʿAlī asked Sayyida Fāṭima for her patched shawl—her only garment of value. Taking it in hand, he went to a Jewish merchant and offered it as collateral: "O Jew, accept this as a pledge and give me something in return."

When ʿAlī returned with provisions, the Jew called out to him: "Wait, O Abū al-Ḥasan! I have a question for thee. Thy brother Muḥammad claims to be the Messenger of God and the leader of all the prophets. Why does he not pray to the Almighty to relieve thee from this poverty and grant thee wealth through supplication?"

ʿAlī paused and bowed his head in silence for a moment. Then, with a gesture of his blessed finger, he struck the sandy wall beside him and spoke: "O Jew, I swear by God that I am the brother of the Messenger of God, and he is the Messenger of God. Now hear what I say: if the servant of God swears by God that this wall shall become gold, then it shall become gold!"

The words had scarcely left his lips when the wall of sand transformed before all eyes into shimmering gold. Then ʿAlī commanded: "Return to thy original state." And in an instant, the golden wall reverted to sand.

The wonder of this deed lay not in prayer, not in supplication, not even in explicit command—but in the mere utterance, the spontaneous manifestation of divine will through his words. Such is the station of one whom the Creator has clothed with authority over all creation. ʿAlī explained to the astonished Jew: "The example I have shown thee is but an illustration. I spoke, and the wall obeyed the utterance of my tongue. Know, O Jew, that it is not our practice to accumulate worldly goods. Our wealth lies in the knowledge of the Unseen and dominion over creation itself. This is why we concern ourselves not with earthly riches." It is upon such wonders of ʿAlī's power and his station of absolute certainty that we declare: ⟪ar-quote:لَوکُشِفَ الغِطاَءُ ماَ اَزدَتُّ یَقِیناَ⟫ Even if all the veils were lifted, his certainty would not increase in the slightest.[^cite-6]

## The Summons of Muʿāwiya to ʿAlī's Court

Sayyidnā Ḥātim al-ʿAṣamī has recorded in his book an incident of profound significance. In the court of the Commander of the Faithful, there arrived a Bedouin of Egypt named ʿUbayd. Two men came before ʿAlī, locked in bitter dispute. ʿAlī heard both and rendered judgment: to one he said, "Thou art in the right"; to the other he said, "Thou art in the wrong." The man against whom judgment had been rendered cried out in protest: "O ʿAlī, thou hast wronged me!"

ʿAlī replied with measured calm: "I have judged according to the commandments of God and the verses of the Qurʾān."

Yet the man persisted, repeating his accusation: "Nay, O ʿAlī, thou hast wronged me!"

The Commander of the Faithful grew stern and warned him: "If thou utterest that phrase once more—'Thou hast wronged me'—I swear by God, the Almighty shall transform thee into a dog."

But the man, blinded by rage or folly, repeated the very words. Before they had fully left his mouth, his form shifted and contorted; he became a black dog, howling and confused.

ʿUbayd recounts: "I trembled when I witnessed this transformation. My heart shook within my breast." The companion of the man who had been transformed approached ʿAlī with profound humility and entreaty: "O Master of the Universe! Thou art the Most Merciful of the Merciful. I beseech thee, restore this wretched soul to his human form, for surely the mercy of God encompasses all things."

When ʿAlī perceived the sincerity of this plea, he consented: "Very well, I shall restore him. But first, he must understand that he must accept my judgment and refrain from speaking against it." Then the Commander of the Faithful raised his hands in supplication and spoke: "O God, I pray to Thee by the truth of Muḥammad, Thy Messenger, by the truth of Moses and Jesus, the Spirit of Thy breath, and by the truth of all the Prophets whom Thou hast sent—restore this man to his original state."

As the prayer concluded, the dog's form dissolved, and the man stood once more in human shape, humbled and transformed. He fell at ʿAlī's feet crying: "O ʿAlī, I bear witness that there is no god but God, and I testify that thou art the true guardian (*walī*) of God. I repent before thee and surrender myself to thy authority!" Both men departed in peace.

ʿUbayd then turned to the Commander of the Faithful and spoke with wonder: "O Lord, thy miracle is magnificent beyond measure, yet I am astonished by one thing—why didst thou remain silent regarding Muʿāwiya, who rebels against thee and gathers armies?"

ʿAlī replied with prophetic wisdom: "O ʿUbayd, that which hath been decreed in the tablets of fate shall come to pass, no matter how grievously the flesh may wish otherwise. But if thou desirest to witness an even greater marvel, then behold what God shall do!" He raised his hands heavenward, and his lips moved in silent prayer. Then he commanded: "O Muʿāwiya, come before me!"

ʿUbayd recounts in awe: "Before my eyes, I saw Muʿāwiya lifted by the wind itself from Damascus and carried through the air to Kūfa, arriving before ʿAlī in his court. His knees buckled beneath him, his eyes cast downward, his tongue quivering with fear."

The Commander of the Faithful addressed him with stern authority: "O thou who rebellest against God and His saints! Hast thou no fear of the Divine Power? Know that I am the executor of God's command and the upholder of His law!" He warned Muʿāwiya with words that pierced through to the soul, then spoke with finality: "Return to thy place. There remains yet a term appointed for thee that must be fulfilled." In that instant, Muʿāwiya vanished, swept away by the same unseen force that had brought him.

When Muʿāwiya found himself suddenly transported back to Damascus, he was sitting upon his throne, his mind occupied with plans of war against ʿAlī. All those in his court were bewildered, crying out: "O Muʿāwiya, where didst thou go? What hath befallen thee?" When Muʿāwiya recounted the entire incident to them, they counseled him in fear: "Keep this matter hidden; reveal it to none, lest our enemies learn of it and our shame become known throughout the world."

ʿAlī then turned to ʿUbayd and asked: "O ʿUbayd, hast thou read in the Qurʾān the story of Solomon (*Sulaymān*)?" The Commander of the Faithful recited: "When Solomon gathered the jinn and the humans, he asked them all, 'Which of you shall bring me the throne of Bilqīs before she and her armies arrive?' One of the ʿifrit jinn replied, 'I shall bring it to thee before thou rise from thy place—for I have strength and trustworthiness.' But then one who possessed knowledge of the Book said, 'I shall bring it to thee before thy gaze returns to thee'—and he brought the throne in the twinkling of an eye."

ʿAlī continued with profound authority: "That ʿifrit possessed only a small measure of power, and the one with knowledge of the Book possessed a portion thereof—yet with these fragments, they accomplished such deeds. And as for me, O ʿUbayd, I have been given the complete knowledge of the Book (*al-kitāb*). I am God's command, God's wisdom, God's cause, and God's light in the heavens and the earth. What difficulty can there be for me in such matters?"[^cite-7]

In that moment, ʿUbayd fell in prostration before ʿAlī and cried out: "I testify to thy guardianship, O Guardian of God. Thou art indeed capable of all things, and thou art the Truthful One."

## The Dead Man's Testimony

In the *Kitāb al-Muʿajizāt al-Kulliyya* (The Book of Universal Miracles), Maythim al-Sammār relates an extraordinary account of the Commander of the Faithful's knowledge and authority. One day, as Maythim sat with ʿAlī in the mosque of Kūfa, a tall man entered and called out: "Which among you is the just one? Which is the judge? Who is ʿAlī?"

The Commander of the Faithful raised his head and said with quiet certainty: "I am he who possesses the knowledge that belonged to the prophets. I am ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib. What dost thou seek to say?"

The man replied: "I have come on behalf of seven thousand people. A man among us hath been slain, and the people are divided in doubt about who committed this deed. Thou art God's proof upon the earth; therefore, tell us truly—who killed this man?"

ʿAlī commanded Maythim: "Go forth and announce that the brother of Muḥammad, upon whom be peace, hath been granted complete knowledge. Let all who wish to witness this knowledge gather in Najaf." Soon the place was thronged with multitudes of people.

The body of the slain man was brought before ʿAlī. Drawing near, he instructed Maythim to uncover the face of the corpse. The dead man was a young man named Madrik ibn ʿAnzala ibn Salāma, and forty days had passed since his murder. Fifty men had been arrested on suspicion of the crime. ʿAlī pronounced with absolute clarity: "His killer is his own uncle, who is also his father-in-law. This young man married without the uncle's permission, and in his rage, the uncle slew him."

Yet the gathered people cried out in doubt: "How can we accept this judgment? We will not believe until the dead man himself arises and testifies!" At that moment, the Commander of the Faithful smiled a smile of radiance. He gazed upon the corpse and spoke words of power: "Rise up, O Madrik ibn ʿAnzala ibn Salāma, by the command of God and His proof upon earth!"

In that instant, the dead man arose and sat upright. His eyes opened; his lips parted. He cried out in a voice resonant with life: "Here am I, here am I, O proof of God upon the earth!"

The Arabs who stood witnessing this miracle asked the resurrected youth: "Who killed thee?"

He answered clearly: "My uncle killed me."

All doubt was erased. When ʿAlī departed that place, the young man clung to his feet in desperate supplication: "O Master, do not deliver me back to them, for they will slay me again!" And ʿAlī said: "May the curse of God fall upon all who would stand between thee and thy truth." He took Madrik with him, and the young man remained in ʿAlī's protection, serving faithfully until he was martyred at the Battle of Ṣiffīn, fighting alongside the Commander of the Faithful.[^cite-8]

## The Return of the Prophet in the Veils of Light

When the Commander of the Faithful rebuked the first caliph for seizing a station to which he was not equal, saying, "Thou hast taken upon thyself a burden that thou canst not bear—how wilt thou answer for this on the Day of Judgment?"—Abū Bakr responded by denying that the Prophet had appointed ʿAlī as his successor.

ʿAlī asked him: "If the Messenger of God were to come now, in the presence of all, and testify that he appointed me as successor and caliph, wouldst thou then surrender the caliphate to me?"

Abū Bakr replied: "How can the Messenger of God come again? He hath already passed from this world."

Then the Commander of the Faithful emptied the mosque of all but a chosen few, closed the doors, and stood in the courtyard to pray. He raised his hands to heaven and called out: "O my Lord, have mercy upon me. Send to me my brother Muḥammad in the veils of light, for Thou hast power over all things."

As ʿAlī's prayer drew to completion, the heavens opened. The Messenger of God descended, surrounded by the luminous angels, and the takbīr—"Allāhu Akbar, God is Most Great"—echoed throughout. ʿAlī cautioned the witnesses not to advance, and he and the Prophet engaged in profound discourse.

Then the Prophet turned to Abū Bakr and said with sternness: "O cursed one! Woe unto thee! What presumption hath led thee to occupy the station of ʿAlī? This is the place appointed for him, the station of incomparable honor!" The Prophet then ascended back into the veils of light, accompanied by the angels.

In that moment, ʿAlī was overcome with longing and weeping. He spoke from the depths of his heart: "How greatly do I yearn to accompany thee to that exalted realm and sublime dwelling-place!"[^cite-9]

## The Battle of the Covenant

There was an elderly companion of the Prophet named Zamārī who witnessed the events at the Battle of the Covenant (*Ghazwat al-ʿAhd*) alongside the Prophet Muḥammad and ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib. A fierce engagement unfolded upon the battlefield, and at one point, Zamārī observed both the Prophet and ʿAlī locked in combat with the enemy, fighting with supreme valor. Later, when Zamārī withdrew from the field to rest in a tent, he looked within and saw the Prophet and ʿAlī sitting together in calm discourse, conversing with one another. His expectations were overturned—how could they be at rest when moments before he had seen them upon the battlefield?

When he returned to the fighting, he once again beheld both the Prophet and ʿAlī engaged in combat upon the field. Thus did Zamārī witness that the Commander of the Faithful possessed the capacity to be in multiple states simultaneously, manifesting in different forms according to the needs of the moment and the will of the Creator.

## The Hole in Noah's Ark

There came a day when a man with only one arm sat in the presence of the Messenger of God (may the peace and blessings of God be upon him), conversing with him. Suddenly, when the Commander of the Faithful, ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib, entered the gathering, a remarkable transformation occurred. The man's form shifted and changed; his body became as a bird, and he flew upward to perch upon a branch, trembling with visible fear. He was, in truth, a jinn who had come to the Prophet in human disguise.

The Messenger of God asked him: "What hath caused thee to flee in this manner? Why dost thou tremble so?"

The jinn replied: "O Messenger of God, I am seized with terrible fear of this one!"

The Prophet asked: "What is the reason for thy dread?"

The jinn answered: "O Messenger of God, I was among the peoples during the time of Noah, upon him be peace. When Noah embarked upon the Ark with his faithful companions, I began to bore holes into its hull, intending to sink the vessel and drown them all. But then this man came upon me and struck my arm with his sword with such force that it was severed from my body. From that day to this, I have been gripped with fear whenever I perceive his presence." [^cite-10]

Then the Messenger of God spoke a word of comfort to the jinn, assuring him of safe passage. The jinn resumed his human form and remained in the gathering. This incident reveals a profound truth: there was never a moment in the history of creation when the Essence of the Universe did not exist, ready to aid all the prophets in their tribulations and to stand against the forces of evil that arrayed themselves against God's truth.

## The Well of Knowledge

Once, the Commander of the Faithful was returning from the battle of Banū Muṣṭaliq in the company of the Messenger of God and three companions. The Prophet sent them ahead to fill the water-skins (*mushkizāt*) at a well. When they arrived, the villagers warned them: "There is a well wherein the jinn dwell, and they wreak great disturbance and harm upon us. None who venture near it return unscathed."

When the Prophet heard of this peril, he called to his companions and asked: "Who among you will go and do battle with these jinn? Whosoever fights them, I guarantee him Paradise!" Two valiant Muslims stepped forward, eager to face whatever lay within. The Messenger of God said to them: "Know that ye shall not retreat from the fury that awaits you." They replied with steadfast hearts: "O Messenger of God, thy guarantee of Paradise is sufficient for us."

Both men descended into the well and were martyred there. Then three companions and forty others arrived at the well, and a sight of terrible majesty appeared before them: the trees surrounding the well were engulfed in flames so fierce they seemed to speak to the sky itself. The flames transformed and took shape; countless monstrous heads erupted from each burning tree, and from these heads poured forth screams and howls of such terrifying power that all the people fled in panic, returning to the Messenger of God.

The Messenger of God then sent the Master of the Universe. ʿAlī departed with other companions, and when he arrived at the well, he beheld the same dreadful spectacle. The companions grew paralyzed with fear and would not advance further, despite ʿAlī's encouragement and reassurance. Then the Commander of the Faithful drew forth Dhū al-Fiqār, his legendary sword, and leaped into the depths of the well.

From below echoed screams beyond number and sounds of terrible conflict. When ʿAlī emerged, his garments were soaked with blood, and drops fell from the blade of his sword. The Messenger of God asked him: "O ʿAlī, how didst thou make war upon them?"

The Commander of the Faithful replied: "I fought them in three ways:

| 1 | I slew them by piercing and splitting their forms asunder. |
| --- | --- |
| 2 | I read unto them the verses of the Qurʾān, and by the power of God's word, they were destroyed. |
| 3 | I clothed them in human form and struck them down with this sword. |

This deed was accomplished as an intrinsic part of his nature and station. No other Imām could have performed such a deed, for no other possessed the knowledge and power required. ʿAlī undertook four great battles of this kind—encounters with jinn and forces of evil—and this was one of them. The Prophet was asked: "Did ʿAlī slay all of them?"

He replied: "Nay, not all. There remain jinn yet to be reckoned with, and they shall be slain by the sword of al-Qāʾim at the end of time. But between ʿAlī and al-Qāʾim, there is no other Imām who possesses the capacity to fight these jinn, for they are a company of the most wicked and corrupted among the jinn-kind."[^cite-11]

*(From the Lessons on the Night of Hijra, 2005, by Mullā Ghulām Ḥusayn ibn Ḥaḍrat Nasrullāh)*

## The Horseman of Ḥijāz

There came a day when the Commander of the Faithful sat upon the roof of his dwelling, eating dates, at an age when he was only twenty-seven years old. In the courtyard below, Salmān al-Fārisī sat mending his garment with needle and thread. ʿAlī playfully tossed a bundle of dates down to him. Salmān, sensing something in the gesture, called out: "O ʿAlī! I am an old man, and thou art yet young. Why dost thou mock an elder?"

ʿAlī replied with a gentle smile: "O Salmān, do not measure by years alone. Let me be the child, and allow thyself to be the elder. But hast thou forgotten the story of ʿArzān in the wilderness? Dost thou not recall when a rider came and saved thee from the lion?"

Salmān was astounded. He cried out: "O ʿAlī! I have never spoken of this incident to any soul upon the earth—neither to the Prophet nor to his companions. How hast thou come to know of it?"

ʿAlī said simply: "Tell me first the tale, that we may speak of it together."

Salmān recounted: "When I embraced Islam, my own father became my enemy and sought to take my life. One day, I fled from my home and journeyed to a land called ʿArzān. There I rested for a time. Upon awakening, I made my way to a nearby stream to perform my ablutions and refresh myself. As I was about to enter the water, a great tiger emerged from the wilderness and approached me, standing astride upon my garments. Seized with terror, I cried out from the depths of my heart: 'O Fāris al-Ḥijāz, adrikni!' (O Horseman of Ḥijāz, come to my aid!)

"Scarcely had the words left my lips when I beheld a masked rider upon a noble steed, moving with terrible swiftness. With a single stroke of his sword, he clove the tiger in two. I emerged from the water and threw myself upon the stirrup of the horseman, kissing it in gratitude. Since the season was spring and the desert bloomed with rare flowers, I plucked a blossom of extraordinary beauty and presented it to the young rider as a token of my gratitude. Then the horseman departed, and I never saw him again."

Then ʿAlī reached into the folds of his garment and withdrew that very flower—preserved still, fresh and fragrant as though picked but moments before. He placed it in Salmān's trembling hands.

Salmān gazed upon the flower and wept. In that instant, the truth pierced through all veils: if ʿAlī was higher than him in rank and station, what did it matter that Salmān was elder in years? How could the measure of age compare with the measure of spiritual reality? ʿAlī had read the hidden thought in Salmān's heart—a subtle arrogance that had crept into his soul—and he had removed it with perfect wisdom. Through the throwing of dates and the presentation of the flower, ʿAlī had revealed to Salmān that he transcended the limitations of time and space, age and mortality, that he stood forever superior to all creation in rank and proximity to God.[^cite-12]

## Imām al-Mustʿalī and the Proofs of Imāmate

In the time of Imām al-Mustʿalī Billāh, a trial emerged: his brother Nizār, who had been passed over in the succession, falsely claimed the Imāmate for himself. To establish the truth and the criteria of the Imāmate, Imām al-Mustʿalī placed before all the assembled people a sword—the legendary Dhū al-Fiqār (*Zulfiqār*), the sword of the Commander of the Faithful ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib. He proclaimed: "Behold, this is the sword of our ancestor Amīr al-Muʾminīn. It can only be lifted and wielded by the Imām of the Time, chosen by God and endowed with divine authority."

Nizār and his other brothers, one after another, stepped forward and attempted to lift the sword. Each struggled with all his strength, yet none could so much as move it from the ground. Then Imām al-Mustʿalī approached and, with effortless grace, lifted the sword and held it aloft. Nizār, witnessing this, cried out: "Thou hast demonstrated physical strength, but spiritual strength is another matter! That is the true mark of imāmate."

Then the Imām responded: "Very well. There is a tiger within the palace. Let us bring it forth. If thou art truly an imām, command it to attack me, and if thy imāmate is genuine, it will obey thy command and cease to harm me."

The great tiger was brought before them and released. At the Imām's command, the beast approached Imām al-Mustʿalī and, instead of attacking, began to rub its massive head against his feet in an attitude of submission and reverence. The Imām then said: "Now it is my turn, O Nizār. Watch what occurs when a true Imām commands." He ordered the tiger to seize Nizār, and the beast lunged forward. Nizār fled in panic and hid behind the guards, who barely managed to restrain the raging animal. Thus was the falsity of his claim revealed before all.

## Imām al-ʿĀmir Billāh and the Lion of the Carpet

In the days when an Abbasid Caliph ruled, a certain magician sought to humiliate Imām Mawlānā al-ʿĀmir Billāh and to expose him as a fraud before the court. The magician approached the Caliph and said: "Grant me a favor, O Commander of the Faithful. Arrange a great banquet and invite this so-called Imām to it. I shall demonstrate before all that he possesses no extraordinary powers."

The Caliph accepted the invitation to the banquet, and Imām al-ʿĀmir Billāh attended. When all were seated and the meal was served, the magician began to work his enchantment. As the Imām reached for bread, the magician caused it to move away from him. Again and again, the Imām attempted to take food, and again and again, through magical interference, it eluded him. The courtiers began to laugh and mock.

Then the Imām turned to the Caliph and asked: "O Commander of the Faithful, wouldst thou witness something more extraordinary than this deception?" He gestured toward the hanging carpets that adorned the great hall. Upon one of them was woven the image of a magnificent lion. The Imām spoke to the lion as though it were alive: "O Lion, arise and devour this deceiver!"

In that instant, the woven lion roared forth from the carpet with terrible reality. It leaped down into the hall, seized the magician, and swallowed him whole. Then, as mysteriously as it had come alive, the lion returned to the carpet and became once more a mere image.

The Caliph, witnessing this miracle, fell into profound confusion and besought the Imām: "Surely thou art the Imām! Thou hast demonstrated powers that belong to none but God's chosen. I beseech thee, free this man from the belly of the lion, for surely the mercy of God extends even to the wicked."

The Imām replied with a wisdom born of divine knowledge: "If the staff of Moses swallowed the false serpents and then preserved them whole within itself, how much more shall this lion preserve the magician alive within its belly? There he shall remain until God wills his release."[^cite-13]

## The Back of Imām Jaʿfar al-Ṣādiq

A man came once to the court of Imām Jaʿfar al-Ṣādiq (peace be upon him), and upon seeing him, the man's heart was seized with a thought: "I have heard that the Imām is of exalted rank and station, yet he appears as any other human being. Perhaps the stories are mere exaggeration."

But the Imām, whose knowledge encompasses all that is hidden, perceived this unspoken doubt in the man's heart. He called to him and said: "I have an unbearable itch upon my back. Come and scratch it for me."

When the man removed the Imām's garment to minister to him, a sight of terrible wonder met his eyes. The entire back was covered with festering sores and running abscesses, from which pus flowed like water. The man's doubt transformed into shock and horror. He sat down, trembling, and began to scratch the afflicted back. After some time, the Imām commanded: "Scratch my back once more."

This time, when the man removed the garment, the back was perfectly smooth and clean, entirely healed, as though it had never known disease. Yet the man's astonishment only grew deeper, and a new anxiety gripped his soul.

A third time, the Imām ordered: "Scratch my back again." The man obeyed, and when he removed the garment, he found no back at all—only empty space. He moved his hands through the air where flesh should have been, yet grasped nothing. The Imām had thus revealed to the man, through three successive states, the reality of his absolute authority and dominion over his own being. The flesh, the corruption, the very substance of the body itself—all obey the will of the Imām, for he is the manifestation of divine command upon the earth.[^cite-14]

## Rudeness Against Imām al-Ḥākim

In the era of Imām al-Ḥākim bi-Amr Allāh, a man entered a mosque and beheld three people seated together, loudly insulting the Imam and speaking ill of him with venom and contempt. The man who witnessed this grew filled with righteous anger; his blood boiled within him. Yet, being poor and powerless, he could not raise his hand against them. Instead, he turned to God in sincere supplication, crying out: "O Imām of the Age! Thou art the All-Powerful. Help me and lighten my burden of grief!"

Even before his prayer had fully ended, a miraculous transformation occurred. The three men looked upon one another and beheld a vision: the form of Imām al-Ḥākim appeared before them, his presence overwhelming, his majesty terrible. Because of the hatred and enmity harbored in their hearts, they could not bear the sight. They threw themselves upon one another in blind rage, each believing he was attacking the Imām. They fought with fury until each had slain the other, and all three lay dead upon the ground.

In that moment, the poor man witnessed the Imām standing before him, visible and real. The Imām spoke to him with gentle compassion: "Thou didst remember me with sincerity, and so I came to thee."[^cite-15]

Thus does the Imām of each age stand as the guardian of God's truth, protecting the faithful and punishing those who speak against him with arrogance and malice. His presence encompasses all times and all places, and his knowledge embraces all that is hidden.
