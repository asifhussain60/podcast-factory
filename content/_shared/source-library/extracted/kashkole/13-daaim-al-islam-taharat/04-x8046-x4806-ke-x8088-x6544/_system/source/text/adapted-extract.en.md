## Taʾwīl of Istinjāʾ — Esoteric Exegesis of Personal Cleansing

Maulana ʿAlī (a.s.) teaches that it is customary to begin with cleansing the private areas and to complete this action thoroughly. To neglect combining the two outlets together is disliked (makrūh), as is the use of the right hand; yet if necessity or particular circumstance compels one to do so, an exception may be made.

The believer's shame is not his own possession—it does not constitute a part of faith itself. Rather, shame emerges from the excrement of those who reject faith. The first source consists of components drawn from the realm of Egypt (al-dunyā) and the arrogant (al-Mustakhabirūn), whilst the latter comprises the components of depravity and doubt. Urine and excrement represent the spiritual corruption of those who come and go in denial of truth, for filth perpetually flows from the channel of uncleanness. The private areas serve as a place through which both baseness and defilement enter and exit. Polytheism (shirk) and disbelief (kufr) penetrate the believer's inner being through these outlets. Two forms of personal cleansing (istinjāʾ) are distinguished:[^cite-1]

| **Istinjāʾ al-Kabīr** | **Istinjāʾ al-Ṣaghīr** |
| --- | --- |
| What is obligatory after a natural function | What follows intention when no event has occurred |
| Dispelling the believer's doubts | The believer's dismissal of inherited confusion |

Maulana ʿAlī relates that two places remain perpetually impure and shall require cleansing throughout life: one is the place of excretion (al-Mukhrajāt), the other the heart. Impurities of the worldly realm (dunyā) enter the domain of faith through these outlets, whilst evils of the realm of faith ascend toward the Hereafter through the heart itself. Outward water purifies the body; the heart is purified through inner knowledge (ʿilm bāṭin).[^cite-2]

| **The Outlets** | **The Heart** |
| --- | --- |
| Gateway of worldly impurities | Gateway of the impurities of faith |
| Purification by surface water | Purification through inner knowledge |

## Taʾwīl of Washing the Hands

The foremost obligation in ablution is to wash both hands after one has cleansed the private areas, whether the hands are visibly soiled or apparently clean. Thereafter, the water vessel should be positioned at one's right side. One is commanded to place the right hand into the vessel and to wash both hands with water, using the right hand as the principal agent. The grooves between the finger joints (barājim) must also be thoroughly washed.[^cite-3]

**First Interpretation:**

The vessel of water exemplifies the Summoner (dāʿī), whilst the water within it represents the Summoner's knowledge (ʿilm al-dāʿī). In another circle of meaning, the right hand symbolizes the Summoner and the left hand symbolizes the respondent (al-Mustajīb). Both hands come together in the act of washing, signifying the respondent's joining with the Summoner in order to acquire the Summoner's knowledge and to meet the respondent so that knowledge may be transmitted. The more the left hand makes contact with the right hand, the more the right hand purifies it. The interpretation here is that the more questions the respondent poses, the more the Summoner purifies the respondent's self through the clarity of his knowledge.

| **Right Hand** | **Left Hand** |
| --- | --- |
| The Summoner (al-Dāʿī) | The Respondent (al-Mustajīb) |

The metaphor of the finger-joints (barājim) is understood as follows: just as an Imam possesses twelve supplications, each containing four sacred bounds (ḥudūd), so too are there twelve joints in the four fingers of the hand. To wash these twelve pairs of joints is to confess recognition of the twelve sacred limits (ḥudūd) of the Imam. To wash both hands, freeing the knowledge of all the Imam's supplications from the polytheism (shirk) of the first, second, and third supplicants, and to believe that the knowledge of our supplications stands pure and separate from their polytheistic associations—this is the true meaning.[^cite-4]

**Second Interpretation:**

In a higher circle of meaning, the right hand represents the Summoner whilst the left hand ⟪ar:مُكَلَّـــب⟫ represents the respondent. Knowledge is first granted to the Summoner and then to the respondent, and thereafter both cleanse the believer through this knowledge. There are ten fingers on both hands combined. The five fingers of the right hand symbolize the five daily prayers (al-ṣalawāt al-khamsa) that the Summoner presents at the time of prayer; the five fingers of the left hand are represented in the five categories of almsgiving (zakat).[^cite-5]

| **Five Fingers of the Right Hand** | **Five Fingers of the Left Hand** |
| --- | --- |
| The Five Prayers (al-Ṣalawāt al-Khamsa) | The Five Forms of Almsgiving (Zakat) |

When both hands are washed, the five prayers and the five forms of almsgiving are united. In other words, the Summoner grants the respondent knowledge of the five prayers—*Fajr*, *Ẓuhr*, *ʿAṣr*, *Maghrib*, and *ʿIshāʾ*—and the respondent acquires these five sciences, offering them to the Summoner in five forms of knowledge-giving: alms of wealth (zakat al-māl), the fifth part of spoils (al-khums), alms of the breaking of the fast (fitrah), the alms of breaking (faqīrah), and the alms of the path (sabīl). To obtain knowledge from above and to give knowledge to those below is itself called almsgiving (zakat). The name given to attaining one's connection with one's highest spiritual limits in this life is prayer (ṣalāh), and in the Hereafter, attaining one's highest limits is called almsgiving (zakat). For this reason, throughout the Qur'an, prayer and almsgiving are always mentioned together.

**Third Interpretation:**

In yet another sense, the right hand exemplifies knowledge of the Intellect (ʿilm al-ʿAql), and the left hand exemplifies knowledge of the Foundation (ʿilm al-Asās). Hands are washed immediately after the cleansing of the private areas because they have touched that which is contrary to purification. That is to say: if a believer is told something by an opponent that raises doubt concerning the status of the Intellect and the Foundation, then that believer has indeed denied both to some degree. Therefore, he must repent; and if he repents, he must wash his hands spiritually. The believer's conviction concerning the Speaker (al-Nāṭiq) and the Foundation (al-Asās), whether corrupted or sound, necessitates repentance—just as the outward law requires that even clean hands must be washed and extended toward the water vessel. The interpretation of washing both hands is the acknowledgment of the knowledge of the hierarchy and the supreme status of these two ranks through the Summoner's knowledge.

| Knowledge of the Speaker (ʿilm al-Nāṭiq) | Knowledge of the Foundation (ʿilm al-Asās) |
| Muhammad (peace be upon him) | ʿAlī (a.s.) |

## Taʾwīl of Intention (*Niyya*) in Wuḍūʾ

Muhammad al-Muṣṭafā (peace and blessings of Allah be upon him) declared: "It is not permissible to purify oneself without intention." Before forming one's intention, it is obligatory to recite *Bismillāh ar-Raḥmān ar-Raḥīm* (In the name of Allah, the Most Compassionate, the Most Merciful). 

The Imams (a.s.) have transmitted from Maulana ʿAlī (a.s.) that he said: "If a person performs ablution without intention, his prayer is not valid." For example, if one recites four cycles of the midday prayer (ẓuhr) but does not intend the midday prayer specifically, then the prayer is rendered void. This is because the Prophet (peace and blessings of Allah be upon him) stated: "All actions depend upon intention."[^cite-6] The Holy Prophet further proclaimed: "Honor and miracle are not possible without intention, worship without faith, and dignity without piety."

| **Body** | **Spirit** |
| --- | --- |
| Intention (Niyyah) | Action |
| Certainty (Yaqīn) | Worship (ʿIbāda) |
| Piety (Taqwā) | Dignity (ʿIzzah) |

**Interpretation of *Bismillāh* Before Intention:**

The interpretation of intention is to be under the guardianship (wilāya) of the People of the House (*Ahl al-Bayt*) (a.s.) and to accept their covenant. The guardianship of the *Ahl al-Bayt* is ordained by Allah, and no act of sacred law is acceptable without it.

On the surface, "Allah" is the name of the Divine; in the interpretation (taʾwīl), the true name of Allah is the essence of the Imam of the Age (Imām al-Zamān) (a.s.), for he makes us know Allah just as a person's name identifies him. Similarly, both the Heir (al-Wāṣī) and the Messenger (al-Rasūl) are the true names of Allah in their respective epochs, for it is through them that the believers come to know Allah.

When the believer recites *Bismillāh ar-Raḥmān ar-Raḥīm* during his purification, he thereby testifies that he can approach Allah only through his Guardian (Walī). It is solely through knowledge of his Guardian that he may reach Allah's presence, protecting himself from the enemies of the Guardian's guardianship, as Allah Himself declares: [^cite-7]

## Taʾwīl of Mouth-Rinsing and Use of the Miswāk

Rinsing the mouth and pouring water into the nose are not reckoned among the pillars of ablution, inasmuch as Allah has not mentioned them explicitly in the Qur'an. Yet the Messenger of Allah (peace and blessings of Allah be upon him) established them in his sacred law. Therefore, both rinsing and nasal irrigation are practices of the Sunna (sunnah). They ought not to be deliberately forsaken or treated with indifference. It is permissible to rinse the mouth three times and to irrigate the nose three times; yet if one rinses once and irrigates once, this too is sufficient.

The method is as follows: use the thumb of the right hand upon the right side of the mouth (both upper and lower teeth), then apply the finger of the word upon the left side (both upper and lower teeth).

Sayyid al-Qāḍī al-Nuʿmān narrates in his compendium *Daʿāʾim al-Islām* that the Messenger of Allah (peace and blessings of Allah be upon him) said: "If people knew the benefits of the miswāk (tooth-cleaning twig), then all would place it beneath their pillows before sleep!" He also declared: "Were it not burdensome upon my Community, I would have made the miswāk obligatory alongside ablution! The miswāk is half of ablution, and ablution is half of faith. If a believer wakes in the night and uses the miswāk and then performs the greater ablution (ghusl), his ghusl is most excellent. When he enters the mosque, an angel comes to him and places his mouth upon the believer's mouth, so that whatever issues from the believer's mouth enters the angel's mouth. That same angel shall come as his intercessor and witness on the Day of Resurrection."[^cite-8]

**Key Interpretation:**

The mouth exemplifies the Proof (al-Ḥujja), and the teeth represent the prayers (supplications) that are within the context of the Proof. There are thirty prayers within the rank of a single Proof. The example of mouth-rinsing (kullī) is to know all of them and to love them all. The rinsing also indicates that the Summoner makes clear to the believer: do not attribute this knowledge that you have learned from me to me alone, for this knowledge has been granted to me by the Proof, and the Proof has received it from the Imam. The completion of three rinsings occurs through these three: the Summoner, the Proof, and the Imam. The mouth exemplifies the Proof of the island (Ḥujjat al-Jazīra), because the mouth is the gateway to physical nourishment, whilst the Proof is the gateway to spiritual nourishment. The knowledge of the Imam does not reach the people save through the Proof.

**The Tooth-Brushing:**

There are thirty-two teeth in the mouth, of which four are canine teeth; thus the primary teeth number twenty-eight, which signify the sacred limits (ḥudūd) of the Affirmation of Unity (Kalimат al-Tawḥīd). The twelve teeth on the left side of the mouth (six above, six below) correspond to the twelve prayers of the Proofs, whilst the twelve teeth on the right indicate the twelve prayers of the Imams.

To brush the teeth is an example of the Proof (mouth) granting knowledge to the prayers (teeth) of the ranks beneath it, so that they all may become purified and illuminated through his knowledge, just as the teeth shine in outward appearance. Brushing the teeth represents the knowledge and acknowledgment of the twelve sacred limits of the Proofs and the twelve sacred limits of the Imams. The thumb signifies the upper limit, and the finger of the word signifies the lower limit.

The wood of the miswāk exemplifies the Summoner, who grants the respondent knowledge of the sacred limits.[^cite-9]

In another sense, to pray is to exhort the prayers of one's own soul. This is why the teeth are commanded to be brushed horizontally, for all the sacred limits are counseled equally, and each limit is not counseled separately (i.e., not in a vertical motion).

**Facts:**

The Prophet's custom was to benefit the sacred limits of his temple. He would remove doubts concerning his limits through his teachings, and he taught all the limits at once, not separately. Thus he ordered the miswāk to be used horizontally, not vertically.

Just as the mouth becomes clean and fragrant through the miswāk, the Proof becomes famous and well-known through its prayers. There is a tongue in the mouth, which signifies the Proof of the Imam (the son of the Imam), and it exists within the special circle of the Imam under the protection of thirty-two supplications.

**Second Interpretation:**

When we rinse, we recite the prayer: "O Allah, grant me to drink from the cup of Your Prophet." The mouth comprises a total of thirty-two teeth, a tongue, and two lips—thus thirty-five things in all, which correspond to the thirty-five sects of Egypt and the arrogant. The Prophet's cup is Islam or his sacred law. Through purification, the Proof signifies the purification of its prayers from all the impurities of doubt and confusion sown by the thirty-five sects of Egypt and the arrogant ones.[^cite-10]

**Interpretation:**

By brushing the teeth from top to bottom, the components of leprosy (metaphorically, spiritual corruption) are removed. Leprosy is caused by Maulana ʿAlī's entry into guardianship and his departure from guardianship.

The tongue sits upon the son of the Imam, and the teeth sit upon the prayers of the Imam. Both lips correspond to the Manifest Gate and the Hidden Gate (Bāb al-Ẓāhir wa-Bāb al-Bāṭin). The teeth and lips cover the tongue and protect it, just as all these hierarchies protect the son of the Imam.

Both lips cover all the teeth, which indicates that the gathering of all the sacred limits of the Proofs and the Imams is united with the Manifest Gate and the Hidden Gate, for these gates merge all the limits into their structure. What is intended is that the Summoner explains the hierarchy of all these limits and clarifies their status.

## Taʾwīl of Rinsing Water Through the Nose

After rinsing the mouth, water is poured into the nose—that is, water is drawn directly into both nostrils and then cleansed with the little finger of the left hand.

This teaches that the Summoner makes clear to the respondent: when you have acquired the knowledge of the Proof (mouth), do not associate this knowledge with the Proof alone, nor imagine that it originates from the Proof. Rather, return the knowledge of the Proof to the Imam, who is likened to the nose.[^cite-11]

In one sense, the two nostrils of the nose exemplify the Imam of the Age (a.s.) and his father, who entrusted him with the Imamate, for the Imamate continues perpetually from father to son, just as the process of breathing—inhalation following exhalation—continues throughout life. The example of drawing water into the nostrils is the recognition of the Imam (a.s.) through the Manifest and Hidden Chapters (al-Bābān).

**The Mouth and Nose:**

The metaphor of the Proof and Imam has been drawn from the mouth and nose because the Proof and Imam perform in the refinement of the soul the same function that the mouth and nose perform in the human body. If the mouth and nose are closed, a person suffocates and dies. Similarly, if knowledge of the Proof and the Imam is withheld from the souls of creation, all these souls perish in spiritual death.

Speech issues from the mouth, not from the nose; yet the nose perceives fragrance from a distance. This signifies that the Proof imparts knowledge to the believers through words, whilst the Imam (a.s.) grants knowledge to the Proof through command and thought. There exists a secret passage from the nose to the mouth, indicating a hidden support flowing from the Imam (a.s.) to the Proof. Whether a person sleeps or wakes, the nose breathes continuously, sustaining life. This indicates that regardless of whether all people acknowledge the truth, the Imam (a.s.) perpetually proclaims the truth, forever showering blessings upon people and sustaining their souls in life, whether they are conscious of these blessings or not.

The nose cannot take the place of the mouth in consuming food; yet the mouth can function in place of the nose in breathing. Similarly, the Proof holds the position of the Imam during the era of concealment (al-ghayba), yet even when the Imam is in concealment or in revelation, the Proof is not the successor of the Imam. The position of the Imam remains always distinct and unique.

**The Nostrils (*Mankhārayn*):**

There are two apertures in the nose, yet only one in the mouth, which indicates that the center of knowledge for the Imam is the Speaker (al-Nāṭiq) and the Foundation (al-Asās), whilst the center of knowledge for the Proof is the Imam alone.

There are seven apertures in the face, signifying the seven Imams of each era. The nose exemplifies the Imam, whilst its two principal chapters are the Manifest Chapter and the Hidden Chapter (Bāb al-Ẓāhir wa-Bāb al-Bāṭin). One chapter explains the status of the Imam, the other explains the status of his successor. To pour water into the nose is thus to explain the stations of Imamate and guardianship.

**Facts:**

The successor in sacred law is Ubayy ibn Kaʿb; the successor in exegesis is Salmān al-Fārsī; and the successor in the inner realities is ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib. Ubayy ibn Kaʿb and Salmān al-Fārsī explained the status of the Prophet (s) and ʿAlī.[^cite-12]

## Taʾwīl of Washing the Face

The Imams (a.s.) have commanded that the face be washed from the forehead to the chin on both sides (extending to the ears) after the rinsing and nasal irrigation. One must wash thoroughly with water and rub the face with both hands.

Moreover, it has been emphasized that abundant water be applied to the eyes. The Prophet (peace and blessings of Allah be upon him) said: "Pour abundant water upon your eyes at the time of ablution, so that Allah may preserve you from the blazing fire—the *hamīm*—in which those who do not pray shall burn."[^cite-13]

It is preferable that the face be washed three times; however, if it is washed carefully once or twice, this suffices. If it is not washed carefully even once, then three hasty washings will not avail. The Imams have also commanded that the beard be trimmed.

**Interpretation:**

The face exemplifies the Prophet (peace be upon him) and his five great sacred limits. There are five apertures in the face—two eyes, two nostrils, and one mouth—and to wash them signifies the recognition of all five and the obedience to their commands. The face is open and visible in distinction to all other parts of the body. Similarly, the Prophet stands as the most celebrated and manifest of all other sacred limits.

All the limbs are recognized by the face; that is, when hands or feet are seen without the face, one cannot know to whom they belong. This signifies that all spiritual and physical sacred limits are recognized by the seal of the Speaker (Khātam al-Nāṭiq), for he is the face of religion. It is because of the Messenger that the servants are drawn to Allah.

**Placing Water in the Eyes:**

In ablution, we pour water upon our eyes so that they are guarded from the heat of the fire. In Arabic, the eye is called *ʿayn*, and in the terminology of the invitation, the eyes refer to two states of the eye. The origin and source of anything are also called *ʿayn*. To pour water into both eyes signifies the acquisition of knowledge concerning the Origin (*ʿAyn*) and the Hereafter (*Ākhira*), which protects the servant from the Fire of Hell—that is, the Fire of Doubt and Error.

## The Five Senses of the Face (in the Taʾwīl)

This means that Allah can be known through the five sacred limits of religion, which in one sense are the Five Exalted Ones (Khamsat al-Athār), and in another sense are the Speaker, the Foundation, the Imam, the Proof, and the Summoner.

Thus the status of sight (basīrah) exemplifies the Intellect (al-ʿAql). If there is no obstruction before it, the power of sight can perceive from east to west. The power of hearing does not possess this degree; hearing cannot reach the distance that sight reaches. Thus hearing exemplifies the status of the Foundation (al-Asās). And to the extent that hearing can hear, the power of smell cannot smell; thus smell exemplifies the status of the Imam (al-Imām). And to the extent that smell can smell, the power of taste cannot taste; taste exemplifies the status of the Proof (al-Ḥujja). And the power of taste cannot function unless the object is first felt by placing it in the mouth. Thus the power of feeling (al-ḥiss) exemplifies the status of the Summoner (al-Dāʿī).

| **Sense** | **Name** | **Interpretation** | **Represented** |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| *Baṣīrah* | Sight | The Speaker | Muhammad |
| *Samʿ* | Hearing | The Foundation | ʿAlī |
| *Shamma* | Smell | The Imam | Fāṭima |
| *Ṭaʿm* | Taste | The Proof | al-Ḥusayn |
| *Ḥiss* | Touch | The Summoner | al-Ḥasan |

Unless something is placed in the mouth, it cannot be tasted. This signifies that the meaning (taʾwīl) of a word and action cannot be understood unless it is known through the path. Or, it means that only through the respondent (al-Mustajīb / al-Ḥasan) can one reach the Proof (al-Ḥujja / al-Ḥusayn).

At the command of the heart, the brain's actions reach the senses of the face through three mental powers: memory, imagination, and recollection. Were it not for these three faculties, these senses would be useless. The reality of this is that the command emanating from the Intellect conveys the soul to itself through its three stations—grandfather, victory, and thought—which correspond to the origin, the Imam, the Proof, and the Summoner. The self has been in constant support since time immemorial, which is the foundation of the present.

## Washing the Hands Up to the Elbows

When performing ablution, one should wash the right hand up to and including the elbows, then the left hand, the right foot first, and then the left foot. If one washes the left hand first and then the right, one must wash the left hand again. The Imams (a.s.) have commanded that the limb to which Allah has assigned priority in ablution should not be postponed, and the limb to which has been assigned the second place should not be given priority.

The Imams (a.s.) have commanded that both hands and elbows be washed three times. If the greater ablution is performed thoroughly once, it suffices. Similarly, three washings are insufficient unless at least one is performed thoroughly, and both palms should be rubbed together with the elbows on both hands, for Allah has so commanded. The Imams have further instructed that if a ring is worn upon the finger during ablution, it should be rotated so that water reaches beneath it. When applying water to the hands, do not rub against the direction of the hair.

**First Interpretation:**

Washing the arms up to the elbows exemplifies the Foundation (al-Asās), because a person gains strength through the arm and then expends it upon himself, purifying the body through the arm. This signifies that the soul of the believers benefits from the wisdom of the soul's creation through the Foundation, and the impurity of the believers' souls is removed through knowledge of the Foundation. Labor is performed by the hands, indicating that the Foundation exerts all effort and energy in spreading the law of the Speaker (al-Nāṭiq).

The face is one, whilst the hands are two, which means that the Speaker relies upon outward transmission alone, whilst the Foundation relies upon both outward transmission and inward interpretation. It is obligatory to wash the face first, then to wash the arms, signifying that one must first acknowledge and accept the outward sacred law of the Speaker, and only after gaining knowledge of this outward law should one proceed to the inward interpretation of the Foundation.

There is no limit for washing the face, just as there is an elbow limit for the hands. The fist refers to ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib (a.s.), and the other arm also refers to ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib (a.s.), while the remainder of the arm refers to all the Imams (a.s.) who have been established between them. Washing one's arm is an argument for knowing all the Imams between them and acquiring knowledge from them.

| **The Fist** | **The Arm** | **The Elbow** |
| --- | --- |--- |
| ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib | All the Imams | Qāʾim ʿAlī Dhikr al-Salām |

**Second Interpretation:**

In one sense, the washing of both hands also indicates that the Summoner is unique and the respondent is not unique, and in another sense, it also indicates that the Summoner and the respondent are both known and obeyed.

While washing our hands, we pray: "Allāh give us our book in our right hand and do not give it to us from the left hand or from behind our back." The "book" is meant to be the "noble form." That is, the believer prays: "O Allah! Grant me my form (on the right side) at the time of my death. May it not occur that because of my misdeeds and sins, You hand over my form to someone else (on the left) for the sake of nurturing, training, and cleansing." Then he washes his left hands and recites the prayer: "O Allah, do not give me my book from the north or from behind my back."

**Third Interpretation:**

In another sense, both hands signify the Imam and the Proof. No epoch is devoid of the Imam and his Proof. When there are no Imams, the heavens and the earth do not stand. The son of the Imam is called his Proof during the Imam's lifetime.

There are two types of Proofs: the son of an Imam who has not yet been appointed as Imam and has not yet reached the Imam's station; and the Summoner who rises in rank and becomes the Proof of the age.

Washing both hands is an acknowledgment that whenever there is an Imam, his Proof shall be established with him, and the supplications shall continue in his time.

## Wiping the Head, Ears, and Neck

One is commanded to wipe the front and back portions of the head. When wiping, one should first begin from the crown of the head and draw the fingers toward the forehead hair. Then, lifting the fingers, place them again upon the crown and draw them toward the back of the head. Then wipe both the inner and outer parts of both ears, and finally wipe the neck once. If one wipes the neck three times with the intention of spiritual grace and reward—not with the thought that it is permissible to do otherwise—then it is preferable to do so three times.

**Interpretation:**

The example of the head is the First Intellect (al-ʿAql al-Awwal), for it is the head of the spiritual and physical worlds. Anointing upon the head signifies the acknowledgment of its existence. The anointing begins from the crown in both forward and backward directions; that is, the First Intellect is the originator of all movements of negation (nафī) and affirmation (ithbāt), and from this the Divine Unity (tawḥīd) began and spread everywhere. These two movements also signify the fact that the First Intellect established the relationship between the noble and the noble in Divine Unity. The head is hidden within the hair, signifying that the Intellect is veiled in the first veil and is not visible, yet is recognized through the other visible sacred limits (the hair).[^cite-14]

In another sense, the anointing of the head signifies the hierarchy of ancestral lines, conquest, and imagination. The crown of the head is the place of the ancestors, who divided the hierarchy into the Forward (*Fātih*) and the Imaginary (*Khayāl*).

In another sense, the station of the Messenger of Allah (peace be upon him) is that of the head in religion—the highest position. He ascended from the lower sight to the higher sight, surpassing all the Imams, and all the Imams who came after him follow his sacred law and shall continue to do so until the emergence of the Qāʾim. Anointing upon the head in ablution signifies all the Imams acknowledging him (the Messenger of Allah) and obeying his sacred law.

Anointing the front and back of the head indicates that the sacred law of the Prophet (peace be upon him) would have become meaningless without the interpretation (taʾwīl) of ʿAlī. Wiping the front is an argument for the outward sacred law of the Prophet (peace and blessings of Allah be upon him), for the front of the head is visible and manifest. Wiping the back is an argument for the inward interpretation of ʿAlī, for the back of the head is invisible and hidden.[^cite-15]

## The Fragrance and Call of the Garden

Spiritual aspiration is a garden in the heavens. Fragrances are created from exceedingly subtle and pure essences. Because of their delicate nature, when one inhales them, the body need not labor to convert them into energy and vital force. Rather, they are instantly transformed into brain power, which is the true aim of all nourishment. This state becomes established in the temple of the inward dimension such that no pain remains after entering it. All knowledge and blessings are attained without effort.

For this reason, while cleansing the nasal passages with water, we recite the following prayer: "Bring me to Paradise with the fragrance of Your Paradise." That is: when my form emerges after my death, let it be merged with the inner chamber whilst passing through all the stations. By entering this gathering, I may acquire the sublime and subtle sciences therein. May it not be that because of my misdeeds and sins, my entry into the inner chamber becomes impossible, and I must obtain this inner nourishment from the lower ranks whilst remaining outside. This is because fragrance diminishes as it moves away from its source.[^cite-16]

**The Meaning of the Gospel of the Blessed Sacrament:**

In taking water into the nose, we pray for the "fragrance" of Paradise; yet in wiping the ears, we pray for the "call" of Paradise. Sound is dense in contrast to fragrance, for it is produced by the collision of objects and operates within physicality. At the time of wiping the ears, we recite: "Make me hear the preaching of Paradise from Your Paradise." This paradise refers to the gathering of the Manifest Gate, which is prepared by the Manifest Gates. That is: I should not hear the voices and preaching of this paradise from here and there. As I progress along the Path of the Prophet, I should not go astray, but rather I should progress toward the temple of the Manifest Gate.[^cite-17]

After that, the Imams have commanded the wiping of both feet, as Allah says in this verse:

If a person wipes both feet for the sake of grace and reward after completing ablution, and has washed the toes for the sake of purity, then he has performed an excellent deed. This action suffices for purity and purification, yet should not be regarded as an obligation without which ablution is impermissible.

Anointing upon the feet signifies the acknowledgment of the existence of the Soul (al-Nafs) or the origin of the First Intellect. The feet are two whilst the head is one, just as the hands and the face are single. The feet are confined up to a certain limit, just as the washing of the hands is confined to the elbows. Similarly, there is no limit for wiping the head, just as there is no limit for washing the face.

When the spiritual invitation (daʿwa) was established in the spiritual world by command of the First Intellect, all the labor of the Soul was taken up by the Soul and the seven Intellects were prepared on the basis of its hard work and invitation. In the same way, man walks upon his feet to earn his livelihood. The status of the Soul is limited in comparison to the Intellect, because it has advanced by seeing it in the spiritual world. Similarly, the status of the Foundation in comparison to the Intellect is limited, in that it can only explain the sacred law of the Speaker and cannot establish its own sacred law and interpretation. The Intellect is one in its status and knowledge, as the head is one. The Soul possesses a dual nature in time, wherein the Intellect acquires knowledge from the Soul and imparts knowledge to those below it, just as there are two legs.

In another sense, the parable of both feet is the Soul (al-Nafs) itself, because the First Intellect has placed the entire responsibility of purification and development of the physical world upon the Soul, just as we place the burden of our entire body upon both our feet.

In another circle, anointing upon both feet is the confession of the Imam and his Proof (the successor Imam), who together undertake all necessary measures for the development of the believers' souls.

One cannot wipe the head and feet unless one has washed the face and hands. That is, unless one knows the Intellect and the Foundation, one cannot acknowledge the existence of the First Intellect and the First Source. Or, one cannot know the Imam and his Proof until one knows the Summoner and the Summoner's agent.

**Interpretation of the Anointing of the Feet:**

The correct method of wiping the feet is to pour water on the left hand with the right hand, then wipe the right foot with the left hand. Then water should be poured on the left hand with the right hand, and the left foot should be anointed with the left hand.

There exist sacred limits (ḥudūd) in both hands and both feet. The right foot is the sacred limit of the left foot; the left hand is the sacred limit of the right foot; the right hand is the sacred limit of the left hand. That is: when the left foot advances, it becomes a right foot. When the right foot advances, the left hand is formed. When the left hand develops, it becomes a right hand. The sacred limit of the left hand and the right foot is exalted, and wiping with water is like imparting knowledge. Knowledge can be acquired from the sacred limit. Thus we complete it with the right hand.
