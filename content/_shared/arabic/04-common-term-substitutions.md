# Common Arabic Term Substitutions

Policy for replacing commonly-repeated Arabic terms with their English
equivalents based on context. The goal is **listener accessibility without
loss of meaning** — the audience is general English readers and listeners,
not Islamic studies specialists.

This file is the **decision layer**. The phonetic forms (when the Arabic IS
kept) live in [03-arabic-english-manifest.md](03-arabic-english-manifest.md).

---

## 1. The substitution principle

**Default**: if a clean English equivalent exists and the Arabic is not
carrying technical precision, **substitute the English form**.

**Exception**: keep the Arabic when…

1. The Arabic is a **technical theological term** with no precise English
   counterpart (e.g., *tawakkul*, *ikhlaas*, *ihsaan*, *taqwa*, *taqleed*).
2. The author or speaker is **explicitly contrasting** multiple Arabic terms
   (e.g., *ilm* vs *ma'rifa*; *qalb* vs *nafs* vs *ruh*).
3. The Arabic appears in a **verbatim quotation** (Quran, hadith, classical
   text). Verbatim quotes are never paraphrased.
4. The term is a **proper noun** (a person, place, work, or institution).
5. The term has been **glossed earlier in the same chapter** and the author is
   deliberately building reader vocabulary.

When in doubt, substitute. A clear English line is worth more than an
untranslated Arabic term in a memoir or podcast aimed at general listeners.

## 2. Context-driven substitutions

These are the high-frequency Arabic words that almost always benefit from
English replacement. The right English depends on the surrounding context.

### nafs

The single most over-used un-translated Arabic term in English Islamic prose.
It can mean any of: self, soul, ego, lower self, base self, the part of a
person that resists discipline, the seat of appetite, or the breathing self.
Context decides.

| Surrounding context | Recommended English |
|---------------------|---------------------|
| General reference to the inner person (no specific theological move) | **soul** |
| Contrasted with *ruh* (spirit), *qalb* (heart), or *aql* (intellect) | **soul** (with the others rendered as spirit / heart / intellect respectively) |
| Specifically the part that pulls toward base desire | **lower soul** or **lower self** |
| Specifically the part that drives anger and ferocity | **irascible soul** |
| Specifically the appetitive / pleasure-seeking aspect | **appetitive soul** |
| In *nafs ammara* — the part that commands evil | **the inciting soul** or **the commanding soul** |
| In *nafs lawwama* — the self-reproaching part | **the self-reproaching soul** |
| In *nafs mutmainna* — the part at peace with God | **the soul at peace** |
| When the speaker is explicitly defining the Sufi technical vocabulary | KEEP `nafs` with first-use gloss "the lower self that pulls toward base desire" |

**Rule of thumb**: in narrative or meditative prose, *nafs* almost always reads
better as **lower soul** or **soul** (depending on whether the moral struggle
context is present). Only keep `nafs` when the surrounding text is a technical
discussion of the Sufi psychology in which the term is being defined.

### shaytan / shaitan

| Surrounding context | Recommended English |
|---------------------|---------------------|
| Default — any reference | **Satan** |
| When narrating the Quranic story of *Iblees* by name | **Iblis** (or keep *Iblees*) |
| When listing a category of unseen tempters (plural *shayateen*) | **devils** |

### ruh

| Surrounding context | Recommended English |
|---------------------|---------------------|
| Default | **spirit** |
| Contrasted with *nafs* and *jasad* | **spirit** (the others as soul and body) |
| In the Quranic technical sense (the divine breath at creation) | KEEP *Ruh* with first-use gloss "the divine breath / Spirit of God" |

### qalb

| Surrounding context | Recommended English |
|---------------------|---------------------|
| Default | **heart** |
| In the Sufi technical sense — the spiritual organ of perception | KEEP *qalb* with first-use gloss "the spiritual heart, the inner organ of perception" |

### aql

| Surrounding context | Recommended English |
|---------------------|---------------------|
| Default | **intellect** or **reason** |
| In the Mu'tazili / philosophical technical sense | KEEP *aql* with first-use gloss "the rational faculty" |

### hawa

| Surrounding context | Recommended English |
|---------------------|---------------------|
| Default | **lower desire** or **passion** |
| Paired with *nafs* in a moral struggle scene | **passion** or **base desire** |

### dunya / akhirah

| Surrounding context | Recommended English |
|---------------------|---------------------|
| *dunya* | **this world**, **worldly life**, or simply **the world** |
| *akhirah* | **the Hereafter**, **the next life**, or **the world to come** |

### jannah / jahannam

| Surrounding context | Recommended English |
|---------------------|---------------------|
| *jannah* | **paradise** (lowercase, unless a proper-noun station like *Jannat al-Firdaws*) |
| *jahannam* | **hell** (lowercase) |

### qiyamah

| Surrounding context | Recommended English |
|---------------------|---------------------|
| Default | **the Resurrection** or **the Day of Standing** |
| In an apocalyptic / eschatological scene | **the Day of Judgment** |

### ilm / hikmah

| Surrounding context | Recommended English |
|---------------------|---------------------|
| *ilm* — default | **knowledge** |
| *ilm* — when contrasted with *ma'rifa* (direct knowing) | KEEP *ilm* with gloss "transmitted knowledge" |
| *hikmah* — default | **wisdom** |

### sabr / shukr

| Surrounding context | Recommended English |
|---------------------|---------------------|
| *sabr* | **patience**, **patient endurance**, or **steadfastness** |
| *shukr* | **gratitude** |

### niyyah

| Surrounding context | Recommended English |
|---------------------|---------------------|
| Default | **intention** |
| In the technical jurisprudential sense (validity of an act of worship) | KEEP *niyyah* with gloss "the binding intention that determines whether an act counts as worship" |

### malak / malaa'ika

| Surrounding context | Recommended English |
|---------------------|---------------------|
| Default | **angel** / **angels** |
| When naming a specific angel (Jibreel, Mikail, Israfeel) | KEEP the proper name with first-use English ("the angel Gabriel", etc.) |

### zuhd / wara'

| Surrounding context | Recommended English |
|---------------------|---------------------|
| *zuhd* | **renunciation** or **detachment from the world** |
| *wara'* | **scrupulousness** or **scrupulous restraint** |

### tasawwuf

| Surrounding context | Recommended English |
|---------------------|---------------------|
| Default | **Sufism** |
| When the chapter is specifically about the tradition by its name | KEEP *tasawwuf* with gloss "the Islamic mystical path; what is loosely called Sufism in English" |

## 3. Keep-the-Arabic terms (technical precision)

These rarely have a clean English equivalent. Keep the Arabic with a brief
first-occurrence gloss. Do NOT substitute.

| Arabic | First-occurrence gloss |
|--------|------------------------|
| tawakkul | reliance on God after taking right action |
| ikhlaas | sincerity, performing acts purely for God's sake |
| ihsaan | spiritual excellence — worshipping God as if seeing Him |
| taqwa | God-consciousness, watchful awareness of God's presence |
| taqleed | following recognized authority in religious matters |
| fard al-ayn | a personal obligation incumbent on every individual |
| fard kifaya | a communal obligation discharged on behalf of the community |
| bid'a | innovation outside the established Quran-and-Sunnah principles |
| Tariqa | the spiritual path or order |
| Haqiqa | the inner truth or reality |
| ma'rifa | direct intimate knowledge of God |
| ta'wil | esoteric or disciplined interpretation (vs literal *tanzil*) |
| Wilayah | spiritual authority and guardianship |
| Imamah | the institution of the Imamat |
| da'wah | the call; the missionary teaching of the Imamat |
| Holy Du'a | the Ismaili daily prayer (proper noun) |
| Ginan | devotional poem in the Khoja tradition |

For honorifics (SAW, AS, RA) see [03-arabic-english-manifest.md](03-arabic-english-manifest.md)
§3. Honorifics are always SPOKEN in full English; the typographic
abbreviation is print-only.

## 4. Memoir-specific overrides

The memoir (`content/babu-memoir/`) has its own established translation
choices recorded in `content/babu-memoir/_system/translations-glossary.md`.
**Memoir choices override this policy** for any chapter that has shipped.
The journal skill applies this policy only when a new term first appears in
the memoir.

Key memoir conventions already locked:
- **Single Urdu/Arabic words** are never glossed inline in memoir prose. The
  reader meets *Baba*, *Amma*, *halal*, *haram*, *nikah* without parenthetical
  translation. The voice fingerprint protects this.
- **Multi-word Arabic phrases** get a parenthetical English gloss.
- **ABD** is the named exception, glossed as "slave" because the meaning is
  the load-bearing point.

When the podcast pulls a memoir chapter via Extract Mode, the memoir's
established treatment wins; the podcast does not re-impose this substitution
policy on already-shipped memoir prose.

## 5. Quality-gate enforcement

**Podcast** (`podcast-challenger`, Loop C4): scan the chapter for every term
in the "context-driven substitutions" table above (§2). If the term appears
in Arabic form and the framing's pronunciation hooks do NOT carry a
documented justification ("we are keeping *nafs* because chapter 4 builds the
Sufi tripartite-soul vocabulary"), flag as **P1**.

**Journal**: when rebuilding a chapter scratchpad and encountering a new
Arabic term, consult this file before applying a parenthetical gloss. If the
term has a §2 entry, prefer the substitution unless Asif's existing voice
choices in adjacent chapters argue against it.

---

## 6. Adding a substitution rule

When a new high-frequency Arabic term appears in a chapter:

1. Add the canonical phonetic to
   [03-arabic-english-manifest.md](03-arabic-english-manifest.md).
2. If it has a clean English equivalent (or several context-driven equivalents),
   add a §2 entry here.
3. If it is technical and stays in Arabic, add it to §3.
4. Update `content/babu-memoir/_system/translations-glossary.md` if the term
   appears in any memoir chapter.
5. Commit alongside the chapter that introduced the term.
