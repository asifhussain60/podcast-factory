# The Method of Living and the Closing Prayer

*From Ayyuhal Walad ("O Beloved Son") by Imam Abu Hamid Muhammad al-Ghazali. This chapter covers the closing movement of the letter: the four things the student should actively do, how to relate to God, how to relate to people, how to study, and how to handle his food and provisions, and the long supplication Ghazali writes for him to carry afterward.*

## The other half of the eight

After the four cautions, debate, preaching, rulers, gifts, Imam Abu Hamid Muhammad al-Ghazali turns to the four things his student should do. Where the cautions were about restraint, the four methods are about active orientation. Each one is structured around a relationship: with God, with other people, with knowledge, and with material provision. Each one carries a different test. Together they describe the shape of an ordinary day for a person who is trying to live the inner truths the earlier sections of the letter laid out.

The structure is built on a single principle that runs underneath all four. *Live in such a way that the way you treat God, and the way you treat His creatures, and the way you spend your hours, and the way you handle your bread, all line up with each other.* The principle is the same; the four methods are the principle expressed in the four domains where most lives are actually lived.

## The first method: relating to God

The first method is about how the student conducts himself in everything that is between him and God. Ghazali names it through an analogy.

Every matter that is between you and Allah, he says, should be conducted in such a way that if your servant did the same thing for you, you would not be sad, you would not be angry, you would in fact like it and commend it. And conversely, anything that you would not like your servant doing for you, you should also recognize that if you do it in your service to your Lord, your Creator will not like it either.

The image is so close to ordinary experience that it is hard to miss. You have a sense of how a servant should behave toward you. You feel it without having to argue about it. The half-effort. The hiding behind technicality. The work done in front of you that disappears when you turn your back. The performance for credit. Each of these is something you would notice if it were done to you. Most of us do not realize we do the same things to God every day.

Ghazali makes the asymmetry explicit. The servant in his analogy is hired by you. You did not create him. You did not raise him. You do not own him in any deep sense. But you yourself are created, raised, and owned by your Real Creator and Owner. Your obligation to Him, to not displease Him in thought, word, or deed, is incomparably greater than your servant's obligation to you. Yet the inner test you apply to your servant is sharper than the test you apply to yourself before God.

The Prophet Muhammad, peace and blessings of Allah be upon him, gave the same teaching from another direction:

> Worship Allah as if you see Him, for though you do not see Him, He certainly sees you.
> (Sahih Muslim, the famous hadith of Gabriel, Book 1, Hadith 1.)

This is the inner state called *Ihsan*, spiritual excellence. It is what the first method asks for. Conduct your service to God as if He were a witness in the room. Because He is.

## The second method: relating to people

The second method is the horizontal mirror of the first. Every matter between you and other people, the slaves of Allah, as Ghazali calls them, since all human beings stand in the same relation of dependence on God, should be conducted by you in such a way that if they did the same thing back to you, you would like it. You would not be grieved by it.

Ghazali quotes a hadith *qudsi*, a saying in which Allah speaks through the Prophet:

> My slave's faith is never complete unless he likes for all human beings what he likes for himself.

The same teaching, in slightly different wording, appears in the canonical hadith collections under the Prophet's own voice:

> None of you truly believes until he loves for his brother what he loves for himself.
> (Sahih Bukhari, Hadith 13; Sahih Muslim, Hadith 45.)

It is one of the most-quoted lines in the entire Islamic tradition for a reason. It collapses the whole question of ethics into a single test the listener can run on himself in real time. *Would I want this done to me? If not, I should not do it.*

Imam Ali ibn Abi Talib, peace be upon him, expanded the same teaching into the longer form that appears in his letter to his son al-Hasan, recorded in *Nahj al-Balagha*:

> O my son, make yourself the measure between you and others. Love for them what you love for yourself, and dislike for them what you dislike for yourself. Do not oppress, as you do not want to be oppressed. Do good, as you wish good for yourself. Consider bad for yourself what you consider bad for others. Be content with others as you wish them to be content with you. Do not say what you do not know, even if your knowledge is little. Do not say to others what you would dislike being said to you.
> Source: Imam Ali (AS), *Nahj al-Balagha*, Letter 31 (to his son).

The same measuring stick. Yourself, applied to the other. Inverted, applied to yourself. The whole letter to Imam Hasan repeats it in seven different ways, because that is how often a person has to be reminded.

In the Ismaili daily prayer, the *Holy Du'a*, the same architecture surfaces in the second part: the believer prays for the well-being of all the creatures of God, naming his own well-being inside theirs. He cannot ask for himself what he is not also asking for others. The shape is consistent across the traditions because the shape is true.

## The third method: study what will benefit you

The third method is about how to handle knowledge, which is, after all, the very thing the student's letter was asking about in the first place. Ghazali frames it through a thought experiment.

Imagine you want to read a scholarly book. Before you open it, ask yourself this question: *what if my life is going to be over in no more than a week?* In that situation, what kind of knowledge will benefit me? Then only indulge in that kind.

If you genuinely believed you had a week left, Ghazali says, you would not read books on religious debate, on dictionaries, on linguistics and grammar, on poetry collections, on essay writing. You would understand, in your bones, that these will not help you in what is coming. Instead you would spend the week in contemplation of the heart and recognizing the faults of your own soul. You would turn your face away from the world. You would purify the heart of what was wrong with it. You would embellish it with the love of Allah and with the beautiful inner qualities. You would get busy with God's worship and service. You might not even leave the house to visit anyone for the entire week, even though you would have no certain reason to believe that you would die on any given day.

The thought experiment is severe, and it is meant to be. Ghazali immediately gives a softer version that gets at the same thing. *If someone told you that next week the king will visit your house,* he says, *what would you do? You would spend the whole week not on any other work, but on the obsession that the king's glance should not fall on something dirty. You would clean everything. You would clean your house. You would clean your body and your dress. You would clean the walls and the floors.* Now think this through, Ghazali says. You are intelligent. The hint should be enough.

Because the Prophet has said:

> Verily, Allah does not look at your faces and your actions, but He looks at your hearts and your intentions.
> (Sahih Muslim, Book 45, Hadith 14.)

When the One who actually matters is looking at your heart and your intention, why would you spend the week before His glance on anything other than cleaning what He will see?

Ghazali sends his student back to *Ihya Ulum al-Din* (the Revival of the Religious Sciences) for the longer treatment of the inner knowledge that matters. He notes that acquiring this knowledge, the knowledge of the matters of the heart, is *fard al-ayn*, a personal obligation incumbent on every individual Muslim. To acquire the other branches of knowledge is *fard kifaya*, a communal obligation discharged when some members of the community attend to it on behalf of the rest. The student does not need all the knowledge. He needs enough to obey God's commands. If God grants him the capability, the *tawfeeq*, as Ghazali names it, to go further than the minimum, he should.

## The fourth method: do not stockpile food

The fourth method is the most ground-level of the four. Do not accumulate more than one year's worth of provision for your family from the goods of this world.

Ghazali grounds this directly in the Prophet's example. The Prophet accumulated one year's worth of provision for some of his wives, and prayed:

> O Allah, make this provision sufficient for the family of Muhammad.
> (Sahih Bukhari, Hadith 6460; Sahih Muslim, Hadith 7459.)

But, and Ghazali presses the point, the Prophet did not stockpile for all his wives. He stockpiled only for those whose reliance on God, whose *Tawakkul*, was still weak. For those whose reliance was strong and whose certitude in Allah was perfect, he did not accumulate even one day's worth. He names Aisha (may Allah be pleased with her) among the latter group. For Aisha and the other Mothers of the Believers in her station, no stockpile at all.

The principle is exact. The stockpile is calibrated to the weakness of the relier, not to the abundance available. You stockpile less in proportion as your *Tawakkul* grows. The one-year ceiling is the upper bound, not a target. The target is to need less and less of a buffer over time.

Stockpiling beyond a year, Ghazali implies, is the sign that you have stopped trusting Allah's promise of daily sustenance and started trusting your own accumulation. The fourth method is about catching this drift before it sets.

The Prophet names the inner state behind the practice:

> If you trusted in Allah with true reliance, He would provide for you as He provides for the birds. They leave their nests in the morning hungry and return in the evening full.
> (Sunan Tirmidhi, Hadith 2344.)

The birds leave the nest. They work. They do not, however, store grain for twenty years. The right amount of storage is the amount that matches the level of trust the person has reached. Less storage tracks more trust. Both are real. Neither one is performed.

## The supplication

At the end of the letter Ghazali addresses his student directly. *In this book, I have answered all of your questions. Now you should gather courage and act on them all. And do not forget me in your supplications.*

The student had asked for a supplication to recite. Ghazali first directs him to two older sources. Look in the six canonical hadith collections, *Sahih Sitta*, as Ghazali names them, the most authentic books of the Prophet's sayings, and memorize the supplications recorded there. Also look in the supplications that come down through the *Ahl al-Bayt*, the family of the Prophet. Many of the most beautiful supplications in the Islamic tradition come through that lineage, including those preserved in the *Sahifa al-Sajjadiyya* of Imam Zayn al-Abidin.

Then Ghazali gives him a supplication of his own. He says to recite it especially after every prayer, every Salah.

> O my Allah, I desire from You the completion and the abundance of Your blessings. From purity, inner and outer, I desire its permanence. From Your mercy, I desire its inclusion in all matters. From health, I desire its attainment. From sustenance, I desire its increase. From life, I desire its happiness and its real success. From good qualities, I desire their completion. From Your rewards, I desire the reward which is most common. From Your bounty, I desire the bounty which is sweetest. From Your favors, I desire the favor which is most plentiful. From deeds, I desire the deed which is best. From knowledge, I desire the knowledge which is most beneficial. From sustenance, I desire the sustenance which is most plentiful.

> O my Allah, be on our side. Do not burden us. Grant us salvation when we die. Reform our deeds. Grant us safety in Your days and nights. Grant us Your mercy in our houses and in our possessions. Cover our sins and our faults with the cover of Your forgiveness. Do us favors by correcting all our faults, inner and outer. Give us confidence and reliance on You.

> O my Allah, grant us perseverance in our path to You. Save us from such deeds in this world as will become a source of shame on the Day of Judgment. Lessen the burdens of our sins. Grant us a life of the pious ones. Be sufficient to us in all our affairs. Protect us from the evil of the evildoers. Liberate us and our ancestors from the fire, from debts, and from oppression.

> O Victorious One. O Forgiving One. O Generous One. O Coverer of faults. O Tolerant One. O Powerful One. O Magnificent One. O You Who repays. O Allah. O Allah. O Allah. O Compassionate One in this world. O Merciful One in the next life. O Most Merciful of the Merciful. You are the Granter of excessive mercy. And may the mercy and blessings of Allah always descend upon Muhammad, peace and blessings be upon him, who is the best of all creations, from eternity to eternity, and upon his descendants and his companions. All praise belongs to Allah, the Lord of all the Universes.

Then Ghazali closes the book.

> This book comes to an end with the help of the Absolute Sovereign and the Giver of all.

The supplication is a summary of the whole letter in the form of a request. It asks for the inner qualities the letter has named, sincerity, reliance, *taqwa*, mercy, the deed that is best, the knowledge that is most beneficial. It asks for safety from the very traps the earlier sections cautioned against. It asks for the right inner state, again, the one Ghazali has been pressing toward from the first page.

The supplication is also a structure for the student's daily life. After every prayer, recite this. The recitation will keep the letter alive in his mouth. The mouth will keep the inner orientation alive in his heart. The heart will, slowly, do the work the letter asked of it.

The letter does not promise that the work will be easy. The opening sections were clear: the path is hard, and most people who try it do not finish. What the closing promises is that the work, if undertaken, will produce what was promised. The seeker who acts on the four refusals and the four methods, who attaches to a real guide, who carries the supplication in his mouth after every prayer, will reach a place from which the original question, *which knowledge will save me?*, no longer needs to be asked. He will be living the answer.

That is what Ghazali has been writing toward, the whole way through.
