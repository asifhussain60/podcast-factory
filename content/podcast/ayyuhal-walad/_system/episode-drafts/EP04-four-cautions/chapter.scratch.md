# The Four Cautions

*From Ayyuhal Walad ("O Beloved Son") by Imam Abu Hamid Muhammad al-Ghazali. This chapter covers the first half of what Ghazali calls the Eight Admonitions: the four things you should not do. Religious debate, public preaching, keeping company with rulers, and accepting their gifts. Each one is a trap that scholars in particular fall into, and Ghazali tells his student to refuse all four.*

## The shape of the cautions

After laying out the inner architecture of the path, Ghazali turns to practical advice. He names eight admonitions in two halves. The first half, covered here, is the four things the student should not do, so that his knowledge does not become his enemy on the Day of Judgment.

He says it like that, very plainly, and it is worth pausing on. *So that your knowledge does not become your enemy.* In the rhetoric of the letter, knowledge is not neutral. The knowledge that does not serve as your defender on the Day of Judgment will testify against you. The four cautions in this chapter are the four most reliable ways a scholar's knowledge turns into his accuser.

Imam Abu Hamid Muhammad al-Ghazali knows the four traps because he has lived inside them. The autobiographical work he wrote alongside this letter, *Munqidh min al-Dalal* (Deliverance from Error), records his crisis in Baghdad: at the height of his career as a teacher of jurisprudence, surrounded by students and the favor of the Nizami court, he realized that what he was teaching was making him worse, not better. He left. The letter to his old student is written by a man who has seen what each of these four traps does from the inside.

## The first caution: do not enter religious debate

Ghazali tells his student to avoid religious debate as much as possible. Not to argue with anyone on any issue, religious or non-religious, because there are many hidden harms in it.

He names the harms exactly. Debate is the fountainhead of ostentation, jealousy, arrogance, resentment, enmity, pride, and egoism. The man who debates discovers that he wants to win. The wanting-to-win then quietly reshapes his relationship to truth. Truth becomes the thing his words point to, instead of the thing his life points to.

Ghazali allows one carve-out. If a debate begins between you and another person, and you genuinely want the truth to prevail, yours or theirs, it does not matter, then your intention is correct. The signs that your intention is correct are two. First, if the truth comes out of the other person's mouth instead of yours, you are satisfied either way. Second, you prefer to have this conversation in private rather than in front of an audience.

If both signs are present, the dialogue can proceed. If the second sign is absent, if you would rather have an audience for the exchange, that is the giveaway. The wanting-to-win has shown up.

> If you argue or debate on an issue and you are certain that you are correct and the opponent is only arguing for the sake of arguing, beware. Do not argue with him, and end the discussion right there. Otherwise grudges and resentments will be born from it, and no benefit will be achieved.

The Prophet Muhammad, peace and blessings of Allah be upon him, named this same principle from another angle:

> I guarantee a house in the outskirts of paradise for one who leaves arguments, even if he is right, and a house in the middle of paradise for one who abandons lying even when jesting, and a house in the upper part of paradise for one who has good character.
> (Sunan Abu Dawud, Hadith 4800.)

The guarantee is for the man who leaves arguments *even when he is right*. Especially when he is right, since that is when the temptation to debate is greatest.

## Categories of patients: who can be helped

Ghazali then introduces an analogy that organizes the rest of his thinking about debate, preaching, and counsel. Asking a question about a spiritual matter, he says, is like coming to a doctor and describing your illness. Answering the question is like the doctor's effort to cure you.

Some patients can be cured. Some cannot. He names four categories. Three are incurable. One is the one to which advice should be given.

The first kind of patient asks questions out of jealousy. He does not want an answer. He wants to expose the teacher as inadequate. Whatever answer you give him, he will treat as proof that you are his enemy, and his envy will be inflamed further. There is a couplet Ghazali quotes here, of unclear authorship in the original:

> Every animosity can be corrected, except the animosity born from jealousy. That one cannot be undone.

The cure is not engagement. The cure is to leave such a person alone with his illness. The Quran names the same disengagement plainly:

> So turn away from the one who turns away from Our remembrance and desires nothing but the life of this world.
> (Quran, An-Najm 53:29; English: Yusuf Ali. Subsequent Quranic translations in this chapter follow the same translator.)

The second kind of patient is the one whose disease is lack of intelligence and foolishness. Ghazali quotes a saying attributed to the Prophet Isa, peace be upon him: *I am not, by Allah's permission, incapable of bringing the dead back to life. But I am incapable of curing the foolish.* The foolish person Ghazali is naming here is not someone with low natural intelligence. It is the person who has barely studied, has perhaps cracked open one book and skimmed a few pages, and who, on that thin basis, objects to scholars who have spent their whole lives in the work. His objection is not made out of love of truth. It is made out of an inability to see how far he is from the depth he is criticizing. Such a person cannot be cured, because the disease is not in his knowledge but in his self-estimate. Stay away from him. Do not respond.

The third kind of patient is the one whose problem is restlessness and impatience. He hears the teaching of the spiritually elevated, but he does not understand it. He understands it only according to what he wants it to mean. He turns every saying into a confirmation of his existing position. Ghazali says this kind of person should also not be given an answer, citing the saying of the Prophet:

> Our group of prophets have been ordered by Allah to address people according to the level of their understanding.
> (A saying recorded in the early Sufi corpus; cited by Ghazali here.)

If the listener's understanding is too narrow to receive the truth, the truth will be misrepresented when it is given to him.

The fourth kind of patient is the one who can be helped. He is seeking the straight path. He is obedient, sharp, and intelligent. He is not jealous of his teacher. He is not in a hurry. He asks questions because he wants to understand. Ghazali says: *To answer the questions of this person is not just legitimate. It is obligatory.*

The four categories are not just diagnostic. They are a permission structure for the scholar's silence. Most people who come to a scholar with a religious question are not the fourth kind. The scholar is allowed, required, even, to refuse to engage with the first three. The student who absorbs this lesson saves himself the harms Ghazali named at the start of this section.

## The second caution: do not become a preacher

Ghazali tells his student to refrain from preaching and giving sermons. The reason is not that public teaching is wrong in principle. The reason is that almost everyone who steps onto the pulpit does so for the wrong reasons, and the cost of doing so for the wrong reasons is enormous.

He starts with a verse that the Quran attributes to Allah's address to the Prophet Isa:

> O son of Mary, admonish your own self first. If it accepts the advice, then advise others. Otherwise, have shame before Me.

The Prophet Isa is named here because his model is the one Ghazali wants. Begin with yourself. If you cannot admonish your own soul successfully, do not pretend to admonish others.

If a situation does arise that requires the student to preach, Ghazali gives him two boundaries.

The first is to refrain from ornament. The sermon should not be decorated with colorful narration, secretive gestures, flowery language, pleasing poetry, talk that violates the Shari'ah, or lies of false Sufis. Allah does not like the pretentious. If the sermon exceeds the boundaries of pretense, if the preacher is openly trying to impress others with eloquence, then the preacher's heart is bad. His heart is heedless of God's remembrance.

The purpose of the sermon, Ghazali says, is not to display the preacher's capabilities. The purpose is to mention the punishment of the next life. To narrate the shortcomings of the listener's servitude to God. To express regret over the wasted time. To name the difficult stations of the next life that lie in everyone's path. To remind the audience of the spectacle of death, when the Angel of Death arrives, and the questioning in the grave by the two angels assigned to that task, and the assembly on the Day of Judgment, and the weighing of deeds on the great Scale, and the bridge over hell that all human beings must cross.

These are not topics for a clever speech. They are topics for a man trying to wake another man up.

Ghazali gives an image. *If the water from a flood reaches the door of someone's house, threatening to engulf the house and drown his children, at that moment, the owner of the house will scream, "Beware! Beware! Run! The flood water has arrived!"* He will not say it in flowery sentences or in embellished poetry. He will say it in the words of imminent danger. *The example of the sermon should be like this, to have fear oneself, and to instill that same fear in others, with the urgency of an impending disaster.*

The second boundary is the inner one. The preacher must not, while preaching, entertain the thought that the audience will praise him afterward. He must not desire that they will tear their clothes in ecstasy, raise an uproar, or report to others that the gathering was good and that so-and-so gave a great sermon. These thoughts are pure ostentation. Being pleased by such things is proof that the preacher lacks discernment.

The right intention, in Ghazali's words, is to summon God's creatures from this world to the next. To summon them from sins to the servitude of God, from greed to detachment, from miserliness to generosity, from ostentation to sincerity, from arrogance to humility, from heedlessness to consciousness, from pride to piety. To instill in their hearts the love of the next life and detachment from this one. To create in those dominated by fear so much hope that some inner quality is born in them by the time they stand up to leave. To make the lazy worshipper devoted, and the bold sinner afraid of God.

A sermon that does not produce these effects is a calamity for the preacher and for the audience. Such a preacher, Ghazali says, becomes a Satan on the pulpit, *misguiding creations from the path of true guidance, shedding their blood, involving them in eternal destruction*. The corruption such a preacher spreads is greater than what Satan himself spreads. Anyone with the power to remove him from the pulpit is obligated to do so.

Hasan al-Basri, the early Muslim ascetic of Basra, said something that names this same warning from his own side of the pulpit:

> The signs of the hypocrite are three: his tongue does not match his heart, his heart does not match his deeds, and his outward does not match his inward. Beware of the preacher whose three are out of joint.
> Source: Attributed to Hasan al-Basri, recorded in *Riyad al-Salihin*, Hadith 7 (introductory commentary on hypocrisy).

The match must hold all the way through. If it fails at any joint, what is preached will not save anyone.

## The third caution: stay away from rulers

The third thing the student should avoid is the company of kings, the affluent, and rulers in general. Ghazali is direct here. Do not greet them. Do not enter their gatherings. Do not even lift your eye to look at them. The harm hidden in their proximity is enormous, and most people who enter their company do not recover.

If, by some accident, you do end up in such a company, Ghazali gives one rule: do not praise them.

> Verily, Allah, the Exalted, is angry when a transgressor or an oppressor is praised. And the one who prays for the long life of an oppressor, it is as if he liked that this man would walk on the earth as a sinner.
> (A saying recorded in the early *Adab al-Mufrad* tradition of al-Bukhari; cited by Ghazali here.)

This is the corollary of the caution about preaching. Praising the powerful is preaching in reverse. It is not telling them what they need to hear; it is telling them what they want to hear. Doing so makes the praiser complicit in whatever the praised one will do next.

Imam Ali ibn Abi Talib framed the same caution from another angle, in a saying recorded in *Nahj al-Balagha*:

> The companion of a tyrant is the tyrant's accomplice. There is no neutrality at his table.
> Source: Imam Ali (AS), recorded in *Ghurar al-Hikam* §4310.

You cannot sit close to power without absorbing its shape. The only safe distance is real distance.

## The fourth caution: do not accept gifts from rulers

The fourth caution follows directly from the third. Do not accept gifts from rulers, even when the source of what they are giving is technically permitted by the Shari'ah. The reason is not that the gift itself is forbidden. The reason is what the gift does to the receiver's heart.

To have any expectation upon the rulers' property, Ghazali says, causes great damage and turmoil in the path of God. The concessions and rewards that flow from rulers give birth to oppression, transgression, and sin, all of which damage the path of God. The least corruption that is born of accepting their gifts is that the receiver begins to love the giver. And when a person loves another, he prays for the giver's long life. If an oppressor has a long life, the oppression continues longer, and there is more turmoil in the world. What could be worse than that?

Ghazali warns his student that Satan will whisper a clever-sounding objection in his heart: *Take the money from these rulers and then distribute it to the poor. Bring them some relief.* Ghazali says: never accept this advice from any Satan, whether from among the *jinn* or from among human beings. Do not get trapped in their machinations. Satan has used this strategy to spill the blood of many, and he continues to use it.

The further harms of this caution Ghazali addresses in his book *Ihya Ulum al-Din* (the Revival of the Religious Sciences). He tells his student to look there for the fuller treatment.

The principle running through all four cautions is the same. The trap closes when the scholar, the preacher, or the student begins to operate inside the value-system of the people he is supposed to be guiding. He starts to debate because the people around him admire debaters. He starts to preach because the people around him reward preachers. He starts to sit with rulers because the people around him are impressed by his proximity to power. He accepts their gifts because the people around him judge a man by the gifts he can pull from above. Each step looks small. Together they remake the man.

Ghazali is asking his student to refuse the value-system before the trap closes. Not because the four activities are equally bad. Because the four activities are equally good at corrupting the heart that was supposed to be doing the work that none of them is.
