# The Frame and the First Counsel

*From Ayyuhal Walad ("O Beloved Son") by Imam Abu Hamid Muhammad al-Ghazali. English translation by Irfan Hasan, from the Urdu rendering of the Arabic original (compiled within Majmu'a Rasail Imam Ghazali). This chapter covers the framing letter from one of Ghazali's former students and Ghazali's first long answer, which sets the central claim the rest of the treatise will defend: knowledge that you do not act on will not save you.*

## The letter that triggered everything

A student who had spent years studying with Imam Abu Hamid Muhammad al-Ghazali (may Allah have mercy upon him) wrote his teacher a letter. He had been a serious student. He had collected branches of religious knowledge from him at considerable cost, and he could no longer settle a question that had begun to keep him up at night.

Of all the things he had studied, which would actually help him? Which would illumine his grave? Which would carry him through the Day of Judgment? And which were so useless that he should walk away from them now, before he ran out of time? He remembered the words of the Prophet Muhammad, peace and blessings of Allah be upon him: *"I seek Allah's refuge from the knowledge which is of no benefit."*

He turned over the question for several days. Then he wrote. He asked Ghazali to send him an answer, a piece of practical advice he could rest his life on, and a supplication he could carry with him every day. In passing, he acknowledged Ghazali's earlier work, naming *Ihya Ulum al-Din* (the Revival of the Religious Sciences), *Kimiya al-Sa'ada* (the Alchemy of Happiness), *Jawahir al-Quran* (Gems of the Quran), *Arba'in* (Forty Steps), and *Minhaj al-Abidin* (the Path of Worshippers). He had read them. He was not asking out of ignorance. He was a man with a library, asking for a sentence.

What he received in reply is this letter, *Ayyuhal Walad*. *O beloved son.* What follows is its first long counsel.

## All counsel is rooted in the Prophet's example

Ghazali opens in the only way someone of his training could open: with praise of God, blessings on the Prophet, and a statement that the foundation of all useful counsel is the example of the Prophet, his sayings and his conduct, both outward and inward. Any advice that pushes against that foundation will not help you, no matter how clever it sounds.

So before he answers, he asks his student a counter-question. *In all these years, what knowledge did you actually take from me?* If you already have what the Prophet's example offers, you do not need my letter. If you do not, then nothing I write will substitute for going back to the source.

Among the Prophet's sayings, Ghazali quotes one as the keynote:

> A servant of Allah's involvement in useless things is an indication that Allah has removed His special favors from him. And if the servant is spending even a moment from his life on things other than what Allah created him for, then it is a matter of great sorrow.

The danger is not that you will fail at hard religion. The danger is that you will spend your hours on what does not feed you, and you will not even notice the loss until it is too late to recover.

He follows with a second saying:

> An individual whose state, even after forty years of age, is such that good in him does not prevail over evil in him, then this individual should get ready for the Fire.

Forty is not arbitrary. It is the age at which, the tradition holds, a person is no longer growing into himself, no longer building. After forty, what you are is what you have become.

## The first hard truth: knowledge without action does not save you

Ghazali names the trap directly. It is easy to give this advice to other people. It is hard to take it yourself. The reason is simple: the heart that has fallen in love with this world finds the truth bitter. The man who has built his reputation around what he knows cannot bear to be told that what he knows is not enough. He tells himself, *Yes, but in my case, the knowledge itself is the work.* Ghazali calls this the special delusion of those who study philosophy and theology and never live by what they teach.

He stacks the verses on each other so that none of them can be argued away.

> And so, the one who shall have done an atom's weight of good shall see it, and the one who shall have done an atom's weight of evil shall see it.
> (Quran, Az-Zalzalah 99:7-8; English translation: Yusuf Ali. Subsequent Quranic translations in this chapter follow the same translator.)

> So whoever hopes for the meeting with his Lord, let him work righteousness, and in the worship of his Lord, admit no one as partner.
> (Quran, Al-Kahf 18:110)

> Verily, those who believe and do righteous deeds, theirs are the Gardens of Paradise for an abode.
> (Quran, Al-Kahf 18:107)

The pattern is unmistakable. The Quran does not separate believing from doing. The two are bound. Ghazali also reminds his student that the Prophet defined faith with the same architecture:

> Faith is to testify with the tongue, to certify with the heart, and to act upon the limbs.

Knowing is not enough. Saying is not enough. Acting is what proves the rest.

A subtle student will protest at this point: surely we enter paradise by Allah's mercy, not by our deeds. Ghazali agrees. Paradise is a gift. But, he says, you do not become *eligible* for the gift until you have made yourself worthy of receiving it. He cites:

> Verily, the Mercy of Allah is close to the doers of good.
> (Quran, Al-A'raf 7:56)

The mercy is the cause. The deeds are how you come within reach.

## The lion in the desert, the medicine on the shelf

He gives two images so concrete that no listener can miss them.

Imagine a man crossing a wilderness, armed with ten sharp swords and the finest bow, fully trained in their use. A lion appears on the path. Will the man be saved? Of course not, unless he draws a weapon. Knowing the art of war does not protect you from the lion. Drawing the sword does.

Now imagine a sick man who has read every medical book ever written. He knows the exact prescription that will heal him. The bottle is on his shelf. Will the knowledge of the cure cure him? Only the medicine cures.

> Acquiring a lot of knowledge and continually browsing a lot of books but not acting on that knowledge will not benefit you unless and until you make yourself deserving of Allah's mercy through deeds of righteousness.

This is the entire argument of the treatise in one line. Everything that follows in the letter, the night prayer, the inner stations, the cautions, the four matters, is the unfolding of this same claim.

## The angel and the worshipper

Ghazali tells a small story to land the point about intention. Once there was a man among the Children of Israel who worshipped Allah constantly. Allah sent an angel to test him. The angel said: *Your worship is not accepted. You are going to the Fire.* The man answered, *Allah, the Exalted, has created us for His worship. Our job is to serve Him. He is the Owner. He is the Powerful One.*

The angel returned and reported the man's words. Allah replied:

> If this slave of Ours does not turn his face away from Our worship, then We will not turn Our Face away from him. O My angels, all of you, be My witnesses that I have forgiven him.

The worshipper had no expectation of return. He worshipped because the worship was the right shape of his life, not because it bought him anything. That is the inner posture Ghazali is naming. The man whose deeds are transactional collapses the moment he is told the deal is off. The man whose deeds are obedient continues.

## The first question at the grave

He intensifies the pressure. He cites a tradition that says when the corpse is placed in the grave, Allah asks the soul forty questions. The first one is this:

> O My slave, you decorated yourself with knowledge of outward matters for many years to show off to My creations. But for My sake alone, did you ever purify your heart, even for a moment?

This is the question that all the showing-off was a hedge against. The Prophet said in an authentic tradition: *Allah does not look at your faces or your bodies, but He looks at your hearts.* Imam Ali ibn Abi Talib, peace be upon him, said the same shape another way:

> A person who thinks he will reach paradise without striving is deluded. A person who thinks he will reach paradise by striving alone is exhausting himself.
> Source: Imam Ali (AS), recorded in *Nahj al-Balagha*, Aphorism 90.

The middle path between the two is the path of the man who strives and trusts.

> The reality of knowledge is that one acts on it. To desire paradise without good deeds is a sin from among the sins.
> Source: Attributed to Hasan al-Basri.

Ghazali quotes Hasan al-Basri here because Hasan's authority is older than his own and the desert simplicity of the line cuts through the elaborations of his student's philosophy lectures.

## Madness, sadness, blessing

Ghazali then sharpens the contrast. *Knowledge without actions is madness, and actions without knowledge are useless.* The knowledge that does not pull you away from sin today will not save you from the Fire tomorrow.

He addresses the student directly. *You stayed awake at night, you sacrificed sleep, to memorize knowledge.* If you did it for the world, for status and reputation:

> There is sadness for you, and again there is sadness for you.

But if you did it to establish the Path of God in yourself first and then in others, to lift your own character, and to root out evil:

> For you is the happiness, and again for you is the happiness.

The same long nights buy two different outcomes. Intention decides which.

## The dove that knows

He widens the lens to the night itself. The Prophet lifted up the night prayer, Tahajjud, as the practice that distinguishes the sincere from the merely active. Allah Himself, Ghazali reminds his student, addresses the Prophet in the Quran:

> And in the last part of the night perform the Tahajjud prayer, an additional offering for you.
> (Quran, Al-Isra 17:79)

And of His true servants:

> They were in the habit of sleeping but little by night, and in the hours of early dawn they were found praying for forgiveness.
> (Quran, Adh-Dhariyat 51:17-18)

Sufyan al-Thawri, the early jurist-ascetic of Kufa, said that Allah created a wind that blows in the last part of the night, and that this wind carries the prayers of those who are awake at that hour up to the throne of God. Sufyan also said that four angels make four proclamations across the night: the first at nightfall, calling worshippers to rise; the second at the midpoint, calling the obedient; the third near dawn, calling those who seek forgiveness; the fourth at the day's break, calling out to the heedless, *Get up! The day has come!* And then, Sufyan said, those people rise from their beds the way the dead will rise from their graves on the Day of Resurrection.

Ghazali closes this beat with the rooster, recalling the will Luqman left to his son. *My son, do not let the rooster be wiser than you. He gets up in the last part of the night and calls out, while you keep sleeping.* He follows with a couplet:

> At night, the dove on the branch is calling to her Lord, while I am in a deep sleep. By the Lord of the House, if I had truly been a lover, the doves would not have outpaced me in weeping. I claim to be in love, yet I do not even wet my eyes, while the animals weep for their Creator.

The animal that has not been entrusted with knowledge keeps vigil. The scholar who has been entrusted with knowledge sleeps. The shame is structural.

## Worship means obedience, not display

What is worship, finally? Ghazali defines it bluntly. Worship is following the Shari'ah of the Prophet. Doing or not doing, speaking or not speaking, every act takes its shape from the Prophet's example.

He gives examples sharp enough that no listener can blur them. Fasting on one of the two Eid days is a sin, even though fasting is normally worship, because the Prophet forbade it. Praying at a forbidden time, or on illegally occupied land, makes the person a sinner even though he is performing the form of prayer. On the other hand, intimacy between a husband and wife is not a sin, even though to an outside eye it looks ordinary, because the Prophet's example permits it.

The form of an act does not determine whether it is worship. The conformity of the act to the Prophet's example does.

> Therefore, you cannot walk on the Path of Allah merely with the knowledge you have acquired. This Path must be traveled through hard work and Mujahadah.

*Mujahadah*, the inner spiritual struggle, is Ghazali's word for the cost. It is not metaphorical. He calls it a sword that cuts the lower desires from the heart. Without it, what comes out of your mouth is just the language of the Sufis, which the false ones learn to mimic. The true Sufis are those whose souls have been purified by struggle until the love of God has room to take up residence. The false ones speak the same words while their hearts continue to worship heedlessness.

> The tongue speaking the words of deep wisdom while the heart worships heedlessness and lower desire, this is a sign of mighty misfortune.

## Some things cannot be written

His student has asked about advanced spiritual states, and Ghazali pauses. Some of what you are asking, he says, cannot be conveyed in writing or speech. When you reach the station, you will know it from inside. The lesson of love is not taught. It is born.

He likens it to the taste of sweetness or sourness. You cannot describe sweet to someone who has never tasted it. The description fails. So with the higher stations of love and longing. If you reach them, you will know. If you do not, no amount of writing will get you there.

He directs his student to his other works, *Ihya Ulum al-Din* among them, for the answers that can be written, and then he turns to what is left.

## The four conditions of the seeker

The student had asked, *What is essential for those walking on the Path of Allah?* Ghazali gives him four conditions to start with, and then later three more, and the seventh lands the section.

The first condition is *correct belief without innovation*, without *bid'a*, that is, beliefs and practices that depart from what is established in Quran and Sunnah.

The second is *true repentance*, of the kind that does not loop back to the same sin.

The third is *to satisfy every grievance against you*, no person has a claim on you that you have not addressed, because on the Day of Judgment those claims will be presented.

The fourth is *to learn enough of the Shari'ah* to obey what God commands, no more than that. Knowledge beyond that minimum is not obligatory on every individual. It is a *fard kifaya*, a communal obligation that some people in the community discharge on behalf of the rest. The seeker does not need a library. He needs enough to know what God asks of him today.

To press the point, Ghazali tells the story of Shibli. Shibli said: *I served four hundred spiritual masters and I memorized four thousand sayings of the Prophet from them, and then I acted on only one of those four thousand, because when I deliberated on it I found my salvation in it and the knowledge of all those who came before and all those who came after.* The one hadith was this:

> Work for this world in proportion to how long you will live in it. Work for the next life in proportion to how long you will live there. Work for Allah in proportion to how much you need Him. Work for the Fire in proportion to how much you can bear its pain.

Each clause cancels the one before it. You will not live long in the world. You will live forever in the next. You need God in every breath. You cannot bear the Fire for a single moment. The geometry of the saying tells you, without any further argument, where your life should pour itself.

## What the letter is doing

Notice what Ghazali has not yet done. He has not told his student to abandon his books. He has not told him to leave the city, or to enter a Sufi order, or to take any specific outward step. He has done one thing only: he has put the central claim in front of his student so squarely that the rest of the treatise can do its work. *The knowledge you have will not save you. Acting on it might. The acting is hard. The acting is what we have been avoiding by reading.*

Everything that follows, the eight benefits of Haatim, the inner architecture of the path, the four cautions about debate and preaching and rulers and gifts, the four methods of living, the closing prayer, is held together by this one beam. The student wrote because he was afraid he had wasted his life. Ghazali is answering, *You might have. Here is what to do about it.*
