# Episode 5 — The Path: Guide, Discipline, and Inner Reality

The student had asked Ghazali several questions about the spiritual path. This episode is Ghazali's answer to four of them, gathered: who can guide a seeker, what discipline the seeker owes the guide, what the discipline of the path actually is, and what the inner realities look like once the seeker is on it. Ghazali presents these as four pillars. Take any one of them out and the whole structure stops standing.

## Pillar One — The qualities of a spiritual guide

The heir of the Prophet, peace and blessings be upon him, who is to become a seeker's Shaykh, must be a scholar — but Ghazali is careful here. Every scholar is not yet a Shaykh al-Kaa-mil, a perfected spiritual guide. The work of guiding a soul is not the work of explaining doctrine. It requires specific qualities, and without them the title is empty.

Ghazali names the qualities so that no every fool and misguided person can claim the role. A real Shaykh, he says, is one who has turned his face away from the love of the world — from the desire for honor and the desire for worldly rank. He has given the Bay-a, the spiritual oath, on the hand of his own perfected guide; that guide gave the Bay-a to his guide; and the chain runs back, link by link, all the way to the Prophet. This chain is what authenticates the work. Without it, the seeker is following someone who is following no one.

Beyond the chain, the Shaykh must have lived the practice. Ri-yaa-da — spiritual exertion. Mu-jaa-ha-da — inner struggle. He must obey every commandment of the Prophet. He must eat less than his appetite demands, sleep less than his body asks for, pray the obligatory prayers and add the voluntary ones, fast more than the calendar requires, spend more than his comfort allows in charity. His character must carry the visible marks of long discipline — patience, gratitude, certitude in Allah, generosity, contentment, trustworthiness, tolerance, seriousness of purpose, humility, truthfulness, modesty, dignity, tranquility. And from him the evil qualities must have been driven out: stinginess, jealousy, enmity, expectation from the world, anger, rebellion.

That is the standard. Anyone short of it may still be a scholar; he is not a Shaykh in the sense Ghazali means.

## Pillar Two — Obedience to a spiritual guide

Such a Shaykh is very hard to find. But if a seeker is given this wealth — if such a guide accepts him among his disciples — then a corresponding discipline is owed in return. The guide is not a friend; he is a doctor for the soul. Without obedience, the medicine does nothing.

Ghazali divides the discipline into two: the outer etiquettes and the inner ones.

### The outer etiquettes

The seeker does not argue with the Shaykh. If a debate begins, and the seeker believes the master has forgotten something, the seeker does not press it. He does not stand permanently in the place of prayer — he goes to prayer when prayer is due, and when he is done, he rolls up his mat. He does not perform mountains of voluntary prayer in front of the Shaykh after each obligatory prayer; that is performance, not worship. Whatever the master commands within his capacity, the seeker obeys — with this critical limit, which Ghazali makes explicit: a real Shaykh will never command a thing forbidden by the Sha-ree-ah. If a guide ever does, he is by that command revealed to be one of the impersonators, and the seeker is freed of obligation to him.

### The inner etiquettes

The inner discipline is harder. Whatever the seeker hears from his Mur-shid — the guide — he does not allow even a grain of inner doubt to take hold about the master's words or deeds. If he cannot manage this — if his inside cannot match his outside — then he should withdraw from the master's company until it can. Hypocritical discipleship corrupts the seeker faster than no discipleship at all.

Beyond doubt, the seeker must guard against the deceptions of the Nafs, the lower self. The Nafs whispers evil into the heart, and the only way to silence the whispers is to leave the company of bad-character people and ignorant people. Once such company is left, Shay-taan's grip loosens, and the Satanic influences — from human or from Jinn — fall away at the root.

And finally, in every circumstance, humility over pride, contentment over greed, satisfaction with what Allah has decreed over the chase for wealth. These seven inner conditions — no doubt, no hypocrisy, no bad company, no Nafs-deception, no lust for wealth, humility, contentment — are essential for the traveler on the path.

## Pillar Three — The discipline of Tasawwuf

You asked, what is Ta-saw-wuf? Ta-saw-wuf — what the world today often calls Sufism, and what the Prophet, peace and blessings be upon him, called Ih-saan, spiritual excellence — rests on two qualities.

The first is obedience to Allah. The seeker acts on the Sha-ree-ah: he keeps its obligations, follows its commandments, observes its prohibitions. Without this outer discipline, there is no inner path.

The second is mercy toward Allah's creation. The seeker is sympathetic to every living thing and active in their good. This is not possible until the soul has been purified of its evil qualities and then adorned with the good ones. A heart still ruled by envy, pride, or greed cannot be sincerely useful to anyone else.

A Soo-fee is the one in whom both qualities hold together — perseverance in the Sha-ree-ah, and real desire for the welfare of all humanity. That is the term used correctly. Most of what passes today under the name has nothing to do with it.

Loyalty to Allah means setting aside one's own pleasure for the sake of God. Sincerity toward people means relating to them without private motive — never using a relationship to serve oneself, but giving oneself to the good of every person one meets. With one condition that cannot be removed: this giving must remain inside the bounds of the Sha-ree-ah. A surgeon is not free to operate by intuition alone, however good her intentions; she is bound by the discipline of her training. The seeker who serves humanity is bound, in the same way, by the law that defines what real service is.

## Pillar Four — The inner realities

The fourth pillar is the inner anatomy of the path — three answers to three of the student's questions.

### Servitude

You asked, what is servitude? Servitude has three components. First, obey the commandments of the Sha-ree-ah, the outer law. Second, be content with whatever Allah has decreed for you — your share of fortune and your share of difficulty. Third, set aside your own desires and preferences, and let what Allah desires and prefers be enough. The three together make the slave a slave in the spiritual sense Ghazali means — not subjugated, but aligned.

### Reliance — Tawakkul

You asked, what is Ta-wak-kul, reliance on God? Ta-wak-kul is firm certainty in what Allah has promised. Whatever Allah has decreed for you, you will receive — and if the entire world tries to stop it, it cannot be stopped. Whatever is not in your decree, you and the world together cannot achieve it. Ta-wak-kul is not passivity; the seeker still acts. Ta-wak-kul is the conviction that the outcome of one's action is not, in the end, the seeker's department. It is Allah's. The seeker does what he can; the result is decreed.

### Sincerity — Ikhlas

You asked, what is Ikh-laas, sincerity? Ikh-laas is that every deed you do is exclusively for the sake of Allah and His pleasure. No deed for show. No deed for praise. When you do good, your heart does not lean toward the people watching you do it, hoping to impress them. The heart is not lifted by praise; the heart is not dropped by complaint.

The diagnostic for Ri-yaa, showing off, is exactly this: from where do praises come? They come from people. The cure, then, is to see all created beings as subservient to Allah's power — to see them, in the matter of one's spiritual standing, as no more capable of awarding or withholding than pebbles and stones. A stone cannot grieve you. A stone cannot comfort you. People can do neither if you stop investing them with the power to. Until the seeker reaches this view, Ri-yaa will never leave his heart. After he reaches it, Ri-yaa has nothing to feed on.

## Closing the four pillars

Ghazali ends this section of the letter with a gentle remark to his disciple. Some of the student's remaining questions, he says, have been answered in the books already written; some, it is forbidden to write the answer to. Whatever has been written, put it into practice — the practice itself will clarify what the words alone cannot.

And if difficulties remain after that, do not ask anyone about them other than with the tongue of one's heart. He cites the Quran twice — first the verse about those who shouted outside the Prophet's house, who would have done better to wait until he came out; second the counsel of Khi-dir to Moses, peace be upon them both: do not question me about anything until I mention it to you. Do not hurry. When the time comes, you yourself will be shown.

The four pillars are not in series. They stand together. The guide without obedience does nothing. Obedience without the discipline of Ta-saw-wuf becomes mere imitation. The discipline of Ta-saw-wuf without the inner realities of servitude, reliance, and sincerity becomes a husk. And the inner realities have no place to grow without a guide. Each pillar requires the others. The seeker does not enter the path through one and the rest later. He enters all four at once.
