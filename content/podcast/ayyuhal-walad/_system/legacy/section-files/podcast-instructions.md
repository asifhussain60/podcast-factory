# Podcast Instructions — *Ayyuhal Walad — My Dear Beloved Son (or Daughter)*

**Source:** *Ayyuhal Walad*, written by Imam Abu Hamid Muhammad Al-Ghazali (1058-1111 CE) near the end of his life. Translated into English by Irfan Hasan from an Urdu translation of the Arabic original.

**Series shape:** 22 sections. Each section is its own NotebookLM Audio Overview episode. The combined runtime of all 22 episodes will form the complete podcast series. For per-episode focus, voice guidance, and pronunciation hooks, see the corresponding `section-NN-*.instructions.md` file alongside this one.

**Hosts' overall mission:** convey Ghazali's letter to a listener who has not read it — preserve the reverent tone, the instructional weight, and the argumentative precision of the original, while keeping the conversation grounded in the chapter content rather than drifting into general Islamic history or biographical asides.

## Global anti-noise rules — apply to every episode

1. **Stay on the chapter content.** Do not drift into general Islamic history, Ghazali's biography beyond what the chapter mentions, comparative theology, or modern politics. If a tangent would last more than ten seconds, cut it.

2. **Never abbreviate honorifics.** When you encounter "(SAW)," "(RA)," "(AS)" or similar, speak the full English form — "peace and blessings be upon him," "may Allah shower His mercy upon him," "peace be upon him." Speak them once per chapter on first occurrence per named figure, then naturally as needed.

3. **Speak transliterations from the pronunciation guide.** Use the phonetic spellings in `03-pronunciation.md`, not the source's looser transliteration. Pair each term with its English gloss on first appearance per episode; thereafter, speak the term alone.

4. **Quranic verses and hadith are landmarks, not decoration.** When a Quranic transliteration or hadith appears in the refined text, deliver it in full transliteration first, then the English meaning. Do not paraphrase the original around it.

5. **Do not invent supporting material.** Do not add scholars, citations, dates, or anecdotes that are not in the refined section text. If a host wants to introduce a parallel, it must be a modern analogy already present in the refined text — not a fresh citation.

6. **Hold the tone the section was written in.** Reverent sections stay reverent — no familiar address to Allah, the Prophet, or the Imams. Argumentative sections preserve premises and conclusions — do not soften "Waa-jib" (obligatory) into "recommended." Narrative sections preserve scene and dialogue — do not summarize away movement.

7. **The questions hosts ask each other should come from the section.** Frame questions around the actual content. Avoid generic interview questions ("So what does this mean for us today?") unless the answer is grounded in something the section actually says.

8. **End where the section ends.** Do not over-wrap with a meta-commentary about what Ghazali was trying to do, or what the next episode will cover. The closing line of the refined section is the end of the episode.
