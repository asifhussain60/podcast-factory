# Section 13 — Admonition One: The Principles of Debate

The first admonition is this. As far as you are able, do not enter into religious debates with anyone, and do not argue on any matter, religious or otherwise. The harms hidden inside argument far outweigh whatever good might come of it. Argument is the spring from which most evils flow: showing off — what is called Ri-yaa — and from there, jealousy, arrogance, resentment, enmity, pride, egoism, and the rest.

There is, however, a narrow case where argument may be legitimate. If a dispute begins between you and another person, and your only desire is that the truth should prevail, then your intention to argue may be correct. You can know whether your intention is in fact correct by two signs.

The first sign is that it makes no difference to you whether the truth comes out of your mouth or out of your opponent's. Either way, you are content, because in both cases the truth has been revealed.

The second sign is that you would rather have the conversation in private than in front of an audience. The desire for spectators is the desire for victory, not the desire for truth.

Even when both signs are present, there is a further test. If during the discussion you become certain that you are right and your opponent is only arguing for the sake of arguing, stop. End the discussion at that point. Nothing further will be gained, and grudges and resentments will be born from it.

Here a benefit needs to be explained.

Asking questions about religious and spiritual matters is like a patient describing the diseases of his heart, and their causes, to a doctor who specializes in the heart. The doctor's effort to cure those diseases is the answer he gives to the questions. Ignorant people, then, are patients with sickness in their hearts. True scholars are the physicians. Imperfect scholars cannot cure these diseases. Only the perfected scholar — the Shaykh al-Kaa-mil — can. The perfected scholar can find the root of the disease and treat it. If the disease has progressed beyond him, an expert physician must be consulted, one who can also say honestly that a particular disease cannot be cured by any medicine at all. To spend one's time trying to cure an incurable disease is wasted time.

Understand, then, that ignorant patients fall into four categories. Of the four, only one is curable. The other three are not.

**The first patient — the jealous one.** This is the one who asks questions not to learn but out of envy, or in order to object. Jealousy is among the most lethal diseases of the heart, and it has no cure. Whatever answer you give him, no matter how good, he will treat you as an enemy. The fire of his envy will be further fed by your reply. Better, then, not to answer. The poet has said:

"Kul-lul A-da-waa-ti Qad Tur-jaa I-zaa-la-tu-haa
Il-laa A-daa-wa-ta Mun Aa-daa-ka Min Ha-sa-din."

"Every kind of animosity can be corrected, except the animosity born of jealousy. That one cannot be rectified."

The remedy, then, is to leave the jealous person alone with his disease, exactly as Allah commands in the Quran:

"Fa A-rid Um Mun Ta-wal-la An Zik-ri-na Wa-lam Yu-rid Il-lal Ha-yaa-tad Dun-yaa."

"So turn away from the one who turns his face from Our remembrance and desires nothing but the life of this world."

**The second patient — the foolish one.** This patient's disease is the lack of intelligence and the abundance of foolishness. This is also incurable. The Prophet Eee-sa, Jesus, peace be upon him, said: with God's permission, I am not incapable of bringing the dead to life — but I am incapable of curing the fool and the ignorant.

The fool is recognizable. He has spent very little time acquiring knowledge. He has not yet begun the disciplined study of the intellectual and the transmitted sciences. And yet, on the strength of that very little, he raises objections against scholars who have spent their lives in those very sciences. He does not know what is obvious to everyone else — that his objection is wounding only to himself. He does not see the depth of the scholar's knowledge, nor could he, given how little of the field he has crossed. A person who cannot even register the existence of the scholar's depth is in the grip of his own foolishness. Stay away from him. Do not respond.

**The third patient — the one who hears only what he wants to hear.** This patient is restless and impatient. He does not understand the teachings of the spiritually elevated, the saints. He relies instead on his own limited intelligence, and whatever reaches him he interprets according to his own self-interest. He is simpleminded; his mind cannot grasp the truth. This person should also not be answered. The Prophet Muhammad, peace and blessings be upon him, said:

"Nah-nu Ma-aa-shi-rul An-bi-yaa-ee U-mir-naa Un Na-ta-kal-la-mun Naa-sa A-laa Qad-re U-qoo-li-him."

"We the company of prophets have been ordered by Allah to address people according to the measure of their intellects."

**The fourth patient — the one who can be cured.** This patient is seeking the straight path, the Si-raat al-Mus-ta-qeem. He is obedient. He is sharp and intelligent. He is not in the grip of anger, of the desires of the lower self — the Nafs — of jealousy, or of the lust for wealth and fame. When this person asks a question or raises an objection, it is not to envy you, not to find fault, not to test you. He genuinely wants to find the truth and the right way to live by it. This patient is curable. Answering his question is not merely allowed; it is obligatory — Waa-jib.

Without this discipline of discernment, the seeker who answers every question equally is like a teacher in a public forum today, where any voice can be raised, any objection broadcast, any envy disguised as inquiry — and the well-meaning teacher exhausts himself responding to the three categories that cannot be cured, while the one who could have been guided is left without an answer. Spend your attention where it can do good.
