# Section 8 — The Reality of Tasawwuf

You asked: what is Ta-saw-wuf? Ta-saw-wuf — what the world today often calls Sufism, and what the Prophet, peace and blessings be upon him, called Ih-saan, spiritual excellence — rests on two qualities.

The first is obedience to Allah. The seeker acts on the Sha-ree-ah, the revealed law: he or she keeps its obligations, follows its commandments, observes its prohibitions. Without this outer discipline, there is no inner path.

The second is mercy toward Allah's creation. The seeker is sympathetic to every living thing and active in their good. This is not possible until the soul has been purified of its evil qualities and then adorned with the good ones. A heart still ruled by envy, pride, or greed cannot be sincerely useful to anyone else.

A Soo-fee is the one in whom both qualities hold together — perseverance in the Sha-ree-ah, and real desire for the welfare of all humanity. That is the term used correctly. Most of what passes today under the name has nothing to do with it.

Loyalty to Allah means setting aside one's own pleasure for the sake of God. Sincerity toward people means relating to them without private motive — never using a relationship to serve oneself, but giving oneself to the good of every person one meets. There is one condition, and it cannot be removed: this giving must remain inside the bounds of the Sha-ree-ah. A surgeon is not free to operate by intuition alone, however good her intentions; she is bound by the discipline of her training. The seeker who serves humanity is bound, in the same way, by the law that defines what real service is.

That is Ta-saw-wuf.
