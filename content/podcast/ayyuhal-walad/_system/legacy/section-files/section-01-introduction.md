# Section 1 — Introduction of the Book

One of Imam Ghazali's students, after spending years acquiring religious knowledge and spiritual benefit at the master's side, found himself troubled by a single question. He had given much of his life to study. He had sacrificed for it. And yet he did not know which of the many branches of knowledge he had learned would actually serve him — which would illuminate his grave, which would benefit him on the Day of Judgment, and which would not. He wanted to know so that he could turn away from any knowledge that would not help him. He remembered the saying of the Prophet Muhammad, peace and blessings be upon him:

"A-oo-zoo Bil-laa-hi Min Ilm-il La Yan-fa-oo."
"I seek refuge in Allah from knowledge that is of no benefit."

The student carried this question for several days, and then he wrote a letter to Imam Ghazali. He asked for an answer to his dilemma, asked a number of further questions about the spiritual path, and asked for two specific things: a piece of personal counsel that he could carry through his life, and a supplication that he could always recite.

He acknowledged in the letter that Imam Ghazali had already written extensively on these themes. He named several of the master's works. There was Ih-yaa al-U-loom ad-Deen, the Revival of the Religious Sciences. There was Kee-mi-yaa as-Sa-aa-da, the Alchemy of Happiness. There was Ja-waa-hir ul-Quran, Priceless Gems of the Holy Quran. There was Ar-ba-een, Forty Steps. There was Min-haaj ul-Aa-bi-deen ee-la Jan-na-tu Rab-bul Aa-la-meen, the Best Way for the Worshippers of God Leading them Toward the Paradise of the Lord of all Universes. But this student wanted something different. He wanted a short companion — a text he could keep with him, read often, and act on every day.

In reply, Imam Ghazali sent him the letter that became this book. What follows is that reply.
