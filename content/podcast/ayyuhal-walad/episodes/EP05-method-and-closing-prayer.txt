## The completion the listener is hearing

The original student wrote to Ghazali asking for two things in particular. First, a supplication he could always recite. Second, a piece of advice he could always act on. ## Audience

General thoughtful adult. Not a specialist in Sufism, not necessarily Muslim, but someone willing to take a classical religious text on its own terms. Assume the listener may be coming to this fresh. Gloss terms like *Tawakkul* (ta-WAK-kul; reliance on God), *Du'a* (du-AAH; supplication), *Fard Kifaya* (fard ki-FAA-yah; communal obligation) on first use; do not re-gloss.

## Angle

Faithful exposition. The hosts treat Ghazali's prescription as it is given. They do not modernize the four matters into productivity tips. They do not reduce the du'a to a wellness invocation. They let the supplication breathe; it is the text Ghazali himself leaves on the table.

## Central tensions to reach

1. **The student's two requests, answered together.** The student asked for daily-action advice and for a daily prayer. Ghazali, near the end of the letter, gives both. The four matters are the action. The du'a is the prayer. The hosts should make this connection explicit, ideally early.

2. **Why "do not stockpile food" sits beside "relate intimately to God."** The four matters are not a graded hierarchy from spiritual to mundane. They are a single ethic of daily life. The pantry and the heart are governed by the same posture. The hosts should not laugh at the inclusion of the food rule; they should hold it as Ghazali holds it.

3. **Du'a as communion, not request.** In the Islamic tradition the supplication is not primarily a list of wants delivered to a vending-machine God. It is the act of standing before Allah. The closing du'a names many things asked for, several things refused, and ends in pure address: *O Allah, O Allah, O Allah*. The hosts should let the listener feel the shift from petition into pure invocation.

4. **The letter ends, and now what?** Ghazali says, *In this book, I have answered all of your questions. Now you should gather courage and act on all of them and do not forget me in your supplications.* The student receives the letter. The teacher disappears from the room. The question lands on the listener, who is now in the student's seat.

# The Method of Living and the Closing Prayer

*From* Ayyuhal Walad (My Dear Beloved Son or Daughter)
*Author:* Imam Abu Hamid Muhammad al-Ghazali (1058 to 1111 CE)
*Translator:* Irfan Hasan

---

## The setting

This is the end of the letter. Earlier in the same epistle, Ghazali named four things the student must refrain from: religious debate for its own sake, hollow preaching, the company of rulers, and the acceptance of their gifts. The closing stretch opens by saying: enough of what to avoid. Here are four things to do.

---

## Movement 1: The frame of the four matters

> My dear beloved son! Refrain from the [above mentioned] four things but the things that you have to do are also four in number and it will be appropriate that you take full care of them.

Four cautions, four matters. A matched pair. The four actions are not ranked, not arranged from inward to outward. They sit beside each other as a single ethic of daily life: how the student relates to his Creator, how he relates to other human beings, what he studies, and what he keeps in his pantry. The pantry is on the list.

---

## Movement 2: Matter 1, the method of relating to God

> The first thing is that, every matter that is between you and Allah should be conducted [by you] in such a way that if your servant does the same for you then you, instead of being sad, will like it, commend it, and will not be in any way angry about it.

A mirror test, run inward. Picture your servant. Would what he does please you, or anger you? Whatever you would resent in him is what Allah will not be pleased with from you.

Ghazali tightens the analogy:

> Likewise, if there is anything you do not like about your servant doing it for you, you should also realize that if you fell short in your servitude to your Lord then your Creator will also not like it. The reality that needs to be mentioned here is that your servant is not created by you but only hired by you. But you been created, raised (cherished), and owned by your Real Creator and Owner.

Exact and asymmetric. The servant is *hired*. The student himself is *created*. He leases a body and a life from the One who made them. Du'a (du-AAH; supplication), worship, and daily conduct all follow from that.

---

## Movement 3: Matter 2, the method of relating to others

> The second thing is that every matter, that is between you and other slaves of Allah, should be conducted by you in such a way that if they do the same to you [what you are doing to them] then you will like it and will not be grieved by it.

The same mirror, turned outward. The first matter ran the test between the student and his Lord. The second runs it between him and his neighbors. Ghazali anchors this in a *Hadith Qudsi* (ha-DEETH QUD-see; Allah speaking through the Prophet):

> Fala Yakmalu Eemanu 'Abdi Hatta Yuhibba Lisaa-irin Nasi Ma Yuhibba Li Nafsihi.
> (fa-laa yak-mu-lu ee-MAA-nu AB-dee HAT-taa yu-HIB-ba li saa-i-rin NAA-si maa yu-HIB-ba li NAF-si-hee)
> My (Allah's) slave's faith (Iman) is never complete unless he/she likes for all human beings what he/she likes for himself/herself.

Not *some* human beings. Not the believers. Not the worthy. *All human beings*. *Iman* (ee-MAAN; faith) is incomplete until that posture is in place.

Imam Ali (AS) gave the same instruction to his son Imam Hasan (AS) in a letter that has become one of the most read passages of the *Nahj al-Balagha*:

> O my son, make yourself the measure between you and others. Wish for others what you wish for yourself, and dislike for them what you dislike for yourself. Do not oppress, as you do not wish to be oppressed. Do good to others, as you would like good to be done to you. Regard as bad for yourself what you regard as bad for others, and accept for the people what you accept for yourself.
> *(Nahj al-Balagha, Letter 31; Imam Ali ibn Abi Talib to his son Imam Hasan ibn Ali)*

Where Ghazali speaks to a student of religious sciences, Imam Ali is writing as a father to his own son. The substance is the same. The self is the measuring stick. What the student finds intolerable in others is what he himself must refuse to do. The reverse, equally, what he hopes for from others is what he must extend first.

---

## Movement 4: Matter 3, the insistent obligation to study

> The third thing is that if you want to increase your knowledge and want to read (study) a scholarly book then think that your lifetime will be over in no more than a week.

A thought experiment. Imagine the week is your last.

> If you have the knowledge that your life will last for no more than a week, then in that week you will never read such scholarly books in which you will learn things like religious debates, argumentation, dictionary, linguistics and grammar, poetry, poetic collections, and essay writing. The reason is that you have developed an understanding that such knowledge will provide no benefit to you.

The argument is not that grammar is worthless. The proximity of death sorts the shelf. The student would close the books on debate and open the books on the heart, would, Ghazali says, *keep yourself busy the whole week with contemplation of the heart and recognizing the faults in your Nafs (self)*.

Ghazali drives the experiment with a domestic image:

> If you are given the news and told that the next week the king will visit your house, then certainly in the whole week, you will not do any work other than having the obsession that king's glance should not fell on such-and-such thing so I should clean it.

Then Ghazali stops:

> Now you should yourself think and understand! What should I explain to you about this hint? You yourself are intelligent so a hint should be enough for you.

If a king's visit triggers a week of cleaning, what does the King's eye on your heart trigger? Ghazali quotes the Prophet:

> InnAllaha La Yunzuru Ila Suwarikum Wa La Ila 'Aamalikum Wa Lakin Yunzuru Ila Qulubikum Wa Niyyatikum.
> (in-nal-LAA-ha laa yan-ZU-ru i-laa SU-wa-ri-kum wa laa i-laa aa-MAA-li-kum wa LAA-kin yan-ZU-ru i-laa qu-LOO-bi-kum wa nee-yaa-ti-kum)
> Verily, Allah does not look at your faces and your actions but he looks at your hearts and your intentions.

The Supreme Ruler's sight is already on the heart. The student must purify it. Knowledge that purifies the heart, Ghazali says, is *Fard al-'ayn* (fard al-AYN; obligatory on each individual). Other branches are *Fard Kifaya* (fard ki-FAA-yah; a communal obligation, discharged if some within the community carry it). He names his own book as the door:

> If your desire is to acquire knowledge of the matters of the heart then you should study [my book] *Ahya al-Uloom ad-Deen* (Revival of the Knowledge of the Path to God) and our other books.

The third matter is not *study widely*. It is *study what will still matter when the week is up*.

The Quran sets the same urgency as a flat statement of fact, repeated three times across the Book:

> Kullu nafsin dha'iqatu al-mawt, wa innama tuwaffawna ujurakum yawma al-qiyamah.
> (KUL-lu NAF-sin DHAA-i-qa-tul-MAWT, wa IN-na-MAA tu-WAF-fa-WO-na u-JU-ra-kum YAW-mal-qi-YAA-mah.)
> Every soul shall taste death, and you will be paid your wages in full only on the Day of Resurrection.
> *(Quran, Aal `Imran 3:185, also 21:35 and 29:57; English: Yusuf Ali. Subsequent Quranic English in this chapter follows the same translation.)*

The verse is the same diagnostic Ghazali's week-experiment dramatizes. The pay does not come during the work. It comes after. The student who chooses what to study by what gets paid in this life is reading the wrong ledger.

---

## Movement 5: Matter 4, not stockpiling food

The fourth matter is the one a modern listener will want to skip.

> The fourth thing is that you should not accumulate more than one year's provision for your family from the possessions of the world.

One year. Ghazali grounds the rule in the Prophet's practice. The Prophet accumulated one year's provisions for some of his wives, and recited:

> Allahumma Aj'al Quta Aale Muhammadin Kafafun.
> (al-LAA-hum-ma aj-AL QOO-ta AA-li mu-HAM-ma-din ka-FAA-fan)
> O my Allah! Make this provision (food) sufficient for the family of Muhammad.

But the Prophet did not stockpile for all his wives. He stockpiled for *some*:

> Prophet Muhammad did not accumulate provisions (food) for all of his wives (Azwaj al-Mutahharat), but instead, he accumulated provisions (food) for one year for those of his wives only whose reliance (Tawakkul) on Allah was weak. And for those Ummahatil Mu'mineen (Mothers of the Believers), whose certitude in Allah was perfect and had strong Tawakkul, for them the Prophet did not even accumulate even one day's worth of provisions. For instance, for 'Aisha Siddiqa (Aa-i-sha Sid-dee-qa) and for other Mothers of Believers like her.

The principle is sharper than the rule. One year is the upper bound for the spiritually weak. The strong stockpile nothing, because *Tawakkul* (ta-WAK-kul; reliance on Allah) is the substance of the pantry. Food, like prayer and conduct, is governed by the same posture toward the One who provides.

---

## Movement 6: The closing du'a

Now Ghazali turns to the last thing the student asked for.

> My dear beloved son! In this book, I have answered all of your questions. Now you should gather courage and act on all of them and do not forget me in your supplications (Du'a). You have also desired that I should write a supplication for you, so you should search for supplications in the book of Ahadith, especially in Sahah Sitta (Sa-hah Sit-tah; the six most authentic collections of the traditions of Prophet Muhammad) and memorize them. Likewise, many supplications have come to us from the methods of the Ahl al-Bayt (Ahl al-Bayt; the family of Prophet Muhammad). Recite the following supplication, in particular, after every prayer (Salaat).

Then the prayer itself, the artifact the student takes with him.

> O my Allah! I desire from You the completion (abundance/perfection) of Your blessings and from purity (inner and outer) I desire its permanence and from Your mercy [I desire] its inclusion [in all matters] and from health [I desire] its attainment and from sustenance [I desire] its increase and from life [I desire] its happiness and from life [I desire] its real success and from good qualities [I desire] their completion and from your rewards [I desire] the reward which is the most common and from your plenitude [I desire] the plenitude which is the sweetest and from your favors [I desire] the favor which is the most plentiful and from deeds [I desire] the deed which is the best and from knowledge [I desire] the knowledge which is the most beneficial and from the sustenance [I desire] the sustenance which is the most plentiful.

> O my Allah! Be on our side (grant us benefits) and do not burden us (protect us from harms). O my Allah! Grant us salvation when we die and reform our deeds and grant us safety in your days and nights and grant us your mercy in our houses and in our possessions and cover our sins and fault with the cover of your forgiveness and do us favors by correcting all our faults [inner and outer] and give us confidence and reliance on You.

> O my Allah! Grant us perseverance and persistence in our Deen (religion/Path to God). Save us from such deeds in the world that will become a source of shame (humiliation) on the Day of Judgment and lessen the burdens of our sins on us and grant us a life of the pious ones and be sufficient to us in all our affairs and protect us from the evil of the evildoers. Liberate us and our ancestors from the fire of Hell, from loans, and from oppression O You the Victorious One (Ya Aziz)! O You the Forgiving One (Ya Ghaffar)! O You the Generous One (Ya Kareem)! O You the Coverer of faults (Ya Sattar)! O You the Tolerant One (Ya Haleem)! O You the Powerful One (Ya Jabbar)! O You the Magnificent One (Ya Azeem)! O You the One Who Punishes (Ya Qahhar)! O Allah! O Allah! O Allah! O You the Compassionate One (Ya Rahman) in this world! O You the Merciful One (Ya Raheem) in the life hereafter! O You the Supreme Merciful One (Ya Arhum ur-Rahimeen)! You are the Grantor of the Excessive Mercy! And may the mercy and blessings of Allah always descend on Muhammad who is the best of all creations and on his descendants and on his companions. All praises are solely due to Allah the Lord of all the Universes (Rabb ul-'Alameen).

The supplication has a structure. It opens by asking for *the best of every category*: the best blessing, the most beneficial knowledge, the most plentiful sustenance. It moves into protection: salvation at death, safety in the days and nights, the cover of forgiveness. It names what is refused: shame on the Day of Judgment, the burden of sin, the evil of evildoers, the fire of Hell, the weight of loans, oppression. Then it dissolves into pure address. Eight divine names in sequence. *O Allah, O Allah, O Allah*. The closing names of mercy. Blessings on the Prophet, his family, his companions. The final praise to the Lord of the Worlds.

By the end the petition becomes simply standing in front of God and naming Him. That is what du'a (du-AAH) becomes when given its full length.

The structure Ghazali describes, petition for the best of every category, then protection from every harm, then a sequence of divine names that dissolves the petitioner into pure address, is the same structure the Ismaili community lives every day in its *Holy Du`a*, which closes each obligatory prayer. The *Du`a* opens with praise, names the line of Imams of the household of the Prophet, asks for the increase of the seeker in faith and knowledge and well-being, asks for forgiveness and shelter, and resolves into the recitation of divine names. The wording is different across the Sunni, Shia, and Ismaili formulations. The architecture is the same. A petition that begins with "I want" and ends with "*Ya Aziz, Ya Ghaffar, Ya Kareem*" has discovered that what was wanted, in the end, was simply Him.

Ghazali closes the letter:

> This book comes to an end with the help of the Absolute Sovereign (Al-Malik) and the Giver of all (Al-Wahhab)!

The student has the prayer. He has the four matters. The teacher's voice ends. The letter is in his hand.
