# Key Passages: Ayyuhal Walad, Chapter 1

Verbatim quotations from *Imam Ghazali's Response to the Letter*, in the order they appear in the chapter. Arabic transliteration is preserved exactly as printed in the Irfan Hasan English translation. Each passage carries an attribution and a brief note on why it matters for the conversation.

---

## 1. The framing prayer of the entire chapter

> A'auzu Billahi Min 'Ilm-il La Yanfa'au.
> I seek Allah's refuge from the knowledge which is of no benefit.


**Why this matters:** The student's letter quotes this prayer back to Ghazali, and Ghazali's entire reply is an unfolding of what the prayer commits the praying person to. Quote this first when discussing the chapter.

---

## 2. The diagnostic on wasted time

> A servant of Allah's involvement in useless things is an indication that Allah has removed His special favors from him or her. And if the servant is spending even a moment from his life on things other than for which Allah has created him, then it is a matter of great sorrow.


**Why this matters:** Wasted time is not a neutral category in this text. It is evidence that grace has already withdrawn. This is the first concrete thing Ghazali presses on his student.

---

## 3. The most severe torment

> Inna Ashaddan Nasi 'Azabun Yaumal Qiyamati 'Alimu Lum Yunfa'huAllahu Bi 'Ilmihi.
> On the Day of Judgment, the most severe torment will be given to the scholar (person of knowledge) to whom Allah had not benefited from his knowledge. That is, he did not act upon the acquired knowledge.


**Why this matters:** The educated person is in the most dangerous position of all. Ghazali addresses a student precisely because the student is the figure most at risk. The hosts should hold this hadith open long enough for the listener to feel its weight.

---

## 4. Junaid Baghdadi in the dream

> Worships and signs all turned out to be useless. But some cycles of prayers I did at the time of Tahajjud (the third part of the night) were of benefit.


**Why this matters:** One of the most revered figures of early Sufism reports that the bulk of his religious life turned out to be nothing, and that only the silent prayers of the last third of the night survived. This is the chapter's first announcement of what survives at death.

---

## 5. The Quranic anchor on striving

> Wa Anna Laeysa Lil Insaani illa Maa Sa'aa.
> Human beings will get only what they have strived for.


**Why this matters:** Ghazali knows the verse is held in some readings to be abrogated. He cites it anyway and then doubles down with three more verses. The accumulation is structural, not rhetorical.

---

## 6. The atom's weight

> Famaey Ya'mal Mithqaala Zarratin Khairaey Yarahu Wa Maey Ya'mal Mithqaala Zarratin Sharraey Yarahu.
> And so, the one who shall have done an atom's weight of good shall see it, and the one who shall have done an atom's weight of evil shall see it.


**Why this matters:** The smallest action is registered. This is the verse Ghazali uses to close any escape route from the demand of deeds.

---

## 7. The conjunction that decides

> Famun Kana Yurju Liqaa-a Rabbihi Fal Ya'mal 'Amalun Salihun.
> Whoever looks forward, with hope and awe, to meeting his Lord on the Day of Judgment, let him do righteous deeds, and let him not ascribe unto anyone or anything a share in the worship due to his Lord.


**Why this matters:** Faith *and* deeds. The conjunction is structural. Ghazali piles three more verses on top of this one to make the point unavoidable.

---

## 8. The angel's verdict

> Allah, the Holy, says that you put yourself in trouble for no use since your worship is not accepted by Him and you will go to Hell.


**Why this matters:** The setup of the chapter's emotional pivot. The angel announces, in Allah's name, that the man's life of worship is refused.

---

## 9. The worshipper's reply: the chapter's pivot

> Allah, the Exalted, has created us for His worship and servitude and our job is to serve Him no matter what, and He is the Owner of each and everything and everyone including myself, and the Powerful One.


**Why this matters:** This is the emotional center of the chapter. Service without expectation of acceptance is, paradoxically, the only service that is accepted. Quote this in full when discussing.

---

## 10. The forgiveness

> If this slave of Ours does not turn his face away from Our worship and servitude, then We will also not turn Our Face away from him.


> Ishhadu Ya Malaaikatey Anni Qad Ghafartu Lahu.
> O My angels! All of you be My witnesses that I have forgiven him.


**Why this matters:** The verdict reverses on the spot. The decisive thing is not the worship itself but the worshipper's posture in the face of refusal.

---

## 11. The self-audit

> Haasibu Qabala Un Tuhasabu Wa Zinu Qabala Un Tuuzanu.
> Take account of yourselves, that is, judge and correct yourselves, before you are taken into account on the Day of Judgment. And weigh your deeds before your deeds will be weighed.


**Why this matters:** The chapter's recurring instruction. The seeker performs the judgment on himself, in advance, so that the judgment performed on him will not surprise him.

---

## 12. Ali on the two errors

> A person who thinks that he will go to Paradise without righteous deeds is misguided, and the person who thinks that he will go to Paradise only through striving is merely busy with hard work.


**Why this matters:** The path is between two ditches. Confidence in grace without deeds is misguided. Confidence in deeds without grace is busywork. Ghazali keeps the seeker on the narrow road.

---

## 13. Hasan al-Basri on desiring Paradise without deeds

> Talabul Jannati Bila 'Amalin Zambum Minaz Zunubi.
> To desire Paradise without good deeds is a sin from among the sins.


**Why this matters:** The wish itself, untethered from action, is a sin. The chapter forecloses passive hope as a religious posture.

---

## 14. The intelligent and the mindless

> Al Kayyeysu Mun Daana Nafsahu Wa 'Amila Lima Ba'dal Mauti Wal Ahmaqu Manit Taba'a Nafsahu Hawaha Wa Tamanna 'Ala Allahi Illa Mani Wafi Riwayati 'Ala Allahil Mughfirati.
> Intelligent is the one who subdued his Nafs (self, the lower self of evil desires and inclinations) and performed good pious actions for the life hereafter. And mindless is the one who subdued his Nafs to pleasures, greed, and evil and vain desires, and thought that Allah is with him. In some traditions: this mindless person hopes for Allah's forgiveness.


**Why this matters:** Hope without action is foolishness, by name. The hadith renames the figure the listener might recognize as devout-but-passive.

---

## 15. The intention test: the chapter's piercing line

> You stayed awake at nights to continually rehearse knowledge and sacrificed your sleep to study it. If your intention was to gain worldly benefits and worldly prestige and status, then *Fawailul Laka Thumma Wailul Laka*, there is sadness and woe for you and again there is sadness and woe for you. However, if your intentions were to establish the Path to God brought by Prophet Muhammad, attaining moral uplift and civilization, and eliminating evil from your self, then *Fatuba Laka Thumma Tuba Laka*, for you is the happiness and blessings and again for you is the happiness and blessings.


**Why this matters:** Same act, opposite destinations. Intention is the discriminator. This is where the chapter turns from Ghazali addressing all students to Ghazali addressing this one student. Quote in full when discussing intention.

---

## 16. The first question at the grave

> 'Abdi Qad Tahherta Munzaral Khalqi Sineena Fahul Tahherta Munzari Sa'atun.
> O My slave! You decorated yourself with knowledge of outward matters for many years to show off to the mortal creations of Mine. But for My sake only, did you ever purify your heart from evil and heedlessness even for a moment?


**Why this matters:** The most piercing line in the chapter. The decoration of knowledge is the indictment. The hidden purification of the heart, even for a moment, is the defense.

---

## 17. The chapter's central thesis

> Knowledge without actions is madness, and actions without knowledge are useless.


**Why this matters:** The spine of the entire epistle. *Madness* is the stronger word. The two halves are not symmetric.

---

## 18. The wakefulness verse

> Sahrul 'Uyuna Bighairi Wajhika Sana ay'un
> Wa Baka uhunna Bighairi Faqdika Batilun.
> O my Lord, the wakefulness of the eyes, except for beholding the beauty of Your Countenance, is useless.
> And unless it is for the sorrow of being separate from You, the weeping of the eyes for someone or something else, other than You, O Lord, is gravely wrong.


**Why this matters:** Pairs with the night-prayer material. Wakefulness without God is waste. Tears without God are waste.

---

## 19. The dove on the branch

> At night, the dove sitting on the branch of a tree is calling on to its Lord
> While I am in a deep sleep.
>
> By the Lord of the House of Allah, I am a liar. If I had been a true lover of Allah,
> Then the doves would not have won over me in weeping in the pain of separation from my Beloved Allah.
>
> Alas, I do not even wet my eyes with tears while claiming to love my Beloved Lord,
> While the animals keep weeping in their love for their Creator.


**Why this matters:** The chapter's most haunting image. The dove beats the man at love. Lands the night-prayer section.

---

## 20. The chapter's hinge into Mujahadah

> You should not do anything without the guidance of Prophet Muhammad, and be certain that you cannot walk on the Path of Allah, the Exalted, with merely the knowledge that you have acquired. However, this Path of God must be traveled through hard work and Mujahadah (intense inner spiritual struggle and exercises), and the pleasures of the self and desires from the lower self must be cut with the sword of Mujahadah.


**Why this matters:** Knowledge is not the vehicle. *Mujahadah* is. The chapter pivots here from the diagnostic phase to the prescription that the rest of the treatise will unfold.

---

## 21. The warning on hollow Sufis

> The tongue speaking the words of deep wisdom and beauty while the heart worshipping heedlessness from God and desires of the lower self, this is a sign of mighty misfortune.


**Why this matters:** Ghazali calls out the imitation Sufis of his day. The chapter is not only an argument against the dry scholar but also against the eloquent fake. Both fail the intention test in their own way.

---

## 22. Shibli on the single hadith

> Aymal Lid Dunya Be Qadre Maqamika Feeha Wa'mal Le Akhirateka Be Qadre Baqaa'ika Feeha Wa'mal Lillahi Be Qadre Hajateka Ilaeyhe Wa'mal Lin Naari Be Qadre Sabrika Alaeyha.
> Work for the world as much as you live in it, and work for the life hereafter as much as you want to live there, and work for Allah as much as you are in need of Him, and work for the Hell as much as you can be patient with its pain and sufferings.


**Why this matters:** The closing balance. Four proportions, each measured against an honest assessment of where the seeker actually stands. Ghazali presents this as the single hadith Shibli found contained all knowledge.
