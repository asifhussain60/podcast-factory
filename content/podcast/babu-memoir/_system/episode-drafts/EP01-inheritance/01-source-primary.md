# What I inherited

*Source:* `ch01-inheritance.txt` (verbatim mirror; the refinement target)
*Source type:* memoir
*Adaptation mode:* faithful

---

Babu, what does it mean to be a man?

I grew up in Kuwait, in a middle-class, highly conservative and religious family where boys were surrounded by men, rules, religion, expectations, and endless lectures about respect and responsibility. None of it added up to an answer. The men around me could tell me what to do. They could not show me what to be. And the man I watched most closely was the one I understood the least.

What confused me most about Baba was the gap between the man he was with others and the man he was with me. He did not speak much at home except to discipline me or talk at the dinner table. But put him among his peers, and he came alive. Jolly, warm, present. A completely different man, joking around in his typical Hyderabadi accent, which would disappear during normal conversation. With my mother, he was a gentleman. Kind, patient, always protective of her, never let her carry weight. I think he knew he had married a woman he knew was out of his league. He was not a very educated man, and she held double Master's degrees. He was always grateful for that, and it showed in the tenderness he offered her. But that tenderness never crossed the room to reach my brother or me. His way of loving my mother seemed to include punishing us on her behalf. Maybe that was how he understood loyalty. Maybe that was his version of protecting her. Either way, the man who was gentle with his wife was the same man who reached for the cane without asking a single question.

I can't remember a time when Baba sat me down and explained what manhood truly meant. Looking back, I doubt he fully understood it himself. There are qualities in me that I can trace back to him, and I am grateful for those. But I also know, as clearly as ever, that I do not want to be the kind of man he was. Not because he was a bad man in any way. He was a great provider, very honest in his dealings, loved socializing, and was generally respected, so he was a great father when it came to provision. I had the best food, the best education, the best clothes he could afford, and more than I can even remember. Maybe that should have been enough, but it wasn't. I had a father, uncles, teachers, religious scholars, an entire world of men and rules and expectations around me, and still, no one actually taught me how to be a man.

And yet, not everything between us was silence and punishment. I remember Baba used to take me to a computer class in Salmiyah, which was an hour's drive from our apartment in Kuwait. Every day, he would wait outside patiently until I finished. He never complained. Never rushed me. Afterward, he would sometimes take me to a small restaurant in Salmiyah that served pani puri, and we would sit there together and enjoy a snack. He loved those kinds of snacks. I do not have many memories of warmth between us, but that one is real. Two people, a plate of pani puri, and the kind of quiet that did not feel like withdrawal. It felt like company. I sometimes wonder if that was his version of closeness. Not words. Not advice. Just being there.

But warmth and the cane lived in the same man, and you never knew which one was home.

I must have been eight or nine when Atif and I were playing in the apartment. The way brothers play, which is to say, badly. Things got rough. Pushing turned into shoving, shoving turned into a proper fight, and I, being 4 years older, kicked his butt. Convincingly. Atif ran to Baba in tears, and within seconds, my father appeared with the cane and the look that meant the courtroom was already in session.

Say sorry to him. I apologized. Quickly and with the enthusiasm of a boy who recognized the cane. He accepted my apology the way judges accept guilty pleas. Now shake his hand. I shook his hand. Now hug him. I hugged him. Every time I complied, he raised the price. Each demand came with the cane hovering just close enough to remind me what the alternative looked like. I was passing every test, swallowing every humiliation, doing whatever it took to survive the moment. Then he said, now kiss him on the cheek.
I remember pausing, staring at my father, then at my brother, factoring the cane in the equation, and doing the math. I'll take the beating I said.

There are limits to how fear pushes me before I begin pushing back. Apparently, mine was somewhere between a hug and a kiss.

My family was always comfortable. Baba made sure of that. But I always had to make a justifiable case for every dinar I asked for. Unnecessary things were not an option. So eventually, I started stealing from his cupboard. Small amounts. Twenty or forty at a time, nothing that would raise an alarm. I got very good at it. Suspiciously good, in retrospect. My father noticed the discrepancies but never suspected me. I am not proud of it. I rationalized it, thinking that if asking is not going to work, I will get better at taking. Not because I am a thief by nature, but because the distance between wanting and receiving felt too wide to cross with words. I did not want to create fake urgencies to justify a frivolous item, but I was not willing to beg either, nor give up my desire.

In fact, he got so frustrated trying to catch the culprit that he eventually turned to the supernatural. He found a man who claimed to speak with spirits, and apparently, the spirit confirmed that the thief was his eldest son. I cannot confirm or deny whether spirits have reliable intelligence, but when my parents confronted me with this revelation, I looked them both in the eye and said, "If your spirit is willing to testify in court, I will admit to stealing." They dropped the subject after that.

Years later, after I moved to the United States and started earning an income, I repaid everything I stole. The first time I visited Pakistan after working for a year, I took a briefcase filled with Pakistani rupees, over two hundred thousand, like a rich son willing to pay ransom for his father. When my father asked about it, I admitted everything. It was I who stole from your cupboard, I told him. And here it is back, with interest. He laughed, hugged me, and said he always knew it had to be me, but could never catch me.

But the missing money was never the real problem between us.

That is the contradiction I have never fully resolved. A man can be decent in one direction and destructive in another, and still not see the gap. Baba was not cruel in his nature. He was loyal to the wrong blueprint. He confused obedience to my mother with the protection of his family, and in doing so, he left the people who needed his protection the most standing on the wrong side of his loyalty. I think many men of his time inherited this confusion. They protected the institution, the reputation, the religious traditions, the peace of the house, but never noticed that the people inside it were suffering.

And that confusion was not limited to our household.

On the unique little "Desi" planet I was raised on, opinions were everywhere. Unsolicited advice, correction, pride, ego, criticism. Everybody had something to say. Very little of it was useful. Most of it was not even original.

I came to understand that noise does not lead the way. True control isn't about exerting power. Manhood is found in silence rather than chaos. It involves less show, ego, panic, ignorance, and blind faith, and rejecting the slavish adherence to outdated traditions disguised as authority. Being male is not the same thing as being a "man".

Some of my earliest ideas about manhood probably (definitely) came from old Bollywood films. Idols like Amitabh Bachchan, Vinod Khanna, and Rishi Kapoor shaped my imagination more than the men around me did. Those films look ridiculous now. A man gets thrown off a building, punched through three walls, and still lands with perfect hair and a moral lesson. But buried under all that drama was a consistent code. A silent lesson.
A man protects. A man endures. A man stands up to injustice. He stands up for the woman he loves and sacrifices his needs for his loved ones. He is dependable. His word means something to him and others. And he does not have to be cruel to be strong.

The school systems in Kuwait and Karachi were equally oppressive. Getting beaten, slapped, and kicked was a very normal thing. Not an incident. Not a scandal. Just the way authority reminded you it was there. Teachers did not need a reason, and parents did not ask for one. That was the world outside the house, and it matched the world inside it perfectly. Violence was not an exception. It was the common language spoken by every adult who had power over a child.

Perhaps that is why I took up Taekwondo. I was never great at it and lost more matches than I won, but something in me kept pushing until I reached 2nd Dan black belt. I cannot say for certain what that something was. Maybe it was the need to stop being the one who flinches. Maybe it was stubbornness dressed up as discipline. Whatever it was, it branched my life in a direction it might not have taken otherwise. But that is a story for another day.

The dojo taught me how to take a hit without falling apart. The schoolyard taught me when it was worth taking one. My first physical fight happened in the eighth grade. A boy named Atif, ironically the same name as my brother, used to stammer. For some reason, he had a thing for me. He would always try to show me up in aggressive ways, bully me, and make fun of me. Maybe it was his way of overcoming his own insecurities. One day, I had had enough. But as fate would have it, he stopped teasing me that very week. Thanks to my ADHD, patience was never going to be my strategy. I could not wait for him to decide when to bully me next, so I decided to handle the scheduling myself.

I remember we were in the school playground during recess. A group of us boys was fooling around. Atif said something I do not remember, but just to provoke him, I stammered the answer back at him, knowing exactly what it would do. Of course, he came at me and shoved me. Already prepared, I closed my fist tight and swung with all my might against his face. I heard a loud thud, almost the kind I had heard in Bollywood movies, but duller. The next thing I knew, my face received a similar blow. And here is the interesting thing. In that moment, a thought arrived with surprising clarity: this does not hurt as much as I expected. After that, it was a back-and-forth of punches across the face, two roosters in a schoolyard, neither willing to be the first to stop. Finally, Saif jumped in and separated us. It was not until the next morning that I understood the difference between Bollywood fights and real ones. In the movies, a man takes forty punches and walks into the next scene with better hair than he started with. I woke up looking like someone had tried to reshape my face using only their fists and poor judgment.

A few minutes later, Atif came up behind me and tapped my shoulder. I spun around and immediately ducked, ready for another round. But he was not swinging. He said, "Please do not tell your aunt about this." As fate would have it, Afifa Khala was the principal of the school at the time. I told him I was never going to. That was the truth. After that day, we actually became friends and shared a few laughs. Funny how life works. Sometimes a fight clears the air in a way that talking never could.

Physical courage was the easy part. A fist, a swing, a moment of clarity. The harder lesson was everything that came after it, the kind of strength that has nothing to do with your hands. I think part of why that took me so long is that my first understanding of women was badly distorted by my own family dynamics. Amma was domineering, aggressive, and cruelly sarcastic with me and with others. People say the man is the head of the family. Maybe. But if my father was the head, my mother was the neck, turning him wherever she wanted. And Baba's failure to lead shaped me more than I knew at the time.

My Amma was a smart, sharp, and psychologically perceptive woman. Holding a Master's degree in both Urdu and psychology made her especially articulate at causing psychological harm. I mostly felt hurt and trapped around her, as I know many others did. The more trapped and hurt I felt, the more defiant I became. Truly defiant. I toughened myself up so much that her tears stopped affecting me and began to amuse me. They felt less like grief and more like a tactic. Maybe that reveals something ugly about me, or maybe it reveals something ugly about what I endured. Probably both.

But that experience changed me. It taught me to mistrust tears, to brace myself against emotion, to see vulnerability as manipulation. I had hardened where I was supposed to soften. I did not know that then. I know it now. I once read that hardships are meant to soften us, not harden us, and that if they are making us harder inside, it is a sign we are depending on ourselves instead of God. I was depending on myself entirely. I had no other option, or so I believed. Even now, because of the yelling, the silent treatment, the mind games, and the emotional abandonment, silence can still affect me strangely. A delayed response to a simple question can send my mind into dark places faster than I'd like to admit. It's amazing what childhood can do to break a person. Amazing in the worst way.

My younger brother, Atif, is proof that the same house can break two people in different ways. We never got along growing up. Probably hated each other, if I am being honest. He received the same treatment from my mother that I did. The same yelling. The same guilt. The same weapons. But where I rebelled and eventually escaped, Atif imploded.

My mother used him the way she used me, just for a different currency. She gained social mileage from my intelligence, my speeches, and my grades. From Atif, she gained entertainment. She made him replay comedic accents from television dramas, mimicking actors and their ridiculous clownish styles for the amusement of guests. It got laughs. It got attention. And over time, it became his personality. The performance ate the person underneath. Instead of rising to the challenge of becoming his own man, he folded inward until there was almost nothing left. His confidence did not just weaken. It ceased to exist.

At thirty-eight years old, my brother could not walk to a Western Union to pick up money I sent him. I had to route it through my uncle, who would collect it and deliver it to him. He held a Master's degree in Computer Science from Karachi and once called me to ask how to turn on a computer and log in. That is how easy a Master's degree was to get in Karachi, and how little it meant when it sat inside a man who had been hollowed out.

He never held a job. Not one. My mother had told him they had enough savings from Kuwait to last a lifetime, so why would he ever need to work? He believed her. Once during a job interview, the interviewer began with the basics, and Atif interrupted to ask how much the position paid. Five thousand rupees a month, the man said. Atif told him we pay our maid more than that, and walked out. That was the entire interview. This was a man with no skills, no experience, and the confidence of someone who had never been told no.

When I moved to the United States, I vowed to take full financial responsibility for my parents. My father kept asking me for large sums. Money for courses I knew Atif would never use. Money for medical bills. I kept my word and provided every amount he ever asked for. In return, Atif turned my father against me. He told him I was enjoying life in America while they rotted in misery in Pakistan. Abandoned. Deserted. Those were the words. And my father, weakened by a stroke at sixty-two that had paralyzed his left side and taken most of his speech, had no choice but to believe him. That was one of the hardest losses. Not the money. The trust. The fact that the man I had been providing for chose to believe the son who had never provided a thing.

Years after my mother passed, on my last trip to Karachi, Afifa Khala insisted I collect the jewelry Amma had left behind. She had divided it equally between her two sons. When we went to the bank and opened the box, my share was noticeably lighter. Several pieces were missing. There was a third unallocated pile of mixed jewelry. Afifa Khala asked my father to divide what was there with his own hands. He separated the real from the artificial, handed me the artificial, and gave Atif the gold.

That broke my heart. Not because the pieces were fake. Because even after everything, even after the money I had sent, the promises I had kept, the years I had provided, he could not bring himself to divide equally. I would have given Atif my share anyway. And I did. I handed him everything and walked away.

That was the last time I saw my father. I have not been back to Pakistan since.

I do not say any of this with contempt. I say it with sadness and with distance. Atif is what happens when the oppressive cycle wins. When a boy is crushed under the weight of a mother's dominance and never finds the stubbornness, the luck, or the right people to pull him back out. I rebelled. He submitted. I found someone who could hold my weight. He found nothing that could hold his. The same home made both of us, and it made us into very different kinds of broken. I see that clearly now. It does not make what he did less painful. But it does make it less mysterious.

People assume that tests only come in the form of hardships. But there is another kind of test, and it is the one most people fail. The test of ease. God gives you money, health, intelligence, comfort, and then watches what you do with it. Are you generous or a hoarder? Are you humble or inflated? Are you grateful or entitled? My brother was given ease by my mother, the ease of never needing to work, never needing to struggle, and that ease destroyed him more thoroughly than any hardship could have. Hardship at least has the decency to announce itself. Ease does its damage in silence.

What I eventually understood is that a man's character cannot depend on how others treat him. If it does, then every betrayal, every ingratitude, every lie told about him will reshape who he is, and he will spend his whole life being molded by the worst people in the room. Manhood, if it means anything, has to hold its shape regardless of the weather. Not because a man does not feel the storm. Because he has decided what he will and will not become, and the decision is not up for renegotiation every time someone disappoints him. I had to learn that the hard way. Atif never did because of the easy way.

But understanding something and living it are two different things. For a long time, I was still figuring it out the hard way.
