During my college days in Karachi, I got involved with a political party called “ People's Public Party”, PPP. That meant rallies, arguments, and more than a few street fights. I was not playing politics from a distance. I was in it, fists and all.

That was when I met a man named Nadeem Moosa. I called him Nadeem bhai because he was at least ten years older than me. He ran a computer shop called Danish Computers, and we became very close. For me, he took on more of a mentor's role than a friend's. Nadeem bhai always admired my intelligence. He could see that I was wasting it on violence and lost causes, so to protect me, he wisely had me join his shop and kept me busy with work. Whenever I needed him for anything involving a fight, he would turn me down, saying he was busy. All the while, he kept encouraging me to study. To get better. To stop throwing my potential into streets that had nothing good to give back.

I did not fully appreciate it then. But looking back, Nadeem bhai was the first man outside my family to see something in me worth protecting and to do something about it. Not with lectures. Not with punishment. Just by giving me something better to do with my time and refusing to let me drag him into what would have dragged me under. If Atif is what happens when nobody intervenes, Nadeem bhai is proof that sometimes one person, positioned right and stubborn enough, can redirect a life.

But even Nadeem bhai could not fix what was broken on the inside. He could keep me out of trouble, but he could not undo what home had already done. Before any of that changed, I was still the same man underneath. I was not cold or shut down, but I had spent years treating my own emotions like enemies, bracing against everything. I was controlling at times. The worst part was that I used the same tactics my mother had used on me. Guilt-tripping. Jealousy games. Emotional pressure. I would try them, watch them backfire, and then try them again, caught in a loop I could not name or stop. Every failed attempt left me more confused, more angry, and more convinced that something in me was permanently wired wrong. I had toughened myself into a man who could survive anything and connect with almost nothing. That was the man who existed before everything changed.

I spent years asking God to cure me of my hardships. I never stopped to consider that my hardships were curing me. That the pain was not the disease. It was the treatment. Ugly, yes. Unwanted, absolutely. But doing its work all the same.

I have come to believe that failure is not the opposite of growth. It is the soil of it. Every painful lesson I failed my way through, every relationship I damaged with tactics I did not even know I was using, every loop I could not break, these were not proof that I was beyond repair. They were the curriculum. A harsh one. An ugly one. But the only one that could have reached me through the thickness of everything I had built around myself. If someone had told me that at twenty-five, I would have dismissed it. At fifty-four, I know it is the truest thing I can say about my own life.

Ali (AS, peace be upon him) once said plainly: do not be a slave to others when Allah has created you free. I did not hear that in time to spare myself the years I spent enslaved to my mother's voice, my father's silence, bowing down at the altar of other people’s ego with my own broken patterns. But I heard it eventually. And it changed the direction of everything that followed.

Then my wonderful wife, Ishrat, came into my life and showed me an entirely different side of womanhood. She helped me understand that softness is not manipulation. That a person's tears can be mercy, not a weapon. That feeling deeply is not the same thing as controlling others emotionally. She once told me, "I will show you normal." Boy, did she ever. Through her, I began to separate what was wounded and distorted in my past from what was actually tender, sincere, and feminine. She taught me that a woman's emotional life is not something to fear or defend myself against. Very often, it is something to honor, to make room for, and to handle with care.

Watching Ishrat and other women dear to my heart silently endure pain opened my eyes even more. I saw that a woman can be wounded not only by physical force but also by contempt, humiliation, control, and disrespectful words used like a blade to cut deep. Some men know exactly how to injure a woman without ever leaving a bruise. They strip away her peace, her confidence, and her sense of safety, then act surprised when love disappears from the room. I know firsthand that kind of damage lingers. It settles into the heart and changes how she thinks, speaks, and tenses up in places where she should be able to rest.

I realized that a man can provide financially, speak well in public, and appear religious, yet still fail where it matters most. If the women closest to him do not feel safe, respected, cherished, and emotionally at ease, then something is deeply wrong. Allah tells us in the Quran that He placed affection and mercy between spouses, and that marriage is meant to bring tranquility. So why do so many homes contain fear, tension, and quiet hurt instead?

I have come to believe that a real man does not humiliate a woman and then expect loyalty from her. He does not control her, guilt her, pressure her, or weaponize religion, status, or money and then call himself a husband. He does not demand love as if tenderness can be extracted by authority. He creates the kind of space where love can grow on its own. That is a very different thing.

More and more, I believe one of a man's greatest responsibilities is to give a woman room to blossom. He does not suffocate her spirit. He does not make her smaller so he can feel bigger. If he is steady, respectful, protective, and emotionally safe, then her heart has room to breathe. What grows freely is always more beautiful than what is forced. Turns out domination is not romance. Who knew.

My daughter, Erum, taught me something just as important in a completely different dimension. I did not grow up with sisters, so there were parts of a girl's heart I never understood firsthand. Having a daughter changed that. It taught me, with a lot of help from Ishrat, how tender a girl's heart can be. Not weak. Tender. Sensitive. Deeply impressionable. Easily bruised by carelessness. Deeply shaped by the tone of the men around her. A daughter teaches a man very quickly that strength must know how to handle softness. Do not crush it. Do not ignore it. Protect it. Respect it. Be worthy of its trust.

Perhaps the purpose of being broken in those particular ways was to build something I could not have built from comfort. A capacity for mercy. A sensitivity to the damage other people carry quietly. I do not say that to dress up the pain in noble clothing. I say it because the alternative, that it was all pointless, is something I refuse to accept. Sorrow was never meant to drown me. It was meant to deepen me, to humble me, to soften what had become too hard to reach any other way. The brokenness carved something in me. Whether it was compassion or just a deeper kind of attention, I am not sure. But it is there now, and it was not there before.

Over time, I have come to see that a true man is not the loudest person in the room. He is the steadiest. He does not need to keep proving he is in charge. He listens. He pays attention. He stays composed. Restraint is not weakness. It is one of the clearest signs of strength. Anyone can lose control. That takes no training at all.

And with time, I have had to admit something harder. If I had understood these things earlier, I would have treated my own mother better than I did. She had a very difficult past that shaped her into who she needed to be to survive. I believe the same for my dad. Just like me, they did not receive a handbook on life and raising kids. That realization humbles me.

So then, if time could be reversed, I wish my dad had sat my teenage self down and taught me how to be a real man. In my imagination, I see him saying something along these lines.

Asif, you were created male, just as there are male and female distinctions across all living species, but there is a difference between being male and being a man. Religion is imposed on the man and the woman, not merely on the male and the female, so become a man first before you try to become a good Muslim. God has given you physical strength over women, but do not ever confuse that with being greater than her. Women possess a kind of strength that most men cannot even begin to comprehend. They have been given the miracle of bleeding for days every month, often in pain and discomfort, and still they carry on, smiling, serving, nurturing, cooking, cleaning, raising children, and holding the emotional weight of a household together. Reverse those roles, and you would likely complain by lunchtime.

God designed you for order, steadiness, and the protection of what is fragile. But He also gave you the full capacity to violate everything you were built to protect. That is the real masculine wound. Every man who has ever broken his word, hurt his family, or used his strength against the people he was supposed to guard was simply that capacity going unchecked. Knowing this is not permission to fail. It is the reason the work of becoming a man never stops.

Yes, you will earn a living for your family, and that responsibility matters, but that is only one part of the burden. The women in your life will be carrying family pressures, personal struggles, emotional strain, social expectations, and responsibilities you may never fully see, and still doing their best not to let anything fall apart. So never look at a woman who keeps your home running and say, "What do you do sitting home all day?"

Do not become the kind of man who thinks leadership means volume, anger, or control. The Prophet, peace be upon him, told us that the strongest man is not the one who can wrestle others to the ground, but the one who controls himself when he is angry. Let that sink in. The world will hand you plenty of reasons to rage. Your strength will be measured by what you do with that rage, not by how much of it you can produce. 
A woman is not beneath you. She is not there to absorb your frustration, your insecurity, your ego, or your bad day at work because life bruised your pride a little. She is an amanah. A trust. If Allah places a woman in your care as a wife, a daughter, a sister, or even simply as someone more vulnerable than you, then your job is not to dominate her but to protect her peace. Speak gently, especially when you are upset. Lower your voice before you raise your authority. Learn to apologize without acting like someone is having a limb removed. Learn to notice what she carries without waiting for applause because you managed to show basic decency.

A real man is not measured by how feared he is, how loud he is, or how many people obey him. He is measured by how safe others feel around him, especially the women and children closest to him. Ali (AS, peace be upon him) said it plainly: a man's worth depends upon the nobility of his aspirations. Not his volume. Not his title. Not his ability to make others submit. His aspirations. What he reaches toward when no one is looking. So be firm when firmness is needed, but never harsh just because you can. Be protective, but never possessive. Be present, not just useful. And if one day you find yourself wondering whether you are acting like a man or just a male inflated by ego, volume, and entitlement, that should be your sign to sit down, be quiet, and correct yourself.

Understand that true manhood is also reflected in how you conduct yourself when no one is watching, when there are no praises, and when life challenges you. Ali (AS) also taught that the best form of devotion to God’s service is not to make a show of it. Relying on praise and the urge to impress others will become the heaviest chains you carry in life. Never let praise make you arrogant or complacent. Instead, let it serve to boost your humility and ensure it is genuinely earned. Weak men find joy in superficial praise.

A man takes responsibility without constantly looking for someone else to blame. He tells the truth even when lying would make him look better. He keeps his word, especially when it becomes inconvenient, because a promise that only survives comfort is not a promise at all. He learns discipline, because feelings are real, but they are terrible leaders. He gets up, goes to work, handles his duties, controls his appetites, and does not let laziness, lust, anger, or self-pity drag him around by the neck like a stray dog. He does not spend his life making excuses for his weaknesses and calls that honesty. He faces himself. That alone is harder than most things men brag about.

Because the honest inventory of most men, including myself at my worst, goes something like this: we feel slighted far more easily than we forgive. We desire more than we ever use. We eat past what we need, lust with great efficiency, and love with terrible infrequency. That is not a random condition. That is a pattern. And patterns, unlike wounds, can be interrupted. But only by a man willing to look at himself with enough honesty to name what he sees in the mirror, and enough courage not to look away.

And remember that what you are given is never yours to hoard. Not your money. Not your health. Not your intelligence. These are trusts placed in your hands to see what you will do with them. The man who forgets that will build his whole life on something that was only ever lent to him.

Do not wait for pain to teach you everything the hard way, though it is apparently a favorite family tradition. Learn now that a man must know how to endure boredom, frustration, failure, and rejection without collapsing into drama or bitterness. You will not always be admired. You will not always be understood. You will not always win. Good. Let that make you deeper, not crueler. 

Learn patience. And understand what patience actually is, because most men misunderstand it entirely. Patience is not resignation. It is not deciding that nothing can change, and you must simply absorb whatever life sends your way. Patience is internal quiet in the midst of external noise. It is the refuge inside the storm. It is what keeps your inner state from being dictated by outer circumstances. It frees you from the reflex, the automatic flinch, the man who reacts before he has thought and regrets it after the damage is already done. That kind of patience is hard to build. It is also the only kind worth having. And understand that pain, in both the body and the soul, is not the enemy. It is an alarm for the sleeping soul. God created pain as a wakeup call, a push toward movement and change. A man who numbs his pain and calls it patience is not patient. He is dying slowly and calling it strength. 

Learn restraint. Learn how to sit in discomfort without immediately trying to escape it through distraction, arrogance, or some shiny little addiction. And remember, no one can break you unless you hand them the permission to do so. Guard yourself, but guard your character more.

Build a private life of discipline that nobody claps for: prayer, work, reading, honesty, self-control, keeping your body healthy, guarding your eyes, guarding your tongue, and keeping company with people who make you better instead of merely making you feel bigger. That is the kind of strength that lasts. Build yourself on character. Learn how to think clearly. Learn how to stand alone without becoming proud. Learn how to serve without needing a medal for it. Be the man who brings steadiness into chaos, not more chaos wearing expensive shoes. Protect the weak. Respect other men without imitating their unchallenged patterns. Walk away from what is beneath you. Admit when you are wrong. Ask for help when you need it. Keep learning long after your ego tells you that you already know enough. A real man is not finished when he grows older. He is finished when he stops correcting himself.

But before any of that, one thing has to happen first. You have to break the cycle within your own heart. Not your father's heart. Not your mother's. Yours. The cycle of bracing, of flinching, of repeating what was done to you because it is the only grammar you were given. That cycle lives in you as a tendency, not a destiny. Name it. Find it in the moment it rises in you. Then choose differently. And let yesterday die. It already has. Every moment brings a new chance to get it right. The universe you failed in yesterday is gone. This one is still yours. Everything else begins there.

In the end, becoming a man was never about closing the gap between my father and me. It was about closing the one inside myself. The distance between the man I was with others and the man I was at home. The distance between knowing better and doing better. Baba never closed his. I am still working on mine. But at least now I know where to look.
