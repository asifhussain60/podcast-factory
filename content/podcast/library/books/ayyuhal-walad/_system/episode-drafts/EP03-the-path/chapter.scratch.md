# The Path of the Seeker

*From Ayyuhal Walad ("O Beloved Son") by Imam Abu Hamid Muhammad al-Ghazali. This chapter covers the inner architecture of the seeker: the qualities of a true spiritual guide, the outer and inner conduct a disciple owes him, the deceptions of the lower soul that he must refuse, and the four inner stations Ghazali names by their classical Arabic terms, Tasawwuf, servitude, Tawakkul, and Ikhlas.*

## The shape of the inner journey

Ghazali, returning to the question his student first asked, *what is essential for those walking on the Path of Allah?*, gives him three more conditions to add to the four he has already named. He has already named correct belief without innovation, true repentance, settling all claims against you, and acquiring enough knowledge of the Shari'ah to obey God's commands. He now names a fifth, a sixth, and a seventh.

The fifth condition is the one that turns the four preceding ones into a path you can actually walk. *The seeker must have a spiritual guide.*

## The fifth condition: the perfected guide

Imam Abu Hamid Muhammad al-Ghazali names the guide by an old term: *Shaykh al-Kamil*, the perfected spiritual guide. The seeker who has resolved his belief, repented honestly, settled his outer life, and acquired the Shari'ah he needs still cannot make spiritual progress alone. He needs someone who has walked the path to walk in front of him.

Ghazali compares the work of the guide to the work of a farmer. The farmer goes out to the field every day. He looks for the weeds that have grown overnight, and he pulls them out by the roots. He waters and fertilizes the crop so that what was planted can grow into its true form. The spiritual guide does the same with the seeker. He spots the inner weeds, the vanity, the resentment, the appetites that have not been examined, and uproots them. He nourishes what the seeker is meant to become.

> In every condition, the traveler on the Path of Allah has no cure or solution but to have a *Murshid al-Kamil*, a true Shaykh, a perfected spiritual guide.

Ghazali explains why this is structural and not optional. Allah, the Exalted, sent the Prophet Muhammad, peace and blessings of Allah be upon him, into the world to be an illuminated proof, leading people on the way to God. When the Prophet departed from this world, he appointed his deputies and successors to continue that work, so that the proof would remain until the Day of Judgment.

That is the lineage the seeker is asking to enter when he attaches himself to a true Shaykh. He is not signing up for a self-help arrangement. He is joining a chain that runs back to the Prophet himself.

## What a perfected guide looks like

Because the institution of the guide can be impersonated, Ghazali next gives his student a hard list of qualities. Anyone calling himself a Shaykh who does not meet these is not a Shaykh, no matter how many followers he has.

A perfected guide is, first, a scholar, but Ghazali notes immediately that not every scholar is a *Shaykh al-Kamil*. Beyond scholarship, this person has turned his face away from the love of the world, the love of status, and the love of worldly rank. He has given the *bay'ah*, the oath of allegiance, to a perfected guide of his own, who has given it to his guide before him, in a chain that goes back to the Prophet.

He has done every kind of inner struggle, every kind of *Mujahadah*. He has obeyed the Prophet's example without exception. He eats less than he wants. He sleeps less than is comfortable. He prays the obligatory prayers and many optional ones. He fasts beyond what is required. He spends and gives in the way of God.

His inner character carries: patience, gratitude, certitude in God, generosity, contentment, trustworthiness, tolerance, seriousness of purpose, humility, obedience, truthfulness, modesty, dignity, and tranquility. He has acquired a light from the lights of the Prophet, and the evil qualities, miserliness, jealousy, enmity, the expectation of reward from the world, anger, rebellion, have been eradicated from him.

> This person should have acquired a light from the lights of the Messenger of Allah, peace and blessings be upon him.

Following such a person is the correct way. The point Ghazali is making here is not theoretical. He is warning his student: do not attach yourself to anyone who does not have the marks. The price of attaching yourself to a false guide is the corruption of the disciple. The Quran names the same logic when it warns:

> O you who believe, be conscious of Allah and be with the truthful.
> (Quran, At-Tawbah 9:119; English: Yusuf Ali. Subsequent Quranic translations in this chapter follow the same translator.)

The being-with is itself the discipline. Who you keep company with shapes who you become.

## Outer etiquettes the disciple owes the guide

True spiritual guides are difficult to find. But the disciple who finds one, and is accepted as a *mureed*, a disciple under his guidance, has a series of obligations toward the guide that take shape in both his outer conduct and his inner state.

The outer etiquettes are mostly about not making the relationship into a stage for the disciple's self-display.

The disciple does not argue with the guide. If a disagreement begins, and the disciple thinks the guide has forgotten something, the disciple does not object in the moment. He waits. He revisits in private if at all. He does not turn the relationship into a debate.

The disciple does not stand in the place of prayer all the time, performing extra prayers to look pious in front of the guide. He prays the obligatory prayers when their time comes. When he is done, he wraps up the prayer mat. He does not pile up optional prayers to be seen.

Whatever the guide commands within the bounds of the Shari'ah, the disciple obeys to the best of his capacity. Ghazali is careful here: a true guide will never command anything that is forbidden in the Shari'ah. The disciple's obedience runs only inside the Shari'ah. The impersonators, the false guides, are the ones who command disciples to do what the law forbids. Such commands are themselves the signal that the person giving them is not what he claims to be.

## Inner etiquettes the disciple owes the guide

The outer rules are visible. The inner rules are the harder ones, and they are where the disciple either really begins or really stops.

> Whatever the disciple apparently hears from the *Murshid*, the spiritual guide, he should not entertain even a small grain of doubt in his heart about the Shaykh's words or deeds, otherwise the disciple will be called a hypocrite.

This is a hard sentence, and it needs the right context to be heard correctly. Ghazali is not asking for blind obedience. He has already named the qualifications a true Shaykh must have, and the disciple is supposed to have verified those qualifications before attaching. Once the attachment is made, to a Shaykh who is what he is supposed to be, the disciple has to bring his inner life into alignment with the relationship. Outer compliance with inner suspicion is hypocrisy. If the disciple cannot align his inside to his outside, the right move is not to maintain the broken alignment. The right move is to leave the company of the Shaykh until the alignment can be honest.

> If the disciple cannot do that, then he should abstain from the company of the spiritual guide until his inside becomes like his outside.

This is the gift inside the demand. Ghazali is not asking for permanence. He is asking for honesty. If the heart is not aligned, the body should not pretend. Step away. Work on the inner state. Come back when there is something real to bring.

The same demand for inner-outer alignment shows up across the Sufi tradition. Ibn Ata Allah al-Iskandari, in his *Hikam* (Aphorisms), names it this way:

> Bury your existence in the earth of obscurity, for whatever sprouts forth without first being buried, flowers imperfectly.
> Source: Ibn Ata Allah, *Hikam*, Aphorism 11.

The disciple who is in a hurry to be seen as a disciple is not yet a disciple. The disciple who can sit silently in obscurity, doing the work, is the one who eventually flowers.

## The sixth condition: refusing the deceptions of the lower soul

The sixth condition is closely related but operates inside the disciple's own head. The seeker must refuse the deceptions of the *nafs*, the lower soul, which is a subtle inner faculty that commands toward evil by whispering into the heart of the person it inhabits.

The lower soul does not announce itself. It speaks in the disciple's own voice. It tells him what he wants to hear. It offers him reasons to skip the night prayer, to argue with the guide, to attend the gathering of the wrong people. Ghazali gives one practical instruction: abandon the gatherings of those whose character is corrupt and those who are ignorant. When you stop sitting in those gatherings, the lower soul loses access to the language it uses to whisper. The domination of Satan over the heart begins to come to an end, because Satan's recruits among humans and *jinn* lose their access to the disciple.

Imam Ali ibn Abi Talib, peace be upon him, named this same dynamic plainly:

> The companion is the one who imitates the companion. Look at whom you have made your companion. He is the index of your soul.
> Source: Imam Ali (AS), recorded in *Ghurar al-Hikam* §3920.

The lower soul has friends. The friends are how it gets in.

## The seventh condition: humility over status, contentment over wealth

The seventh condition Ghazali names tightly. In every situation, the seeker should give preference to humility over the desire for status, and to contentment over the desire for wealth. These two preferences are mutually reinforcing. The man who is content with his portion does not need status to feel large. The man who is humble does not need wealth to feel safe.

> These seven things are essential for the traveler on the Path of Allah, the Exalted, and for the seeker of the truth.

Seven conditions in total: correct belief, true repentance, settling outer claims, the necessary knowledge of the Shari'ah, attaching to a perfected guide, refusing the deceptions of the lower soul, and the preference for humility and contentment over status and wealth. None of the seven is optional. Each one supports the next. Each one fails on its own.

## Tasawwuf

His student had asked, *What is Tasawwuf?*, the inner discipline that English readers loosely call Sufism. Ghazali names it in two qualities.

The first is that the seeker should be obedient to Allah, acting on the Shari'ah, the outer law of Islam, with its obligations, commands, and prohibitions.

The second is that the seeker should be sympathetic and a doer of good toward all of Allah's creations. This second quality is not possible without first purifying the soul of its evil qualities and then embellishing it with good ones.

A *Sufi* is one who has perseverance in acting on the Shari'ah and the qualities of desiring true betterment for all human beings. Ghazali adds a sharp aside here: this is what the term *Sufi* actually means, as opposed to the wrong uses that are common today. The corruption of the term, by people who wear the cloak and use the language without the inner work, was an old problem in his time and is older now.

The two qualities together describe the architecture: vertical and horizontal. *Loyalty to Allah is that the seeker sacrifices his own happiness for the sake of Allah. Sincerity to all people is that the seeker does not keep relationships with people for his own motives, but instead dedicates himself for the good of all the people, on the condition that this welfare is in accordance with the Shari'ah.*

Both ends matter. Vertical alone produces a hermit who has no obligations to his neighbor. Horizontal alone produces a social worker with no anchor. *Tasawwuf*, in Ghazali's definition, is both at once.

Dhu'l-Nun al-Misri, the early Egyptian Sufi master, once said something Ghazali quotes in the next passage of the letter:

> If you have the courage to exert your utmost with your soul in this way, then come and step in. Otherwise, do not just get indulged in the nice things that the Sufis say.
> Source: Dhu'l-Nun al-Misri.

The warning is exact. The vocabulary is the easy part. The cost is what most people will not pay.

## Servitude

The student had asked, *What is servitude?* Ghazali names it in three things.

The first is to obey the commands of the Shari'ah, the outer law.

The second is to be content with the decree and destiny that Allah, the Exalted, has appointed.

The third is to leave one's own desires and prerogatives behind, and to be happy with the desire and prerogative of Allah.

The three are progressive. The first is outer behavior. The second is inner acceptance. The third is the surrender of preference itself. You can do the first without the second. You can do the first and the second without the third. Only the person who has done all three is fully a servant.

The Prophet named the third level, the surrender of preference, in the famous saying:

> None of you truly believes until his desire follows what I have brought.
> (Sahih Bukhari, recorded in *Riyad al-Salihin*, Hadith 159.)

The desire follows. The believer does not negotiate with the revelation. He lets his wanting be reshaped by the truth.

## Tawakkul

The student had asked, *What is Tawakkul?*, reliance on God. Ghazali defines it precisely: *Tawakkul is to have firm belief and certitude in what Allah, the Exalted, has promised.*

He spells out what this looks like in practice. You should believe that you will receive whatever Allah has destined for you. If the whole world tries to prevent it, it will not be prevented. And what is not written in your destiny, if you and the whole world strive for it, you will never receive.

This is not passivity. Ghazali is careful elsewhere to insist that the seeker must work. The point of *Tawakkul* is the inner posture that accompanies the work. You work because the working is your part. You do not anxiously try to control the outcome, because the outcome is not your part.

The classic saying attributed to the Prophet names the same architecture:

> Tie your camel and trust in Allah.
> (Sunan Tirmidhi, Hadith 2517.)

Tie the camel, that is the work. Trust in Allah, that is *Tawakkul*. Both clauses are required. Neither one is enough by itself.

## Ikhlas

The last of the four inner stations is *Ikhlas*, sincerity. The student had asked, *What is sincerity to God?* Ghazali defines it: *Ikhlas is that all of your deeds should be exclusively for the sake of Allah, and for His pleasure.*

The negative form is sharper than the positive. The deed must not be done for show, for *riya*, ostentation. When you do a good deed, your heart should not be turned toward people: not to impress them, not to gain their praise. Your heart should not be pleased by their praise, nor grieved by their criticism.

Ghazali names the source of *riya*. It is born out of the desire for the praises and honors of people. The cure he prescribes is to look at the entire created universe and see it as subservient to the power of Allah, the Exalted. The creations, he says, are like pebbles and stones. Pebbles cannot bring you grief. Pebbles cannot bring you comfort. They do not have the power to give or to withhold. Once you really see this, that no human being and no human approval has independent power over your state, the desire to perform for them dissolves.

> Until you have this belief, that the creations have power to bring you grief and comfort, *riya* will never get out of your heart.

The cure of ostentation is not to perform less. The cure is to see correctly who is watching.

## What the letter is doing here

Ghazali pauses at the end of this section to address his student. *Your remaining questions are such that some of them have been addressed in books that we have written, which you can see there. Some of your questions are such that writing their answers is forbidden.* He asks his student to act on what is written, so that the unwritten matters can be clarified to him through practice.

> After this, whatever matter you find difficult and do not understand, do not ask about it other than with the tongue of your heart.

The tongue of the heart is a Sufi phrase for the inner ask, the question you ask God in silence. It is the practice that opens what no further reading will open. Ghazali quotes the Quran's gentle correction of those who lacked patience for the Prophet's example:

> If those people had exercised patience until you, O Prophet, came out yourself, it would have been better for them.
> (Quran, Al-Hujurat 49:5.)

And he quotes Khidr, the figure who guided the Prophet Musa, in the Quranic story:

> If you want to follow me, then do not question me about anything that I may do until I myself mention it to you.
> (Quran, Al-Kahf 18:70.)

Don't rush. When the time comes, you yourself will be shown.

> By Allah! If you illuminate your heart, you will certainly see amazing things. You should strive your utmost in every step of the way toward your destination. Other than that, the purpose will not be achieved.

That is the close of this beat. The architecture of the seeker has been laid out: the seven conditions, the four inner stations. The work is not light. The reward, the seeing, is real, but the work comes first.
