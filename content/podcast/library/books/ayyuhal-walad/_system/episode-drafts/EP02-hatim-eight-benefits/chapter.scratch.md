# Haatim's Eight Benefits

*From Ayyuhal Walad ("O Beloved Son") by Imam Abu Hamid Muhammad al-Ghazali. This chapter covers the single self-contained vignette in the letter: a teacher named Shaqiq al-Balkhi asks his student of thirty-three years, Hatim bin Ism al-Asamm, what he has actually learned. The student gives eight numbered benefits, each anchored in a verse of the Quran.*

## A question that lands hard

In the middle of his answer to his former student, Ghazali pauses the argument and tells a story. He says: pay close attention to what follows, because it will give you the kind of certainty you have been asking for.

Among the students and disciples of Shaqiq al-Balkhi, may Allah have mercy upon him, was a young man named Hatim bin Ism al-Asamm, who would go on to become a great spiritual master in his own right. One day Shaqeeq turned to his student and asked him a question.

> O Haatim, how long have you been in my company and listened to what I have been saying?

Haatim answered: *Thirty-three years.*

Shaqeeq pressed on: *In all that time, what benefit have you derived from me?*

Haatim said: *I have obtained eight benefits.*

It is at this point that Shaqeeq's reaction has the quality of a parent hearing the smallest tally he could have feared. He said the verse from the Quran that Muslims say at moments of loss and grief:

> Indeed, to Allah we belong, and to Him we shall return.
> (Quran, Al-Baqarah 2:156; English: Yusuf Ali. Subsequent Quranic translations in this chapter follow the same translator.)

Then he said: *O Haatim, I have spent my lifetime teaching you, and you have just gained eight benefits from me?*

Haatim replied without flinching. *My teacher, if you ask me for the truth, this is the reality. I do not need more. These eight are enough to carry me through this life and into the next.*

Then, at his teacher's invitation, he gave them.

What follows is the structure that has kept this small vignette alive in Sufi memory for more than a thousand years. Notice the shape. Each benefit begins the same way. Haatim looks at the people around him. He sees what they are doing. He sees that what they are doing does not work. Then he meditates on a verse of the Quran. The verse names what does work. And he reorganizes his life around that.

Eight times he does this. Eight pivots in thirty-three years. The arithmetic is not as small as it first sounded.

## The first benefit: the beloved that follows you in

> I looked at the people of this world and saw that everyone has a beloved. But these beloveds of the people are such that some of them stay with the lover until the illness that brings death, some until death itself, some until the burial. After the grave is closed, the lovers all go home. None of these beloveds enters the grave with the one who loved them.

This is the observation. The lovers are real. The bond is real. The departure is also real. The wife or husband or child or friend or honor, none of these accompanies the dead person into the grave.

So Haatim contemplated which beloved would.

> I saw that the only beloved with these qualities, to go with you into the grave, to keep you company there, to illuminate the grave, to stand beside you on the Day of Judgment, and to never leave, was my good deeds.

He made his good deeds his beloved. Not in the sentimental sense. In the sense that they got the affection and attention that a beloved normally receives. The Prophet Muhammad, peace and blessings of Allah be upon him, taught this same architecture:

> When a person dies, his actions come to an end except for three: a continuing charity, a knowledge from which benefit is gained, and a pious child who prays for him.
> (Sahih Muslim, Book 13, Hadith 4005.)

The three things that survive a person are themselves all deeds. They are the things that you did that keep on doing after you. Haatim had built his life so that what survived him was what he had done, not what he had been given.

## The second benefit: the lower soul does not get what it asks for

> I glanced at the people of the world and saw that everyone was following after the pleasures and desires of the *nafs*, the lower soul, and was subservient to it.

In Sufi technical vocabulary, the *nafs* is the part of you that pulls toward base appetite, that wants what feels good now regardless of what it costs later. The Quran names three states of this lower soul: the soul that incites to evil, the soul that reproaches itself, and the soul at peace. Most people, Haatim saw, were stuck at the first stage, and they did not know it. They believed they were free. They were obeying their appetites without examining them.

He thought about this verse:

> But the one who has stood in fear of his Lord's presence, and restrained his lower soul from base desire, paradise will be his abode.
> (Quran, An-Nazi'at 79:40-41)

He decided the Quran was telling the truth and that the rest of the world was wrong. So he set himself against his own lower soul.

> I established a front against my *nafs* and became determined to oppose it. I did not fulfill any of its desires until it began to find peace in the worship of Allah, the Exalted.

Notice what he did. He did not negotiate with the lower soul. He did not strike a balance. He did not say, *fine, you can have half of what you want.* He starved it of yeses until it stopped asking. The same pattern shows up in the saying of Imam Ali ibn Abi Talib, peace be upon him, recorded in *Nahj al-Balagha*:

> Do not let the lower soul be a partner in your decisions, for it is your greatest enemy. It speaks in your own voice, so you will not notice its arguments.
> Source: Imam Ali (AS), recorded as *Ghurar al-Hikam* §3960.

The lower soul wins by sounding like you.

## The third benefit: the wealth that does not come to an end

> When I glanced at the people, I saw that every person, with great pain and hard work, is busy accumulating the possessions of this mortal world and is very happy that he has a lot of them.

Then he thought about this verse:

> All that is with you is bound to come to an end. All that, which is with Allah, is everlasting.
> (Quran, An-Nahl 16:96)

So whatever he had accumulated in this world, he spent it in the way of Allah. He distributed it to the pious poor and the destitute, so that what he had built up would be deposited with God rather than left behind in the dust.

The Prophet named this dynamic plainly:

> The son of Adam says, *My wealth, my wealth!* What is yours, O son of Adam, except what you ate and consumed, what you wore and used up, and what you gave in charity and let go ahead of you?
> (Sahih Muslim, Book 42, Hadith 7088.)

The food you ate is gone. The clothes you wore are gone. Only what you gave away is still arriving somewhere.

## The fourth benefit: grandeur belongs to God-consciousness, not to tribe or money

> I looked at the people and saw that some of them think grandeur and honor belong to the big and powerful tribe, so they take pride in their tribe. Some think it is in wealth, so they take pride in their wealth and their children. Some think it is in anger, beating, killing, and pillage, so they take pride in their violence. Some think it is in lavish spending, so they take pride in their waste.

Each of these is a candidate for what gives a life its weight. Each one is wrong. Haatim thought about this verse:

> Indeed, the most honored of you in the sight of Allah is the one who is most deeply conscious of Him.
> (Quran, Al-Hujurat 49:13)

The Quran is clear: *taqwa* is what counts. It is the watchful awareness of God's presence that shapes how a person speaks and acts when no one is watching. None of the four worldly candidates for honor, bloodline, wealth, force, ostentation, has anything to do with *taqwa*. So Haatim adopted *taqwa* as the inner discipline of his life, and he stopped using the other four candidates as scoreboards.

The shape of this benefit reappears in a saying of HH Aga Khan IV, in a farman to his community: *Knowledge is honored, not because of the diplomas, but because of what it makes a person do.* The principle is older than the modern voice, Haatim is naming it here from a Quranic verse eight hundred years before, and *Nahj al-Balagha* names it too:

> Honor lies in the heart, not in the position. The lowest of people is the one who thinks his honor is what he owns.
> Source: Imam Ali, *Nahj al-Balagha*, Aphorism 113.

## The fifth benefit: envy ends when you trust the distribution

> I looked at the people, and they were complaining about each other. They were doing this out of envy and jealousy and enmity. And the main cause of all that was the competition for greatness, for possessions, for wealth, for knowledge.

Then he thought about this verse:

> It is We who distribute their means of livelihood among them in the life of this world.
> (Quran, Az-Zukhruf 43:32)

Allah, he understood, has already decided who gets what. The distribution happened long before any of us showed up. No amount of competing changes the outcome of that distribution. So he stopped competing. He stopped envying. He stopped having grudges. He let the others have what they had been given, because they had been given it by the same hand that had given him what he had.

The Prophet named this exact discipline:

> Look at those who are below you, and do not look at those who are above you, for that is more likely to keep you from belittling the blessings of Allah upon you.
> (Sahih Muslim, Book 53, Hadith 21.)

The trick is not to look around at all. The trick is to look down, toward the ones who have less than you, and let that view define the size of what you have.

## The sixth benefit: there is only one enemy

> When I glanced at the people, I saw that every person has enmity with someone else for some reason.

Then he thought about this verse:

> Verily, Satan is your enemy, so treat him as your enemy.
> (Quran, Fatir 35:6)

If Satan is the enemy named by God, then no human being is your enemy. The person you think is your enemy is just a person, doing his thing, mostly without thinking about you. The enemy is the inner voice that tells you the person is your enemy. That voice is what Haatim decided to oppose.

> Since then, I considered Satan as my enemy and did not obey any of his orders. Instead, I followed the commandments of Allah and adopted only His worship.

The Quran names the alternative directly:

> Did I not make a covenant with you, O children of Adam, that you should not worship Satan? Verily, he is your open enemy. Worship Me alone, this is the straight path.
> (Quran, Ya-Sin 36:60-61)

The straight path, the *Sirat al-Mustaqeem*, is the path you walk when you have finally figured out who the enemy actually is.

## The seventh benefit: God has guaranteed the rations

> I looked at the people and saw that every person is preoccupied with his livelihood, in an intense effort to find sustenance. In this, he is not even differentiating between what is permitted and what is forbidden. He is humiliating himself with doubtful and forbidden earnings.

Then he thought about this verse:

> And there is no living creature on earth whose sustenance is not the responsibility of Allah.
> (Quran, Hud 11:6)

He realized he was also a living creature on earth. The same promise covered him. So he got engaged in the worship of Allah and trusted that the sustenance would arrive, because the sustenance had been promised by the one who keeps promises.

This is the beat that anchors the next benefit, and it requires care. Haatim is not saying *do nothing and food will appear*. He is saying *do not violate the Shari'ah to chase what God has already promised you*. The Prophet said:

> If you trusted in Allah with true reliance, He would provide for you as He provides for the birds. They leave their nests in the morning hungry and return in the evening full.
> (Sunan Tirmidhi, Hadith 2344.)

The birds work. They leave the nest. But they leave the nest without the panic that they will not be provided for.

## The eighth benefit: reliance on the only one worth relying on

> I found that every person's reliance was on someone or something. Some trusted in their wealth. Some trusted in other people.

Then he thought about this verse:

> And whoever places his trust in Allah, He alone is enough for him.
> (Quran, At-Talaq 65:3)

And the verse that follows:

> And He is sufficient for me, and He is the best Disposer of affairs.
> (Quran, Aal-Imran 3:173 / At-Tawbah 9:129.)

So Haatim placed his complete reliance on Allah in every matter. Tawakkul, reliance on God after taking right action, became the architecture of his decisions. Not a sentiment. An architecture.

## What Shaqeeq said when he heard the eight

When Shaqeeq, his teacher of thirty-three years, heard the eight benefits, he said:

> O Haatim, may Allah grant you the success to do righteous deeds and walk on His path. You have told us very good things. I have seen in the Torah, in the Gospel, in the Psalms, and in the Holy Quran, that all four of these books name these eight benefits. Whoever acts on these eight has acted on all four books.

This is the most important sentence in the vignette, and it is easy to miss. The teacher does not say, *good summary*. He says, *these eight are what every revealed book has been trying to teach you*. Eight inner pivots: who you actually love, who has authority over your appetites, where your wealth ends up, what gives a life weight, why envy ends, who the real enemy is, who feeds you, who you rely on. Each pivot is the answer to a question every revealed scripture has been pressing.

Ghazali ends his retelling with a sentence aimed straight at his former student.

> My dear beloved son, you now know from this story that you do not need a lot of knowledge.

You need eight pivots. You can find them in any honest reading of the Quran. Most people walk past them. Haatim did not.
