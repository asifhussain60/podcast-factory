# Soul and Spirit: One Substance or Two?

*This chapter covers all six sub-chapters of* Chapter Three *of* Kitab al-Riyad — *al-Kirmani's judgment among the* hudud *on a question the previous chapter set up but did not answer: the Second (the universal Soul) and the lower soul (al-Hayuli, prime matter) — are they similar to the First or not? The author of* al-Islah *had argued that this world contains darkness because the Second is born from the First and resembles him, while* al-Hayuli *is only a trace of the Second and so does not resemble it. The author of* al-Nusra *had refused: if light and darkness are absolute and untouching, there is no contact between worlds. Al-Kirmani settles the dispute. The First and Second are counterparts, "the mineral of honor and perfection"; al-Hayuli is the trace of the Second, the essence of darkness manifested by the power learned from the First. Resemblance is not the condition of contact. The chapter then works the same doctrine through the architecture of cause and effect (ma'lul), through the question of whether the Second has parts or sides, through the claim that the Second "fell" by distance from the First, and through the closing problem of whether nature is born of soul as soul is born of intellect. The whole chapter is a defense of one settled formula: the Second is the like-counterpart of the First; al-Hayuli is the trace beneath, brought forth not by resemblance but by the power of the First operating through the Second.*

## Where this chapter picks up

*Chapter Two* held the line on what the First Intellect is not — not a body, not in motion, not joined to a co-present "creativity" alongside it. The categorical mismatch had been the master-error: bodies and intellects do not share predicates. Now *Chapter Three* takes the matching step downward. If the First Intellect is what it is, and the Second is the Soul that the First emits, then what stands one further step down? The author of *al-Islah* had a name for it from the philosophers — *al-Hayuli*, prime matter, the substrate of the natural world. The author of *al-Nusra* called the same matter "the lower soul" or simply "matter," and pressed a different question: how can the higher and lower worlds touch at all if one is light and the other is darkness?

That, more than any other question, is what *Chapter Three* exists to settle. The chapter's title in the manuscript records it bluntly: *On the judgment among the* hudud *concerning what was discussed about the soul and the lower soul: are they similar to the First or not?* In the perceiving language of al-Kirmani's day, "soul" meant the universal Soul, the Second, the rank emitted from the First Intellect. "Lower soul" meant either *al-Hayuli* itself — prime matter as the essence of the lower world — or, sometimes, the rational soul as it inhabits the natural composite. The question across the chapter is the same: are these substances continuous with the First, or categorically separated from it?

The answer the chapter builds — sub-chapter by sub-chapter — is neither. The Second is the like-counterpart of the First. *Al-Hayuli* is the trace of the Second, the essence of darkness in this world. The two worlds do not need to resemble each other to be in contact. Contact happens by emission and by the power learned from the One who emits. The doctrine is precise because the architecture of the *da'wa* rests on it. If the Second cannot be linked to the First by resemblance, and *al-Hayuli* cannot be linked to the Second by birth, then the *natiq*, the *imam*, and the *hudud* must be linked to their principles by something other than likeness. They are linked by the same chain of emission al-Kirmani has been describing since *Chapter One*.

## Sub-chapter one — the trace, not the birth

The author of *al-Islah* opened *Chapter Three* with a defense of his earlier position. The author of *al-Nusra* had proved, on his own reading, that nature and darkness are born of the Second the way the Second is born of the First. *Al-Islah* refused. We do not say that, he wrote. The Second is born of the First because that world is the world of honor and virtue, and the Second resembles the First even in being one rank lower. This world, by contrast, is the world of darkness and ignorance. *Al-Hayuli* is not born of the Second; it is only a trace of it. The Second resembles its principle; *al-Hayuli* does not.

The author of *al-Nusra* counter-pressed. This is wrong, he said. If that world were light with no darkness in it, and this world were dark with no light in it, then there would be no connection between this world and that world. The two would be sealed off from each other. Rather we say — *al-Nusra* proposed — that the Soul has two extremes. One toward the intellect, and on its horizon is the luminous, noble side. The other toward nature, in which there is some darkness, as it were a circle, because nature is on its horizon. And nature itself has two ends. One toward the partial soul, in which there is some light because it is on the horizon of the Soul; the other facing the elements, which are the darkness in which there is no light at all. So both worlds are mixed at their edges. We do not say that this whole world is the world of light with no darkness in it; nor that this whole world is a world of darkness with no light in it. If it were as *al-Islah* described, there would be no contact between them at all.

*Al-Nusra* pressed further. It is true, he said, that the Talmanic spheres are born of the Soul although they do not resemble it — just as the Soul is born of the First although it does not resemble it. The Soul has fallen into a state of frailty and darkness because of its distance from the First Cause, and this is what made it possible to be born from the Soul. Likewise the diminution that fell upon the Soul, because it is beyond the First Cause, is what enables the further chain of generation. *This is what he said.*

Al-Kirmani's answer is careful — and consequential.

*The wording of what the author of* al-Islah *said in his book is different from what the author of* al-Nusra *said.* The gist of *al-Nusra's* saying, al-Kirmani records, is that the Second is born of the First and is not like the First. *Al-Islah* refused that formulation. Rather, *al-Islah* said, the Second is born of the First, is not like the First, and yet *resembles* him — though the First is of higher rank and the more honorable station. Like the male and the female in every race and species. The male, even though he is higher and more honorable than the female, is nonetheless her counterpart and her likeness. The Second is the counterpart of the First in the rank below.

Then al-Kirmani gives the doctrine that the whole chapter will lean on. The First and the Second are two counterparts — *the mineral of honor, perfection, and elevation*. There is no honor above them. Allah has made them the mineral of perfect honor and perfect light, complete elevation, complete knowledge — with no ignorance there, no cycle, no darkness whatsoever. They founded the higher world, which is the mineral of purity, *al-Quds* (sanctity), and life. *Al-Hayuli* is a trace of the Second, which is the essence of this world; it is the essence of darkness, which cannot be described by mercy or power — yet the Second has manifested it. By means of the power learned from the First, the Second then brought forth from *al-Hayuli* the image, which is nature.

The settlement is not that *al-Nusra* is wrong on every word. *Al-Islah* was not obliged to claim that the entire higher world is light without any darkness in it as an axiomatic premise; the proof that *al-Hayuli* is not born from the Second is enough on its own. There is no need to belabor the discussion with matters that are not the question. The question is the relation of *al-Hayuli* to the Second, and the answer is that *al-Hayuli* is the trace of the Second, not its birth.

*Al-Kirmani* puts it in his own formula: *al-Hayuli is not like the Second, nor like the First, because the existence of the Second and the First is by their emission* from the First — at the lower proportion that I have shown in our earlier words and in our well-known book *Rahat al-'Aql* (*The Repose of the Intellect*). That is enough.

The reference to *Rahat al-'Aql* is precise. There al-Kirmani had laid out the cosmology in full: the First Intellect emerges from the act of the Almighty Creator in the realm of origination (*dar al-Ibda'*) in a single complete eternal existence. The Second is emitted from the First in the same act. From the Second's overflow, the spheres of the natural world (*dar al-Inbi'ath* — the realm of emanation) are generated, and *al-Hayuli* receives the impression of the Second's power — not as a child receives the form of a parent, but as a mirror catches a shape that does not resemble the light that casts it. *Rahat al-'Aql* maps the seven seas through which the overflow chain runs; *al-Riyad* assumes that map and uses it to adjudicate where *al-Islah* and *al-Nusra* misplaced the categories.

The image that al-Kirmani is reaching for, and that *al-Nusra* missed, is something like this. Two adjacent regions of being do not need to share a color to share a border. The border is the work of the power that joins them. The First and Second share honor because they are like-counterparts. *Al-Hayuli* shares no honor with the Second; *al-Hayuli* is darkness. But the Second meets *al-Hayuli* through the power learned from the First, and from that meeting nature comes forth. *Al-Nusra's* worry that light and dark cannot touch is the worry of someone who has imagined the two worlds as two opaque substances. They are not. The higher world is the agent of contact. The lower world is the patient. There is no need for the two to resemble each other for the work to be done.

Imam Ali (peace be upon him) is reported in the aphorisms of his tradition as having said that *the intellect is the messenger of the truth*. The messenger is not the truth, nor is the messenger like what he carries. The messenger is the means by which the truth reaches the one who is to receive it. The messenger does not need to share the form of what is being delivered. He needs only to be able to deliver. That is precisely the function al-Kirmani assigns to the Second in its relation to *al-Hayuli*: the Second is the messenger of the First's power into the world of darkness, and the trace that *al-Hayuli* carries is not the Second's likeness but the impression of the power that was delivered through it.

## Sub-chapter two — the Soul has two aspects, neither of them like the First

The author of *al-Nusra* repeats his objection — that *al-Islah's* picture leaves no contact between the worlds — and al-Kirmani works through the same ground again, but now from a different angle. The Soul, he says, is from the world of creativity and renewal (*dar al-Ibda'*). What is common to the Soul suits what is above it by being emitted, and what is below it suits being a body. The Soul has two aspects. Neither of them is like the First from which the Soul exists.

The Second resembles the First on one side and does not resemble the First on the other. The side from which the Second resembles the First is that it is emitted — emission is the principle of the higher world, and the Second shares emission with the First. The side from which the Second does not resemble the First is that the Second is also that which receives the natural body — the form of nature that was added to the world of creativity and renewal — became a life that is sound from that world without its direct presence, and gives nature what nature has the capacity to accept of backwardness and perception. *And if it is added to this world, it would be the principle of movement, and would be in the thing that is in it itself, not by way of presentation, and it would become nature — even if it is emitted in its existence, or a medium between the two emissions, the First and the Second.*

The Soul, in al-Kirmani's careful formulation, is the medium between the two emissions. Above it stands the First — pure emission, complete actuality, no admixture of body or nature. Below it stands what the Soul gives rise to through the power learned from the First — the natural world, *al-Hayuli* receiving the image, the spheres rolling in their orbits, the bodies of the elemental world coming to be. The Soul is what holds these two together. It is the rank at which emission meets receivership. From above it receives; below it gives. Its two aspects are not "two halves" of a single substance, the way the philosophers of antiquity imagined the soul as a divided thing. They are the two relations of one rank: the relation upward to its principle, and the relation downward to what its overflow generates.

The image that the Cairo-court treatises of Imam al-Mu'izz li-Din Allah make explicit — Imam al-Mu'izz being the source al-Kirmani repeatedly reaches for when settling Tawhid questions — is that of a single light that is one in itself but lights two different regions of what stands below it. The light does not divide. The regions differ in their capacity. The higher region receives the light as light. The lower region receives the light as warmth, as movement, as the patterned change we call nature. To name the Soul as having two aspects is not to bifurcate the Soul; it is to name the two ways the one Soul stands in relation to the ranks above and below.

This formulation refuses *al-Nusra's* picture of light and darkness as opposed substances meeting at a border. There is no border in the picture al-Kirmani is drawing. There is one chain of existence. What looks like a border to *al-Nusra* is in fact the difference of capacity at different ranks. The Soul is the rank at which the chain bends from emission into receivership — but the Soul itself is still wholly within the world of creativity. It does not "fall" into darkness. *Al-Hayuli* falls; the Soul does not.

## Sub-chapter three — no parts, no sides, no diameters

The author of *al-Nusra* now presses a more specific version of his earlier claim. By "the Soul" we mean the Second, he says, and the Second has two sides. One side toward the intellect, on its horizon — the noble luminous limit. Another part turned toward nature, lying upon nature's horizon — the side in which there is some darkness.

*Al-Kirmani* refuses. *This is not correct.*

The proof he gives is sharp. The one possessing parts is the one possessing diameters estimated by area. Parts are the diameters of bodies. *Al-Nusra* has called the Second a Soul — and on the basis al-Kirmani has already explained, the Second is not an embodiment. If the Second is not an embodiment, then it cannot have parts in the sense *al-Nusra* requires. To say the Second has "a luminous limb that lies upon the horizon of the intellect" and "a limb in which there is some darkness lying within the interpretation of nature that faces the intellect" is to talk as though the Second were a body with two sides. *Al-Nusra* has imported the geometry of bodies into a rank where bodies cannot reach.

The doctrine al-Kirmani substitutes is one he has been building since *Chapter One*. The Second's existence is derived from a noble proportion in the First — the proportion of being a cause. The Second is also itself an effect (*ma'lul*); and being an effect adds another proportion below the cause-proportion. *And I have already said that the first is that which exists and is not in itself a cause.* The First is what exists pure. The cause-proportion in the First is what gives rise to the Second. The effect-proportion in the First — that is, the First considered as itself standing in relation to what is below it — is what determines that the Second's existence is from the lower proportion.

The effect (*ma'lul*), by being a cause in addition to what stands below it (which is the lower proportion in the First), and the effect, by being added to itself (which is the lower proportion in the First), is from its own intellect. It is a cause from which something like itself emanates, for the cause has its effect upon it. From its own intellect, the effect is a *malaisa* — a non-being, in the technical sense — from which emanates that which is not like it, by being an emanation from the lower proportion in the First.

This is dense and al-Kirmani knows it. The point that matters most for the chapter is the next one. The honor of the effect's proportion is greater than that of the cause's proportion. *The cause receives what is higher than what comes about from the cause, except for that which is below the effect.* The relation runs both ways. The cause receives more than it emits, in one direction. The effect emits less than it receives, in the other.

What this denies to *al-Nusra* is the picture of the Second as a body-like thing with luminous and dark sides. The Second has no sides. It has proportions — a cause-proportion and an effect-proportion — and those proportions are not parts in space. They are differences in rank within the same single self. The Second is *one* self that acquires two proportions in its additions. To call those proportions "limbs" is to confuse the language of relation with the language of geometry.

The image al-Kirmani reaches for to make this concrete is the relation of *the speaker and the foundation*. He has used this image before in the earlier chapters. The "foundation" is the rank that bears existence; the "speaker" is the one whose presence in the foundation indicates the existence of what stands above. Being invisible to the speaker, all that was originally there is present in the speaker in whom the existence of the foundation lay. And likewise, not all that the speaker has is present in the foundation, because the speaker has what the foundation does not share — the visual in which the foundation does not participate. The foundation in which the one within it is a participant of the speaker, and there is nothing in the speaker that he does not indicate. If he does not indicate it, then it is the likeness by which the existence of the Second is inferred according to its rank.

The point of the image is to refuse the language of "sides." The Second's existence is inferred from its rank in the chain, not from a survey of its dimensions. To imagine it as having a "luminous side" and a "dark side" is to make exactly the categorical mistake *Chapter Two* exposed: applying body-categories to a rank that is not embodied. *And its rank was the second. It is not made manifest to the first, and if it is not indicated, then its existence is what was mentioned, namely that it lies upon the horizon of nature, which is impossible.*

What *al-Nusra* needed in order for his picture to work was a Second that could be split. There is no such Second.

## Sub-chapter four — distance from the First does not make a thing

The author of *al-Nusra* now offers a different reason for why *al-Hayuli* fell into darkness: because of its distance from the First Cause, in order that the Soul might be born from it.

The reformulation tries to lean the explanation on *distance* — as though *al-Hayuli's* darkness, and its capacity to give birth to the Soul, both flow from the simple fact of being far from the First. *Al-Kirmani* refuses both the distance-talk and the birth-talk.

The fact that *al-Hayuli* is not like the First, and that the Second also is not like the First, is not because either is "far" from the First by the proportion that is the case of the first one — namely, its being *ma'lul* (effect). The Second is *ma'lul* because it is a combination of two ratios relative to being a cause. The proportion of being *ma'lul*, and the existence of something that exists from the proportion that is *ma'lul*, is not like the existence of something that exists from the proportion that is a cause. The cause is more honorable than the effect in every respect, and precedes it in rank and nature. From the beginning, what is said to be a cause and what is said to be an effect is one single self that acquires the proportion in additions.

The doctrine cuts the ground out from under *al-Nusra's* image. There is no "distance" in the chain in the sense of spatial removal. There are proportions added to one self at one rank, which then determine what stands at the next rank by their character. The Second is what it is because of the proportions added to the First, not because the Second has "traveled away" from the First. And *al-Hayuli* is what it is because of the proportions added to the Second — not because *al-Hayuli* has "fallen further" from the First.

The same correction al-Kirmani gave in *Chapter Two* to the language of "motion" — that motion is for bodies, not for the ranks of origination — now applies to the language of "distance." Distance is for bodies that occupy space. The ranks of origination do not occupy space. To speak of the Second as "far from the First" by way of being *ma'lul*, and of *al-Hayuli* as "further still" — to imagine the chain of existence as a line stretching outward from the First into greater and greater remove — is to import a body-category into a rank where bodies cannot reach. *Al-Nusra* did exactly that.

The more accurate image is one al-Kirmani's own *Rahat al-'Aql* gives in the language of the seven seas of the universal Intellect. The ranks of the Intellect are not separated by intervening distance. They are concentric proportions of one single light, each one determined in its character by the proportion at which it stands within the originating act. The First Intellect is the act of origination itself. The Second is the same act considered under the proportion of being an effect — *ma'lul* — and so is the medium through which what stands below the Second comes to be. *Al-Hayuli* is the trace impressed on what stands below the Second when the Second exercises the power learned from the First. Nothing has traveled. The ranks have not unfolded across a distance. The same single act has been seen under the proportions that determine each rank.

When the chain is read this way, *al-Nusra's* "distance" language collapses. *Al-Hayuli* is not far from the First because it has gone far. *Al-Hayuli* is what it is because the proportion at which it stands is that of the trace impressed by the lower proportion in the First. There is no "further" for it to fall to. There is the rank it occupies, and there are the ranks above it.

## Sub-chapter five — no deficiency in the Second

The author of *al-Nusra* presses one more variation of the same picture. The diminution, he says, occurred in the Soul because the Soul is beyond the First Cause — *I mean the previous one.* The Soul is *less than* the First in a way that admits of being called a diminution.

*Al-Kirmani* answers carefully. There is no deficiency in the Second, which *al-Nusra* called a deficiency, as I have already mentioned; nor is the Second far from the First. Rather, the Second is *like* the First — like its counterpart, as the earlier sub-chapters established. And the diminution that *al-Nusra* speaks of is necessary, but not from the very nature of the Second. It came about not from the side of the Second's own selves — which is the side of the Second's perfection — but from the side of which its second perfection is acquired by way of benefit.

The distinction is delicate and load-bearing. The Second has, considered from one side, no deficiency at all. From the side of its own self, the Second is perfect. This is the formula that has carried *al-Riyad* from the opening pages of *Chapter One*: *perfect in self; deficient only in addition to what stands above.* The Second is perfect in itself the way the First is perfect in itself. The two are like-counterparts. The Second's "diminution" is only the formal fact that, considered in relation to the First, it stands at a lower proportion — and that lower proportion is what makes possible the Second's second perfection, which is the perfection it acquires by receiving the benefit that the First emits to it.

The trap *al-Nusra* fell into was to imagine the Second's lower proportion as if it were a defect *within* the Second's own being. A defect is a lack inside something. The Second has no lack inside it. What the Second has, on one side, is a relation of being-emitted-from above — and the lower-ness of that relation is not a defect in the Second; it is the form of the gift the First gives. To call this "diminution" in the ordinary sense of the word — to imagine the Second as a damaged or incomplete version of the First — is the same body-category mistake al-Kirmani has been correcting all along. Bodies can be diminished. The Second cannot.

Imam al-Mu'izz li-Din Allah, in *Ta'wil al-Shari'a*, returns again and again to a kindred formula about the Imam in his time. The Imam is not the Imam by being less than the Prophet who founded the *shari'a* — the Imam is the Imam by virtue of the rank at which he stands in the chain of guidance, where his perfection in self is intact and his benefit comes from his relation to the rank above him. The structural form is the same: each rank is perfect in itself; each rank receives benefit, not defect, from what stands above. There is no diminution in the rank itself; there is only the form of the giving by which the rank stands where it stands.

The image al-Kirmani's own corpus reinforces is that of light. The light that fills a higher chamber is not diminished when it also illumines a lower one. The lower chamber receives less light than the higher because the lower chamber's capacity is less. But the light has no defect. Whatever is in the higher chamber's light is in the lower chamber's light too — in the measure the lower chamber can hold. *The receiver, not the giver, sets the limit.* This is the doctrine sub-chapter seven of *Chapter Two* had already framed in the language of overflow and capacity; *Chapter Three* now applies the same doctrine to the Second's relation to the First.

## Sub-chapter six — nature is born of soul; the chain holds

The author of *al-Nusra* makes one last argument — and on this one, *al-Kirmani* concedes more than he refuses. *Al-Nusra* sets up a careful parallel. The proportion of the birth of nature from the Soul is no greater, he says, than the generation of the Soul from the Intellect. The Soul is less than nature in their capacity to be generated from what stands above them. So as it is permissible for the Soul to be generated from the Intellect — with the Soul's perfection and honor and its closeness to the First Cause of the Soul — it is equally permissible for nature to be born from the Soul. The reason: nature is more like the Soul in all its states, in its actions and powers, than the Soul is like the Intellect in *its* actions and powers.

Nature, among the early teachers, is labeled as the principle of motion. Some of them said it is the active force. If one looks at nature within these two limits, *al-Nusra* observes, it is the best fortune and the best portion — because the Soul, among the first, is the enduring moving essence, and is nevertheless labeled with the Intellect, though not in the way that the Intellect is one of the determinations of the Soul. The Soul also seriously challenges the Intellect, since the definition of the Intellect is that it is the first of things, that it is the creator, that it is the first effect, and that it is the first middle. It is not permissible to say that the Soul is the first thing, nor that it is creative, nor that it is the first effect. So it is permissible for the Soul to be born from the Intellect with its perfection and honor and its closeness to the word — like the Soul that does not bear the limitation of the Intellect. And it is not denied that it is permissible for nature to be born from the Soul like nature, which resembles in its limits the limits of the Soul.

*Al-Nusra* then makes the move that, in his own judgment, settles the case. Even if one looks at the matter from the perspective of consideration, this amount of knowledge would not be hidden. Even if the author of the Reform — meaning *al-Islah*, his master Abu Hatim al-Razi — stood upon the wonders and wisdom of nature, and the splendor and honor in nature's actions, and the beauty from which the partial soul desires to incline toward here and to follow it, *al-Nusra* would still hold his ground. *For you know and are certain that nature is born of the Soul* — even though it does not resemble it — because effects are never desirable in order to regulate their causes. From this fact, the partial soul desires to follow nature, and that all goodness, splendor, honor, and adornment is from the higher noble world. If nature were not born of the Soul, the Soul would not be able to speak of beauty, splendor, and adornment at all. Rather, in order for nature to be generated from the Soul, the Soul said: what she saw in her world of beauty and honor was an incomplete story — incomplete without the story of the Soul. *So understand what he said.*

The argument has real weight. The partial soul — the human soul in the natural composite — is drawn to nature's beauty. The beauty is real. The drawing is real. If nature were not in some way the offspring of the Soul, then nothing the partial soul finds beautiful in nature could connect back to the higher world. But what the partial soul actually does — every act of perceiving beauty in nature and tracing it back to the splendor of the higher rank — is testimony that nature is, in fact, born of the Soul. *Al-Nusra* takes this as decisive.

*Al-Kirmani's* response is terse, and it is the chapter's closing word: *If there is any precedence in the discussion over the intellect, the soul, nature, and the existence of the above — its existence over others — and otherwise, it implies the correct statement, then I quote it; and we do not need to elaborate with certainty upon what continued the mistake in perception. And in that is enough.*

The reading is concessive but careful. *Al-Nusra* is, in this sub-chapter, recovering the doctrinal order — Intellect, Soul, nature, in that order of priority — and is right to say that the chain is what makes the partial soul's longing for beauty intelligible. To the extent his statement carries that correct chain, al-Kirmani lets it stand without further argument. *I quote it.* The matters where *al-Nusra* continued the mistakes earlier in the chapter — the talk of sides, the talk of distance, the talk of diminution — do not need to be revisited. The chain itself, when stated cleanly, holds.

But the word *al-Kirmani* uses for "born" is to be read in the precise sense the chapter has established. Nature is born of the Soul only in the sense that the Soul, by the power learned from the First, brings forth the image in *al-Hayuli*, and that image is what we call nature. Nature is *al-Hayuli* informed by the Soul's power. The "birth" is not a likeness-birth. It is the impression of the higher rank's power on the lower rank's substrate. *Al-Hayuli* is still the trace, not the offspring. Nature is still the image of the trace, not the image of the Soul. The chain holds because the power runs through it, not because the lower ranks resemble the higher.

The quranic anchor that runs beneath this whole closing sub-chapter, though al-Kirmani does not quote the verse here, is the verse the *da'wa* tradition routinely reaches for when explaining the Soul's office in the natural composite: *Indeed We offered the trust to the heavens and the earth and the mountains, and they declined to bear it, and were afraid of it; but the human being undertook to bear it. Indeed he was unjust to himself and ignorant.* (Quran 33:72). The trust — *al-amanah* — is, in the *ta'wil* of Imam al-Mu'izz and al-Kirmani's own *Rahat al-'Aql*, the deposit of the rational soul in the natural composite, the rank at which the higher world entrusts its light to a substrate that does not resemble it. The natural composite carries the trust without being the trust. The partial soul's desire for beauty in nature is its dim awareness that the substrate it inhabits is the bearer of a deposit that does not match the substrate.

That is also the sense in which the verse of the spirit applies: *And they ask you about the spirit. Say: the spirit is from the affair of my Lord, and you have not been given of knowledge except a little.* (Quran 17:85). The spirit's "affair" — *amr* — is the affair of the First whose power reaches the Second and through the Second reaches the natural composite. The spirit is not the natural substrate; the spirit is the power borne by the rank above the substrate. To ask whether the Soul and the spirit are one substance is to ask the wrong question if one is asking about substrate-likeness. The Soul is the rank in the chain of origination; the spirit is the affair carried by that rank; *al-Hayuli* is the trace impressed by the affair in the substrate. They are not three substances in a row. They are three names for what happens at one continuous overflow of power from the First.

## The chapter's settled formula

The six sub-chapters land on one settled formula. The First and the Second are like-counterparts — *the mineral of honor, perfection, and elevation* — without ignorance, without darkness, without diminution, in the world of *al-Quds*. *Al-Hayuli* is the trace of the Second, not its child; the essence of this world; the substrate impressed by the power learned from the First. Nature is the image of *al-Hayuli* informed by the Soul's power. The chain holds not by likeness from rank to rank but by the running of the same originating power through the proportions of cause and effect. *Al-Nusra's* questions across the chapter — how can light touch darkness? does the Second have parts? has the Second fallen by distance? is there a diminution in the Second? is nature born of soul as soul is born of intellect? — each receive the same kind of answer. The category is wrong. The picture has imported body-talk into a rank where bodies cannot reach. The right answer comes through the chain of proportions, not through the image of resemblance.

The political stake of this is real and it sits one layer below the metaphysics. If the *hudud* are linked to their principles by *likeness*, then any believer who happens to resemble the *imam* in some quality could claim equivalent rank. If the *hudud* are linked to their principles by the *chain of proportions*, then no amount of resemblance closes the gap; the gap is closed only by the office that the rank carries. This is why al-Kirmani has fought every step of *Chapters One through Three* to refuse the talk of sides, parts, distances, diminutions. The talk of sides is the talk of a substance one could imitate. The talk of proportions is the talk of an office one cannot. The *natiq* is the *natiq* by the office; the *wali* is the *wali* by the office; the *imam* is the *imam* by the office. None of these are similarities one could acquire. They are the proportions through which the originating power runs.

The closing prayer of *Chapter Two* — *that the ranks of the* hudud *be kept clear, so that there be no confusion about the matter of perceiving things in their realities; for such confusion would lead to the corruption of the essence of souls* — applies again here. To confuse the Soul with the spirit, the Second with the First, the trace with the birth, is not an academic mistake. It corrupts the souls that hold the confusion, because it leaves them unable to distinguish what they can and cannot receive from each rank.

## What comes next

*Chapter Four* of *al-Riyad* will take the next step in the same architecture. If the Soul is what stands between the First and *al-Hayuli*, and if the natural composite is *al-Hayuli* informed by the Soul's power, then the partial souls — the human souls that inhabit the natural composite — what are they? Are they parts of the First Truths, as some of the older masters supposed? Or are they only traces of the Second, the way *al-Hayuli* is the trace of the Second? The eight sub-chapters of *Chapter Four* settle that question with the same kind of move al-Kirmani has used here. The categories of part-and-whole are body-categories; the partial souls are not parts of any higher rank; they are something subtler — a trace of a trace, an impression at the lowest rank carrying the deposit of the chain that runs above it. The formula remains exact. Perfect in self; deficient only in addition. Each rank is what it is in its place. The chain holds because the power runs through it, not because the ranks resemble each other.

And before that next chapter opens, *Chapter Three* leaves the reader with the image it has earned: the First and the Second standing together as the mineral of honor, with no ignorance or darkness between them — and below them, *al-Hayuli*, the trace, the essence of this world, receiving the impression of a power that does not resemble it. The light and the darkness do not need to look alike to be in contact. The contact is the work of the power that runs from one through the other to what stands below. That is the doctrine. That is what the chapter exists to settle. And it is settled.
