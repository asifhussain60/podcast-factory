# Soul, Intellect, and the Power of Emanation

*This chapter covers sub-chapters eleven through twenty-three of Chapter One of* Kitab al-Riyad *— the middle stretch of the perfection-of-the-soul argument, where the dispute between* al-Islah *and* al-Nusra *moves from abstract terms into the analogies that make the doctrine visible. The sun and the moon, the Book and the* 'Itrah*, the writer and the writing — each pair carries the same formula: perfect in itself, deficient only in relation. The chapter ends at the threshold of sub-chapter twenty-four, where the analogy of the sperm and the man opens a new line of attack.*

## Where this chapter picks up

The first ten sub-chapters of *Chapter One* established the doctrine that every existent is perfect in itself, and deficient only in relation to what stands above it. *Abu Hatim al-Razi* in *al-Islah* asserted that the Soul is perfect because it emanates from the perfect Intellect. *Abu Ya'qub al-Sijistani* in *al-Nusra* objected, in two long lines: first that "the perfect" is more honorable than "perfection" and that *al-Razi* had inverted the rank; second that emanation downstream of perfection must produce deficiency, because the chain dilutes as it extends. *Al-Kirmani* held the line through both attacks: the formula is consistent, and what fails is *al-Sijistani's* attempt to carry the categories of the natural world into the supra-natural realm where they do not apply.

What follows now — sub-chapters eleven through twenty-three — is the working-out. *Al-Sijistani* tries the same claim from a series of new angles. Each time, *al-Kirmani* gives him his due where the claim holds in the natural realm, and corrects him where the claim is smuggled into the supra-natural realm. The reader who has the formula in hand will recognize the move at each repetition. What is new in this stretch is the analogies. *Al-Sijistani* is reaching for whatever pairings he can find — sun and moon, the wise men's likeness of intellect to soul as soul is to body, male and female, sperm and man, writer and writing, builder and building, the Speaker (*natiq*) and the Foundation (*asas*). *Al-Kirmani* takes each in turn.

## Sub-chapter eleven — the sun and the moon

*Al-Sijistani* opens with a Quranic anchor. One of the clearest indications, he says, of the deficiency of the Soul and the integrity of the Intellect is the saying of God:

> And of His signs are the night, the day, the sun, and the moon. (Quran 41:37)

He set them upon two bases, the sun and the moon upon two bases, so that the sun is upon the first and the moon is upon the next. There is no dispute among the people of knowledge and wisdom that the moon draws light from the sun — less light and luminance — and that it benefits from the light of the sun, while the sun benefits it with light. The moon, then, is *al-Sijistani's* image of the Soul: drawing its light from elsewhere, lesser than what it draws from, dependent.

*Al-Kirmani* concedes the optical fact, and refuses the inference. It is clear that the moon is of lesser light than the sun, and of lesser luminance, and that it benefits from the sun in light. This decrease obliges the addition of the moon to the sun — that is, the moon's lower rank emerges only when set beside the sun.

But the self of the moon is not deficient in itself. If it were incomplete in itself, it would not have been able to receive light from the sun at all. A receiver that is wholly incomplete cannot receive. The moon receives because it is, in itself, fit to receive. Receivership is itself a kind of completeness. When the self is complete, it accepts what flows toward it; and if it is so, then the saying of the author of *al-Islah* concerned the integrity of the self, and the saying of the author of *al-Nusra* concerned the moon's deficiency in its conditions and its symptoms — symptoms which are still perfect in their own way. The two are not saying contradictory things; they are pointing to different aspects of the same moon.

The author of *al-Islah*, *al-Kirmani* says, became like one wronged by being argued against in what was not from his purpose, in what he says, nor in whom he intends.

## Sub-chapter twelve — the likeness of the wise men

*Al-Sijistani* tries another analogy. It is also indicative of the deficiency of the Soul, he says, that the wise men likened the Intellect to the soul *for the soul*, and they made the soul itself like the body *for the intellect*. They did so when they learned of the deficiency of the soul and the perfection of the intellect; so they likened the soul to the imperfect thing and likened the intellect to the perfect thing.

The argument is one of inherited authority. Even the ancients used the same pattern: intellect is to soul as soul is to body. Soul is the body's perfection; intellect is the soul's perfection. If the wise men set it up this way, the wise men were teaching deficiency of the soul.

*Al-Kirmani*: what *al-Sijistani* has added by this saying is a statement of what we have already said — that the deficiency is not in itself, but in other respects. Even though the meaning of *al-Sijistani's* words is only directed to the universes of the second revival. The benefit is only for the souls that are in the world of nature, in order to reach their second perfection. In itself it is wholly complete, but it is incomplete in some respect — namely, the senses by which the soul's stream runs through the body.

As for the second — that which is the First Resurrection — it is not the way of these souls, because its power and completeness were given at the beginning of its existence, not through time and benefit. The First Resurrection's deficiency, if it can even be named such, is only by a lesser degree relative to that which precedes it in rank, in progression and in lag. *Al-Sijistani's* analogy works at the natural level. It collapses when the same chain is applied to the supra-natural Intellects.

## Sub-chapter thirteen — nature on the horizon is not a deficiency

If, *al-Sijistani* presses, there is no deficiency in the Soul except that nature is upon its horizon, then the Soul has two extremities, one toward the Intellect and one toward nature. It is not so with the Intellect, whose two extremities are toward the Word on one side and the Soul on the other. Nothing clung to the dark nature out of its perfection and completion, even if it clung to nature as a part of the Soul, because nature falls upon the Soul's horizon — when it contemplates it, the observer stood upon the fact of the deficiency of the Soul and the perfection of the Intellect.

The picture: the Intellect's lower edge is the Soul, which is light; the Soul's lower edge is nature, which is dark. Touching darkness is a deficiency. The Soul touches darkness. The Intellect does not. Therefore the Soul is deficient and the Intellect is perfect.

*Al-Kirmani* says this statement is made irregularly. The occurrence of nature upon the horizon of what *al-Sijistani* calls the Soul does not earn it a deficiency. On the contrary: it indicates the Soul's completeness — for the Soul's movement, because it is incomplete in addition to the Intellect, is in addition to the Intellect a complete and honorable thing by its competence in what nature is not specialized in, along with its subjectivity. The Soul does what nature cannot do; the Intellect's reach does not go where the Soul's reach goes. Each is perfect in the work that belongs to it.

*Al-Sijistani* also said: the Intellect is not anything other than the Word, nor is the Word anything other than the Intellect. That, *al-Kirmani* notes, is true on its own. The self is one, the eye is one, and the names that are scattered are worthy of meanings. But it is not, as *al-Sijistani* thinks, the Next that clings to nature in love; it is nature that loves the Next, and it is nature that moves toward it and seeks communication with it. The belief that the Next clung to a part of itself by nature is wrong; the best way to dismiss the meaning of what *al-Sijistani* said is to interpret his purpose as the claim that the Next overflowed over nature, so that the overflow would be called "clinging."

The direction of dependence matters. Light does not need darkness; darkness needs light. Nature does not give the Soul anything; nature receives from the Soul. To call the Soul's overflow-toward-nature a clinging is to invert the actual flow.

## Sub-chapter fourteen — incomplete action does not imply incomplete doer

*Al-Razi* now returns to the formula in *al-Islah*: what is imperfect is the action of the soul, not the soul itself; rather, the soul completes its action, not its self.

*Al-Sijistani* attacks the logic of this. The statement is contradictory, he says, because if the action is incomplete, it must be either from the deficiency of the subject itself, or from the deficiency of the substance from which the verb is made, or from the deficiency of both. If the deficiency of the action is a deficiency of the doer itself, then *al-Razi* has said that the soul is lacking because it is the doer — although because of the lack of matter, the soul has been proved to be an eternal substance, and this is a departure from the doctrine of religion. *Al-Razi*, *al-Sijistani* charges, is inclined toward the doctrine of Abu Zakariyya al-Razi — Abu Bakr Muhammad ibn Zakariya al-Razi, the physician — and those who said that the soul is eternal. The author of this book imagined that *al-Razi* was inclined to do so, because *al-Razi* made time eternal alongside the intellect, and that is the lobe of his doctrine. Even if the lack of action is from both — the doer and the substance — *al-Sijistani* says it; and he says that the soul is the doer.

*Al-Kirmani*: the statement of the author of *al-Islah*, and the statement of his purpose in saying what he said about deficiency and perfection, may apply. The saying of the author of *al-Nusra* in what he said is fallacy, prejudice, and departure from the limit of knowledge.

First, after *al-Sijistani* divided the matter into the three divisions mentioned above, he said that if the deficiency of the action is from the doer itself, and the doer is the soul, then the soul is incomplete. But the author of *al-Islah* has clarified that the deficiency is not incumbent upon the soul itself, since the deficiency is an act in the sense of its effect; so be rid of these.

Secondly, *al-Sijistani* said that if the deficiency is from the substance itself, then it has been proved to be eternal — for perfection and completion, which are the first and the second, by the proof of their eternity, are eternal primally. So how is it diminished? If the substance is eternal, then the decrease is only in the substances that accept its completeness, not in what accepts their completeness, as is famous in naturalism. The decrease occurred from the side of the recipients, not from the side of what is accepted from it. Heat from the sun is received by bodies that yield to it — like wax, which it softens — while other bodies refuse to accept it, like stone and iron, and others which do not react quickly. The eye learned from them that the heat itself is the same; the lack is in the receptivity of the others.

Thirdly, *al-Sijistani* said that *al-Razi* leaned toward the doctrine of Abu Zakariyya in the pattern that *al-Sijistani* mentioned, and that is a departure from the limit of knowledge and a transgression. *Al-Kirmani* will not do *al-Sijistani* justice in the structure of his division, nor in what follows from what he said about the two sayings of the decrease and the eternity of the *Hayuli* — prime matter. The slander against *al-Razi* — that he was secretly an eternalist of the kind Abu Bakr al-Razi the physician was — is rejected outright. The doctrine of *al-Islah*, when stated in its own terms, does not require that the soul's substance be eternal in the impermissible sense. It only requires that the soul's perfection, which is its acquired second perfection, occur in time. That is a different claim, and *al-Sijistani* has run the two together.

## Sub-chapter fifteen — emanation does not flatten the world into uniform perfection

*Al-Sijistani* tries an extreme: as for *al-Razi's* claim that the soul has become complete because it emanated from the first intellect — which is perfect, and from which what emanates cannot be other than perfect — this is not true. Because the soul does not have to be complete because it emanates from the perfect intellect. Even if this were obligatory, then by the same chain, the form and image that emanate from the soul must also be complete. And from the form and image, what emanates from them must be complete. And so on, until it is communicated to the people. The undivided singular is all complete. It follows from this saying that all the world is perfect, and there is no deficiency in it, nor in any part of it.

The argument is a reductio. If perfection propagates downward by emanation, every part of the world is perfect, which is plainly contrary to experience.

*Al-Kirmani*: the product of *al-Sijistani's* saying is that if the soul were complete by being emanating from the first intellect, which is the whole, then the form and image emanating from the soul would be complete as well — and the same for what emanates from those, until all that is in the world is complete, and there is no deficiency. But the soul is not emanating from the soul nor from people, as *al-Sijistani* claimed. It is indivisible; all people are fragmented into their organs, and the hyacinths — the *hayuliyyat*, the matter-receiving substrates — are emitted together with the soul which precedes it in existence, and not from it.

The chain *al-Sijistani* has built is not an emanation chain. The first Soul does not emanate from itself further souls; the souls of human beings, and the hyacinths, are emitted *together with* the first Soul, downstream of the First Intellect — not below the first Soul in a chain. *Al-Sijistani's* reductio fails because the picture he is reducing was never the picture.

*Al-Kirmani* now lays out the ratio that does hold. The Soul which is the Next — that is, the second Intellect — has an existence which is not a first existence; there is no such thing as its existence with it that is not above it in rank, like the first, which is the first Intellect. The Soul's existence is a second existence, and what is of second existence — its existence is not unless it has an existence of the kind that it and another share in quality. That which *al-Kirmani* found, this Soul that is the Next, is the thing: the first being is that which is not preceded by the type of its existence, and it is one in its existence. But it was for the sake of the glory of God Almighty, who created from Him relations, additions, and multitudes; and all that is described by the thing in its divisions which they found means relations.

The attributes in the active causes are a cause, because there is more than one thing about them. One — because there is much information according to what its causes and causes are — has a relation. When God is too far transcendent that He should have anything before Him, or other than Him, or there be another with Him, or that He be differentiated in attributes and relations, then He, the Almighty, the Creator of the universe, and the cause for which there is no cause, it is impossible — with His safety, His holiness, and His exaltation from multiplication — that there exist from Him except one.

And this one, which is the first Intellect, when it was in two proportions — one of which is a cause for the existence of what is below it, and the latter of which is that it is an effect, for it is creative and inventive — what exists must be two according to the same proportions. What was found from the first ratio was the second Intellect, and from the other proportion was the orbits of the heavens, and the other creatures of the body world. Then, if it transgresses from one to the universe, it is only two; for there is not, after the rank of the one in existence, except two; and after the individual, except the husband, who is plurality. And if the first thing in the number of assets is one, then what follows — when it makes sense to the intellect — is two, which is the husband or the multitude. And if there were two, one of which is what we call the Soul, and the other what we call the Soul, including the heavens, the planets, and those below them.

The Soul does not propagate further Souls. The Soul stands alongside the celestial orders, both of them emanating from the First Intellect under different proportions. *Al-Sijistani's* infinite chain dissolves because it never existed.

## Sub-chapter sixteen — the Book and the 'Itrah

The sixteenth sub-chapter is the doctrinal climax of the middle movement. *Al-Kirmani* names the proof that the soul-and-the-*hayuli*-together stand to the First Intellect as the Book and the Foundation stand to the Speaker. He cites the hadith of the Two Weighty Things, in his rendering:

> Indeed, I leave among you the two weighty things: the Book of God and my 'Itrah, a rope extended from the sky to the earth, one end of it in the hand of God and one end in your hands. Hold fast to them, and you will not go astray as long as you hold to them. I have asked my Lord to answer me concerning them at the basin.

The Prophet (peace and blessings be upon him) combined the two — the Book and his 'Itrah — and held them in his hands and said: I do not say such two, with his rosary fingers and the middle of his hand, one of them preceding the other. They are the two rosaries: one does not precede the other, so the Prophet was disposed in the realm of bodies. The First Intellect proceeded in *dar al-Ibda'* — the realm of origination — and acted as it stated in its book. Whoever associates with the First Intellect from his 'Itrah, which is the Foundation, and the other Imams — may the prayers of God be upon them — proceeds in the same course as what is found of the First Intellect from the Second.

The Intellect is founded upon what is found within it, as *al-Kirmani* mentioned in *Rahat al-'Aql*. The *Hayuli* does not manifest its honor, nor does what is potentially within it manifest its strength toward the good image, except by the power of the Soul, which is the follower, and the brightness of the light is in them. Just as the Book and the *Shari'a* do not proclaim themselves, nor do they manifest the wonders of governance within and beneath them except through the interpretation (*ta'wil*) of the Foundation and the one who stands in his place — likewise, the existence of the *Hayuli* is from the First Intellect together with the Second.

The Two Weighty Things hadith is doing a great deal of work here. It is not merely a citation. It is the analogical key that lets the entire cosmological doctrine be heard at one stroke. As the Prophet held the Book and his 'Itrah in his two hands and refused to say one preceded the other — but said that they were together, one rope from sky to earth — so the First Intellect holds the Soul and the *Hayuli* together. They are not a series. They are a pair.

## Sub-chapter seventeen — the First Intellect is alone in its rank

*Al-Kirmani* now says it explicitly. The First Intellect does not have alongside it anything of the type of its own existence — because its existence is not generalized to it except as the existence of the First, and the First is not other than one. Otherwise the paths of monotheism would be disrupted.

If the First Intellect had an analogue in its existence, then their existence together would not be one, and one would not precede the other; and this is required by what precedes them. What their existence means is that which is restrained from its own existence. The existence of duality is a multiplicity of selves, and the multiplicity of selves does not arise from the multiplicity of souls; rather, its existence is from that which precedes it from the One — specifically on one side, and full of meanings on the other. The One specifically, which is multiplied in meanings, its existence not being in itself but in others, is not multiplied — neither in meanings nor in itself.

If the First Intellect had alongside it something of the type of its existence — such as two accidents about one preceding it — then the existence of that one would be because it is constrained in meanings, not in itself, but in what precedes it and renders it one. And since nothing is above the Intellect, it is not possible that it have an analogue of its existence. Thus it has been proven that it is one.

As for the proof that the Intellect is one — there being two things about it, namely the Soul — if the existence of the Soul and the supposed phantoms together rests on what *al-Kirmani* has already shown, then it has appeared that the claim of the Soul's appearance as a separate principle, parallel to the First Intellect, is wrong.

## Sub-chapter eighteen — al-Sijistani's own self-contradiction

The eighteenth sub-chapter catches *al-Sijistani* in a contradiction with one of his other books. This is what the author of *al-Nusra* is committed to in speaking about the deficiency of the Illuminated Intellect and the Image, and the deficiency of all that is in the world.

*Al-Kirmani*: the Intellect and the Image are perfect, and the world is perfect. Its parts are complete in themselves, as evidenced by the fact that the perfect is that outside of which there is nothing of the type of its own existence. The world, which is the sum of the celestial spheres, and the image assembled from the parts of the body — and the body in itself is perfect in its three dimensions — has nothing obliging its limitation unless it is outside it. If it is outside, then it is complete; and if it is complete, then the position of the author of *al-Nusra* — that the world is complete — is rebutted, and the impossible follows.

If among *al-Sijistani's* words there are passages that prejudice him, it is enough to cite what he said in his book known as *al-Ikhtisar* (in some readings, *al-Ikhtiyar*), in the chapter of "the command," where there is an indication of the perfection of the world. He said: "The image of the dispensation of the world in its entirety is nothing else, and wisdom suffices with what is in the world's totality — rich and perfect." *Al-Kirmani* does not know how *al-Sijistani* went on to contradict that. Perhaps, *al-Kirmani* speculates, he classified the book *al-Ikhtisar* after the book *al-Nusra*, and his views shifted.

As for the disparity that exists in the parts of the world, by which the author of *al-Nusra* is obliged to claim deficiency — that arises from the addition of some of them to others in terms of their images and conditions, which is the First Perfection that exists. Some parts of the world have been given their First Perfection, and the second is the second First; and some have been given their Second Perfection, which is the last of the acquisitions. As speech has resulted, the deficiency that the author of *al-Nusra* speaks of belongs rather to the later states of natural selves which appear when they are added to one another. The Perfect that the author of *al-Islah* speaks of is the self whose existence is first and original.

The contradiction matters because *al-Sijistani's* whole case has rested on the world's deficiency as evidence of the Soul's deficiency. If *al-Sijistani* himself, in another of his books, has affirmed the world's perfection, the case collapses. *Al-Kirmani* is generous enough to suggest the contradiction may be chronological — perhaps *al-Sijistani's* views developed. But the contradiction stands.

## Sub-chapter nineteen — the effect need not share every condition of the cause

*Al-Sijistani* opens a new line. If it is necessary for the Soul to emanate from the Intellect — which is the Perfect — then the Soul has a stature, because it is also necessary for the Soul to emanate from the Intellect, which is the first intermediate and the first creator. So the Soul must be intermediate like the First and creative, such that the Soul attains the virtues that the Intellect obtains, as does the whole world.

*Al-Kirmani*: the statement that it is impossible to say the Intellect is the first intermediate has already been advanced. In sum, what *al-Sijistani* has mentioned is a corrupt issue. For if something arises from another — one being a cause and the other an effect — it is not necessary that the effect share with it in all its conditions, such that its very identity would be its identity; for then nothing could be said of it except what is said of that particular identity.

When the effect is more original, and when it is said that it is found about the cause just as it was — nor when it must share with the cause in all its conditions — it must share with the cause in some of its conditions; for if it does not share with the cause in some of its conditions, the effect cannot be its effect, because the cause would not be the cause of its existence. And when the cause gave the effect existence — and if the way of the existence of such things is in multiplication, then the effect must share in the face of the cause. And if necessary, then the next one must be a soul of things, and emanating from the First Intellect, the cause of its existence, so as to be an example of it in one face and in the opposite way in another.

If the Soul is an example of the Intellect in a different face from the Intellect's own face, it has been shown that it is not necessary, if the existence of what follows — named the Soul — comes from the Intellect, that it be like the Intellect in all its states. Nor is it necessary to say of what follows the same as what is said of the Intellect in terms of being the cause of its existence. This refutes what the author of *al-Nusra* said: that if it were obligatory for the Soul to be like the Intellect in some respect, then it would be obligatory for the Soul to be like the Intellect in every respect.

The logic of cause-and-effect, *al-Kirmani* is saying, does not require the effect to inherit every property of the cause. It requires only some sharing — enough that the cause can be the cause. The Soul shares with the Intellect in being from a single principle. It does not share in being the First. *Al-Sijistani's* demand for total inheritance is not a feature of causation.

## Sub-chapter twenty — the partial soul, and what is "thought"

*Al-Sijistani* now tries to anchor his case in the familiar human mind. For the partial-soul / total-soul relation, he says, there are many analogues in the visible scenes, such as the idea and the partial soul. The partial soul that is in us is like the Intellect, and the idea is like the Soul. And the idea — even though it comes from the self — may not be said to be like the self, because the idea perceives what it perceives part by part, time after time, whereas the soul in sum is aware of what it perceives without time.

*Al-Kirmani* responds with a careful inventory. The soul — by which he means the soul of human beings — has actions, and each of them has a name specific to it. If it requires the realization of something, it is called thought. If it observes what has occurred in itself of the forms of information given to one, it is called memory. If it engraves itself with the landmarks it captures from the side of inference or from the side of the senses, that pattern is called perception. If it knows that there are things outside it which it wishes to conceive, it is called the will inclined toward them.

If the soul has actions, the idea is an act of it that does not exceed the soul itself, so that the idea has an existence other than the soul's own existence. How then is the analogy of thought correct — so that the so-called Soul has an existence other than the existence of the Intellect, and is said to perceive part by part, when perception is nothing other than the soul itself acting, which act is called the idea in itself? Or how is it correct that perception of the soul be in sum without time, when there is no way for the human soul to hunt knowledge except from the side of the senses and inference, from the side of their parts to their totality in time?

It is not the case that the idea, if it is not permissible to say it is like the soul, obliges that the Second Intellect — which is called the Universal Soul — not be like the First Intellect.

If the idea is not from the souls of human beings, the Second Intellect runs the same course as the First Intellect — the First Intellect being the Second Intellect's prior, which is the second existence of origination, self-contained, even though it is in existence without the first rank. The idea has no existence unique from the soul; rather it is the very identity of the soul, and has no existence of its own. For every act of the soul there is a cause for which it is needed: that it is in the world of nature; and if it is separated from this world, these actions are nullified by the nullification of the cause. What remains is only the perceived self as a pure Intellect.

*Al-Sijistani* made the Second Intellect — called the Soul — proceed from the First Intellect, and the stream of thought from the Soul. The existence of thought was no existence other than the existence of the Soul which he likened to the Intellect, nor did it have a self perceiving the soul — as if *al-Sijistani* had denied that the Second Intellect, called the Soul, has any existence other than the existence of the First Intellect, and no perception like the first perception. And that it is impossible for what follows to have an existence other than the existence of the First, or that it have a perception like the first one. Nor is it appropriate to say that the Second is creative, just as the First Being does not deserve to be called emanated. Both are pure intellects; each has encompassed itself and preserved itself in itself, and they are similar in one way and not similar in another.

If this is so, then there is no point in likening the idea to what *al-Sijistani* likens it to. Likewise, it makes no sense to make the human soul the First Intellect, nor to make the idea hold the status of the Universal Soul. The honor of the human soul and its movement from potency to act is only in acquisition, and acquisition is only from the senses as work, and from the side of perception as knowledge. If the honor of souls is in the acquisition by which they conceive through thought and reasoning — and the idea belongs to the souls only insofar as it is honored by it — then it is as if *al-Sijistani* has made the cause of the honor of the First Intellect to be the Second Intellect, called the Soul. This is an inverted issue: the honor of what follows, and its rank, comes from what is ahead of its existence — the previous — because the honor of the previous is arranged by what is behind it.

The ruling on what has another existence in perfection and completion — if it is contrary to what exists in the first place — then it does not run in the same course as this in that. This is not the way to proceed, even if they share on one side. As for the author of *al-Nusra*, he has judged what its existence was first because of the following: that it must have something whose existence is another existence — and that is impossible.

## Sub-chapter twenty-one — action does not always seek benefit or repel harm

*Al-Sijistani* opens with what he calls one of the clearest signs of the deficiency of the Soul and the perfection of the First Intellect. *Abu Hatim al-Razi* and others, he says, all said that action belongs to the Second, and they did not attribute action to the First. Because action is always a sign of deficiency and need, and the abandonment of action is evidence of perfection and richness. Action is only for the purpose of drawing a benefit or repelling a harm.

He cites the physician Abu Zakariyya — Abu Bakr al-Razi: the Initiator is already the Soul by way of manipulation and tampering, and the people of religion do not say so. So there remains nothing but the drawing of a benefit or the repelling of a harm; and when this is found, there is deficiency. The Soul, in doing this, seeks to draw the benefit, which is to reach its purpose destined for it; so it begins with action. The Intellect, when it has no rank above it, is not deficient and does not move to action in any way. The Soul, when it found a station above it, remained moving and remained in its quest. By this method, *al-Sijistani* concludes: in the case of the deficiency of the Soul and the integrity of the Intellect, this statement is clear, praise be to God.

*Al-Kirmani's* counter is one of the most beautiful pages of the chapter. The words of the author of *al-Islah*, he says, if they had been about the Soul that is the soul of human beings, would have been a better expression of what the author of *al-Nusra* said. But when *al-Razi's* words were about that which is called the Soul — the universal Soul, the Next, the Second Intellect — the saying of the author of *al-Nusra* does not hold. His inference from the state of human souls — that they act only to draw a benefit, to repel a harm, or to tamper — for the invalidity of his statement, and to extend his ruling in the universe to the Following which is complete in its totality, is a clear prejudice.

The statement of the author of *al-Nusra* concerning the act and its divisions is incorrect, for not every act is undertaken to draw a benefit, or to repel a harm, or to engage in vain effort. An act may proceed from an agent in such a way that it is beneficial to the object of the act, not to the one who produced it.

The evidence: heat from fire is an act of the fire, yet this act does not return to the fire and is not a reward for it; there is neither benefit nor harm to the fire itself, but rather the benefit and the harm fall upon whatever the heat reaches. The same applies to the act of the sun in illuminating the world. Humans were able to act by their own will, and through their elevation and the existence of the universe of plants, animals, and minerals, the benefits of these acts accrue to others and not to themselves; they do not benefit from this act, nor are they harmed by it. So too with the whales that are in the Sea of Oman, and others called "dolphins," which, if a person of old should drown in the sea, help him escape and push him toward safety — yet that act does not return to them with benefit nor with harm; rather the benefit reaches the one it affects.

And if this is the case in the natural world, where it makes no sense to attribute such strange acts to it — even though nature is deficient in its conditions and dependent on others in its actions — there is no denying that the pure abstract intellects, which have been granted their perfections in the first instance of their existence, can produce greater acts than those that emerge from the natural realm. Their perfections require this, according to the proximity of their ranks and the elevation of their grades — noble acts, not for the sake of drawing a benefit to themselves nor warding off harm, but for the sake of others.

So these acts are acts of wisdom, not acts of drawing benefit or repelling harm, nor acts of futility. The perfect power from which the perfect and well-ordered act is produced exists only in the perfect being. Hence, what *al-Sijistani* has called "the soul" is not in motion as he claimed; rather, it possesses what is necessary for nature. This motion belongs only to bodies themselves, to their natural forms, and it is of a higher rank than nature, while being below what *al-Kirmani* has explained in the book concerning the rest of the intellect.

The sun warms the earth. The sun does not warm itself. The fire heats the wax. The fire does not heat itself. The dolphin pushes the drowning man toward shore. The dolphin does not benefit. To insist that every act must benefit its agent — and therefore that every act is a mark of deficiency — is to take the small case of human striving as the model for all action whatever. It is a category error, and the natural world itself refutes it.

## Sub-chapter twenty-two — the act of the soul, and what is "done"

*Al-Sijistani* presses on. The upshot of the statement of the author of *al-Islah*, he says, is that the act of the soul is something done, not the soul itself — even though the soul, in *al-Razi's* view, is completed; the act of the soul becomes a soul. This is forbidden by manifest abstention, because acts have never been seen, in observable experience, to become subjects. For example: the writer and the writing, the builder and the building — writing does not become a writer, nor does building become a builder; rather it is the man, who is a writer in potency and a builder in potency, who becomes a writer and a builder in actuality. And likewise, *al-Sijistani* says, we say of the soul and the intellect: the intellect is already perfect, and the soul is already imperfect, ranked in potency; it becomes already existent if it gains perfection. But if its act were to become perfect, this would be impossible.

*Al-Kirmani*: the aim of the author of *al-Islah* in saying that the act of the soul is what is done, and not the soul itself, is that the second perfection is attained through it — from the soul's side flows that which overflows upon it. Thus the soul issues forth from potency to the measure of the act. And if the aim of the saying is the act of the soul — namely, what issues from its abundance from potency into act — and the second perfection is obtained, and nothing crosses the limit from potency to act except by what already exists, then it is not to be denied.

If what was in potency issues forth and attains the second perfection through acquisition, so as to be like that which exists in what was given of perfection in the first existence, then they share in the sense that they already exist, even if each of them has received something. They share in it, though not from the side from which the other received it, and it is not impossible for them to be two equivalents in terms of commonality.

It is known that everything is gathered, and any other thing of meaning is, in that sense, like it. The proof is *al-Kirmani's* saying that everything which goes out from the limit of potency to the limit of act is already existent, and everyone who is already existent has had the second perfection occur to him. Everyone who passes from the limit of potency to act has the second perfection occur to him. If the fulfillment of what is already existent is the second perfection, and the existence of what was established in potency has become already established by means of the second perfection, then they are common and similar in attaining the second perfection — though on one side they differ from each other. What *al-Kirmani* has mentioned is neither impossible nor evil; rather it is necessary. So the statement of the author of *al-Nusra* is nothing but prejudice.

## Sub-chapter twenty-three — when acts ARE agents

*Al-Sijistani* presses the same point once more: this is a manifest abstention, because acts have never been seen, in observable experience, to become agents, given what *al-Sijistani* has mentioned concerning the matter of the writer and the builder.

*Al-Kirmani's* response is one of the deepest moves in the chapter. We say, he writes: acts are the subjects of the truth. The writing that *al-Sijistani* mentioned, which is the act of the writer, is the very thing that addresses its reader and speaks to him; if the writing in its forms and arrangement were not effective upon the reader — even as if it were speaking about its subject and translating it — then its writer would not have engraved it with these forms so that the reader might know what is in the soul of its writer.

The image which the builder creates is what conveys to the viewer the image in his square structures and their disposition, so that he may conceive of it. If the image of the building did not already exist, it would not be acquired. The same applies to the observable scenes which carry the pictures of what they depict. So too the striker: the one who strikes is the one who inflicts pain, and the pain itself, as *al-Kirmani* has mentioned in his two well-known epistles, *al-Rawda* and *al-Idah*.

And the saying of God Almighty — there is in it evidence that acts are also agents, as most of those who speak attest:

> And there is nothing but praises Him. (Quran 17:44, in the rendering al-Kirmani gives.)

This is the act of the Almighty, and though it is an act, it praises Him. And His saying:

> And We sent the winds. (Quran 15:22, in the rendering al-Kirmani gives.)

The wind is one thing, and the insolent thing performs an act through it. The explanation of this is that it is clear that the protest of the author of *al-Nusra* against the author of *al-Islah* in this chapter is impossible.

Then the precision: the aim of the author of *al-Nusra* in saying that we have not seen acts in observable experience become agents at all is that the very acts have not become identical to the very subject in the observable scene — the writing has not become identical to its writer, nor has the building become identical to its builder. This is a correct statement as *al-Sijistani* said it, because an act does not become identical to the one from which the act issued; rather it is something done, similar to its doer in the existence of the act in it. Even though the ranks differ and the relations differ, the author of *al-Nusra* wanted to defend those whom he represents by prejudice against the author of *al-Islah* — may God sanctify their souls — and there are many mixtures layered upon one another.

*Al-Kirmani* threads the eye of the needle. *Al-Sijistani* is right that writing is not the writer. Writing does not become a writer. The act does not become identical to the agent. So far, no quarrel. But *al-Sijistani* slides from "acts do not become agents" to "acts cannot act," and that slide is the error. Acts can act. The writing speaks. The building shows. The wind sent by God carries out the sending. Acts are not their agents, but acts are agents. They have agency of their own kind, downstream of the agent that produced them. *Al-Razi's* claim that the soul's action achieves the soul's second perfection — that the act of the soul does the work of completing the soul — rests on this same recognition: that an act has its own efficacy, distinct from the agent but referred back to the agent.

This is where the middle stretch of *Chapter One* ends. The next sub-chapter — the twenty-fourth — will open with a new analogy: the sperm and the man, the soul and the intellect, body and form. *Al-Sijistani* will try once more to pin the Soul to a chain of deficiency. *Al-Kirmani* will once more apply the formula: perfect in itself, deficient only in relation, and not even that when the relation is misconstrued.
