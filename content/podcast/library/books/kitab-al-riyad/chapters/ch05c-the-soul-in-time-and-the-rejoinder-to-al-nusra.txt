# The Soul in Time and the Rejoinder to al-Nusra

*This chapter covers sub-chapters twenty-four through thirty-eight — the closing stretch of* Chapter One *of* Kitab al-Riyad*. The pace of the dialectic accelerates as* al-Sijistani *exhausts his line of attack and* al-Kirmani *gathers the threads into a final formulation. The analogies of sperm-and-man, Speaker-and-Foundation, sun-and-moon, male-and-female, bowing-and-prostration carry the argument to its end. The last great move is the dispute over whether souls "forgot" their world — and what kind of soul could forget anything at all. The chapter closes with the prayer that* al-Kirmani *uses to seal the whole argument: that God enlighten our minds, seal our affairs with happiness, and grant us success in good words and good deeds.*

## Where this chapter picks up

The first twenty-three sub-chapters of *Chapter One* established the formula — perfect in itself, deficient only in relation to what stands above — and traced it through the disputes over what "perfection" names, the relation of Intellect to Soul, the meaning of "mean," the chain of emanation, the unity of the First Intellect, and the question of whether acts can act. *Al-Sijistani* tried in *al-Nusra* to fix the Soul to a single scale where being lower than the Intellect would make it deficient in itself. *Al-Kirmani* refused that scale at every turn.

What now follows is the last movement of the argument. The dispute moves through a new wave of analogies — sperm-and-man, Speaker-and-Foundation, sun-and-moon under a fresh lens, male-and-female, bowing-and-prostration. *Al-Sijistani* finds a softer place to land: the souls of human beings who are subject to nature, who incline to its pleasures, who forget the splendor of their own world. Surely *these* souls are deficient. *Al-Kirmani* draws a distinction that the whole chapter has been pressing toward: such souls exist, and they are deficient, but they are not the Soul that *al-Razi* meant. *Al-Razi* meant the Successor Soul — the universal Soul — which is perfect at the first instant of its existence and confers perfection on the souls that descend through nature. To take the human soul's subjection as evidence of the universal Soul's deficiency is to mix two distinct existents into one slogan.

The chapter ends with *al-Kirmani's* prayer.

## Sub-chapter twenty-four — sperm and man, soul and intellect

*Al-Sijistani* tries a vivid biological analogy. *Al-Razi*, he says, likened the Intellect and the Soul, in their perfection and imperfection, to a man and to sperm — because the sperm is the image of a man in potency in truth, and it becomes a man in actuality. Likewise, the Soul is the image of the Intellect in truth, and it becomes perfect like the Intellect, if it benefits from the Intellect — its benefits being measured out to it.

*Al-Kirmani*: *al-Sijistani's* statement that the sperm is the image of the man in potency in truth, and likewise that the Soul is the image of the Intellect in truth, becoming perfect — is not correct. The sperm is not the image of the man, nor is the Soul, which is in potency, the image of the Intellect.

Here is the correction. The sperm is matter prepared for receiving form, free of forms, which then acquires them in stages. Likewise the soul is a natural preparation, vacant of forms, which would acquire the forms of knowledge so that the soul becomes self-fulfilled by them.

Yet even if the soul were the image of the Intellect in truth, then the Intellect would have to have an image of what it is, so that it would be one thing, and if the image had an image, it would be something else — and how can this be true, when the Intellect's existence is a creative existence, and otherness has no place in it except by addition, and the essence is one? This discourse and analogy applies only to what is natural — except for that which was outside the separation of bodies.

The point is sharp. A sperm is not a "small man." A sperm is matter prepared to receive the form of a man. The sperm does not contain the man in miniature; it contains the receptivity that the soul, in its development, will furnish with form. The same is true of the soul in relation to knowledge. The soul does not contain the Intellect in miniature; the soul is the receptivity that, by acquiring forms of knowledge, comes to bear the print of the Intellect's content. *Al-Sijistani's* "image of a man in potency" trades on a confusion between an image and a vessel. They are not the same.

And the analogy fails for a second reason. If the soul were truly the image of the Intellect, then the Intellect would need to have an image of itself for the soul to be the image of. The Intellect would split. But the Intellect, in its creative existence, does not split; its essence is one. The image-of-image regress cannot start. The biological analogy that seemed to make the Soul-as-image-of-Intellect intuitive only works in the natural realm where bodies have images and images have images. It does not work where the original is one and indivisible.

## Sub-chapter twenty-five — Speaker and Foundation, the sacrifice of camels

*Al-Sijistani* now invokes the *natiq*/*asas* pair — the Speaker-Prophet and the Foundation who stands in his place. The rank of the Speaker in the physical world is like the rank of the Intellect in the world of the spiritual, he says, and the rank of the Foundation is like the rank of the Next — that is, the rank of the Soul. Thus, just as it is possible for *al-Razi* to say that the Soul is perfect like the Intellect, it is possible to say that the Foundation is perfect like the Speaker. But this is not correct, because the rank of the Foundation is below the rank of the Speaker, and this has been explained in the meaning of prayer.

*Al-Kirmani*: the Second is like the First, even if it is in a lower rank, and the Foundation is like the Speaker, even if he is in a lower rank. The Second is — in its existence — that of which nothing remains in the form of a degree to be attained later, by which it would gain perfection. Just as the Foundation is perfect in his rank, with no degree left for him to ascend to and acquire. Yet the Second is not in himself complete.

*Al-Kirmani* anchors the doctrine in an act of the Prophet. It suffices as witness to this the act of the Prophet (peace be upon him) and his indication concerning what *al-Kirmani* has said — namely, that it is obligatory to sacrifice camels, and not to perform the sacrifice on others, because slaughter consists in delivering the iron to the head of the *fawad* (the heart), through the wound, so that no blood remains corrupted — by the degree of the report, the contemplation of the secrets, and the dispelling of doubts and suspicions in the ranks. The sacrifice is grounded in the taking of the covenant and the release in the call, and the slaughter is based upon it.

It is as if the Prophet had displayed this deed and informed the *Ummah* who followed him on one side. The proof of his role is that the basis of his standing in supplication is that he did not establish it in the inner call — which is the continuation of the worship of God leading to the unity of God and the knowledge of His limits — unless he teaches him what is in it of perfection, and informs him of what is in its perfection. So the highest degree is in his reporting, and his path is in attaining perfection and completion — not like any other path of his *Ummah*, by which he obtains communication and completion finally, after taking the covenant first, and teaching second.

The Prophet did not perform this fundamentally except to indicate that the Second — which is the Foundation in the world of intellect — has attained perfection first. There is no contradiction in taking the covenant first from the sons of the *da'wah*, then informing them of the degree of perfection; and the communication is only for the purpose of pointing out and indicating that the natural souls, which are below the Second, are first in potency, then issue forth by acquisition into actuality at last — which is their perfection and completion — so that each of them may believe what is fitting to believe in it.

And then it was not limited to indicating that the Prophet was indicating the completion of perfection on the other side, in the two *rak'ahs* of the dawn prayer, which are the first and the second. In the first *rak'ah*, it is obligatory to recite the praise (*hamd*); in the second *rak'ah*, it is obligatory to recite *al-Qunut* in place of the praise that is in the first *rak'ah*, so that the second is in the same state as the first from the standpoint of perfection. Their distinction is made only in terms of rank in existence: this is the beginning of existence, like the first *rak'ah*, and that is the second *rak'ah*.

The same formula: each *rak'ah* is perfect as a *rak'ah*. The two stand in a rank-relation, not in a perfection-relation. *Al-Sijistani's* attempt to derive the Soul's deficiency from the Foundation's rank below the Speaker repeats the error: rank below is not deficiency in self.

## Sub-chapter twenty-six — the Foundation cannot be other than perfect

*Al-Kirmani* drives the point home from another side. If it is permissible to say that the Soul is as perfect as the Intellect, then it is permissible to say that the Foundation is like the Speaker — but this is not right, *al-Sijistani* claims. *Al-Kirmani*: how could it be right, when the Foundation is the one who stands in the place of the Prophet and his family, even if he falls short of standing in his place, and is not as complete as he is? When God enjoins obedience to him, how could God Almighty enforce obedience to one who is like others in deficiency?

Both of *al-Sijistani's* positions are untenable. It is not permissible in wisdom that the Prophet be a messenger to all those who are in his time and those who are born after him, and that it would be an excuse for His creation, since He sends the community to its appointed lots, and there is no grandfather (*jadd*) like Him. The proof is therefore established against them, for the Foundation stands as the one who speaks after the Prophet on one side, and as the one spoken for on the other.

It is sufficient proof that the disparity between them in rank does not lie in the self, since the rank of the Foundation is below the rank of the Speaker. *Al-Sijistani* could not invoke anything except by virtue of rank. It is clear from what *al-Kirmani* has set out that the position of the author of *al-Islah* has been upheld against the objection of the author of *al-Nusra*, and *al-Razi's* statement that the Second is deficient — even though it is *al-Sijistani's* own view — is correct in the sense that must be said of it: deficient in rank, perfect in self.

## Sub-chapter twenty-seven — bowing, prostration, male and female

*Al-Sijistani* tries the prayer-cycle as an argument. He says: bowing is upon the Foundation, prostration is upon the Speaker, and the supplication in bowing — *Glory be to my Lord the Great, and praise be to Him, and He is the purification of the righteous* — is from the side of the Foundation's Cultivator (*murabbi*), the Greatest. The implication is that the one being followed (the Prophet) and the Great One — while his Cultivator is the Greatest — is the one being followed. And in prostration, *Glory be to my Lord the Most High and praise be to Him* — this is the analogue (*ta'ziya*) of the Speaker, the Prophet, from the side of his Cultivator. The Foundation is the Second, and the Higher is upon the prior side, since the Foundation's Cultivator is the Higher and the Prior. The Speaker has mentioned this in revelation.

The sense is that the Speaker here stands in the rank of the Intellect, so that the Foundation hangs from him and is at his corner — these being the two principles — or that the lower one means the Prior. The Prophet (peace and blessings be upon him) and his family attained the highest degree of prophethood, to the point that no veil remained between him and the Prior, while the Prior himself had no separation between himself and the Word (*kalima*). The Foundation has not reached this level and rank. Likewise, the Second has not reached the rank of the Prior, but remains below his rank.

This obtains in every pair, *al-Sijistani* says, because the male is always more complete and more noble, and the female is more deficient. So too with the heavens and the earth: the heaven stands in the position of the male, and the earth in the position of the female; the heaven is more noble than the earth, and the earth more deficient than the heaven.

*Al-Kirmani* responds in three steps.

First: the statement of the author of *al-Nusra* that the rank of the Prior is higher than that of the Second is a correct statement, and it is what the author of *al-Islah* said. The fact that the rank of the Second is below the rank of the Prior is not a deficiency in himself.

Second: as for *al-Sijistani's* statement that the Prophet and his family reached the rank of the Prior in perfection, while the Foundation did not reach it — this is hardly correct. The Foundation, even if he did not reach that degree by himself, has reached it by means of the Speaker: through the Speaker's teaching and his making him aware of the blessings sent down to him, the Foundation reached the degree of perfection. By his complete acceptance of it, he illumined himself and became as the moon at the full of the fourteenth night.

This is borne witness to by the conduct of the Prophet and his family in delivering the sermon on Friday before the prayer, and in delaying it on the day of *Eid al-Fitr* — because God made Friday a face of interpretation (*ta'wil*) upon Himself: it is one of the seven days. The Prophet's ascending the pulpit is the first sermon; then he prays, so that he attains the degree of perfection and completion and rises to the highest ranks of honor from the side of the Origins. He was first, then he called for his *shari'a*, for accepting his revelation and obeying his Foundation. As for the Day of *Fitr*, it is upon his Foundation, for it is one of the days of God the Almighty. Through obedience to Him, the religion of God is fulfilled. He offers prayer first on the basis of his Foundation under obedience to Him, and the acceptance of His command and His law, and through that he attains perfection and rises in the ranks of honor. Through his intermediation, he is acquainted with the degree of the Successor (*wasi*), who is perfect in himself, even if his attainment of the degree of perfection is from the side of the Speaker.

Third: the male-female analogy. The Foundation's rank is below the rank of the Speaker, yet the existence of the female is not a deficient existence — so it is possible to invoke in its support what supports it. For its existence is a complete existence. Were its deficiency due to what it carries out that is exclusive to the male, then the existence of the male would also be deficient in respect of what he carries out that is exclusive to the female. So it is ruled out that the male's existence is complete and the female's incomplete; therefore the male is complete in himself and the female is complete in herself. By the joining of one to the other, their rank is rendered unequal, so the female is ranked below the male. This is sufficient proof that the disparity is in ranks, not in selves.

The formula again: complete in self, deficient only in addition. The female is not less in herself than the male; she is less in rank only in the addition of one to the other. *Al-Sijistani's* leap from "the female is in a lower rank" to "the female is deficient in self" is the same leap he has been making at every step.

*Al-Sijistani's* own conclusion was: the Foundation has not reached this level. Therefore the statement of the author of *al-Islah* is correct that the Second is complete.

## Sub-chapter twenty-eight — sensual souls are not the Successor Soul

*Al-Sijistani* now lands on what he hopes will be the decisive case. It is a sign of the deficiency of the soul and the perfection of the intellect, he says, that many of the speaking souls are subject to nature, and are led by it and by what it commands them, and incline to its desires and pleasures, and forget their own knowledge, light, and splendor, and desire the world of nature — its forms and effects — and remember nothing of the splendor of their own world and its light, to seek it. This is because of their lack of desire for natural desires and pleasures, and their asceticism in their own world and its pleasures, goodness, and splendor. As for the intellect, on account of his agency, beauty, and perfection, he is not forgetful of the pleasures of his world and its splendor, and does not incline to nature, nor desire it for its own sake or its desires, and does not look at it; and if he does look at it, he looks at it from the side of pity, mercy, and contempt for what it has. He informed her of himself that she would be little if she were attributed to what is in his world. If the soul were like the intellect, it would be steadfast in what it has seen in its own world, and would not desire nature when affected by it.

The argument has an experiential pull. We see human souls every day enslaved to natural appetite, forgetting the higher splendor. The Intellect does not seem to be enslaved this way. Surely this is the difference between a perfect and an imperfect being.

*Al-Kirmani's* response is the chapter's pivotal distinction. The submissive one that is led, and the selflessness and pleasures and following of natural desires and the abandonment of worship — this is the sensual soul (*al-nafs al-hissiyya*), not the speaking soul (*al-nafs al-natiqa*) to which *al-Sijistani* has alluded. The sensual soul is only persuaded, and it is persuaded by the onset of ugliness and vices, if they are affected by the intellect by way of the bonds of discipleship in education, so that it prevents her from committing what she would otherwise commit.

These souls are not the Successor souls that emanate from the First Intellect, upon which the author of *al-Islah* spoke. *Al-Kirmani* has already established *al-Razi's* statement of what is implied in it: that the Successor is distinct from these souls, and these souls are others. The Successor is perfect and complete, in a state in which nothing remains in himself that he has not attained, so that he is deficient in it — as is the case with these natural souls, which exist in the first perfection and have a second perfection that they attain through time and acquisition.

The same word — "soul" — has been doing two jobs in *al-Sijistani's* attack. One job is the universal Soul, the Next, the Successor, the second Intellect. The other job is the soul of an individual human being struggling in the world of nature. *Al-Sijistani* has slipped between the two senses, and used the obvious deficiency of the second to attack *al-Razi's* claim about the first. *Al-Kirmani* refuses the slippage. The Successor is one thing. The sensual soul is another. Failure to distinguish them is the whole engine of *al-Sijistani's* attack.

The distinction is older than the dispute. The Quran itself names the soul in stages, and the stages map onto the very partition *al-Kirmani* is pressing.

> *Inna al-nafsa la-ammāratun bi-l-sū' illā mā raḥima rabbī.*
> *Indeed, the soul is ever inclined to evil, except those upon which my Lord has mercy.* — (Quran 12:53; Sahih International translation)

> *Wa lā uqsimu bi-n-nafsi al-lawwāmah.*
> *And I swear by the self-reproaching soul.* — (Quran 75:2; Sahih International translation)

> *Yā ayyatuhā an-nafsu al-muṭma'innah, irji'ī ilā rabbiki rāḍiyatan marḍiyyah.*
> *O soul at peace, return to your Lord, well-pleased and pleasing.* — (Quran 89:27–28; Sahih International translation)

The soul *al-Sijistani* points to, enslaved to natural appetite, is the first of these, the soul commanding to evil. The soul placed under the discipline of the Intellect, blaming itself for its inclinations, is the self-reproaching soul. The soul that has been perfected by the Intellect's overflow and returns to its origin in peace is the soul at peace. Three states, one word. *Al-Sijistani's* attack on the Successor Soul borrows the deficiency of the first and assigns it to the third, and the Quran's own grammar refuses the move.

## Sub-chapter twenty-nine — only the complete can confer completion

*Al-Kirmani* now consolidates. The Successor is perfect, and the one who confers his perfection and his completion at the beginning of his existence all at once: he exists, and through him and through his overflow the imperfect attain the acceptance of the degree of perfection. The proof is that the existents in the natural world exist by potentiality first, then by actuality afterwards; and there is no passing of what exists in potentiality into actuality except by the presence of something already actualized.

If there is no passing of what exists in potentiality into actuality except by something already actualized, the existence of what is already actualized, and external to actuality, exists — for the existence of what is already actualized is a necessary existence. If the existence of what is already actualized is a necessary existence whose existence has not been disturbed, then it is either the First Intellect or another. It is invalid for the First Intellect to abstain from existence in act except by the presence of another, or to require in mind something existing alongside him outside himself, so that he acts upon it.

The First Intellect is independent in his action, for there is nothing within him other than himself, since he is the perfect whose existence is from what comes to be (the Almighty being above him), and he grows up as one existence having no parallel in his existence, and nothing else is present with him for him to act through. For if it were, the Almighty would have become an idol worshipped. It has been said that this is sufficient, and *al-Kirmani* has explained more than this in *Rahat al-'Aql* (*The Repose of the Intellect*).

The doctrine of the First Intellect lands on a tradition that the Imami corpus opens with. *Al-Kulayni* placed it at the head of *al-Kafi*, in the *Kitab al-'Aql wa al-Jahl*, the Book of Intellect and Ignorance, the very first chapter of the entire collection. It is reported on the authority of *Imam Ja'far al-Sadiq*:

> *Awwal mā khalaqa Allāhu al-'aqla, fa-qāla lahu: aqbil, fa-aqbal, thumma qāla lahu: adbir, fa-adbar. Thumma qāla: wa-'izzatī wa-jalālī mā khalaqtu khalqan aḥabba ilayya minka.*
> *The first thing Allah created was the Intellect. He said to it: come forward, and it came forward; then He said to it: go back, and it went back. Then He said: by My might and majesty, I have not created any creation more beloved to Me than you.* — (al-Kafi, Vol. 1, Kitab al-'Aql wa al-Jahl, Hadith 1; al-Kulayni, reported from Imam Ja'far al-Sadiq)

The tradition does not picture the Intellect learning a command. It pictures an existent whose first instant of being is already perfect, who answers the call to come forward and the call to go back in the same self-completed motion. By such an existent, things are taken and things are given; by such an existent, what is in potency in the world below is brought into actuality. *Al-Kirmani's* whole argument in this sub-chapter, that only the complete can confer completion, that the First Intellect is independent in his action and acts by himself, is the philosophical formulation of what the tradition has already imaged.

What is in this position alone — there is nothing alongside him, nor any need for him in his action, but rather he acts by himself. If the First Intellect is acted upon in himself, then he is the one who brings out the existent by potentiality in him, and by his overflow upon another it is ruled out that what is in him is graduated to bring out what exists in potentiality into actuality. This is the First Intellect. If it is not the First Intellect, then it has been established that the one who brings out potentiality into actuality is distinct from him and is the Second.

This bears witness to the soundness of *al-Kirmani's* statement that the Prophet and his family did not invoke the greatness of his rank and integrity in regard to other human beings, so that he might complete in his place his deed — which is the revelation and the law — and that he in that uniqueness of himself and from himself issued forth according to what he derived from what gave him a degree. He found what he had recited and prescribed it perfectly, so he conducted himself in a bodily mode in the descent (*tanzil*) along the course of the First Intellect, in the same creative manner, so that his action in its rank led to contemplation of the truth of belief, in knowing what is hidden from the senses of the principles of origination (*ibda'*) and renewal (*inbi'ath*), so that the path of monotheism (*tawhid*) is unaltered.

As for the Foundation, even if he is at the highest degree of perfection, he has not had that degree by which he could be unique in his action in the matter of the call (*da'wah*) and interpretation (*ta'wil*), of teaching and guidance, by himself. So he did not dispense with what he found with him, which is the revelation and the *shari'a*. *Al-Kirmani* does not mean that the Foundation dispenses with this — that is, with the revelation and the *shari'a* — nor that the Speaker dispenses with something existing alongside him in the bodily world, which he uses to act through, as the Foundation does, and therefore his action follows the course of the Second.

That which obtains in the Speaker is what *al-Kirmani* has mentioned as proof of knowing the rank of the Second: that the Prophet and his family follow the course of the First, who is the perfect one who dispenses with anything other than himself in his action and does not employ anything other than himself. The Foundation follows the course of the Second, who is the perfect one in whom there is no action of his without revelation and *shari'a* — without which he does not dispense in his action. There is no act, and the subordinate community follows a sub-orbital course in the natural world, in which its existents pass from potentiality to actuality through the overflow of the Second.

The souls that are in potentiality need that by which they attain the degree of connection and perfection. From the entity of the Foundation's call — just as the Second is the one who supports the one ready for acceptance from the world of nature, so too is the Foundation, whose place in the call is to make the one willing to obey attain the degree of connection and perfection. That is the pronouncement of what they would have been from the world of nature, had they not been perfected. The high degree is from the side of the First Intellect, but from the side of the Second Intellect first; just as none of us reaches the degree of connection from the side of the Speaker, but only on the side of the Foundation's call first — so they run from those above them as we run from them. The sole follows the sole.

Whatever exists with such conditions is evidence of its deficiency, and the objection of the owner of *al-Nusra* against the owner of *al-Islah* is therefore an impossible objection.

## Sub-chapter thirty — the impossibility of "forgetting" if the soul was always knowing

*Al-Sijistani* tries one more line. The Soul, he says, is incomplete because it forgets its world and does not remember it. *Al-Kirmani* responds: how can this be true, since forgetting does not occur except after knowledge?

If these souls existed beforehand while they were knowing, it would have been impossible for them to come into being by descending into the abode of nature after dwelling in majesty and attaining the perfection which is intellectual knowledge. The existence of what exists in the world of creativity and emanation from the intellects is an existence resembling that which is necessary in the world of the body, which is not subject to the impossibility of what it was. Such a thing must exist, and it is therefore eternal — and how can the eternal come to be? How can it have the conditions of possessing knowledge at one time and the removal of it at another? And the abode in which it resides is not the abode of the impossibility *al-Sijistani* has affirmed by his saying "forget."

The dilemma sharpens. If these souls were granted to these people from this world, and came to learn and to be instructed in knowledge, then it is not without their being ignorant at the very outset of their existence in it, either in themselves or for themselves. If they are ignorant in themselves, then they are barred from knowing anything, and ignorance is intrinsic to them — and that is impossible, since we find that the soul learns after not knowing.

And even if it is for an accident, it is not free from that accident being either in that world or in this one. If it is the abode of eternity and perfection, not the abode of impossibility and deficiency, then it is impossible for an impossible accident to befall it. And even if it has reached that world and its terminus is to know and to return to the world it was in before, then the impossible follows it — for it is impossible in wisdom that this be so, because the soul remains in the first state, the very state from which it was rendered impossible to depart, and then to return to it. This is invalid in the case of accepting it casually, whether it was ignorance or general knowledge in this world, given that it exists without that.

If the soul's existence is not from this lower world, then it is impossible — by virtue of the perfection within it, the benefit derived from it is the perfection which is the blessings of that world, for the people of knowledge, not of this world. If that is so, and it is impossible that the soul's ignorance be in itself, or that an accident befall it whose origin is from that world — and if that accident is one whose origin is from that world — then it is fitting to say that it forgot its world.

The whole "forgetting" argument either contradicts itself or describes natural souls instead of the universal Soul. If the universal Soul is eternal in the higher world, it cannot forget, because forgetting presupposes a time of knowing and a time of not-knowing, and the eternal admits no such alternation. If what is being described is natural human souls forgetting, then the claim is true, but it has nothing to do with the universal Soul that *al-Razi* meant.

The Quran has its own scene that holds the whole question of "forgetting" in a single image. It is the scene of the primordial covenant, the *Mīthāq*, in which the souls of Adam's descendants testified to God's lordship before their entry into the abode of nature.

> *Wa-idh akhadha rabbuka min banī Ādama min ẓuhūrihim dhurriyyatahum wa-ashhadahum 'alā anfusihim: a-lastu bi-rabbikum? Qālū balā shahidnā, an taqūlū yawma al-qiyāmati innā kunnā 'an hādhā ghāfilīn.*
> *And when your Lord took from the children of Adam, from their loins, their descendants and made them testify of themselves: Am I not your Lord? They said: Yes, we have testified. Lest you should say on the Day of Resurrection: Indeed, of this we were unaware.* — (Quran 7:172; Sahih International translation)

The verse names exactly the *kind* of soul to which the language of "forgetting" applies. The souls of the children of Adam, the souls that exist in the abode of nature, the souls that are warned in advance against the plea of unawareness on the day of return, are natural souls that came into being knowing, and that are answerable for what they then come to forget under the weight of nature. The verse does not say of the First Intellect, nor of the Successor Soul that emanates from the First Intellect, that it forgot. The verse speaks of the children of Adam. *Al-Kirmani's* refusal of *al-Sijistani's* "forgetting" attack is, at root, a refusal to slide from this Quranic register of soul, the natural soul that genuinely forgets, into the universal Soul whose perfection was complete at the first instant of its existence.

The same image lives, in a different idiom, in the most famous opening in the Sufi tradition. *Jalal al-Din Rumi* opens his *Mathnawi* with the figure of the reed cut from the reed-bed, complaining of its separation.

> *Bishnaw īn nay chūn shikāyat mī-kunad, az judā'ī-hā ḥikāyat mī-kunad.*
> *Listen to the reed, how it tells a tale, complaining of separations.* — (Rumi, Mathnawi, Book 1, Verse 1; R. A. Nicholson translation)

The reed of the *Mathnawi* is the same kind of soul *al-Kirmani* has just sorted out from *al-Sijistani's* attack. It is the soul of the world of nature, the soul whose lament is precisely that it remembers a higher world from which it has been cut, and whose path home is the path of knowledge and discipline. *Rumi* and *al-Kirmani* are working in different idioms in different centuries, but the soul they are speaking about is the same soul. Neither of them mistakes that soul for the universal Soul whose perfection was complete from the first instant of its existence.

## Sub-chapter thirty-one — souls of the creative world cannot accept accidents

*Al-Kirmani* presses the same point from another side. If the soul's arrival is from the world of creativity, then its existence is first of all on the side of creativity and emancipation from the highest rank. What already exists in this manner is barred from accepting any accident, since the acceptance of accidents follows from the way existing things are divided — hovering between potentiality and actuality — which belongs to the abode of the body. But that which is in the abode of creativity has had its perfection and completion granted from the very beginning of its existence, which is eternal, and which is in this state.

It is therefore impossible for the soul of the creative world to accept any accident, or for any accident to befall it impossible to its existence. If that is so, and it is on the view of the author of *al-Nusra* that the soul forgot — that being for it an impossible accident — then *al-Sijistani* must cease saying that the soul forgot its world.

The logic is simple. Forgetting is an accident. Accidents only befall things that hover between potentiality and actuality. The creative-world Soul does not hover; it is fully actualized from the beginning. Therefore the creative-world Soul cannot forget. *Al-Sijistani's* claim refutes itself.

## Sub-chapter thirty-two — to whom forgetting actually applies

*Al-Kirmani* now says where forgetting does apply. Forgetting befalls souls that are prepared from the world of nature to accept knowledge by their entering into the accidents of it according to the causes of their existence. There then occur to them states pertaining to its being the richest of souls, if it is contented with the legal rites, and if the things that precede it in existence have been rationally connected to the First Principle — which *al-Kirmani* calls the Intellect, the Second Revival, and the Latter — being the First Resurrection according to what he has explained in his well-known book *With Peace of Mind*.

It is impossible for these souls to come from the world of intellect, for which there is no evidence at all; and it suffices, from what the author of *al-Nusra* mentioned in the forty-fourth proposition of Euclid in his well-known book *al-Maqalid*, concerning the impossibility of these souls coming from the world of intellect, as an indication of the refutation of what *al-Sijistani* said in another place. *Al-Kirmani* does not know how that escaped *al-Sijistani*. And on what he estimates, the book *al-Maqalid* was composed after *al-Nusra* — may God seat its author with our pure loyalists in the Holy House, and seal for us with goodness in our religion and our world.

The catch is exquisite. *Al-Sijistani's* own later book *al-Maqalid* contains, in its forty-fourth proposition, a proof that these natural souls cannot have come from the world of intellect — which contradicts the very position *al-Sijistani* takes here in *al-Nusra*. *Al-Kirmani* notes the contradiction, attributes it to chronological development, and prays for *al-Sijistani's* seat with the pure loyalists. The praying-for-the-opponent-while-correcting-him gesture is characteristic. *Al-Kirmani* never forgets that *al-Sijistani* is his own teacher.

## Sub-chapter thirty-three — the Intellect in its entirety does not forget

*Al-Sijistani* concedes: the intellect, in its entirety, does not forget its world, does not incline toward nature, and does not desire it. *Al-Kirmani*: this is a correct statement. If it is said of souls which have acquired knowledge and works, their perfection, and have thereby become attached to the abode of nature, that they have come to resemble the intellects which have gone forth in perfection — like the souls of the prophets and the foundations who stand in their place (the prayers of God be upon them) — and the outgoing intellects, which are the First, the Second, the Previous, the Latter, the Creative and the Emanated, then the First is higher and loftier than to have any share of the natural world, or any inclination toward it. Rather, the First's overflow is connected to it in order to complete what is not yet perfect.

*Al-Sijistani* then continued: if the soul were as perfect as the intellect, it would have remained steadfast in what it witnessed in its world, and it would not have desired to bring about the effect that it was relieved of. Its condition, that the First does not pertain to it — and it has been said in this sense that it is unique.

And he said: *al-Sijistani's* statement that the soul did not desire that effect when it was effected is an affirmation that nature is its effect. But how is it true to say that nature is the effect of souls, and that their existence is in the presence of human beings who belong to the natural world? Or how does one imagine that the universe of the heavens, planets, natures, and births is confined to human beings who can comprehend the number? Or how is it true that the perfect should desire its own condition in what is beneath it? That is impossible — and if it is impossible, then it is necessary that the existence of the natural world together with the First Emanator, even though the perfection of its assets is abundant. And to explain this is a contract that demonstrates the partisanship of the owner of *al-Nusra* against the owner of *al-Islah*, even if the words of the owner of *al-Nusra* are true in the manner *al-Kirmani* has explained, not in the way *al-Sijistani* himself described them in the rebuttal.

## Sub-chapter thirty-four — the four divisions

*Al-Sijistani* now invokes the philosophers' fourfold division. The Wise One mentioned in logic that all things exist in four senses: either in the objects of things, or in the idea, or in the words, or in writing. The things in themselves are those that the intellect has encompassed, and it has not failed to encompass them — for there is nothing that has no self, and if it were not the one that has a self, it would have eluded the sphere of the intellect. For the intellect, if it has encompassed it, is gathered in its self-possessed wealth. But the soul has encompassed only what the idea has presented to its encompassing.

If a thing is one of the things that has its self, and the idea is not presented for the soul to take care of, then the soul has not gathered its own substance, and remains incomplete in degree compared with the intellect, because it has not stooped to the collection of that which has a self — whereas the intellect has encompassed all that has a self. There is nothing that has no self — and this, *al-Sijistani* says, is one of the clearest indications of the perfection of the intellect.

*Al-Kirmani*: the discourse on this matter is irregular. The Wise One meant to say — if he were to say it — that it is to show that the assets are divided into four parts in the nature of things. What he means is: of the things that exist outside the soul and which have a meaning in themselves in it, there is what is like the body, which itself exists outside the soul, and the meaning that it has is what it is in itself — namely length, width, and depth.

He said: but in the idea — what he means is: of things that have no self outside the soul, but which are present in the mind, like genus, place, and the circles that are drawn in the soul concerning the motion of the imagined creature, and so on.

He said: as for "saying" — what he means is: of the things that exist in speech, like propositions in the affirmative and the negative concerning subjects in which the subjects do not change.

He said: but in writing — what he means is: of the things that exist in writing. And the ruling on its existence in writing is like the superabundant divine matters from the world of the intellect bestowed upon the prophets and the supporters (the prayers of God be upon them) — which, if they are not controlled by the written letters, and if the forms are not deposited in the whole, the soul has no perception of them, and the futility of its existence and its use follow.

The Wise One had restricted the assets to these four divisions, although upon investigation they reduce and dwindle beneath them. It is therefore permissible that the Wise One should wish to say what indicates that writing is a deputy for speech, and that speech is a deputy for the soul in what it wishes to display, and that the soul likewise represents those things that are outside it by imagining them, and in its own place attaches itself to them in the sense of being the same — through the acquisition of meanings by which they are.

And not in the words of the Wise One is there a proof of what *al-Sijistani* wished to support — that the speaker lacks that great extent. If the words of the owner of *al-Nusra* are carried upon what is in the natural world of souls that have risen in rank and become already existing intellects called the Second Emanation — which is still a list of forces beneath the First Emanator in the world of Holiness — then *al-Sijistani* is right.

The fourfold division — object, idea, word, writing — was meant by the philosophers as a description of how things exist for souls in the natural world. To turn it into a proof of the universal Soul's inferiority to the Intellect is to misread the philosopher and to misapply his schema.

## Sub-chapter thirty-five — the natural soul, again

The author of *al-Nusra* has made it obligatory in his statement to explain the words of the Wise One that *al-Razi's* reading is not true — if the Wise One's words are based on what the owner of *al-Islah* has to say. The owner of *al-Islah* speaks: because thought, for the souls that exist in the natural world, is a power belonging to it to acquire what it does not yet possess the image of. The end of this power, namely its becoming dispensable, is proof that the soul does not need what it has already encompassed, since it has its image with it, and has already risen in it, to think. But the soul needs thought in the things it has not descended to, and the image of which it has not yet acquired. Through these it attains the degree of perfection and dispensation. And if the soul dispenses with the thought of another —

*Al-Kirmani* completes the thought. The next thing meant is the flood of blessings upon the world's existents — by which he means the newborns. *Al-Razi* has given his power first and enriched it by what *al-Kirmani* has given of the words for *al-Razi's* perfection, which the First had attained at the beginning of His existence; and the soul became richer.

If it is so, it has become clear that the partisanship of the owner of *al-Nusra* against the owner of *al-Islah*, when *al-Sijistani* discussed him, appears — and the truth of what *al-Sijistani* said is that it pertains to the natural, not to what is contained in the Holy House.

## Sub-chapter thirty-six — deficiency is not caused by duration

*Al-Sijistani* tries the deficiency-and-time argument once more. As for the one who said that the deficiency is in the action of the soul and not in itself — because the action of the soul can only be done in time — this is mistaken and wrong, *al-Sijistani* says, as it is not necessary that what is done in the past is incomplete. If the problem lay in the diminution of things only in time, then it would be supposed that what is done not in a long time is deficient, and that the shorter the time, the more complete it would be. But we see otherwise in observation — for things that may be more complete are accomplished in a shorter time, and would be less so if the matter were as the owner of *al-Islah* described. Then it would have to be against him: time is not the cause of the deficiency of things or of their perfection, but rather the deficiency of a thing comes from the deficiency of the doer, or from the deficiency of the substances, or from the deficiency of all of them.

*Al-Kirmani*: the owner of *al-Islah* did not claim, nor say, that the action is in time in the sense *al-Sijistani* reads. *Al-Razi* said that what is implicated in the meaning is attained through time, and the duration is the deficiency. If the meaning is obtained, then it is complete; and everything in observation has its perfection — if it receives it, it is perfect. *Al-Razi* has already advanced the saying that the meaning of his saying that the deficiency is in the action of the soul and not in itself — which is to want the soul to do what attains for it the degree of perfection from its side and to acquire the image so that it becomes complete through its abundance — is nothing other than the following: the saying of the author of *al-Islah* that the deficiency is in the action of the soul, not in itself, is only an indication that the deficiency in the capable self comes from the soul that follows the flood. It cannot accept any payment except in time — days, months, and years.

And if it is so, then the saying of the owner of *al-Islah* in the first place, that of which he is speaking, is true; and the saying of the owner of *al-Nusra*, if it is carried upon the natural, is true. *Al-Sijistani* wished to be an advocate for his teacher — may God bless them and bring us with the loyal (the prayers of God be upon them).

## Sub-chapter thirty-seven — Intellect and time were created together?

*Al-Razi* in *al-Islah* said: the Most High Creator (*al-Bari*) created the intellect and time together in one fell swoop. *Al-Sijistani*'s response: it is known that time is the measure of motion, and motion belongs only to the heavens; so it must be said that the heavens, and the time generated from their motions, were with the intellect at once. And it is permissible to say that the powerful and the corrupt beings were with the intellect at once, even though it is permissible to say that the intellect and time were together at once.

This analogy entails that time is deprived of the virtues and lights given to the intellect — as the intellect receives them and is thereby perfected — and that is impossible. Although it may be said that the intellect and time were together at once, they are not without being either two things or one thing. If they are one thing, then the plural does not apply to the same thing by name alone, for it is impossible to say that the stone was with the rock, or the tide was with the knife, while they yet remained two distinct things. The intellect did not produce its own mind either without time or with time; the intellect itself is without time. For if it were together with time, then time would precede it — yet what the intellect precedes is not time, but eternity and pre-eternity.

*Al-Kirmani*: the substance of *al-Sijistani's* statement, though incomplete in proof and demonstration, is that the existence of time together with the intellect is impossible. This is correct in our doctrine, and it has already been established that it is impossible for anything to be co-present with the first intellect. That is sufficient in significance. However, the matter differs from what the author of *al-Islah* mentioned in this chapter.

The author of *al-Nusra* said that what precedes the intellect is not time, but the eternal and pre-eternal — and he erred in this, for there is nothing ahead of the first intellect, as *al-Kirmani* has explained in the foregoing statement of this book and in *Rahat al-'Aql*. What is correct is that the law of belief and doctrine concerning the eternal and pre-eternal in the world of origination requires what *al-Kirmani* has explained in the epistle known as *al-Riyad*, and in what he has mentioned therein, which need not be elaborated at length.

On the deeper question, what does and does not precede the First, *Imam Ali ibn Abi Talib* speaks in the opening sermon of *Nahj al-Balagha* in a register *al-Kirmani* would recognize as exact.

> *Lā taṣḥabuhu al-awqātu wa-lā tarfiduhu al-adwāt, sabaqa al-awqāta kawnuhu, wa-l-'adama wujūduhu, wa-l-ibtidā'a azaliyyatuhu.*
> *Times do not accompany Him, nor do instruments aid Him. His being preceded times; His existence preceded non-existence; His eternity preceded any beginning.* — (Nahj al-Balagha, Sermon 1; compiled by al-Sharif al-Radi)

This is the same logic *al-Kirmani* is enforcing. The First does not lie alongside time as one thing alongside another. To ask whether the Intellect was *with* time, or before time, or after time, is to import the category of natural duration into the place where it does not apply. *Imam Ali* refuses that category, in the same way, four centuries before *al-Kirmani*. The pre-eternal is not earlier in time. It is the rank that has no *earlier* and no *later* of the kind by which times are measured.

This is the pattern that recurs: both *al-Razi* and *al-Sijistani* are partly correct, partly mistaken; *al-Kirmani* extracts the correct from each and replaces the mistaken with his own settled doctrine.

## Sub-chapter thirty-eight — closing the chapter

The thirty-eighth sub-chapter is brief and is the closing of the chapter. Among the proofs that the intellect became perfect, *al-Sijistani* says, is that it was not subject to anything except the Almighty, while the soul became imperfect because it was subject to nature.

*Al-Kirmani's* reply ranges wider than the immediate claim. The philosophers, he says, and the most advanced among them — though virtuous in their time — continued to err in many of the matters of intellect they discussed. Had they lived in our time, they would have seen the springs of grace and how they overflow from the House of Revelation. Indeed, were things as they are in the *Alawite* call, with their diligence they would have done themselves justice and would have corrected much of their belief in what the senses fall short of grasping. With the traces of the One for whom God has made a light in the mysteries of things, they would have been convinced.

For in their own times the philosophers followed their own opinions and intellects in what they pursued, until each of them in his belief reached only what his works indicate — like the people of the exoteric in the Hanafi school, when they relied on their intellects to follow the paths of religion and their opinions diverged, distinctions arising among them: some more correct, others more in error. He who relies on what the balance of religion testifies — namely the horizons and the souls — finds in the foregoing discourse the explanation that the existence of such a belief is false, and it need not be expounded at length.

## The closing prayer

*Al-Kirmani* seals the chapter with the prayer that has been waiting through the whole argument:

> We ask God to enlighten our intellects, to seal our affairs with happiness, and to grant us success in good words and good deeds, by virtue of His being, His kindness, and His generosity.

The whole sweep of *Chapter One* — the foundational dispute over whether the Soul is perfect or deficient, the long parade of analogies through which *al-Sijistani* tried to fix the Soul on a single scale where below-the-Intellect would mean less-than-perfect, *al-Kirmani's* steady distinction between rank and self, his rescue of *al-Razi's* doctrine in formulations cleaner than *al-Razi's* own, and his refusal to grant *al-Sijistani's* slippage between the universal Soul and the sensual soul of a struggling human being — comes to rest on a prayer for enlightened minds and good deeds. The doctrinal work is preparation for the lived work. *Tawhid* is not, in *al-Kirmani's* sense, only a thesis to defend. It is the kind of clarity that lets souls move through the natural world toward the second perfection that is theirs by right.

The next chapter will narrow to the First Intellect, the First Creator — nine sub-chapters, sharper, with one foot already in the explicit critique of *al-Mahsul* that will close the book. But the formula that the second chapter, and all the chapters after it, will continue to apply has now been established. Perfect in self. Deficient only in addition. Rank is not identity. And the soul who hears this, and lives by it, walks the path that the prayer asks for.
