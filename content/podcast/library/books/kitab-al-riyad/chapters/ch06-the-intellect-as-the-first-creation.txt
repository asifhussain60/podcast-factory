# The Intellect as the First Creation

*This chapter covers all nine sub-chapters of* Chapter Two *of* Kitab al-Riyad — *al-Kirmani's pivot from the long argument over the Soul to the underlying question of what the First Intellect actually is, and why nothing said of bodies — motion, stillness, need, dwelling, encompassing — can be said of it at all. The author of* al-Islah *had imagined motion and stillness as two effects "united in the First and the Second." The author of* al-Nusra *had counter-imagined motion for the Soul and stillness for the Intellect. Al-Kirmani refuses both. The First and Second Intellects are not embodied; motion and stillness are categories of bodies; therefore neither applies. From that simple proof a far-reaching argument unfolds — about creativity and the Creator, potentiality and actuality, the overflow of light from the* hudud *to those who can receive it, and the political stake the* da'wa *has in holding firm to this distinction. The chapter closes on the prayer that the ranks of the* hudud *be kept clear, lest souls fall into confusion about the realities of things.*

## Where this chapter picks up

The thirty-eight sub-chapters of *Chapter One* established the doctrinal formula — perfect in self, deficient only in addition to what stands above — and walked it through every analogy *al-Sijistani* could deploy in *al-Nusra* against *al-Razi*'s positions in *al-Islah*. The closing pages of that chapter narrowed to the question of whether the philosophers of antiquity erred in their reasoning. They erred, *al-Kirmani* said, because they followed only their own intellects, in their own times, without the witness of the *Alawite da'wa* and the lights of revelation.

That closing accusation now narrows further. *Chapter Two* turns from the Soul to the First Intellect itself — and to the specific terms in which *al-Islah* and *al-Nusra* described it. The chapter is short by the standards of *al-Riyad* — only nine sub-chapters where *Chapter One* held thirty-eight — but the doctrinal payload is dense. *Al-Kirmani* is no longer arguing across analogies. He is arguing about a single point of cosmology: what kind of existent the First Intellect is, and what predicates can and cannot be attached to it.

The whole chapter turns on one proof. Motion and stillness are predicates of bodies. The First and Second Intellects are not bodies. Therefore neither motion nor stillness can be said of them, in potency or in actuality, in any sense whatsoever. Both *al-Islah* and *al-Nusra* erred in the way they applied these categories to the supra-natural realm. *Al-Kirmani* takes their language one piece at a time and dismantles it.

The reader who has followed the first chapter will recognize the move. The natural and the supra-natural are different orders of existence. What is true of bodies in the world of nature is not true of intellects in the world of origination (*dar al-Ibda'*). To carry the categories of one into the other is the master-error that the whole book is correcting.

## Sub-chapter one — motion is for bodies, not for the First Intellect

The author of *al-Islah* opened *Chapter Two* with a complex formulation. Motion and stillness, he said, are two effects united in the First and the Second. For the First, when it became creative, united with creativity, and that union had two effects upon it in potentiality. The reason for two effects is that creativity and the creative are two — one being creativity itself, the other being the creative itself; though they are understood as one essence, the whole. These two effects exist in the First in potentiality. Then the Second is emitted from the First, so the emission has two effects upon the intellect — one the emission itself, the other the union of the two origins in the matter. One of the two effects is motion, which is higher than the other (stillness); and one of them belongs to the First, which is higher than the other (the Second). The motion of the First is the conveyance of the Second of the essences in all, and stillness is the Second's acceptance of the benefit of the First of all the essences and its inclusion within it.

*Al-Islah* drew a careful safeguard at the end. *I do not say that motion and stillness are born of the soul by force, learning them from the intellect; rather, I say that they are two effects of the soul by the power learned from the intellect in the image and the form, so that the image and the form became the foundation of this world.*

The product of his statement, the author of *al-Islah* summarized, is that motion and stillness are two effects united in the First and the Second; that they exist in the First in potentiality; that one of them — motion — is higher than the other; that motion is the benefit of the First, and stillness the acceptance by the Second of the benefit; that the soul received them by the power learned from the intellect in the image; and that the First, when it became creative, united with creativity — creativity and the creator being two even if they are one thing.

The author of *al-Nusra* objected on different grounds. This statement contains some confusion, he said, for motion is only for the sake of seeking. The motion of fire upward is to seek its place; the motion of earth downward is to seek its place; the motion of souls is to seek benefit. When seeking is established, there is need, for seeking arises only from need. It is impossible to say that the intellect needs something for which it moves. Even if it were possible to ascribe motion to the two origins, ascribing it to the soul is more fitting than to the intellect, because the soul needs to benefit from the intellect — moving toward it with the motion of longing, the motion of the effect toward the cause. If motion belongs to the soul, then stillness belongs to the intellect — stillness in what it receives from the virtues and lights bestowed upon it by its principle, not seeking increase, because its principle has overflowed upon it from the lights and the light whose limit the intellect does not know. Moreover, the intellect has not poured forth upon the soul all that overflowed upon it, but only some of it, so that the soul might dwell upon it and grasp it when it was emitted. The substance of the statement, *al-Nusra* said, is that motion is only from need; that the intellect is not in need of motion; that ascribing motion to the soul is more fitting than to the intellect; that motion belongs to the soul and stillness to the intellect; and that the intellect has not poured forth upon the soul all that it has, but only some — so as to be the cause of the soul's motion in seeking the rest. This is the sum of what they all say.

*Al-Kirmani's* reply is the proof on which the whole chapter rests. As for the saying of the author of *al-Islah* that motion and stillness are effects united in the First and the Second, and that in the First they exist in potentiality and in the Second in actuality — this affirms that the First is at rest, moving in potentiality, because motion and stillness exist in it potentially; and that the Second is already at rest by virtue of motion and stillness being in it in potentiality, and that the Second is moving — and this is impossible in several ways.

The saying of the author of *al-Nusra* — that the intellect is not in need, and therefore is not in motion — is correct, but only in part. *Al-Kirmani* will not give *al-Sijistani* the conclusion he wanted. The deeper truth is that motion in all its kinds is found only in natural bodies. The First and Second Intellects, being beings of the first existence, are not embodied, so it is not permissible that they be in motion or at rest.

The proof: the body requires being a substance, and the dependence of one on another in existence such that its existence would not be from God Almighty as the first existence — because what exists from God is the first existence. The very self of the First Intellect is none other than the act of which it is the first existence, by God the Almighty and Great; and the same act has one subject and not two. If the First Intellect's existence were not the first existence, it would be preceded by another in existence, and so it would be something else. If the Intellect were a body, it would be preceded in existence by others, and it would not be the first creation. Nothing precedes the first intellect in its existence, since it is itself the act of creation, behind which is only God the Creator Almighty, whose existence is indicated. If it is impossible that anything precede it in existence, then it is invalid that it be a body. And if it is impossible to be a body, and there is no motion except when one is a body, then the intellect has no motion; and if this is invalid, then the statement of the author of *al-Islah* — that the intellect has motion in potentiality — is nullified.

The same proof bears on *al-Nusra's* counter. *Al-Sijistani* tried to relocate motion from the Intellect to the Soul. He kept the category. He just moved it down a rank. *Al-Kirmani* refuses the move at root. Motion is for bodies. The Second is not a body either. Neither motion nor stillness can be predicated of the universal Soul any more than of the First Intellect. *Al-Sijistani* won the small skirmish — the intellect is not in motion — but lost the larger ground he wanted to hold.

*Al-Kirmani's* own treatise on the architecture of the Intellect, *Rahat al-'Aql* (*The Repose of the Intellect*), spells the cosmology out at length. The First Intellect emerges from the Almighty Creator's act in a single, complete, eternal existence; the Second is emanated from the First in the same instant; both are beyond the reach of the categories that apply to bodies in the world of nature. The reader who wants the full mapping is sent there. The point in *al-Riyad* is just this: the categorical mismatch is the master-error, and from it every other error in *al-Islah* and *al-Nusra* on this question flows.

## Sub-chapter two — creativity and the Creator are not two

The author of *al-Islah* had said: *we said two effects because creativity and the creative are two — one being creativity itself, the other being the creative itself — and creativity and the creative, even if two, are nothing but a whole, in an image not to be believed.*

The formulation is meant to safeguard the unity of the First while allowing a duality of effects. *Al-Kirmani* refuses the safeguard. Creativity and the creator are matters contrary to what *al-Islah* mentioned. They are not two. Creativity is the creator and the creator is creativity, and there is only one. If creativity is added to what its existence began with, it is an act of His, and the same as the act. And if creativity is added to itself, it is the principle that it is the object of another, whose existence comes from Him.

The doctrinal precision matters. The First Intellect is not the joining of two — creativity and the creator — that then has effects upon it as two. The First Intellect is one act; one principle; one existence. The verbal distinction between "creativity" and "the creative" is a distinction in our naming, not in the underlying reality. *Al-Islah* moved from a verbal duality (two words for the same essence) to a real duality (two effects in the First in potency). That move is unauthorized.

The deeper claim is even sharper. To speak of creativity as something other than the Creator's act is to introduce, alongside the Creator, a co-present principle whose existence is from the Creator. The two-acted picture invites the very confusion *al-Riyad* exists to refuse: that something is alongside the Almighty in the moment of the first existence. There is nothing alongside. The First Intellect is the act of creation, and behind it is only the Creator. *Al-Kirmani* keeps the wall standing.

This is also, in its own way, a defense of *Tawhid*. The unity of God, as the Imam *al-Mu'izz li-Din Allah* explained in *Ta'wil al-Shari'a* — the work *al-Kirmani* repeatedly draws on in the later books of *al-Riyad* — does not admit of any second alongside Him in His act. Creativity is not a thing that the Creator joins to in order to create. The Creator creates; the creativity is the act; the First Intellect is the existence emerging from that act. To split creativity from the creative, even as a doctrinal subtlety, is to risk opening a crack in the *Tawhid* itself.

## Sub-chapter three — actuality is more honorable than potentiality

On the statement of the author of *al-Islah* that motion and stillness in the First are in potentiality, and in the Second in actuality, *al-Kirmani* makes a brief, decisive logical observation.

What pertains to the first existence is only the mere act and the right of action. Even if it were permissible — for the sake of argument — for motion and stillness to exist in the First in potentiality, the ruling of the Second would then be more honorable than that of the First. Because the ruling of the one in actuality is more honorable than the ruling of the one in potentiality. It is impossible for the Second to precede the First in honor. What *al-Islah* mentioned is therefore among the attributes of what comes later — of the existence that needs action in its outward manifestation, and pertains to what already exists.

The First Intellect is indeed intellectual, and so too is the Second; what follows is what is paradoxical for the substances of the intellects. It is not permissible to believe that what necessarily exists from the House of Origination is specialized in matters that fall outside the highest limits.

The reductio is clean. *Al-Islah* wanted two ranks: motion higher than stillness, and the First higher than the Second. By placing motion-and-stillness in the First in potency and in the Second in actuality, he reversed his own ranks. Whatever is in actuality is more honorable than whatever is in potency — that is a settled philosophical maxim *al-Islah* himself would not deny. So *al-Islah's* own framing makes the Second more honorable than the First. That is impossible. Therefore the framing is wrong.

The lesson generalizes. The categories of potency and actuality belong to existents that come into being and pass through stages of attainment. They are categories of becoming. The First Intellect does not become. It is. Whatever predicates we attach to it must be predicates compatible with its mode of existence, which is the first existence, fully actualized at the instant of its origination, with no stage of attainment to pass through. To say the First is "in potency" with respect to anything is to misdescribe its mode of being.

## Sub-chapter four — the hayula and the image

The author of *al-Islah* now tried a recovery move. Motion and stillness are effects of the soul by the power learned from the intellect, in the case of the *hayula* (prime matter, the *phylis*) and the image (*al-sura*).

The reformulation is meant to displace the difficulty downward. The Intellect does not move; the Soul does not move; but the natural world that the Soul informs — the world of *hayula* and image — that world moves and rests by a power the soul learned from the intellect. The motion is in the bodies; the source of the power is the intellect; the agent of the transmission is the soul.

*Al-Kirmani* allows this — but only with a careful gloss. What is meant, he says, is that the bodies, which are the *hayula* together with the image, are moved — that which is moved among them — by the natural force whose presence within them is from what is outside the first and second intellects. This is a motion of longing. *Al-Islah* placed the two effects in the hallways: the image is the mover, and its being moved is impossible. This is learned from the intellect in the sense that its existence is from it — and this is correct in this way, and it is the best interpretation to which what *al-Islah* said may be attributed.

The concession is real. If the language about "two effects united in the First and the Second" is read down — as a description of the natural world's motion-and-rest, where bodies do indeed have motion and rest, and where the natural force that animates them descends ultimately from the intellect's overflow — then the language can stand. The image is the mover for the body; the body is what is moved. And the power of the image to move the body is "learned from the intellect" in the sense that the chain of existence reaches up through the soul to the intellect.

But — and this is the constraint *al-Kirmani* will not relax — the language only stands in this natural-world reading. The moment it is taken to apply to the First and Second Intellects themselves, it falls back into the error of sub-chapter one. *Al-Kirmani's* generosity is doctrinal: he is willing to read *al-Islah's* most defensible meaning into a passage that on its face says something stronger. This is the "best interpretation" gesture *al-Kirmani* uses throughout *al-Riyad* when his deceased master *al-Razi* has spoken loosely. Where *al-Razi* can be read into compatibility with the doctrine, *al-Kirmani* reads him there. *Al-Sijistani* does not extend the same courtesy. He reads *al-Razi* at his most exposed and presses there.

## Sub-chapter five — al-Nusra's softer line, and why it also fails

The author of *al-Nusra* had landed, after his attacks, on what he hoped would be the settled formulation. Motion is for the Soul and stillness for the Intellect, so it is not permissible to believe such things of those transcendent boundaries.

*Al-Kirmani* takes the formula apart in summary.

The world of origination is the collective of before and after, and the pure intellects which are the paradoxical higher beings of the objects. The aim of what is fanciful — what is imagined within them — is motion or stillness. But motion, in that which is moving, is that which is moving in it; it is something else from the thing itself, and the moving has the existence of the self. If motion is in what is moving, it is the one in which the other resides, having the existence of the self and what it is. As for the existence of the self in the presence of different things, the self becomes lacking and needy.

The Next — what *al-Nusra* called the Soul — being from the world of origination, is specialized in completion and perfection. The same does not become imperfect and needy. So it is invalid that the Soul differ in itself. If it is impossible for there to be in the Soul something other than itself, and if it is impossible for the Soul to be what is not itself, then it could not be in motion. It would be impossible for motion to be in it. For motion is that in which the thing wherein it resides moves away from its first state to the next — either in the self or in its states. The Next, which *al-Nusra* called the Soul, has an eternal existence by virtue of its arising from the side of origination in the second sense — namely, that it is intellectual. If it is eternal, then it is impossible for it to be transferred from what it was upon at first, and it is invalid that there be motion in it. The saying concerning stillness is like the saying concerning motion. Neither does the first intellect have any stillness, nor does the next, which *al-Nusra* called the Soul, have any motion.

The careful close: rather, the author of *al-Nusra* wished to say what he said about those souls and intellects that are in the House of Nature, where a second emanation is emitted; the being is moved according to what *al-Kirmani* has explained in his book known as *Rahat al-'Aql* (*The Repose of the Intellect*). Motion and stillness are specialized in the world of the body and what it contains, because they are among the causes of its perfection. They are not what is specific to the House of Origination. But *al-Nusra* conveyed the meaning to assimilation in what he said concerning the deficiency, in support of his teacher (*sheikh*), Abu Hatim al-Razi — may God make his dignity, and gather them and us with our pure followers.

The structure of the rebuttal repeats *al-Kirmani's* characteristic move. The opponent is partly correct (motion does not befit the Intellect; stillness in some sense fits it) and partly wrong (motion does not befit the universal Soul either). The way to correct him is to relocate his true claim to the part of the cosmos where it applies, and to refuse its extension into the part where it does not. And the prayer for the opponent — gathering *al-Sijistani* with the loyal — is, again, the gesture of a disciple who is correcting his own master without rancor.

## Sub-chapter six — motion is not only from need

The author of *al-Nusra* had built his earlier argument on the maxim: motion is from need; the Soul is more in need than the Intellect; therefore motion belongs to the Soul. *Al-Kirmani* now returns to the maxim and shows that even where motion is permissible, the inference from "motion" to "need-and-therefore-deficiency" does not hold.

Movement and stillness, *al-Kirmani* reaffirms, are appropriate only for what is a body. The First and the Second of whom we speak are not embodied, so attributing movement and stillness to them is excluded, in the manner *al-Kirmani* has shown in his earlier words and in his well-known book *Rahat al-'Aql* (*The Repose of the Intellect*). The statement holds true in what comes about by way of generation: the Second is not a creative agent, and its existence is in the First Emission, not by movement.

But — and here is the deeper correction — as for what *al-Nusra* mentioned about everything's need: movement in a natural thing may be among the causes of its perfection, not from need that betrays deficiency. The motion of the heavens is the standing example. The heavens move not out of need in themselves to attain what was not granted at the first instant of their creation, but rather toward perfection — toward the rolling-out of what they already possess in completeness.

The example *al-Kirmani* reaches for next is the most consequential. Likewise the movement of the *natiq* (the Speaker-Prophet) and his diligence in gathering the word upon worship, and his engagement with the affairs of governance — this belongs to the completeness that is required, because something is required of him after he has attained perfection and risen to the rank of *natiq* while he yet exists.

The *natiq* is not deficient. He has reached the highest rank of human existence. Yet he moves — he preaches, he founds the community, he establishes the *shari'a*, he governs. His motion is not a sign of need-as-lack. His motion is the working-out of his perfection in the temporal order, the unfolding of what he already is in actuality into the lives of those who follow him. The same logic applies to the heavens: the heavens already possess their perfection; their motion is its expression, not its acquisition.

This shows, *al-Kirmani* concludes, that the statement of the author of *al-Nusra* — that movement for need is not realized at all — is not correct. *Al-Nusra's* ruling is sound only for some natural things, on the basis that movement is inseparable from them, as *al-Kirmani* has already mentioned. The general maxim collapses. Motion can express perfection as well as betray deficiency. Which it does depends on what kind of existent is in motion and toward what.

Imam Ali (peace be upon him) had said of the intellect itself — in the aphorisms gathered in his tradition — that *the intellect is the messenger of the truth*. The intellect's work is to convey, to deliver, to make visible. Where the work involves motion, the motion is the delivery, not a sign of unfinished business in the messenger. This is the spirit of *al-Kirmani's* correction.

## Sub-chapter seven — overflow, and the limits of receivership

The author of *al-Nusra* now levels a politically charged accusation. *What does he have over the soul, except in some respect?* he asks of *al-Razi*. What *al-Razi* meant by saying that the First is distinguished by the rank of priority and the highest standing in what is not specific without it — what he called a Soul — is the best thing attributed to him by way of interpretation.

Otherwise, *al-Nusra* presses, if the matter were as *al-Razi* described, and each of the *hudud* received from the rank above it overflowed onto the rank below, then the Prophet (peace and blessings of Allah be upon him and his family) would have been deluded. The blessings that reached him from the world of *al-Quds* (holiness) would have flowed onward to the *wali* and his followers without leaving any trace, and the Prophet would not have informed anyone of it — which is invalidated by the invalidity of its use. So the *wali* (peace be upon him) becomes doubtful in his perfection and merit, and his standing in the place of the Prophet (peace and blessings of Allah be upon him and his family) and obedience to him fall under suspicion. On that view it would not be permissible to believe in the *natiq*, nor in the *wali*, nor in the *imam*, nor in the *hudud*.

The accusation is well-aimed. If the overflow doctrine means that every blessing from above falls through every rank without obstruction, then the higher ranks are emptied. The Prophet and the *wali* and the *imam* become interchangeable conduits. The distinctions among the *hudud* dissolve, and the obligation to recognize and obey the *imam* of one's time loses its grip. This is not a theoretical worry. The whole architecture of the Fatimid *da'wa* — the *natiq*, the *asas*, the *imam*, the *hujja*, the *da'i* — rests on the reality of the distinctions among ranks.

*Al-Kirmani's* answer is one of the central doctrinal passages of *al-Riyad*. Rather, *we say*: the blessings overflow from the side of all the *hudud*. The reason being that there is no shortage in those blessings by which perfection is attained in all beings, nor any withholding on the part of the *hudud*. The limitation lies rather in the incapacity of the receivers to accept them — each according to the capacity of the one able to receive. Some receivers accept the blessings fully, others accept only what they are able to. There are natural learners, and those who require compassionate teachers, and willing learners. It is not permissible for the *hudud* to withhold benefit from one who would benefit from it, so long as the receiver is in his right when willed — lest he fail to ascend to a rank that was his before the spiritual birth.

The doctrine is precise. The *hudud* withhold nothing. The light pours forth in fullness. What differs is the receiver's capacity. The Prophet receives more because the Prophet is fitted by the Almighty to receive more. The *wali* receives in his measure; the *imam* in his measure; the *hujja* in his measure; the believer in his measure. The structure of ranks is the structure of receivership, not of withholding. *Al-Sijistani's* worry was that overflow without remainder would empty the higher ranks. The answer is that the higher ranks are not made by what they refuse to pass on; they are made by their fitness to receive at the higher measure to begin with.

*Al-Kirmani* anchors the principle in two Quranic passages of striking force. The Prophet (peace and blessings of Allah be upon him) said in the words of revelation: *Do not kill your children for fear of poverty* (Quran 17:31). Whoever does that has sinned, exceeded his proper stage, and fallen from the rank of the virtuous. How then could it be permissible to lock up the spiritual wealth — namely the sciences and the blessings of light — from their rightful owners?

The argument is striking. To withhold the higher knowledges from those who can receive them is a kind of spiritual infanticide. As one is forbidden to kill one's own offspring out of fear that there will not be enough to go around, so the *hudud* are forbidden to withhold the lights of knowledge from those whose capacity makes them rightful recipients. The fear that there will not be enough is the original error; the *hudud* never run out.

The second anchor reinforces the first. The Prophet (peace and blessings of Allah be upon him) commanded the guardians of orphans, by the word of revelation: *If you find them of sound mind, then deliver to them their wealth, and do not consume it wastefully and in haste* (Quran 4:6). What the guardian holds is not his own; it must be delivered to the rightful owner when the owner is of sound mind to receive it. So with the *hudud* and the wealth of knowledge. They are not the proprietors of the light; they are its guardians, charged with delivering it to whoever is of sound capacity to receive it.

No — *al-Kirmani* closes the section — the blessings overflow only that they may be conveyed to those who deserve them. The record of this stands in its proper place, as *al-Kirmani* has explained in his hermeneutic letters and in his Basran and Baghdadi assemblies (*majalis*) — may Allah and a company of our brethren make us perform the trust to its rightful possessors and to those faithful to its terms. Perhaps the author of *al-Nusra*, by way of analogy, has narrated what he said.

The closing prayer is again characteristic. *Al-Sijistani's* error is real, and answered carefully; but *al-Sijistani* himself is included in the prayer for the loyal. The doctrine is firm; the tone toward the master who erred is gentle. This is *al-Kirmani's* posture throughout the book.

The same doctrine appears in *al-Kirmani's* own *Rahat al-'Aql*, where the seven seas of the universal Intellect are described, and where the architecture of overflow from the higher *hudud* to the lower is laid out in the language of mediation and capacity. The reader who needs the full diagram is sent there. The point in *al-Riyad* is the doctrinal core: light does not fall through ranks because no rank holds it; light falls through ranks because each rank passes on what those below can receive. The structure of grace is the structure of capacity.

## Sub-chapter eight — the intellect encompasses; the soul moves to reach the intellect

The author of *al-Nusra* now tries a fresh angle. Action, he says, is the essence of encompassing, and it encompasses things — and this is not the state of that which is movable. The movable does not encompass things but rather needs something to dwell in, in complete stillness, so that it may encompass the still. Yet the intellect moves when it encompasses something, and the soul does not encompass itself by benefiting from itself.

When we find the intellect to be an ocean gathering things, with nothing descending and encompassing it — when we find that whoever encompasses what he attains and gains from his light and his virtues — we know that the intellect is utterly at rest, and there is no illusion of movement in it. The soul, on this view, moves only to reach the door of the intellect, and then to dwell there. It does not move in order to encompass the intellect by benefiting from it as it would encompass something else. So long as the soul is in motion, *al-Nusra* says, it does not encompass; nor does it attain virtues and lights until it dwells by benefiting from the intellect; only then does it encompass things. The soul thus moves a movement of subsistence, which comes to dwell by benefiting from the One who is settled.

*This is what he said.*

*Al-Kirmani's* answer pulls the rug. It might be said, he replies, that the intellect is not in motion regarding what it does not need to be extended toward. But it does not follow — if the intellect is not moving — that it must be at rest in the manner of bodies, which are not free of being either at rest or in motion. The intellect's existence is not like the existence of objects, which are never free of the values already given. This is a sign that movement and stillness do not apply to it.

The same principle is required by the saying that, so long as the soul is in motion, it does not encompass anything and does not attain virtues. For that meaning is not correct, whatever the case may be — neither in what is differentiated from the Second, as *al-Kirmani* mentioned, nor in what is not so differentiated. The souls that are in the house of nature are not acquired except by being movable and embodied, using the senses, deductions, and instruments — and the existence of the command, in establishing evidence for it, suffices to show that the error here persists.

The catch is sharp. *Al-Nusra's* picture imagines the soul as needing to "come to rest at the door of the intellect" before it can encompass anything. But natural souls — the only souls that strictly speaking move — only come to encompass things *through* their motion. They use their senses; they reason; they extend instruments of inquiry; they move from premise to conclusion. The whole acquisition of knowledge in the natural soul happens *in motion*. *Al-Nusra's* claim that motion precludes encompassing is empirically refuted by the way actual natural souls come to know.

So *al-Nusra's* picture errs twice. It errs first by importing the body's category — never-free-of-motion-or-rest — into the intellect, where neither applies. And it errs second by misdescribing the natural soul, where motion is precisely how encompassing happens, not its obstacle. The "soul that moves to dwell in stillness" picture works for no soul that actually exists.

## Sub-chapter nine — the movement of subsistence

The chapter's closing sub-chapter takes up *al-Nusra's* formula. *The soul moves a movement of subsistence, which comes to dwell by benefiting from the One who is settled.*

*Al-Kirmani* offers a careful judgment. *Al-Nusra* is truthful in this when he has assigned a meaning to it, and he is in error if he carries it on the surface of what he says.

The truthful meaning. *Al-Nusra* speaks correctly if he is assigning the formula to the class of natural souls. One says that the soul moves because the natural souls, which exist in potentiality, are moving toward acquisition. The movement is a movement of subsistence because its acquisition leads it to subsist — to persist, to stand, to have stable being. And the soul dwells by benefiting because it passes from potency to act by acquiring and benefiting — by gathering knowledge, by attaining virtues in knowledge and in action.

The error. As for *al-Nusra's* saying that the soul is settled by Him from whom already exists the perfection, and that he is to be understood on the surface meaning — this is the place *al-Nusra* errs. The Second is not in motion, nor is the intellect at rest in the sense in which bodies are at rest. They have no need of movement, stillness, or attentive care.

*Al-Kirmani* draws the formula together. *One must take care to know the ranks of the* hudud, *so that there be no confusion about the matter of perceiving things in their realities — for such confusion would lead to the corruption of the essence of souls.*

This is the chapter's deepest warning, and it ties the whole nine sub-chapters together. The categorical mismatch — applying body-categories to non-bodies — is not merely a logical error. It corrupts the soul that thinks it. To imagine the First Intellect as moving in potency is to take a step toward imagining God as alongside another. To imagine the universal Soul as moving for need is to take a step toward emptying the higher *hudud* of their distinction. To imagine the *natiq's* work as a sign of his deficiency is to take a step toward refusing his rank altogether. Doctrinal precision in cosmology is not academic. It guards the soul's capacity to perceive the realities for what they are.

## The closing prayer

The chapter seals on *al-Kirmani's* prayer:

> May Allah make us light by their means, and grant us success in following the pure stages of the divine sciences and the treasuries of the lights of sanctity; may Allah's prayers be upon them.

The prayer carries the chapter's whole motion. By their means — by the means of the *hudud* whose ranks must be kept clear in the perceiving soul — let us become light. By following their pure stages — not by collapsing their distinctions, not by importing body-categories into supra-natural existents, not by imagining motion or need or dwelling where none applies — let us be granted success. The treasuries of the lights of sanctity are not to be reached by confusion. They are reached by exactness in perception, which is itself a grace.

The next chapter narrows further still. *Chapter Three* takes up the question of whether the Soul and the Spirit are one. Where *Chapter Two* held the line on what the First Intellect is *not* (it is not a body; it does not move; it does not have a counterpart-creativity alongside it), *Chapter Three* will move into the architecture of what comes next in the chain — the relation between the Soul, the Spirit, the human composite, and the world that the Soul informs. The formula remains the same. Perfect in self; deficient only in addition. Each rank is what it is in its place. The *hudud* are kept distinct so that the lights may reach those whose capacity is fitted to receive them. The discipline that holds those distinctions clear is the discipline that lets the soul become, in time, what it was created to be.
