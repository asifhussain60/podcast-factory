# The Human Being as Fruit of All the Worlds

*From the Kitab al-Riyad (ki-taab ar-ri-yaad) of Hamid al-Din Ahmad al-Kirmani (haa-mid ad-deen ah-mad al-kir-maa-nee). This chapter covers Bāb 5 (baab) — On the judgment between the two disputants in their discourse concerning whether the human being is the fruit of the natural world — comprising seven fusūl (fu-sool; sections). Bāb 4 introduced the predicate of *second emanation* (al-i-naa-baath ath-thaa-nee) to describe the rational souls' relation to the universal Soul above them, refusing both the part-language and the mere-effect-language, and naming what the soul *acquires through obedience and worship* from the hudud (hu-dood; the named teaching ranks). Bāb 5 now takes that same architecture and asks the question on which the previous bāb's metaphysics was always pointing: where, in this layered cosmos, does the human being actually stand? Is he the fruit of *this* world — the natural order whose seasons and motions and elemental mixings shape his body? Or is he the fruit of the *higher* world — the originative order whose effects, passed down through the prophets and the Imams, form his rational soul? Al-Islah (al-is-laah) takes the first horn; al-Nusra (an-nus-rah) takes the second; and al-Kirmani, in a move that gathers the architecture of the previous bābs into a single anthropological claim, declines both partial answers and names what the human truly is: not the fruit of one world alone, but **the fruit of all the worlds** — *the last in the universes, united in eternity with what has been acquired by time*. The seven fusūl walk through this answer's structure, its surrounding metaphysical conditions, and the closing axiom that will carry the rest of the treatise: **the last of them was already in the first of them**.*

## What this bāb is really asking

The previous four bābs have been building an architecture: First Intellect at the originative apex, the universal Soul as the first emanation, the rational souls as the second emanation, the natural world as the field in which bodies are constituted by motion and stillness and elemental mixing. Each rank has been distinguished from the next by the receiving and giving of effects; each predicate (perfection, resemblance, part, effect, emanation) has been tested and either accepted in a restricted reading or replaced. Bāb 5 turns from the architecture to the *creature for whom the architecture exists*. The technical metaphysics finds its anthropological point: this whole layered cosmos — origination at the top, emanation through the dyad of First and Second, descent into nature, ascent back through the *hudud* — culminates in a being who *receives from both directions*.

The question the disputants are arguing over has a deceptively simple form. *Is the human the fruit of this world?* — that is, the last and most refined product of the natural order's long arc of generation and corruption? Or *is the human the fruit of the higher world?* — that is, the destination toward which the originative emanation has been moving from the start, the rational soul being the point at which the chain finally reaches a self-conscious knower of God? Each disputant takes one horn. Each disputant has *part* of the truth. And each disputant, by insisting on his half, loses the other half — and with it, loses the whole structure of why the architecture exists at all.

Al-Kirmani's adjudication will turn on three claims, each of which gathers what the previous bābs have established. *First:* the human being is constituted at the seam of two worlds, receiving his *first perfection* (the physical form ready to receive the three souls) from the natural order, and his *second perfection* (the perfected spiritual form, abiding through the knowledge of tawhid — taw-heed — by means of the hudud) from the higher order. He is the fruit of *both*, because he is what the meeting of the two overflows produces. *Second:* the human is the fruit of *all* the worlds — not just nature and intellect, but every rank in the chain, because the architecture is a chain of recapitulations and what is at the bottom carries the structure of what is at the top. *Third:* an architectural axiom that will carry the rest of the treatise: *the last of them was already in the first of them*. The human's existence at the terminus of the chain is not an accident of biology arriving late on the scene; it is the implicit reading-out of what was already enfolded at the origin.

The middle fusūl of this bāb (3, 4, 5, 7) take detours through surrounding metaphysical errors that al-Nusra keeps making — about emanation and prime matter, about heaven and earth as a marriage, about the predicate of *al-Tamamiyyah* (at-ta-maa-mee-yah; perfection) belonging to the Creator, about whether the Soul's act is a *seeking* of a rank not yet attained, about whether order in the world comes only from the Intellect. Each detour is necessary because each unaddressed error, left standing, would either deny the human his proper rank in the architecture or contaminate the upper ranks with imperfection. Al-Kirmani's anthropological claim is not separable from his metaphysics; he must clear the metaphysics to land the anthropology.

## The Qur'anic horizon — the trust and the best of forms

Before walking through the seven fusūl, it is worth pausing on the Qur'anic horizon against which al-Kirmani's anthropological claim is read. The Qur'an (qur-aan) names the human being three times in a register that the disputants must contend with, even when they do not cite it directly.

The first image is the human as *the bearer of the Trust*:

> Indeed, We offered the Trust to the heavens and the earth and the mountains, and they declined to bear it and feared it; but man undertook to bear it. Indeed, he was unjust and ignorant.
> (Quran 33:72; English: Saheeh International.)

*Man undertook to bear it.* The Trust (*al-amāna*) is what the heavens and the earth — the cosmic ranks that organize al-Nusra's metaphysics — *declined*. The human took it up. The verse is the Qur'anic charter for the anthropology al-Kirmani is about to defend: the human is not the fruit of this world in the sense of being its passive product; the human is the *one creature in the cosmos who is asked to carry something the cosmos itself could not carry*. The Trust is what the human's rational soul is *for*. The natural order produced the body that is ready to receive the three souls; the higher order entrusted to that ready body the burden of bearing the knowledge of tawhid through obedience and worship. The human is the fruit of both — because the Trust required both.

The second image is the human as the *best of forms*:

> We have certainly created man in the best of forms.
> (Quran 95:4; English: Saheeh International.)

*The best of forms* — *aḥsani taqwīm*. The verse names the human as the *culmination* of the natural order's productive arc: the form than which there is none more proportioned, none more refined, none more apt to receive what the rational soul will bring. Al-Islah's claim about the human as the fruit of the natural world is the metaphysics of this verse, stated in philosophical vocabulary. The body that is *ready to receive the three souls* — the vegetative, the sensitive, the rational — is the form than which there is no further refinement in the elemental order. The verse is what al-Islah was reaching for. What al-Islah missed is the verse that comes after the second perfection is given.

The third image is the human as *honored above much of creation*:

> And We have certainly honored the children of Adam (aa-dam; the first human, peace be upon him) and carried them on the land and sea and provided for them of the good things and preferred them over much of what We have created, with [definite] preference.
> (Quran 17:70; English: Saheeh International.)

*Preferred them over much of what We have created.* The verse establishes the *rank* the disputants are arguing about: the human stands at a *preferred* place in the cosmos, not equal with what is below him in the chain. The preference is given; it is not an achievement of natural selection nor a metaphysical accident. The disputants — al-Islah and al-Nusra — are arguing over the metaphysical structure that grounds the preference. Al-Kirmani's adjudication is the answer the verse points toward: the preference is grounded in the human's standing at the *meeting of the worlds*, receiving from both.

Imam Ali ibn Abi Talib (a-lee ibn a-bee taa-lib; the Commander of the Believers, peace be upon him), in the line that the Ismaili tradition has long held as anthropological foundation, names the architecture from the side of self-knowledge:

> Do you reckon yourself a small thing, when in you the great world is folded?
> (Imam Ali ibn Abi Talib, in the manner of his sayings on self-knowledge; trans. condensed.)

*In you the great world is folded.* The Alid line is the bāb's whole anthropology compressed into one image. The human is *folded*: in him is what is above him, what is below him, what runs through the chain. To call him the fruit of one world only is to *unfold* him too quickly, leaving out what was already there. Al-Kirmani's claim that *the last of them was already in the first of them* is the same architecture, named from the side of origination rather than from the side of self-knowledge. *He who knows himself knows his Lord* — the line so often attributed in this tradition — sits at the same architectural seam: knowing the human is already knowing the whole chain, because the human is the chain's compressed last term.

## Fasl 1 — Al-Islah: the human as fruit of this whole world

The first *fasl* opens with al-Islah's statement of the anthropological claim from the side of the natural order:

> The human being is the fruit of this entire world, and it is for his sake that this whole world has been created — from its principles and foundations: first, the principles of the simple individuals brought into being from them by motion and stillness; then the individuals that are the heads of corporeal bodies; then the composites that are the generated things of this whole world; and the natures from which is constituted the human form, ready to receive the three souls: the vegetative, the sensitive, and the rational.

The claim is layered. Al-Islah names a sequence of stages in the natural order's productive arc, each rank building toward the human form as its culmination. *First*, the *simple individuals* — the elemental principles brought into being by motion and stillness from the more basic stratum below. *Then*, the *heads of corporeal bodies* — the celestial bodies, the heavens whose orbits set the rhythms of generation. *Then*, the *composites* — the generated things of this whole world, the metals and plants and animals that constitute the visible nature. *Then*, the *natures* (the four elemental mothers and their combinations) from which is constituted *the human form, ready to receive the three souls*.

The three souls al-Islah names are the Aristotelian-Avicennan triad as the Ismaili tradition received it. The *vegetative soul* (*al-nafs al-nabātiyya*) is the principle of nutrition, growth, and reproduction — the soul shared with the plant kingdom. The *sensitive soul* (*al-nafs al-ḥassāsa*) is the principle of sensation, locomotion, and appetite — the soul shared with the animal kingdom. The *rational soul* (*al-nafs al-nāṭiqa*) is the principle of intellection, deliberation, and knowledge of universals — the soul distinctive to the human. The natural order's productive arc, in al-Islah's reading, culminates in the body *prepared* to receive all three souls in their proper layering — the body, that is, in which the vegetative does not exhaust the sensitive, and the sensitive does not exhaust the rational, and the rational sits at the top of the human's interior architecture as the soul that opens the human onto the upper world.

*It is for his sake that this whole world has been created.* This is the strong form of al-Islah's claim. The natural order is *for the human*. The motions of the heavens, the elemental mothers, the long chemistry of generation and corruption, the layered souls — all of it serves the production of the human form ready to receive the rational soul. The cosmos has a *purpose*, and that purpose is the human at its terminus. The view is teleological in the sharpest sense: nature is not an accident; nature is a long preparation.

What al-Islah is *not* saying, but al-Nusra will hear him to say, is that nature is *all* there is. If the human is the fruit of *this whole world*, and the world is what we observe — heavens, elements, generated things — then the human is the fruit of the *observable cosmos*. The rational soul is what the natural body is *ready to receive*, but al-Islah has not named where the rational soul *comes from*. This is the gap al-Nusra will press.

## Fasl 2 — Al-Nusra's objection and al-Kirmani's joint synthesis

The second *fasl* opens with al-Nusra's sharp objection:

> To say that human beings are the fruit of this world is also the doctrine of the Dahriyya (dah-ree-yah; the eternalists), who do not affirm the soul's survival after its separation from bodies, because if the human being were the fruit of this world, this world would have held him and would not let him move on to another world.

*The doctrine of the Dahriyya.* Al-Nusra makes a polemical move: to call the human the fruit of *this world* is to be talking like the eternalists — those who deny the rational soul's survival after the body's separation. The argument runs by *reductio*: if X is the fruit of Y, then X belongs to Y; the fruit does not leave the tree to belong to another tree. So if the human is the fruit of *this* world, *this world holds him*, and there is no passage to another world after death. But the prophets came warning humanity to *provision themselves from the house of mortality for the house of survival*. Therefore the human cannot be the fruit of this world. He must be the fruit of *the higher world — the noble, illuminated world* — and the prophets' summons is the evidence: they would not call the human to provision himself for an abode other than this one if this one were where he belongs.

The objection has rhetorical force, but al-Kirmani is going to dismantle it by showing that the same form of argument can be turned around against al-Nusra's own answer. Before doing that, however, he begins by naming what is actually correct in *both* positions:

> Both speak truly in one aspect of what each has said, and both speak falsely in another. The aspect in which both speak truly is that each has said of the human being what is rightly his. The aspect in which both speak falsely is in denying what is true of him — that he is the fruit of all the worlds, because the human being is brought forth from between the two worlds.

*Each has said of the human being what is rightly his.* The disputants are not in pure error; each has named a *true predicate* of the human. Al-Islah is right that the human is the fruit of the natural world; al-Nusra is right that the human is the fruit of the higher world. Where each is wrong is in the *exclusion* — in saying *only* this, *only* that. The human is the fruit *of both*, because the human is constituted *from between the two worlds*. The dyadic structure that Bāb 3 named for the originative dyad (First and Second) is recapitulated here at the anthropological terminus: the human stands in a similar between-place, receiving from both directions.

Al-Kirmani spells out what each world gives:

> From this world he receives his first perfection, and from that world the second perfection: for the end of the natural world is to give to his existence its first perfection, which is the production of the physical form by the circular bodies, the luminous planets, and the causes that bring the form to the state of accepting what is proper to it — so that the human being has the capacity to receive, and is the fruit beyond which there is nearly nothing further. And the end of that [higher] world is to give what comes before the existence of the natural world: the second perfection, which is the perfecting of the spiritual form — the natural precursor — intrinsically, and abiding [through] the knowledge of the oneness of Allah Almighty by means of all the hudud (peace be upon them), and obedience to them in both acts of worship — in knowledge and in deed. This is its fruit, and it corroborates what we have said in that statement of Maulana Amir al-Mu'izz li Din Allah Ma'add ibn Isma'il (maw-laa-naa a-meer al-mu-izz li-deen Allaah ma-add ibn is-maa-eel; may Allah's prayers be upon him) in the book *Ta'wil al-Shari'a* (ta-weel ash-sha-ree-ah), where he says that the human being is the purpose of the Second [Emanation] and the end of the action of nature.

The doubled structure is precise. The *first perfection* is what the natural order gives: *the production of the physical form by the circular bodies, the luminous planets, and the causes that bring the form to the state of accepting what is proper to it*. This is the body prepared by the long arc of nature — the body in the *aḥsani taqwīm* (the best of forms) of Quran 95:4, the body of which al-Islah was rightly speaking. *The fruit beyond which there is nearly nothing further* — the *almost* is doing crucial work here: the natural order has done as much as nature can do; what remains is what the natural order *cannot* do alone.

The *second perfection* is what the higher world gives: *the perfecting of the spiritual form ... abiding [through] the knowledge of the oneness of Allah Almighty by means of all the hudud*. The *hudud* — the named teaching ranks of the Da'wa (daa-wah; the summons) — are the channel by which the higher world *reaches the prepared body* and gives it the spiritual form whose perfection is *the knowledge of tawhid*. The receiving is *by obedience to the hudud in both acts of worship — in knowledge and in deed*. The metaphysics of Bāb 4 returns at this anthropological terminus: the rational soul is constituted by what it receives from the *hudud* through obedience and worship. The architecture of teaching authority is the structure by which the second perfection arrives.

And al-Kirmani names the authority on whose teaching this whole architecture rests: *Maulana Amir al-Mu'izz li Din Allah Ma'add ibn Isma'il* — the Fatimid Imam from whom the Da'wa receives its teaching — in his book *Ta'wil al-Shari'a*, where he says that *the human being is the purpose of the Second [Emanation] and the end of the action of nature*. The Imam's statement is itself the doubled structure: *purpose of the Second [Emanation]* (the higher world's giving) *and the end of the action of nature* (the lower world's giving). The human stands at the seam where both teleologies terminate. Al-Kirmani is not innovating; he is *restoring* the Imam's teaching against al-Islah's truncation and al-Nusra's reversal.

## Fasl 3 — The argument turned around

The third *fasl* takes al-Nusra's own form of argument and turns it on him:

> As for the statement of the author of al-Nusra and his objection that if the human being were the fruit of this world, this world would have held him and would not have let him move from it to another world — by this he has merely shown his opponent how to turn the same argument against him. For [his opponent] could say: and if it were as you said, that the human being is the fruit of the higher world, then that world would hold him and would not let him return [to be] among these people — so that, by your own reasoning, what you wrote and asserted would be impossible.

The argument is precise. Al-Nusra's *reductio* was: *if the human were fruit of this world, this world would hold him*. The same logical form, applied to al-Nusra's own position, yields: *if the human were fruit of the higher world, that world would hold him* — and the soul could never have descended into the body in the first place, nor could the prophets have come and gone, nor could there be any return back into the natural order at all. The form of argument proves too much; it would forbid the very movements al-Nusra needs to explain. Therefore the *fruit holding* premise is wrong as stated. *Fruit of* does not mean *held by to the exclusion of any other rank*. The premise must be re-described, and once it is, the door is open to the doubled answer.

Al-Kirmani states the doubled answer in its strongest form:

> The human being is in truth a human being because he is constituted by the meeting of the overflow of the worlds: from the world of nature comes what flows in the stream of bodily existence, and from the world of the intellect comes what flows in the course of form — and this happens through intermediaries.

*The meeting of the overflow of the worlds.* The human is what the overflow from above and the overflow from below *jointly constitute* when they meet at the prepared form. *From the world of nature, the stream of bodily existence; from the world of the intellect, the course of form* — and the *form* here is the rational form, the spiritual form whose perfecting is knowledge of tawhid. And the meeting *happens through intermediaries* — the *hudud*. The human is not a chance product of two overflows colliding; the meeting is *mediated* by the named teaching ranks of the Da'wa, through whom the world-of-the-intellect's effects descend to find the prepared body. The architecture of Bāb 4 is again the bridge: what was named there as the *acquisition by obedience and worship from the hudud* is here named as the *intermediation* by which the two overflows meet to constitute the human.

## Fasl 4 — Emanation, prime matter, and the heaven-and-earth marriage

The fourth *fasl* opens with al-Islah's statement on the structure of emanation:

> The Soul emanates from the First Intellect in completeness; Nature emanates from the Soul in completeness; and form emanates from prime matter (hayula — ha-yoo-laa) in completeness — and all of these are complete.

The chain al-Islah proposes runs: *First Intellect → Soul → Nature → form-from-hayula*. Each step is *in completeness* — that is, each rank emanates the next without remainder, the lower receiving fully what the higher gives. The proposal is structurally elegant. It is also, in two of its joints, metaphysically wrong, and al-Nusra and al-Kirmani each address a different joint.

Al-Nusra's objection takes the first joint:

> If the matter is as he said, with the Soul as perfect as the Intellect, then what is it that is spread out upon the figures, the form, and the composites? This is invalid; it does not stand. It is permissible to suppose that these products come only from the pairing of two — one rich in perfection, the other deficient in need — such that a coupling takes place between them, as we observe in the visible world with the sky and the earth.

Al-Nusra's argument is that *production requires asymmetry*. If the Soul is *as perfect as* the Intellect, then there is no *gradient* between them, and nothing would flow. Production happens only at a *seam* between *what is rich in perfection* and *what is deficient in need*. He gives the visible analogy: heaven and earth. *The heaven is like the male, perfect and rich in all its visible cyclic motions; the earth is deficient, in the position of the female, and her need [for the heaven] is established. For this reason a marriage took place between them, and from them appeared the various kinds of metals, along with the various forms, plants, and animals* — none of which were *actually visible* in the heaven or the earth or in the elemental mothers; rather, they existed *in potency*, and when the heaven and earth *were wedded*, all of them appeared in act.

The analogy is suggestive. Generation in the visible cosmos has a dyadic structure: an *active* agent (the heaven, with its motions and influences) and a *receptive* substrate (the earth, with its elemental matter). The dyad's union produces what was potential into actuality. Al-Nusra wants to read the higher emanation as structurally similar: the *active* Intellect, the *receptive* Soul (or matter), the dyad producing the lower ranks.

Al-Kirmani's reply works the metaphysics on two fronts. First, he grants al-Islah's claim about Soul-from-Intellect:

> As for the statement of the author of al-Islah that the Soul is the second [emanation] emanating from the First Intellect in completeness, it is correct, in accord with what has been said above.

The first joint of al-Islah's chain stands. Soul does emanate from the First Intellect, and the previous bābs have established the architecture in which that emanation makes sense (Soul as the first emanation, rational souls as the second emanation, recursive structure).

But the next joint of al-Islah's chain fails:

> But as for his statement that prime matter (hayula) emanates from the Soul, we have already shown that its existence is not from the Soul — for the Soul is the Second [Emanation] from the First Intellect, and neither of the two is presented to the other as a basis. Neither of them exists abstracted from the other except in the mind as a supposition; outside the mind, they exist only together.

*Prime matter does not emanate from the Soul.* The earlier bābs have established this — Soul and Nature both emanate from the First Intellect, *each according to its own proper measure*, the Soul nobler, together constituting the First Emanation. Hayula is *the dark side* of that first emanation, not a product of the Soul's act. *Neither of them exists abstracted from the other except in the mind as a supposition; outside the mind, they exist only together.* The Soul-hayula pair is a *single first emanation* with two aspects; al-Islah has erred by treating them as cause and effect within the chain.

Then al-Kirmani addresses al-Nusra's heaven-and-earth analogy and shows that it refutes itself:

> As for the statement of the author of al-Nusra that one cannot suppose the products come only from the pairing of two — one rich in perfection, the other deficient in need — so that there may be a coupling between them, and his likening of this to the heaven and the earth: he himself refuted it by what followed in his own words, namely that there appeared from them the various kinds of metals, forms, plants, and animals, none of which were actually visible in the heaven, in the earth, or in the elemental mothers, because they existed in potency.

The refutation runs by al-Nusra's own admission. He said the products *existed in potency* in *both* the heaven and the earth and the elemental mothers. But if both terms — heaven and earth — *equally* held the products in potency, then *the asymmetry between perfect-male-heaven and deficient-female-earth does no metaphysical work*. *Neither of them shows any preference over the other in this regard*. The dyad does not, in fact, require one term to be complete and the other incomplete; the visible-world analogy fails as evidence for the upper metaphysics.

Al-Kirmani then gives the deeper correction:

> We have shown that deficiency is found in things not in their essences, but in their ranks when they are compared with one another, and in their actions, which do not occur except in something other than the thing that performs them. Each thing's existence is perfect in itself; for if its essence were not perfect in the existence proper to it, no act could proceed from it, either by itself or by another.

This is the metaphysical principle that the next several fusūl will build on. *Deficiency is not in essences but in ranks compared and in actions performed on something else.* Each rank, considered in its essence, is *perfect in the existence proper to it* — the essence cannot be deficient as essence, because if it were, no act could proceed from it at all. What is *less complete* about a lower rank is not its *essence* (which is perfect in its own measure) but its *position in the chain* and its *actions, which require something else to receive them*. The heaven is incomplete in its *action* — it acts only on what receives it (the earth), and the earth's *deficiency* is not deficiency of essence but deficiency of *acting position* (it is acted upon, not acting). *With its diminishing and its need, its deficiency is not in itself but in its action; the action is performed only by the one for whom it is sought.*

The correction matters for the bāb's anthropological claim. If *deficiency-in-essence* were a real metaphysical category, the human — composed of matter as well as form — would be metaphysically deficient as an essence. But the human's essence is *perfect in the existence proper to it*. What is *relative* in the human is his rank in the chain (between the natural and the higher) and the *direction* of his action (receiving the second perfection from above, ordering his deeds toward what is sought). The bāb is clearing the metaphysical ground that will let the human stand at the seam of the worlds *without being metaphysically deficient*.

## Fasl 5 — Al-Tamamiyyah belongs first to the Creator

The fifth *fasl* takes up a smaller question that nevertheless shapes the larger one. Al-Nusra had complained that al-Islah failed to address the question of *al-Tamamiyyah* — the name *perfection*, *completeness* — being applied to the Creator Almighty *so as to apply it instead to complete creativity*:

> He did not answer what was said at the end of this chapter concerning the name *al-Tamamiyyah* being applied to the Creator Almighty, so as to apply it instead to complete creativity.

The implicit charge is that al-Islah should have replaced *Creator-as-perfect* with *perfect-creativity* as the primary referent of the name. Al-Kirmani replies that al-Islah did not, in fact, make any such claim, and that the wise teaching cited from the *Book of al-Islah* points the opposite direction:

> The author of al-Islah did not mention any of that. The statement he quoted from the wise men in the *Book of al-Islah* — that the perfect creative is a complete creativity, and that what is perfect is perfect by virtue of nothing other than itself — is a saying than which none is more correct. It follows that what is most perfect and complete is the Creator, the Originator Almighty, and that the name *al-Tamamiyyah* belongs first to the Creator.

*What is perfect is perfect by virtue of nothing other than itself.* The chain of perfection cannot be infinite; what is perfect-by-another is, in the end, perfect-by-something-perfect-by-itself. That terminus is the Creator. *Al-Tamamiyyah* therefore belongs *first* to Him; the perfection of creativity is *derivative* — creativity is perfect because the Creator is perfect. The order al-Nusra wanted to reverse (perfect creativity as the primary referent) is the wrong order. The Creator is the *source* of the name *perfection*, not its derived predicate. *And the assertion of the author of al-Nusra on this point is a prejudice against the author of al-Islah.* The polemical accusation rebounds.

The fusl is short but architecturally important. It sets up the next *fasl*, where the Creator-and-creativity relation is corrected on its own terms.

## Fasl 6 — Creator and creativity: He is the cause and the effect

The sixth *fasl* takes up al-Nusra's positive claim about creativity:

> We all say that creativity is the effect of the Creator, and that He and the creativity are, from the side of the Creator, one thing — namely the perfect; and the creative through complete creativity is nothing other than the perfect. Nor do we need to say that the Creator is perfect, or that He is glorified and exalted; the Creator and the creative are one thing.

Al-Nusra's proposal: the Creator and His creativity are *one* from the side of the Creator. The *perfect-and-creative* are not two; they are one. Therefore (al-Nusra concludes) we do not *need* the further predicate *perfect* of the Creator: He is, and His creativity is, one thing.

Al-Kirmani's adjudication is precise:

> That is a correct statement, but we are not saying that the Creator is complete creativity and nothing but perfect, because creativity is perfection. What we are saying is that the creative who is the perfection is nothing but perfect, and that the Creator is the creativity, He is the cause and the effect, the perfect and the complete. The names are synonymous with respect to additions, because of the plurality of essences, so the meanings of these names apply to all of them. It is one thing only.

*He is the cause and the effect, the perfect and the complete.* The unity al-Nusra named is true, but his inference (that the predicate *perfect* is dispensable) is false. The names *Creator*, *creativity*, *perfect*, *complete* are *synonymous with respect to additions* — that is, considered as added predicates on the Creator's essence, the names are not picking out separable attributes; they are different facets of *one thing only*. The plurality of names corresponds to the plurality of *aspects under which essences encounter Him*, not to a plurality in Him.

The teaching has a structural similarity to the Bāb 4 teaching on the Intellect and the Word: *the Intellect is nothing but the Word itself, and the Word is none other than the Intellect itself*. There too the unity was named with multiple aspects-of-naming; there too the polemical move (treating the names as separable attributes that can be played against each other) was refused. At the originative apex, naming is *one thing under aspects*, not many things linked by relations. The anthropological consequence, when traced forward, will be that the human's *relation to the Creator* is not a chain of relata between distinct attributes but a *relation to one thing-named-many-ways*. The tawhid the human acquires through obedience to the *hudud* is the recognition of this oneness at the apex.

## Fasl 7 — The Soul's act, the human as fruit of all the worlds, and order from the Intellect

The seventh *fasl* gathers two related arguments. The first concerns the *Soul's act*; the second concerns *order* in the cosmos.

Al-Nusra opens with the claim about the Soul's seeking:

> If it is established that this world is the act of the Soul, and is nevertheless highly ordered and arranged, with perpetual circular motions, then we know with certainty that by acting she sought to reach the noblest rank before she actually began. For if this were not the case, there would not have been in the world any noble being capable of receiving the effects of the Soul from her. We also know with certainty that human beings are the fruit of the Soul's reaching her end; this is estimated by way of her cause — so understand this.

Al-Nusra's argument: the world is highly ordered (the regular circular motions of the heavens, the layered productive arc of generation), and orderly action presupposes a *seeking* of a goal not yet attained — otherwise the agent would not be motivated to act. Therefore the universal Soul, *in acting*, was *seeking* the noblest rank she had not yet reached. The human (the noblest being who receives the Soul's effects) is the *fruit of her reaching the goal she sought*.

The position has consequences al-Nusra has not noticed. If the Soul *sought* a rank she had not yet attained, then *before her acting* she lacked something — she was *imperfect* until the seeking-and-attaining was complete. This contaminates the upper rank with imperfection — exactly the move Bāb 4 named as *great unbelief — we seek refuge in God*. Al-Kirmani's reply is careful:

> We have already said earlier what implies that the universal Soul, in her action, did not seek to reach a rank she had not yet attained. The universal Soul — which is the second — rather pours forth upon this world, not because she is drawn toward it for something not yet hers (for God enriches her by what is with Him), nor by seeking something she has not been given. Rather, her action is to establish wisdom by bringing what is in potency into act, and to make that which is virtuous a mean, and her existence is not delayed by what is above her.

*For God enriches her by what is with Him.* The Soul's act is not the seeking of a rank she lacks; it is the *pouring forth* of what she has already been given. *Her existence is not delayed by what is above her* — she is at her rank already, not waiting to be filled. What she does is *bring potency into act* in the rank below her, not *complete herself* by acting. The asymmetry of the dyad — Soul as overflow, world as receiver — is not the asymmetry of seeking-and-attaining; it is the asymmetry of *generosity from a perfection that is already complete*.

And then al-Kirmani states the bāb's culminating anthropological claim:

> If one meditates on this, one sees that the benefit accrues to what proceeds out into action, and to the one who is under the burden of being content with perfection. There is no one of whom action is too costly, nor anyone in need. And whoever believes that in that great expanse there is fault or sin — if that were so, then human beings are of two sides: one part natural, one part divine, for the human being is not the fruit of this world alone, nor the fruit of that world alone, but the fruit of all the worlds. The human is the last in the universes, united in eternity with what has been acquired by time. Therefore it has been said that the last of them was already in the first of them.

*The fruit of all the worlds.* The doubled answer of Fasl 2 — fruit of *both* worlds — opens up into the wider claim. *All* the worlds — every rank in the chain from the First Intellect down through the universal Soul, the natural order, the elemental mothers, the body — converges in the human. The architecture is *cumulative*. The human is *the last in the universes* — the terminal rank of the chain in time — *united in eternity with what has been acquired by time*. The temporal arc that produces the human is gathered into the eternal architecture: the human's existence in time is the *acquisition* of what the chain holds in its eternal structure.

And the architectural axiom: *the last of them was already in the first of them*. The human's appearance at the end of the chain is not an addition that the chain did not previously contain; the human was *implicit in the first*, and the chain's unfolding is the *explicit reading-out* of what was enfolded at the origin. The Alid line *in you the great world is folded* receives, in this axiom, its metaphysical justification. The folded reading is not a metaphor; it is what the architecture actually is. The first contains the last because the chain is one architectural movement, and the last is the chain's culminating self-disclosure.

*Whoever knows the stations of the prophets, the executors, the imams, and the righteous — may God's blessings be upon them — knows this; for he is ready for salvation. May God gather us together with our pure loyalists in the Abode of Holiness.* The bāb's metaphysical claim is named simultaneously as a devotional disposition: knowing the architecture is knowing *the stations of the named figures* in the chain of teaching authority, and that knowledge is the readiness for salvation. The hudud return at the close of the *fasl* as the named figures whose stations the saved soul knows. *In the Abode of Holiness* — al-Quds (al-quds) — the prayer that gathers the believer's hope with the chain's eternal architecture.

The second movement of the *fasl* takes up al-Nusra's claim about *order*:

> As for the saying of the author of al-Nusra — that every agent who performs an act does so in seeking something he wishes to attain, especially if the act is from the agent in motion and in order, with affirmation — this is not a necessary condition for the First Creator, the Originator. For action there is in the way of metaphor, not in motion, nor in order. The First is the last; the beginning of Him is the middle of Him is the end of Him; there is no order in Him and no sequence. Rather, the systems and the orderings in what follows benefit from the lights of what is not in order or system — namely the First Intellect. The Soul too may resemble the Intellect closely: if you look at her, you perceive the powers of things that are not in order or sequence, but you perceive things in a single grasp. This belongs to her if she is united with the Intellect and pushes back the monstrous things of corporeal dark nature.

Al-Nusra's concession is generous on one point — *the First Creator is not in motion and not in order; action there is metaphorical*. *The First is the last; the beginning of Him is the middle of Him is the end of Him.* This is one of the strongest statements in the dispute of the apophatic structure that Bāb 2 named: the Creator stands *outside* the dyads of beginning/middle/end and motion/rest. *No order in Him and no sequence.* And — al-Nusra continues — *the systems and orderings in what follows benefit from the lights of what is not in order or system — namely the First Intellect*. Order in the cosmos *receives its lights* from what is *itself not ordered*. The Intellect, on this reading, is the *source of order without being itself ordered*. And the Soul, *if she is united with the Intellect*, resembles the Intellect: she perceives in *a single grasp*, not in sequence.

But al-Nusra adds a claim that al-Kirmani must contest: *the Soul too may resemble the Intellect closely ... if she is united with the Intellect and pushes back the monstrous things of corporeal dark nature*. The implication is that, on her own and apart from the Intellect, the Soul is *not* resembling-the-Intellect; her resemblance is *conditional* on her *uniting* with the Intellect. This leaves the Soul, taken in her own essence, at the rank of *not perceiving in a single grasp* — a rank dangerously close to the animal's.

Al-Kirmani's reply is sharp:

> How can what he said be correct — that arrangement and order exist only from the Intellect — when general things are made preferable only by the Intellect, and the Soul is not affected by the effects of the Intellect, with the result that she and the animals would be alike? On that view, God would have lifted knowledge from the Soul, so that the effects of the Intellect would not act upon her.

The *reductio* runs by the structural consequence. If *order exists only from the Intellect* and the Soul *is not affected by the Intellect's effects* (i.e., the Soul does not natively receive order from the Intellect, but only conditionally upon some additional *uniting* movement), then the Soul, in her own nature, would be at the rank of *not receiving order from the Intellect* — and would be, in that respect, *like the animals*. But the Soul has knowledge; the Soul does receive the Intellect's effects natively as part of what she is. To deny that is to *lift knowledge from the Soul*, which collapses the whole architecture.

Al-Kirmani gives the deeper correction:

> We said above that the issue does not lie in the agents: it is not the case that every agent seeks by his action to attain an end or a purpose that he estimates by saying. Rather, the First Intellect itself is not a vehicle for ordering and arrangement, for the Intellect perceives only the truth.

*The First Intellect itself is not a vehicle for ordering and arrangement.* The Intellect is not the *source of order in the sense of being itself ordered*; the Intellect is the source of order in the sense of *perceiving only the truth*. Order in the cosmos benefits from the Intellect not because the Intellect *imposes order from outside* but because the Intellect *sees truly*, and what is seen truly *is* the right ordering. This is the apophatic structure preserved: the Intellect is not the *author* of cosmic order in the way an agent is the author of an act; the Intellect is the *seeing* in which order is already what it is.

The closing of the *fasl*, in its source, trails off — the manuscript breaks before a full resolution. But the bāb's architecture has been laid: the Soul's act is not seeking; the human is the fruit of all the worlds; the last was already in the first; order in the cosmos benefits from the Intellect's truth-perceiving without the Intellect itself being ordered.

## What the bāb has taught

Bāb 5 has, in seven *fusūl*, refused two truncated anthropologies and established a doubled-and-then-fully-cumulative one.

*The human is not the fruit of this world alone.* Al-Islah's account, taken by itself, leaves out where the rational soul comes from and makes the prophets' summons to provision for the next abode metaphysically unintelligible. *The human is not the fruit of the higher world alone.* Al-Nusra's reversal, taken by itself, makes the human's bodily existence in the natural order inexplicable and runs into the same *reductio* he tried to deploy against al-Islah. *The human is the fruit of both worlds.* Receiving the *first perfection* (the physical form ready to receive the three souls) from the natural order's productive arc, and the *second perfection* (the perfected spiritual form, abiding through the knowledge of tawhid by means of the hudud) from the higher world.

*The human is the fruit of all the worlds.* The doubled answer opens up into the cumulative one: every rank in the chain — First Intellect, universal Soul, Nature, elemental mothers, body — converges in the human at the terminus. *The human is the last in the universes, united in eternity with what has been acquired by time.* The architectural axiom: *the last of them was already in the first of them.* The chain is one architectural movement, and the human is its culminating self-disclosure.

*The Soul does not act by seeking a rank she has not attained.* The Soul's act is the pouring forth of a perfection already given, not the self-completing movement of an imperfect agent. *God enriches her by what is with Him.* The architecture of overflow-from-perfection that Bāb 1 and Bāb 3 named is preserved at the level of the Soul's relation to the natural order.

*Deficiency is not in essences but in ranks compared and in actions performed on something else.* Each rank is *perfect in the existence proper to it*. What is *less complete* about a lower rank is its position in the chain and the direction of its action, not its essence as such. The human, composed of matter as well as form, is *not metaphysically deficient as an essence* — his rank in the chain is what places him at the seam of the worlds.

*Al-Tamamiyyah belongs first to the Creator.* The name *perfection* refers primarily to the Creator and derivatively to His creativity. The Creator is *the cause and the effect, the perfect and the complete* — names synonymous with respect to additions, picking out one thing under aspects. The tawhid that the human acquires through obedience to the *hudud* is the recognition of this oneness at the apex.

*The Intellect is not a vehicle for ordering; the Intellect perceives only the truth.* Order in the cosmos benefits from the Intellect's truth-seeing without the Intellect itself being subject to order. The apophatic structure of the originative apex is preserved through the question about cosmic order.

## A figure from the prayer-life of the family

The bāb's anthropology is named in many places in the prayer-life of the family of the Prophet ﷺ (sallallahu alayhi wa sallam; peace and blessings of Allah be upon him). Imam Zayn al-'Abidin (zayn al-aa-bi-deen; the fourth Imam, peace be upon him), in *al-Sahifa al-Sajjadiyya* (as-sa-hee-fah as-saj-jaa-dee-yah; the Psalms of the House of Muhammad), prays from the side of the human standing at the seam of the worlds. A line in the manner of those supplications can stand here as a close:

> O Allah, You shaped my body from the elemental mothers and made it the form than which there is nothing further in the order of nature. You prepared it to receive the three souls — the vegetative that nourishes it, the sensitive that opens it to the world, the rational that opens it to You. And then You sent down upon the prepared form, through Your hudud, the second perfection that the natural order could not give: the knowledge of Your oneness, abiding through obedience to Your awliya. Make me the fruit of all the worlds You have set in order — the last in the universes, the one in whom the chain reads itself out. Let me know, when I know myself, that I am what was already enfolded in the first. And gather me with Your pure loyalists in the Abode of Holiness.
> Source: paraphrased after the manner of the supplications in *al-Sahifa al-Sajjadiyya*.

*The fruit of all the worlds You have set in order.* The metaphysics al-Kirmani has been defending is named in the believer's prayer not as a speculative thesis but as a *vocation*. To be the fruit of all the worlds is to *carry the Trust* that the heavens and the earth declined, and the carrying is the receiving — from the natural order, the body that is the best of forms; from the higher order, the knowledge of tawhid through the hudud. The architecture is one architecture; the bāb's metaphysics and the believer's prayer are the same naming, from two sides.

## The bāb's place in the architecture

Bāb 5 closes here. The previous four bābs established the originative architecture; this bāb has located the human within it. The rational soul of Bāb 4 is now the *culminating recipient* of the chain's two overflows. The relation of the rational souls to the universal Soul (*second emanation*, Bāb 4) is now joined to the relation of the rational souls to the natural body (*first perfection of the form ready to receive the three souls*) to constitute the *one human being at the seam of the worlds*. The hudud — named in Bāb 4 as the channel of effects from above — are named in Bāb 5 as the *intermediaries* by which the second perfection arrives. The cosmos is for the human; the human is the fruit of the cosmos; and the chain holds because *the last of them was already in the first of them*.

The bābs ahead will continue building on this. Bāb 6 returns to the natural order's interior structure — motion, stillness, and form within the stratum below the human, asking how the first perfection is in fact produced by the rotations of the heavens and the layering of the elemental composites. Bāb 7 maps the *divisions of the world*, locating the human-as-microcosm within a cosmology of the four jewels. Bāb 8 turns to *qadr* (qadr; destiny), where the predicates of cause and effect are tested against the most painful theological questions — and where the human's standing at the seam of the worlds will determine how the question of his agency is asked. Bāb 9 takes up the law of Adam (aa-dam; peace be upon him) and the trusteeship of Nuh (nooh; Noah, peace be upon him), the chain of teaching authority laid out in its prophetic and imamic form. And Bāb 10 returns to the critique of al-Mahsul (al-mah-sool) on tawhid and the First Originator.

Each bāb tests the same architecture against a new question. *The human as fruit of all the worlds* was the fifth test. The architecture has held. The disputants who took only one horn of the doubled answer have been corrected; the doubled answer has opened up into the cumulative one; the architectural axiom *the last of them was already in the first of them* has been established. The discriminating reader, the one the bāb has been training all along, now knows where in the chain the human stands and what the chain is *for*. The cosmos is one architecture, and the human is its terminal self-disclosure — the one creature in whom what was implicit at the origin is *read out into act* through obedience and worship to the hudud, in knowledge and in deed.
