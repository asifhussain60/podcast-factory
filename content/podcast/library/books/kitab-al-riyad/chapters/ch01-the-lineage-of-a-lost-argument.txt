# The Lineage of a Lost Argument

*From* Kitab al-Riyad *(The Book of the Gardens) by the Ismaili da'i and philosopher Hamid al-Din Ahmad ibn Abdullah al-Kirmani, known by the honorific* Hujjat al-Iraqayn *— the Proof of the Two Iraqs. Edited by Aref Tamer, Dar ath-Thaqafa, Beirut, 1960. This chapter draws on Tamer's editorial introduction and al-Kirmani's own preface to frame the book itself: who wrote it, who he was writing against, and why this particular book survives when the three it answers do not.*

## A book that judges between two judgments of a third

The book you are about to spend the next several chapters inside is not a treatise. It is a verdict. *Kitab al-Riyad*, written sometime before the year 411 of the Hijra — around 1020 of the common era — is al-Kirmani sitting as judge in a dispute between two earlier philosophers, themselves arguing over a third philosopher's now-lost book. To explain what al-Kirmani is actually doing in any given chapter, the listener needs to know the chain of books that produced the dispute. The chain has four links, and three of those links are gone.

The chain runs like this. Sometime at the very start of the fourth Hijri century — roughly 920 of the common era — a Syrian Ismaili da'i named Muhammad ibn Ahmad al-Nasafi wrote a book called *al-Mahsul* — "the Yield". Al-Nasafi was a major figure; he was the man who converted the Samanid rulers of what is now Uzbekistan to the Ismaili branch of Islam, and he was put to death for his troubles in the year 331 of the Hijra. His book *al-Mahsul* set down a particular philosophical and theological account of the Ismaili faith. It was famous in his lifetime, and it was famous after his death. The followers of the Ismaili da'wa — the Ismaili mission to spread the faith — read it and used it.

A second Ismaili da'i, working in the northwest of Persia, took up *al-Mahsul* and decided that parts of it were mistaken. His name was Abu Hatim Ahmad ibn Hamdan al-Razi — Abu Hatim al-Razi for short. He was a contemporary of, but not the same person as, the famous physician Abu Bakr Muhammad ibn Zakariya al-Razi. The two of them met more than once and argued ferociously about whether prophecy is a real category, and that is a story for later. The Abu Hatim al-Razi who matters for this book wrote his own treatise, which he called *al-Islah* — "the Correction". *Al-Islah* was a critique of *al-Mahsul*. It said: my predecessor al-Nasafi got these things right, but he got these other things wrong, and here is how I would correct him.

A third Ismaili da'i, named Abu Ya'qub Ishaq al-Sijistani, read both *al-Mahsul* and *al-Islah*, and concluded that Abu Hatim al-Razi was the one who had it wrong. He wrote his own book to vindicate al-Nasafi against al-Razi. He called his book *al-Nusra* — "the Vindication". The title is a play on words: *al-Nusra* means "the help" or "the support", and al-Sijistani was lending his philosophical support to the author of *al-Mahsul*. In doing so he attacked al-Razi without restraint, which was his style.

Three books. Three philosophers. A fourth man steps in.

His name is Hamid al-Din Ahmad ibn Abdullah al-Kirmani, may Allah have mercy upon him. He was the greatest of the Ismaili philosophers of the Fatimid era, the one whose other masterwork, *Rahat al-Aql* — "the Repose of the Intellect" — is among the densest works of medieval Islamic philosophy in existence. He held the title *Hujjat al-Iraqayn*, the Proof of the Two Iraqs, meaning he was the senior da'i responsible for the Ismaili mission across what we would call today both Iraq and western Persia. Later in his career he was brought to Cairo, to the court of the Fatimid imam-caliph al-Hakim bi-Amr Allah, and was made president of the Dar al-Hikma — the House of Wisdom, which the editor of this book Aref Tamer fairly calls the first university ever instituted in the world.

Al-Kirmani read *al-Mahsul*, *al-Islah*, and *al-Nusra*. He decided that the dispute as al-Sijistani had left it was unresolved, that some of al-Sijistani's defenses of *al-Mahsul* were wrong, that some of al-Razi's corrections of *al-Mahsul* were right but incomplete, and that on the most important questions — the ones touching on the unity of God and on the structure of the cosmos — the matter could not be left where the two earlier disputants had left it. So he wrote a fourth book. This one, *Kitab al-Riyad*. It works through *al-Islah* and *al-Nusra* chapter by chapter, ten chapters in all, comparing what each says against what the other says against what the canon of the Ismaili Fatimid mission says, and rendering verdicts.

And here is the strange thing. *Al-Mahsul* is lost. *Al-Islah* is lost. *Al-Nusra* is lost. Only *al-Riyad* — the youngest book, the last one written, the adjudicator's verdict — survives.

Everything we know about what al-Nasafi argued, we know because al-Kirmani quoted him. Everything we know about how Abu Hatim al-Razi corrected him, we know because al-Kirmani quoted him. Everything we know about how al-Sijistani defended him, we know because al-Kirmani quoted him. Three of the four most important Ismaili philosophical books of the late ninth and early tenth century survive only as quotations inside the fourth. *Al-Riyad* is the only witness to its own argument.

## Why the youngest book outlived the older three

The simplest answer is the institutional one. By the time al-Kirmani was writing, the Fatimid state in Cairo was at its high point. The Fatimid imam-caliph al-Hakim bi-Amr Allah was on the throne. The Dar al-Hikma was open. The official apparatus of the Ismaili da'wa was strong enough, and centralized enough, that texts written under its patronage and approval were copied, preserved, and transmitted through stable institutional channels. Al-Kirmani's book was written within that system. The three earlier books were not. *Al-Mahsul* was written in a community on the eastern edge of the Islamic world that was outlawed by its own ruling power, and its author was executed. *Al-Islah* was written by a man who spent the last years of his life in hiding from enemies in Daylam. *Al-Nusra* was written in the lands of what is now Uzbekistan under the precaution of *taqiyya* — strategic concealment of religious identity to avoid persecution. Books written under those conditions are not preserved. Books written under the Fatimid imamate in Cairo were.

But there is a second answer that the first does not exhaust. Al-Kirmani's book was preserved because the Ismaili community wanted to preserve it. *Al-Riyad* is the only one of the four that anyone, in the centuries after the Fatimid state declined, considered worth carrying forward. We have al-Kirmani's quotations of the three earlier books because al-Kirmani's book itself was thought worth copying by hand, generation after generation, all the way down to two surviving manuscripts: one from India, dated 1354 of the Hijra, which is 1935 of the common era; and one from Tehran, dated 1355 of the Hijra. Both of these manuscripts are copies of older originals; both are 20th-century pages. The medieval transmission line is thin. Aref Tamer, the editor whose 1960 edition the present chapter is reading, says simply that the first manuscript he received was full of grammatical errors and gaps; only by comparing it against the second manuscript, sent to him on microfilm from Tehran by the great orientalist W. Ivanow, was he able to produce a reading that he himself trusted.

The book very nearly didn't survive at all.

> When I was assigned by the Ismailia Scientific Society in India, the great orientalist Professor W. Ivanow, secretary to it, to authenticate the book *al-Riyad* and supervise its edition, he sent me the first copy — symbolized by the letter (A) and adopted as the basis of the investigation. After perusing it and finding it full of grammatical errors as well as gaps and texts, this prompted me to write him apologizing that it is definitely not possible to verify the idea and reach the goal of producing the correct text if it is not met with at least one other copy. About a month passed, and then he sent me another copy filmed on microfilm from Tehran, symbolized by the letter (B). It benefited me greatly because it came correct and aimed, more correct than the first, although the first was the most beautiful in handwriting and coordination, as the photographs show.
> Source: Aref Tamer, editor's afterword to *Kitab al-Riyad*, Beirut 1960, p. 38.

If neither manuscript had been preserved, we would have no surviving witness, in the round, to any of the four books in this chain.

## The apophatic backbone

Before going further it is worth pausing on what kind of God this whole argument is about. Al-Kirmani's account of the divine is not the warm personal God of popular piety, although it is not incompatible with that God. It is the apophatic God of the philosophers — apophatic, from a Greek root meaning "to deny" or "to say-no-to", because in this tradition the way you get closer to God in your understanding is by denying what He is not. He is not a body. He is not in a body. He is not located in space. He has no contrary; no equal; no rival; no peer. He is not subject to any of the categories under which created things fall. He is not "an existent" alongside other existents; He is the cause by virtue of whose existing the existents exist. The mind reaching upward toward Him is confounded; the imagination is humiliated; the senses do not reach Him. Al-Kirmani writes, in his *Rahat al-Aql*:

> He is exalted above resembling any thing; He is transcendent above being compared to the limited. The minds are perplexed in praising the foremost-One, and the understandings fall short of grasping His mightiness. The eyes are blinded before glimpsing His greatness. He has nothing similar to Him from among the things; no equal; no rival; no like.
> Source: al-Kirmani, *Rahat al-Aql*, opening, paraphrased from the Tamer edition reading of the same lineage.

This is the doctrinal floor of the book you are about to read. Every philosophical argument in *al-Riyad* — about whether the Soul is complete, about whether prime matter resembles the Intellect, about whether the human is the fruit of the corporeal world or the spiritual world — proceeds within this prior commitment. God is not in the system. God is what allows the system to exist at all.

The Quran's own framing carries this weight. The opening verses of Surah al-Mulk:

> Blessed be He in whose hands is Dominion; and He over all things hath Power; — He who created Death and Life, that He may try which of you is best in deed: and He is the Exalted in Might, Oft-Forgiving.
> Source: Quran, Surah al-Mulk 67:1–2, English translation by Abdullah Yusuf Ali.

Two beats in this verse run through everything al-Kirmani will argue. First, the *creating death and life* — God's act is not the engineering of a stable order; it is the bringing-forth of the contradicting pair that makes time and choice possible. Second, *to try which of you is best in deed*: the human person is not addressed as a thinker but as a doer. Knowledge is the means; *amal*, deed, is the end. Al-Kirmani returns to this — knowledge that is not enacted is not knowledge in the sense the revelation cares about — repeatedly through *al-Riyad*, and even more centrally in the Ismaili doctrinal apparatus that *al-Riyad* defends.

## What kind of book this is

It will help to be precise. *Al-Riyad* is not a book of stories. It is not a book of prayers. It is not, for the most part, a book of laws. It is a book of arguments — philosophical arguments — about the structure of reality, the nature of the human soul, the order of the cosmos, and the relationship between revealed religion and the cosmos's structure.

Al-Kirmani is working inside a particular philosophical tradition. He is a Neoplatonist with an Aristotelian education and a deeply Ismaili theological commitment. The Neoplatonic schema he inherits from the late-antique Greek tradition runs like this. At the top of reality stands a First Originator — *al-mubdi'* in the Arabic — who is described by no attribute, contained by no category, perceived by no sense, similar to nothing. The First Originator brings forth, by an act called *al-ibda'* — origination, meaning bringing-forth out of nothing, by contrast with *al-khalq*, creation, which presupposes some pre-existing material — a single First Originated. That First Originated is called the First Intellect, *al-aql al-awwal*. From the First Intellect proceeds the second principle, called the Soul, *al-nafs* — by which is meant not your personal soul but the cosmic Soul, the principle that mediates between the world of pure intellect and the corporeal world. From the Soul proceeds the world of matter, the world of body, the world of nature, ending at the lowest principle, *al-hayula* — prime matter, which is the formless substrate of corporeal existence. The whole sequence cascades downward. The standing question for any Neoplatonist working in this tradition is: how exactly do these levels relate to each other? In what does the Soul resemble the Intellect, and in what does it differ? And how does the prophetic dispensation — the revelation, the *shari'a*, the structure of religion — map onto the cosmic structure?

These are the questions al-Mahsul tried to answer, that al-Islah tried to correct, that al-Nusra tried to vindicate, and that al-Riyad tries to settle. The verdicts are the substance of the next seven conversations. This opening chapter is about understanding that the verdicts have a history.

## The first scholarly dispute among the Ismailis

There is something Tamer surfaces in his introduction that is easy to miss in our own century, because we have grown so used to the caricature of medieval Islamic thought as monolithic, that it deserves naming directly. The dispute between al-Razi, al-Sijistani, and al-Kirmani is the first sustained intellectual dispute that the Ismailis had *with each other*, after they were done with the kalam debate they had with the other Islamic schools. They had argued the unity of God and the legitimacy of the imamate and the meaning of prophethood against the Mu'tazila and the Ash'aris and the various other theological camps of the third Hijri century. They had won those arguments to their own satisfaction. And then, instead of stopping, they kept arguing — among themselves. Tamer puts the point this way:

> What is striking is that we do not see this group concluding their kalam dispute with the other sects only to turn around and embark on a fresh argument of a different kind — an argument among their own da'is themselves. In this clear evidence of intellectual freedom that suffused their lives, and of the liberation from constraints that held conservative societies in check and ringed them with a refusal of irregularity, the Ismailis stood apart from the inherited classical schema.
> Source: Aref Tamer, editor's introduction to *Kitab al-Riyad*, Beirut 1960, p. 5.

This is not an incidental observation. The intellectual climate of the late third and early fourth Hijri century — roughly the 880s through the 940s of the common era — was the great open century of medieval Islamic thought. The translation movement out of Baghdad had brought Aristotle and Plato and Plotinus into Arabic. The Fatimid state was being built in North Africa. The philosophical tradition was alive. And inside that climate, the Ismaili intellectuals took the freedom to disagree with each other in public, in book form, and to keep the disagreement going across three generations of philosophers, until al-Kirmani picks it up and adjudicates it.

This same Tamer paragraph quietly anticipates the doctrinal claim Imam Ali ibn Abi Talib, peace be upon him, makes in *Nahj al-Balagha* and that the Imams after him insist on: that the worth of a person is what that person has the courage to master.

> The value of every man is what he masters well.
> Source: Imam Ali, peace be upon him, *Nahj al-Balagha*, Saying 81.

Imam Ali extends the same point in a longer aphorism that is the philosophical seed of much of the Ismaili intellectual project. Knowledge that the holder will not stand behind, and that the holder will not act upon, is — in his framing — a kind of debt.

> Knowledge is twinned with action. He who knows acts; and knowledge calls to action — if it answers, well; if not, knowledge departs from it.
> Source: Imam Ali, peace be upon him, *Nahj al-Balagha*, Saying 366.

The four Ismaili philosophers in this chain mastered their argument well enough to argue with one another for eighty years. They were not arguing for the sake of arguing. They were arguing because the doctrine they shared made acting on it conditional on understanding it correctly. Each one believed that getting the metaphysics wrong would put the practice of religion wrong, would put the structure of the imamate wrong, would put the soul's path back to God wrong. The high stakes are why the dispute went on so long.

## The men in the chain

The chapter would be incomplete without sketching the four. The portrayals here are deliberately brief; what matters for the listener is to know which face goes with which book.

**Al-Nasafi** — author of *al-Mahsul*. We know very little about him. He was a Syrian by origin who took up the Ismaili da'wa in the lands of what is now Uzbekistan — the region the medieval Arabic sources call "what is beyond the river", Transoxiana. He converted the Samanid ruler of Khurasan, Nasr ibn Ahmad al-Samani, to the Ismaili faith. The conversion was a major political event and it backfired on him: the conservative religious establishment of the region had him executed in the year 331 of the Hijra, which is 942 of the common era. *Al-Mahsul* was already in circulation by then. The book made his reputation; the conversion of the Samanid prince made his death.

There is an interesting wrinkle here that Tamer notes and that any careful reader of *al-Riyad* will eventually trip over. Al-Kirmani never actually names al-Nasafi as the author of *al-Mahsul*. He refers to the work itself often, sometimes as "the author of *al-Mahsul*" — *sahib al-mahsul* in the Arabic — but never attaches al-Nasafi's name to the citation. The attribution is inferred from al-Baghdadi, a Sunni heresiographer who lists al-Nasafi as the author in his own book *al-Farq bayna al-Firaq* — "the Difference Between the Sects". It is possible that the attribution is wrong; it is possible that al-Kirmani knew the attribution and declined to name it; it is possible that there is a reason for the silence we do not yet understand. The mystery remains.

**Abu Hatim al-Razi** — author of *al-Islah*. He is the most colorful of the four. He was the head da'i in Rayy, a major city in northwest Persia, and Baghdad was his secondary base. He had a gift, the sources say, for charming political leaders into the faith: the emir of Rayy, the local Daylamite commanders Asfar ibn Shiruwayh and Mardawij — all of these were brought into the Ismaili da'wa by Abu Hatim al-Razi's personal persuasion. He carried the title of senior representative of the Ismaili mission in the time of the Mahdi billah Ubaydullah, the founder of the Fatimid state in the Maghrib. He survived to roughly the year 322 of the Hijra. The man whose book he was correcting — al-Nasafi — would outlive him by nine years and meet a worse end.

Abu Hatim al-Razi is also the man who staged a debate, in Rayy, with the famous physician Abu Bakr Muhammad ibn Zakariya al-Razi — the medical Razi, whose pioneering work in clinical medicine is still admired today — about whether prophecy is a real category of revealed knowledge or whether philosophy alone can do the work of guiding the human person to truth. Abu Bakr al-Razi held the latter view; he denied that prophecy was a real category. Abu Hatim recorded the debate in his other book, *A'lam al-Nubuwwa* — "the Signs of Prophethood" — in which he never names his opponent directly, calling him only "the heretic", *al-mulhid*. By the testimony of al-Kirmani himself, in another of al-Kirmani's books called *al-Aqwal al-Dhahabiyya* — "the Golden Sayings" — Abu Hatim won the debate. He left Abu Bakr al-Razi unable to defend his position. He sealed every escape route, the report goes, for the obstinate one and the rejecter.

Abu Hatim al-Razi's other monumental work — outside the Ismaili argument entirely — is *al-Zina*, "the Adornment". It is a book on the Arabic alphabet, the original Arabic words, the borrowed words, the technical terminology by which the Quran spoke and through which the Muslims built the vocabulary of Islamic civilization. It runs to twelve hundred pages.

**Al-Sijistani** — author of *al-Nusra*. He was named for the province of Sijistan, in the south of what is now Afghanistan. He was a philosopher of major standing in his own right, contemporaneous with the great Fatimid imam-caliph al-Mu'izz li-Din Allah, and a teacher to al-Kirmani. He produced more than thirty books. The most ambitious, called *al-Yanabi'* — "the Springs" — was divided into forty chapters, each named for one of the forty ranks of the Ismaili Supreme Council; it was a deliberately late-career book aimed at the senior tier of the da'wa, the men who had reached the summit in their philosophical studies. He was eventually accused of unbelief by the conservative establishment in the lands where he worked, and was killed for his faith. His exact date of death is uncertain; the records do not survive. He wrote in *taqiyya* his whole life and lived in a country whose people followed a different doctrine. Like Abu Hatim and al-Nasafi, the obscurity of his end is consistent with the conditions of his life.

**Al-Kirmani** himself — author of *al-Riyad*. He was al-Sijistani's student, philosophically and in the da'wa. He moved through the senior ranks of the Ismaili mission in Persia and Iraq — hence *Hujjat al-Iraqayn*, the Proof of the Two Iraqs — and was brought to the Fatimid court in Cairo in the year 408 of the Hijra, at the request of al-Sadiq al-Ma'mun, the chief da'i of the Fatimid state under al-Hakim bi-Amr Allah. By that point the religious atmosphere in Cairo had grown hot. The doctrinal disputes had become heated, new religious movements had risen up at the edges of the Ismaili community — some of them, like the followers of al-Hasan al-Fargani, holding the divinity of the Fatimid imam-caliph al-Hakim, a claim that the official Ismaili da'wa categorically rejected. Al-Kirmani was brought in to be the philosopher who would put the doctrinal apparatus on a firm scientific foundation, who would refute the extremists and shore up the foundations against the new innovations. He wrote *al-Risala al-Wa'iza* — "the Admonishing Epistle" — refuting al-Fargani; he wrote *al-Risala al-Mudi'a* — "the Lightning Epistle"; he wrote *al-Masabih* — "the Lamps" — establishing the imamate against the doubters. And he was made president of the Dar al-Hikma, the House of Wisdom, the cultural institution that the editor Tamer is willing to call the first university in the world.

His other masterwork, *Rahat al-Aql* — "the Repose of the Intellect" — is the densest single book of Ismaili philosophy ever written. Al-Kirmani himself describes it as a city ringed by seven walls, each wall pierced by streets that branch into yet smaller streets. The reader who enters one wall finds himself in one of the seven domains of the philosophical system; the seven walls together constitute the whole. The book is not for beginners; the medieval reception of it was that it circulated only among the senior philosophers, the men trained in the highest ranks of the da'wa. The Yemeni Ismaili historian Idris Imad al-Din writes of al-Kirmani in his book *Uyun al-Akhbar* — "the Springs of Reports": *He is the foundation of the da'wa upon which it relied; with him the difficulties were settled and the perplexities cleared away*. The Syrian Ismaili da'i Nur al-Din Ahmad writes simply: *Had the Ismaili da'wa produced no one but al-Kirmani, that would have been pride enough and glory enough*.

In *al-Riyad*, the reader gets al-Kirmani in adjudicator mode rather than system-builder mode. He is showing his philosophical hand by judging others. It is a different kind of book.

There is one further witness we owe a moment to. The great Persian poet, traveller, philosopher, and Ismaili da'i Nasir-i Khusraw — who lived a generation after al-Kirmani and is himself one of the major figures of medieval Ismaili literature — names al-Sijistani's bibliography in his own book *Zad al-Musafirin*, "Provisions for Travellers". Without Nasir-i Khusraw's catalogue, we would not know that al-Sijistani wrote *Usus al-Da'wa* — "Foundations of the Mission" — or *Ta'wil al-Shara'i'* — "Interpretation of the Revealed Laws" — or *Sus al-Na'im*, "the Source of Bliss". Nasir-i Khusraw is the medieval witness who keeps the philosopher's name in the historical record. He, in turn, was a student of al-Mu'ayyad fi al-Din al-Shirazi — the Fatimid chief da'i a generation after al-Kirmani — who wrote with his own hand a short biography of al-Kirmani and a record of al-Kirmani's debates with the Syrian poet Abu al-Ala' al-Ma'arri. The chain of attestation runs: al-Kirmani argues with al-Sijistani; al-Mu'ayyad records the line down from al-Kirmani; Nasir-i Khusraw carries the line further. Without those two later witnesses, the obscurity Tamer notes around al-Sijistani's life would have been total.

This is the texture of medieval Islamic intellectual life that the popular caricature misses. The names that mattered were carried forward only by other names that mattered. The chain held because each link in it took the work of keeping it seriously.

## Al-Kirmani's stated method

In his preface, after the customary praise of God and the salutations on the Prophet ﷺ and on the imam al-Hakim bi-Amr Allah, al-Kirmani names what he is about to do, in his own voice. The translation here is from the Tamer edition, paraphrased into modern English:

> I had seen Abu Hatim al-Razi correct what he saw as errors in the book *al-Mahsul*. Then Abu Ya'qub al-Sijistani came and defended the author of *al-Mahsul*, asserting the soundness of his proofs and the strength of his arguments. What al-Sijistani discussed was not of the secondary matters or of the philosophical Ismaili branches such that we might tolerate paying no attention — and all this in the harming of the doctrine and the disabling of the foundations of the principles and of the primary tenets. I had therefore determined that I shall set forth herein the original views that came in *al-Islah*, correcting some of what came in *al-Mahsul*, and what came in *al-Nusra*, presenting my view and my comments upon them. My aim is to demonstrate the true sound foundations, and to separate between the two — and when we arrive at this limit, it shall be easy to make manifest the error, and to keep firm the right teachings, saved from every dispute, doubt, opposition, and discussion.
> Source: al-Kirmani, *Kitab al-Riyad*, preface, p. 49–50 of the Tamer edition.

Two things to register. First: al-Kirmani himself frames his book as a judgment between two earlier judgments. He is not pretending neutrality. He has a position. He is going to set forth what *al-Islah* said, then what *al-Nusra* said in reply, then *his view and his comments*. The structure is courtroom-shaped. Three parties present their cases; the judge renders verdict.

Second: he names *al-Riyad* — "the Gardens" — in the same paragraph and explains the choice. The book is named for the freshness of gardens to the eye and the fruit of pasture to the religion. The image is deliberate. The verdicts to come are not, in al-Kirmani's framing, hostile interventions; they are clearings of weeds and waterings of root. The garden image will return at the end of the book.

## What is at stake — why these men argued

There is one more piece the listener needs in hand before getting to the structure of the book. *Why* did this argument run for eighty years across three generations of philosophers? What did each of them think was on the line?

The simplest answer is that they thought the order of the cosmos and the order of religion were two readings of the same text. If the philosopher gets the cosmic schema right — what comes first, what comes from what, what stands as cause and what stands as effect — then the religious schema falls into place: the *natiq* — the speaker-prophet — is to the corporeal world what the cosmic Soul is to the spiritual realm; the *asas* — the Foundation, the first imam after a speaker-prophet — is to the natiq what prime matter is to the Soul; the cycles of revealed law are to the unfolding of human history what the cycles of the celestial spheres are to the unfolding of cosmic time. The two structures are not parallel by coincidence; they are one structure read in two registers. Getting the cosmic register wrong puts the religious register wrong. Putting the religious register wrong means the imam-of-the-time cannot do his work; the path to salvation is closed; the soul, denied right knowledge, cannot complete itself.

This is what the author of *al-Mahsul* was trying to set out. This is what Abu Hatim al-Razi was trying to correct. This is what al-Sijistani was trying to defend. This is what al-Kirmani is trying to settle. None of them is doing philosophy as a kind of high game. Each of them thinks that if he gets it wrong, real human souls — including his own and those of his disciples — will end up oriented to a false cosmos, and that the consequences for them, in the cosmic order, will be eternal.

It is hard to overstate how foreign this stake is to the modern reader of philosophy. The default modern frame is that philosophical disagreement is intellectual housekeeping; one writes a paper, one's opponent writes a paper, one teaches a graduate seminar, the dispute either resolves or fades. None of these men had that frame. Each of them wrote his book under the conviction that what he was writing was either an act of help — *nusra*, the word al-Sijistani used in his title — or an act of correction — *islah*, the word Abu Hatim al-Razi used in his — or an act of judgment — *al-hukm bayna al-sadayn*, "the judgment between the two masters", the subtitle of *al-Riyad*. They thought the stakes were ultimate, and they wrote books in which they expected one another to recognize the stakes.

The same Imam Ali whose value-of-a-man aphorism opened this section frames the underlying stake of philosophical knowledge in a sentence that the Ismaili tradition treats as definitive:

> A worshipper without knowledge is like a traveller without provisions, and knowledge without action is like a tree without fruit.
> Source: Imam Ali, peace be upon him, in the tradition of *Nahj al-Balagha* and the Ismaili commentaries upon it.

The traveller-without-provisions image is what each of these four philosophers thought he was working against. Travel here is the soul's journey back to God. Provisions are right knowledge of the cosmos and right enactment of the revealed law. A community of believers operating on a wrong cosmic schema is provisioning the traveller wrong. The provisioning is what the book is for.

## The shape of the verdict

For the remaining seven conversations in this series, the book proceeds through ten chapters which al-Kirmani calls *abwab* — singular *bab*, meaning "gate" or "door" — and a hundred and fifty-seven sections beneath them which he calls *fusul* — singular *fasl*, meaning "section" or "division". The ten chapters address, in order:

Chapter One — the Soul, conceived as the first emanation from the First Intellect. This is the longest and most disputed chapter, thirty-eight sections, and the dispute is over whether the Soul is to be called *tamma*, complete, perfected, or *naqisa*, deficient and in need of completion. The author of *al-Islah* says one thing; the author of *al-Nusra* says the opposite; al-Kirmani sides with *al-Islah*.

Chapter Two — the First Intellect itself, conceived as the First Originated, the first thing brought forth by the First Originator.

Chapter Three — whether the Soul and prime matter, both of which descend from the First Intellect, resemble the First in the same way or differently.

Chapter Four — whether human souls are "parts" of the cosmic Soul or only "traces" of it. A technical question with deep doctrinal consequences.

Chapter Five — whether the human being is the fruit of the corporeal world or of the spiritual world above it. *Al-Mahsul* said the corporeal; *al-Nusra* said the spiritual; al-Kirmani says both.

Chapter Six — motion, rest, prime matter, and form. Aristotelian categories adapted to the Ismaili schema.

Chapter Seven — the four-fold division of the world. The four substances, the four compounds, the four classes of humanity, the four faculties of the human soul.

Chapter Eight — decree and destiny: *qada' wa qadar* in the Arabic. This is the longest doctrinal chapter, twenty-four sections, working through the meanings of the two Quranic terms and how they map onto the cosmic order.

Chapter Nine — the shari'ah of Adam, peace be upon him, and the testament of Noah. Thirty-three sections, the heart of the Ismaili theory of prophetic cycles: the seven speaker-prophets — Adam, Noah, Abraham, Moses, Jesus, Muhammad, and the Qa'im — and how the revealed law unfolds across human history.

Chapter Ten — the closing turn. After spending nine chapters adjudicating between *al-Islah* and *al-Nusra*, al-Kirmani turns and lists sixteen places where Abu Hatim al-Razi himself failed: places where *al-Mahsul* contained errors that *al-Islah* should have corrected but did not. The verdict ends not with vindication for al-Razi but with the adjudicator stepping over al-Razi and the others both. It is the structural surprise of the book.

## The verdict's afterlife

A book that is the only surviving witness to four lost books has a peculiar afterlife. *Al-Riyad* is, on one level, an act of preservation: every time al-Kirmani quotes *sahib al-mahsul* — "the author of *al-Mahsul*" — or *sahib al-islah* — "the author of *al-Islah*" — or *sahib al-nusra* — "the author of *al-Nusra*" — he is preserving in his own pages a fragment of a book that would otherwise be entirely gone. But on another level the book is an act of selection: al-Kirmani chose which fragments of the lost books to preserve, in service of his own argument. We have what *al-Riyad* needed us to have. The rest is gone.

This is not unique to *al-Riyad*. Much of late antiquity comes down to us through this same channel: through the writings of those who argued with the works they were preserving. We have what Aristotle's commentators preserved of Aristotle's lost works. We have what the Church Fathers preserved of the Gnostic schools they refuted. We have what Maimonides preserved of the rabbinic disputes he resolved.

The first lesson of *al-Riyad*, before any of its specific philosophical verdicts are reached, is this. The history of ideas is not what was written; it is what was kept.

And the posture al-Kirmani takes toward the four philosophers in the chain is the posture the Ismaili tradition recommends in its accounts of God's relation to the servant who turns toward Him: the one who does not turn his face away from worship is one whose face is not turned away from in return. Al-Kirmani does not turn his face away from al-Nasafi, al-Razi, or al-Sijistani — even where he rules against them. He works through them. He preserves them by working through them.

The next seven chapters work through the verdicts. The verdicts themselves are dense. The cosmic philosophy, the doctrine of the Soul, the decree-and-destiny chapter, the prophetic cycles — none of these are easy. But they have a history now. They have men attached to them: al-Nasafi the Syrian convert-maker, al-Razi the Persian persuader, al-Sijistani the teacher-in-hiding, al-Kirmani the judge. They have books attached to them, even when the books no longer exist: *al-Mahsul*, *al-Islah*, *al-Nusra*. They have a question they were all working on: how the structure of the cosmos and the structure of religion match each other, and what the consequences are for the human being who is trying to know God.

That is the lineage of a lost argument. The argument itself, in al-Kirmani's own hand, begins with the dispute over whether the Soul is complete or deficient — Bab One of *al-Riyad*.
