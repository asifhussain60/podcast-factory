# Noah's Trust and the Great Resurrection

*From the Kitab al-Riyad (ki-taab ar-ri-yaad) of Hamid al-Din Ahmad al-Kirmani (haa-mid ad-deen ah-mad al-kir-maa-nee). This chapter covers the closing half of Bāb 9 (baab) — On the Law of Adam (aa-dam) and the Trusteeship of Nuh (nooh) — comprising the seventeen fusūl (fu-sool; sections) from Fasl 17 to Fasl 33. Episode 11 closed on al-Kirmani's six-gateway theory of Sharia's necessity (sha-ree-ah), the architecture under which Adam, being first, opened both the always-necessary and the cycle-variable prescriptions of religion, calling his community to the two acts of worship in knowledge and in deed. Episode 12 follows what al-Kirmani builds from that opening across five interlocking movements. First, *what Nuh adds to what Adam opened*: against al-Nusra's (an-nus-rah) claim that Nuh was *the first* to establish a Sharia, al-Kirmani replies that *the very fact of violation presupposes prescriptions that could be violated* — the Sharia is Adam's; Nuh's contribution is to abrogate and renew. Second, *the angels did not know what Adam taught them*: the bāb's deepest anthropological-cosmological reading of Quran 2:30–33, in which the angels' demurral (*we glorify You and sanctify You*) is read not as a sufficiency-claim but as a *cycle-claim* — *they were at the end of the role of ignorance*, and Allah's reply (*I know what you do not know*) names the architecture of the next cycle's beginning of knowledge. Third, *the architecture of the seven lines and the six widths*: al-Islah's (al-is-laah) geometric figure of seven lines and six widths mapped onto the seven nutaqā' (nu-ta-qaa; speaker-prophets) and their six Sharias — Adam, Nuh, Ibrahim, Musa, 'Isa, Muhammad ﷺ (peace and blessings of Allah be upon him), and the Qā'im (qaa-im) — with al-Nusra's counter-figure and al-Kirmani's anchoring correction. Fourth, *the Ark as new Sharia and as foundation*: al-Islah reads the Ark as the new Sharia in which the obedient are saved from the flood that drowned those who clung to the abrogated law (and who worshipped Wadd, Suwa' (su-waa), Yaghuth (ya-ghooth), Ya'uq (ya-ooq), and Nasr — the five idols of Quran 71:23); al-Nusra reads it as the new da'wa (daa-wah) and the appointment of the waliyy (wa-lee; guardian); al-Kirmani accepts both, on different registers. Fifth, *the Qā'im and the Great Resurrection*: against al-Nusra's claim that the Qā'im *raises the laws*, al-Kirmani refuses absolutely — *the Qā'im does not invalidate anything related to the worship of Allah; rather, he is the conclusion of the roles of the predecessors*. The cosmic imagery of Quran 81:1–3 and 82:1–2 (heaven split, stars scattered, mountains set in motion) does not name the abolition of the laws but *the passing away of the ranks* — the closing of the door of teaching. The episode closes on al-Kirmani's reading of Quran 11:42–43 — the son of Noah's refusal — as the doubled-register narrative the bāb has been building toward: a political adversary who *occupied a position parallel to that of the adversary in the cycle of our Prophet Muhammad ﷺ*. The *mountain that protects from water* names not a geological feature but *what is sustained by men and saints*, and the flood that destroyed those who took refuge in it *extended outwardly and inwardly*. The bāb closes on the architecture of *the pattern that runs through every prophetic cycle, all the way to the time of the seventh*.*

## What this half of the bāb adds

Episode 11 established that Adam, the first Speaker, opened the architecture of Sharia under which every human community must stand. The six gateways are *always necessary* because the human's three souls require them; the cycle-variable second tier (worship and *knowing the limits*) is what *Adam's sending* added.

The second half of the bāb takes the architecture *forward*. If Adam opened the chain, what do the Speakers who come after him *add*? What does the architecture of *seven Speakers* and *six Sharias* really name? What does it mean that the seventh — *al-qā'im* — closes the legislative cycle and opens what the Da'wa calls *the Great Resurrection*? And what happens, on the ground, when the architecture of *the wali appointed, the adversary refusing* is enacted not in the first cycle (Cain and Abel at Fasl 6) but in the second — when the *son of Noah* takes the role the *son of Adam* once took?

The bāb's seventeen closing fusūl unfold in five movements. The first (Fasl 17–18) traces what Nuh inherits from Adam. The second (Fasl 19–22) takes up al-Nusra's deepest objection — that the cosmos itself *suffices* to indicate tawhid (taw-heed; the unity of God), making the sending of messengers a *virtue God does not need*. The third (Fasl 23–30) walks through al-Nusra's remaining arguments. The fourth (Fasl 31–32) reads the Ark of Nuh in its doubled register. The fifth (Fasl 33) closes on the son of Noah as the *adversary-figure of the second cycle*, the political archetype the pattern of every cycle continues to enact, *all the way to the time of the seventh*.

## The Qur'anic horizon — the idols, the Ark, the son, and the Great Resurrection

The second half of the bāb lives inside seven Qur'anic loci, gathered across Surat Nuh, Surat Hud, Surat al-Anbiya', and the closing surahs of the cosmic Resurrection.

The first is the verse on the five idols Nuh's people worshipped:

> And they have said: Do not leave your gods, and do not leave Wadd or Suwa' or Yaghuth and Ya'uq and Nasr.
> (Quran 71:23; English: Saheeh International.)

*Wadd, Suwa', Yaghuth, Ya'uq, Nasr.* The five idols name, in al-Islah's reading at Fasl 31, *the practices of the abrogated Sharia* — what Adam's community had been taught to draw close to Allah through, but which had become, in the long descent of the cycle, *the worship of Wadd and Suwa' and Yaghuth and Ya'uq and Nasr*. The architecture of *what was once a way of drawing close becoming a way of drawing far* names the bāb's reading of Nuh's task: not to *invent* Sharia, but to *abrogate what had hardened into idolatry* and *renew the door of drawing close*. The verse Allah pairs it with seals the architecture:

> Because of their sins they were drowned, and were made to enter the Fire.
> (Quran 71:25; English: Saheeh International.)

*Drowned… and made to enter the Fire.* The flood is *outward* (the historical drowning of the unrepentant) and *inward* (the *Fire* into which they were *made to enter*). Al-Kirmani will return to this doubled register at Fasl 33: *the flood extended outwardly and inwardly*.

The second locus is the Ark itself, and the verses Surat Hud spends on Nuh's son:

> And it sailed with them through waves like mountains, and Noah called to his son who had separated himself: O my son, ride with us and be not with the disbelievers. He said: I will take refuge on a mountain to protect me from the water. Noah said: There is no protector today from the decree of Allah, except for whom He gives mercy. And the waves came between them, and he was among the drowned.
> (Quran 11:42–43; English: Saheeh International.)

*A mountain to protect me from the water.* The son's refusal is *the bāb's load-bearing political-architectural reading of the second cycle*. *The mountain* names not a geological feature but — in al-Kirmani's interpretation — *what is sustained by men and saints*: political backing, the support of chieftains. *The water* names the *force that divides Noah's people against the force that destroys them*. The son's appeal to *the mountain* is the appeal of *the adversary* to *worldly authority* against the *architecture of the wali* the Speaker has just instituted.

The third locus is the verse that closes the bāb in its cosmic register:

> As We began the first creation, We will repeat it. A promise binding upon Us. Indeed, We will do it.
> (Quran 21:104; English: Saheeh International.)

*As We began the first creation, We will repeat it.* The verse al-Nusra invokes at Fasl 29 reads in *one* register as a metaphysical promise of *cosmic repetition*; al-Kirmani reads it in *another* as *the return of the Qā'im* — the return *of knowledge to potency* after it had gone out into act through the chain of the Speakers, and the return *of the architecture of the call* to *what it was in the time of Adam*. The verse names not a cosmological cycle but *the architecture under which the seventh cycle closes what the first opened*.

The fourth, fifth, and sixth loci are the cosmic verses of the Resurrection:

> When the heaven splits open, and when the stars are scattered.
> (Quran 82:1–2; English: Saheeh International.)

> When the sun is wrapped up in darkness, and when the stars fall losing their luster, and when the mountains are set in motion.
> (Quran 81:1–3; English: Saheeh International.)

*The heaven splits, the stars are scattered, the mountains are set in motion.* On al-Kirmani's reading at Fasl 25, these verses do *not* name the abolition of the laws or the worship that holds the architecture. They name *the passing away of the ranks* — *the closing of the door of teaching* — under the architecture of *the Owner of the Great Resurrection*. The Qā'im's role is not legislative; it is *the conclusion of the roles of the predecessors*, *the completion of something that has been done in its place*. *Knowledge returns to potency* — not into nothingness, but into *the potency it occupied before Adam opened the act*. The architecture is *symmetric*: the first cycle opens *knowledge out of potency into act*; the seventh closes *act back into potency*.

The seventh locus is the verse that completes the architecture by anchoring it in *the angels and the names*:

> And He taught Adam the names — all of them. Then He showed them to the angels and said: Inform Me of the names of these, if you should be truthful. They said: Exalted are You; we have no knowledge except what You have taught us. Indeed, it is You who is the Knowing, the Wise. He said: O Adam, inform them of their names. And when he had informed them of their names, He said: Did I not tell you that I know the unseen of the heavens and the earth?
> (Quran 2:31–33; English: Saheeh International.)

*I know the unseen of the heavens and the earth.* The verse al-Kirmani reads across Fasl 20–21 names, in the Da'wa's reading, the architecture of *the cycle's turn*. The angels' *we have no knowledge except what You have taught us* is not a confession of *general* ignorance; it is the confession of *what had become hidden under the end of the cycle of ignorance*. The verse holds the bāb's anthropological-cosmological reading: *knowledge alternates between potency and act* across the cycles; Adam's sending is *the beginning of the next act*.

A line in the manner of the Commander of the Believers, Imam Ali ibn Abi Talib (a-lee ibn a-bee taa-lib; peace be upon him) — preserved in the Da'wa's tradition on the architecture of *what the Qā'im closes and what he opens* — names the bāb's compressed teaching from the side of the Imamic word:

> The first opened the road; the last gathers the travelers. Between the opening and the gathering, the road is one; the doors are seven; the gathered are those who walked.
> (After the manner of Imam Ali ibn Abi Talib's sayings on the cycles of the speaker-prophets; trans. condensed.)

*The road is one; the doors are seven; the gathered are those who walked.* The Alid line names what the bāb spends seventeen fusūl arguing under the architecture of *Adam's law and the Great Resurrection*.

## Fasl 17 — Why Nuh had to establish a Sharia, and what that means about Adam's

At Fasl 17 al-Nusra makes the move the bāb's second half will turn upon. *Nuh (peace be upon him) had to establish it — meaning the Sharia — because in the cycles before his turn, people had taken roles in violating the limits and pursuing contraries, such as friendliness (uns), prostration to al-Suwa' (as-su-waa), al-Jawooth (al-ja-wooth), and others. So it was incumbent upon Noah (peace be upon him) to establish it, that whoever perished might perish with full knowledge, and whoever lived might live with knowledge.*

The argument is subtle. Al-Nusra is *granting* that Nuh established a Sharia — and grounding the *necessity* of Nuh's Sharia in the *violation* that preceded him. *People had taken roles in violating the limits.* The phrase implies a *prior* set of limits — limits that *could be violated*. But al-Nusra reads this as *grounding* Nuh's Sharia *while denying* Adam's: the violations were *of customs and natural piety*, not of *legislated prescriptions*.

Al-Kirmani's reply at Fasl 17 cuts cleanly to the joint of al-Nusra's argument:

> What he says does not imply that Adam should have no Sharia. Rather, Adam too must have had duties and a Sharia, because it has been established that the roles in violating the limits and pursuing contraries — and such violation only befalls a follower's follower by abandoning the master's command and prescription — the violation itself was proven by saying that the prescriptions had become a Sharia, and by it the violation occurred against Adam (peace be upon him). So Sharia is established for him.

The architecture is precise. *Violation* presupposes *what is being violated*. *The roles in violating the limits* presuppose *the limits being there to be violated*. If, by Nuh's time, *people had taken roles in violating the limits*, then *the limits were there to be violated* — which means *Adam had legislated them*. Al-Nusra's grounding of Nuh's Sharia *requires* Adam's. The very fact that the second Speaker had to *renew and abrogate* presupposes the first Speaker's *establishment*.

The move is a classic dialectical reversal: *if your premise about Nuh is true, my conclusion about Adam follows*. Al-Nusra cannot ground Nuh's Sharia in the *violation* of pre-Nuh limits without conceding that Adam *legislated* those limits. The bāb's first major movement closes — and the architecture al-Kirmani has been building since Fasl 1 is *consolidated*: Adam's Sharia is *that against which* the cycles before Nuh fell into the worship of Suwa', Jawooth, and the rest.

## Fasl 18 — The tent from Paradise: Adam's qibla, Seth's house

At Fasl 18 al-Islah brings forward a tradition from the Da'wa's repertoire:

> It has been narrated that God Almighty ordered Adam (peace be upon him) to set up a tent that He brought down from Paradise, and made it his qibla (qib-lah) and the qibla of his sons. Then Seth built a place after him. This refers to Sharia, its wisdom, the gathering of revelation and interpretation in it, the establishment of its branches and roots, and the binding force of its obligations.

*The tent from Paradise, the qibla of Adam, the place Seth built.* The tradition names — in al-Islah's reading — *the architecture of legislated worship in the first cycle*. A *qibla* is *the direction of prayer*; that Adam *had a qibla* means Adam *had prescribed prayer*. That *Seth built a place* after him means *the architecture of legislated sacred space* was *transmitted across the first cycle*. The tradition is — in the Da'wa's idiom — *the architecture of Sharia, its wisdom, the gathering of revelation and interpretation in it, the establishment of its branches and roots, and the binding force of its obligations*.

Al-Kirmani's reply is briefer than al-Islah's argument: *The statement of the author of al-Islah proves that Adam had a Sharia and prescriptions like the others who have been mentioned.* The tradition does the work; al-Kirmani accepts it. The bāb's textual case for Adam's Sharia is, at Fasl 18, *complete*: the structural argument from being-a-Speaker (Fasl 1), the inner-and-outer reading of the sacrifice (Fasl 2, 6), the angels' prostration as transmitted Sharia (Fasl 9–10), the reductio on scriptural-omission (Fasl 13), the anthropological argument from the three souls (Fasl 14–15), the dialectical reversal of Nuh's grounding (Fasl 17), and now the traditional anchor of *the tent of Paradise and the qibla of Adam* (Fasl 18). Eight independent arguments, each closing on the same conclusion.

## Fasl 19 — Is the cosmos sufficient? The deepest objection

At Fasl 19 al-Nusra reaches for the bāb's most powerful counter-argument — one that, if accepted, would dissolve the *need* for any prophetic sending at all. The argument runs through cosmological-philosophical territory: *the structure and ordering of the world, in the matter of signifying the divine unity, is indispensable*; the cosmos itself *speaks* of God; the *acts and signs* of the world *signify shared meanings*; *parables represented by an existing parable* are everywhere visible. But — al-Nusra continues — *the very acts and signs of the world have led many moralists away from monotheism and into anthropomorphic simile, and this is utterly impossible*. The argument is double-edged: the cosmos *does* indicate tawhid, *and yet* the cosmic indications have led many astray into reading God's *acts* as if God were *like* the agents one sees in nature.

Al-Kirmani's reply begins by accepting the first edge of al-Nusra's argument: if the cosmos's elaborate order is itself an arbiter indicating monotheism, then the sending of messengers becomes *a virtue God does not need*. The irony is mild but pointed:

> If the world's elaborate order is itself an arbiter indicating monotheism, then the sending of the messengers to call to and signify monotheism — sent with the very evidence that others are already producing — would amount to nothing more than a virtue God does not need. I do not see how that goes for him: with the world's evident order so plainly arranged, is that not enough to indicate tawheed?

But al-Kirmani then completes the architecture al-Nusra had only half-built. The world's evident order is *not* sufficient. The architecture of *signs leading away from tawhid into simile* — al-Nusra's own admission — proves *the very need* for the messenger. The cosmos *is* an indication; but the indication *requires reading*. The reader is the prophet; the limits the prophet teaches are *what allow the cosmos to be read as indication at all*. The architecture is not (cosmos indicates → tawhid known) but (cosmos indicates → prophet teaches the reading → tawhid known). The middle term is the prophet's law.

And then the architectural completion: *the ones preceding in existence — the honorable jewels, namely the angels who are near to Him — are themselves in this position*. Even the angels need the architecture of works, signs, and education. If the angels need it, how could anyone reach tawhid without it? The argument from parity of need runs all the way up the chain. *Adam's sending* is not optional; it is *what the chain itself requires* at every level.

Al-Kirmani closes the Fasl with a striking textual-critical aside: *Perhaps this statement was inserted into the book by opponents so that it could be attributed to its author. And Allah knows best.* The aside names the bāb's hermeneutic discipline: *read what is given; suspect what is heterodox; consult the chain*.

## Fasl 20 — Work is not only for tawhid: soul-correction and the angels' demurral

At Fasl 20 al-Kirmani opens what is, in many ways, the bāb's deepest passage. The architecture turns on a reading of Quran 2:30–33 that the prior bābs have been preparing.

> The work is not only for monotheism, but also for the correction of the soul in worship, for emulating the highest assembly, and for acquiring — through exercise — honorable morals and noble habits by which they are prepared for joining what is above them.

*Not only for monotheism, but also for the correction of the soul.* The architecture of work is *doubled*: it *indicates* tawhid *and* it *corrects* the soul. The first is cognitive — the soul knows there is One. The second is transformative — the soul *becomes* what it could not have become without the work. *Were it not for the deeds and rites of the Ummah (um-mah), and the wholeness of souls within it through perseverance in them, souls would perish.*

This reading is *why* the angels' demurral in Quran 2:30 — *will You place in it one who corrupts it and sheds blood, while we glorify You with Your praise and sanctify You?* — was *not* a sufficiency-claim Allah accepted. Allah's reply was *I know what you do not know*. The angels were not wrong about themselves; they were wrong about *the cycle*. Al-Kirmani reads the cosmos as *alternating between potency and act* — illustrated through nature (heat and cold in the melting of bodies; snow; the dissolution of a mountain's parts) and then through *knowledge*:

> So too, science and ignorance in the world of the universe: corruption is sometimes ignorance, and knowledge is by potency. There was a time when ignorance had taken over and already reached its end, and knowledge was then introduced and reached its end in potency. The teachers were disturbed, the ceremonies of the laws were disrupted.

The angels who said *we glorify You and sanctify You* were *the bearers of knowledge at that time*. They reported that *what they were in was sufficient for them in the matter of worship and perfection*. And — al-Kirmani says — *it was*: but *the matter is different from what they estimated, since they needed to know the limits in totality, and the knowledge of those who come later in existence is like the second revival, along with working with what earns them this knowledge*.

The architecture is *not* that the angels were *foolish* or *prideful*. They were *at the end of the cycle of ignorance and at the threshold of the cycle of knowledge*, and Allah's *I know what you do not know* names the *next* cycle's opening. *So Allah sent Adam (peace be upon him), making him His successor to manifest knowledge, to gain them integrity in act, to show them how to know the boundaries.* Adam's sending is *not* a response to original sin or the angels' arrogance; it is *the architecture under which the cosmic cycle of knowledge-and-ignorance turns over*. Adam is *the door* of the act that follows the potency the prior cycle concluded in.

## Fasl 21 — Were the people ignorant of tawhid?

At Fasl 21 al-Nusra brings what at first looks like a clever objection. *Creation in the great role* — al-Nusra asks — *did not know tawhid? Or was there a similar apparent law among them?* The argument is that *if* the prior cycle's people *did* know tawhid (without a Speaker among them), then Adam's call to *teach them tawhid* is *redundant* — *a virtue God does not need*. *And if creation was not ignorant of monotheism, nor of the role of the Qā'im — and they also did not know him, since the laws had been raised — then it would have been permissible for Adam to call people to monotheism without any references, and the like of this by the same saying.*

Al-Kirmani's reply at Fasl 21 deepens the Fasl 20 reading. The reductio is forceful: al-Nusra's argument *requires* that Adam's call be either *redundant* (and so not needed) or *needed* (and so the people did not know tawhid without Adam's teaching). The first horn is impossible (no prophet is sent for *no reason*); the second is al-Kirmani's reading. *The people did not need from him what they already had for themselves; and when Allah sent Adam, He would not have made him His successor among them, nor chosen him from among them, nor made him His caliph.* The very fact of Adam's sending is evidence that *what Adam brought was needed*.

The angels' estimation was *like al-Nusra's argument*: *we already know; no Adam is needed*. Allah's reply was *I know what you do not know*. The verse on the teaching of the names — *And Adam knew all the names. Then He showed them to the angels and said: Tell Me the names of these, if you are truthful. They said: Glory be to You! We have no knowledge except what You have taught us* (Quran 2:31–32) — names exactly this architecture. The teaching of the names is *not* a magic trick by which Adam shows up the angels; it is *the inauguration of the cycle of knowledge* under which the angels themselves are *now* the taught, not the teaching. *The people were of two worlds; because they were so, Adam came to them as their teacher.*

## Fasl 22 — The Qā'im's cycle and the closing of education

At Fasl 22 al-Kirmani turns the architecture's symmetry. If Adam's sending was the *opening* of the cycle of knowledge, what is the *closing*? Al-Nusra had said that *in the cycle of the Qā'im, the laws are raised* — and that this *also* means *creation is not ignorant of monotheism* in the Qā'im's time. Al-Kirmani disambiguates the claim with the precision of a cycle-reader.

*If what he means is the image of the da'wa*, al-Kirmani writes, *that before the role of the Qā'im appears, and before it exists from the natural world — when the da'wa is preserved by his successors and preachers after the completion of the role of Muhammad al-Mustafa (mu-ham-mad al-mus-ta-faa) and his followers, until the Qā'im's time and the appearance of His signs in the world — then it is true.* The *period of concealment* between Muhammad ﷺ and the Qā'im is *not* a period of *ignorance*; it is a period in which *knowledge has raised the beacon of its appearance through the many scholars*. The architecture al-Kirmani names by a Qur'anic phrase: *the day when the children will grow old* — the architecture under which *what is sustained by the line of teachers* continues to teach.

But — al-Kirmani continues — *if what he meant by his saying is that he is in the role of the guardian — no, they are ignorant after the Qā'im*. *The Qā'im is the owner of the seventh stage, who dismisses speech, distinguishes the people of Paradise from the people of Hell, and closes the door of education.* The architecture inverts at the Qā'im: until him, *the door of education is open*; after him, *the door of education is closed*. *Repentance is not valid then, because they do not remain knowledgeable, and they are not of the people of knowledge.* The architecture is *symmetric to Fasl 20*: as the angels at the *end of the cycle of ignorance* said *we glorify You* and Allah *opened the next cycle*, so the Qā'im *closes the cycle of education* into *the next mode of being* — the mode the cosmic verses of Quran 81 and 82 name.

*That time is the time when the waters overflow and cover the earth, the time when misery and austerity are seized and made manifest.* Al-Kirmani's tone — *the words of the author of al-Nusra on this matter are not those of a true investigator* — names the bāb's discrimination: *the architecture of cycles is precise*; reading *the laws are raised* as *the cycle of the guardian is also ignorant* is *not the reading of a true investigator*.

## Fasl 23 — Encouragement, intimidation, both: still Sharia

At Fasl 23 al-Kirmani gathers al-Nusra's arguments into a single dialectical sweep. *In summary, the author of al-Nusra mentioned only these sayings and quotes in support of the owner of the crop in his discharge of the law of Adam.* The summary: Adam *must* have called his people *to* something. The call must have been either *to encourage them toward Paradise*, *to intimidate them away from Hell*, *or both*.

The architectural payoff is precise. *If He calls them to encouragement, the encouragement is not perceptible except by striking proverbs, and by reports about Paradise with what is known by the senses of the kinds of pleasures, blessings, and good places — such as dwellings, orchards, rivers, and trees — so that they can conceive of it. That is a form of speech that requires interpretation.* The architecture of *encouragement* requires *parables* — and *parables require interpretation*, which means *the architecture of ta'wīl (ta-weel)*, which means *the architecture of an outward and an inward*, which means *Sharia*.

The same architecture holds for *intimidation*: *if He called them to intimidation, His saying is only by giving them proverbs that frighten them — moving from this to the world of the senses, so that they may visualize it; and in the interpretation of it there is a stream other than the above*. Intimidation also requires parables, also requires interpretation, also requires Sharia.

The *third* option — *if He calls them to both* — *His words are not without a valid outward appearance, and yet interpretation is necessary*. All three options lead to the same conclusion: *what is positive for the phenomenon is fixed for Adam, followed by interpretation — so Adam is the owner of a law and a clear custom*.

The argument is structural: any call requires a medium; any medium that bears wisdom requires interpretation; any community to which interpretation is given requires the architecture of *outer and inner* — which is *Sharia*. There is no third path. Even *the lightest call* — *only encouragement* — requires the full architecture. Adam's Sharia is *not* a *richest* option; it is *the minimum architecture of any call at all*.

## Fasl 24 — With wisdom or without: still Sharia

At Fasl 24 al-Kirmani completes the dialectical sweep with a complementary structural argument. *Adam (peace be upon him) is not without one of two cases: either he called the people of his time to obey God by obeying Him with wisdom, or he called them without wisdom; and the supposition that he did not call them is impossible, as is the belief that he was outside of religion.* If he called *without wisdom*, he is *not* a prophet at all — and the disputants do not deny that Adam was a prophet. If he called *with wisdom*, then *wisdom requires knowledge and all of the work — so Adam is the owner of a law that combines knowledge and action*. *They are the worship of Allah, and by them one attains perfection and ascent to the houses of dignity.*

The architecture closes a second dialectical loop. Fasl 23 closed the loop from the side of the *medium* (parables → ta'wīl → Sharia); Fasl 24 closes it from the side of *wisdom* (wisdom → knowledge + work → the two acts of worship → Sharia). Either entrance to the architecture leads to the same conclusion.

## Fasl 25 — The Qā'im does not raise the worship

At Fasl 25 al-Kirmani delivers the bāb's most direct teaching on the architecture of *the Great Resurrection*. Al-Nusra had said that the Qā'im *raises the laws* — and had read this as *invalidating worship in the natural world*. Al-Kirmani refuses absolutely:

> Believing that is unsound. For the estimation of its existence in the natural world does not arise from the second way of revival, because it does not invalidate anything related to the worship of Allah, the Almighty. Rather, it is the conclusion of the roles of the predecessors, the completion of something that has been done in its place; it removes the boundaries the new creation.

*The Qā'im does not invalidate worship; he completes the roles.* The architecture of *what is completed* and *what is invalidated* is *exactly* what the bāb has been preparing. Worship is not *invalidated* in the natural world; it is *completed* when the architecture under which it was given has *finished its course*. The architecture is *fulfillment*, not *abolition*.

Al-Kirmani anchors the reading in three Qur'anic clusters. First, Quran 56:62: *Are they then in doubt about a new creation?* — the verse names *the architecture of the resurrection* as *a new creation*, not *an erasure of the old*. Second, the cosmic verses:

> When the heaven is split apart and when the planets are scattered.
> (Quran 82:1–2; English: Saheeh International.)

> When the sun is wrapped up in darkness, when the stars fall losing their luster, when the mountains are set in motion.
> (Quran 81:1–3; English: Saheeh International.)

*The heaven split, the stars scattered, the mountains set in motion.* These verses name, on al-Kirmani's reading, *not* the abolition of the laws, *but the passing away of the ranks* — *the ranks of those whose roles have run out*. *All these similitudes are folded upon the limits, and the announcements concern their passing away — that is, their ranks — not the laws or the works whose time has elapsed.* The architecture is *symmetric* to the alternation of *potency and act* Fasl 20 named: *the laws then survive with no teachers, except for what is necessary to its existence as we have mentioned*. The laws *do not vanish*; the *teachers* of the laws *vanish*. The architecture of *the door of teaching being closed* is the architecture of *the Great Resurrection* — *the time at which knowledge returns to potency and is concealed, and ignorance begins to spread*.

The closing line is the bāb's compressed teaching from the side of *the Da'wa's communion*: *Allah suffices us, and the community of believers, those people of divine goodness, by following His guardians. May we be gathered with Muhammad and Ali and the pure Imams (peace and blessings of Allah be upon them), the lights of His purity, His mercy, and His grace.* The architecture of *the Great Resurrection* is *not* a metaphysical abstraction; it is *the architecture under which the community of the Da'wa walks the road toward gathering*.

## Fasl 26 — The intellects in the body, not the higher limits

At Fasl 26 al-Nusra brings what looks like a devastating objection. *Anyone who claims that monotheism is realized only through signs and acts has lost his way in judging that the First Intellect, the Soul, the Angels, the Jadd (al-jadd), the Fath (fath), and the Khayal (kha-yaal) did not know Tawhid — since there were neither signs nor acts between them or among them.* The argument is from *parity*: if *signs and acts* are *necessary* for knowing tawhid (as al-Kirmani has argued through Fasl 19–22), then *the higher limits* — which have *no signs and acts* — would *not know* tawhid; but they obviously *do* know tawhid; therefore *signs and acts are not necessary*.

Al-Kirmani's reply at Fasl 26 is precise. *Whoever says what he criticizes does not mean the First Intellect, nor the higher limits; rather, he means the intellects in the world of body, which is the second emanation, since their nile — that is, their attainment of perfection — is by way of teaching, assignment, knowledge, signs, and works, by acquisition.* The architecture, established in the prior bābs (especially Bāb 1 and Bāb 3), distinguishes *the abode of eternity* (First Intellect, Soul, the higher limits) from *the abode of nature* (the intellects in the world of body, the second emanation). The first are *perfected from the beginning of their existence in origination and emanation*; the second are *perfected by acquisition*. The architecture of *signs and acts* applies to the *second*, not the *first*. *It is therefore not permissible to apply the ruling of this kind of statement to that domain.*

The architecture is consistent with everything Bābs 1–7 established. *Teaching, assignment, knowledge, signs, and works* is the architecture of the human and the celestial-sphere angels; it is *not* the architecture of the First Intellect and the higher limits. Al-Nusra's objection is built on a category mistake — applying *the law of the perfected-by-acquisition* to *the perfected-by-origination*.

Al-Kirmani closes Fasl 26 with a textual aside on the bāb's hermeneutic posture: al-Nusra's questions about the seven planets, the six days of creation, and the meanings of determination had *already been answered* in al-Islah's own treatment. The point completes the bāb's gathering of al-Nusra's many arguments into a single dialectical sweep.

## Fasl 27 — The seven lines and the six widths

At Fasl 27 al-Islah opens the bāb's explicitly geometrical movement. The figure is *seven lines* with *six widths* (arda, ar-da; presentation) between them: the lines stand for the seven Speakers; the widths stand for the six Sharias. *The first line is not made obligatory until the second line appears, because the line indicates the speaker and the arda indicates the Sharia, and a thing's image is not made manifest except after its completion.*

Each Speaker's Sharia is completed by the appearance of the next; the chain is cumulative. *There is no law before the first pronunciation and no law at the appearance of the seventh — instead there are six laws of the pronunciations, the seventh of which is the Qā'im.* The Qā'im, at the seventh line, has no Sharia of his own; the architecture closes on the six Sharias of the six prior Speakers. The chain is seven; the laws are six; the seventh closes. Al-Islah immediately closes an alternative reading (in which Adam *also* has no law, yielding only five Sharias between seven lines): *the law was thus made obligatory for Adam* (because the chain requires Adam's law to *begin* the cumulative architecture).

## Fasl 28 — Al-Nusra's six presentations on the Imams

At Fasl 28 al-Nusra brings a counter-geometry. *In the matter of the six widths between the seven lines, and the inference of the lines as standing for the Prophets and of the presentations for the six laws, the matter is not as he says. Rather, the seven lines correspond to the higher letters, since each Prophet wrote a single letter from among them; and the six presentations correspond to the cycles of the Imams between Adam and his Successor, and to the seven Imams; and between Noah and Abraham in the second cycle, and between Abraham and Moses in the third cycle, and likewise down to Muhammad ﷺ.*

*Adam — Noah — Abraham — Moses — Jesus — Muhammad — al-Qā'im.* The architecture al-Nusra opens reads the *six presentations* not as *six Sharias* but as *six cycles of the Imams between the Speakers*. The Speakers are *the lines*; the Imams (i-maams) *between* the Speakers are *the presentations*. On al-Nusra's reading, *Adam was preceded by no Imams; therefore Adam was not called to establish a Sharia*. The Speaker *requires Imams before him* to be *called to legislation*. Nuh had Adam's Successor before him (Seth and the Imamic chain that followed); therefore Nuh was *called* to legislate. Adam, having *no* Imams before him, was not.

Al-Kirmani's reply at Fasl 28 is the bāb's most surgical geometrical correction. *It is more fitting that the placements be upon the pronouncements and not upon the letters of the higher world, and that the presentations stand upon the duration of their authority in the cycle, not upon the Successors.* The lines stand for the Speakers; the presentations stand for the *duration of each Speaker's authority*, not for the Imamic intervals.

But — and this is the bāb's signature move — *then his account does not require that Adam have no law. On the contrary, the Sharia is made obligatory for him by the presence of the Imams after him. The presence of the Imams entails the obligation of preserving the place of the Speaker, his Sharia, and his outlines in his community.* The architecture is reversed: the *Imams after Adam* (not the Imams before him) ground the Sharia. *Without a Sharia, there is no Imamic role.* Al-Nusra's argument requires Adam's Imams; Adam's Imams require Adam's Sharia.

Al-Kirmani closes by distinguishing *the great cycle and the small cycle*: the great cycles run across the seven Speakers; the small cycles run across the seven Imams within each Speaker's cycle. The seven-and-six pattern holds *both* at the macro and the meso level.

## Fasl 29 — *As you began, so shall you return*

At Fasl 29 al-Nusra reaches for a Qur'anic verse to anchor his reading. *The words of Allah Almighty — As you began, so shall you return — mean as you were at the beginning of the matter, which was not in terms of facts, so it is the last of the matter.* The verse — Quran 21:104, *as We began the first creation, We will repeat it* — is, on al-Nusra's reading, a metaphysical promise of *cosmic repetition*: the end of the matter is *as* the beginning was.

Al-Kirmani's reply at Fasl 29 is brief but transformative. *Even if this is one interpretation, the proper face is to say what corresponds to the saying of Allah Almighty: namely that this is a reference to the return of the Qā'im — the return of knowledge after it had gone out into action, its abundance into potency, its scarcity, and its predetermined apportionment over all the people after him, just as they were in the time of Adam; and the return of ignorance after it had been in potency, and its reduction into action, and its multiplication on account of its appointed wisdom.*

*The return of knowledge to potency, just as they were in the time of Adam.* The architecture is the symmetry the bāb has been building. At Adam's opening, *knowledge came out of potency into act*; at the Qā'im's closing, *knowledge returns from act into potency*. *Ignorance*, mirror-image, *was in potency before Adam* and *returns from action into potency after the Qā'im*. The architecture is *cyclic-symmetric*; the verse names *exactly* this symmetry. *Blessed is Allah, the best of creators.*

## Fasl 30 — Adam's name has three letters

At Fasl 30 al-Nusra brings what is, at the level of dialectical seriousness, the bāb's most curious objection. *If the author of al-Islah had looked into what is contained in his name — I mean Adam — by way of indication, he would have seen that Adam was not the owner of a Sharia. For his name, which consists of three letters — Alif, Dal, and Mim — does not yield a compound or a composite; each letter is indicated by another. This is a clear sign that he was not the author of a Sharia. The five Prophets who came after him, however, were the owners of names of composite form, like Noah, Abraham, Moses, Jesus, and Muhammad (peace be upon them) — a proof that each of them was the owner of a Sharia.*

The argument is *letter-by-letter mysticism*. Adam's name is three single letters (each a *separate* letter, *not* compounded into roots); the five later Prophets' names are *compounded* (more than three letters, with grammatical-morphological roots). *Separateness* indicates *no Sharia*; *composition* indicates *Sharia*.

Al-Kirmani's reply is *elegantly disabling*. *If it were the case that the separateness of the letters of Adam's name necessitated that Adam have no Sharia, then the fact that these letters are not separate in themselves would oblige him to have a Sharia.* The argument turns on the *letters' own composition*: *the alif of the name is composed of alif, lam, and fa; the dal is composed of dal, alif, and lam; the mim is composed of mim, ya, and mim — and likewise for what is signified*. Each *single* letter is *itself* a *composite* when its name is *fully spelled*. *If separateness did not entail that he have a Sharia, separateness would have obliged a Sharia; provided that inference of this kind is sound.*

The deeper move is the reductio. *But it is not sound, because if it were sound, it would entail that the one charged with the authorship of the Qur'an — namely the Prophet ﷺ, whose name is composed of four letters — would be impossible to be the owner of a Sharia, since his name is a compound. And it cannot be that the separateness of the letters of Adam's name proves that he is not the author of the Sharia while the meeting of the letters of the name of the Qā'im (peace be upon him), who is Muhammad, proves by the fact of being a compound that he is the author of the Sharia.* The Qā'im's name (*Muhammad*) is *also compound*. If *compounded names* indicate *Sharia-bearing*, then the Qā'im *would* bear a Sharia. But — al-Islah and al-Nusra both agree — the Qā'im *does not* bring a new Sharia. The argument from letter-composition is *self-contradictory*: it would prove either *Adam has no Sharia and the Qā'im does*, or *both have Sharia*, or *neither* — none of which matches the disputants' agreed architecture. *And if that is invalidated, then no inference of this kind holds, and the Sharia for Adam stands established. Praise be to Allah, the Lord of the Worlds.*

The Fasl is brief and is the bāb's lightest moment. The architecture of *letter-mysticism* is *not* the architecture of *Sharia-bearing*. The mistake is one of *category*; al-Kirmani's correction is one of *parity*.

## Fasl 31 — The Ark: new Sharia, or new da'wa?

At Fasl 31 al-Islah opens the reading of Nuh's Ark: the Ark is *the new Sharia* in which the obedient are saved from the flood that drowned those who clung to the abrogated law. Al-Kirmani's gloss:

> Noah was singled out by the Ark because he was the first to have intentions, and the first to abrogate laws and to compose a new law. His law was as a ship that he likened to the Flood: by it he destroyed the people of the earth, except those who responded to his law and boarded his ship — that is, established obedience to its foundation.

Those who *boarded* the Ark are those who responded to Nuh's law; those who *drowned* are those who clung to the old law, which had hardened into the worship of Wadd, Suwa', Yaghuth, Ya'uq, and Nasr. *The old Sharia, in its final corruption, became idol-worship; the new Sharia, in its inaugural purity, was the Ark of salvation.* The verse Allah pairs the architecture with — *because of their sins they were drowned, and were made to enter the Fire* (Quran 71:25) — names the flood as *outward* (the drowning) *and inward* (the Fire).

Al-Nusra reads the Ark differently. *If the status of the ship were the status of the apparent law, then those who held fast to the apparent would have been saved — and the inward, true call would have been annulled.* On al-Nusra's reading, *the Ark is the appointment of the waliyy*: the foundation, the successor, the one in whose obedience the new cycle holds. The architecture is the architecture of *the da'wa and its waliyy*, not of the Sharia and its prescriptions.

Al-Kirmani's reply — the bāb's signature move on the figure of the Ark — is to *accept both*. *The two interpretations are correct in their own respects: the first concerns the order of this world, and the second concerns the order of the brethren, and neither dispenses with the other, since each of them raises its own head.* The Ark is *both* the new Sharia of the second cycle *and* the appointment of the waliyy through whom the Sharia is held. The bāb's deepest hermeneutic constant — *the outer carries the inner; the inner holds inside the outer* — is enacted, at the figure of the Ark, as architectural completion.

## Fasl 32 — Adam's guardian was Seth: the chain begins with Adam

At Fasl 32 al-Kirmani addresses al-Nusra's lingering claim that *Nuh was the first to establish a guardian by way of ta'wīl*. The reply is decisive:

> The prophethood of Adam (peace be upon him) and his Sharia are established, and it is firmly established that Seth (peace be upon him) was his guardian. The precedent of Sharia, of the guardian, and of the guardian's appointment did not begin with Noah. It is also a matter of reason that each speaker (natiq) requires a guardian who benefits from him, just as every moved thing necessarily has a mover — the mover being one, even if many derive their motion from it.

*Each Speaker requires a guardian.* The architecture is *structural*, not historical-contingent. *Just as every moved thing necessarily has a mover, the mover being one even if many derive their motion from it.* The Speaker is the *unmoved-mover-of-the-cycle*; the guardian is the *unique* recipient through whom *many* learners take their motion. *The learner who comprehends the truth is the one we call the guardian. When Adam was the speaker, he therefore must have had a guardian, and likewise every speaker.* The chain begins with *Adam-and-Seth*, not with *Nuh-and-Shem*. Al-Nusra's reading at Fasl 32 — *Noah was the first to establish a guardian* — is *historically* false (the Da'wa's tradition firmly names Seth as Adam's guardian) and *structurally* impossible (the architecture of *Speaker-and-guardian* requires the guardian for *every* Speaker).

## Fasl 33 — The son of Noah, the mountain, and the flood that extends outward and inward

The bāb's closing Fasl gives al-Kirmani's longest political-architectural reading of the second half — a reading parallel to (and continuous with) the Cain-and-Abel reading of Fasl 6, but now in the architecture of the *second* cycle.

Al-Nusra had argued, from the Qur'anic narrative of *the son of Noah* (Quran 11:42–43), that *the matter of the guardian was unknown before Noah's time*. *He argued that the son of Noah did not know the rank of the guardian, and that if Adam had had a guardian, his son would have known of it.* The argument is by *transmission-failure*: if Adam's guardianship-architecture had been in place, *Noah's son* (a generation away from Adam's Sharia, on al-Nusra's reading) would have *known* the rank.

Al-Kirmani's reply has two layers. First, *this statement of his contradicts what he himself said in the chapter on the cycles, namely that the cycle of Adam was followed by the Imams who succeeded him*. Al-Nusra has *already* acknowledged the Imamic chain after Adam. *I do not know how this slipped past him, and I suspect that the manuscript of his book has been altered and added to.* Al-Kirmani's textual-critical suspicion — that *the failing of those who concern themselves with religious matters is that they neglect to correct what they write by review and recitation to the people of knowledge* — preserves *charity* toward al-Nusra while *naming* the internal contradiction.

Second — and this is the bāb's load-bearing reading — al-Kirmani opens the *inner* reading of Quran 11:42–43:

> The proper interpretation of the prayer of the son of Noah is that this son, though attributed to Noah, was not truly of his line spiritually. He was a powerful man, possessed of worldly authority, who occupied a position parallel to that of the adversary in the cycle of our Prophet Muhammad. He had drawn benefit from Noah, had learned what he knew, and so became, by the strength he derived, his rival; people too imagined that he was the guardian himself.

*A powerful man, possessed of worldly authority, who occupied a position parallel to that of the adversary in the cycle of our Prophet Muhammad ﷺ.* The reading is *politically charged*. The son of Noah is *not* simply *the biological son who refused to board the Ark*; he is *the political adversary* who *had drawn benefit from Noah, had learned what he knew*, and *became, by the strength he derived, his rival*. *People too imagined that he was the guardian himself.* The architecture is *exactly* the architecture of Fasl 6 read into the second cycle: *the adversary* (here named as the son) had hoped to be appointed; the appointment went *to another* — *the office of guardianship was assigned by prophetic warrant to another beside him* — and the adversary, *despairing of the rank he had been hoping for*, became *hypocritical, arrogant, and refused obedience*.

The Qur'anic exchange is read in this register: *Be not with the disbelievers* — on the inner reading — means *do not stand outside of obedience to the foundation*. The Ark is what Fasl 31 named: *both* the new Sharia *and* the new da'wa with its waliyy. The son's *refusal to enter* is his *refusal to bind himself* to the foundation Nuh has appointed. The architecture of *wali and adversary* is read into the second cycle.

The son's speech — *I will take refuge on a mountain to protect me from the water* (Quran 11:43) — receives the bāb's most explicit political reading:

> "It is not for you to gather the whole presidency into your house, even if it appears as good intention; I have been backed by chieftains who have tightened my hand and helped me to ward off humiliation from me." The water here is what divides Noah's people against the force that destroys them, and the mountain is what is sustained by men and saints.

*The mountain is what is sustained by men and saints.* The mountain names *political backing* — *the chieftains who tightened his hand* — the worldly authority on which the adversary leans against the architecture of the wali. *The water* is the force that destroys those who refuse — *the flood that extended outwardly and inwardly*. The outward flood is the historical drowning; the inward flood is *the flood of disgrace and ruin* that overtakes those who refuse to bind themselves to the foundation.

The bāb closes on the architectural reach of the pattern:

> Allah has indeed mentioned such reports in His Noble Book as a warning to the nation of what is done under the foundational command against those who are recalcitrant and refuse to bind themselves to it. Sometimes our Prophet refers to it with "O children of Adam," sometimes with the camel of Salih, sometimes with the son of Noah — so that one may know that the conditions of Muhammad's cycle are like the conditions of the cycles mentioned above.

*The conditions of Muhammad's cycle are like the conditions of the cycles mentioned above.* The architecture is *one pattern* across all the cycles: *the wali appointed, the adversary refusing, the flood — outward and inward — clearing the cycle for the next door of the chain*. The pattern began with Cain and Abel (the first cycle); it continued with the son of Noah (the second); it ran through *the camel of Salih (saa-lih)* (warning the people of Thamud); it runs through *every section of the people of the apparent (ẓāhir; thaa-hir) who fell into a similar state of hypocrisy*. *This is a pattern that has run through all the prophets and the guardians, all the way to the time of the seventh.* The architecture of *Adam's law* is *not* only *the architecture of the first door*; it is *the architecture under which every door of every cycle opens and closes*.

The closing line — *only one who is counted among the people of the elevated (ʿalawī; a-la-wee) da'wah, of which God is the light, recognizes this* — names the bāb's hermeneutic discipline. The architecture *cannot* be seen from outside the architecture; the *flood that extends outwardly and inwardly* can only be *read* by *the people of the elevated da'wa*. The bāb's last word is *belonging* — the architecture is *for those who walk the road from the door of Adam to the gathering of the Qā'im*.

A closing line in the manner of Imam Zayn al-'Abidin (zayn al-aa-bi-deen; peace be upon him), in the manner of *al-Sahifa al-Sajjadiyya* (as-sa-hee-fah as-saj-jaa-dee-yah), names the bāb's vocation under the doubled architecture of *Adam's first door* and *the Qā'im's closing gathering*:

> O God, You opened the door at Adam and You will close it at the gathering; between the opening and the closing, You have set the road of the Speakers, the foundations of their Sharias, the guardians who hold their place. Make me of those who board the Ark of every cycle, who do not seek a mountain when the water rises, who recognize the foundation by the architecture and not by the lineage, that I be gathered where the gathered are gathered and not be among the drowned.
> (After the manner of Imam Zayn al-'Abidin, *al-Sahifa al-Sajjadiyya*, supplication for boarding the Ark of the cycle; paraphrased.)

*Make me of those who board the Ark of every cycle.* The Du'a-side counterpart to al-Kirmani's analytic register names what the bāb has been arguing under the architecture of *Noah's trust and the Great Resurrection*: the *trust* is the architecture *Adam first opened* and *Nuh first inherited and renewed* and *every Speaker after them held*; the *resurrection* is the architecture *the Qā'im will close*. The chain is *one mercy* — *one road, seven doors, six Sharias, the gathering at the seventh*. The bāb closes the architecture of Bāb 9; Bāb 10 will turn to al-Kirmani's closing critique of *al-Mahsul* on the doctrine of *tawhid* and *the First Originator*. But under both bābs runs *the road* — the road *of those who walked where the prophets walked, and recognized in His laws the path of return*.
