# Motion, Stillness, and the Word "Be"

*From the Kitab al-Riyad (ki-taab ar-ri-yaad) of Hamid al-Din Ahmad al-Kirmani (haa-mid ad-deen ah-mad al-kir-maa-nee). This chapter covers Bāb 6 (baab) — On Motion, Stillness, and Form — comprising nine fusūl (fu-sool; sections). Bāb 5 closed on a cumulative anthropology — the human as the fruit of all the worlds, the last in the universes, united in eternity with what has been acquired by time. Bāb 6 now turns from the chain's terminal recipient back to the chain's interior joints, asking whether motion and stillness, *hayula* (ha-yoo-laa; prime matter) and form, and the divine word *kun* (kun; Be) can be analogized to soul and body without breaking the architecture the previous bābs have built. Al-Islah (al-is-laah) proposes a graded ladder of refinement — motion and stillness gentler than *hayula* and form, *hayula* and form gentler than body — and al-Nusra (an-nus-rah) counters with a structural resemblance in the kind of division — motion divisible into three just as body divides into length, width, depth. Al-Kirmani refuses both analogies on the same fundamental ground — *the soul is not embodied*, so density and gentleness do not apply to it; *motion and stillness are contraries that cannot coexist*, while *hayula and form are correlatives that cannot exist apart*; the dyads belong to different logical genera and cannot be analogized. The bāb then turns to the reading of the First Cause as the word *kun*, whose two letters (moving *kaf* — kaaf — and stationary *nun* — noon) stand in for body, and whose motion and stillness stand in for soul. Al-Kirmani accepts the reading only at the level of *analogical understanding*: the two states correspond to *two honorable relations* of the originative apex — its being a cause and its being effected — not to properties of the Origin's essence. *Above the First Effect, there is no kaf, no nun, no motion, and no stillness — what is there is pure ibda (ib-daa; creativity) and complete causation.* The bāb's compressed final teaching: *contradictions occur in bodies, not in spirits — bodies are bodies because they are objects; difference and contrast lie in the images by which what animates them is animated, not insofar as they are bodies*.*

## What this bāb is really asking

Bāb 5 closed on the human as the fruit of all the worlds, the last in the universes. Each of the bābs preceding this one tested the architecture against a question about *the chain's recipients* — what stands at this rank, how the higher gives to the lower. Bāb 6 turns the inquiry sideways. The question is no longer *what stands at this rank* but *what is the structure of the joints between ranks*. Motion and stillness, *hayula* and form, the word *kun* — these are the *internal articulations* of the chain. If the architecture is to hold under pressure, the joints must be named in such a way that they neither over-spiritualize what is bodily nor over-embody what is spiritual.

Al-Islah and al-Nusra both feel the pressure and both reach for *analogy*. Al-Islah reaches for a graded ladder: motion and stillness gentler than *hayula* and form, *hayula* and form gentler than body — the soul-and-body distinction recapitulated up the chain. Al-Nusra reaches for a structural resemblance: motion divides in three just as body divides in three, *hayula* is undivided just as soul's parts resemble each other. Each disputant is trying to secure the architecture by importing a familiar dyad into an unfamiliar place. Each, in al-Kirmani's reading, breaks the architecture at the joint they were trying to secure.

Al-Kirmani's adjudication will turn on three moves. *First:* the soul is not embodied at all, so *density and gentleness* — which are predicates of bodies — cannot apply to it; the graded ladder loses its first term. *Second:* motion and stillness are *contraries that cannot coexist*, whereas *hayula* and form are *correlatives that cannot exist apart*; analogizing the dyads confuses contraries with correlatives. *Third:* the reading of the First Cause as the word *kun* — moving *kaf* and stationary *nun* — works only as *analogical understanding*; at the level of the Self itself, *above the First Effect, there is no kaf, no nun, no motion, and no stillness*. The apophatic structure of the originative apex, named in Bāb 2 and reaffirmed throughout the dispute, must be preserved here too.

The bāb is more sharply polemical than Bāb 5 — al-Kirmani uses the phrases *built upon a corrupt root* and *irregular disorder*. The sharpness names where the disputants have taken the architecture's load onto a joint that cannot bear it. The nine *fusūl* walk through the failures one at a time, then return at Fasl 9 to a compressed metaphysical principle: *contradictions occur in bodies, not in spirits*.

## The Qur'anic horizon — *kun fa-yakun* and *laysa ka-mithlihi shay'*

Two Qur'anic verses stand behind the bāb, and naming them at the outset clarifies what is at stake when the disputants reach for *the word kun* and what is at stake when al-Kirmani reaches for the apophatic correction.

The first is the Qur'an's (qur-aan) own naming of the divine creative word:

> His command, when He intends a thing, is only that He says to it: "Be" (*kun*), and it is.
> (Quran 36:82; English: Saheeh International.)

*Kun fa-yakun* (kun fa-ya-kun). The word *kun* — the imperative of *kāna* (to be) — is the Qur'anic name of the creative command itself. Surat Ya-Sin (soo-rat yaa-seen) names it at the close of the chapter that has been describing the sweep of God's creative power over the heavens, the earth, the sun, the moon, and the night-and-day. The verse is the Qur'anic anchor for any reading of the First Cause as *a word*. Al-Islah, when he reads the First Cause as *the word of the Almighty whose form is kun*, is offering a philosophical-cosmological gloss on Surat Ya-Sin. The question al-Kirmani must answer is *how far the gloss can be pressed* — whether *kun* is the *literal structure* of the Origin, or whether it is *the structure of the originative relation* between the Origin and what is originated.

The second verse establishes the apophatic limit:

> There is nothing like Him, and He is the Hearing, the Seeing.
> (Quran 42:11; English: Saheeh International.)

*Laysa ka-mithlihi shay'* (lay-sa ka-mith-li-hi shay). *There is nothing like Him.* Surat al-Shura (soo-rat ash-shoo-raa) gives the Qur'an's most direct apophatic formula: no created thing — no body, no soul, no motion, no stillness, no letter, no word — stands as a *likeness* of the Origin. Every analogy that imports a created predicate into the Origin's essence runs against this verse. The hosts should hear the apophatic claim ringing in the background whenever al-Kirmani writes *one cannot say of what is above the First Effect that it is in motion or at rest*: he is rendering *laysa ka-mithlihi shay'* into the technical vocabulary of *ibda* and *first effect*.

Between these two verses lies the bāb's whole work. The first verse authorizes the analogy; the second verse limits it. Al-Kirmani's task is to read the analogy *under the limit* — neither refusing it (it has Qur'anic warrant) nor literalizing it (the literalization breaks the apophatic structure). The reading-under-the-limit is what he calls *analogical understanding*.

A line in the manner of Imam Ali ibn Abi Talib (i-maam a-lee ibn a-bee taa-lib; the Commander of the Believers, peace be upon him), from the opening of *Nahj al-Balagha* (nahj al-ba-laa-ghah), names the apophatic claim from the side of the *khutbah*:

> He created creation without imagined thought, without need experienced, without motion brought into being, without restlessness of soul.
> (After the manner of Imam Ali ibn Abi Talib, *Nahj al-Balagha*, Sermon 1; paraphrased.)

*Without motion brought into being, without restlessness of soul.* The Alid line names the same architecture al-Kirmani will defend: at the originative apex there is no motion, no stillness, no restlessness — and creation comes forth without the Origin's needing to *move* or *be still*. The technical vocabulary of *ibda* renders philosophically what the Imam's sermon names liturgically: the Creator creates *without being subject to the predicates under which what He creates runs*.

## Fasl 1 — The two analogies and the corrupt root

The first *fasl* opens with al-Islah's proposal:

> The first *hayula* (prime matter) is illusory motion in the vehicles, and the *hayulanis* (hay-oo-laa-nees; matter-like things) in a single statement of three. What has been said of that in this book contains much confusion. I say, in brief, that motion and stillness are to *hayula* and form as form and *hayula* are to body — for motion and stillness are gentler than body, and body is denser than soul; likewise motion and stillness are gentler than *hayula* and form, and *hayula* and form are denser than motion and stillness. To represent the gentler with the soul and the denser with the body is appropriate, but to represent the denser with the body and the gentler with the soul is too early. This is clear, and the proof goes with it.

Al-Islah's structure is a proportional analogy. The proportion runs: *motion and stillness : hayula and form :: form and hayula : body*. At each step, the first term is *gentler* and the second is *denser*. Motion and stillness are gentler than *hayula* and form, which are themselves gentler than body. The architecture is a graded ladder of refinement: as one ascends from body to *hayula*-form to motion-stillness, density gives way to gentleness, and the soul-side of the soul-body distinction is approached.

The analogy is supposed to do *philosophical work*. The work is: the soul-body distinction is not a *unique* dyad found only at the human level; it is the *recurrent structure* of the chain, recapitulated at every joint. Motion and stillness are the *soul* of the *hayula*-and-form layer; *hayula* and form are the *soul* of the body layer; body itself is dense matter at the lowest rank. The chain is one architectural movement, and the movement is the gradual densification of what is gentle at the top.

Al-Nusra rejects the analogy at its first joint:

> The situation of the Soul and the body, and the comparison between them in terms of gentlest and densest, is not as he has described. For if it were as he mentioned, it would have been required that the tangible essences be objects of the symptoms — since they are denser than the symptoms — and that the symptoms be souls for them, since they are gentler than the essences. Rather, what is required is that we look at the limit of body and soul, and then liken to the soul what resembles the limit of the soul, and to the body what resembles the limit of the body.

The objection is precise. If *gentleness* is the predicate that makes something soul-like, and *density* is the predicate that makes something body-like, then *symptoms* (the accidents of bodies — color, weight, temperature, the qualia that adhere to substances) would be *souls* for the substances they adhere to, since accidents are *gentler* than the substances they qualify. And the *tangible essences* — the substances themselves — would be *bodies* for the accidents. The proportion turns the metaphysical categories inside out. Al-Nusra concludes that the proportional method is wrong: we must look at the *limit* (the definition) of body and the *limit* of soul, and then *liken to the soul what resembles the limit of the soul, and to the body what resembles the limit of the body*. Resemblance must run through definitions, not through graded ladders.

Al-Nusra then offers his own analogy, this time in terms of *structural resemblance*:

> We have found that the body is what accepts the three dimensions — length, width, depth — and we have found that the soul is what has parts resembling each other. Likewise we have found that motion is divided into three sections — comparable to the divisions of body — such as motion from the center, motion toward the center, and motion around the middle. So motion must resemble body: when it is divided, it divides as body divides into length, width, and depth. *Hayula* is not like this, because the parts of *hayula* resemble each other; and because motion resembles body if they are alike in the kind of division, and soul if they are alike in the parts, the first thing that motion resembles is soul — that is, the one motion which is the undivided body. The resemblance of *hayula* is that it is not divided, while form is the body that is divided.

The analogy is more careful than al-Islah's. Al-Nusra builds it on *limits*: body's limit is *accepting the three dimensions*, soul's limit is *having parts that resemble each other*. Motion divides into three (from-the-center, toward-the-center, around-the-middle); the divisions are *like* the body's three dimensions; therefore motion *resembles body* in the *kind of division*. *Hayula*, by contrast, is *undivided*; its parts resemble each other; therefore *hayula* resembles soul. The resemblance now runs through a structural kinship: same kind of division → body-resembling; same kind of parts → soul-resembling.

But al-Nusra also adds a subtler claim: *the first thing that motion resembles is soul — that is, the one motion which is the undivided body*. Beneath the divisions, motion is *one*; the *first* motion is the undivided motion that runs through the three divided ones. The first motion is *soul-like* even when its divisions are *body-like*. The proposal is layered.

Al-Kirmani's response in *fasl* 1 cuts through both analogies on the same ground:

> In this chapter the two have departed from likeness and simile, and between us and both of them stands the fact that they have built upon a corrupt root upon which the proof rests; neither the simile nor the analogy by which they represent it is true.

*Built upon a corrupt root.* The phrase is sharp — sharper than anything al-Kirmani has used in the dispute so far. The disputants have not made a *contained* error that could be patched by reframing; they have *built the analogy on a foundation that cannot bear it*. The al-Islah's version reads the soul-body distinction as a *recapitulating* dyad up the chain — but the soul-body distinction is not recapitulating, because *soul is not embodied at all*. The al-Nusra's version reads motion and *hayula* as belonging to the same logical genus — but they belong to different genera, because motion and stillness are *contraries* while *hayula* and form are *correlatives*. The corrupt root is in both cases the same: a *category confusion* about what kind of dyad each pair is.

Al-Kirmani spells out the deeper metaphysical claim that breaks the analogies:

> Motion and stillness, even when they are actual, are two opposites that do not meet in the same place at the same time, whereas *hayula* and form: their existence together cannot dispense with the other, on condition that one of them is an agent — which is form — and the other is an object in which the action occurs — which is *al-hayula*. Our book known as *Rahat al-'Aql* (Repose of the Intellect) contains an explanation of this.

The distinction is the load-bearing one for the rest of the bāb. *Motion and stillness are two opposites that do not meet in the same place at the same time.* In a given place, at a given time, a body is either in motion *or* at rest — not both. They are *contraries*. *Hayula* and form, by contrast, *exist only together*; their existence *cannot dispense with the other*. There is no *hayula* without some form qualifying it, and no form except as the form of some *hayula* (in the world of natural existence — al-Kirmani is bracketing the originative apex). Form is the *agent*; *hayula* is the *object in which the action occurs*. They are *correlatives*.

Contraries do not analogize to correlatives, because the metaphysical relations are different. A contrary *excludes* its opposite from the same place at the same time; a correlative *requires* its mate at every moment of its existence. To say *motion is to stillness as form is to hayula* is to import an exclusion-structure into a requirement-structure (or vice versa). The proportional analogy that al-Islah proposed, and the structural analogy that al-Nusra proposed, both run aground on this distinction. *Neither the simile nor the analogy by which they represent it is true.*

Al-Kirmani also makes a polemical point against al-Islah's specific framing:

> The author of al-Islah has held that motion and stillness are to the soul as the mover is the act of the engine, and the engine is what we call the soul. So the soul, and stillness as well, is the engine's letting go of moving what it moves. If the engine is the soul, then to represent motion — which is her action — by her is to represent the impossible.

If the *soul* is the *engine* (the principle of motion), then motion is *the soul's action*. But to *represent motion by the soul* — to say *motion is gentle as soul is gentle* — is to *represent an action by the agent of the action*, which is a category confusion of a different kind: the action and the agent are not in the same metaphysical category. Motion is not *what soul is*; motion is *what soul does* (in the natural order, when it moves what it moves). To run the analogy *motion : soul* is to treat the action as if it were the agent.

The reference to *Rahat al-'Aql* — al-Kirmani's *Repose of the Intellect*, the great philosophical summa he wrote earlier in his career — situates the present argument within his own corpus. The *hayula*-and-form architecture receives its full exposition there; the present bāb is in dialogue with it, and the reader who wants the full demonstration is referred to that book.

## Fasl 2 — The soul is not embodied

The second *fasl* takes up the soul-and-body distinction at its sharpest point. Al-Islah's analogy required the soul to admit *gentleness* and *density* — at minimum, *gentleness as a relative predicate*. Al-Kirmani refuses:

> Then he required himself to say that the soul is gentler than the body — because the soul is a body, and that is impossible: the soul is not embodied to have density or gentleness, for density and gentleness are specific to that which is a body, just as air, which is a body, is specifically gentle, and earth, which is a body, is specifically dense. The soul is the soul, and the evidence is established that it is not embodied. Once the soul is not embodied, then to say of it that it is gentler or more dense, or to make a likeness on that basis, is impossible.

The argument is short and decisive. *Density* and *gentleness* are predicates of bodies — *specific to that which is a body*. Air is *specifically gentle* because air is *a body of a certain kind*. Earth is *specifically dense* because earth is *a body of a certain kind*. The predicates *gentle* and *dense* are *modes of embodiment*; they describe how a body *fills space*, *receives impressions*, *transmits forces*. Apply them to a non-body, and they have no purchase.

The soul is *not embodied*. The earlier bābs have established this — the soul does not occupy space, does not have parts disposed in length-width-depth, is not subject to the nine accidents of body. *The soul is the soul, and the evidence is established that it is not embodied.* To call the soul *gentler than the body* is therefore to *apply a body-predicate to a non-body*: not just a category error but an *impossibility*.

The consequence for al-Islah's analogy is destructive. The whole proportional ladder — *motion-and-stillness gentler than hayula-and-form, hayula-and-form gentler than body* — was supposed to be a graded refinement, with *the soul* as the limit-case of gentleness at the top. Once the soul is removed from the gentle-dense axis altogether, the *direction* of the proportion has no destination. The ladder leads upward toward *no rung at all*, because the rung it was leading toward turns out to be in a different building.

The *fasl* is short, but its work for the bāb is structural: it eliminates *density-and-gentleness* as a predicate-pair that could carry the analogy. From here on, any analogy between soul and what is below soul will have to find a different axis.

## Fasl 3 — The limit of body and the limit of soul

The third *fasl* takes up al-Nusra's alternative method: define body and soul *by their limits*, then liken to soul what resembles the limit of soul, and to body what resembles the limit of body. Al-Nusra gave body's limit as *accepting the three dimensions* and soul's limit as *having parts resembling each other* (illustrated with water, fire, and air, each homogeneous in its parts).

Al-Kirmani's reply works at the foundation, not at the limits:

> The author of al-Nusra then came forward and undertook to give the limit of body and soul. He set the limit of body as that which accepts the three dimensions, and the limit of the soul as the unity of parts in resemblance, and provided this with a flow of water, fire, and air — each of which is similar in its parts. It is impossible to go beyond saying that the soul is not embodied. Soul and self are two names synonymous for one thing, and that thing is not a body. Were it a body, it would have been required of it the nine qualities and symptoms of which body has need, and it could have been perceived by a sense; but since it is not perceived by a sense, it is not subject to illusion. It has nine symptoms which do not exist in it; and since the physical symptoms — which are the very mark of being a body — do not exist in it, our well-known book *(With Lamps)* contains what heals this point.

The argument has two moves. *First:* al-Nusra's *limit of the soul* (parts resembling each other, illustrated with water-fire-air) is a *limit of homogeneous bodies* — water and fire and air are *bodies* whose parts resemble each other, but they are still *bodies*. The illustration *imports embodied homogeneity* into the soul, which is *not embodied at all*. The mistake is the same as al-Islah's, but reached by a different path: where al-Islah projected *gentleness* onto the soul, al-Nusra projects *part-homogeneity* onto the soul. Both projections require the soul to admit a body-predicate.

*Second:* al-Kirmani gives the demonstrative argument that *the soul is not a body*. *Were it a body, it would have been required of it the nine qualities and symptoms of which body has need, and it could have been perceived by a sense.* The *nine qualities and symptoms* — the categories of accidents that adhere to bodies in the Aristotelian-Ismaili taxonomy: quantity, quality, relation, place, time, position, possession, action, passion — are the predicates that *cannot* fail to attach to anything that is a body. But the soul is *not perceived by a sense*; and *what is not perceived by a sense is not subject to illusion* (i.e., is not the kind of thing about which sensory deception could occur). The *nine symptoms which do not exist in it* are the very mark of *not being a body*.

The reference to *With Lamps* (*al-Masabih* — al-ma-saa-beeh) is al-Kirmani's pointer to his own *al-Masabih fi al-Imama*, his treatise on the imamate that takes up the not-embodiment of the soul as part of its argument for the spiritual reality of the *hudud* (hu-dood; the named teaching ranks). *Our well-known book contains what heals this point.* The full demonstration is housed elsewhere; the bāb is naming the conclusion and pointing toward the demonstration's location.

The *fasl*'s structural work, like Fasl 2's, is to *strip the soul of body-predicates*. Once *gentleness*, *density*, and *part-homogeneity* are all refused as predicates of the soul, both al-Islah's and al-Nusra's analogies are *deprived of the predicate-pair* that was supposed to ground the resemblance. The architecture's joints will have to be named in some other vocabulary.

## Fasl 4 — Motion's three sections, body's nine, and the principled definition

The fourth *fasl* takes up al-Nusra's specific claim that motion divides into three sections like the body. The reply is the most technical of the bāb:

> Then he made motion divisible into three parts resembling the body, on the grounds that the body is divided into length, width, and depth. This is impossible. The three sections into which the body is allegedly divided — namely the three dimensions — each one of these three dimensions is not embodied, and is not said to be a body. The motion of each of its sections is itself motion, and is said to be motion. So how is it likened to the body, which is divisible into nine sections, as we have shown in our well-known book *(Milestones of Religion)*, by a motion that is divided into three sections, or six sections? That is far off. And the discussion in this chapter on the part of the author of the harvest, the author of *al-Islah*, and the author of *al-Nusra* is irregular disorder.

Two corrections. *First:* the *three dimensions* (length, width, depth) are *not bodies* and are *not said to be bodies*. They are *predicates* of body — *what body accepts*, on al-Nusra's own definition. To say *body divides into length, width, depth* is to say *body has three dimensions*, not *body is composed of three sub-bodies each of which is itself a body*. The dimensions are categories under which body is described; they are not parts that have independent embodiment. *Motion*, by contrast, *each one of whose sections is itself motion* — each of the three motions (from-the-center, toward-the-center, around-the-middle) *is itself a motion*. The proposed analogy is between (a) dimensions which are not themselves bodies and (b) motions each of which is itself a motion. The two halves of the analogy do not have the same logical character.

*Second:* the body is *not* in fact divisible into three sections; on al-Kirmani's own count (defended in his *Ma'alim al-Din* — ma-aa-lim ad-deen; *Milestones of Religion*) the body is divisible into *nine* sections. Whether one takes the nine as the nine categories of accident, the nine spheres of the planetary cosmology, or the nine internal divisions of the elemental body — al-Kirmani refers the reader to his own treatise for the demonstration — the *count* is nine, not three. The analogy gets the count wrong even on its own terms.

*Irregular disorder.* The phrase is the harshest in the bāb. *The discussion in this chapter on the part of the author of the harvest, the author of al-Islah, and the author of al-Nusra is irregular disorder.* All three disputants are named — the author of *al-Mahsul* (al-mah-sool; the harvest), al-Islah, and al-Nusra — and the *whole* dispute about motion's resemblance to body is dismissed as *disorder that is not even regular*. The architecture cannot be built on this kind of analogizing.

Al-Kirmani then gives the principled definition that the rest of the bāb will use:

> We say: Motion is that by which a thing's selfsame state changes — either by place, or by quality, or by corruption, or by increase, or by decrease, or by impossibility — and its occurrence is from the mover which is the principle in the thing in which it inheres, naturally and not by width. *Al-hayula* and form are the first emanations following in proportion as emission. The second is regarding the noble proportion in it. There is no existence of the soul outside the soul in the first place, together with the form, including the heavens and the earth and what is between them. God Almighty created all of this through the First Intellect, which was created by God and not from anything — a great elevation above.

The definition is broad enough to subsume the Aristotelian *kinēsis* under one architectural rubric. Motion is *change of the thing's selfsame state*, and the change runs through six modes: *by place* (locomotion), *by quality* (alteration), *by corruption* (perishing), *by increase* (growth), *by decrease* (diminution), *by impossibility* (the mode under which what *cannot be* runs into its own non-being). The six modes are the categories of natural change; motion is the *predicate that holds across all of them*.

The mover, in the definition, is *the principle in the thing in which it inheres, naturally and not by width*. The mover is *immanent* in what moves — *the principle in the thing*, not an external agent reaching across a gap. *Naturally and not by width* (the Arabic ʿarḍan can be read as either *by accident* or *by width*; al-Kirmani's sense is that the mover is *not* a *width-wise* or *peripheral* cause but the *natural* principle of the thing itself). The architecture is consistent with what the previous bābs have established: motion in the natural order does not require *separable* movers; the principle is in what moves.

*Hayula and form are the first emanations following in proportion as emission.* The phrasing returns to the architecture of Bāb 5 Fasl 4. *Hayula* and form are the *first emanations*, related to each other *in proportion as emission* — not cause-and-effect within a chain, but two aspects of the *single first emanation*. They follow *in proportion* (the word can also be read *in succession*), but the succession is in the order of *emission*, not in the order of *temporal-causal generation*. *The second is regarding the noble proportion in it* — the second emanation (the rational souls of Bāb 4) takes its place in this same architectural proportion.

*There is no existence of the soul outside the soul in the first place, together with the form, including the heavens and the earth and what is between them.* The Soul and the form do not have separable existences; they exist *together*, and the *heavens and the earth and what is between them* are constituted in this togetherness. The natural cosmos is *one architecture*; the Soul does not stand *outside* it as an external creator but pervades it *together with the form* it animates.

*God Almighty created all of this through the First Intellect, which was created by God and not from anything — a great elevation above.* The originative apex returns at the close of the *fasl*. The First Intellect — the first *originated* — is the *means* by which God created all of this; the First Intellect *itself* was created *not from anything* (the technical content of *ibda*: origination *from no antecedent material*). The Qur'anic *a great elevation above* (*ta'ālā 'uluwwan kabīran* — found at Surat al-Isra 17:43, in the context of the Qur'an's apophatic insistence against those who would associate with Allah) names the apophatic gap that the *fasl* has just defended.

## Fasl 5 — The word *kun* and the analogy of analogical understanding

The fifth *fasl* opens with al-Islah's reading of the First Cause as the divine word:

> The First Cause, which is creativity, is the word of the Almighty and Exalted. Its form is *kun* (Be), which is two voweled letters (kaf and nun), and the unvoweled letter — the kaf and the nun — of the word stand in the position of body; motion and stillness stand as the soul. This is the example of motion and stillness with the word. The first cause, which is the word, will be the whole world, for example. It must therefore be said that motion and stillness are in all the essences of *al-hayula*, and form is below it in all the worlds, and that the whole of the discussion concerns the three worlds. The first beings are illusory: they are motion and stillness; they are *al-hayula*. Their form is the second *hayula*, which are individuals and the begotten of the first *hayula*. The third *hayulas* (hay-oo-laas) are the four composite mothers from the second *hayulas*.

The reading is layered. The *First Cause* is *creativity* — *ibda* itself — and *ibda* is *the word of the Almighty*. The *form* of the word is *kun*, the imperative of *to be*, which consists of two letters: *kaf* and *nun*. The *kaf* carries the vowel (voweled, moving); the *nun* is *sukūn* (unvoweled, stationary). The letters *as letters* are body-like; the *motion and stillness* in them are soul-like. The word is a *miniature cosmos*: two letters in body-position, two states in soul-position.

From there al-Islah extends the analogy outward. *The first cause, which is the word, will be the whole world.* Just as the word *kun* contains body (two letters) and soul (motion-and-stillness), the *first cause* contains *the whole world* — all the *hayulas*, all the forms, all the three worlds (originative, celestial, natural). The first *hayulas* are *illusory* (insubstantial, prior to the substantialization of nature) — they *are* motion and stillness; their *form* is the *second hayula* — the individuals begotten of the first; the *third hayulas* are *the four composite mothers* — fire, water, earth, air — from the second. The Qur'anic *kun fa-yakun* becomes, in al-Islah's hands, the cosmological diagram of the chain's joints.

Then al-Islah adds the metaphysical claim that completes the reading:

> The Houthi and its form are the act of the command, which is creativity, as we have said. *Hayula* is illusory, and so creativity is imaginary; it is shown only in the first creative.

The phrasing is dense and partly corrupted in the manuscript (*the Houthi* — likely a scribal error for *al-hayula* or a related term), but the claim is clear enough: *hayula and its form are the act of the command, which is creativity*; *hayula is illusory, and so creativity is imaginary*. The act of the originative command is *not substantialized in the natural sense*; it is shown *only in the first creative* — the First Intellect.

Al-Nusra raises an objection at the apex:

> As for the Self itself, it is not established for Him that these two letters are the words about which speech here applies — because at this level there is no kaf, no nun, no motion, and no stillness. One cannot say of what is above the First Effect that it is in motion or at rest, or anything like such expressions; rather, what is there is pure ibda (creativity) and complete causation. This is what they say.

The objection is sharp and al-Kirmani will endorse it. *As for the Self itself* — *li-l-dhāt nafsihā*, the Origin in its own essence — *it is not established for Him that these two letters are the words about which speech here applies*. The literal grammar of *kun* — moving *kaf*, stationary *nun* — *does not apply at the level of the Self itself*. *At this level there is no kaf, no nun, no motion, and no stillness.* The apophatic claim is total: every created predicate (letter, motion, stillness, the structural-grammatical features of an Arabic word) is *too small for what is named*. *One cannot say of what is above the First Effect that it is in motion or at rest, or anything like such expressions.* What is there is *pure ibda and complete causation* — origination undivided by any structure that could be analogized from below.

Al-Kirmani's adjudication accepts al-Nusra's apophatic correction and rereads al-Islah's analogy under it:

> The claim of the author of al-Islah — that the First Cause, which is ibda, is the word of God, and that its image is two letters — is a remote analogy in the sense he intended, and it scarcely holds. If we say "image," it entails something that has an image, and the word is not a picture. If we say "picture," meaning what is above nature, we mean the abstract image; and if we say "the image in question," we mean the image that subsists in matter, in the natural world.

The first move is to name the *equivocation* in *image* (*ṣūrah*). On any of three readings — *image* as picture, as abstract-image-above-nature, or as image-subsisting-in-matter — the analogy *scarcely holds*: words are not pictures; the originative apex cannot have a grammatical structure; and the natural-world reading displaces the analogy from where al-Islah was aiming.

Then al-Kirmani gives the *rereading* that lets the analogy survive — at the cost of demoting its claim:

> And when he says that ibda — which is the word of God Almighty and His command as expressed by it — has two letters, one movable and the other a consonant, indicating the very union of two attributes, this points to the inference of the noble Essence and its attributes.

The two letters of *kun* point — *by inference* (*istidlāl*) — to the *union of two attributes* in the *noble Essence*. The two attributes are about to be named: *cause* and *effected*. The analogy is rereading: not *Allah is literally a word with two letters*, but *the analogy of a word with two letters indicates, by inference, the structure of the union of two attributes in the Essence*. The analogy is *demoted from constitutive to indicative*: it does not say *what the Essence is*; it *points* (*istinbāṭ qiyāsī*) to the structure of relations one may inferentially read in the Essence.

And then the inferential content:

> It combines two states, just as the word "Be" combines motion and stillness; so the two states are attributed to that Essence in respect of two honorable relations, one of which is its being a cause, and the other its being effected. The motion that terminates in stillness corresponds to the relation whose cause terminates in an effect, and the stillness whose end is motion corresponds to the relation in which the effect terminates in the cause — for there is no motion or stillness here except by way of analogical understanding. Otherwise the First would have been a cause only incidentally, in which case the speaker would have to affirm that He is the agent of His own Essence.

*Two honorable relations.* The Essence stands in *two relations* — being a *cause* and being *effected*. The *kun*-analogy renders these two relations: *the motion that terminates in stillness* (movement coming to rest) corresponds to *the relation whose cause terminates in an effect* (the originative act reaching its terminus in what is originated); *the stillness whose end is motion* (rest from which movement emerges) corresponds to *the relation in which the effect terminates in the cause* (the originated thing referring itself back to its origin).

*There is no motion or stillness here except by way of analogical understanding.* The apophatic clause is reaffirmed: the *literal* motion and stillness do not apply; only the *analogical* understanding does. The two states in *kun* are *symbols* of the two relations in the Essence — not *images* of them, not *constitutive* of them, but *symbols by which the discriminating reader infers the structure of the relations*.

The closing clause is precise. If the *kun*-analogy were *literal*, then the First would be a cause *only incidentally* — His causality would be *contingent on the analogy holding* — and *He would be the agent of His own Essence*, an internal-acting structure within the Origin, which is what *ibda* must *not* be (*ibda* is origination *from no antecedent*, not the Origin acting on itself). The reading under *analogical understanding* preserves the Origin's simplicity; the literal reading would compromise it. The teaching has the same structure as Bāb 5 Fasl 6: *He is the cause and the effect, the perfect and the complete*, with the names *synonymous with respect to additions*. The *kun*-analogy is the naming of this oneness-under-aspects from the side of the word.

## Fasl 6 — Above the First Effect, no kaf, no nun, no motion, no stillness

The sixth *fasl* turns the *kun*-analogy back toward al-Islah and presses what the analogy *cannot do*:

> Then he said: The First [Effect] is imaginal, and He made it motion and stillness; then He made it motion, stillness, and hayula. The image is not these hayulas, but the motions and the stillnesses are the supports, and the image is an essence emanating from the First upon what has been discussed above — including the lower world. Motion and stillness have their existence in bodies, upon their principles concerning which the hayulas and the image appear. Together they constitute the body as a shape and a picture in the body.

Al-Islah's extended claim: the First *Effect* — the First Intellect — is *imaginal* (carries an *image*). The First *made it* motion and stillness; then motion, stillness, and *hayula*. The *image* is *not the hayulas themselves* — they are *supports* — but is an *essence emanating from the First* that runs *through what has been discussed above and including the lower world*. Motion and stillness *have their existence in bodies* (in the natural order), *upon their principles* (the principles by which the body is structured); the *hayulas and the image* appear *upon* those principles; *together they constitute the body as a shape and a picture in the body*.

The proposal is that the *image*-vocabulary runs through all the ranks: First Effect has an image, the chain emanates the image downward, motion-and-stillness *support* the image's appearance in the natural body, and the body is *the image's shape in the natural order*. The architecture is held together by *image*.

Al-Nusra's apophatic objection — already heard at the close of Fasl 5 — now lands directly on al-Islah's *image*-cascade. *Above the First Effect there is no kaf, no nun, no motion, and no stillness.* If the architecture is *image-all-the-way-up*, then *the Origin* would have an image too, which the apophatic claim refuses. *Pure ibda and complete causation* are not *imaged*; they are *what stands above the regime of image*.

The *fasl* itself is mostly al-Islah's expanded reading, but the apophatic correction governs how al-Kirmani receives it. *The First Effect* — the First Intellect — *is imaginal*; the *image* runs downward through the chain. But *above the First Effect* — at the originative apex itself — there is no image, no letter, no motion, no stillness. The First Intellect is the *highest rank at which image-language has purchase*; the Origin Itself is *outside the regime of image* altogether. The verse *laysa ka-mithlihi shay'* of Surat al-Shura is the Qur'anic anchor for this boundary: not *no image among created things*, but *no image-relation at all to the Creator*.

## Fasl 7 — The grave error: *ibda* is nothing other than the Creator Himself

The seventh *fasl* names what al-Kirmani considers the gravest error in al-Islah's whole reading:

> When he made the First Hayula correspond to the matter that is ibda, the First Hayula is imaginal just as ibda is imaginal, and it shows itself only in the First Creator. He said that this must hold in the presence of the Almighty, because of His pride in that: since ibda had not yet appeared, He became Creative through ibda. This is a grave error, because the doctrine and belief in monotheism is that what is other than Allah Almighty — and He is the Creative One and the Originator — had no existence at all; and even if it had, ibda is nothing other than the Creator Himself. We have built upon this the demonstration sufficient to our argument.

Two claims of al-Islah are at stake. *First:* the First *Hayula* corresponds to the matter that is *ibda*; the First *Hayula* is *imaginal* just as *ibda* is *imaginal*; it shows itself *only in the First Creator*. *Second:* this *must hold in the presence of the Almighty* — and the reason offered is that *Allah, in His pride*, made the analogy hold, *because, since ibda had not yet appeared, He became Creative through ibda*.

The second claim is the grave error. To say *Allah became Creative through ibda* is to suggest that *before ibda appeared*, Allah was *not yet Creative* — Creativity was a *property acquired* when *ibda came into being*. This runs against strict monotheism in two ways: it implies that *something other than Allah* (namely, *ibda*) came into existence with Allah's Creativity depending on it — *shirk* at the level of structure; and it implies a *temporal interior* to the Origin's essence in which Creativity was *not yet* present.

Al-Kirmani's correction is structural. *The doctrine and belief in monotheism is that what is other than Allah Almighty — and He is the Creative One and the Originator — had no existence at all.* There is no *other-than-Allah* that has independent existence prior to or alongside Him. *And even if it had, ibda is nothing other than the Creator Himself.* *Ibda* is not *something Allah does to or with* an existing reality; *ibda is the Creator Himself under the aspect of His originative act*. The originative act is *not a property acquired*; it *is* the Origin under one of His aspects of relation.

The teaching is continuous with Bāb 5 Fasl 6: *the Creator is the creativity, He is the cause and the effect, the perfect and the complete, the names synonymous with respect to additions*. *Ibda*, *Creator*, *Originator* — these names are *not added attributes* whose acquisition would require a *before-and-after* in the Origin; they are *the same one thing, named under aspects*. The architecture preserves the *non-acquisition* of the divine names: the Creator does not *become* what He is by anything; He *is* what His names name, eternally and without addition.

*We have built upon this the demonstration sufficient to our argument.* Al-Kirmani has, in *Rahat al-'Aql* and in the other treatises of his corpus, defended this teaching at length. The bāb is naming the correction and pointing to the demonstration's location.

## Fasl 8 — Al-Nusra's general approval and the final apophatic correction

The eighth *fasl* records al-Nusra's overall judgment on al-Islah's argument:

> What the author of al-Islah said, and the points on which he differed, is correct, except that what he said — that above the First Effect there is motion and rest — affirms them positively for the First Effect and for what is below it. We have shown that the First Effect is not subject to motion, so motion does not befit it; nor is there stillness for it, because it is other than a body.

Al-Nusra's verdict is largely *approving*: *what the author of al-Islah said ... is correct*. The exception is the single claim *that above the First Effect there is motion and rest* — that is, the projection of motion and stillness *upward beyond the First Effect*. Al-Nusra had already shown (in Fasl 6) that *the First Effect is not subject to motion*. Motion *does not befit it*. *Nor is there stillness for it, because it is other than a body* — stillness, like motion, is a predicate of bodies, and the First Effect is not a body.

The *fasl* tightens the apophatic structure. The First Effect — the First Intellect, the highest *originated* — is *not subject to motion*, *not at rest*, *not a body*. The vocabulary of the natural cosmos (motion, stillness, body) stops at the rank *below* the First Effect; the First Effect, and a fortiori the Origin above it, *cannot be reached by these predicates*. The apophatic gap is the architecture's own internal joint at this rank.

Al-Kirmani does not contest al-Nusra's verdict here. The bāb's argument has converged on the apophatic correction: from al-Islah's *graded ladder* (Fasl 1), through the *not-embodied soul* (Fasl 2-3), through the *correlatives-vs-contraries* distinction (Fasl 1, expanded in 4), through the *analogical understanding* under which *kun*'s two letters point to two relations of cause-and-effect (Fasl 5), through the refusal of motion and stillness above the First Effect (Fasl 6, 8), and through the grave-error correction of *ibda* (Fasl 7), the bāb has been steadily clearing the originative apex of every predicate that the disputants tried to import into it.

## Fasl 9 — Contradictions in bodies, not in spirits

The ninth and final *fasl* is the shortest in the bāb. Al-Nusra opens with a metaphysical principle:

> Contradictions occur in bodies, not in spirits.

The claim is compressed. *Contradictions* (*tanāquḍāt*) — the meeting of opposites — *occur in bodies*, where motion and stillness, hot and cold, generation and corruption, light and dark, can stand against each other. *Not in spirits* — spirits are not subject to the meeting of contraries because they are not under the regime of bodily predicates that admit contraries.

Al-Kirmani's reply takes the principle and refines it:

> Bodies are bodies because they are objects; there is no differentiation among them and no contradiction between them in that respect. Difference and contrast lie in the images by which what animates them is animated — not insofar as they are bodies.

The refinement is important. *Bodies are bodies because they are objects* — they are the *receptacles*, *the things-upon-which* acts are performed. *In that respect* — in their being-objects, their *bodiliness as such* — *there is no differentiation among them and no contradiction between them*. All bodies, *qua* bodies, are the same kind of thing: receptacles. The *contradictions* and *differences* that show up among bodies do not arise from their being bodies; they arise from *the images by which what animates them is animated*. The image is the *form-from-above* that informs the body; the *difference* in bodies is *the difference of the forms* that animate them.

The architecture closes the bāb tightly. Motion and stillness, hot and cold, generation and corruption — all these *contraries* are *predicates of bodies under their animating images*. The contrariety is not *in* the body *as body*; it is *in the image by which the body is animated*. *Spirits*, considered in their essence, do not stand in contrariety, because spirits are not under the regime of image-as-bodily-predicate.

Al-Kirmani records al-Nusra's closing line, which the manuscript marks as imperfectly transmitted:

> And because what is in it is similar to what is already there, the matter follows what he said, if the very speaker were such.

The line is opaque in the manuscript, and the bāb ends there. The architecture, however, is now in place. *Contradictions occur in bodies, not in spirits.* *Difference and contrast lie in the images by which what animates them is animated.* The bāb's compressed final teaching has named where opposition belongs in the architecture: *not at the originative apex, not at the rank of spirits, but at the rank of bodies under their animating forms*.

## What the bāb has taught

Bāb 6 has, in nine *fusūl*, refused two analogies that would have imported the wrong structure into the chain's joints, and clarified what the joints actually are.

*The graded ladder fails.* Al-Islah's proportional analogy runs through *gentleness* and *density* — predicates of *bodies*. The soul is *not embodied*, so the ladder leads upward toward a rung that is not on the ladder. *The dimensional resemblance fails.* Al-Nusra's proposal that *motion divides in three as body divides in three* gets the count wrong (body is divisible into *nine*, not three), confuses *dimensions* with *motions*, and projects *part-homogeneity* (a body-predicate) onto the soul.

*The deep distinction.* Motion and stillness are *contraries* that *cannot coexist*; *hayula* and form are *correlatives* that *cannot exist apart*. The dyads belong to *different logical genera*; analogizing them is a category error. *Hayula* and form, the first emanations, are *one emanation with two aspects* — form as agent, *hayula* as the object in which the action occurs.

*The principled definition of motion.* *Motion is that by which a thing's selfsame state changes — by place, or by quality, or by corruption, or by increase, or by decrease, or by impossibility — and its occurrence is from the mover which is the principle in the thing in which it inheres, naturally and not by width.* The mover is *immanent* in what moves, not *external*.

*The word kun read under analogical understanding.* The two states of the word *point*, by inference, to *two honorable relations* in the Essence — *being a cause* and *being effected*. The motion-that-terminates-in-stillness names the cause-terminating-in-effect relation; the stillness-whose-end-is-motion names the effect-terminating-in-cause relation. *There is no motion or stillness here except by way of analogical understanding.*

*The apophatic claim.* *Above the First Effect there is no kaf, no nun, no motion, and no stillness.* The Qur'anic *laysa ka-mithlihi shay'* is rendered in the technical vocabulary of *ibda* and *first effect*. *The grave error.* To say that *Allah became Creative through ibda* is to imply that *something other than Allah* came into being prior to His Creativity. *Ibda is nothing other than the Creator Himself.* The names of the Origin are *synonymous with respect to additions* — one thing only, named under aspects.

*The location of contrariety.* *Contradictions occur in bodies, not in spirits.* And even in bodies, the contradictions are *not in their being-bodies* but *in the images by which what animates them is animated*. The architecture locates opposition where opposition belongs.

## A figure from the apophatic prayer-tradition

Imam Zayn al-'Abidin (zayn al-aa-bi-deen; the fourth Imam, peace be upon him), in *al-Sahifa al-Sajjadiyya* (as-sa-hee-fah as-saj-jaa-dee-yah; the Psalms of the House of Muhammad), turns again and again to the apophatic register when he addresses Allah. A line in the manner of those supplications can stand here as a close to the bāb:

> O Allah, You spoke the word *kun*, and the heavens and the earth came to be — without Your being a speaker who needed a tongue, without Your saying being a saying that needed motion, without Your stillness being a stillness that needed a body to be still. You are the Creator whose Creativity is You; the Originator whose origination is no addition to Your Essence; the Cause whose causality is no property acquired. To You belongs the *kun*, and to creation belongs the *fa-yakun*. Make us discriminating readers of Your word, that we may not import into Your essence what You have not named there.
> Source: paraphrased after the manner of the supplications in *al-Sahifa al-Sajjadiyya*.

*To You belongs the kun, and to creation belongs the fa-yakun.* The line names the apophatic structure Bāb 6 has been defending: the *kun* is on the side of Allah (the side of *ibda* and pure originative speech), and the *fa-yakun* — the coming-to-be of what is originated — is on the side of creation (the side of motion, stillness, body, image). To confuse the two sides is to import the predicates of *fa-yakun* into *kun* — which is what al-Islah's *kun*-as-cosmological-diagram risked, and what al-Kirmani's *analogical understanding* corrects.

## The bāb's place in the architecture

Bāb 6 closes here. The bāb sits at the architectural seam between the *recipient-focused* bābs (Bāb 5's human, Bāb 4's rational souls, Bāb 3's Soul-and-Spirit) and the *cosmology-focused* bābs that are coming (Bāb 7's divisions of the world, Bāb 8's *qadr* — qadr — and destiny). It is the bāb where the architecture's *internal joints* — motion-stillness, *hayula*-form, *kun*-and-its-letters — are tested under pressure of analogy, and the apophatic correction is reaffirmed at every joint that would otherwise leak.

The bābs ahead will build on this. Bāb 7 maps the *divisions of the world*, locating the human-as-microcosm within a cosmology of the four jewels. Bāb 8 turns to *qadr* and destiny, where the architecture must hold under the most painful theological questions — God's foreknowledge, human freedom, the predicates of cause and effect tested against the lived experience of suffering. Bāb 9 takes up the law of Adam (aa-dam; peace be upon him) and the trusteeship of Nuh (nooh; Noah, peace be upon him), the chain of teaching authority laid out in its prophetic and imamic form. Bāb 10 returns to the critique of *al-Mahsul* on *tawhid* (taw-heed) and the First Originator — the apophatic teaching of Bāb 6 will be drawn upon there as the standard against which *al-Mahsul*'s tawhid-formulations are measured.

Each bāb tests the same architecture against a new question. *Motion, stillness, and the word kun* was the sixth test. The architecture has held: the analogies that would have broken it have been refused; the principled definition of motion has been laid down; the *kun*-analogy has been received under *analogical understanding*; the apophatic claim *above the First Effect there is no kaf, no nun, no motion, and no stillness* has been preserved against every attempt to overrun it; the grave error of treating *ibda* as something *acquired* has been corrected; and the bāb's compressed final teaching has located opposition where opposition belongs — in the images by which what animates bodies is animated, not in spirits, not at the originative apex. The discriminating reader, the one al-Kirmani has been training all along, now knows how to read the chain's interior joints without importing the wrong structure into them — and knows that the Origin remains, beneath every analogy, what Surat al-Shura named: *laysa ka-mithlihi shay'* — there is nothing like Him.
