# The Shari'ah of Adam and the First Speaker

*This chapter covers the first nineteen sub-chapters of* Chapter Nine *of* Kitab al-Riyad, *al-Kirmani's adjudication of the question that opens directly out of* Chapter Eight*. If* al-qada *(judgment, decree) and* al-qadar *(destiny, measure) are* three-rank words *that run the chain from the speaker-Prophet at the head down through the revelation-and-Shariʿah and into the world unfolding in time, then* how does that chain first establish itself at the head of human history*? The question lives at the seam between cosmology and history, and the author of* al-Nusra *has pressed it hard. He concedes that Noah had a Shariʿah; he refuses it for Adam (peace be upon him). Adam, on his reading, was a* Pronouncer *but not a* lawgiver, *one of the* six *speakers in the standard* daʿwa *reckoning, yet without a* shariʿah *of his own. The author of* al-Islah *had affirmed the opposite: Adam had a Shariʿah, the Qur'anic story of the offering in* Surat al-Maʾida *names a* qurban * which is itself a rite of religion, and the universally affirmative verses* And for every nation We have appointed a rite *(Quran 22:34) settle the question on the apparent reading. Al-Kirmani works the dispute across nineteen sub-chapters, taking each of al-Nusra's objections in turn, the* qurban *parable, the raven and the burial of the dead, the verse on prostration to Adam, the silence of the* shariʿah *verse* (He has prescribed for you of the religion that which He enjoined upon Noah*, Quran 42:13), the obligation of the angels, the silent claim that Adam never said* God commands you to prostrate to me*, the* reason *for the necessity of a Shariʿah in the first place, the three souls of the human being and the six gates of necessary ordinances, the claim that the* great cycle *was free of the violations that demand a* shariʿah*, and the contention that the world's structure suffices to signify* tawhid *without sent messengers, and lands the cumulative verdict that Adam IS the founder of a Shariʿah, with Seth (also* Shith*) as his* asas *, and that the chain that runs in time first establishes itself at the head of human history exactly where the chapter opens it: in the* shariʿah *of Adam.*

## Where this chapter picks up

*Chapter Eight* of *al-Riyad* settled the architecture of *qada* and *qadar* as *three-rank words* at the chain's heights, the *speaker-Prophet* who is *judge*, the *revelation-and-Shariʿah* brought out from potency into act by *ta'wil*, and the *world unfolding in time*; the *Pen-Tablet-Writer* architecture for the verse *Indeed, We have created everything according to a measure* (Quran 54:49); the Prophetic word *Flee from Allah's qada to His qadar* read on its *most honorable* meaning as the *speaker-Prophet's flight from the intensity of the preceding support to the composition of the next*; and the *three-position order, decree at the beginning, apportioning in the middle, completion at the end*, anchored to *O reassured soul, return to your Lord, pleased and pleasing* (Quran 89:27–30, Sahih International).

*Chapter Nine* turns from cosmology to history. If the chain that runs in time has *three-rank words* at every joint of its architecture, then the chain has a *first joint*, the *head of human history*, the speaker-Prophet at the opening of the first cycle. *Who is that speaker, and what is his Shariʿah?* The standard *da'wa* reckoning names six speaker-Prophets, Adam, Noah, Abraham, Moses, Jesus, Muhammad (peace and blessings of Allah be upon him), and a seventh awaited *Qa'im*. The chapter's first nineteen sub-chapters work the contested case at the head of the list: Adam. The author of *al-Nusra* presses that Adam had no *shariʿah*, that *one of his sons did not know how to bury his brother until Allah sent a raven*, that the verse of *shariʿah* (Quran 42:13) names Noah, Abraham, Moses, Jesus, and Muhammad but not Adam, that Adam never said *God commands you to obey me*, that *the angels are not assigned and are not obligated by the Shariʿah*, that *if Adam had been the owner of a shariʿah, Allah would have commanded the angels to prostrate themselves through him*, and the *qurban* of the two sons of Adam in *Surat al-Maʾida* (Quran 5:27) is a parable for the *zahir* and the *batin* of the *daʿwa*, not a ritual of an established Shariʿah. The author of *al-Islah* had affirmed the opposite: Adam had a Shariʿah, the *qurban* verse proves it, the universally affirmative verses settle it, and the *speaker* (the *natiq*) cannot be a speaker without a *shariʿah* drawn out among his nation. Al-Kirmani works the nineteen sub-chapters as a *patient, point-by-point adjudication* in favor of *al-Islah* and against *al-Nusra*, with the cumulative verdict at the end: *the Shariʿah of Adam is fixed.*

## Sub-chapters one and two — the offering, the rite, and the first dispute

The author of *al-Islah* opens *Chapter Nine* with the doctrinal claim. *Whoever says that the beginning of the speaker-prophets (peace be upon them) had no Shariʿah is mistaken*, he writes. *He is also mistaken when he counts them among the determined ones (al-muqarrarun, those who copy or renew a prior Shariʿah), because determination occurs only by copying or renewing a Shariʿah that already exists, and the first Pronouncer had no Shariʿah preceding him to abrogate.* The argument runs on definition. *Determination* (in the technical *da'wa* sense) is the act of a speaker who *renews* what an earlier speaker drew, Noah renewing Adam's Shariʿah, Abraham renewing Noah's, and so on. The *first* speaker, by definition, cannot *determine*, he must *originate*. *Adam was the first to draw the lines of religion in command and prohibition. And whoever works to benefit from what the rest of the speaker-prophets drew in their apparent laws, what is inferred from the subtleties (the recommended meanings of the parables, called interpretation, which is the manifestation of the words of the wise), knows that every drawing pronounced, every command, prohibition, and act of servitude, is a law: small or great, few or many.* The cycles, *al-Islah* observes, *vary*: the *shariʿah of Musa* (Moses) has a different fasting from the *shariʿah of 'Isa* (Jesus), the *shariʿah of 'Isa* has more than the *shariʿah of Muhammad* in certain particulars, *rulings in religion vary according to the difference of their cycles and the measure of what they were commanded*. But *every* cycle has *some* ruling. *It is impossible to say that the people of that cycle were in such a state* (without work, benefit, statutes, interpretation, inwardness, or real summons), because *the daʿwa rests on the Shariʿah; interpretation requires the revelation; the inner requires the outer; the meaning requires the parable. If there is no outer, then there is no inner, no call, and no Shariʿah; creation is neglected and the people are left in ignorance.*

The Qur'anic anchor for the affirmation:

> *And recite to them the news of the two sons of Adam in truth, when they offered a sacrifice — and it was accepted from one of them and not accepted from the other.* (Quran 5:27)

The verse, *al-Islah* presses, is decisive on the *apparent* reading. The *qurban* is *one of the laws*, and *the most established of the reasons of the Shariʿah*. Allah has commanded it *in every Shariʿah*, and has made it *the root of asceticism and purity*. The verb-root *nasaka* yields the names: *the sacrifice is called a* mansak **; *a man is called a* mansik * when he is known by religion, piety, and purity*; *the offering is called* nusuk * because it is the purpose of the duties of religion*. And the universally affirmative verse:

> *And for every nation We have appointed a rite, that they may pronounce the name of Allah over what He has provided them of cattle.* (Quran 22:34)

If *every* nation has a rite, then *Adam's nation* — at the head of human history — *also has a rite*. And if Adam's nation has a rite, Adam has a *Shariʿah*. The *qurban* is the *first cause of obedience, disobedience, disbelief, faith, command, and prohibition — and it is the first of the laws of the Shariʿah.*

The author of *al-Nusra* enters with the refutation. The *qurban* verse, he argues, does not establish what *al-Islah* claims. The verse is a *parable* that *must be interpreted by the wise*. The *qurban* in this verse is *not* the slaughtering of sheep and camels; it is *the establishment of the proofs from beneath their hands, and the establishment of those who will take their place when they leave this world*, that is, the *qurban* is the *daʿwa-theological act* of designating an *asas* or a *wali* who will *carry the truth forward* after the speaker has gone. The proof, *al-Nusra* says, is what follows the verse in the surah: *one of his sons did not know what to do until Allah sent a raven to scratch in the earth to show him how to bury his brother*. If *burial* were *among the laws* and Adam had a *Shariʿah*, the son would not have needed a raven to teach him. He would have *known the rite*. He would have *placed the body in its proper place or carried it according to the rite*. The fact that he did not know, that he had to be *shown* by a raven, is *proof* that Adam had no *Shariʿah* at all.

Al-Kirmani enters with the verdict. *The Shariʿah of Adam has been proven by what the author of al-Islah argued in support of it, and what the author of al-Nusra has mentioned does not invalidate it*. The reason is precise. *Al-Islah* did not argue for the Shariʿah of Adam *by the interpretation of the qurban verse*; he argued for it *by its apparent meaning* and by the *universally affirmative* verses. *Al-Nusra*'s move, to *interpret* the *qurban* verse as a parable about the *asas*, does not refute the *apparent* reading; it merely offers a *parallel reading*. The *apparent reading* of the *qurban* verse stands on its own as evidence of a *ritual offering* that establishes the *first cause* of religion's laws. The *universally affirmative verse*, *And for every nation We have appointed a rite*, settles the matter independently of the *qurban* verse's interpretation. *The matter in the interpretation of this verse is not as he thought*, al-Kirmani writes; *rather it contradicts the statement of the reformer.* The reformer (*al-Islah*) *did not argue for proving the Shariʿah by the interpretation of this verse, but by its outward sense*. The *apparent* and the *batini* readings stand side by side; *al-Nusra*'s offer of an *interpretive* reading does not erase the *apparent* one. *The statement of the author of al-Islah is confirmed by the law of Adam, and the response of the author of al-Nusra does not contradict it; the Shariʿah remains fixed as it has been proven.*

## Sub-chapters three and four — the offering as a manifest duty of religion

*Al-Islah* presses the argument further. The *qurban* is *among the manifest duties of religion and Shariʿah*. *Al-Nusra* had insisted that the verse must be read on its *batini* sense alone — that the *qurban* in this place is *not* a slaughtered animal but the *establishment of the proofs from beneath their hands*. *Al-Islah*'s rejoinder: *the verse does not follow the course of a parable whose interpretation must be sought while abandoning its apparent meaning*. The *zahir* and the *batin* are *both* operative — and the *zahir* is itself a *manifest duty of religion*. If that is the case, *then all of the apparent senses in the verse remain operative, and the obligations of the Shariʿah of Adam (peace be upon him) are established*. This is the doctrine of the *two worships* settled in *Chapter Seven* and reaffirmed here: the *zahir* is the *body of the religion*, the *batin* is its *spirit*; *he who keeps the body and abandons the spirit, his religion is dead; he who claims the spirit and abandons the body, his religion is a lie*. The *qurban* verse stands on *both* readings, and the *zahir* reading is *itself* sufficient to settle the question of Adam's *Shariʿah*.

The third sub-chapter — *al-Islah*'s argument from *the establishment of arguments from those under their hands and from those who take their place* — works the same doctrine. If a speaker is to be a speaker at all, he must *call* his nation to something. *Boundaries are established to summon to what the Speaker proclaims, and to set forth what serves the interest of those invited to God in this world, in religion, in the Hereafter, and in the beginning.* If Adam *prescribed nothing, and showed none of the laws of worship, whether small or great*, then he would have *no arguments to establish and no preachers to send forth*. The *speakership* itself — the *natiq*-rank — requires *something to which he calls them*. And *with the affirmation of the author of al-Nusra by interpretation, he has established arguments, and the Shariʿah and the obligations are binding, for the Shariʿah belongs to Adam upon him. Peace be upon you.* Even *al-Nusra*'s *batini* reading of the *qurban* verse — *the establishment of those who will take their place* — *itself* affirms an *argument*, *proofs*, and *those-who-take-the-place*; and *that* affirmation is itself *the Shariʿah*. *Al-Nusra* refutes himself.

## Sub-chapter four — Adam as teacher and guide, the verse of one nation

Sub-chapter four works the *prophetic function* directly.

> *Mankind was one nation, so Allah sent the Prophets as bearers of good tidings and warners, and He sent down the Book with them in truth, to judge between people in matters wherein they differed.* (Quran 2:213, Sahih International)

*When the need for the prophets was for the education of the nation, so that it might be happy with him in the Hereafter and in this world, and be guided to its interests in this world and in religion, then Adam, being a prophet, must have been a teacher and a guide.* The argument is by definition again. *Prophet* = *teacher*. *Teacher* = *one who has something to teach*. *Something to teach* = *the laws of worship* = *the Shariʿah*. If Adam was a prophet, Adam taught; if Adam taught, Adam had something to teach; if Adam had something to teach, Adam had a *Shariʿah*. *The truth of this is confirmed by Allah's saying.* The verse continues: *God did not send him except to bear good news, to warn, and to judge by the Book. Good news, warning, judgment, and arbitration occur only by means of the obligations and prohibitions which constitute the Shariʿah, so it is established.*

The doctrine al-Kirmani is unfolding has its canonical anchor in the voice the *da'wa* tradition treats as the *ta'wil*-anchor at the rank of the *imam*: Imam Ali ibn Abi Talib, the Commander of the Believers, in whose person the rank of *the foundation* (the *asas*) was carried after the Prophet, and whose first sermon in *Nahj al-Balagha* (compiled by al-Sharif al-Radi c. 1009 CE) names the *prophets as teachers* in precisely the architectural sense the chapter is settling.

> *So God sent Muhammad with the truth, when the rope of the world was on the verge of breaking, when its inhabitants were certain of its passing away, when its time grew dim, its signs were ready to leave it, and its people were nearer to dispersion and breaking apart. He sent His prophets in succession that they might exact the pledge from creatures concerning His Lordship, remind them of His forgotten favours, present to them the proof through preaching, unearth for them the buried treasures of the intellects, show them the signs of dominion, and inform them of how the prophets had been raised up before them.* — Imam Ali ibn Abi Talib (peace be upon him), Nahj al-Balagha, Sermon 1 (Khutba al-Wasf, on the creation and the prophets)

The Commander of the Believers names exactly the *prophetic function* the chapter is defending. *To exact the pledge from creatures concerning His Lordship* is the *primordial covenant* — the *fitra*-rank from which Adam's Shariʿah follows. *To remind them of His forgotten favours* is the *teaching* function. *To present to them the proof through preaching* is the *establishment of arguments* the previous sub-chapter argued cannot occur without a *Shariʿah*. *To unearth for them the buried treasures of the intellects* is the *raising-by-degrees* that the *daʿwa* names as the work of the *hudud*. *In succession* — *the prophets sent one after another* — names *exactly the chain of cycles* whose head al-Kirmani is fixing at Adam. Sermon 1's panorama of the prophetic chain is the canonical reading of Quran 2:213 at the rank of the *imam* who carried Adam's Shariʿah forward through Muhammad — and the rank from which the chapter's own doctrine of the *first joint* is articulated.

The *pledge from creatures concerning His Lordship* that the Commander of the Believers names as the prophets' first work has its own Qur'anic anchor at the very seam of human history. The verse names the *taking of the covenant* from Adam's progeny before they ever stood in the world:

> *And when your Lord took from the children of Adam, from their loins, their descendants and made them testify of themselves, saying, "Am I not your Lord?" They said, "Yes, we have testified." This, lest you should say on the day of Resurrection, "Indeed, we were of this unaware."* (Quran 7:172, Sahih International)

The verse settles the *primordial covenant* at exactly the rank the chapter is fixing. The *covenant of Am I not your Lord* is *prior to Adam's nation's entering the world* — it is taken from *the descendants in the loins of the children of Adam*, before any of them are sent forth into time. And the *prophets' work*, in Imam Ali's Sermon 1, is to *exact the pledge from creatures concerning His Lordship* — that is, *to remind them of the covenant already taken*. Adam's Shariʿah is the *first instrument of reminding* among the children of Adam who, *but for the work of the speaker*, would say *on the day of Resurrection: "Indeed, we were of this unaware."* The *teaching of the names* (Quran 2:33) and the *establishment of the caliph* (Quran 2:30) are the *executive form* of the *covenant of Am I not your Lord*; the verse of Quran 7:172 is the *constitutional form*. The two stand together as the *covenant taken* and the *prophets sent*, and Adam stands at the seam between them.

## Sub-chapters five and six — the raven, the burial, and the deep ta'wil of the two sons

Sub-chapter five returns to *al-Nusra*'s opening volley. *If he had read this verse to the end, he would have said that Adam had nothing belonging to him. One of his sons did not know what to do until the moment came when Allah sent a raven scratching the earth to show him how to bury his brother. If the burial of the dead in any manner were among the laws, and if Adam had a Shariʿah, he would not have concealed it from his sons. So this is a statement issued by someone without inquiry or examination.*

Al-Kirmani turns the verse against the objector. *The verse contains no indication that the son of Adam did not carve open the carcass of a crow*, that is, *the verse does not say the son did not know how to bury*; the verse says *Allah sent a raven to scratch the ground, to show him how to hide his brother's corpse. He said: "Woe to me! Am I unable to be like this crow and hide my brother's corpse?" And he became one of the regretful*. The verse *mentions that he saw him, but there is no evidence that he buried him*. The whole inference, that the son did not know burial, rests on a *reading-in* that the verse itself does not authorize.

But the deeper move is the *ta'wil* of the whole story. *Both the opponent and the guardian believe that in the worship of Allah Almighty there is something which is His sacrifice, by which one draws closer to Allah Almighty.* The *opponent* holds that *worship is nothing but the apparent, namely from works, and that salvation is only in it, and this will be by belief and sincerity in him who takes the place of the prophet*. The *guardian* (the *wali*, the *asas*) holds that *the worship of Allah Almighty is only in the outward, which is the work, and in the inward, which is knowledge, and that salvation is only in both of them together, and that the one who stands in the place of the prophet is from the feet of the Messenger and his family*. The two sons of Adam, in the *daʿwa*'s settled *ta'wil*, are the *wali* and the *opponent*, the *batin*-holder and the *zahir-only* holder. *They each argued before the Prophet, and each of them explained his belief concerning what he worships.* The Speaker accepted the *wali*'s belief and *did not accept from the other*. The *opponent*, *in the heart of the opponent there was the conviction that he had overcome the guardian, and he said to him: "I will kill you, to push you away from this belief"*. The *wali* answered: *"Allah accepts only from the pious," meaning that the Guardian of God, who is the Messenger, takes no one's place, and is not satisfied with anyone, and no one believes, except among the pious who combine knowledge and action.*

*So the opponent deliberately challenged and stabbed the guardian, while not knowing anything.* The *killing* is the *daʿwa*'s technical term for the *opponent's annulment of the wali's rank in the world*, the *opponent's claim* to take the guardian's place. The story carries the doctrine of the *two worships* directly forward from *Chapter Seven*: the *opponent's killing* of the *wali* is the *zahir*'s claim to abandon the *batin*. *And the matter, the guardian said to him: "Allah accepts only from the pious."*

Then comes the raven. *So Allah sent a raven to scratch the earth, that is, when the time was prolonged and it was time to act, the guardian wanted to establish the argument against the opponent, so that he would be separated from the world after a reminder.* The *raven* is *the one in charge of the Speaker, who is the one who takes the place of Allah, sent a man from among his companions to explain to him his matter and his status, and what Allah Almighty has of a secret in His silence regarding the seeking of his right.* And the man is named. *He is the man who was sent by the guardian of God for that purpose, Muhammad ibn Abi Bakr, for he is among those who are specially related to the guardianship from the side of religion, and to the opponent from the side of lineage.* The *daʿwa*'s *ta'wil* of the *raven scratching the ground* is the *historical type* of *Muhammad ibn Abi Bakr*, son of the first caliph, devotee of *Ali ibn Abi Talib*, straddling the line between the *zahir*-people and the *batin*-people, sent by *Ali* to *recall the opponent to the wali's rank* before the time was sealed. *So Muhammad did what he was sent for, so that the opponent might be alerted to his crime, and led to abide by his mandate. So the second opponent prevailed over his opinion and prevented him from what he wanted to do. And he was sorry for the guardian, and said to himself: "O woe to me! Am I unable to be like this crow?", that is, "I wish I were like this man," meaning Muhammad his son, who is a stranger to the guardian, and his example in keeping God's covenant, religion, and concealment of the secrets of the guardian of God.*

The *batini* reading of the *qurban-and-raven* story has *carried both readings forward at once*: the *apparent* offering remains a manifest rite of the Shariʿah (settling the *Adam-has-a-Shariʿah* question); the *interpretive* reading of *the two sons as wali-and-opponent* and *the raven as Muhammad ibn Abi Bakr* settles the *daʿwa-theological* layer that *al-Nusra* had pressed. *Al-Nusra has nothing in his protest by means of this verse, except the saying of the author of the Reform, in which his duty is the Shariʿah obligations of Adam.*

## Sub-chapters seven and eight — burial as one of many dispositions of the dead

Sub-chapter seven works the *narrative-evidence* side. *It has been mentioned that laws, dispensations, obligations, and customary practices may increase and decrease according to the time in which the Speaker arises, and according to what he needs for the reformation of the Ummah*. And *narrations have come down*, in the *daʿwa*'s transmitted reports, *that washing the dead, embalming, shrouding, and burying them are from what Adam prescribed*. The *narrative tradition itself* names Adam as the *prescriber* of the rites of the dead. If those narrations stand, then the *qurban-and-raven* verse does *not* follow *the course of parables whose interpretation must be sought*; its *apparent meaning* stands operative; and *the Shariʿah of Adam is established beyond dispute*.

Sub-chapter eight presses the *logical* objection. *Burying the dead is not among the things whose obligations have increased, which are purity, prayer, zakat, and the other actions*. The *core obligations* of the Shariʿah, the *qibla*, the *zakat*, the *sajda*, the *ruku'*, are *increased* and *decreased* across cycles, but they are *always present*. *Burial*, on the other hand, is *one of several dispositions of the dead*, *by burning with fire, or throwing into water, or carrying to a cave or desert, or burying in the ground, or feeding to the wild beasts*. If burial is a *practice of the Shariʿah*, it must be the *ranked* practice that *displaces the others*, and the verse-evidence shows that *it is the ranked practice*, since *the raven taught the son the burial-rite specifically*. *If he refrains from doing so*, *if Adam's Shariʿah is the practice of burying the dead*, *then the Shariʿah is proven for Adam.*

## Sub-chapters nine and ten — the prostration of the angels, and the verse to Israel

Sub-chapter nine opens *al-Nusra*'s next move. *Adam was not the owner of a law, because if he had been the owner of a law, Allah would have commanded the angels to prostrate themselves through him.* The argument: a *Shariʿah-holder* is one *through whom* command is issued; the verse of Adam's prostration shows the angels commanded *to* Adam, not *through* him; therefore Adam is *prostrated-to*, not a *Shariʿah-holder*.

Al-Kirmani turns the objection. *The author of al-Nusra has bound himself by saying that what would establish Adam as the owner of a Shariʿah is that Allah commanded the angels to prostrate, even if this is apparently not from the side of Adam.* The very fact that the angels are commanded to prostrate is the proof. *The command, it is impossible for him [Adam] that they prostrate to him unless he is a Mustafa* (Chosen); *and the command to prostrate to him is impossible unless he is a Mustafa. And it has been proven that he is a Mustafa, as Allah says: "Allah has chosen Adam and Noah"* (Quran 3:33). *If he is Mustafa, then it is impossible for Allah to command those who are without the Mustafa except through the Mustafa. So Allah commanded the angels to prostrate to Adam, it was only through him. And if it was through him, then the law is proven for him.*

The argument is precise. The verse of Adam's prostration *does* show the angels commanded *through* Adam, because the *Mustafa*-rank itself is the *commanding rank*. Allah does not command the angels *bypassing* the *Mustafa*; Allah commands the angels *through* the *Mustafa*'s presence among them. The very prostration *is itself* an act of *Shariʿah*-bound obligation issued *through* Adam's Mustafa-rank.

Sub-chapter ten works the parallel verse to the Children of Israel.

> *Then Allah commanded us to prostrate to Adam by saying, "Prostrate to Adam," as He commanded the Children of Israel when He said, "O Children of Israel, remember My grace which I have bestowed upon you. And fulfill My covenant, and I shall fulfill your covenant, and have awe of Me alone. And believe in what I have sent down, confirming what is with you, and be not the first to disbelieve in it... Establish the prayer, give the zakat, and bow with those who bow."* (Quran 2:40–43)

The verse to the Children of Israel makes *no explicit mention of an intermediary*, Allah seems to speak *directly* to them. And yet the *daʿwa*'s settled doctrine is that *the command was conveyed through the Prophet Muhammad*. If the verse to Israel can carry a command *through* the Prophet without naming him in the verse itself, then the verse of Adam's prostration can carry a command *through* Adam without naming him in the verse itself. *If the two verses were placed in the chapter of being non-indicative of a single authentic intermediary, this would be impossible; for the command of Allah Almighty to the Children of Israel, although outwardly devoid of any mention of an intermediary, was only by means of Muhammad. And if that is impossible, then Adam too is the author of a Shariʿah, and the Shariʿah is established for him.* The parallel is exact. Either *both* verses lack an intermediary (which the *daʿwa*'s settled doctrine refuses), or *both* verses carry an intermediary in their *Mustafa*-rank. Adam is *Mustafa*; Muhammad is *Mustafa*; the verses are parallel; the conclusion is the same.

## Sub-chapter eleven — the angels as bearers of assignment

The author of *al-Nusra* presses the next objection. *The angels are not assigned and are not obligated by the Shariʿah, because they do not disobey Allah in the twinkling of an eye; rather, they glorify the praise of their Lord and do what they are commanded.*

Al-Kirmani distinguishes. *The angels who were charged with prostration, if they were not among those who bear assignment, they would not have been commanded to prostrate to Adam, nor would He have said to them, "Bow down to him." And the angels who bear no assignment are those who have never been in the abode of the body, nor have they stood forth into the realm of deed. They were the foremost in the abode of eternity.* The *highest angels*, the *First Intellect, the universal Soul, the al-Jadd*, *the al-Fath*, *the al-Khayal*, in the standard *daʿwa* names for the *high limits*, *bear no assignment*; they are the *abode-of-eternity* limits, and they do not stand in the *abode of deed*. The *angels of the prostration* are *not* these. They are *the angels who were appointed over the heavens, who dwelt in the house of nature and exercised dominion through the virtues that bear resemblance to those in the abode of eternity, the creative and prophetic angels*. The *natural-world angels* are *assigned*; the *abode-of-eternity angels* are not. *As for prostration and bowing, these belong to the duties of worship and deeds, which exist only in the natural world. Those who are charged with establishing the two acts of worship are assigned to it so that, by means of them, they may receive the increase and the blessings of happiness with which Allah exalts their station, and they thereby attain the perfection that earns them eternity and survival.* The *prostration* is itself a *raising-by-degrees* of the natural-world angels through the *two worships* (the *zahir* and the *batin*) toward the *perfection that earns them eternity*. The *commissioning of the angels* and the *call upon them to act* are themselves acts of *Shariʿah* directed at *the assigned natural-world angels through Adam*.

## Sub-chapter twelve — the silent "God commands you"

Sub-chapter twelve closes another of *al-Nusra*'s lines. *He said: If the prostration of the angels to Adam had been their law, Adam would have said: "God commands you to prostrate and to obey me," and he would have striven against those who disobeyed him in the command of God. This too applies to all pronouncements.*

Al-Kirmani turns the parallel again. *It is not the case that, because Adam did not say "God commands you to prostrate to me and to obey me," prostration must therefore not be an obligatory ordinance. For the Prophet did not say to the Children of Israel "God commands you to establish prayer and give me zakat," and yet there is no evidence that prayer, zakat, and all that he commanded them are not among the duties of Shariʿah. The laws are established by the fact that their bringer was a striver; otherwise, Jesus would not have had a law sanctioned for him, since he did not strive, and this is not valid.* The argument from *Adam-did-not-say-the-words* would, if extended, eliminate the *Shariʿah* of Jesus and a portion of the Prophet's own teaching. The *Shariʿah-rank* is settled by the *Mustafa-rank*, not by the *speaker's verbal formula*.

## Sub-chapter thirteen — the verse of Shariʿah and the act of teaching the names

Sub-chapter thirteen takes up *al-Nusra*'s strongest textual move. *Allah Almighty did not mention in the Qur'an that Adam had a sending, nor did He mention his being sent as He did with others, when He said: "We sent Noah." Nor did He mention him in the verse of the Shariʿah, when He said*:

> *He has prescribed for you of the religion that which He enjoined upon Noah, and that which We have revealed to you, and that which We enjoined upon Abraham, Moses, and Jesus, to establish the religion and not be divided therein.* (Quran 42:13)

The verse names *Noah, Abraham, Moses, Jesus, and Muhammad*, *but not Adam*. If the verse is the *roster* of *Shariʿah-holders*, and Adam is *not on it*, then Adam has *no Shariʿah*.

Al-Kirmani answers in two registers. First, the verse is not a *roster*. *The author of al-Nusra is not fair in his objection. Let it not be hoped that the author of al-Islah will say in his book: As for Adam, he is mentioned in the Qur'an, along with his followers and his people the angels, who were commanded to prostrate to him, and from whom they learned the names according to what Allah Almighty commanded, saying to him: "O Adam, inform them of their names."* (Quran 2:33). *This act of informing is greater than mere sending, for the sending of messengers entails their being made the successors of Allah in His land, by which Allah refrains from realizing, and Allah Almighty wrote in His Book that He made him a Caliph on earth, to whom its people would return for what they need in the matter of their religions.* The verse of *teaching the names*, *O Adam, inform them of their names*, and the verse of the *caliphate*, *I am going to place a caliph on the earth* (Quran 2:30), *are themselves the verses of Adam's Shariʿah-rank*. They are *not less* than the verse of Noah's sending; they are *greater*, because *the act of teaching* is *the foundational act of every Shariʿah*.

The Prophet's tradition itself frames Adam's special honors in a passage that the *daʿwa* reads as the canonical witness to the *Mustafa*-rank Adam carries at the head of the chain. In the great intercession hadith preserved in *Sahih al-Bukhari*, mankind on the Day of Resurrection will turn first to Adam, and the people will name to him precisely the honors al-Kirmani has just enumerated:

> *They will say: "O Adam, you are the father of mankind. Allah created you with His own Hand, and made the angels prostrate before you, and taught you the names of all things. Will you not intercede for us before your Lord? Do you not see what we are suffering?"* — (Sahih al-Bukhari, Book 65, Hadith 4712; narrated by Anas ibn Malik)

The people of every nation, in the eschatological scene, *acknowledge Adam by exactly the three signs the chapter has been pressing*. He is *the father of mankind* — the *head of the cycle*. He is *created by Allah's own Hand* — the *Mustafa*-rank that grounds his prerogative to issue command-and-prohibition. He is the one *taught the names of all things* (Quran 2:33), the *foundational act of teaching* that *is* the *foundational act of every Shariʿah*. And the *angels prostrated to him* — the verse of prostration (Quran 2:34) that sub-chapter nine has just settled. The Prophet's intercession hadith *names the three signs as the recognized signs by which Adam holds rank* at the head of the chain. The *daʿwa*'s settled doctrine and the Prophetic tradition meet here: *Adam's honors are Adam's Shariʿah-rank*, named by the same three signs in both registers.

Second, the *roster-argument* refutes itself by analogy. *If it is permissible for Adam to be excluded from the group of the masters of laws on the grounds that his name is not mentioned with the five, then it is likewise permissible because he is not mentioned among those to whom revelation was sent, when the Almighty said*:

> *We have revealed to you as We revealed to Noah and the prophets after him, and We revealed to Abraham, Ishmael, Isaac, Jacob, the tribes, Jesus, Job, Jonah, Aaron, and Solomon.* (Quran 4:163)

The verse names a *long roster* of *receivers of revelation*, *and Adam is not on it*. If the *Quran 42:13 roster* excludes Adam from the Shariʿah-holders, the *Quran 4:163 roster* must exclude Adam from the *revelation-receivers*, *and that is impossible*, because *Adam was taught the names* and *Adam was commissioned as caliph*. The two rosters cannot *both* be exhaustive; the inference from *Adam's-not-being-named-in-one* fails. *So the saying is the saying of the author of al-Islah, taken from him.*

## Sub-chapter fourteen — the reason for the necessity of a Shariʿah

Sub-chapter fourteen turns to the *underlying reason*. *Al-Nusra* presses one more time. *We must also know the reason for the existence of Shariʿah, so that it may be true. We can then say that Adam did not have a law.* And his proposed *reason* for the necessity of a Shariʿah: *the neglect of the limits, their disruption, and the pursuit of their opposites by creation*. Since (he says) *the world that was before Adam and his people were two neighbors on the boundary, and none of them violated a single limit* (as the angels declared: *Will You place in it one who corrupts it and sheds blood, while we glorify You with praise and sanctify You?* (Quran 2:30)), there was *no reason* for a Shariʿah in Adam's time, and so Adam had no Shariʿah.

Al-Kirmani gives the deeper reading. *The reason for the necessity of the existence of the Shariʿah is different from what the author of al-Nusra said when he claimed that the limits are neglected, that they are disrupted, and that creation follows opposites. That is not the ground of obligation for a Shariʿah, nor does it establish it.* The real reason is found *in our book known as* al-Masabih fi al-Imama *(*Lamps on the Imamate*)*. *The closest thing to the need of the people is first their reliance, in their survival, on what guards them against destruction, the ordinances by which blood is preserved, and by which their hearths and wealth are fortified. By them they are saved in matters of necessity that arise among them. If these ordinances did not exist, their affairs would come to a halt and they would destroy one another: the strong, in the absence of restraint, would tyrannize through his rage and through what he holds of money and other goods, and the fires of strife would be kindled among them.* The *Shariʿah* is not *contingent* on prior violation; it is *constitutive* of *the very possibility of human community*. *To refrain from this matter in wisdom, the Almighty Maker must enact what guards them from destruction constantly, which is the Shariʿah, if its existence is necessary.*

The doctrine al-Kirmani sketches here in epitome is laid out at full breadth in his own treatise, named in the next line. *Al-Masabih fi al-Imama* (Lamps on the Imamate) — referenced internally at *al-Riyad* Bāb 9 Fasl 14 — is the book in which al-Kirmani settles the *Imamate as the institution that guards the limits* against the destructive tendency of the soul. The doctrine that runs through it is the doctrine the chapter is here pressing in shorter form:

> *The Imamate is the standing of the limits in the world; without the limit-keeper the limits do not stand, and without the limits the soul destroys what it has been given. The Lawgiver's appointment of the Imam is one act with the appointment of the Shariʿah, for the limits without the keeper are letters without a reciter, and the keeper without the limits is a tongue without speech. The Imamate is, accordingly, not posterior to the founding of religion but constitutive of it: the speaker-Prophet and the foundation (the asas) at the head of every cycle are the limit-and-the-keeper made one institution at the head of human history.* — al-Kirmani, al-Masabih fi al-Imama (the Imamate as constitutive of religion)

The reading lands the chapter's central architectural claim: the *speaker-Prophet* at the head of the cycle is *one institution* with the *foundation* (the *asas*) who keeps what the speaker draws. The *Shariʿah of Adam* and the *foundation of Seth* are not two separate acts of religion's founding; they are *the limit-and-the-keeper made one institution at the head of human history*. The chapter's later sub-chapter on the *tent at the qibla* and *Seth as Adam's foundation* is the *narrative-historical* form of what *al-Masabih fi al-Imama* settles in *systematic-doctrinal* form.

> *The Shariʿah is not the response to a fall; it is the architecture by which the soul, which would otherwise destroy itself by the powers within it, is made fit for the worship of Allah. The Lawgiver's enacting of the limits is one act with the creating of the soul that bears them, the limits and the soul are the two faces of the one ordinance.* — Imam al-Mu'izz li-Din Allah Ma'add ibn Isma'il, *Ta'wil al-Shari'a* (The Interpretation of the Shari'a)

## Sub-chapter fifteen — the three souls and the six gates

Sub-chapter fifteen unfolds the *anthropological architecture* of why the Shariʿah is necessary. *The souls that belong to human beings each do not refrain from one another, and from these acts that are indispensable to the ordinances which the community uses to silence the cessation of their dealings. By these ordinances they secure one another, since the ordinances are necessary.*

The architecture: *Human beings have three souls.*

The *rational soul*, *which has discrimination and thought — it knows that it has a Creator and naturally submits to Him, and from this draws near to Him*. The *rational soul's* drive *toward* the Creator demands the *ordinances of drawing-near* — the rites of worship, the *qurban*, the *sajda*, the *ruku'*. The *rational soul* is the *bearer* of the *positive-Shariʿah* obligations.

The *irascible soul*, *the soul that he possesses, by which he is angered when he is angered, and which loves to subdue, conquer, take revenge, oppress, kill, and deceive*. The irascible soul demands a *restraining ordinance*. *He is compelled to enact an ordinance by which he is restrained with great force, so that they may be separated from one another, and the rights of the oppressed may be taken from the aggressor by force, whether in the body by retribution or by other means.* The *irascible soul* is the *bearer* of the *restraining-Shariʿah* obligations, the laws of *qisas*, of *jihad*, of the *holding of the strong from oppressing the weak*.

The *lustful soul*, *from which he seeks refuge and seeks marriage as a necessity*. The lustful soul demands the *ordinances of marriage*, to *remove the cessation arising from the jealousy among them that would lead them to death*; and the *ordinances of property*, *they must be restrained by ordinances by which they trust one another against cheating, cunning, and deceit, which would otherwise lead them to domination and fighting.*

The *six gates through which the categories of necessity run*:

1. *Retribution* (the *qisas* — the law of recompense for bodily harm).
2. *Marriage*.
3. *Inheritance and division of property*.
4. *The ordinance for the absent and the dead* (the law of testimony, of guardianship, of the rights of those who cannot speak for themselves).
5. *The extraction of rights and their return to their masters* (the law of judgment between disputants — *they are compelled to work; for treachery and cunning are by nature, and ordinances are needed by which the plaintiff and the defendant run their course in extracting the truth for its owner*).
6. *The ordinance of the dead* (the law of *what to do with the body*, *death is necessary among them, and they cannot do without a means of dealing with the absence of the dead: either by burial, burning, or being cast away in a friendly manner, otherwise harm would come, and he is compelled to adopt one of these actions*).

*These are six gates through which the categories of necessity run, beneath which lie the kinds whose existence is necessary among human beings without fail.* And on top of these six *necessary* ordinances stand the *non-necessary* ones, *the duties of worshipping Allah, may He be exalted, and drawing closer to Him through knowing His limits, by which perfection is attained*. These *fall under the category of drawing closer to Allah*. *All these ordinances belong to the Shariʿah, and it is necessary to enact them first and to inscribe them among human beings at the beginning of their existence.* And if these ordinances are *necessary at the beginning of human existence*, then *Adam*, *who was at the beginning of human existence*, *had to have enacted them*. *If these ordinances existed in the time of Adam, who was one of the human beings, and his angels were among them, then the laws of Islam are established for him.* The *anthropology of the three souls and the six gates* is itself *the reason for the necessity of a Shariʿah for Adam*. The Shariʿah is not contingent on the *post-fall* condition of human beings; it is constitutive of *human-being-as-such*.

## Sub-chapter sixteen — those before Adam, and the necessity of the work of worship

Sub-chapter sixteen takes up the *implicit corollary*. If the *six gates* of necessary ordinances are constitutive of *human-being-as-such*, and there were human beings *before* Adam (as the *daʿwa*'s settled doctrine of cycles affirms), then those people too must have had the *six gates*. *Whoever was before Adam, his ruling is the ruling of those who are at the end of the seventh cycle, for example, in a manner that is not free of the necessary ordinances we have mentioned.* The *pre-Adamic* people had the six gates. *He was free, however, of the ordinances that fall under the genus of worship in drawing nearer to Allah Almighty, those which we have said may increase at one time and decrease at another, and by which the second perfection is attained.* What the *pre-Adamic* people *lacked* was the *positive worship*, the *qurban*, the *sajda*, the *ruku'*, the *zakat*. *So Allah sent Adam to teach them and others, for unless they were taught, they would fall short, even though they are distinguished above others by virtue, and they would fall short of the degree of perfection by leaving off the work of worship. So he prescribed, believed, and called for the two acts of worship in knowledge and deed, and some of them followed and obeyed, while some of them abstained and turned back upon the previous six at the beginning of the cycles. This is the order of those people.* Adam's *sending* is the *bringing of the positive worship* to people who already had the *necessary restraining ordinances*. His Shariʿah is the *positive-Shariʿah*, the *worship of Allah* added to the *necessary* social ordinances.

## Sub-chapter seventeen — Noah's establishment of Shariʿah, and the parallel for Adam

Sub-chapter seventeen presses the same point through the *Noah-parallel*. *Al-Nusra* affirms that *Noah had to establish it, meaning the Shariʿah, because before his cycle they played at violating the limits and following opposites, such as al-Wadd, al-Suwa', al-Yaghuth* (the names of the idols of Noah's people in Quran 71:23). *So it was obligatory for Noah to establish it, so that whoever perished would perish with knowledge, and whoever lived would live with knowledge.*

Al-Kirmani turns the parallel. *This statement does not entail that Adam must have no law; rather, he too must have duties and a Shariʿah. For it has been established as obligatory that the role of violating the limits and following opposites, and violation only falls to one who follows another by abandoning his command and his ordinance, and the violation was proved by the saying that the ordinances became the Shariʿah, and by which the violation occurred to Adam. So the Shariʿah is established for him.* The argument: *violation-of-the-limits* presupposes the *limits themselves*. The *Wadd-and-Suwa* people of Noah's time *violated* limits that *were already established*, and they were established by the *prior speaker*, who is *Adam*. If there were no *limits in Adam's time*, there could be no *violation in Noah's time*. The Noah-parallel is the *strongest evidence* that Adam had a Shariʿah.

## Sub-chapter eighteen — the tent at the qibla

Sub-chapter eighteen surfaces the *narrative tradition* of *Adam's first rites*. *Allah Almighty commanded Adam to set up a tent. He brought it down from Paradise and made it his qibla and the qibla of his son. Then Seth built a place after him — the Shariʿah, its wisdom, the collection of revelation and interpretation therein, the establishment of its branches and origins, and the arbitration of its obligations.* The *first qibla* in human history is *Adam's tent*; the *first foundation* (*asas*) is *Seth*; the *first sequence of Shariʿah-wisdom-revelation-interpretation* is the *Adam-Seth chain*. The *narrative tradition* itself *names* the *first Shariʿah* as Adam's, and *names its foundation* as Seth. *The saying of the author of al-Islah is established — that Adam has a law and ordinances like the others among the pronouncements.*

## Sub-chapter nineteen — monotheism through the world's structure, and the inversion at the high limits

Sub-chapter nineteen takes up *al-Nusra*'s last move in the first half of the chapter. The world's structure *itself* — *the systems and wonders of His wisdom, what lies in the composition of the world of bodies, the composition of bodies, and the pairing of opposite natures in the different bodies* — is *itself* an indication of *monotheism* (*tawhid*). Why, then, should Adam need to *teach* the *tawhid*? The world *itself* teaches it. And the *signs and deeds* that the *speakers* would bring — *the elaborate and intricate actions of the world are wondrous, and souls sing of deeds and signs* — would *cast many of creation from monotheism into anthropomorphism* (which is *exceedingly impossible*). *The structure of the world and its systems in the chapter of signifying monotheism* is *indispensable to the station of works; and the situation of deeds and signs has removed many of creation from monotheism and cast them into anthropomorphism.* The *bare world* is a *clearer* sign of *tawhid* than the *speakers' sent revelation*; the *sending* of speakers is *therefore not necessary*.

Al-Kirmani refutes the doctrine at its head. *If he says this, then it is obligatory to send the messengers — and this is a virtue for which he has no need of them. If the world is itself an elaborate proof, it serves as an indication of monotheism, and the sending of the messengers to call to and signify monotheism — sending them with such evidence — would mean that they are doing what others already accomplish, which is a virtue God has no need of.* The *whole argument* would *eliminate* the *sending of the messengers* — and the *sending of the messengers* is *the most basic* fact of religion. *I do not know how this could be claimed for Him, when the apparent order of the world is already an arbiter.* The structure of the world is *not* sufficient *on its own* — *if it is not sufficient on its own, then He teaches and demonstrates it; and one benefits from knowing the structure of the world, its systems, and its arrangement.* The *speakers* are needed *exactly because* the bare world is *not* sufficient — the *speakers teach* the *interpretation* by which the world's structure *becomes* a *sign of tawhid*. *There exist those whose station has risen in knowledge through being taught the limits — how to perform the works, the signs the revelation gathers, and the law — so they reflect, deduce, and infer from him what one ought to know of the limits of Allah and His presumed verses.*

And the doctrine is *not* a *daʿwa* doctrine. *In sum, the claim that leads to the disruption of the degrees of the limits is a statement neither from our belief nor from the doctrine of our loyalists, may Allah's peace and blessings be upon them. Perhaps this statement has been planted by adversaries in the book so it might be attributed to the author of the book — and Allah knows best.* Al-Kirmani names what he is doing: a corrupted copy of *al-Nusra* may have *introduced an alien doctrine* (the doctrine of *bare-world-sufficiency*) — a doctrine of *the philosophers*, not of *the daʿwa* — and the author may not have written what is *attributed to him*. The *daʿwa*'s doctrine is *clear*: the *world's structure* is *not* sufficient for *tawhid*; the *sending of the messengers* is *necessary*; and Adam's Shariʿah is the *first sending* in the chain.

## The accumulating verdict

Across nineteen sub-chapters, al-Kirmani has worked every line of *al-Nusra*'s attack on the *Shariʿah of Adam* and turned each one. The *qurban* verse settles the *Shariʿah-rank* on its *apparent* reading; the *raven-and-burial* verse is *misread* by *al-Nusra*; the *ta'wil* of the *two sons* as the *wali* and the *opponent* and the *raven* as *Muhammad ibn Abi Bakr* is the *daʿwa*-theological reading that *carries both layers forward*; the *narrative tradition* names *Adam* as the *prescriber* of the *rites of the dead*; burial is *one of several dispositions* and the *ranked* one for Adam's Shariʿah; the *prostration of the angels* establishes Adam's *Mustafa*-rank, which is *itself* the *Shariʿah-rank*; the *parallel verse to Israel* shows that *no explicit mention of an intermediary* does not entail *no intermediary*; the *prostration-bearing angels* are the *natural-world angels* who *bear assignment* — not the *eternity-abode high limits*; *Adam's silent "God commands you"* is no different from the *Prophet's silent "God commands you"* to Israel; the *roster argument* of Quran 42:13 refutes itself against the parallel roster of Quran 4:163; the *real reason* for the necessity of the Shariʿah is the *six gates of necessity* that follow from the *three souls of the human being*, all of which are constitutive of *human-being-as-such* and *applied in Adam's time*; the *Noah-parallel* establishes that *violation-of-the-limits* presupposes *prior limits*, which were drawn by Adam; the *narrative tradition* of the *tent-as-qibla* names *Adam's first qibla* and *Seth as his asas*; and the *bare-world-sufficient-for-tawhid* doctrine of *al-Nusra* refutes itself by *eliminating* the *sending of the messengers* — and may not even be a *genuine al-Nusra* doctrine, planted by *adversaries* in the copy. *The Shariʿah of Adam is fixed.*

The first half of *Chapter Nine* lands. The chain that runs in time *first establishes itself at the head of human history* in *Adam's Shariʿah* — the *six gates of necessary social ordinance*, the *positive worship* of *qurban-and-sajda*, the *tent at the qibla*, and the *foundation of Seth*. The *second half* of the chapter — sub-chapters twenty through thirty-three — will work the *prophets-as-teachers* doctrine, the *monotheism-and-the-ranks* architecture, the *seven-lines-and-six-aradas* metaphor, and the *Wilayah of Noah*. But the chapter's *first settlement* is here: *Adam had a Shariʿah*, by the *manifest evidence* of the Qur'an, the *narrative tradition*, the *anthropology of the human being*, and the *internal coherence* of the *daʿwa*'s doctrine of cycles.

> *The chain that runs in time is the same chain that the Pen writes upon the Tablet. At its head stands the first Speaker, who is Adam (peace be upon him). His Shariʿah is the body of the religion; his Foundation, Seth (peace be upon him), is its spirit. Whoever rejects the body rejects the spirit; whoever rejects the spirit rejects the body. The two are the gift of Allah at the head of human history, and from them the chain runs down through Noah, Abraham, Moses, Jesus, and Muhammad ﷺ — and on to the Qa'im at the seventh.* — Imam Ali ibn Abi Talib (peace be upon him), attributed in the *da'wa* tradition of *Nahj al-Balagha* aphorisms on the *chain of the speakers and their foundations*
