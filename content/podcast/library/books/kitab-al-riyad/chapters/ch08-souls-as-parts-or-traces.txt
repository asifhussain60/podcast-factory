# Souls: Parts of the First Truths, or Only Traces?

*This chapter covers all eight sub-chapters of* Chapter Four *of* Kitab al-Riyad, *al-Kirmani's adjudication of the longest-running disagreement between the two reformers: are the speaking souls in us* parts *of the First Truths (the universal Second, the Soul that the First Intellect emits) or only* traces *of those Truths, as* al-Hayuli *(prime matter) is the trace of the Second? The author of* al-Islah *had insisted on "traces" because "parts" smelled of body-talk; the author of* al-Nusra *had insisted on "parts" because without parts, he argued, the architecture of prophethood, imamate, walaya, and revelation collapses. Al-Kirmani works the eight sub-chapters as a careful triangulation. He refuses al-Islah's flat denial of partness. He refuses al-Nusra's flat assertion of partness. He lands on a settled formula: the speaking souls are the* second emanation, *the first emanation being the universal Soul itself. They are "parts" only in the restricted sense that what is in the whole is found in the part by rank, not by composition. And the integrity of prophethood, imamate, walaya, and the divine laws does not stand or fall by the parts-claim at all. It rests on something more foundational, that the Intellect is nothing but the Word, and the Word is nothing but the Intellect, the formula Imam al-Mu'izz li-Din Allah taught in* Ta'wil al-Shari'a *and that al-Kirmani's entire corpus has been protecting.*

## Where this chapter picks up

*Chapter Three* of *al-Riyad* closed on the chain. The First and the Second are like-counterparts, *the mineral of honor, perfection, and elevation*. *Al-Hayuli* is the trace of the Second, the substrate of the lower world, impressed by the power that the Second learned from the First. The two worlds do not need to resemble each other to be in contact. The contact is the work of the power that runs from the First through the Second into the substrate. *Chapter Three* settled that architecture and left the reader with the next question. If the natural composite is *al-Hayuli* informed by the Soul's power, then the partial souls, *us*, the human souls inhabiting that composite, what are *we*? Parts of the First Truths? Traces of them? Something else again?

*Chapter Four* is the chapter that answers the question. Eight sub-chapters. The two reformers, *al-Islah* (the late Abu Hatim al-Razi) and *al-Nusra* (his successor in the school, Abu Ya'qub al-Sijistani), disagreed sharply on what *kind* of relation the human soul stands in to the rank above it. The disagreement looked, on its surface, like a vocabulary disagreement: "traces" or "parts"? But the stake underneath was the integrity of the whole *da'wa* architecture. If the souls in us are *only* traces, impressions thrown off by the Second the way *al-Hayuli* is, then on what basis can a prophet reach back up through the chain to receive the message? But if the souls in us are *parts* of the Second, in the literal sense of pieces of a single substance, then the entire categorical refusal that *Chapters Two* and *Three* laboriously built against body-talk in the higher ranks falls apart.

*Chapter Four* threads the needle. The answer is neither "parts" nor "traces" simpliciter. The answer is *second emanation*, a precise relation in the chain that grounds both the souls' capacity to receive prophecy and the categorical refusal of body-talk in the ranks. The chapter walks eight sub-chapters to arrive there, and each sub-chapter is a different angle on the same triangulation.

The Quranic verse the *da'wa* tradition has always taken as the foundational anchor on the soul's order runs beneath the whole chapter:

> *They are asking thee concerning the Spirit. Say: The Spirit is by command of my Lord, and of knowledge ye have been vouchsafed but little.* (Quran 17:85, Pickthall translation)

*By command of my Lord*: the soul belongs to the order of the divine *amr*, not to the order of bodies. The verse positions the soul at the exact rank al-Kirmani's eight sub-chapters will adjudicate. It is not a body; it is not a piece of a larger substance; it belongs to what God's command brings forth. *And of knowledge ye have been vouchsafed but little*: the warning that the relation is real but hard to name. The author of *al-Islah* reaches for "trace"; the author of *al-Nusra* reaches for "parts"; al-Kirmani will reach for "second emanation". The verse names the difficulty; the chapter does the naming.

## Sub-chapter one: traces, but in what sense?

The author of *al-Islah* opens *Chapter Four* by stating his position. *As what the author of* al-Nusra *has proved*, *al-Islah* concedes, *the parts that are in us are traces of the first essence, not parts of it*. Our union with the first is by the *effects* of its essence, not by *parts* of its essence. And the union of the first is not by its parts at all, because the first is united by the very word. The parts in us, even though they are not parts of the first essence, nor is the first united by them, are united by its *monuments*: by the union of its products with our productions within it, not by the union of the first essence itself, which is likewise in the word, because the first essence is united by the very word, until he and the word became one thing. This is what *al-Islah* said.

The author of *al-Nusra* refuses. This statement, he says, is confused and contradictory on its face. *Al-Islah* called them "the parts that are in us," and then called them "traces of the first essence." How can the parts be relics, yet be essences in their own right? And the effects do not belong to the thing in question by any strength in themselves; they only borrow being from the agent that produces them. If *al-Islah* is going to use the word "parts" at all, then he must indicate that they are parts of an essence. And if they were not parts of the first essence, the word "parts" should never have been deployed. Once "parts" is deployed, the conclusion is that they *are* parts of an essence, and from that the absurdity follows of saying they are traces, because the parts of a thing are not the traces of another. This is what *al-Nusra* said.

Al-Kirmani then enters with his usual half-credit move.

*The statement of the author of* al-Nusra *in what he said, that the souls are jewels existing by themselves after acquisition, and that the effects cannot exist by themselves, is true.* That is the first granting: *al-Nusra* is right that effects are not self-existent essences. If the souls in us are essences existing by themselves, then they cannot be reduced to "effects" in the bare sense of consequences with no being of their own. So *al-Nusra's* logical pressure on *al-Islah* has real teeth at that point.

But then comes the second granting, and this one goes to *al-Islah*. *Al-Islah's* refusal to give souls "the partial name of a thing," his refusal to call them "parts," was not careless. It came from a precise concern. The things that are *integral* (the things you can break into parts in the body-sense) are *bodies*, the bodies that are inseparable from the nine accidents and that are perceived by the five senses. The soul is not a body. The soul does not overflow with an object the way a body does. So *al-Islah* did not see fit to give the soul the name "part," lest it be reduced to the course of bodies, lest the soul be made to match what is said of bodies. He used "trace" precisely to *keep* the soul outside body-talk while still naming the relation it stands in to the rank above it.

What *al-Islah* meant by "trace," al-Kirmani explains, is *not* what *al-Hayuli* is, the bare impression of the Second's power in a substrate that does not resemble it. What *al-Islah* meant is that these souls have *their own essence and their own survival together with* the effects of those paradoxical essences (the *hudud*, the ranks of the *da'wa*: the *natiq*, the *asas*, the *imam*, the *hujja*, and the ranks beneath them) that reach them *from the side of the limits*. The intermediaries, *peace be upon them*, are how the gift of the Second arrives in the soul of a believer. The soul has its own essence; the effects reaching it from the *hudud* give it its survival.

This is the key clarification. When *al-Islah* said "traces," he did not mean ephemeral marks. He meant *essences kept in being by what reaches them from the side of the* hudud. The soul is a real thing. It has its own subsistence. But what *gives* the soul that subsistence in the chain, what makes the soul *persist*, is the running of the power down from the First, through the Second, through the *hudud*, into the soul. That is what the word "trace" was carrying.

And *al-Nusra's* formulation, that what is going on in the soul is *the stream of al-hayuli, which is accepted*, has its place too, al-Kirmani notes, by way of analogy and approximation to understandings. *Al-Nusra* was trying to name how the higher reaches the lower; he reached for the language of stream-and-reception, which has the merit of preserving the directionality of the gift even if it leans body-ward.

So sub-chapter one ends without a single "winner." Both reformers were trying to name the same relation. *Al-Islah* used "trace" to protect the soul from being reduced to body. *Al-Nusra* used "parts" to protect the soul's continuity with what stands above. In al-Kirmani's reading, each is right in what he was trying to protect; neither's word, taken alone, settles the relation. The relation needs a third word, which the next seven sub-chapters will build toward.

Imam Ali ibn Abi Talib (peace be upon him) is reported in the aphorisms of his tradition as having said that *your soul is a precious jewel; whoever guards it has elevated it, and whoever lets it slip has lowered it.* The aphorism names precisely the half-credit that *Chapter Four's* opening sub-chapter pivots on. The soul *is* a jewel, *al-Nusra's* word, granted. And the soul's elevation or lowering depends on the guarding, *al-Islah's* concern that the wrong word ("parts," reduced to body-status) would let the jewel slip. Both reformers, in al-Kirmani's reading, were guarding the same jewel from opposite directions.

## Sub-chapter two: souls as parts of the total soul

In sub-chapter two, *al-Nusra* lays out his positive proposal. *The souls are parts of the total soul*, and by "the total soul" he means the universal Soul, the Second. The claim, *al-Nusra* admits, is not based on direct evidence; it is based on a dilemma. If the universal Soul is the *sum* of the parts that are these souls, then all of them divide into two parts. All these parts oblige that the souls are parts of the total soul. The souls in the abode of this world are extended by the evidence given in earlier and later discussions. The Second is called the soul whose existence is *not* in the house of the body, which is the house of the world. And if there is no absurdity in this world's being the second, both of these souls, if it is not possible to be a whole composed of these souls, then these are not parts of them.

The argument is dense and *al-Nusra* knows it. The point he is making, stripped of its careful negations: the universal Soul *is* in some sense the sum of the partial souls. If the partial souls are not parts of the universal Soul, then there is no universal Soul that they sum to. And yet the doctrine requires there to be one. So the partial souls must be parts of it. The whole stands or falls on whether the universal Soul can be construed as a whole composed of the partial souls.

## Sub-chapter three: the dilemma against parts

Al-Kirmani enters in sub-chapter three with the formal refusal. *To assert that the souls are parts of the total soul is not correct with his intention that the total soul is the second.* The Second cannot be what the partial souls sum to. The proof runs by dilemma.

The matter is not free in the soul, if it has parts, from one of these alternatives. The Second must be either: (a) *the whole of them*, the whole that is in the house of the world; or (b) *some of them*, some in the higher world and some in this world; or (c) *not in the sense of a part in this world at all*.

The first alternative, that the Second is the whole of these souls in the house of the world, is invalid because in this world the soul stands *by potentiality*, and there is no way for it to do anything except by what already exists. And the first intellect refuses to be that by which the existing souls come out by potentiality into actuality, because of the precedence that would be required of it, which is impossible for God Almighty, based on what has been said in books other than this one.

The second alternative, that some are in this world and some in the higher world, is invalid because some of them in the higher world, if they are such as those in this world (standing by potentiality for what they are), would face the same problem there too. And this is invalidated by the evidence that *the existence of the second is an already existing existence, not like the existence of the abode of the world, which is a potentiality standing*.

After the invalidity of those two divisions, the third, that the Second is not part of the world, and the souls are not parts of it, follows by elimination. *It has been proven that the second is not part of the world, and it is invalid that the souls should be a part of it.*

The dilemma is precise. The Second exists in *actuality*, that is what *Chapter Three* established. The partial souls exist in *potentiality* in this world. A whole composed of partial souls would itself be in potentiality, because a whole has only the actuality of its parts. So the Second cannot be a whole composed of partial souls. And the Second is also not divisible into "some here, some there" by the same proof. So the partial souls are not parts of the Second in any of the alternatives that the word "parts" admits.

But al-Kirmani has not yet said what the souls *are*. He has only said what they are *not*. Sub-chapter four turns to the positive answer.

## Sub-chapter four: second emanation, the souls' real rank

Al-Kirmani opens sub-chapter four with a careful unfolding. *After the parts, he gives what he gives all of them according to them.* If these souls were parts of the Second, then we should find them all knowledgeable, all wise, for a whole bestows its qualities on its parts. But we find them neither knowledgeable nor wise. Each of them, and the materials of the Messengers (God's prayers be upon them) along with them, would be parts of this category. *A belief such as this, to that extent of greatness, is unbelief; we seek refuge in God if it be known that the materials of the Messengers, God's prayers be upon them, are from it, that being the closest cause of the overflow upon the world below.*

The souls, then, if they are not parts of the following (i.e., not parts of the universal Soul), then *what* are they?

Here al-Kirmani gives the doctrine the chapter has been building toward. *The souls belong to the nature of those paradoxical things whose existence is creative; their appearance and existence are only among the natural and phenomenal worlds. It is a natural force that acquires what is gained from the limits through obedience and worship. So it emits from itself an abstract remaining image that is like the abstract things in the House of the Hereafter on the one hand, and the opposite of it on the other.*

Then the closing line that names what they are: *This is the second source, and our writings on the* Ma'ad *include mention of that, God willing.*

The reference to *Asrar al-Ma'ad* (The Secrets of the Return), al-Kirmani's own treatise on the eschaton and the soul's afterlife, is precise. There he had laid out the architecture in full: the first emanation is the universal Soul itself (the Second), brought forth from the First in the act of origination at the realm of *dar al-Ibda'*. The second emanation is the *partial* speaking souls, the souls in us, brought forth from the universal Soul by the same originating act, considered now under the proportion of receivership rather than the proportion of cause. The souls are *of* the Second, by emanation; they are not *parts of* the Second, by composition. The relation is the same relation the universal Soul stands in to the First: emission, not composition. Each rank is what it is in its place; the rank is given by its proportion in the chain, not by being a piece of a substance above it.

This is the architecture *Chapter Three* had laid down in the abstract: each rank perfect in itself; each rank receiving from above without diminishing what it receives from; the chain holding by power, not by likeness. *Chapter Four* now applies that architecture to the question of the partial souls. The partial souls are perfect in themselves, they are real essences, jewels (as *al-Nusra* said). They are deficient only in addition: in relation to the rank above them, they receive. They receive through the *hudud*, through obedience and worship. And what they produce, the *abstract remaining image*, has two faces: the face that is like the abstract things in the House of the Hereafter (when the soul has lived in obedience and worship), and the opposite face (when it has not).

The Quranic anchor that runs beneath this whole sub-chapter, and that the *da'wa* tradition routinely reaches for when explaining the soul's two faces, is the oath at the opening of *Surat al-Shams*:

> *By the soul, and Him Who fashioned it, and inspired it as to its wickedness and its righteousness. He has succeeded who purifies it, and he has failed who corrupts it.* (Quran 91:7–10)

The verse names the structure exactly. *He has succeeded who purifies it*, that is the face of the abstract remaining image that is like the abstract things in the House of the Hereafter. *He has failed who corrupts it*, that is the opposite face. The soul is a *natural force*, the verse calls it the thing that has been *fashioned* and *inspired*, and what it emits depends on the obedience and worship through which it has acquired what it acquires from the *hudud*. The verse does not say the soul is a *part* of anything. It says the soul has been fashioned, inspired, and that what it becomes is up to the work it does on itself. That is precisely the doctrine al-Kirmani is settling in sub-chapter four. The soul is the second emanation, fashioned in its place, inspired through the chain, perfecting itself by the work of obedience and worship.

## Sub-chapter five: the limit of the body, the limit of the soul

Sub-chapter five turns from the metaphysics of the souls to a contrast that runs through al-Kirmani's anti-body argument across the book. *Al-Nusra* offers an observation. *Likewise, our antiquities, joined to our manufactures, do not accept our antiquities after we have finished our work; nor do they accept any artifact ready to be received from us, except by the introduction of corruption into them and the change of them from their states. Because they are not parts of us, nor are they existing essences. They are merely ephemeral accidents.* So *al-Nusra* said.

Al-Kirmani's response is direct. *What is meant by this is that bodies are limited in what they can accept, to the point that they receive nothing further.* The image is iron held in fire: once it has gone red, that is its end; it grows no hotter. Or a black body: it does not grow blacker. It may end in a blackness past which it cannot be further blackened. The body has a *terminus*. The capacity of any substrate to receive an accident is finite. And al-Kirmani turns the point back on *al-Nusra* himself: *that is from the very meaning he himself first acknowledged, as his own phrase requires: it is not the traces that are the receiver, but the receiver, that is, the body subject to the succession of accidents and effects.*

The move is subtle but it sets up sub-chapter six. What *al-Nusra* has actually proved with his iron-in-fire and black-body example is *not* that the souls are mere ephemeral accidents. He has proved that *bodies* have a fixed capacity past which they receive nothing further. The traces are not the receivers; the body is the receiver. And the body's reception is bounded. That is *al-Nusra's* real contribution to the chapter, even though *al-Nusra* deployed it intending the opposite conclusion. Al-Kirmani will take this point and turn it against any reading of the soul as body-like.

## Sub-chapter six: the soul's capacity is unlimited; "parts" only by rank

*Al-Nusra* now makes the move that, if al-Kirmani had to single out one *al-Nusra* sentence that finally states the matter right, would be it: *But the speaking souls within us continue to gain from the light of the Most High and Most Noble world, and they increase in honor in themselves without any change occurring in themselves; nor does any corruption enter into them from the soul when it wishes to benefit them. It is true that they are parts and essences of the soul that bear no traces upon them.*

Al-Kirmani's response begins with a doctrinal endorsement. *The matter is that the soul is unlimited in its capacity to accept what he described, and this is one of the proofs that the soul is not embodied.* This is the proof that sub-chapter five had set up: bodies have a fixed terminus of reception; the soul does not. The soul keeps gaining from the light without itself being changed. Therefore the soul is not a body. *Al-Nusra* has, almost by accident, supplied al-Kirmani with the very proof of non-embodiment that *Chapter Two* had been building.

The Prophetic tradition holds a Hadith Qudsi that names this same architecture of unbounded reception from another direction. God says, in the report Sahih al-Bukhari preserves on the authority of Abu Hurayrah:

> *My servant continues to draw near to Me with voluntary works until I love him. And when I love him, I am his hearing with which he hears, his sight with which he sees, his hand with which he strikes, and his foot with which he walks.* (Sahih al-Bukhari, Book 81, Hadith 38; narrated by Abu Hurayrah)

The Hadith Qudsi's structure is precisely the structure al-Kirmani is settling. The servant draws near through voluntary works, the *nawafil*, and the drawing-near is unbounded. The servant's faculties become, by rank, what they were always *of*: the hearing becomes the hearing by which God hears in him, the sight the sight by which God sees in him. The faculties are not pieces of God; they are the rank at which God's power is found in the servant. What is in the whole is found in the part by rank, not by composition. The Hadith Qudsi says, in the prophetic register, what the chapter is settling in the metaphysical register. The reception is by rank; the rank deepens through obedience and worship; the soul's capacity has no terminus.

But on the substantive claim, that the souls are parts of the Second, al-Kirmani does not give *al-Nusra* the whole loaf. *And the matter, regarding the soul being parts of the Second, is not as he claimed and laid out.* The dilemma of sub-chapter three still stands. The Second is not a whole composed of partial souls; the partial souls are not pieces of the Second in any spatial or compositional sense.

What al-Kirmani *will* grant is this: *The most we can grant him is to say: as for those who wish to call them parts, these speaking souls are receivers if they are rational. As such, the soul and what it has produced are one thing; the speaking soul is identical with it and not distinguished from it except by rank, like the part of a thing in which there exists what is found in the whole, their path in themselves being one and the same from this side.*

This is the third word the chapter has been building toward. "Parts," yes, in this restricted sense only. The speaking soul is a "part" of the universal Soul *not* because it is a piece broken off from a larger substance, but because *what is in the whole is found in the part by rank*. The soul of a believer who has reached perfection finds in itself, by its rank, what the universal Soul has, though not in the same proportion, and not by composition. The relation is the relation of *one thing* differentiated only by rank, not by substance.

The image al-Kirmani is reaching for is the same image *Chapter Three* used for the Second's relation to the First. The Second is one self with two proportions, cause-proportion (toward what stands below) and effect-proportion (toward what stands above). The Second and the First are *one in self, differentiated by proportion*. Now al-Kirmani is saying: the speaking soul and the universal Soul are *one in self, differentiated by rank*. The architecture of the chain is the architecture of one continuous self, refracted through successive proportions. The "parts" language is admitted only on that strict reading.

This is the move that reconciles *al-Islah* and *al-Nusra*. *Al-Islah* refused "parts" because parts meant body-pieces. Al-Kirmani agrees: in *that* sense, no. *Al-Nusra* insisted on "parts" because the soul's continuity with the rank above is real. Al-Kirmani agrees: in *that* sense, the continuity of one thing by rank, yes.

The Sufi tradition has the same teaching in compressed form. Ibn Ata Allah al-Iskandari (Ibn Ata Allah), the Shadhili teacher of Mamluk Alexandria, condensed it into a single line in his *Hikam*:

> *Lights are the riding-mounts of hearts and innermost beings.* (Ibn Ata Allah, Hikam, Aphorism 96)

Lights ride; hearts are ridden. The light is what carries the heart to the rank at which it can receive. The heart is not the light, and the light is not the heart; but the heart is what the light brings to the place where the gift arrives. That is the architecture of "one in self, differentiated by rank" rendered in the vocabulary of a different tradition. The two formulations stand at the same point: the rank receives by what runs through it; the running is not the running of pieces toward pieces, but the running of a single power through proportions of receivership.

## Sub-chapter seven: what really grounds prophethood and imamate

Sub-chapter seven is the chapter's hottest moment. *Al-Nusra* presses the polemical stake. *Also, to claim that the speaking souls existing in this world are not parts of the Universal Soul nullifies prophethood, imamate, walaya, revelation, and the divine laws.* For if the souls are not parts of the Universal Soul, *al-Nusra* argues, it would not be possible for the soul to perceive what influences it, nor to encompass it, nor to benefit from it. Yet the prophets (peace be upon them) claimed that they had *seen the noble Alawi world, and stood upon the spiritual* hudud*, and benefited from what they brought back of light and manifestation, from those above them, from the origin and the three branches, and that they had seen the angels*. If their pure souls were *only* traces of the Universal Soul, they would not have been able to comprehend what was inspired in them, nor to stand upon those spiritual objects which are parts of the Universal Soul.

The argument leans on what is undeniable in the *da'wa* tradition: the prophets really did receive. The reception was real. The vision was real. The benefit was real. So the souls of the prophets must have been more than ephemeral traces. They must have been *of the same kind* as what they received. *Al-Nusra* takes that to require partness.

Al-Kirmani's response is patient and load-bearing.

*It has already been said that the souls are not mere traces but parts, and that they are a second emanation.* This is the first move. *Al-Nusra* is right that the souls are not mere traces. But the right name for what they are is not just "parts"; it is *second emanation*. That is the doctrinal anchor sub-chapter four already placed.

What is meant by the one who said they are effects, *al-Islah* with his "traces," is that *what is done with them belongs essentially to the souls and earns them their survival*. *Al-Islah's* "traces" carried the truth that the work the soul does on itself is essentially its own. The soul's survival is earned, through obedience and worship, by what is done with the soul by the *hudud* and what the soul does in response.

What is meant by those who said they are parts, *al-Nusra* with his "parts," is that *the souls, by their own intellect, are like the things that have produced them*. *Al-Nusra's* "parts" carried the truth that the soul has, in itself, the rational character of what stands above it. Like the part in which is found what is in the whole.

And then comes the doctrinal release. *Prophethood is not invalidated by this, nor imamate, nor anything else, for it has been established that these ranks are not of these.* The ranks of prophethood, imamate, walaya, revelation, and the laws are *not derived* from the parts-question at all. *Al-Nusra's* polemical move, that without parts the whole architecture collapses, assumed that the architecture *rests* on the partness of the souls. It does not. It rests on something else.

*The prophets were able to perceive these absent things from the upper world, after they reached the rank of perfection, becoming, at its end, in the Sixties.* The phrase *in the Sixties* refers to the doctrine, set out elsewhere in al-Kirmani's corpus (notably *Rahat al-'Aql*), that the soul of the prophet, having traversed the ranks of obedience and worship and ascended through the *hudud*, arrives at a station, counted in the technical reckoning as the Sixties, where it stands at the brink of the upper world. From that station, what the upper world holds becomes perceptible to it. The reception is not because the prophet's soul is a *piece* of the universal Soul. The reception is because the prophet's soul has reached the *station* at which the upper world is perceived.

*When they return to themselves, it is as though they have realized that the transcendent selves are one thing in respect of the whole, with no difference except in rank. We have set this out in our well-known book, and the peace of mind that comes of that is sufficient and curative.*

"Our well-known book" is *Rahat al-'Aql*. *The peace of mind that comes of that is sufficient and curative*, the doctrinal settlement that prophethood does not depend on substance-composition with the universal Soul; it depends on the rank attained by the prophet's soul through the chain of obedience and worship, the chain along which the *hudud* convey the gift of the upper world.

The Quranic anchor for this whole sub-chapter is the verse the *da'wa* tradition reaches for when explaining the Prophet ﷺ's vision:

> *And he was at the highest horizon. Then he approached and descended, and was at a distance of two bow-lengths or nearer. So He revealed to His servant what He revealed. The heart did not lie about what it saw.* (Quran 53:7–11)

*Two bow-lengths or nearer*, the closest reach of the chain, the station at the brink. The reveal happens *there*. The servant is at the station; the revelation comes down through the chain. The verse describes the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ *seeing*, at the highest horizon, the boundary station between the lower and the upper worlds. The seeing is real. The vision is not a metaphor. But the verse does not say the Prophet ﷺ was a *piece* of what he saw. It says he was at the *boundary*. That is precisely al-Kirmani's reading. The prophet sees from the station the chain has brought him to; the seeing is enabled by the rank, not by substance-composition. The verses that follow, *the eye did not deviate, nor did it transgress; he certainly saw of the greatest signs of his Lord* (Quran 53:17–18), make the point firmer still: the seeing is a real seeing, but it is the seeing of *signs* delivered by the chain, not the seeing of one's own substance.

## Sub-chapter eight: the Word is the Intellect

Sub-chapter eight closes the chapter with the deepest move. *Al-Nusra* now proposes the formulation that, on his own reading, would settle the architecture once and for all: *Rather, it must be said that the Intellect is creative through the Word, the Soul is the image of the Intellect, nature is born of the Soul, and the speaking souls are parts of the Universal Soul, so that in this order tawhid, reward, punishment, prophethood, imamate, sharia, and revelation become coherent.*

*Al-Nusra* is offering a four-rung ladder. (1) Intellect creative through Word. (2) Soul as image of Intellect. (3) Nature born of Soul. (4) Speaking souls as parts of the Universal Soul. With that ladder in place, *al-Nusra* claims, every Ismaili doctrine, *tawhid*, reward and punishment, prophethood, imamate, *sharia*, and revelation, falls into place.

Al-Kirmani's response is the unrelenting closing word the chapter has earned: *The validity of tawhid, of reward and punishment, of prophethood and imamate, of the message and of the laws drawn from revelation, is not proved from the side that the Intellect is creative through the Word and that the Soul is the image of the Intellect. Rather, it is established from another side, set out in our letters and our writings about them.*

The doctrines of *tawhid*, prophethood, imamate, and the rest are *not* derived from the four-rung ladder. They are established by other means. The ladder *al-Nusra* is offering is, in fact, *itself* in error, and al-Kirmani goes after the first rung.

*As for the Intellect, it is not permissible to say that it is creative through the Word, because the Word must be presented to the Intellect.* The Word does not stand *behind* the Intellect as the Intellect's instrument; the Word is what the Intellect *receives* in order to be what it is. If the Word were what the Intellect *uses* in order to create, then the Word would be prior and the Intellect would be a tool wielded by the Word. That picture has the priority backwards.

Then the deepest move. *The Intellect is nothing but the Word itself, and the Word is nothing but the Intellect itself.* The Intellect IS the Word. The Word IS the Intellect. Not two things in relation. One thing, called by two names according to the aspect under which it is being considered.

And al-Kirmani brings the authority that for *al-Riyad* is the highest authority: *It is correct to say that Mawlana Imam al-Mu'izz li-Din Allah, in* The Interpretation of Sharia *(* Ta'wil al-Shari'a *), where he discusses intelligibles, says: the Word is the Intellect, and these names properly belong to it.* Imam al-Mu'izz li-Din Allah (Imam al-Mu'izz), the fourth Fatimid Imam-Caliph, the Imam at whose court the *da'wa* of al-Kirmani's intellectual ancestors was given its Cairo settlement, in his *Ta'wil al-Shari'a*, the great Cairo-court treatise on the inner meaning of the divine law, says that the Word is the Intellect, and that the names "Word" and "Intellect" properly belong to the same single rank. That is the authority. *Al-Nusra's* four-rung ladder, taken at the first rung, is already at odds with the doctrine of the Imam at whose name the *da'wa* gathers.

And from that doctrine, al-Kirmani gives the order that *is* correct: *The Soul and nature emanate from the First Intellect, each of them according to its own proportion, so that one of them is more excellent than the other, the first emanation being the Soul, and the speaking souls being the second emanation. What occurs in the first of them is repeated in the last of them.*

There it is, the formula the chapter has been building toward since sub-chapter four. The first emanation is the universal Soul. The speaking souls are the *second emanation*. What occurs in the first (the rank, the proportion, the relation of receivership to what stands above) is *repeated* in the last (the speaking souls receive, in their own rank, what the universal Soul receives in its rank, in the measure each rank can hold). The chain is the chain of emanation, not the chain of composition. The "what is in the whole is in the part" of sub-chapter six is the same point at the level of the rational soul: the speaking soul finds in itself, by its rank, what the universal Soul has, because the same originating power, the Intellect-which-is-the-Word, runs through the whole chain.

The chapter closes on a long prayer for the believer to fulfill his covenant. *A gesture suffices for the seeking student; others reject it because it is hard to reach and hard to know. May Allah help the believer who knows this according to his strength in devotion to Allah and His guardians, and help him if he is sincere to His face, and condemn him and his guardians if he renounces them. May Allah make us among the followers able to fulfill their covenant; we are blessed with His praise and glory.*

The prayer is the chapter's signature. The doctrine has been settled. The dispute between *al-Islah* and *al-Nusra*, which started as a vocabulary dispute over "trace" or "part," has been adjudicated and given a third word: *second emanation*, on the architecture where the Intellect is the Word and the chain runs by proportions of receivership. And the integrity of *tawhid*, prophethood, imamate, *sharia*, and revelation is shown to rest *not* on the partness of the souls, but on the architecture of the chain, the architecture that Imam al-Mu'izz taught and that al-Kirmani's whole corpus has been protecting.

## The chapter's settled formula

The eight sub-chapters land on one settled formula. The speaking souls are not mere traces, as *al-Islah's* isolated word would suggest. They are not parts of the Second in the sense of pieces broken off from a substance, as *al-Nusra's* isolated word would suggest. They are the *second emanation*, brought forth from the universal Soul by the same originating act under which the universal Soul was brought forth from the First. They are jewels existing by themselves, with their own essence and their own survival, *and* they are continuous by rank with what stands above them: what is in the whole is found in the part by rank, not by composition. The Intellect is the Word; the Word is the Intellect; the Soul is the first emanation; the speaking souls are the second emanation. The architecture of *tawhid*, prophethood, imamate, walaya, *sharia*, and revelation rests on this chain, on the running of the single originating power through the proportions of receivership at each rank, not on a substance-partness that would smuggle body-categories back into ranks where bodies cannot reach.

The political stake of *Chapter Four*, like that of *Chapter Three*, sits one layer below the metaphysics. If the speaking souls were *parts* of the universal Soul in any literal sense, then a believer's soul could claim continuity with the upper world by sheer composition, by being a *piece* of it. The *natiq*, the *imam*, the *hudud* would lose their function as the convoy of the gift down through the ranks; the believer could go around them on the basis of being already, by composition, of the same substance as the rank above. Al-Kirmani's refusal of that picture is the protection of the *da'wa* architecture itself. The speaking soul does not stand *in* the universal Soul as a piece in a whole. The speaking soul stands *under* the universal Soul as a rank under a rank, receiving through the *hudud* what the chain conveys. The path is the path of obedience and worship; the *hudud* are the convoy; the rank perfected is the soul's seat at the brink of the upper world, *in the Sixties*, where the Prophet ﷺ saw at the boundary, where the gift arrives.

Imam al-Mu'izz's formulation in *Ta'wil al-Shari'a*, that the Word is the Intellect, and these names properly belong to it, is the Cairo-court doctrine al-Kirmani is *protecting* by refusing *al-Nusra's* four-rung ladder. The Imam's doctrine holds that the highest rank is not a *use* of any instrument; it is one self under two names. From that single doctrine, the whole architecture of the chain unfolds in clean proportions: the Intellect-which-is-the-Word at the top, the Soul as the first emanation, the speaking souls as the second emanation, the natural composite as the substrate the chain reaches at its lowest extent. There is nothing in the chain that needs to be a *piece* of anything else. The chain holds because the single originating power, called by the names that belong to it at each rank, runs through it.

## What comes next

*Chapter Five* of *al-Riyad* will take the next step in the same architecture. If the speaking souls are the second emanation, brought forth by the chain into the natural composite, then what is the human being, the full creature, soul plus natural composite, in the order of the worlds? *Chapter Four* gave the rank of the soul; *Chapter Five* gives the place of the whole human being. The answer the next chapter builds is that the human being is the *fruit* of the worlds, the rank at which the chain comes to its harvest, the creature in which the gift of the upper world finds its bearer in the lower. That is what the seven sub-chapters of *Chapter Five* will settle.

And before that next chapter opens, *Chapter Four* leaves the reader with the image it has earned. *Al-Islah* and *al-Nusra* arguing across decades about whether the souls in us are *parts* or *traces*. Al-Kirmani, generations later, working the same passage and arriving at a third word: *second emanation*. The speaking souls in their place. The Intellect-which-is-the-Word at the source. The *hudud* between, conveying the gift. The believer who knows this according to his strength, in devotion to Allah and His guardians. *A gesture suffices for the seeking student; others reject it because it is hard to reach and hard to know.* The chapter is the gesture. The doctrine is settled. The architecture holds.
