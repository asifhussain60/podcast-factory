# The Human as Fruit of the Worlds

*This chapter covers all seven sub-chapters of* Chapter Five *of* this book, *the author's adjudication of the next argument the two reformers handed down. The author of* the book *The Correction* *had said that the human being is the* fruit *of the natural world, the harvest at which the chain of cause-and-effect from origin through compound bodies finds its bearer, the form prepared to receive the three souls (vegetative, sensitive, speaking). The author of* the book *The Defense* *had refused: that, he said, is what the materialists say, those who denied the soul's survival after the body, and if it were true, this world would have* held *the human being and not let him move to the* Ma'ad*. So* the book *The Defense* *flipped the formula: the human being is the fruit of the* higher *world. The author works the seven sub-chapters as the same triangulating adjudication he gave* Chapter Four*, but on a different question. Both reformers are half-right; both reformers are half-wrong. The human being is not the fruit of this world* alone, *nor of that world* alone; *the human being is the fruit of* all the worlds. *From the natural world the human being receives his first perfection, the bodily form prepared to receive the three souls; from the higher world he receives his second perfection, the consummation of the spiritual image, the knowledge of the Oneness of Allah Almighty by means of the* limits, *and obedience to them in knowledge and deed. The doctrine the author lands on is the one Imam the Fatimid caliph taught in* The Interpretation of Sharia (the Fatimid caliph's interpretive treatise): *the human being is the purpose of the second world and the end of the action of nature. And along the way the chapter settles the related sub-arguments, the book *The Defense*'s hung objection about emanation and the heaven-and-earth marriage analogy; the dispute over the name* the doctrine of perfection *(perfection) and where it properly falls; the question of whether the First Creator acts in order and arrangement at all, all of them reduce to the same architecture the rest of* this book *has been building. The chain runs by emanation, not by composition; the names belong to the ranks by proportion, not by sharing of substance; the human being stands at the rank where the two great overflows meet.*

## Where this chapter picks up

*Chapter Four* of *this book* settled the rank of the speaking soul. The souls are the *second emanation*, brought forth from the universal Soul by the same originating act under which the universal Soul itself was brought forth from the First. They are not pieces of any substance above them. They stand by their own essence, jewels existing by themselves, continuous by rank with what stands above through the running of the single originating power, the Intellect-which-is-the-Word, through the chain. That settled the *what* of the soul.

*Chapter Five* turns to the *whom*. If the speaking souls are the second emanation, brought forth into the natural composite, the body fashioned by the circular bodies, the luminous planets, the proximate causes, what then is the *full creature*? Not the soul considered apart, not the body considered apart, but the creature in whom the soul has its seat in the body and the body has its meaning from the soul: the *human being*. What rank does the human being hold in the order of the worlds? Is the human being the fruit of *this* world, the harvest at which the chain of natural cause-and-effect finds its bearer? Or the fruit of *that* world, the harvest at which the higher world's overflow finds its receiver?

Seven sub-chapters. The two reformers, again, disagreed. The author of the book *The Correction* said: fruit of *this* world. The author of the book *The Defense* refused: that is the saying of the materialists; the human being is the fruit of the *higher* world. The author, with the patience the book has trained him in, works through the seven sub-chapters and arrives at the third word: the human being is the fruit of *all* the worlds. Each side is right in what he affirms; each side is wrong in what he denies of the other.

The political stake sits one layer below the metaphysics, as it always does in *this book*. If the human being were the fruit of *this* world only, then the *Ma'ad* (the return, the second world) would be a story told for the consolation of mortals, not a real destination. The materialists drew exactly that conclusion. But if the human being were the fruit of *that* world only, pure spirit borrowed into a body it never properly inhabited, then the bodily life would be incidental to the believer's vocation, and obedience and worship in the *body* would not be the work that perfects the soul. The architecture of *sharia*, of the *the limits*, of the long discipline of the believer in the world, would lose its hold. The author's settlement protects both: the body is the field in which the human being is harvested for the higher world; the higher world is the rank in which the human being's first perfection is consummated. The fruit, by being the fruit of *both* worlds, is the rank at which the two overflows meet.

## Sub-chapter one — the fruit of *which* world?

The author of the book *The Correction* opens the chapter with his claim, set out in full. *Man is the fruit of this whole world, and it is for him that this world has been created in all its parts: its origins, its foundations, the first of which are the principles of the individuals that are born of them by motion and rest; then the individuals, which are the foundation of vehicles; then the compounds, which are the very productions of this whole world; and the natures from which the form of the human being is composed — the form which is prepared to receive the three souls: the vegetative, the sensitive, and the speaking.*

The picture is the standard *falsafa* picture. The natural world has stages. The most basic stage is the principles of the individuals, what later traditions will call the elemental natures, the four qualities, the matter informed by the elemental forms. From these, by motion and rest, the *individuals* arise, the simple bodies that carry their qualities. From the individuals come the *compounds*, what is produced when individuals combine, and from the compounds, in a long ascent, comes the natural form that is *prepared to receive the three souls*. The three souls are the standard *falsafa* triad: the *vegetative* soul (which carries growth and nourishment, shared with plants), the *sensitive* soul (which carries perception, shared with animals), and the *speaking* soul (the rational soul, *the soul al-the speaker-propheta*, peculiar to the human being). The natural world is the long preparation; the human form is what the preparation produces; the human being is the *fruit* of this work.

The author of the book *The Defense* refuses with full polemical force. *To say that human beings are the fruit of this world is also the saying of the materialists, those who did not affirm the survival of the soul after the dissolution of bodies. For if human beings were the fruit of this world, this world would have held him and would not have let him move to another world.*

*The saying of the materialists.* The materialists, the *dahriyya*, those who hold that nothing exists but matter in motion, that the soul dies with the body, drew the very conclusion the book *The Correction* seems to be drawing. If the human being is the fruit of this world, then the human being *belongs* to this world, and the *Ma'ad* (the return) is a story. The book *The Defense* reaches for the structural argument. *A fruit belongs to its tree.* If the human being were the fruit of this world, this world would *hold* the human being — keep him as its own. The fact that the human being *moves*, moves to the *Ma'ad*, moves to the higher world after the body, proves that the human being is *not* the fruit of this world. The human being must be the fruit of the *higher* world, *the honorable and illuminating world*. *And for this reason the prophets (peace be upon them) came and warned them to provide themselves, from the house of mortality to the house of survival, and from this world to the abode of the* Ma'ad, *because they knew that this world is not a home, and that man is not the fruit of this world.*

This is the polemical setup of the chapter. Materialism, or its opposite. Fruit of this world, or fruit of that. The book *The Defense* is pushing the book *The Correction* toward the disjunction.

The author enters with the move that has become his signature in this book.

*Each is truthful in what he said on one side, and each is untruthful on another. The side on which they are both truthful is that each of them has said of human beings what belongs to him. The side on which they are both untruthful is in denying the other its share.*

Each is half-right. Each is half-wrong. And then the author states the third word that the rest of the chapter will unfold. *For man rightly bears the title of fruit of all the worlds, because human beings issue from between the two worlds. From this world he receives his first perfection; from that world he receives his second.*

*Fruit of all the worlds.* That is the formula. And it has a precise architecture. The natural world gives the human being his *first perfection*: the issuance of the bodily form, by way of the circular bodies, the luminous planets, and the proximate causes, into the form by which the human being is ready to accept what belongs to him. The natural form, prepared, is the human being's first fruit, the harvest at which the chain of natural cause-and-effect arrives. The higher world gives the human being his *second perfection*: *the consummation of the spiritual image, the precursor to the natural thing, intrinsic to it, and which endures: the knowledge of the Oneness of Allah Almighty by means of all the* limits *(may Allah's peace be upon them), and obedience to them in both modes of worship, in knowledge and in deed.* That is the human being's second fruit, the harvest at which the chain of the higher world arrives.

The second perfection is *the precursor to the natural thing, intrinsic to it, and which endures*. That is the doctrinal weight. The spiritual image, the *abstract remaining image* of *Chapter Four*, the *second source*, the work the speaking soul does on itself through obedience and worship, is the *precursor* to the natural form, *intrinsic* to it, and *enduring*. The bodily form passes. The work the soul does in and through the bodily form does not. That is the fruit of the higher world.

And the author then names the highest authority for the doctrine. *This corrects what we have said in that statement of Mawlana Amir al-Mu'minin the Fatimid caliph Ma'add ibn Isma'il (may Allah's prayers be upon him) in* The Interpretation of Sharia, *where he says that man is the purpose of the second world and the end of the action of nature.*

Imam the Fatimid caliph, the fourth Fatimid Imam-Caliph, named in the author's citation by his given name (Ma'add ibn Isma'il), by his regnal name (*the Fatimid caliph*, the Strengthener of God's Religion), and by his office (*Amir al-Mu'minin*, Commander of the Faithful). And his *the Fatimid caliph's interpretive treatise*, the same Cairo-court treatise that *Chapter Four* invoked for the doctrine that the Intellect is the Word. There, on this question, Imam the Fatimid caliph teaches that the human being is the *purpose of the second world*, the *Ma'ad*, the higher world, *and the end of the action of nature*, the natural world's terminal harvest. The human being stands at the rank where the two overflows meet. The Imam's doctrine is the architecture the author is settling on.

The Quranic anchor that runs beneath this whole sub-chapter, the one the *the call* tradition reaches for when explaining the human being's place, is the verses near the close of *Surat at-Tin*:

> *Surely We created man of the best stature. Then We reduced him to the lowest of the low — except those who believe and do righteous deeds, for them is a reward unfailing.* — (Quran 95:4–6, Surah at-Tin; translation rendered after Pickthall)

The verses name the architecture exactly. *Of the best stature,* that is the first perfection, the bodily form prepared for the three souls, the harvest of the natural world. *Reduced to the lowest of the low,* that is what becomes of the form when it is left to itself, with no work done on it from the side of the higher world. *Except those who believe and do righteous deeds,* that is the second perfection, the consummation of the spiritual image through obedience and worship, the harvest of the higher world. *A reward unfailing,* meaning *the precursor to the natural thing, intrinsic to it, and which endures*. The verse settles into the same shape *Chapter Five's* opening sub-chapter settles into: the human being is the fruit of *both* worlds; the first perfection is given; the second is *earned* through the work the soul does on itself.

The dignity of the *first* perfection, the bodily form prepared, the harvest the natural chain works up to, is named, in turn, in a verse of *Surat the chapter on the night journey* the tradition routinely pairs with the *at-Tin* passage:

> *Verily We have honoured the children of Adam. We carry them on the land and the sea, and have made provision of good things for them, and have preferred them above many of those whom We created with a marked preferment.* — (Quran 17:70, the chapter on the night journey; translation rendered after Pickthall)

*Honoured the children of Adam.* The verse names the bodily creature as already, in its first perfection, the bearer of a *karama* given before any work the soul has yet done on itself. The form prepared is not a neutral substrate; it is already, in the chain's first harvest, the dignified recipient of what the higher world will pour into it. *Preferred above many of those whom We created* — the bodily form is preferred not because the natural chain perfects itself in the human being, but because the form is the one prepared to *receive* what the higher chain conveys. The two verses, *at-Tin* and *the chapter on the night journey*, hold the two perfections in balance. The *honour* of the form is the first perfection. The *reward unfailing*, earned through belief and righteous deed, is the second.

Imam the Commander of the Faithful (peace be upon him), in the opening sermon of the book *The Path of Eloquence*, the sermon on the creation that the compiler of the sermons placed at the head of the collection, names the architecture of the first perfection in a single dense passage:

> *Then Allah, glorified is He, collected from the hard, the soft, the sweet, and the saline earth, clay which He dripped in water till it got pure, and kneaded it with moisture till it became gluey. From it He carved a form with curves, joints, limbs, and segments; He solidified it till it dried for a fixed time and a known duration. Then He blew into it of His spirit, whereupon it took the pattern of a human being — with a mind that governs him, with intelligence which he makes use of, with limbs that serve him, with organs that change his positions, with sagacity that differentiates between truth and untruth, and with the tastes and smells, the colours and forms.* — (the book *The Path of Eloquence*, Sermon 1)

The sermon names the two-perfection architecture in the order it occurs. The bodily form is *collected* from the four corners of the natural world, hard and soft, sweet and saline, kneaded, dried, carved into shape with curves, joints, limbs, segments. That is the first perfection: the harvest at which the chain of natural cause-and-effect arrives. And then *He blew into it of His spirit*, the second perfection, the gift from the higher world poured into the form prepared to receive. the Commander of the Faithful's sermon condenses into one paragraph what the author spreads across seven sub-chapters. The human being is the rank at which the two overflows meet, the carved form below receiving the *blown spirit* from above.

The saying that the Sufi and Ismaili tradition attributes to the Prophet ﷺ (peace and blessings of Allah be upon him) is the closing seal of the sub-chapter's doctrine: *Whoever knows himself knows his Lord.* To know oneself is to know what one *is*, the rank one occupies, the harvest of which one is the fruit. And to know the *full* rank, fruit of all the worlds, body from below and spirit from above, is to know the One from whom both overflows run.

## Sub-chapter two — *the book *The Defense*'s* argument turned back

In sub-chapter two, the author returns to *the book *The Defense*'s* specific objection, *if human beings were the fruit of this world, this world would have held him and would not have let him move from it to another world*, and demonstrates that the objection cuts both ways. *He has handed his opponent the reversal of the matter against him.*

The reversal is precise. *The book *The Defense** says: *If, as I have said, man is the fruit of the higher world, then that world would hold him and not let him return to these people, so that he forgets — and that is impossible, as I have written and required.* The structure is the same as the objection the book *The Defense* leveled against the book *The Correction*. *The book *The Defense** tried to use the "fruit-belongs-to-its-tree" rule against the book *The Correction*: if human beings are the fruit of this world, this world keeps them. But the same rule applies in reverse: if human beings are the fruit of the higher world, the *higher* world keeps them. Yet the human being is not kept in the higher world; the human being *descends*, the human being takes up bodily life. So by *the book *The Defense*'s* own rule, the human being is *not* the fruit of the higher world either. *The book *The Defense** has refuted his own position with the same instrument he tried to use against the book *The Correction*.

*This is neither a statement that proves what he intends to demonstrate in the abstract, nor is it what he himself has said about the prophets being sent: that their being sent is proof that human beings are the fruit of this world.* The author is precise. *The book *The Defense*'s* polemical move was: the sending of the prophets to *warn* people, to call them to provide for the *Ma'ad*, *proves* that the human being is the fruit of the higher world. But the prophets' being sent to *this* world, to walk among bodies, to live the bodily life, to speak in the language of the world they walked through, equally proves that the human being is the fruit of *this* world. The prophets' mission has *both* feet planted in *both* worlds. They came in bodies, into bodies. They left for the higher world. The mission is the mission of the rank at which the two overflows meet.

And then comes the chapter's structural formula. *Human beings are in fact human beings precisely because they are formed by the meeting of the overflow of the worlds: from the natural world, what flows in the channel of the bodies; and from the world of the Intellect, what flows in the channel of the form. And this is in the middle.*

*The meeting of the overflow of the worlds.* The natural world overflows downward, in the channel of bodies, the chain of motion and rest, of elemental compound and natural form, that produces the human bodily composite. The world of the Intellect overflows downward, in the channel of form, the chain of emanation that *Chapter Four* settled, the Intellect-which-is-the-Word at the source, the Soul as the first emanation, the speaking souls as the second emanation, arriving at the human form prepared to receive. *And this is in the middle.* The human being is not at the top of the chain; not at the bottom. The human being is at the rank where the two overflows meet, the middle, the rank at which the chain comes to its harvest.

This is the architecture *the author's other treatise*, the author's own *well-known book*, lays out in the abstract: the seven seas, the Intellect, the Soul, the *the limits*, the chain that runs from *dar an earlier work on origination'* down to the natural world. *Chapter Five* now applies the architecture to the question of what the human being *is*. The human being is the creature at the rank where the chain of emanation from above meets the chain of natural cause-and-effect from below. The *the limits*, the ranks of the *the call* (the *the speaker-prophet*, the *the foundation*, the *imam*, the *hujja*, and the ranks beneath them), are the conveyors of the gift from the higher overflow into the soul of the believer who has reached the form prepared to receive. The bodily form is the field; the soul is the receiver; the *the limits* convey what the higher world gives.

the Commander of the Faithful, in the verses the tradition has preserved under his name and gathered in the *Diwan* compiled in his memory, is said to have named this whole architecture in a single image:

> *Do you suppose yourself to be a small body? Within you is folded the greater world.* — (Verses traditionally attributed to Imam the Commander of the Faithful in the *Diwan the Imam the Commander of the Faithful*; the longer stanza continues: *You are the manifest Book by whose letters the hidden becomes manifest.*)

The aphorism is the precise image *Chapter Five* is reaching for. *A small body*, the human bodily form, the harvest of the natural world, the channel of *what flows in the channel of the bodies*. *The greater world*, folded *within*, the higher world that overflows into the form, the *world of the Intellect*, the channel of *what flows in the channel of the form*. The human being is the *folding*, the rank at which the *greater world* is folded into the *small body*. That is what it is to be the fruit of all the worlds.

## Sub-chapter three — emanation is not marriage

In sub-chapter three, the reformers' argument shifts. The author of the book *The Correction* lays out a positive doctrine of emanation. *The Soul emanates from the First Intellect in perfection, and nature emanates from the Soul in perfection, and the image emanates from the Hayula in perfection — and the whole was perfect.* That is the emanation-chain the book *The Correction* affirms: Intellect → Soul → nature → image emanating from *prime matter*. Each rank is *perfect*; each emanation issues *in perfection*; the whole is perfect.

The author of the book *The Defense* attacks. *If the matter is as he said about the perfection of the Soul being like the Intellect, then his statement about what is spread out upon the figures, the image, and the compounds is invalid and is not reduced.* The objection runs: if the Soul is as perfect as the Intellect, then the lower ranks (the figures, the image, the compounds) cannot be derivative *from* the Soul — for what is perfect lacks nothing, and what lacks nothing is not the source of *more* being. There must be a *deficiency* in what is producing in order for production to happen.

And then the book *The Defense* reaches for the analogy. *It is not permissible to imagine that these manufactures arise only from the pairing of one rich and perfect with another deficient and in need, so that a marriage takes place between them, as is found among witnessed things — such as the heavens and the earth.* The heavens are like the male — *perfect and self-sufficient in all their visible motions and orbits*. The earth is like the female — *deficient in the rank of the female, and her need for him is established*. The marriage of the heavens and the earth produces *various kinds of metals, forms of differing shape, plants, and animals* — *none of which actually appears visibly in the heavens, nor in the earth, nor in the mothers, but they were in them in potency*. When the heavens and the earth are married, what was in potency comes into actuality.

The analogy is the classical *falsafa* analogy. *The book *The Defense** wants to extend it to the upper ranks: the upper rank must be like the male (perfect, self-sufficient); the lower rank must be like the female (deficient, in need); production at every rank works by the marriage of the perfect with the deficient.

The author works the refutation in two stages.

First, on the book *The Correction* side. *As for the statement of the author of* the book *The Correction* *that the Soul is the second emanation from the First Intellect in perfection — that is correct, according to what has been said above.* The Soul *is* the first emanation from the First (taking the First as the Intellect; the universal Soul as the *second* in the count from First to Second). That much is endorsed. *As for his statement that the Hayula emanates from the Soul: we have shown above that its existence is not from the Soul, for the Soul is the second from the First Intellect, and the second of them is not contingent upon the other on the basis of the other.* The Soul is not the source of *prime matter*. The chain is not Intellect → Soul → *prime matter*. The First and the Second exist *together*; outside the soul (in the technical sense, outside the imagination), they exist only together. *Prime matter* is not produced by the Second as an effect; it is the impressed substrate of the lower world, which the chain reaches in another mode. *Chapter Three* of *this book* had spent its length on this very point. *The book *The Correction*'s* emanation-chain, in the order he gave it, is not quite right at the *prime matter* link.

Then, on the book *The Defense* side, the longer refutation. *As for the statement of the author of* the book *The Defense* *that it is not permissible to imagine that the products issue only from the pairing of one perfect and rich with another deficient and in need, so that they may be coupled by what is between them — and his likening of this to the heavens and the earth — he has refuted himself with the very words that followed.* The author uses *the book *The Defense*'s* own admission against him. *The book *The Defense** admitted that the metals, the forms, the plants, the animals — *none of which actually appear visibly in the heavens, nor in the earth, nor in the mothers, because they were in potency*. The whole catalog was *in potency in both* the heavens and the earth. The heavens and the earth are *equal* in respect of their being in potency for these products. The fact that the products appear *from* one of them (the earth) rather than the other (the heavens) is not because the heavens are perfect and the earth deficient. It is because of the *action* — the action goes from the heavens (which carry the active power) into the earth (which receives the action and brings what was in potency into actuality).

The author then states the doctrine of perfection that the whole chapter has been protecting. *We have shown that deficiency is found only in things — not in their selves, but in their ranks when they are added to one another, and in their actions when they do not exist except in something other than that which is done with their existence. In itself each is perfect; for if their selves were not perfect in the existence that befits them, nothing could be done by them — neither by themselves nor by another.*

This is the doctrinal weight. Every rank, *in itself*, is *perfect*, perfect in the existence that befits *it*, not in the existence that befits another rank. Deficiency is found *only* in things, not in their selves, but in their ranks *when added to one another*. The earth is not deficient *in itself*; the earth is deficient *only when added to the heavens*, in the comparison of two ranks. The heavens are not deficient *in themselves*; but the heavens are also *not absolutely perfect* — their perfection too lives in a rank, in a proportion. *The state of created things bears witness to this: the heaven, which has been singled out for perfection and artistry, is incomplete in its action, since the action is not performed except by that which it is performed upon, and the earth is specific in this regard.* The heaven, for all its perfection and artistry, *cannot act without the earth* — the action of the heaven is *performed* on the earth, and apart from the earth there is no action. *By way of its diminishing and its need, its deficiency lies not in itself but in its action; and even then, the action is performed only by the one who seeks it. The same holds for the animal and for newborns.*

The architecture the author is protecting is the architecture *Chapter Three* settled. Each rank, in itself, perfect. The chain holds by the proportions of the ranks added to one another, not by the literal deficiency of any rank in itself. *The book *The Defense*'s* heaven-and-earth marriage analogy, taken as a doctrine of substance-deficiency producing being through union with substance-perfection, smuggles body-categories back into ranks where bodies do not reach. The chain runs by emanation, by the running of power, by proportion, not by the *marriage* of substances.

And the closing turn. *If this is so, then the saying of the author of* the book *The Defense* — *that the statement of the author of* the book *The Correction* *is a contradictory and prejudiced statement — is itself contradictory and prejudiced.* The polemical accusation rebounds. *The book *The Defense** charged the book *The Correction* with contradiction; the author has shown that *the book *The Defense*'s* charge is itself the contradiction. The careful reader can hear the half-credit move again: the book *The Correction* was wrong at the *prime matter* link; the book *The Defense* was wrong on the marriage analogy. Each erred in his own way. Neither's word, taken alone, gives the whole architecture.

The Quranic picture of the heaven-and-earth relation is, in fact, not the picture of marriage between a perfect male and a deficient female. It is the picture of common obedience to a single originating call. *Surat Fussilat* puts the relation in the form of a question and an answer:

> *Then He turned to the heaven when it was smoke, and said unto it and unto the earth: Come both of you, willingly or loth. They said: We come, obedient.* — (Quran 41:11, Surah Fussilat; translation rendered after Pickthall)

Both ranks *come*. Neither is the male overflowing onto a deficient female; both respond *together* to the One who calls. The verse prefigures the doctrine the author has just settled. Deficiency lies not in any rank's *self* but in its proportion when added to another rank. The chain runs by the running of the originating power — *come, willingly or loth* — not by the marriage of substance to substance. The Quranic anchor closes sub-chapter three on the architecture *Chapter Three* of *this book* had already built: each rank, in itself, perfect in the existence that befits it; the chain holding by the proportion in which each rank receives, not by the splicing of perfect-with-deficient into a hybrid third.

## Sub-chapter four — the name *the doctrine of perfection*

Sub-chapter four turns to a narrower controversy. The author of the book *The Defense* complains that the author of the book *The Correction* *did not answer what was raised at the end of this chapter about the name* the doctrine of perfection *(perfection) falling upon the Creator Almighty, in order to make it fall upon complete creativity.*

The complaint is about a specific word — *the doctrine of perfection*, *perfection* — and where the word properly attaches. *The book *The Defense** charges that the book *The Correction* failed to answer a question about whether *the doctrine of perfection* (perfection) falls upon the Creator (and is then derivatively transferred to *complete creativity*), or whether it falls upon *complete creativity* directly.

The author's reply is short and decisive. *The author of* the book *The Correction* *mentioned nothing of the sort. The statement he quoted from the sages in the book of* the book *The Correction* — *that the perfect Creative is complete creativity, and that what is perfect was only perfect — is a saying than which none is more correct.* *The book *The Correction** did not, in fact, lay out the position the book *The Defense* charges him with. *The book *The Correction** quoted the sages on the doctrine: *the perfect Creative is complete creativity*. That is the saying. It is the saying *than which none is more correct*. And the saying obliges, by its own internal logic, the very conclusion the book *The Defense* wants to credit only to himself. *It obliges us to say that the Perfect and the perfected is the Creator Almighty, and that the name* the doctrine of perfection *is attributed to the Creator (swt). His statement contains nothing of what the author of* the book *The Defense* *ascribes to him. The author of* the book *The Defense**'s claim here is prejudice against the author of* the book *The Correction*.

The word *the doctrine of perfection* belongs to the Creator. The Creator is the Perfect and the perfected — the one to whom the name *the doctrine of perfection* attaches. The careful reading does not require the convoluted route the book *The Defense* charges the book *The Correction* with not having taken. *The book *The Correction** did not need to take that route. The doctrine arrives by the saying itself: the perfect Creative is complete creativity; the Perfect and the perfected is the Creator Almighty.

*The author of* the book *The Defense**'s claim here is prejudice against the author of* the book *The Correction*. That is the chapter's verdict. *The book *The Defense** is, here, not arguing in good faith; he is taking the chance to land a polemical hit on a saying he has misread.

## Sub-chapter five — the names that are synonyms by addition

Sub-chapter five extends the *the doctrine of perfection* discussion into a question about how the divine names work. The author of the book *The Defense* lays out his position. *We all say that creativity is the effect of the Creator, and that he and the Creator are, on the side of the Creator, one thing — which is perfection; and the Creative with complete creativity is only perfect. Nor do we need to say that the Creator is perfect, or that he is the cause, or that he — glorified and exalted — and the Creative are one thing.*

*The book *The Defense** is saying: we say creativity is the *effect* of the Creator; we say that on the side of the Creator he and his action (creativity) are *one thing* — namely, *perfection*; and we say that the *Creative* (the agent considered with his complete creativity) is *only perfect*. But — and this is the key — we do not need to say *additionally* that the Creator is *perfect*, or that he is *the cause*, or that he and the Creative are *one thing*. The names should not pile up beyond what is needed.

The author's reply is the chapter's most precise doctrinal turn on the divine names. *That is a correct statement, but we do not say that the Creator is complete creativity — only perfection — because creativity is perfection. Rather we say that the Creative is the perfection, only perfect, and the Creator is the creativity; he is the cause, the effect (al-the effect), the perfect, and the perfected. These names are synonymous according to the additions, because there is a multiplicity of meanings upon which these names fall, yet there is only one thing.*

The doctrine is dense. Let it land slowly. We do not say the Creator is *complete creativity*, because creativity is *itself* perfection. We say instead that the Creative is the *perfection*, *only perfect*; and the Creator is the *creativity*. And then the doctrinal payload: he is *the cause, the effect (al-the effect), the perfect, and the perfected*. Cause and effect — al-the effect — perfect and perfected. *These names are synonymous according to the additions.* Synonymous *according to the additions* — that is, the names are not literally one name; they differ in the aspect under which the named is considered. But on the side of the *named*, there is *only one thing*. The multiplicity is in the *meanings* upon which the names fall; the One upon which the names fall is *one*.

This is the same architecture *Chapter Four* settled at the close: the Intellect is the Word; the Word is the Intellect; not two things in relation, one thing called by two names according to the aspect under which it is being considered. Now the author is extending the same architecture to the divine names. Cause, effect, perfect, perfected — four names, multiple aspects, one named. The Creator is *not* a *thing with attributes*; the Creator is the One upon whom the names *fall* according to the additions. *Enough of the length.*

The phrase *enough of the length* is the author's signature close to a sub-argument he does not want to extend further. He has settled the names. The doctrine is in *the author's other treatise* — the author's *well-known book* — and the careful reader knows where to go for the full treatment. The chapter moves on.

## Sub-chapter six — the universal Soul did not seek; the human's two sides

Sub-chapter six brings the chapter to its widest doctrinal claim. The author of the book *The Defense* makes the structural argument that grounds his *fruit-of-the-higher-world* position. *If it is proven that this world is the act of the soul — and that it is nevertheless highly ordered and arranged, with perpetual circular motions — we know with certainty that in her action she sought to reach the noble rank before she actually began. Since this was so, no honorable being existed in the world that was not able to receive the effects of the soul from her. We know with certainty that human beings are the fruit of the soul's attainment of her end, in measure of her cause.*

The picture: the universal Soul acts upon this world; her action is ordered and arranged; therefore she was *seeking* something in her action — *the noble rank she had not yet attained*. The human being, in *the book *The Defense*'s* reading, is the *terminus* of her seeking — the fruit of her *attainment of her end*. *In measure of her cause.* So understand that.

The author refuses the picture of seeking.

*We have already said the following: the universal soul, in what is indicated of her, did not seek in her action to reach a rank she had not attained. The universal soul, which is the second, overflows upon this world not to be drawn to it for what is not hers — God enriched her when he originated her, with no need to seek what she was not given.*

The doctrine is crucial. The universal Soul — the Second — is not *needy*. God *enriched her when he originated her*. She was not given a partial gift to be supplemented by what she could seek in the act of overflow; she was given her *full* rank at origination. The overflow is *not* the Soul's instrument of self-completion. *Her action is to establish wisdom in bringing what is in potentiality into actuality.* The Soul does not act *to acquire* something for *herself*; the Soul acts to *establish wisdom* in the lower world — to bring what is in potentiality (in the substrate of *prime matter*) into actuality. Her action is the work of giving, not the work of getting. She is enriched at origination; her overflow is the surplus of her enrichment, not a search for what she lacks.

This refusal protects the architecture all the way up. If the Soul *sought* to perfect herself by her action upon this world, then the chain would run *up* — the higher would depend on the lower. The whole categorical refusal *Chapter Three* and *Chapter Four* laboriously built — that the higher does not stand in the relation of *needing* the lower, that the chain runs by overflow downward, that no rank above is *completed by* what occurs below — would collapse. *The book *The Defense*'s* seeking-formula smuggles in dependence of the higher on the lower. The author refuses it at the root.

And then the chapter's widest formula. *His existence is not delayed by what is above him, and if he meditates, the benefit accrues from what comes out of the action, and to the one who is beneath the task of being satisfied in perfection. No one is without cost, no one without need; and if he believes that in that great expanse there lies a mistake and a sin — if that is the case, then human beings have two sides: one part natural, one part divine. He is not the fruit of this world alone, nor the fruit of that world alone, but the fruit of all the worlds. He is the last in the universes, united in eternity with what has been acquired by time. Therefore it has been said that what came first of them was the last of them.*

*Human beings have two sides: one part natural, one part divine.* That is the chapter's settled formula on the human being. *One part natural* — from the channel of the bodies, the harvest of the natural world. *One part divine* — from the channel of the form, the harvest of the higher world. *He is not the fruit of this world alone, nor the fruit of that world alone, but the fruit of all the worlds.*

And then the deepest line, the one that has cost the chapter its labor: *He is the last in the universes, united in eternity with what has been acquired by time.*

*The last in the universes.* The human being is at the *end* of the chain — the rank at which the overflow comes to its harvest, beneath which there is no further rank. *United in eternity with what has been acquired by time.* The two faces of the human being held in one creature: *eternity* (the higher world, the rank that does not pass) *united* with *what has been acquired by time* (the bodily form, the work the soul does in and through the body, the precursor to the natural thing that endures). *In eternity, what has been acquired by time.* The work the soul does in the body in time *endures* in eternity. The bodily life is not lost in the higher harvest; the bodily life is *what the higher harvest is the harvest of*. *Therefore it has been said that what came first of them was the last of them.* What was first (the Intellect-which-is-the-Word, the originating rank) *is* the last (the rank at which the harvest arrives) — the chain is folded, the first and the last are one thing under two aspects, exactly the architecture *Chapter Four* settled.

The Quranic verse the *the call* tradition reaches for at this beat is the question put to the human being at the close of *Surat al-Mu'minun*:

> *Did you then think that We had created you in vain, and that you would not be returned to Us?* — (Quran 23:115, Surah al-Mu'minun; translation rendered after Yusuf Ali and Sahih International)

The verse names the question *Chapter Five's* sixth sub-chapter exists to settle. *Created you in vain* — that is the picture *the book *The Correction*'s* reading would underwrite if it were taken alone, the human being as fruit of *this* world and nothing else, the bodily life as the whole story. *Not be returned to Us* — that is the picture the book *The Defense* charged the book *The Correction* with underwriting, the materialist picture. The verse refuses the question's premise. The human being was *not* created in vain; the human being *will* be returned. The returning is *what* the human being is for. The fruit of all the worlds, *last in the universes, united in eternity with what has been acquired by time* — that is the rank at which the *return* is the harvest.

And the chapter's closing prayer. *Whoever knew the houses of the Prophets, the guardians, the imams, and the righteous — may God's prayers be upon them — from that, then he is ready for salvation. May God gather us with our pure loyalists in Dar the sanctified.*

*Whoever knew the houses of the Prophets, the guardians, the imams, and the righteous.* That is the work of the believer in time — to *know* the *the limits* — the *the speaker-prophet* (the Prophet ), the guardians (the *awsiya'*), the imams (the line that holds the *walaya*), and the righteous (those who have kept the covenant). To *know the houses* — to know the architecture, to recognize the rank, to receive the gift the chain conveys. *That, then he is ready for salvation.* The reception is the readiness; the readiness is the harvest of the human being's second perfection. *Dar the sanctified* — the House of the Holy, the abode at the brink of the upper world that is the human being's destination. *May God gather us with our pure loyalists.* The prayer is the chapter's signature: the human being's vocation is to be gathered, with the loyalists of the *the call*, in the abode the chain leads to.

## Sub-chapter seven — order, system, and the First's metaphorical action

Sub-chapter seven closes the chapter on a tighter philosophical point that runs beneath the whole. The author of the book *The Defense* — apparently in response to the sub-chapter-six denial that the universal Soul *seeks* — has pressed the question of *order* and *system*. *Every doer does what he does in pursuit of what he wishes to achieve, especially when the action is from the doer in movement, in order, and in affirmation.* If every doer acts in pursuit of what he wishes to achieve, and if the universal Soul's action *is* in movement, order, and affirmation, then the universal Soul *must* be pursuing something — which is the seeking-doctrine the author has just refused.

The author's reply distinguishes the action of the universal Soul from the action of the *First Creator and Originator*. *This is not necessary for the First Creator and Originator, because the action there is metaphorical, not in motion nor in order. Rather, the first is the last; the beginning of it is the middle, and the middle of it is the end. There is no order in it, no system; rather, the systems and order in what comes after benefit from the lights of what is not in order or system — that is the First Intellect.*

The *First Creator* (the absolute First, beyond all the ranks of emanation) is *not* a *doer* in the sense that *the book *The Defense*'s* general principle requires. The First's action is *metaphorical, not in motion nor in order*. In the First, *the first is the last; the beginning of it is the middle, and the middle of it is the end*. The First is not in time; the First is not in sequence; the First is not in arrangement. The order, the system, the arrangement that we see in the lower ranks — *the systems and order in what comes after* — *benefit from* the First's not being in order or system. They draw their order from what is *above* order. *The lights of what is not in order or system* — the First Intellect, the First's first issuance, the originating power that runs through the chain.

Then on the relation of the Soul to the Intellect. *The soul may be very similar to the intellect: if you look to her, you perceive the forces of things that are not orderly and not in arrangement, but you perceive them as a whole. Yet she has that character only if she unites with the intellect and pushes back the deformed religious things of dark nature.*

The Soul, considered apart from her union with the Intellect, sees in arrangement — sees in the order and system the lower world wears. The Soul, *united* with the Intellect, sees as the Intellect sees — *as a whole*, without the divisions of order and system. This is the character the Soul has *only* in her union with the Intellect. Apart from that union — when she lets the *deformed religious things of dark nature* (the corrupted doctrines that mistake the lower for the higher) press in upon her — she loses that character. The work of the believer is the work of the Soul: to push back what is deformed and to remain in her union with the Intellect.

The author then turns the polemical pressure back on the book *The Defense*. *How can what he said be true — that order and arrangement exist only from the intellect? Or how is it true that general things are preferred only by the intellect, and the soul is not affected by the effects of the intellect — so that she and the animals are alike? Why then has God lifted knowledge from the soul until the effects of the intellect bear upon her?*

The questions are rhetorical. *The book *The Defense** has, by his own statement, made the Soul into something less than what she is — as if the Soul stood off from the effects of the Intellect altogether, as if the *animals* (which have only the sensitive soul, not the speaking) and the speaking Soul were on the same footing. But that is absurd. The Soul *is* affected by the effects of the Intellect; the Soul *is* the rank at which the effects of the Intellect bear upon the lower world. *The book *The Defense*'s* framing has effectively *lifted knowledge from the soul* — and that the book *The Defense* cannot have meant, because his whole architecture of prophethood depends on the Soul being the rank at which knowledge from above reaches the lower world.

And the closing doctrinal turn. *We have already said that the issue does not lie with the actors: not every actor seeks by his action to attain a goal or purpose, which one might appreciate by saying that the First Intellect itself is not a vehicle, so it is orderly and arranged. But as for perception — the intellect perceives only the truth.*

The First Intellect is *not a vehicle* — not an instrument in *order to* something. The First Intellect *is* what it is, perfectly, in its place. From it the order and arrangement of the lower ranks unfold; but the First Intellect is not itself *in* order, *in* arrangement, *in* the seeking-after that *the book *The Defense*'s* general principle would require. *The intellect perceives only the truth.* That is the chapter's final word on the matter. The Intellect is the rank at which truth is *perceived* — not earned by seeking, not constructed by arrangement, but *perceived* as it is. The Soul, in her union with the Intellect, sees what the Intellect sees. The human being, fruit of all the worlds, receives what the Soul has perceived. The chain runs by perception, not by seeking; by overflow, not by acquisition; by emanation, not by marriage.

## The chapter's settled formula

The seven sub-chapters land on one settled formula. The human being is not the fruit of this world *alone*, as the author of the book *The Correction* (taken in isolation) had said. The human being is not the fruit of that world *alone*, as the author of the book *The Defense* (taken in isolation) had said. The human being is the fruit of *all* the worlds — *the last in the universes, united in eternity with what has been acquired by time*. From the natural world, the channel of the bodies, the human being receives his first perfection — the bodily form prepared to receive the three souls (vegetative, sensitive, speaking). From the world of the Intellect, the channel of the form, the human being receives his second perfection — the consummation of the spiritual image, the precursor to the natural thing, intrinsic to it, and which endures: the knowledge of the Oneness of Allah Almighty by means of the *the limits*, and obedience to them in knowledge and in deed. The doctrine is Imam the Fatimid caliph's, as stated in *the Fatimid caliph's interpretive treatise*: the human being is the *purpose of the second world and the end of the action of nature*. *Two sides — one part natural, one part divine.* That is the human being.

The architecture of the chain holds. The chain runs *by emanation* (not by marriage of perfect-and-deficient). The ranks are *perfect in themselves* (deficient only in their proportions when added to one another). The names of the Creator — cause, effect, perfect, perfected — are synonymous *according to the additions*, falling on a *multiplicity of meanings*, but on a *one thing* on the side of the named. The universal Soul *did not seek* a rank she had not attained — God enriched her at origination, and her overflow is the work of giving. The First Creator's action is *metaphorical, not in motion nor in order* — the lower ranks draw their order from what is *above* order. And the believer's work, in the human being's middle station, is the work of *knowing the houses of the Prophets, the guardians, the imams, and the righteous* — the architecture of the *the limits* — and thereby being *ready for salvation*, gathered in *Dar the sanctified*.

The political stake sits, as always, one layer below the metaphysics. If the human being were the fruit of this world only, the *Ma'ad* would be a story; the *the call* architecture — the *the limits*, the chain of conveyance of the gift from the higher world — would be empty machinery. If the human being were the fruit of that world only, the bodily life would be incidental — and the *sharia*, the long discipline of obedience and worship in and through the body, would not be the work that *earns* the second perfection. The author's settlement protects both. The bodily life is the *field* in which the work of the soul is done. The work of the soul, done in the body in time, is what is *acquired by time* and *united in eternity*. The *the limits* convey the gift through the chain into the soul of the believer who is doing the work. The believer who knows the houses is the one who is *ready for salvation*. The architecture of *sharia*, of *walaya*, of the *Ma'ad*, of the *Dar the sanctified*, is the architecture of the rank at which the two overflows meet — the rank where the human being stands, the fruit of all the worlds, the last in the universes, united in eternity with what has been acquired by time.

The Sufi tradition has the same architecture of the *fruit* in compressed form. a later teacher, the Shadhili teacher of Mamluk Alexandria, condensed it into a single aphorism in his *Hikam*:

> *Bury your existence in the earth of obscurity, for whatever sprouts forth, without first being buried, flowers imperfectly.* — (a later teacher, Hikam, Aphorism 11)

The aphorism names, in the vocabulary of a different tradition, the architecture *Chapter Five* has been protecting. The first perfection — the bodily form, the natural composite, the field that the chain of nature works up to — is the *earth of obscurity* in which the soul is buried. The second perfection — the consummation of the spiritual image, the harvest of the higher world — is the *fruit* that grows from the burial. *Whatever sprouts forth, without first being buried, flowers imperfectly*: that is the work that is *acquired by time* and *united in eternity*. The Shadhili master and the Fatimid a teacher, working five centuries and a continent apart, converge on the same picture. The human being is the fruit of all the worlds *because* the burial in the bodily field is what makes the harvest of the higher world possible. The two perfections are not two stories told in parallel; they are one architecture, in which the lower is the *condition* of the higher's harvest.

## What comes next

*Chapter Six* of *this book* will take the next step. If the human being is the fruit of all the worlds, formed in the meeting of two overflows, then the question becomes one about the *form* — about motion and stillness, about *prime matter* (the substrate, prime matter) and the form impressed upon it, about how the natural composite that the chain reaches comes to be the *natural composite at all*. *Chapter Six* will work the nine sub-chapters of *Bab 6* on these terms and settle the doctrine of the substrate that *Chapters Four* and *Five* relied on. The reader carries forward what *Chapter Five* has settled: that the human being is the rank at which the chain comes to its harvest, that the architecture of *sharia* and the *Ma'ad* rests on this rank, and that the work the soul does in the body in time is the *precursor to the natural thing, intrinsic to it, and which endures*.

And before that next chapter opens, *Chapter Five* leaves the reader with the image it has earned. The two reformers arguing across decades, the book *The Correction* with his *fruit of this world*, the book *The Defense* with his *fruit of the higher world*. The author, generations later, arriving at the third word: *fruit of all the worlds*. The believer who knows the houses, ready for salvation, gathered in *Dar the sanctified*. The architecture holds. The doctrine is settled. The human being stands, fruit of all the worlds, last in the universes, united in eternity with what has been acquired by time.
