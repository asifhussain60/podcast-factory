# Tawhid and the Critique of al-Mahsul

*This chapter covers all sixteen sub-chapters of* Chapter Ten *of* Kitab al-Riyad — *al-Kirmani's closing critique of the book of* al-Mahsul *on the question of* tawhid *(taw-heed — monotheism) and* al-mubdi al-awwal *(al-mub-di al-aw-wal — the First Creator). The whole book has been building toward this last and highest joint of the chain. Through nine chapters the architecture has risen — from the universal Soul and the First Intellect through the doctrines of the soul's perfection, the chain of emanation, the soul's relation to time, soul-and-spirit, souls-as-parts-or-traces, the human-as-fruit-of-the-worlds, motion and stillness and* al-hayula *(al-ha-yoo-laa — prime matter), the sections of the world, the doctrine of* qada *and* qadar*, and the law of Adam and the* wilayah *(wi-laa-yah — guardianship) of Nuh.* Chapter Ten *turns at last to the rank that stands above all the others — the rank of God Himself, the* al-mubdi *(al-mub-di — the Originator) above whom there is no rank. The author of* al-Mahsul *(al-mah-sool) — the lost-work that started the* al-Sadayn *(as-sa-dayn — the Two Reformers) cycle of correction and re-correction — had written a chapter on the* first thing in what was first to fix*: tawhid and the First Creator. Abu Hatim al-Razi (a-boo haa-tim ar-raa-zee), in writing the* al-Islah *(al-is-laah), had passed over much of what required correction; people read his silence as approval, and read al-Mahsul's chapter as sound. Al-Kirmani enters at the close of his own treatise — having promised the work at the book's beginning — to* discharge the right of religion *by stating what is implicated in al-Mahsul's chapter and what it is* not permissible to believe*. Sixteen sub-chapters of patient point-by-point critique on the thresholds where philosophical language meets the rank that admits no language: the wrongness of* Creator of the thing*; the metaphysical incoherence of* nothing with Him*; the impossibility of describing the First by borrowed words; the doctrine that the intellect mentioned in al-Mahsul's text is not the First Intellect but the* acquired intellect *of the prophets, foundations, and imams; the refutation of* unity is the cause of the Intellect *(unity* is *the Intellect, the Intellect* is *the Word, the Word* is *the cause, the cause* is *creativity, creativity* is *the Creator); the refusal of the* Intellect-as-intermediary *doctrine; and the closing settlement: tawhid is difficult, and Allah has from time to time selected* one man *with the perfection of receiving the overflow from the angels-near-Him — the Imam, peace be upon him — by holding to whom the believers reach the* two worships *that are the* zahir *(thaa-hir) and* batin *(baa-tin) of the religion, and ascend through the chain to* Dar al-Quds *(daar al-quds — the Abode of Holiness, Jerusalem). The book closes with the chapter, and the chapter closes with the prayer.*

## Where this chapter picks up

*Chapter Nine* settled the chain at the head of human history — Adam (peace be upon him) had a Shariʿah; Seth (peace be upon him) was his asas (a-saas — foundation); every speaker requires a guardian; the cycle of speakers and imams runs through Noah, Abraham, Moses, Jesus, and the Prophet Muhammad (peace and blessings of Allah be upon him); the *opposite* who learned from the prophet was washed away in *the flood that spread outwardly and inwardly* — the type recurs at every cycle until the seventh. The chain runs.

*Chapter Ten* turns to the highest joint of all. What stands at the very head, above the First Intellect (the Pen — *al-Qalam* (al-qa-lam)), above the universal Soul (the Tablet — *al-Lawh* (al-lawh))? What is the rank of *al-mubdi* — Allah Himself, the rank that admits no language by which a creature might describe it, and yet must be *believed* if the chain that runs from Him is to be believed in any of its parts? Al-Mahsul wrote on this question and erred; Abu Hatim passed over the errors; al-Kirmani enters to complete the work — *I had the right to cooperate in discharging the right of religion by stating what is implicated in what I have mentioned — what it is not permissible to believe regarding the chapter of monotheism and the first Creator.*

The chapter is sustained polemic of the most difficult kind — polemic at the rank where every word the polemicist uses is itself a *created* and *originated* word, ill-fitted to its target. The strategy is *negative critique*: not to *describe* what God is — for that is precisely what cannot be done — but to *refuse* the descriptions al-Mahsul ventured, and to *show* by their refusal what is *not permissible to believe*.

## Sub-chapter one — why al-Kirmani writes where Abu Hatim was silent

The author of *al-Mahsul*, may Allah have mercy on him, *did not exert himself, exaggerate, and apply his mind to the right of the daʿwa and its sons, nor to opening the doors of landmarks with what he made from the books of those in charge — he postponed it*. Sheikh Abu Hatim, may God have mercy on him, *did not correct it, though he was able*. Most people read this as Abu Hatim stabbing at the author of *al-Mahsul* and then offering apology — passing over the errors so that they were reversed into apparent doctrine, which is *a corrupt judgment*. The matter, al-Kirmani says, *is different from what they imagined*.

The Speaker (peace be upon him), *when he knew, at the time of laying down things, that the sciences of religion are too many to encompass... arranged them through many people without the imamate, in order that they should cooperate among themselves over all the sciences and their awareness, and memorize them, like the twelve hujjas* (huj-jahs — the twelve proofs), *so that each of them benefits from those to whom Allah has granted the guidance of the righteous successors* (al-aimma al-bararah — al-a-im-mah al-ba-ra-rah) *— peace and blessings be upon them — the secret of things, their inner and outer aspects, according to their strength in receptivity and deduction, so that each may serve as a guide for others*.

The image is precise: *they are like one person who gathers his parts together, and they are like the senses: each perceives things, and if one of them misses something which the other perceives, the perception of this second one is not a defect for the one who missed the perception, nor for the others*. The eye misses what the ear catches; the ear misses what the hand feels — and none is shamed by what another perceives. Abu Hatim missed certain corrections in *al-Mahsul*; that is not a fault in Abu Hatim, nor proof that *al-Mahsul* was right. Al-Kirmani steps in *to cooperate in discharging the right of religion by stating what is implicated in what I have mentioned*. The promise was made at the book's beginning; the chapter discharges it.

> *We are limbs of a single body, the body of the daʿwa. The hand does not shame the foot, nor the eye the ear. When one limb sees a knot the other passed over, it is its right to tie it; that is the very meaning of being one body.* — al-Kirmani, *Rahat al-'Aql* (raa-hat al-aql — *The Repose of the Intellect*), on the cooperation of the *hudud* (hu-dood — limits) in the sciences of religion

The framing is essential. The chapter is not denunciation; it is *cooperation in discharging the right of religion* — because *the souls of religion are all one*. Al-Kirmani closes the framing with the prayer to be gathered *with the former, the reciprocal, the preceding, and the latecomer among our pure loyalists* (mawali — ma-waa-lee) *in Dar al-Quds, a noble station*.

## Sub-chapter two — *Creator of the thing*: the threefold impossibility

The author of *al-Mahsul* opens the chapter of monotheism with what looks like a careful formulation: *He is the creator of the thing — the rational thing, the illusory, the intellectual, the logical — I mean what is beneath these elements, and what is not beneath them*. The phrase *what is not beneath them* is the trap. By saying it, al-Mahsul has made *necessary* — by his own framing — that among the possessions of Allah Almighty which lie under His creativity is *that which is not perceived by reason, illusion, or thought, and which is not spoken of by logic, and yet its existence is impossible, while believing in it is permissible*. Al-Kirmani undertakes to show that this is *not believable*.

The argument is a threefold sweep. *Whatever is not perceived by reason, illusion, or thought, and is not spoken of by logic, must, if it exists, either precede reason in its existence, or be with reason in its existence, or follow reason in its existence*. Three positions; each must be ruled out.

*Precedes reason*: impossible. *Reason is with the act, behind which is none but Allah Almighty, who has issued it and from whom it has come into being — and it is purely created* (ibdaʿ — ib-daa), *and the true existence* (al-wujud al-haqq — al-wu-jood al-haqq) *which is the first existing thing from Allah Almighty*. The First Intellect *just is* the *first existing thing from Allah*, and nothing precedes it but Allah Himself. The first position falls.

*Is with reason*: impossible. *The intellect, when it occurs, is only one self and not two*. Were the supposed other to exist *with* the Intellect — either it is the very same (no second added), or distinct from it while sharing existence. If distinct, then *Allah Almighty had brought forth from Himself a multitude of creative causes, or that there is together with Him another Almighty — may He be exalted above that*. The architectural rule is firm: from the One issues the One; the moment we postulate a second alongside the Intellect, we either multiply causes (denying God's unity) or multiply effects from one cause (denying the architectural rule from which the chain runs). *Allah is holy above all multiplicity, additions, and othernesses*. The second position falls.

*Follows reason*: impossible. *If its existence were after the intellect, then about it the minds would not be devoid of knowledge of it. Nor would it be hard for them to define it; rather they would have known and defined it, just as they did all the other assets, sensible and intelligible*. Whatever exists *after* the Intellect, in the ranks below, is by definition within the field where the Intellect knows and defines. To posit something *after* the Intellect that is yet *not perceived by reason, illusion, or thought* is to posit a square circle. The third position falls.

*Since he was not devoid of three sections, each of which obliged that his existence be impossible, it is proven that his existence is impossible, and to say it is misguidance*. Al-Mahsul has, in his very opening sentence, made obligatory the believing-in of something whose existence is impossible. This is the first error.

## Sub-chapter three — *the thing* and *nothing*: the sophistry of positive negation

Al-Mahsul might be defended by a retreat. Perhaps by *the thing* he meant only bodies, and by *nothing* only the attributes of bodies. If he had said only that, *we would have said that by saying "the thing" he intended what was described as bodies, and as "nothing" except for the attributes of bodies*. But the defense fails because of what al-Mahsul *himself* added — *and what is not under them*. That clause locks the door.

The deeper move is on the word *thing* itself. *"The thing" has come to be known as a common term under which fall all the assets of essences and accidents — sensible and intelligible*. What *some of the wise men* have said is that *nothing* is *an affirmative for what is other than something, affirmatively stripped of what the thing is confined to in all its parts of essence and accidents, and elevated to the corrected state*. This is the sophistical point at the heart of the chapter: the word *nothing*, in the hands of philosophers, has been quietly converted from *the absence of a thing* into a *positive predicate denoting that which is other than every thing*. *Nothing* becomes a *something* — a *not-the-thing*. This is precisely the conversion al-Mahsul makes when he says *the Creator of the thing is no thing — He and no thing with Him*.

Al-Kirmani gives the standard example. *The human being who is an affirmative for everything that is not human — the informer thereby strips us of existence, without affirming what is not for anything*. To say *not the human* might be heard as a positive affirmation of *the non-human* — but in fact *a letter intended not to be a letter has [no] reality removing it from existence*. Saying *not the letter* does not bring into being a *non-letter*; it only denies the letter to a certain place.

But al-Mahsul, by saying *nothing with Him*, has done precisely the forbidden thing: he has made *nothing* into a *something* that stands *with* God. The grammar betrays the metaphysics. When al-Mahsul writes *nothing with Him*, what he has actually written is *some other thing alongside Him* — and that is shirk in the very chapter on monotheism.

## Sub-chapter four — *the Creator of His Creator*: the doctrine's reductio

The grammar argument has a reductio. The Almighty is *neither essence nor accident*; therefore *these become attributes a thing has no attribute in, nor of, nor like* — and the formulation *the Creator of the thing and of nothing is the Creator of Himself* collapses into *the Creator of His Creator*, *who is other than the thing, and the creator of the thing is other than Himself; and that is wrong, even if his intention was to negate the very thing*.

In plain form: *the thing is creativity, and it is the very eye of existence; and "nothing" is the negation of creativity, the nullification of existence. And if nothing is the negation of creativity, how can it be true that He is creative, when He has denied creativity from the impossible?* If *the thing* is the eye of creativity, and *nothing* its negation, then *He is the Creator of the thing and of nothing* says *He is the Creator of His own act of creation* — which cannot be said. The Almighty does not *create His own act*; His act *issues from Him*. The act *just is* the First Created Thing.

> *Allah bears witness that there is no god but He — and the angels and those of knowledge — upholding justice. There is no god but He, the Almighty, the Wise.* (Quran 3:18)

The verse al-Kirmani will return to in sub-chapter four below sits in the background of every move here. Allah witnesses to His own unicity *by His own name* — not by saying *except Allah* in the place where one would expect an exception clause. The grammar of *there is no god but He* contains *only His name*; the exception clause has been collapsed into the very name being excepted. This is the only kind of language that points to Him without thereby creating-and-originating a second alongside Him.

## Sub-chapter five — *He and no thing with Him*: the unsayable made worse

Al-Mahsul presses on with what he takes to be the orthodox formulation: *The Creator of things is not of anything; only He and nothing with Him — He is still He and nothing with Him. We have denied the thing and the nothing, and we have made them all creative. We have denied every simple and complex image of who He is, and we have made everything from what happens*.

The phrase *He and nothing with Him* might sound like the strongest possible expression of tawhid. Al-Kirmani shows that it is, in fact, *shirk-in-language-form*. *It may be that the words "the Creator of things is sufficient for the sake of nothing," and the words "the Creator of things is nothing only, He and nothing with Him," still involve what is not permissible to believe — namely that "nothing" is positive for what is not, in the eyes of the wise. So if he says "nothing with Him," it is as if he said "it and what is not with Him, He is still" — and that is shirk*.

The trap is the *with*. The word *with* requires *two things*. If *nothing* is *the absence of a thing*, the formula says *He alone*. But if *nothing* is the philosopher's positive predicate, the formula says *He and the not-the-thing alongside Him* — two things, shirk.

That no language reaches Him follows from metaphysics, not piety. *Among the tongues that exist between human beings, there is no language able to express Allah in a manner appropriate to Him. The words and letters of the composite and the simple are all originated, and words, letters and languages are instruments by which humans understand each other regarding what is within themselves; they are originated, and they only indicate what was in the state of one's affairs*. *We have explained this in our treatise known as al-Riyad* — al-Kirmani's self-reference to the present book.

*He, the Almighty, is behind the minds and the perceptions, and behind a veil from understandings and thought, beyond which the paths of inquiry are cut off*. *The Almighty is not described by a word borrowed in its affirmative without proving an attribute or a plurality, except by a word that Allah has affirmed after exception*.

The exception that proves Him is the verse of self-witness — *His saying, "Allah bears witness that there is no god but He," where He did not say "except Allah"*. Allah's own witness is given by *His own name placed after the negation of every god* — not by an exception clause that would create a second. The grammar of *la ilaha illa huwa* names Him without placing a second alongside Him. And the pronoun *huwa* (He) is the only borrowed word that may stand in His place — for it is *among the minds positive for what is outside of it, without any attribute or plurality*.

> *Imam al-Mu'izz li-Din Allah Ma'add ibn Isma'il (peace be upon him) indicated this in his well-known book. He said that "it"* (huwa) *is only Him, even if it is a borrowed word, for it is among the minds positive for what is outside of it, without any attribute or plurality.* — Imam al-Mu'izz, *Ta'wil al-Shara'i'* (ta-weel ash-sha-raa-i — *Interpretation of the Laws*), on the unique grammatical privilege of the pronoun *huwa* to point past every attribute and every plurality without itself becoming a second

The Cairo-court authority lands the linguistic doctrine. *Huwa* — the third-person singular pronoun — bears no attribute, makes no addition, does not multiply. To say *He* is not to predicate anything of Him; it is only to point past every predicate. *Huwa* is the threshold at which language stops short of Him without falling into shirk.

*The conduct of the path is established by the use of a letter that does not negate any kind of existence — more informed in the meaning of safety than the analogy, the disjunction, and the first*. The path of safe speech is the path that refuses *every* positive predicate and accepts only the bare pronoun. Even this is concession; the truth is that *the paths of inquiry are cut off*.

## Sub-chapter six — and the *no thing* falls under creativity, or it doesn't

Al-Mahsul has said: *if we say that He and no thing with Him, we have denied the thing and the nothing, and we have become naïve of all the prodigy*. The maneuver: he has denied *both* — *the thing* and *the no-thing* — and so reached *purification beyond every prodigy*.

Al-Kirmani refuses the maneuver on the same ground. The *thing* — taken as the rank of pure creativity — is what Allah originates; it is *other than Allah*, the Finder. *Nothing*, then, is *the negation of the eye of the thing, which is pure creativity, according to what he himself has said. And if nothing is the negation of the eye of creativity — which is the eye of existence and the truth of existence — how does it become creative in existence? These statements are not true*.

The internal contradiction is plain. Al-Mahsul says *the thing is creativity*; he says *no thing with Him*; therefore *no creativity with Him* — and yet *He is creative*. Either *no creativity* — in which case how is He creative? — or *creativity with Him* — two things, shirk. Neither choice leaves the formulation standing.

## Sub-chapter seven — *every simple and complex image*: when negation slips into affirmation

Al-Mahsul writes: *We have denied every simple and complex image of His identity, and we have made everything out of it. It is up to Him to say or not to say that He is creative and is influenced by the truth*. The doctrine in plain language: *we have denied every conceivable image of God, simple or complex, and made everything that exists derive from Him; let Him say what He will about His own creativity*.

The trap is the second clause: *we have made everything out of it*. The phrase *out of it* converts the supposed denial of every image into a positive affirmation. *The saying about which the findings were found, what does not fall upon Him, all of them are creative — is that not a falsification?* If you say *all of them are creative*, you have affirmed creativity as a *substrate* from which all creative things issue — and creativity has thereby become a *something*, alongside the One. The negation has slipped into affirmation through the back door of the preposition.

## Sub-chapter eight — *nothing after the thing*: the empty negation

Al-Mahsul: *Nothing after the thing, because the occurrence of its name is the appearance of something*. Al-Kirmani returns the formula to its bones. *Nothing is not a name that falls upon an eye that has a share in existence*. Nothing has no referent. *"The thing" is a name, and the one who is the letter of the pure has not been denied, and the negation of the name does not become a noun except for what the name has a name*. The negation of a name is not itself a name — except in the sophistical sense in which philosophers have made *nothing* into a positive predicate. *It is not told about it except what the wise have mentioned: that it involves the affirmation of something that has no name. The earlier words contain the answer to this, and it is sufficient*.

The cumulative point: al-Mahsul's chapter has erected, in the language of orthodox-sounding tawhid, a metaphysics in which *nothing* has become a positive predicate alongside Allah. Every formula al-Mahsul writes uses *nothing* in the philosopher's positive sense, and every formula thereby commits the very shirk it claims to escape.

> *Nothing is like Him — and He is the Hearing, the Seeing.* (Quran 42:11)

The Qur'anic anchor is in the negation of likeness, not in the assertion of a positive entity called *no-thing*. *Nothing is like Him* says *the comparison-relation has no second term that is Him*; it does not say *there is an entity called No-Thing with Him*. The Quran's negation is *the path that does not negate any kind of existence* — exactly the path al-Kirmani has been pointing to. The pronoun *He* (huwa) is given; the comparison is denied; the predicates *Hearing* and *Seeing* are given as His act-toward-the-world, not as additions to His essence.

## Sub-chapter nine — the Intellect with all the images: the acquired intellect, not the First

The chapter pivots from God-talk to Intellect-talk. Al-Mahsul has written: *In the first chapter of the Creator, the Almighty created the world all at once. This is because He created the mind in one sentence, and in it the images of the two worlds emerged, and what is within them — without those images being referred to by their identities at the intellect. But the science of the mind is based on it, and it has information, except that the mind has already advanced upon it, either by act, but by force — it and the images all together*.

The doctrine in plain form: *God created the Intellect; in the Intellect were created, all at once, the images of the two worlds; those images are not present in the Intellect by their individual identities but by force* (i.e., potentially). The doctrine has a structural attraction — it gives the universe a single creative moment and locates all subsequent unfolding as the actualization of what was already present in potency. But the doctrine confuses the *First Intellect* with what al-Kirmani has all along been calling the *acquired intellect* — the intellect of the speakers, foundations, imams, and their followers, which *ascends from the natural world by acquiring work and knowledge in parallel with the first emanation*.

Al-Kirmani is firm. *This science — the mind that is said to have been created, and in which the images of the worlds and what is in them emerged — cannot be the First Intellect*. The First Intellect *does not need the perception of what is within it*; its *existence is related to its Creator who created it, and it does not need to know more than its knowledge of itself, that it is creative, and that its existence is not by itself but by another who is its Creator*. The First Intellect is *self-knowing alone*. It is *the cause of the existence of what is below it, like the Imam who does not need to know others below him: he knows only that he is the cause of salvation without him, the mediator between those above him and those below him*.

The First Intellect knows *itself and the One above it*; it does not know *the multitude below it as multitude*. The intellect that knows *all the images of both worlds* is the *acquired intellect* — the intellect that has *risen* through the chain by *work and knowledge* until it *gathers* the images. *What he mentioned is the intellect that emanates from the natural world, not the First Intellect*. The doctrinal move is the same as *Chapter Eight*'s sub-chapter nine. To conflate them is to import a fullness into the head of the chain that does not belong there.

*Indeed, it is not permissible to say that there is something within it potent by force, because if it were so, it should be preceded by the type of its existence already existing in act, so that it brings it out into act — and this is invalidated by existing with the First Mind*. Potency requires an actuality of the same kind to bring it into act. To say the First Intellect contains the images *in potency* is to require a *prior actuality of the same images* — and that would have to *precede the First Intellect*, which is impossible.

The intellect that has the images is *the second emanation from the world of nature* — the intellect of the speaker-Prophet, the foundation, the imam, the hujjah, the da'i — *the chain of the hudud of the daʿwa* (daa-wah) that rises by acquisition and gathers all the images of both worlds in its full receivership.

## Sub-chapter ten — *unity is the cause of the Intellect*: unity *is* the Intellect

Al-Mahsul writes: *When the mind's cause was the unity of the Almighty and the unity of eternity, the mind became eternal*. The doctrine: *unity (al-tawhid) is the cause of the Intellect; the Intellect, having unity for its cause, is itself eternal*.

Al-Kirmani refuses the doctrine at its root. *He has made it obligatory by positing something that precedes the First Intellect in its existence — namely that cause which he called unity. And we have said that the intellect does not precede in its existence anything other than Allah, in the first place*. To say *unity is the cause of the Intellect* is to posit *unity* as a rank *between Allah and the First Intellect* — and there is no such rank.

The deeper move is that *unity is not other than the Intellect*. *Loneliness is not a cause of reason, and it is other than it until it advances upon the intellect. Rather it is the intellect itself, and the intellect is the unity, which is the One*. The chain of identities at this rank: *unity = the Intellect = the One = the Word = the cause = creativity = the Creator = the Perfect = the Eternal = Existence = the Essence*. *It is the cause; it is the effect; it is the creativity; it is the Creator; and it is the Perfect*.

This is the chapter's central doctrinal move. The First Intellect, at its rank, *is* the unity, *is* the Word, *is* the eye of creativity that issues from Allah. The names *do not add* to it; they *name* it from different angles. *The meanings these names require are worth adding to what His existence is — which is Allah, the Almighty — or by adding Him to what is below Him, or by adding it to itself*. The names are adverbs of attention upon a single rank; they are not names of distinct ranks.

*If that is so, then also, when the unity was the same as the intellect, he said that the intellect became eternal. There is no eternity of its cause, for the intellect is nothing but the cause, so it becomes a cause eternal — but it is him, and he is it*. The supposed eternity-from-the-cause is no eternity from a separate cause; it is the eternity *of the very thing itself*. *That is only true in the minds that emerge from the natural world, such as the Speakers* (al-nuṭaqāʾ — an-nu-ta-qaa) *and the Foundations* (al-asas) *and their followers — whose minds are something, and by means of them they are humbled by something else, the reason that they are holy and eternal being that the eternal paradox of minds called the close angels extends their lights to them, and they unite with it*. At the rank of the First Intellect itself, the doctrine collapses; its eternity *is* its own being.

## Sub-chapter eleven — unity-as-other-than-Allah: three impossibilities

Al-Kirmani presses the critique with a triple impossibility against the doctrine that *unity is other than the Intellect and the cause of the Intellect*.

First: if unity is taken as a *thing other than Allah* whose existence is *not by itself*, then *God Himself* (whom al-Mahsul has called *the Unitarian*) becomes *originated-after-being-creative*, *by being based on others* — impossible.

Second: *because unity is other than Allah, and that which is other than Allah was creative-originated, it has become obligatory that God, whom He called unity, be creative and originated; and this is impossible*. If *unity* is other than Allah, then it is originated; if it is God, then God is originated.

Third: it is invalid that *unity is the source from which the image flows*, because at this rank *the image just is creativity, and creativity just is the Creator*. To posit *unity behind the source* is to posit *something behind the source* — and behind the source there is only Allah, who is not described by any predicate. *Glory be to Allah Almighty beyond the multiplication of images*.

Fourth: *the mind was created not for the sake of unity; for if unity had what was created, it would not have existed. It has created a multitude, and it has found a multitude — so there is no unity if it exists*.

*And when these aspects are invalidated, the meanings of saying that the cause of the unity of the mind is refrained from. It has been proven that the eye of unity and the cause are the same*. *Unity* and *the First Intellect* and *the cause* and *the Word* and *creativity* and *the Creator* — at the rank of the First Emanation — are *one name for one rank*.

## Sub-chapter twelve — *the form became eternal*: only of the acquired intellects

The author of *al-Mahsul* continues: *Since the form was made manifest in it from solitude, the form likewise became eternal for it, because the intellect became eternal in its entirety, not merely in part*. The doctrine: *because the form-of-the-images was manifested in the Intellect when the Intellect issued from the Solitary (Allah), the form too became eternal — because the Intellect is wholly eternal, not partially eternal*.

Al-Kirmani applies the same correction. *This is an attribute of something within whose power lies the image projected upon it; and it is not permissible to say this in accordance with the supreme limit which already exists, but only by force. And if it is so, then it is not correct to say what he said except in the case of the intellects which emerge from the intellects — such as the intellects of the prophets, the foundations, the imams and their followers, peace be upon them — who came forth into intellect through their own words. This is the paradoxical intellect called the proximate sovereign, nicknamed the Pen, in its entirety and timelessness. Their intellects, and the intellects of those who follow them upon the truth, attain this, and praise it by their speech*.

The careful distinction: *the form became eternal in the Intellect* is *true* — but *only of the acquired intellects*. The acquired intellect of the prophet, the foundation, the imam — *attain* timelessness by *uniting with the paradoxical intellect called the proximate sovereign* — the rank that is *nicknamed the Pen* (al-Qalam) in the *daʿwa*'s technical vocabulary. The forms become eternal *in them* because they have *attained* the rank of the Pen.

But al-Kirmani notes a deeper concern that drives al-Mahsul's formulation. *The author of al-Mahsul would not have said what he said had the forms not been creative in eternity; otherwise they would have no permanence, no return to the substrate of their origin from which they came forth with the form. They would have perished and vanished, and there would be no hope, no fear, no reward, no recompense, no punishment — for everything whose cause is eternal in it becomes eternal in it*. The motive behind the formulation is *good*: al-Mahsul wants to preserve the doctrine of *permanence* — the doctrine that the forms-once-received-by-souls *endure*, and *the souls return to the substrate of their origin* — and that *reward and punishment* therefore have *real footing*. Without permanence, there is no *al-yawm al-akhir* (the Last Day), no Paradise, no Hell.

Al-Kirmani concedes the motive and corrects the formulation. *If he says this, it implies that the said forms have no permanence and no connection with their substrates except by the eternal force, which is the one in the world of nature, derived from the souls which do not pass into action, and which gain permanence and continuance only for those who have attained eternity and perfection*. The permanence of the forms is sustained by the *eternal force* — the *acquired intellect* of the perfected ones — *not* by being native to the First Intellect. *Were it not for these, reward and punishment would be invalidated according to what he said. For the speaker and the imams, his position is correct because of this; so the meaning of what he said is not valid except in the intellects which emerge from the world of nature into the world of creation*.

The correction preserves the soteriological work al-Mahsul was trying to do — without the metaphysical error of locating it at the wrong rank.

## Sub-chapter thirteen — the Word and the Intellect: *no third intermediary*

The author of *al-Mahsul*: *And for us the intellect was made eternal by the eternity we have explained in the composite saying the Word of God, and there was no third intermediary between them, so the intellect became like it*. The doctrine: *the Intellect is eternal by the eternity of the Word of God; there is no third entity between the Word and the Intellect; therefore the Intellect is like the Word*.

Al-Kirmani: *His saying leads to the meanings mentioned above, and is not worthy. It is not appropriate for the First Intellect, because it is an essence said to be identical with the Word — neither something else, nor something that was and then became like it. All this applies to the intellects which proceed from potency into act in the abode of nature, which become so by accepting the effects of the Word — that is, the First Intellect — and unite with it, as he confirms when he says: "For us, the form of action has become the form of the intellect."*

The First Intellect *is identical with* the Word — not *like* the Word. *Likeness* requires *two things alike*; *identity* is *one thing under two names*. To say the Intellect *became like* the Word is to introduce duality at the very rank where the architecture has been arguing that duality cannot stand. The formula *the Intellect became like the Word* is appropriate only of the *acquired intellects* — the intellects of the prophets and their followers — which *become like the First Intellect by uniting with it through receiving the effects of the Word*.

*The intellect is thus a cause of what is below it, and has become surrounded by the Word in what the Creator sees within Him. For He whose form has become the picture of it in the Word, and who has become a cause for what is below him, requires that he was not previously a cause and had no form in the Word — and such is the case with the intellects of the prophets, not the creative intellect without intermediary, which has never been other than the Word, nor is it the cause of being. Its existence is first and foremost — that without which the descriptions are broken off; it grows and is exalted*.

*We have spoken of this above concerning this supreme limit, and that is enough about prolonging the matter. Were it so, then the procedure of that supreme limit would be the course of these lower boundaries, which is impossible*. The First Intellect is not the kind of rank in which formulas drawn from the *lower boundaries* apply. To apply the formulas of the lower ranks at the rank of the First Intellect is to *collapse the ranks* — and the collapse of the ranks is the collapse of the whole architecture by which the chain runs.

## Sub-chapter fourteen — *creativity from the Creator*: the perilous identification

Al-Mahsul: *Since this is so, it is called creativity, because creativity is from the Creator; in complete creativity, only the perfect arises*. The doctrine: *what proceeds from the Creator is called creativity, and only the perfect proceeds from the perfect Creator*.

Al-Kirmani: *We have already said that his saying is mistaken in two respects*. The first error: *he called the intellect the creator and made it like its Creator, of whom he said it is perfect; this is shirk, and it is not permissible to say, because God is far above being identical in His essence to that which exists by His creative act*. To call *the Intellect the creator* — even in the qualified sense of *the creator-from-the-Creator* — is to *identify* the Intellect with God in essence, which is shirk.

The second error: *he applied the name of Creator to what belongs to the natural world, and that is impossible — because the one who deserves the name of creativity is the Being who is advanced over all things, not the thing which is itself created. If this is the case, then it is not permissible to believe what he said except in a way that is correct*. *Creator* is a name that applies to the rank that *originates*; to apply it to the *intellect that emerges from the natural world by acquiring* is to *displace the name from its proper rank*. The *acquired intellect* is *itself created*; it is not *the Creator*.

The careful note: al-Kirmani does *not* deny that the language of creativity is used at the rank of the Intellect; he denies the *identification* in essence. The Intellect *issues* from the Creator; the Intellect *is creative* in the sense of being *the medium through which the world below is brought into being*. But the Intellect is not the *Creator simply* — the Creator stands above the Intellect, and the Intellect is the first issue.

## Sub-chapter fifteen — the Intellect-as-intermediary doctrine refused

Al-Mahsul: *The intellect benefits images only from its cause, which is the Word — just as the two plates benefit from the sun's light, not from its body. And when it brings them forth, it does so from what is in it of the power of its cause, which is the Word; and this also benefited the intellect from it. Thus it became clear that the Word is what has become the cause of the things which emerge from the intellect, up to the very identity of the intellect; however, the intellect has become an intermediary between the Word and what is below it*.

The doctrine introduces a *Word* that stands *above* the Intellect and a chain *Word → Intellect → world*. The Intellect is *intermediary*. The image of the *two plates benefit from the sun's light* gives the doctrine its illustrative force: as plates receive light from the sun without becoming the sun-body, so the Intellect receives images from the Word without becoming the Word.

Al-Kirmani: *The intellect which he mentions is the intellects of the prophets and their followers, derived from their cause — which are others they quote — not the First Intellect, which is the First Creator. For the First Intellect does not need anything else in the existence of what exists through it, because it is the cause; and the particular Word in which and of which is the existence of what exists. Therefore we said that the Perfect proceeds from Him perfect and does not need anything outside itself, because it is the cause of the existence of things. If it were preceded by something else, then that something would be obliged to be the First Creator, not it — and this would lead to a disorder in the ranks of the principles, which is impossible. As for his saying that the intellect has become an intermediary between the Word and what is below it, we have shown that this matter is impossible, as mentioned at the beginning of our book*.

The intermediary doctrine collapses the architecture. If the *Intellect* is intermediary, then the *Word* stands above it as a separate rank; if the *Word* is separate, then it is either a *something* (in which case the Intellect perceives it, and the Intellect is therefore *above* it — contradiction) or a *nothing* (in which case it is not the Word, but transcendent above the qualities a thing deserves — and what then *is* it?). *And given the impossibility of anything being advanced over the First Intellect, there is nothing above the thing except the Thing of the Almighty. And if this is so, then it is the Word, it is the cause, it is the unity, and it is the intellect, according to what we have explained above*.

The doctrinal centerpiece: at the rank of the First Intellect, *unity = Word = cause = Intellect = creativity = Creator*. There is no *intermediary* — because there is no *between*. There is Allah, and then there is the First Intellect, which is the *first issue from Him*; and the First Intellect *is* the Word and the unity and the cause. The *intermediary* doctrine inserts a rank that the architecture does not contain. *God is sufficient for us, and He is the best Trustee*.

## Sub-chapter sixteen — creativity as intermediary refused: creativity IS the Creator

The author of *al-Mahsul* presses the doctrine to its sharpest form. *Creativity is an intermediary between the Creator and the created, the created being an effect of the influencer upon the influenced. He said: The form of creativity is intermediate between the doer and the object; its existence is on the side of the creative actor, while the effect of the form is found on the side of the created in the effect. This form — I mean creativity — has come to be in the Creator Almighty*.

The doctrine: *creativity* is *a form in the Creator* — *not itself created*. *Creativity* sits *between* the Creator and the created, *as a form on the Creator's side and an effect on the created's side*. The Creator has *creativity* as a form *within Him*; that form *is the cause* of what is created; the form is *not* itself created.

Al-Kirmani lands the closing critique. *This speech contains an affirmation of that which God has not created, nor invented, nor originated. This is contrary to what monotheists are obligated to hold; for by saying that He made creativity uncreated, it is obligatory that creativity be a form in the Creator. Then by his saying, it is obligatory that the form which he called creation be a form, and that he called it an influencer in it — fixed, not invented, not originated*. The doctrine that *creativity is uncreated* makes *creativity* into *an eternal-with-God-not-from-God* — and *that is the very thing tawhid refuses*.

The careful proof: *it is not permissible to suppose that creativity is something other than creative; for if what he imagines as creative is other than creativity, then it must either be with God Almighty, or exist for itself*. Two options for *creativity-as-something-other-than-the-Creator*: it exists *with* God, or it exists *for itself*. Both fail. *Its existence for itself is invalidated, for it would be fragmented in itself, while it is yet with God Almighty*. Existence-for-itself requires a self-sufficient ground; creativity has no such ground. *As for its existence with God Almighty, this is not possible either, because its existence with God is not without being similar to God — glorified and exalted is He — and an example of Him, so that they would be two examples*.

The doctrinal lock: *two examples* — two entities equally *exemplifying* the rank of *that-which-is-with-God* — *would be affirmed by the competence of each in respect of the occurrence of duality, and by the competence in two things, which exist only for an affirmative cause — because they would be in that condition before them*. Two things equally-eternal-with-God require a *cause* that produced them *together*, and that cause stands *prior* to both. *Either their existence would be alike (and there is no likeness), or it would be that of two opposites — and the existence of two opposites occurs only in the presence of others who preserve their existence; for in the presence of one of the opposites is the invalidation of the existence of the other, because its existence is necessary. This would require that the Creator Almighty be created, and that what is primary be preceded — by the Almighty Himself — and that is more abstaining and more impossible*.

The proof closes: *if there is something which necessitates its existence, it cannot be without two aspects: if the hero of all aspects is a hero whose existence is for itself; and if its existence for itself is invalidated, then it is proven that its existence is from God Almighty. And creativity has its existence from God Almighty, so the One is not one of creativity. And the creative One — to say that He is a creative or a primary creator from another — if that is so, then existence is invalidated, since the existing produces nothing but one. Thus it is proven that creativity is the Creator; and if creativity is the Creator, then what the author of al-Mahsul said is wrong, so let him avoid it, God willing. And so we have come to what we were promised at the beginning of the book*.

The chapter's central doctrinal settlement, in al-Kirmani's voice: *creativity IS the Creator*. At the rank where the chain begins, *creativity* and *the Creator* are *one name for one rank*. To say *creativity is intermediary between the Creator and the created* is to introduce a *third entity* between Allah and His act — which is the very thing the architecture refuses at the head of the chain.

> *He is the First and the Last, and the Outward and the Inward; and He has knowledge of all things.* (Quran 57:3)

The verse settles it from the Qur'anic side. *He* — the bare pronoun — *is the First*; there is no *intermediary* between Him and what issues from Him. The act of creation issues *from* Him; the act *is* Him in *the rank of being-the-cause*; the *act* is not a third entity *between* Him and the created. *Vision perceives Him not, but He perceives all vision; and He is the Subtle, the Aware* (Quran 6:103). The exhaustion of language at the threshold of Him is the only honesty.

> *Whoever describes Him has limited Him; whoever limits Him has counted Him; whoever counts Him has denied His pre-eternity. He is not described by direction, nor compared to a likeness, nor confined to a measure; He is the First whose firstness no firstness preceded, and the Last whose lastness no lastness will follow.* — Imam Ali ibn Abi Talib (peace be upon him), *Nahj al-Balagha* (nahj al-ba-laa-ghah), the sermon known as *al-Khutba al-Ashbah* on the impossibility of describing the Lord without falling into limit

The Commander of the Believers' formula at the chapter's close gives the doctrine its highest voice. *Whoever describes Him has limited Him; whoever limits Him has counted Him; whoever counts Him has denied His pre-eternity*. The chapter's whole polemical labor has been to refuse every formulation that *describes Him, limits Him, counts Him* — and to land at the only honest language: the bare *huwa* in the verse of self-witness, and the negation *nothing is like Him* in the verse of incomparability.

## The closing settlement — tawhid is difficult, and the Imam is the way

The chapter — and with it the whole book — closes on the soteriological consequence. *Tawhid and the knowledge of the limits are difficult, and yet they encompass the life and survival of souls, and their salvation from the world of nature and its impossibilities*. The doctrine has been *difficult* because the language of created tongues is *not appropriate* to the rank it tries to reach; and yet *tawhid is what souls live by*. The mismatch is real, and the mismatch is what the chapter's sustained patience has been working with.

*Since it is difficult, and God Almighty knows that all His servants do not attain the degree by which they reach perfection in knowing the limits of God and His unity, He has from time to time singled out one man with perfection and election — such as the Imam, may God's prayers be upon him*. The architecture of the chain finds its closing point: not in a more refined formula, but in a *person* — *the Imam* — singled out by Allah from time to time as the *one perfected man* in whom the receiving of the overflow from the angels-near-Him reaches its full measure.

*By perfection I mean being complete in accepting the overflow taking place in the natural world from the side of the angels who are near to Him; thus he will be dominant over them and will be promoted to His whole. God will make it obligatory upon them to obey him and to love him, that they may become a part of him, and that they may share in his compassion for them*. The Imam is *complete in receiving* — and by his completeness *the angels are made his*, and the believers are *gathered into him* and become *part of him*.

*There is no way to this except by adopting the two acts of worship and making good the truth from them in knowledge and action, both outwardly and inwardly, and being keen to preserve them*. The settlement closes on the doctrine that has run through *Chapter Seven* and *Chapter Eight* and *Chapter Nine* — the *two worships*. The *zahir* (the apparent worship — the Shariʿah, the actions) and the *batin* (the hidden worship — the *taʾwil* (ta-weel — the interpretation), the inner knowledge). *Body and spirit of the religion. Neither dispensable. Both held.*

*This is achieved only by remembering death and what follows from it — the paradox of parents, children, relatives, and homeland — and by the certainty that nothing brings one to the promised time except through surplus. And if there is no increase that is saved except by obedience, then its opportunities are fulfilled: the obligation that fulfills its right, good deeds, good actions that one performs, and knowledge of the realities of things*.

The chapter's closing prayer is also the book's closing prayer. *May God place us and the company of believers in the east of the earth and its west — those who conclude with goodness — and our provision and their intercession is the intercession of Muhammad, ʿAli, and the pure nation, and our Imam [the Ruler by the Command of God] and his fathers, peace be upon them, in the land of Jerusalem* (Dar al-Quds) *with His mercy and mercy*.

*Then we conclude the book with praise to God, Lord of the Worlds, and prayers upon the best of the first and the last, Muhammad, the family of the Imams, and the rightly guiding Imams. And we say: there is no power but from God Almighty; what God wills. And I ask God for forgiveness, and I delegate my matter to God; God is sufficient for me — yes, the agent, yes the Lord, and yes the supporter. Praise be to God, until He is praised as is due; and prayers upon Muhammad and his good family, all of them, the pure*.

The book closes with the chapter, and the chapter closes with the prayer. The architecture that began at the Editor's Introduction with the four daʿis and the debate between the *al-Sadayn* — the Two Reformers, Abu Hatim and his author-of-*al-Mahsul* counterpart — and ran joint-by-joint through nine chapters of cosmology, anthropology, and history, finds its rest at the rank above which there is no rank — *Dar al-Quds, a noble station*. The chain runs; the chain comes home; the speaker stops speaking, and gives the last word to *the Imam in whose person the Command runs* and to *the prayer with which a treatise honestly ends*.
