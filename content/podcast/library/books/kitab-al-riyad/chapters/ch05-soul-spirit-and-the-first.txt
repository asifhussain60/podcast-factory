# Soul, Spirit, and the First

*From the Kitab al-Riyad (ki-taab ar-ri-yaad) of Hamid al-Din Ahmad al-Kirmani (haa-mid ad-deen ah-mad al-kir-maa-nee). This chapter covers Bāb 3 (baab) — On the judgment among the disputants concerning what is spoken of in regard to soul and nature: whether they are like the First or not — comprising six fusūl (fu-sool; sections). The bāb at first looks like a continuation of the motion-and-rest dispute from Bāb 2, but its real question is sharper and more theologically charged: when al-Kirmani says the Second emanates from the First, in what sense are they "alike"? And what about the strata further down — nature, al-Hayuli (al-ha-yoo-lee; prime matter, the Greek hyle)? Are they "born from" the Second the way the Second is "born from" the First — that is, in resemblance — or are they merely effects of the Second, traces in which the light of the originative chain has dimmed almost to vanishing? The dispute between al-Islah (al-is-laah) and al-Nusra (an-nus-rah) here turns on the picture of a cosmos divided rigidly into a "light world" and a "dark world." Al-Kirmani's adjudication refines the picture so that resemblance does its proper work where it belongs — between cause and counterpart at the top of the chain — and traces do theirs where they belong, between al-Hayuli and the Second, far below the dyad of light and darkness.*

## What this bāb is really asking

The first half of the Kitab al-Riyad has been progressively building one architecture: the First Intellect is the act of origination, *ibda'* (ib-daa); the Second is emanated from it; the universal Soul is named the Second by virtue of its place in this chain; the natural-world things — bodies, souls of bodies, the elements — exist by overflow from the originative order through the natural order. Bāb 3 takes up a question that has been growing more pressing under the surface: when we say "born from," what does the metaphor actually claim?

In ordinary speech "X is born from Y" carries an implicit claim of resemblance. The child resembles the parent. The river resembles its spring. The author of al-Mahsul (al-mah-sool), whose system al-Kirmani has been working through, had used this resemblance-language freely: the Second is born from the First. The author of al-Islah took it for granted that resemblance is what "born from" means, and worked out a cosmology where this resemblance fails at one specific stratum — *nature does not resemble al-Hayuli, even though nature is born from the Second and al-Hayuli is also from the Second*. The author of al-Nusra spotted a tension in al-Islah's framing: if the upper world is *all* light with no darkness, and the lower world is *all* dark with no light, then nothing links them, and the chain of emanation breaks. He answered al-Islah's picture with another — a cosmology in which the soul has *two extremes*, one luminous toward the intellect and one dark toward nature, and nature itself has *two ends*, one lit by the soul above it and one dark toward the elements below. The cosmos shades smoothly from light to dark; the chain remains continuous; the puzzle of how a wholly luminous world communicates with a wholly dark one disappears.

Al-Kirmani's reading is sympathetic to neither picture in its pure form. The question the bāb really pursues is whether *resemblance* is the right predicate to apply across the whole originative chain, or whether resemblance applies only at the very top — between the First and the Second — and what lies further down is best described not as resembling the Second but as a *trace* of it. If that is right, then al-Islah's instinct to refuse resemblance between al-Hayuli and the Second is correct, but his picture of two sealed worlds (light and dark) is wrong; and al-Nusra's instinct that the chain is continuous is correct, but his "two-sides" picture of the universal Soul collapses the originative order back into the natural-world idiom of sides, diameters, and parts.

The closing of Bāb 2 had named the discipline: *each thing in its rank, each category in its proper domain*. Bāb 3 takes that discipline and applies it to the most delicate predicate in the whole vocabulary — likeness, resemblance, *mathal*. Where does the predicate properly apply? Where does it slip into category error? And what other predicate (trace, effect, emanation-at-the-lower-proportion) takes its place where likeness does not reach?

## The Qur'anic horizon — Light upon Light, and the verse of unlikeness

Before walking through the six *fusūl*, it is worth pausing on the Qur'anic horizon against which this dispute is being conducted. The disputants are working with two scriptural images that pull in opposite directions, and the strain between them is visible in their pictures.

The first image is the Light Verse — the most-cited single verse on the metaphysics of light in Islamic intellectual history:

> Allah is the Light of the heavens and the earth. The example of His light is like a niche within which is a lamp, the lamp is within glass, the glass as if it were a pearly white star lit from the oil of a blessed olive tree, neither of the east nor of the west, whose oil would almost glow even if untouched by fire. Light upon light. Allah guides to His light whom He wills.
> (Quran 24:35; English: Saheeh International.)

*Light upon light.* The image is one of an unbroken chain of luminous transmission — niche, glass, lamp, oil, light upon light. This is the picture that lies behind al-Nusra's intuition that the chain from the First downward must be *continuous* — that if the upper world is pure light and the lower world is pure dark with no overlap, the chain breaks and the verse's structure of nested light is contradicted.

The second image is the verse of unlikeness:

> There is nothing like unto Him, and He is the Hearing, the Seeing.
> (Quran 42:11; English: Saheeh International.)

*There is nothing like unto Him.* This is the image that protects God's transcendence — the predicate of likeness, applied to God, fails. And applied one stratum down — to the First Intellect that exists from God — the same instinct can keep us from confusing the First with the strata below. Likeness, when used carelessly, slides into association, *shirk*. The Qur'an's grammatical care in this matter — *laysa ka-mithlihi shay'*, with the doubled particle of negation reinforcing the impossibility — is itself an exercise in domain-discrimination.

The disputants are pulled between these two scriptural pictures. Al-Islah, hearing the unlikeness verse, refuses resemblance between al-Hayuli and the Second. Al-Nusra, hearing the Light Verse, insists on continuity all the way down. Al-Kirmani's task across Bāb 3 is to honor both pictures simultaneously: *resemblance fails where it would fail (deep down, at the level of dark traces); the chain is continuous in another way (by emanation at lower and lower proportions, not by gradient of light)*.

Imam Ali ibn Abi Talib (a-lee ibn a-bee taa-lib; the Commander of the Believers, peace be upon him), in the first sermon of *Nahj al-Balagha* (nahj al-ba-laa-ghah), holds these two images together with a precision the disputants have not yet reached:

> All praise belongs to Allah… He brought creation into being out of nothing, without anything resembling Him as a model and without anyone helping Him as an associate. He created and existed; He differentiated and existed; He arranged and existed.
> (Imam Ali ibn Abi Talib, *Nahj al-Balagha*, Sermon 1; trans. Sayyid Ali Reza, lightly modernized.)

*Without anything resembling Him as a model.* The unlikeness is absolute at the top. And yet *He differentiated, He arranged* — the world He gives rise to has order, structure, internal continuity. The resemblance fails between Creator and creation; the order does not fail within creation. Al-Kirmani is working out, philosophically, what this Alid intuition already holds together in worship.

## Fasl 1 — The picture of two sealed worlds, and why it cannot stand

The first *fasl* opens with the author of al-Islah laying out his cosmology in defense of the system of the author of al-Mahsul. *We do not say*, al-Islah explains, *that nature and darkness are born of the Second in the way that the Second is born of the First.* Why not? Because *that world is the world of nobility and virtue, while this world is the world of darkness*; the relation "born from" carries resemblance, and the dark world cannot resemble the noble world. *Nature does not resemble the condition of al-Hayuli.* Therefore the language of birth, used between the upper world and the lower, is wrong; some other language must take its place.

The author of al-Nusra answers with the tension al-Kirmani knows the picture will produce:

> This statement is wrong. If that world were light with no darkness in it, and this world were dark with no light in it, then there would be no link between this world and that.

The premise of complete separation produces a corollary that no monotheist can accept: the absence of a link between the two strata. Without a link, no overflow; without overflow, no providence; without providence, no theology. Al-Nusra concludes that the rigid separation must be wrong, and proposes his own picture as the corrective.

In al-Nusra's picture, the universal Soul has two extremes:

> The soul has two extremes: one of them toward the intellect, and it is in the intellect's horizon — this is the luminous, noble side; and a part of the soul lies toward nature, with nature in the soul's horizon. There is some darkness in this side, after the manner of a circle, because nature is in the soul's horizon.

And nature mirrors this two-endedness. One end faces the partial soul — still some light, since the partial soul lies in nature's horizon. The other end faces the elements — the proper darkness, in which there is no light. The cosmos shades from light to dark by degrees; nothing is wholly one or wholly the other; the chain is continuous.

The author of al-Islah's resistance to this picture comes from a *theological* worry, not just a metaphysical one. If the same Soul that lies in the intellect's horizon also has a dark side facing nature, then the noble world has been infected at the seam with the darkness it was supposed to be free of. The Second is no longer the pure overflow of the First; it has degraded by mixture into something half-noble and half-dark. Al-Islah would rather break the metaphor of resemblance than admit that mixture.

Al-Kirmani's verdict, when he weighs both pictures, is patient and discriminating. He grants what al-Islah was trying to do: *al-Hayuli is not like the Second, because al-Hayuli is a trace of the Second*, and a trace is not the same predicate as a resemblance. He grants what al-Nusra was trying to do: *the chain must be continuous, because otherwise emanation has no purchase*. But he refuses al-Islah's picture of two sealed worlds as a way of expressing the first instinct, and he refuses al-Nusra's "two-sides" anatomy of the universal Soul as a way of expressing the second.

The corrective is this. *The First and the Second are counterparts* — male and female of the same noble species, the source of perfect honor and perfect light. Their relation is one of resemblance, and the language of "born from" applies between them. *Al-Hayuli, however, is not born from the Second in this sense at all*; al-Hayuli is an *effect* (ma'lul, ma-lool) of the Second, and the relation between effect and cause is *not* the relation between offspring and parent. *Therefore al-Hayuli does not resemble the Second*, but the lack of resemblance does not break the chain — because al-Hayuli was not supposed to resemble the Second in the first place. The predicate "born from" was the wrong predicate to apply.

Al-Kirmani's own formula stitches this together:

> Al-Hayuli is not similar to the Second, nor to the First, for its existence is by way of emanation from the First, at the lower proportion that we have shown in our previous words and in our well-known book Rahat al-'Aql.

*Rahat al-'Aql* (raa-hat al-aql; *The Repose of the Intellect*) is the work al-Kirmani points his reader to whenever the speculative argument becomes too long for the present treatise. The phrase *emanation at the lower proportion* is the move that does the real work. It is the predicate that takes the place of resemblance where resemblance does not reach. The First emanates the Second at the *higher* proportion — the proportion of resemblance, of cause-and-counterpart. Al-Hayuli emanates from the First *through* the Second at the *lower* proportion — the proportion of trace, of dark effect, of being-from but not being-like. Both are emanations; the chain holds; but the predicates of resemblance and likeness are reserved for the top of the chain, where they belong.

In this small move, al-Kirmani has redrawn the cosmological map. The two sealed worlds of al-Islah collapse into one continuous chain of being. The mixed "two sides" of al-Nusra collapse into a more articulated typology in which different strata are related by different predicates — *resemblance* between the First and Second, *emanation at the lower proportion* between the First-via-Second and al-Hayuli, *natural overflow* between the Second and the natural-world things. The chain is one. The predicates differ.

The figure that the bāb has been circling around — the figure of the noble dyad — al-Kirmani draws explicitly:

> The Second is more honorable and lofty than that its relation to its like should be like its relation to the First. The First and the Second are two counterparts, the very source of honor, perfection, and elevation. There is no honor above them; God has made them the source of perfect honor, of perfect light, of perfect elevation, and of perfect knowledge — and there is no ignorance there, nor revolving, nor darkness.

The dyad is its own cosmos. Above it, only al-Bari (al-baa-ree; the Originator). Below it, the natural-world things. Resemblance lives in the dyad and nowhere else.

## Fasl 2 — Light and darkness, taken on al-Islah's own terms

The second *fasl* is brief and is essentially al-Kirmani's correction of al-Nusra's argument *on its own terms*. Al-Nusra had said: *if that world were light and there were no darkness in it, and this world were dark and there were no light in it, then there would be no link between this world and that world.* As an objection to the version of al-Islah's picture al-Nusra was reading, this is sharp. But al-Kirmani notes that the picture al-Nusra was attacking was a version al-Islah did not really hold, *or* it was correct only against an exaggerated reading.

In al-Kirmani's adjudication:

> This statement, as it stands, is established against the author of al-Islah unless he can be cleared of error. In terms of what he himself has said, it is in fact correct: he is from the world of origination and re-emergence in such a way that what is shared in him befits what is above him by way of being emanated, and what is below him befits him by way of being a body.

The point is that the Second has two aspects, neither of which is itself the First, but neither of which is wholly cut off from the First. *He has two aspects, neither of them like the First, from which he derives his existence. The Second resembles the First on the one side and does not resemble it on the other.* The side from which he resembles is that he is emanated. The side from which he does not resemble is that he is, in the relevant sense, a natural body. *Nature, when it was added to the world of origination and resurgence, was a life that proceeds valid from that world without it.* When the same nature is added to *this* world, it becomes the principle of motion, becoming itself *the very thing in which it exists, by self-attribution, not by accident*.

What al-Kirmani has done is take al-Nusra's continuous-gradient picture and refit it without the "two-sides" anatomy. The continuity is real: the Second emanates; what emanates from the Second propagates through the natural-world things by overflow. But it is not the continuity of *one substance with two halves*. It is the continuity of *one chain with successive strata, related to each other by different relations*. Resemblance at the top, emanation at lower proportions, natural overflow at the natural level. The chain holds without requiring the Second to be a half-light, half-dark hybrid.

The reader trained in Neoplatonic figures may notice that this is *not* the standard Plotinian picture — the picture of a One whose overflow degrades by stages until it reaches matter at the bottom, dim and almost nothing. Al-Kirmani's picture has a stronger structure. The First and Second are a *dyad*, internally complete; the emanation of al-Hayuli is not a *degraded* emanation of the Second but a different *kind* of emanation, drawn from a different proportion of the Second. There is no continuous slope from light to dark. There is a noble dyad at the top, and a separate emanation, at the lower proportion, of the dark trace below.

## Fasl 3 — Why "two sides" fails as a geometry of the soul

The third *fasl* returns to al-Nusra's specific picture of the universal Soul as having two sides. The argument al-Kirmani brings is geometric in form and metaphysical in content:

> The proof is that what has sides is what has diameters measured by area. As for the Second — which we have named "soul" by what we explained above — if it is non-corporeal, then it is no body.

To have sides — *aspects with spatial extension* — is to be the kind of thing measured by area and diameters. But the Second is non-corporeal. The predicate "has two sides" cannot apply to it in the natural-world sense. If we use the predicate, we have once again folded the originative order back into the natural order — exactly the category-mistake al-Kirmani spent Bāb 2 correcting.

The deeper argument is that the Second, the effect of the First, *is itself a cause* of what lies below it. Al-Kirmani's analysis is one of the densest in the book, and worth slowing down for:

> The effect (ma'lul) is a cause in addition to what is below it — which is the lower proportion within the First; and the effect, by addition to itself (which is the lower proportion within the First), is from its own intellect: it is a cause that emanates something like itself, since the cause has its like as its effect. And from its own intellect — in that it is malaisa (maa lay-sa; non-being) — it emits what is not like itself, by being an emission from the lower proportion within the First, namely the proportion of being effect; because the proportion of effect is more honorable than the proportion of cause.

Read slowly, the architecture is this. The Second is *cause* relative to what lies below it — that is, the Second stands in the relation that the First stood in to the Second. As cause, the Second emanates *like* — something that resembles it. This is the principle that *cause has its like as effect*, the principle that protects resemblance at the top of any subsidiary chain. But the Second is *also* an effect (its proportion-as-effect is what it received from the First). And *qua* effect, it emits something *unlike* itself — namely al-Hayuli, the dark trace. The same essence acquires two proportions only by addition: relative to what is above it, it is effect; relative to what is below, it is cause; and the proportion-of-effect and the proportion-of-cause produce different sorts of emanation.

This is not "two sides" in the sense al-Nusra meant. It is *two proportions* of the *same* essence — two relations the same essence stands in. The Second is not half-light and half-dark *within itself*. It is *fully itself*. And what it emanates *as cause* resembles it, while what comes from it *as effect-of-the-First* is the dark trace, al-Hayuli. The geometry of sides fails; the logic of proportions captures what al-Nusra was reaching toward.

Al-Kirmani makes the relation intuitive by drawing on the figure of the natiq (naa-tiq; the speaker, the speaker-prophet) and the foundation:

> [The foundation] is, in this respect, the parable of the Second in the science of the body. The body, being invisible to the speaker, is all that was originally there; it is present in the speaker, from whom the foundation took its existence. And likewise, not everything that the speaker has is there for the foundation, because the speaker has what the foundation does not share — namely the visible, in which the foundation does not participate.

The speaker-prophet and the foundation — the bearer of the apparent law and the bearer of the inner interpretation — are related by *participation* but not by *identity*. The speaker has something the foundation does not have. The foundation has something the speaker does not have *in the same mode*. Yet there is nothing in the speaker that the foundation does not in some way *indicate*. The relation is exact and asymmetrical. This is the model al-Kirmani is pointing toward for understanding First → Second → al-Hayuli: not "two sides," but a chain of indicators and indicated, each related to the next by a proper proportion.

For the reader of the Da'wa (daa-wah; the Ismaili summons), this is not a metaphysical digression. It is the same architecture that organizes teaching authority itself. The Prophet ﷺ (sallallahu alayhi wa sallam; peace and blessings of Allah be upon him) and his *wasi* (al-waa-see; designated successor) stand in this relation; the *wasi* and the Imam stand in it; the Imam and the *hujjah* (huj-jah; Proof) stand in it. At every rank, participation without identity, indication without sharing, proportion without resemblance-of-essence. The metaphysics is the model for the teaching hierarchy; the teaching hierarchy is the lived form of the metaphysics.

## Fasl 4 — The darkness of al-Hayuli, and the proportion of effect

The fourth *fasl* takes up al-Nusra's specific claim that al-Hayuli *fell into darkness because of its distance from the First Cause, so that nature might be born from the soul.* The framing is one of *distance*: as you move farther from the First, you lose light and gain darkness, until you reach al-Hayuli, which is so far away that it has lost light entirely.

Al-Kirmani's correction is precise:

> The fact that al-Hayuli is not like the First, and the Second is not [made al-Hayuli] because of distance from the First in proportion: this is the case of the First's effect, which combines two proportions relative to its being cause — the proportion of being effect, and the existence of something that issues from the proportion that is effect, which is unlike the existence of something that issues from the proportion that is cause; for the cause is more honorable than the effect in every respect, and prior to it in rank and nature.

The darkness of al-Hayuli is not a function of *distance* in any spatial or quasi-spatial sense. It is a function of *which proportion of the Second produces it*. The Second *qua* cause produces what is like itself (the natural-world things at the level immediately below). The Second *qua* effect-of-the-First produces al-Hayuli — a dark trace that is unlike the Second because it issues not from the cause-aspect but from the effect-aspect. Distance is the wrong predicate. Proportion is the right one.

This is one of the most counter-intuitive moves in the bāb, and the reason for it deserves a moment. The natural mind, especially the natural mind trained in Neoplatonic figures, imagines the chain of being as a series of *concentric rings* — the First in the center, the Second around it, then nature, then al-Hayuli at the outermost ring, all but lost in the dark. Al-Nusra is reading the chain this way. Al-Islah was reading it this way. Even readers of al-Kirmani sometimes read him this way.

But al-Kirmani's picture is *not* concentric. It is a picture of *proportions within proportions*. The Second is one essence, but stands in two proportions — cause-to-below, effect-to-above. From its cause-proportion comes its like. From its effect-proportion, taken at the lower proportion of being effect — what we might call *the receding side of having-been-caused* — comes al-Hayuli. The dark trace is not the ring at the outside of a concentric set; it is the dark sub-product of the very thing that is most directly related to the First. The Second is itself the source of both light (its cause-emanation) and dark trace (its effect-side) — not by being half-and-half within itself, but by standing in two different proportions to what is above and below it.

The Qur'an's account of human nature — the verse on the breathing-in of the spirit — names a related architecture from the side of the human:

> So when I have proportioned him and breathed into him of My spirit, then fall down to him in prostration.
> (Quran 38:72; English: Saheeh International. Said by Allah to the angels regarding Adam.)

*Of My spirit.* Not "My spirit entire," not "the same spirit as Me," but *min ruhi* — a phrase that grammatically names a portion-from, a derivative-of. The human carries something *of* the spirit-from-the-divine — not the divine entire, but a trace, a proportion. The Qur'anic grammar is itself doing the work al-Kirmani is doing at the metaphysical level: distinguishing *the source* from *the derivative-from-the-source* from *the further derivative*. The dignity is real; the resemblance is partial; the predicate "from" does not collapse into the predicate "like." Adam (aa-dam; peace be upon him) carries *of* the spirit — not the spirit itself, not a likeness of it, but a trace of it.

The same instinct shapes the verse on the human's poverty before God:

> O mankind, you are those in need of Allah, while Allah is the Free of need, the Praiseworthy.
> (Quran 35:15; English: Saheeh International.)

*You are those in need of Allah, while Allah is the Free of need.* The asymmetry is the architecture. The chain runs only one direction. The Originator does not become like the originated by giving rise to it. The Second does not become like al-Hayuli by emanating it at the lower proportion. The trace does not turn around and become a likeness. The need flows up; the gift flows down; the predicates do not climb.

## Fasl 5 — There is no deficiency in the Second

The fifth *fasl* is brief and corrective. Al-Nusra had said: *the decrease occurred in the soul because it is beyond the First Cause — I mean the one preceding it.* The word "decrease" — *naqs* — carries the implication of *missing something that ought to be there*, of an essence that has shrunk from its proper measure. Al-Nusra is using the word to describe what happens to the Second relative to the First.

Al-Kirmani replies:

> There is no deficiency in the Second. What he called "deficiency," as we have mentioned, is not that it is far from the First, but that it is like it; and the decrease is necessary, not in its very nature, but it became incomplete — not from the side of its own self, which is perfection, but it became incomplete from a side in which the Second's perfection is acquired through its receiving benefit.

The Second is *perfect* in itself, just as the First is perfect in itself. The Second's "incompleteness," to the extent the word applies at all, is not a defect of essence but a feature of being-received-from: the Second is the kind of being whose kamāl (ka-maal; perfection) *lies in its receiving from* the First. The proportion of being-effect is built into what the Second is, and this proportion is *not* a subtraction from its perfection — it is *constitutive* of the kind of perfection appropriate to a second-rank existent.

The reader trained in the language of Plotinus and the Neoplatonic chain may hear a familiar pattern here, but al-Kirmani's reading sharpens it. *Perfection at the second rank is not less perfection than perfection at the first rank; it is a different mode of the same predicate.* The First is perfect *as cause*. The Second is perfect *as caused-and-cause*. Neither is incomplete by reference to the other's mode of perfection. Each is complete in the mode appropriate to its rank.

Imam Ali (peace be upon him) named the same instinct in the same Sermon 1 of *Nahj al-Balagha*, this time in defense of God's absolute perfection over against any sense in which the created order could "lessen" Him:

> The foremost in religion is the acknowledgement of Him; the perfection of acknowledging Him is to testify to Him; the perfection of testifying to Him is to believe in His Oneness; the perfection of believing in His Oneness is to be sincere to Him; and the perfection of sincerity to Him is to deny attributes from Him, because every attribute is a testimony that it is other than that to which it has been attributed.
> (Imam Ali ibn Abi Talib, *Nahj al-Balagha*, Sermon 1; trans. Sayyid Ali Reza.)

To deny attributes from God is *not* to make Him less; it is to refuse to predicate finite categories of Him. The same instinct, exercised at the level of the Second, refuses to predicate "decrease" of it. The Second is not less; it is differently perfect — perfect in the mode of being-caused-and-being-cause. The vocabulary of *decrease* belongs to bodies, which have measurable magnitudes that can grow or shrink. It does not belong to the intellectual order.

And the closing note al-Kirmani sounds in this *fasl* is generous in the way the bāb has been generous throughout: even al-Nusra's wrong predicate was reaching for something true — the Second's *receiving-from* the First *is* an asymmetry, a not-being-the-First. But that asymmetry is not decrease. It is the very shape of the dyad. The receiver is no less than the giver in the dyad of perfect love.

## Fasl 6 — The proportion of birth, and why nature resembles the soul more than the soul resembles the intellect

The sixth and longest *fasl* in the bāb takes up al-Nusra's specific argument about the proportion of generation. Al-Nusra had said:

> The proportion of the birth of nature from the soul is no greater than the generation of the soul from the intellect. The soul is less than nature; so that the soul, although it is permissible for it to be generated from the intellect with [the intellect's] perfection and honor and its closeness to the First Cause of the soul, is itself permissible to give birth to nature — for nature more closely resembles the soul in all its states, in its actions and its powers, than the soul resembles the intellect in its actions and powers.

The argument is intricate. Al-Nusra is observing that nature's resemblance-to-soul (in its dynamism, its principle of motion, its active power) is *closer* than soul's resemblance-to-intellect. This is an empirical claim about how the chain looks from where we stand: soul-and-nature have more in common than intellect-and-soul do. The first pair both move, both organize, both express agency. The second pair share intelligibility, but the intellect is *still*, while the soul moves.

> For nature, among the ancients, is labeled as the principle of movement; and some of them said that it is the active force. If you look at nature from these two limits, it is the best fortune and the best installment, because the soul among the early thinkers is the surviving moving essence, and is nevertheless labeled with mind — not in the sense that "mind" is one of the characteristics that delimit the soul.

Al-Nusra is leveraging this resemblance-asymmetry to argue that the chain of generation is internally consistent: if the intellect can generate the soul (despite the gap of stillness-and-motion between them), then the soul can certainly generate nature, with which it has more in common. And then the famous lyrical claim follows:

> All goodness, splendor, honor, and adornment is from the higher noble world. If nature were not born of the soul, the soul could not tell of beauty, comeliness, and adornment at all; but in order for nature to be generated from the soul, the soul tells what it saw in its world of beauty and honor as an incomplete story — a story without completing the soul.

*An incomplete story.* Nature is, in al-Nusra's image, the universal Soul's *telling* of what the Soul saw in the world above — a telling that necessarily falls short of what was seen, because *telling* is always lossy, but a telling nonetheless that carries the splendor of the original into the lower stratum. The image is moving. Read carefully, it locates the *aesthetic* of the natural world — its beauty, its adornment, its splendor — as the *trace* of the noble world rendered in the partial language of nature. Everything beautiful in nature is the soul's incomplete telling of what was beautiful above.

Al-Kirmani's reply to this lyrical fasl is correspondingly brief and gentle:

> If there is any precedence in speech over the intellect, the soul, nature, and the existence of the above — its existence over others — and otherwise, it entails the correct statement. I quote it without needing to elaborate with certainty on what carried over the error in perception. In this there is sufficient.

The reply has the quality of a teacher saying: *what is true here, I will let stand; what is mistaken, the careful reader will see for himself*. Al-Kirmani is *not* claiming that everything al-Nusra has said in this *fasl* is wrong. He is letting the lyrical core stand — that nature is in some intimate way the soul's incomplete telling of what the soul saw in the higher world — while refusing to commit to the metaphysical claim that *generation* is the right predicate for the relation between soul and nature.

For al-Kirmani's view, the relation between nature and soul is not *birth* in the way the relation between the First and the Second is birth. Nature is *what the soul produces when it gives the matter beneath it form*. The soul is not the *parent* of nature in any biological-analogical sense; the soul is the principle by which the natural world acquires its dynamism. The lyrical image — nature as the soul's incomplete story — captures something real about how nature carries traces of the higher order. But it does not authorize the claim that nature *resembles* the soul in the sense in which "resemblance" properly applies. Resemblance, as Bāb 3 has been arguing throughout, lives at the very top of the chain — between the First and the Second, in the dyad of counterparts — and nowhere else.

What al-Kirmani is leaving unspoken — and what the bāb's structure points the careful reader toward — is that *both kinds of relation are at work at the same time*. The chain is continuous (as the Light Verse suggests). The resemblance fails as you move down (as the verse of unlikeness suggests). Different predicates apply at different strata. The discriminating reader, the one who has been trained by the previous bāb to recognize when categories climb out of their proper domain, is the one who can hold both pictures and know which sentence belongs in which.

Imam Ali (peace be upon him) named the dignity of holding this kind of distinction in another sermon:

> The knowers are those who recognize the One who recognizes nothing in His likeness, and find rest in the worship of the One who finds rest in no station that limits Him.
> (Paraphrased in the manner of *Nahj al-Balagha*, on the attributes of God; trans. Sayyid Ali Reza, condensed.)

To recognize *the One who recognizes nothing in His likeness* — to refuse to predicate *likeness* of God — is the highest discrimination. One stratum down, to recognize that al-Hayuli is a *trace* of the Second and not a *likeness* of it, is the next discrimination. The chain of discriminations is one chain. The bāb that begins by asking whether soul and nature resemble the First ends by teaching the reader how to refuse the predicate of likeness wherever it would mislead, and how to put trace, effect, emanation-at-the-lower-proportion in its place.

## What the bāb has taught

Bāb 3 is short — six *fusūl*, the slimmest bāb in the Kitab al-Riyad. But its work is precise. By the end of it, the reader knows that:

*Resemblance is a predicate proper only at the very top of the originative chain.* The First and the Second are counterparts; their relation is one of likeness, of male-and-female of the same noble species, of "born from" in its full sense. Below this dyad, the predicate of resemblance fades, and other predicates take its place.

*Emanation at the lower proportion takes the place of resemblance where resemblance fails.* Al-Hayuli is an emanation from the First through the Second — but the emanation runs through the Second's effect-proportion, not its cause-proportion, and what issues from the effect-proportion is unlike the Second. The chain holds. The resemblance does not.

*The Second has no deficiency.* What al-Nusra called "decrease" is not subtraction of essence but a feature of being-received — perfection in the mode of being-caused-and-being-cause, not less perfection but differently perfect.

*The "two-sides" picture of the universal Soul collapses on its geometry.* What has sides has spatial extension; the non-corporeal Second has no spatial extension; therefore the Second does not have two sides. What al-Nusra was groping toward is better expressed in the language of *two proportions of one essence* — the same essence standing as cause to what is below and as effect to what is above.

*Different strata of the chain are related by different predicates.* Resemblance between the First and Second. Emanation at the lower proportion between the First-via-Second and al-Hayuli. Natural overflow between the Second and the natural-world things. The careful reader learns to apply the right predicate at the right place — and the bāb has been training that habit *fasl* by *fasl*.

And one more thing, which the bāb teaches by its very structure: *the lyrical and the metaphysical are not enemies*. Al-Nusra's image of nature as the soul's incomplete telling of beauty is *kept*, not refuted. The image is true in the way images are true — by carrying a real proportion into a domain where strict resemblance fails. Al-Kirmani's correction is not the philosopher's victory over the poet. It is the philosopher's care for the poet — making sure the predicates the poet uses do not, when pressed for metaphysical work, claim more than they can sustain. The image stands; the predicate is restricted; the meaning is preserved.

## A figure from the prayer-life of the family

The Light Verse, with which this chapter opened, is the verse on which the Imams of the family of the Prophet ﷺ most often prayed for the gift of illumination. A line in the manner of the supplications of Imam Zayn al-'Abidin (zayn al-aa-bi-deen; the fourth Imam, peace be upon him), in al-Sahifa al-Sajjadiyya (as-sa-hee-fah as-saj-jaa-dee-yah; the Psalms of the House of Muhammad), can stand here as a close:

> O Allah, You are the niche and the lamp and the oil and the light. We are not You; nor is what You have made like You; nor is what comes from what You have made like what You have made. And yet what comes from what You have made is *of* You, by the proportion You have set into it. Increase us in receiving, lessen us in claiming, and bring our intellects to rest in the rank You have appointed for them.
> Source: paraphrased after the manner of the supplications in *al-Sahifa al-Sajjadiyya*.

*We are not You; nor is what You have made like You; nor is what comes from what You have made like what You have made.* The chain of unlikeness runs three steps; the chain of being-from runs the same three steps; and they are the same chain, named twice. To learn to pray this — to receive without claiming, to be of without claiming to be like — is what the bāb on resemblance has been teaching at the speculative level. The metaphysics of trace and emanation is the prayer-life of the family rendered in the idiom of the schools.

The closing words al-Kirmani himself uses to conclude the bāb name his own master-text once more:

> Al-Hayuli is not similar to the Second, nor to the First, for its existence is by way of emanation from the First, at the lower proportion that we have shown in our previous words and in our well-known book Rahat al-'Aql. This is sufficient.

*This is sufficient.* The brevity is itself a teaching. The discriminations he has drawn — between resemblance and emanation, between proportion-as-cause and proportion-as-effect, between trace and likeness — are enough to dissolve the dispute between al-Islah and al-Nusra without requiring the elaborate cosmology either of them had built. The dyad of First and Second; the trace below; the chain held together by proportions. *This is sufficient.*

## The bāb's place in the architecture

Bāb 3 closes here. The first two bābs of the Kitab al-Riyad established the architecture of the originative order — perfection and the perfect, the First Intellect as the act of origination, motion and rest as natural-world categories that do not climb. Bāb 3 has now refined the *predicates* by which strata of this architecture are related to each other: *resemblance* between the First and Second, *emanation at the lower proportion* between the First-via-Second and al-Hayuli, *trace* and *effect* where likeness fails. Three different predicates, three different levels of relation, all subordinated to the same originative act.

The bābs ahead will continue building on this. Bāb 4 takes up souls as parts and effects — extending the analysis of effect-as-trace into the question of how partial souls stand to the universal Soul. Bāb 5 considers the human being as the fruit of the world — the place where all of these strata are gathered into one composite being. Bāb 6 returns to motion, stillness, and form, but now within the natural-world stratum where they properly belong. Bāb 7 maps the divisions of the world, the four-jewel cosmology. Bāb 8 takes up qadr (qadr; destiny) and the shape of providence, where the predicates of cause and effect are tested against the most painful theological questions. Bāb 9 turns to the law of Adam (peace be upon him) and the trusteeship of Nuh (nooh; Noah, peace be upon him), where the chain of teaching authority — speaker-prophets, foundations, hujjahs, hudud (hu-dood; the named ranks of the Da'wa) — is fully laid out. And Bāb 10 returns to the critique of al-Mahsul on tawhid (taw-heed; the affirmation of God's Oneness) and the First Originator, where the discriminations practiced through nine bābs are turned back onto the system that started the whole inquiry.

Each bāb tests the same architecture against a new question. *Soul and spirit, and whether they resemble the First*, was the third test. The architecture has held. The predicate of resemblance has found its proper, restricted place — at the top of the chain, where the First and Second stand as counterparts, and nowhere else. What lies below the dyad is held to the originative act not by likeness but by trace, by effect, by emanation at the lower proportion. The chain is one; the predicates differ; and the discriminating reader — the one the bāb has been training all along — knows when to speak of resemblance, and when to fall silent on the word and reach for another.
