# Qada and Qadar — Fate and Destiny

*This chapter covers all twenty-four sub-chapters of* Chapter Eight *of* Kitab al-Riyad — *al-Kirmani's adjudication of the question that follows directly from* Chapter Seven*. If the world divides into many more sections than four, if the human stands in a* between*-state as the child of both worlds, with matter from below and image from above, then* how does the chain run in time*? What is the relation between* al-qada *(qa-daa — judgment, decree) and* al-qadar *(qa-dar — destiny, measure) — which is prior, which is posterior, what is the* order *by which what is decreed at the head of the chain becomes what is measured at the rank of the receiver*? The author of* al-Islah *(al-is-laah) had argued: destiny precedes decree, because destiny is* estimation *and decree is* detail*, and the prior is before the posterior — like a tailor estimating a garment before he cuts it. The author of* al-Nusra *(an-nus-rah) had inverted the order: decree precedes destiny, like the* spiritual image of the sewing *in the tailor's soul precedes the appreciation by which he estimates — for there is no estimation without a judge who obliges that estimation, and the spiritual is prior to the bodily. Al-Kirmani works the twenty-four sub-chapters as a three-fold adjudication: he settles the definitions (destiny is estimation, decree is detail, in the chapter on the body — but the dispute about prior-and-posterior cannot stand on the bodily reading alone), he settles the* three meanings of decree *(the Speaker — the* natiq *who is the prior, prepared by the support of Allah; the revelation and the Shariʿah brought out from potency into act; the metaphysical impossibilities estimated in the world that admit no reversal), he settles the* three meanings of destiny *(the promised* Qa'im *(qaa-im) called by the speakers as* al-Qadr *(al-qadr); the revelation and Shariʿah carried by the Prophet ﷺ as the* second qadar*; and the world unfolding in its days, months, years, events, and worlds whose presence is necessary by its decree); he gives the* Pen-Tablet-Writer *architecture for the verse* Indeed, We have created everything according to a measure *(Quran 54:49) — Writer as the face of "We," Pen as the Intellect, Tablet as the universal Soul, Measure as what is in front of the Tablet receptive to all the forms; he settles the Prophetic word* Flee from Allah's qada to His qadar *in three meanings, the greatest being that the speaker-Prophet flees from the* preceding *(the support of the First Intellect, in whose intensity of light the* Jadd *almost fades) to the* next *(the composition by which the Shariʿah is laid out in the receiver); he gives the* ta'wil *of Moses (peace be upon him) asking the Author of the Command — Shuʿayb (peace be upon him) — for the agency to lift the affliction from the people of truth at the wrong time, and Moses's repentance when the time is not yet ripe; he refuses the doctrine that the* mind is all things and all things are the mind *as a confusion of the physical and the intellectual; he refuses* al-Nusra*'s reading of Quran 5:93 as a license to abandon the Shariʿah, and gives the verse a different reading altogether about the* du'at *being permitted to study other-tradition sciences as a weapon under three conditions; and he closes by settling the three-position order — decree at the beginning, apportioning in the middle, completion at the end — by which* qada *and* qadar *are best read as the* prior *and the* posterior *of the chain that runs from the First Intellect down through the speaker-Prophet, the Imam, the* hudud *(hu-dood) of the* da'wa *(daa-wah), into the receiver who is being raised by degrees toward the rank of the* reassured soul *that* returns to its Lord, pleased and pleasing.*

## Where this chapter picks up

*Chapter Seven* of *al-Riyad* settled the architecture of the world's sections and the human's place within them: the world divides into many more sections than four; the generated beings divide cleanly into three genera (mineral, vegetable, animal) with the human as a fourth-by-distinction within the animal genus; humankind has *many pairs by occasion* with the strict pair being the angels for the *strict-sense humankind* of the prophets, guardians, imams, and their followers; the architectural map of the microcosm runs joint-by-joint at both the natural-world and the creative-emanative ends; the human is the *counterpart of all the worlds*, standing in a *between*-state like the boy between his two parents, matter from below and image from above, delivered by the *hudud of the da'wa*.

*Chapter Eight* takes that settled architecture and turns to the question of *how the chain runs in time*. If the chain has a *prior* end (the First Intellect, the *al-Qalam* (al-qa-lam — the Pen), the *al-'Arsh* (al-arsh — the Throne)) and a *posterior* end (the receiver-in-human-form, the *reassured soul*), then *which is qada and which is qadar*? Is decree the prior and destiny the posterior, as some have said? Or is destiny the prior and decree the posterior, as others have said? And what *meanings* do the two words bear when taken at the heights of the *creative-emanative* world, at the rank of the *speaker-Prophet* and his *Shariʿah*, and at the rank of the *world unfolding in time*? Twenty-four sub-chapters. The first two settle the opening dispute on the definitions and the two tailor-analogies. Sub-chapters three through five give the three meanings of *qada* (decree). Sub-chapters six through eight give the three meanings of *qadar* (destiny). Sub-chapter nine corrects the doctrine of the First Intellect as *the assembly of the images of the worlds*. Sub-chapters ten through fifteen work the verse *Indeed, We have created everything according to a measure* (Quran 54:49) and give the *Pen-Tablet-Writer* architecture. Sub-chapters sixteen through nineteen work the Prophetic word *Flee from Allah's qada to His qadar* in three meanings. Sub-chapter twenty gives the *ta'wil* of Moses asking *show me, that I may look upon You* (Quran 7:143). Sub-chapters twenty-one and twenty-two refuse the doctrine *the mind is all things and all things are the mind*. Sub-chapter twenty-three refuses *al-Nusra*'s reading of Quran 5:93 as a license to abandon the Shariʿah and gives the verse a different *ta'wil*. Sub-chapter twenty-four lands the three-position order — decree at the beginning, apportioning in the middle, completion at the end.

## Sub-chapters one and two — destiny is estimation, decree is detail; the two tailor-analogies

The author of *al-Islah* opens *Chapter Eight* with a definitional claim. *As for the saying that the decree is prior and the destiny posterior — this is wrong, because destiny is before decree, and the prior is before the posterior.* Destiny precedes decree because of what the *words themselves mean*. *Decree and destiny are two words whose meaning is known from speech: destiny is estimation, and decree is detail. There is no detail except after estimation, and the first is before the second by the farthest measure of priority.*

*Al-Islah* presses the philological evidence. *The matter concerning which you took counsel has been decreed* — that is, *separated and finished*; *when you have finished the prayer* — that is, *when you have completed it*. The verb-form of *qada* is the verb-form of *concluding, separating, finishing*. And he gives the image: a tailor first *estimates* the garment — and at the stage of estimation *it can be increased, decreased, expanded, or narrowed*; but once the garment has been *dismissed* — separated, cut, finished — *it is finished, and increase is no longer possible*. *Destiny is estimation; decree is detail*. The garment in the estimating-phase is *qadar*; the garment after the cut is *qada*. Estimation comes first; detail comes after.

The author of *al-Nusra* enters with a metaphysical inversion. *Decree is not the detail*, he says, *because the case of the eye of detail in the decree is no less than the case of the separated*. And the analogy is wrong on its own terms. *The author of al-Islah has misjudged in likening destiny and decree to the dress that the tailor estimates.* The estimating-phase is what *al-Islah* called destiny; the elaboration-phase is what *al-Islah* called decree. *Al-Nusra* corrects: *he ought to have made the image of the sewing that lies in the very soul of the tailor correspond to the decree, because it is spiritual; and the estimation by which the tailor estimates that sewing correspond to destiny by what it represents — also a spiritual thing, which does not yet emerge beyond the limit of intellect, for this is the meaning of the image of originals.* The *spiritual image of the sewing* in the tailor's soul is the *prior*; the *estimation by which he prepares to cut* is the *posterior*; and only then comes the *physical elaboration*. *Sewing precedes appreciation, and by that sewing the tailor is prepared for the appreciation by which he appreciates. So it is true that the decree is prior and destiny posterior.*

Al-Kirmani enters with the verdict that closes the first two sub-chapters together. *The interpretation of the two contenders concerning decree and destiny, according to what each of them has invoked — even though the meaning of the words destiny and decree, on the basis of what we have shown, makes such an interpretation impossible — is in any case: that decree and destiny are the prior and the posterior.* Both reformers have agreed on the *form* of the dispute (prior-and-posterior) but disagreed on the *content*. And both, in al-Kirmani's reading, have stayed inside the world of the *body* — both have stayed inside the tailor-analogy. The dispute about prior-and-posterior, taken at the architectural heights the chapter will reach, *cannot stand on the bodily reading alone*. The chapter's real work begins.

## Sub-chapters three through five — the three meanings of decree

Sub-chapter three opens the doctrinal work. *As for the decree, it indicates the speaker who was forced to it by the support of God Almighty, and those who take his place — peace be upon them — who have reached the level parallel to the first revival.* The *first meaning of decree* is the *speaker* — the *natiq* (naa-tiq — the speaker-prophet), and those *who take his place*, the *foundations* (the *asas* (a-saas), the *wasi* (al-waa-see — the guardian)) — *all of them are, in their act, command, prohibition, and judgment in the call already in existence (best prayers and peace be upon them), having been in potency, identical with the meaning indicated by the word "judge."* The *speaker-Prophet* IS *al-qada* at the highest rank — the *judge* in whose person the call that was in potency is brought into act.

Sub-chapter four gives the *second meaning*. *Decree is what emerges from the potency of revelation and the Shariʿah — the sciences that are not explained in them but are present in them by potency — into explanation and act, by which souls are taken out of the limit of potency into the limit of act.* The *second decree* is the *bringing-out* of the inner sciences of revelation and Shariʿah by *ta'wil* (ta-weel — interpretation) into the act of the receiving soul — the *raising by degrees* by which the receiver is taken out of potency into perfection. This is what *al-Islah* means by his interpretation of the word "decree" — and al-Kirmani lets it stand on this meaning, even as he refuses *al-Islah*'s bodily analogy.

Sub-chapter five gives the *third meaning*. *Decree is what emerges from action, and the latency of events, and the impossibilities estimated in the world to move the moving and to still the still consonants — those that admit no reversal — therefore, when it happens and comes to pass in the verb of one who decrees a matter that is not due to Allah's decree, these are the things that are said to require recompense.* The *third decree* is the *metaphysical fixity* of the laws of motion and stillness in the world — the laws of nature themselves, which *admit no reversal*. When something happens in the world *contrary to* the decree of Allah, it is named *something requiring recompense* — the metaphysical opposite-of-Allah's-decree, which the world's structure does not permit and the moral order penalizes when a creature pretends to it.

The three decrees stand: the *speaker-Prophet* (the highest); the *bringing-out by ta'wil* (the middle); the *metaphysical fixity of the world's laws* (the lowest). Three ranks of *qada*. The chain runs.

## Sub-chapters six through eight — the three meanings of destiny

Sub-chapter six gives the *first destiny*. *As for the qaʾim, he is first the promised Qaʾim* (qaa-im — the awaited one) *promised by Adam (peace be upon him) and by his summons — the one who guards it — and by the Imams and Messengers (peace be upon them) in the period of concealment, extending the force of the fact that he is destined to come out from estimation to action and existence.* The *first qadar* is the *promised Qaʾim* — the awaited fulfillment of the chain that runs from Adam through the speaker-Prophets and the Imams to the *one who is destined to come out from estimation to action*. The chapter notes the linguistic root: *the interpretation of Laylat al-Qadr* (lay-lat al-qadr — the Night of Destiny) *and the references the shaykhs make to him in their sayings, became "al-Qadr / destiny."* The *Night of Destiny* is the night in which the awaited fulfillment is decreed — and the *speakers of the call* reach for this name when they speak of the awaited Qaʾim.

Sub-chapter seven gives the *second destiny*. *The qadar is, second, upon us — what the Prophet (peace and blessings of Allah be upon him) brought to him and to his family from revelation and Shariʿah: the ruling and the parables that have the force of the sciences and the knowledge that the soul needs in its passage to maturity and action.* The *second qadar* is the *revelation and the Shariʿah* — what the Prophet  brought to his community in plural form, *the ruling and the parables that have the force of the sciences* the soul needs *in its passage to maturity and action*. *There is no knowledge of its outer face except by interpretation of it.* The *zahir* (the apparent) of the Shariʿah is *measured* — it is *qadar* in the sense of *the limited, the appointed, the measured-out by potency*; the *batin* (the hidden) is reached only by the *ta'wil*. The *statement of the author of al-Islah toward that refers to this meaning of qadar.*

Sub-chapter eight gives the *third destiny*. *The qadar is, third, for the world with its parts that are in its power to come out into the universe and into action upon the passage of days, months, years, events, and worlds whose presence is necessary from it in the constant movement of the movers below it.* The *third qadar* is the *world unfolding in time* — the days, months, years, events, and worlds that *come out into action upon the passage* of the chain in the constant movement of the movers below. *Because it is a decree, it is said: what Allah has decreed will be, for it is predestined being in terms of the necessity of its existence; and if it lasts for a long time, it is like the being in actuality.* The Qur'anic anchor lands here:

> *They see it as far, and We see it as near.* (Quran 70:6–7)

The unfolding of the world in time *looks far* to the creatures who watch it; it *looks near* to Allah, in whose view what is *decreed* is *already being*. *There was no estimation but that if there is one, it is a judgment; if the word "judgment" is an indication that what was not in power has gone out to action — these are the meanings of qadaʾ and qadar.*

The three destinies stand: the *promised Qaʾim* (the highest); the *revelation and Shariʿah* (the middle); the *world unfolding in time* (the lowest). Three ranks of *qadar*, mirroring the three ranks of *qada*. The chain runs the same ranks at both ends.

The architectural doctrine the chapter is settling does not float free of the Prophetic anchor at the rank of *iman* itself. The famous *hadith of Jibreel* preserved in *Sahih Muslim* names belief in *al-qadar* — *the good of it and the evil of it* — among the constitutive pillars of faith. The angel, in the form of a man whose clothes were intensely white and whose hair was intensely black, asked the Prophet  to define faith. The Prophet  replied:

> *That you believe in Allah, His angels, His books, His messengers, the Last Day, and that you believe in al-qadar, the good of it and the evil of it.* — (Sahih Muslim, Book 1, Hadith 1; narrated by Umar ibn al-Khattab)

Belief in *al-qadar — the good and the evil of it* — is the principle at the head of the chapter's whole adjudication. The reformers' dispute about prior-and-posterior is the *architectural elaboration* of what the Prophet  named as a single pillar; al-Kirmani's three-rank settlement of *qada* and *qadar* is the *ta'wil*-elaboration of that pillar at the rank of the metaphysical chain. *Al-Islah* and *al-Nusra* are not disputing *whether* to believe in *al-qadar* — both are believers in the pillar; they are disputing the *architecture by which the pillar holds together*.

And al-Kirmani lands the *order* on the basis of the three-and-three reading. *If you consider the words of the author of al-Islah, and his saying that qadar precedes qadaʾ in respect of authenticity — its meaning is correct only in what the Prophet (peace and blessings of Allah be upon him) brought from revelation and the Shariʿah, which precedes the judgment that is the interpretation, as he did, and the first mercy of Allah upon him. And the saying of the author of al-Nusra, that qadaʾ precedes qadar — if you hope to take it, its meaning is correct only in the speaker (peace be upon him), the already-existing one, which is the advanced elimination of what came from the revelation and the Shariʿah, which is qadar.* Both reformers were *partly right* — *al-Islah* in the rank of revelation-and-Shariʿah; *al-Nusra* in the rank of the speaker-Prophet. Neither was wholly right because neither distinguished the three ranks. The chapter's first settlement.

## Sub-chapter nine — the First Intellect is not the assembly of the images

The author of *al-Nusra* had cited a wisdom-tradition: *when Allah Almighty created the intellect, He completed the worlds, because it is the assembly of the images of the worlds.* If the intellect is the *assembly of the images of the worlds*, then it is the *thing already containing all that will be* — and from this *al-Nusra* derived his claim that decree-as-spiritual-image precedes destiny-as-estimation.

Al-Kirmani refuses the doctrine at its head. *The intellect that holds the images of the worlds is not the first creative intellect, nor is it the second intellect, which is the second emanated; rather, it is the intellect that ascends from the world of nature by acquiring work and knowledge in parallel with the first emanation, so it was raised a second raising, thus becoming a compendium of the images of the worlds despite their multitude.* The *intellect that is the assembly of the images* is *not* the First Intellect — it is the *intellect that has been raised by acquisition* through the work and knowledge of the *speakers, executors, and supports* (the *natiqs*, the *asases*, the *imams*, the *hujjas* (huj-jahs — the proofs)) until it gathers all the images. The First Intellect *does not need the intellect below it, and it does not intellect anything, because by virtue of being in its own self of eternity, completeness, subsistence, and unity, and abstention — but for the sake of what is found... it is impossible for him to intellect anything other than himself, because there is no need for that.* The First Intellect is *self-knowing alone*, in its pure unity. The *acquired-intellect-of-the-receivers* — which al-Kirmani says is *the second emanation that is the universal Soul informed by the chain of the hudud* — is the *assembly of the images*. The reference: *Our book known as Rahat al-'Aql* (raa-hat al-aql — *The Repose of the Intellect*) *contains, in evidence of legal proofs for this and of religious depictions, what suffices and heals the inquirer from his adversary.*

> *The first creative intellect is a pure unity in self-knowing alone, abstracted from need; below it the universal Soul receives the gift of the images by way of the hudud, and the souls of the speakers and the executors and the supports are the intellects-by-acquisition that gather the images of both worlds in themselves by the rank of receivership they have reached.* — al-Kirmani, *Rahat al-'Aql* (the rank of the First Intellect and the rank of the acquired intellect)

The First Intellect is *pure unity self-knowing alone*. The intellect that is *the assembly of the images of the worlds* is the *intellect-by-acquisition* that the *speakers and executors and supports* — the *hudud of the da'wa* — gather by the rank they have reached on the chain. *Al-Nusra*'s premise has been corrected at its root.

## Sub-chapters ten through fifteen — *We created every thing according to a measure*, and the Pen-Tablet-Writer architecture

Sub-chapter ten opens the longest dispute of the chapter. *Al-Islah* invokes the verse:

> *Indeed, We have created everything according to a measure.* (Quran 54:49)

*Al-Islah*'s reading: the verse names *qadar* as the *posterior* — *all the essences were emanated from the precedent predestining*; the emanation IS *qadar*; *qada* is what was *done*, what *contains changes and alterations* — the book of which Allah says *Allah erases what He wills and confirms* (Quran 13:39). *Qadar is posterior, as we have said, and qada is what was done.* The reformers' agreement on the *form* of the dispute is preserved; on the *content* (which is which) they continue to disagree.

Sub-chapter eleven through fifteen work *al-Nusra*'s counter-reading of the same verse. *Al-Nusra*'s exegesis takes each word: *the meaning of "We" by which He intends a subject not tainted by description or attribute, and "every thing" by which the intellect (which is the totality of things) is intended, and "We have created it" by which an image is intended — the two worlds are among them. And the measure by which the posterior is intended.* The structure that lands the reading is the *Pen-Tablet-Writer* image. *All the spiritual and physical images appeared among them in the testimony, which is the pen, the tablet, the writing, and the writer. The writer is the face of "We," and He is pure eternity. The pen is the pen of the intellect, which is the pen of the pen, and "We have created it" is the figure of the tablet. The pen, in counterpart to the letters that appear upon the tablet — and the measure is what is in front of the tablet, receptive to all the forms of the letters. As all the physical and spiritual things, the simple and the dense, are located under these intelligible letters that appear from the writer with the pen upon the tablet, so likewise all the upper and lower images were shown by Allah Almighty by means of the intellect and the soul, and He has not singled out anything between them.*

The architecture in brief: *Writer* = the face of "We" = pure eternity (the rank beyond the Intellect). *Pen* = the First Intellect (the *al-Qalam* — *the Pen of the pen*). *Tablet* = the universal Soul (the *al-Lawh* (al-lawh) — the rank that receives the writing of the Pen). *Measure* = what is in front of the Tablet, *receptive to all the forms of the letters* — the rank at which the *unfolding into all the spiritual and physical images* is *measured out* under the *intelligible letters that appear from the writer with the pen upon the tablet*. The metaphysical architecture of the universe is *Writer-Pen-Tablet-Measure* — and the verse names *qadar* (the *measure*) as the rank at which the writing of the Pen on the Tablet is received and unfolded.

Al-Kirmani enters in sub-chapters thirteen through fifteen with the verdict that holds for the chapter's dispute. *The authors have mentioned in this chapter what is unsustainable, no more than this in al-Islah, nor in this case of refutation; because the author of al-Islah did not make it obligatory that qadar be the name of the posterior, and he argued for that by saying that Allah Almighty's "Indeed, We have created everything according to a measure" preceded the words "qadar and qada,"* — that is, the verse's chronology-in-the-text does not force qadar = posterior. *Al-Nusra* has refuted *al-Islah*'s tight syllogism, but his own reading is itself open to the objection that *the word "We" is only to the extent that the Prophet derives, by establishing his rank in the world — not pure non-attribution*. The architectural reading stands, but the *rank-by-rank attribution* of the verse's *We* needs more care than *al-Nusra* gave it.

And al-Kirmani's own settled reading lands. *The decree (qada) is the act of the speaker who has gone out into action and obtained perfection with the support of Allah; the destiny (qadar) is the emanation by which the physical world was determined to be something of it, in accordance with what we have explained in the chapter of qadar.* The chain runs: *speaker-Prophet → revelation and Shariʿah → world unfolding in time*; the *Pen-Tablet-Writer* architecture gives the metaphysical heights at which the verse's *measure* names the *receptive rank* on which the images of both worlds are written. *Qadar* is the *posterior in this sense* — the rank that *receives* what the *Pen* writes on the *Tablet*.

## Sub-chapters sixteen through nineteen — *Flee from Allah's qada to His qadar*, and the three meanings

Sub-chapter sixteen turns to the Prophetic word that has divided the dispute since the early generations:

> *Flee from Allah's qada to His qadar.*

The Prophetic word's historical anchor is preserved in the famous narration of *Sahih al-Bukhari* on the authority of Abdullah ibn Abbas. Umar ibn al-Khattab was on his way to Sham when, at the town of Sargh, news reached him that the plague had broken out in the region. Umar consulted his counselors, then announced that he would return to Madina with the company. Abu Ubayda ibn al-Jarrah objected: *Are you fleeing from Allah's qada?* Umar replied:

> *Yes — we flee from Allah's qada to Allah's qadar. Do you not see that if you had camels and they descended into a valley with two slopes, one fertile and one barren, you would graze them on the fertile slope by Allah's qadar, and you would graze them on the barren slope also by Allah's qadar?* — (Sahih al-Bukhari, Book 76, Hadith 5729; narrated by Abdullah ibn Abbas)

Abd al-Rahman ibn 'Awf then arrived and reported the Prophetic word that closed the matter:

> *If you hear of the plague in a land, do not enter it; and if it falls in a land while you are in it, do not flee from it.* — (Sahih al-Bukhari, Book 76, Hadith 5729; narrated by Abd al-Rahman ibn 'Awf)

The historical episode lays out the *literal frame* on which *al-Islah*'s reading rests: a *flight* from one *decree* (the plague-as-finished, the *qada* that has descended into the encampment of those who entered the stricken land) to another *measure* (the open land where the soul may still be raised by what Allah measures out next). The flight is not from God; it is from one *ordering of His hand* into another *ordering of the same hand* — exactly the architectural pattern *al-Islah*'s contextual image of *the leaning wall* will trace at the rank of the Prophet  himself.

*Al-Islah* gives a literal reading. *He wanted to flee from the latter of the prior endorsement to the posterior statement, like the flight of a man from the fire — which is the proof of the endorsement — to the water, which is the proof of the statement. He wanted to flee from the thing that had been sealed and decreed, lest he be prepared, pushing him toward what he had not yet undergone.* The Prophet  (peace and blessings of Allah be upon him) flees *from the decree* (the *finished*) *to the destiny* (the *still-being-measured*) — *as a man flees from fire to water*. And *al-Islah* offers the contextual image: *the Messenger of Allah  passed from Allah's qada to His qadar because of a cause, and the cause was the leaning wall that he warned against falling, and he hastened his walking.* The reference is the verse of *Surat al-Kahf* on Moses and al-Khidr:

> *And they found in it a wall on the verge of collapse, so he set it up.* (Quran 18:77)

*Al-Islah*'s *ta'wil*: the *leaning wall* is the *limit upon whom apostasy and hypocrisy* had inclined; the Prophet  *hastens past* the wall because *if he apostatized, he fell from his rank*; the *setting upright* of the wall is the *speed of walking* — the *pre-emptive correction* by which the limit-in-danger is *held in place*. The Prophet  addresses *those given to hypocrisy with preaching and warning, in the outward form without the inward statement* — and his *flight from Allah's qada to His qadar* is the *flight from the decreed-finished* (the outward Shariʿah by which the hypocrite is held in place) *to the still-being-measured* (the inward *ta'wil* by which the receiver is raised).

*Al-Nusra* refuses the literal flight-from-fire-to-water reading. *It is not a matter of interpreting "flee from Allah's qada to His qadar" according to what one imagines... that he was fleeing from the next rank to the preceding rank — because the prophets, peace be upon them, never ask for heights, and asking for heights is not asking for...* And *al-Nusra* gives three meanings. *The meaning herein divides into three meanings, and each of these proves that the elimination is of the preceding and the predestining is of the next.*

The *first* meaning, *the greatest, the most accurate in meaning*: the *preceding* is the *support* (*al-sabq* (as-sabq) — the *prior support* of the First Intellect on the chain); the *next* is the *composition* (*al-tarkib* (at-tar-keeb) — the *posterior composition* of the Shariʿah laid out in the receiver). *The end is more like an endorsement, and the composition is more like a synthesis.* The Prophet  flees from the *intensity of the prior support* (the rank that overwhelms the speaker) to the *composition* by which the Shariʿah is *laid out* in the world. He does not flee from the *higher rank to the lower rank*; he flees from *being seized by the prior* to *holding the composition that is his work*.

The *second* meaning: *his nation's media* — the Prophet  *benefits from the first by means of the second*; he is, *in everything he wanted to stand upon from the first, by means of the second referring to him* — *as if he said, "Return to all that I want to understand."* The second rank — the rank of the *Foundation*, the *Imam*, the *hujjah* — is *the reason between me and the intellect*. The Prophet  *flees to the second rank* because the second rank is *how the first rank speaks back into the world*.

The *third* meaning, *the most honorable*, is what *al-Islah* mentioned: *his escape from the intensity of the light of the support of the preceding to the support of the next, as the grandfather* (al-Jadd (al-jadd) — *the Grandfather*, in the *da'wa* technical vocabulary, the highest hierarchical rank below the First Intellect) *was with his spiritual power and could not bear it until his identity almost faded.* The Prophet  flees the *intensity of the prior support* — the same intensity *under which the Jadd nearly fades* — to the *next support* by which the gift can be *carried* into the world *without the carrier being consumed*. This is what Moses says in *the story of Moses (peace be upon him)* — the verse that sub-chapter twenty will take up.

Al-Kirmani enters in sub-chapters seventeen, eighteen, and nineteen with the verdict. *Al-Nusra*'s first two meanings are *not aligned with what the matter actually is* — they distinguish *the first and the second* (the First Intellect and the Second Intellect of *al-Nusra*'s scheme) as the *prior and posterior of qada and qadar*, which is *not what qada and qadar mean*. *Whoever says about them is as if he had said about honey, which is naturally sweet, that it is patience that is naturally bitter.* The first two meanings *cross the natural joints* of the doctrine of decree-and-destiny. But *the third meaning that he mentioned positively, that the speaker fled from the intensity of the light of the preceding support to the support of the next — which is contrary to the duty and interpretation in the case of the grandfather that was cited — needs to be investigated, and the words of the author of al-Islah in the chapter come stronger.* The *third meaning* is sustainable — and *al-Islah*'s contextual image of the *leaning wall*, taken as the *limit in danger* whom the Prophet  *sets upright by the speed of his walking past*, is the *stronger* reading.

## Sub-chapter twenty — Moses, Shu'ayb, and the politics of timing

Sub-chapter twenty turns to the chapter's deepest *ta'wil*. The verse:

> *My Lord, show me, that I may look upon You. He said, "You will not see Me, but look at the mountain; if it settles in its place, you will see Me." And when his Lord appeared to the mountain, He made it Dhaka (crumbled to dust). And Moses fell down struck by a thunderbolt. When he recovered, he said, "Glory be to You, I repent to You."* (Quran 7:143)

*Al-Islah*'s reading: Moses (peace be upon him) was *told he would not see, and was ordered to limit himself to what was occurring to him seriously*; the *mountain* is *the grandfather* in his closeness to the divine; *settle on his rank* means *bear what I have shown him*; the *grandfather did not bear* the manifestation — *and so Moses learned that the lower could not bear what the higher bears*.

Al-Kirmani refuses the reading at its root. *The interpretation of this verse, according to what each of the two persons used to say, did not place Moses (peace be upon him) as the highest of the prophets; rather, it preserves the upper limits. They took him out of the group of scholars and counted him among the ignorant, by claiming that he asked for what he does not perceive from above him among the borders. How could this be, when the matter passes upon him in it? The interpretation of the verse is different from what he said, because it was not addressed to him.*

The *ta'wil* al-Kirmani gives is political and architectural at once. *Moses (peace be upon him) was with the upper borders, but he was addressing him with the borders when he had not yet reached the rank of pronunciation. He asked the author of the command, before his time had come — because he was in the time of a period of intermission, and the people of truth were under affliction, the covering, and concealment from the side of opposites and violators. And Moses, peace be upon him, saw in himself a power that he reckoned he could use in his time, if he commanded the lifting of that ordeal from the people of truth, and taking revenge upon the enemies of Allah, and the politics of the nation by the policy of truth.*

The *Lord* whom Moses addresses is *the author of the command, namely Shu'ayb (peace be upon him), when he came to him and he was his Lord, after he had explained to him his command in the matter.* The *show me, that I may look upon You* is *command me, that I may manage this matter and reveal the grief* — Moses asking for the *agency to lift the affliction from the people of truth in his time* by *the policy of truth*.

*You will not see Me* is *you will not see Me do any of that, nor will those in this time. For everything has its time and season, and this is not its time.* The Author of the Command — Shu'ayb (peace be upon him) — *teaches him that what he asked for is difficult, and in his time it is not done by those who have the power, help, and equipment*. The *mountain* is *this man who has worldly power, equipment, and capability* — and *if he is fixed in what he has been commanded, and is enabled by his power and help, to lay out the provisions of the policy of truth — then you will see Me do what I have asked of them.* And *when he ordered him to erect a beacon of truth, He made it Dhaka — that is, the matter narrowed upon the people, they did not accept the command, they split the staff, and the matter was confused and disturbed. His presidency was about to vanish, when the people went out against him.*

Moses learned, *then, that what he was destined to accomplish at that time was destined to be impossible. The owner of the matter, who is his Lord, knew the story better than him, so he was awakened — he was bewildered by that warning, and he said: "Glory be to Almighty Allah, I repent to You." So he retracted his statement, regretted, and was patient with what the owner of al-Nusrah had ordered him to do. When that period in which he had hired him had elapsed, then the time came, and there came something of which he was right.*

The *ta'wil* lands. The verse is *not about Moses asking to see a higher rank above his own*; it is about *Moses asking for political agency at the wrong time, being shown that the time is not ripe by the failure of the political instrument, repenting the asking, and being given the agency when the time later comes*. The Qur'anic anchor for this *ta'wil*-arc:

> *We will show them Our signs in the horizons and in their own selves until it becomes clear to them that it is the truth.* (Quran 41:53)

The *signs in the horizons* are *the universes whose movement in time reveals the decree*; the *signs in their own selves* are *the souls in their passage from potency to act*. Moses, in *seeing the matter narrowed on the people who would not accept the command*, was being *shown a sign in the horizons* — the *qadar of the world unfolding in time* showed him that *the qada of his agency was not yet ripe*. Destiny showed him the limits of decree. The chapter's *ta'wil* of the verse is the *ta'wil* of the chain itself: *qada and qadar at the rank of the speaker and his time*.

## Sub-chapters twenty-one and twenty-two — the doctrine *the mind is all things, all things are the mind* refused

Sub-chapter twenty-one returns to *al-Nusra*'s exegetical claim about *and the creation of every thing*. *Al-Nusra* had taken the verse to mean *the mind is all things, and all things are the mind* — and from this had derived his reading of *qadar* as the *posterior emanation* of *all things from the precedent mind*.

Al-Kirmani refuses the doctrine. *As for saying that the mind is all things, and all things are the mind, this is a matter that is not true. The physical world, with all it gathers, is not a mind; it is a thing. And better than what is attributed to it is the meaning of saying that the mind is all things, and all things are the mind, is that the intellect is the cause of all things, because the intellect — which is identical with the command of Allah — is the cause of the existence of all things, physical and non-physical alike; and the existence of all things proceeds from the intellect and toward its purpose.*

The *correct doctrine*: the *Intellect* (the *al-'Aql*, identical with *the command of Allah*) is *the cause of the existence of all things*. *All things proceed from the Intellect and toward its purpose*. But the *Intellect is not the things themselves*; the *things are not the Intellect itself*. *The mind is all things* is wrong; *the mind is the cause of all things* is right.

> *The First Intellect is the originated which is the command of Allah; from it the universal Soul flows by emanation; from the Soul the seven heavens, the four elements, the three genera, and the human being descend by the chain of receivership; the Intellect is not the things and the things are not the Intellect — the Intellect is the cause whose work the chain of receivership unfolds.* — al-Kirmani, *Rahat al-'Aql* (the chain from First Intellect to human being)

The architectural correction lands. The *Intellect causes*; the *chain receives*; the *things are not what causes them*. *Al-Nusra*'s identification of *mind* and *things* is a confusion of the *cause* with the *effect*, and the verse *Indeed, We have created everything according to a measure* will not bear the identification. The *measure* is the *receptive rank in front of the Tablet*; the *Pen* is the *Intellect that writes upon the Tablet*; the *things* are *what the writing unfolds into* — not what the Intellect *is*.

## Sub-chapter twenty-three — *no blame upon those who believe* is not a license to abandon the Shariʿah

Sub-chapter twenty-three turns to *al-Nusra*'s most dangerous exegetical move. *Al-Nusra* had cited the verse:

> *There is no blame upon those who believe and do righteous deeds for what they have eaten, so long as they fear Allah and believe and do righteous deeds; then fear Him and believe; then fear Him and do good — and Allah loves the doers of good.* (Quran 5:93)

*Al-Nusra*'s reading: *Allah has permitted the Prophets the inner meanings of the laws and has dispensed with their literal use*. The verse permits, in *al-Nusra*'s reading, *the abandonment of the canons of the Shariʿah by those who have reached the inner meaning* — what the *batiniyya* critics of the *da'wa* have always feared was the secret teaching of the Ismaili tradition.

Al-Kirmani refuses the reading categorically. *The verse contains no mention of permission for the prophets to abandon the canons of the Shariʿah. I do not know how he reached this conclusion before the saying had been properly examined. The interpretation of this verse is other than what he has claimed.* And he gives the architectural setting: *Allah Almighty knew that souls do not attain happiness, do not reach the degree of perfection, and do not pass from the limit of potency to the limit of act except through work and knowledge — both of which require every human being to perform the two acts of worship, in knowledge and in deed, so that they may attain happiness in the two abodes.* The doctrine is the doctrine of *the two worships* (the *zahir* and the *batin*, the outer Shariʿah and the inner *ta'wil*) settled in *Chapter Seven* — *whichever soul abandons both of them, or even one of them, is far from attaining perfection.*

And then the alternative *ta'wil* of Quran 5:93. *The verse has an interpretation other than the first, with a particular bearing on those who summon the preachers to the Tawhid of Allah Almighty and to the knowledge of His limits through the Imams (peace be upon them).* The Prophet , *knowing that his community would differ after him*, granted *in his loyal Imams (peace and blessings of Allah be upon them) licenses for the preachers of the da'wa to consider sciences not of their own creed, on conditions.*

The *what they have eaten* is *what they study from sciences that are not of their own creed*; the *no blame* is *no constraint* on the *preachers of the da'wa* in *considering* the sciences of *other peoples*, so that *those sciences become like a weapon with which they fight opponents in proving the truth*. The three repeated injunctions of the verse (*fear Allah and believe and do righteous deeds; then fear Him and believe; then fear Him and do good*) are *three conditions*. *One*: *their path is straight in obedience to Allah Almighty by following His Messenger, in belief in His limits, and in the establishment of both acts of worship in accordance with what He says*. *Two*: *obey the Messenger in what he commands, and the one in charge after him in his place, and call to them outwardly and inwardly*. *Three*: *obey the Imam in the one whom he establishes as a deputy, and do well in calling to the Tawhid of Allah under his leadership*. The verse is *a license to study other-tradition sciences as a weapon for the work of the da'wa, under the three conditions of straightness, obedience, and the Imam's leadership* — *not* a license to abandon the Shariʿah.

*Our loyal Imams (peace be upon them from Allah Almighty) are pure of what he has said, and of every interpretation that contradicts what the Prophet  and his companions said.*

> *We are the ones of whom Allah said* "those of His servants who believe and do righteous deeds." *Whoever takes us as guides, his guidance is the guidance of Allah; whoever abandons us, his work is in vain. The two worships are the inheritance from the Prophet  — the zahir is the body of the religion, the batin is its spirit; he who keeps the body and abandons the spirit, his religion is dead; he who claims the spirit and abandons the body, his religion is a lie.* — Imam al-Mu'izz li-Din Allah Ma'add ibn Isma'il (peace be upon him), *Ta'wil al-Shari'a*

Imam al-Mu'izz (peace be upon him) — the Cairo-court Imam whose *Ta'wil al-Shari'a* is the Fatimid synthesis of the doctrine of the *two worships* — names exactly the principle al-Kirmani is defending against *al-Nusra*'s misreading: the *zahir* and the *batin* are *body and spirit*; the *abandonment of either* is *not religion*. The verse is no license to abandon either. The chapter's most dangerous gloss has been refused at its source.

## Sub-chapter twenty-four — the three-position order: decree at the beginning, apportioning in the middle, completion at the end

Sub-chapter twenty-four closes the chapter on the three-position order that the dispute has been driving toward all along.

*The author of al-Nusra said: The decree (qada) is the beginning, the apportioning (qadar) is the middle, and completion is the end — because apportioning can only proceed by the decree.* And he had refined: *If the decree is the end, it is not right to call it a decree, because the decree comes before all else, and things come after the decree, and the decree is for a thing. Perfection is not for nothing; rather, perfection is for the very thing that becomes perfect.*

The three-position order names the chain at the heights the chapter has been building. *Decree at the beginning*: the *qada* at the *first rank* — the *speaker-Prophet* who is *judge* in whose person the call that was in potency is brought into act, and (in the metaphysical heights) the *Pen* that writes upon the *Tablet*. *Apportioning in the middle*: the *qadar* at the *middle rank* — the *revelation and Shariʿah* that the speaker-Prophet *brings out in measured form, so that the receiving soul may come out in interpretation to what it deserves*, and (in the metaphysical heights) the *measure in front of the Tablet, receptive to all the forms of the letters*. *Completion at the end*: the *reassured soul* (*al-nafs al-mutma'inna* (an-nafs al-mut-ma-in-nah)) that has been *raised by degrees according to its acceptance* until it has received what is *decreed at the beginning* through *what is apportioned in the middle* and now *returns to its Lord, pleased and pleasing*.

> *O reassured soul, return to your Lord, pleased and pleasing; enter among My servants; enter My Paradise.* (Quran 89:27–30)

The verse — first surfaced in *Chapter Seven* at the seal of the *ta'wil* of Bandhaqlis on the speaking soul and the bestial soul — is now the *completion at the end* of *Chapter Eight*'s three-position order. The *reassured soul* is the *qadar at the last rank*, the *receiver who has been raised by degrees* until the *decree at the first rank* has been carried, *through the apportioning at the middle rank*, into the *completion at the last*.

The personal-supplication parallel to the Qur'anic anchor sits in *Sahifa al-Sajjadiyya*, the prayer-book of Imam Ali Zayn al-Abidin (peace be upon him), the great-grandson of the Prophet  — the fourth Imam, who survived Karbala and whose collected supplications the tradition treasures as the *Psalms of the House of the Prophet*. Du'a 7 — *His Supplication When a Worrisome Task Befell Him or a Misfortune Came Down Upon Him* — addresses precisely the rank of the soul that the chapter is settling. The Imam opens:

> *O He through whom the knots of detestable affairs are untied! O He through whom the sharpness of hardships is dulled! O He from whom is requested the way out to the cool breeze of relief! Difficult matters are made easy by Your power, and means are made smooth by Your kindness, and what is decreed proceeds by Your strength, and things run on toward Your will.* — Imam Ali Zayn al-Abidin (peace be upon him), Sahifa al-Sajjadiyya, Du'a 7 (His Supplication When a Worrisome Task Befell Him)

The supplication's architecture is the architecture the chapter has been building. The *knots of detestable affairs* are the *measures* by which the world unfolds in time — the *third qadar*, *what Allah has decreed will be, predestined being in the necessity of its existence*. The *untying of the knots* is the *raising of the soul by the chain* — the *apportioning in the middle* by which the receiver is taken out of the limit of potency into the limit of act. The *way out to the cool breeze of relief* is the *completion at the end* — the *reassured soul that returns to its Lord, pleased and pleasing*. *What is decreed proceeds by Your strength, and things run on toward Your will* names the metaphysical chain at its full extent: the *qada at the beginning* runs by the *strength* of the Originator; the *qadar at the middle* runs *toward* the *will* by which the writing of the Pen is laid out upon the Tablet. The fourth Imam's supplication, taken on the *ta'wil*, is the personal voice of what *Chapter Eight* has named at the architectural heights — the *Pen-Tablet-Writer* architecture *carried into the heart of the receiver* who is now *being raised through the apportioning in the middle to the completion at the end*.

And al-Kirmani closes. *In all that has gone before in his words, the answer is bound up with fate and destiny — enough has been said to comprehend his words and to extend the discussion — without going beyond what we had intended.* The chapter has done its work.

The Commander of the Believers, Imam Ali ibn Abi Talib (peace be upon him), in whose person the rank of *the foundation* (the *asas*) was carried after the Prophet , and whose voice the *da'wa* tradition treasures as the *ta'wil*-anchor at the rank of the *imam*, gives the chapter its canonical warning. When the Imam was asked about *al-qadar*, he answered:

> *It is a dark path — do not tread it. It is a deep ocean — do not enter it. It is the secret of Allah — so do not burden yourself with it.* — Imam Ali ibn Abi Talib (peace be upon him), (Nahj al-Balagha, Aphorism 287)

The aphorism is the Imam's verdict on the very dispute the chapter has been adjudicating. *Al-Islah* with his tailor-analogy, *al-Nusra* with his identification of *mind* and *things* and his inversion of the order of decree-and-destiny — both reformers reaching by *their own intellect alone* into the *dark path*, both nearly burned by the *intensity of the support* under which even the *Jadd* nearly fades, both struggling against the deep ocean as if the depth were a problem to be solved rather than a *secret of Allah* to be received from the rank that was given. The Imam's warning is *not* a prohibition on the doctrine of *qada* and *qadar*; it is the prohibition on the *reckless rationalism* that would lift the veils without the lamp the chain has been given.

The chapter has named the *two veils* — *qada* at the head, *qadar* at the rank of the receiver — and has named the *lifting of the veils* as the *work of the chain*, the *hudud of the da'wa*. The *intensity of the support* is the very *intensity* in which the *Jadd* nearly fades; the *rank that has been given by the chain* is the *composition* by which the *Prophet * flees from the *preceding* to the *next*. Take *qada* and *qadar* with the chain — with the *speaker-Prophet, the foundation, the imam, the hujjah, the da'i, the ma'dhun, the mukasir* whose hand is the *hand of the conveyance* — and the dark path is given the lamp by which it becomes legible; take *qada* and *qadar* without the chain, and the path remains dark and the deep ocean drowns the unprepared. The chapter's deepest doctrine settles at this rank.

## The chapter's settled formula

The twenty-four sub-chapters land on one settled architecture. *Qada* and *qadar* are not one-rank words — they are *three-rank words*. *Qada* at the first rank is the *speaker-Prophet who is judge*, in whose person the call in potency is brought into act; at the middle rank is the *bringing-out by ta'wil* by which the inner sciences of revelation are taken into act in the soul; at the third rank is the *metaphysical fixity of the world's laws*. *Qadar* at the first rank is the *promised Qa'im*, the awaited fulfillment of the chain; at the middle rank is the *revelation and Shariʿah* brought by the Prophet  in measured form so that the receiving soul may come out *in interpretation to what it deserves*; at the third rank is the *world unfolding in time*, whose presence is *necessary by its decree* and which *what Allah has decreed will be*. At the metaphysical heights, the verse *Indeed, We have created everything according to a measure* (Quran 54:49) names the *Pen-Tablet-Writer* architecture — *Writer* as the face of "We," *Pen* as the First Intellect, *Tablet* as the universal Soul, *Measure* as the receptive rank in front of the Tablet on which all the spiritual and physical images are unfolded. The Prophetic word *Flee from Allah's qada to His qadar* is best read on its *third meaning, the most honorable* — the *speaker flees from the intensity of the preceding support (under which even the Jadd nearly fades) to the next support by which the composition of the Shariʿah is laid out in the world* — and *al-Islah*'s contextual image of the *leaning wall, the limit-in-danger whom the Prophet  sets upright by the speed of his walking past*, is the *stronger reading* of the situation. The verse of Moses *show me, that I may look upon You* (Quran 7:143) is best read on the *political ta'wil* — Moses (peace be upon him) asking the Author of the Command, Shu'ayb (peace be upon him), for the *agency to lift the affliction from the people of truth in his time by the policy of truth*; being shown that the time is not ripe by the failure of the political instrument (the mountain crumbled, the matter narrowed on the people who would not accept the command); repenting the asking; being given the agency when the time later comes — *qada and qadar at the rank of the speaker and his time*. The doctrine that *the mind is all things and all things are the mind* is refused as a confusion of cause and effect — the *Intellect is the cause of all things*, but the *things are not the Intellect itself*. The verse *no blame upon those who believe and do righteous deeds for what they have eaten* (Quran 5:93) is no license to abandon the Shariʿah; it is *a license to the preachers of the da'wa to study other-tradition sciences as a weapon for the work of the da'wa, under the three conditions of straightness, obedience to the Messenger and his deputy, and the Imam's leadership*. And the three-position order — *decree at the beginning, apportioning in the middle, completion at the end* — names the chain at its heights: *qada at the first rank* is the writing of the Pen upon the Tablet; *qadar at the middle rank* is the measure in front of the Tablet by which what is written is unfolded into the world; *completion at the end* is the *reassured soul* that *returns to its Lord, pleased and pleasing*.

The political stake of *Chapter Eight* sits one layer below the metaphysics. If *al-Islah*'s tailor-analogy held in the bodily form alone, the doctrine of *qada* and *qadar* would lose its architectural ranks. If *al-Nusra*'s identification of *mind* and *things* held, the doctrine of the First Intellect as *pure unity self-knowing alone* would fall. If *al-Nusra*'s reading of *Flee from Allah's qada to His qadar* as *flight from the next rank to the preceding rank* held, the rank of the *Prophet  and his composition of the Shariʿah* would be inverted with the rank of the *prior support* — and the doctrine of the *chain by which the gift is conveyed* would lose its order. If *al-Islah*'s reading of *show me, that I may look upon You* held — Moses ignorant, asking for a rank above his — the *ta'wil* by which the verse names the *politics of timing* would be lost. If *al-Nusra*'s reading of *no blame upon those who believe* held, the *two worships* (the *zahir* and the *batin*) would lose their *both-required* discipline, and the *da'wa* would dissolve into the *batiniyya* slander the orthodox have always charged. Al-Kirmani's refusals at each joint are the protection of the *chain* — whose head is *tawhid* (taw-heed), whose rank-at-each-step is the *speaker-Prophet, the foundation, the imam, the hujjah, the da'i, the ma'dhun, the mukasir*, whose midstream is the *hudud of the da'wa* by whose hand *the gift is conveyed*, and whose foot is the *reassured soul that returns to its Lord, pleased and pleasing*.

## What comes next

*Chapter Nine* of *al-Riyad* will turn to *the law of Adam* (the *Shariʿah* of Adam, peace be upon him) *and the wilayah* (wi-laa-yah — the guardianship) *of Nuh* (nooh — Noah, peace be upon him) — *how the chain that runs in time first establishes itself at the head of human history*. If *qada* and *qadar* run from the speaker-Prophet through the revelation-and-Shariʿah down to the world unfolding in time, then *who was the first speaker-Prophet, what was his Shariʿah, and how was the chain of guardianship first established after him* — when his Successor was Noah (peace be upon him), the *first to establish a guardian by way of ta'wil*?

And before that chapter opens, *Chapter Eight* leaves the reader with the image it has earned. *Al-Islah* and *al-Nusra* arguing about whether destiny precedes decree or decree precedes destiny, whether the tailor estimates before he sews or sews-in-soul before he estimates-with-the-cloth, whether the Prophet  flees from the preceding or the next, whether Moses asked for a rank above his or for the agency to lift his people's affliction at the wrong time, whether the *no blame upon those who believe* verse permits the abandonment of the Shariʿah. Al-Kirmani, generations later, arriving at the doctrine that *qada* and *qadar* are *three-rank words* at the heights of the chain — that the Pen writes upon the Tablet what the Measure receives, that the Prophet  flees the *intensity of the preceding support* to the *composition of the next* in which the Shariʿah is laid out, that Moses repented the asking at the wrong time and was given the agency when the time later came, that the *da'wa* studies the sciences of other peoples *only as a weapon under the three conditions and never as a license to abandon the Shariʿah*, that the chain runs *decree at the beginning, apportioning in the middle, completion at the end*, and that the *reassured soul* at the end *returns to its Lord, pleased and pleasing*. *They see it as far, and We see it as near* — the Qur'anic anchor that names *qadar at the rank of the world unfolding in time* as *the same destiny that Allah sees as already-being*. The architecture holds. The chain runs. The *tawhid* the chapter is built to protect stands at every rank, from the *Pen that writes upon the Tablet* to the *reassured soul that returns to its Lord*.
