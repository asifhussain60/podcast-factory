# Where the Disputants Erred

*From the Kitab al-Riyad (ki-taab ar-ri-yaad) of Hamid al-Din Ahmad al-Kirmani (haa-mid ad-deen ah-mad al-kir-maa-nee). This chapter covers Bāb 1, §14 through §26 — the middle of the first book, where al-Kirmani turns from establishing the architecture (essence-completeness, ranked-deficiency, domain-discrimination) to a sustained dissection of each disputant's specific errors. The author of al-Islah (al-is-laah) gets credit where his statement is admissible, but is corrected where his framing slips. The author of al-Nusra (an-nus-rah) is shown, again and again, to be carrying natural-world relations into a domain to which they do not belong — and on one charge, of leaning toward the heresy of Abu Bakr al-Razi (a-boo bakr ar-raa-zee), he is shown to have departed from the limits of legitimate critique altogether.*

## The frame of this middle section

By the end of §13 al-Kirmani had established the two-part formula that organizes the whole *bāb*: things are *perfect in themselves* by virtue of their essence, and *less than what stands above them* by virtue of their rank within an ordered series. The same Soul is *tamm* (tamm; complete, perfect) considered in itself, and *less than the First Intellect* considered by addition. Neither truth cancels the other.

That settled the architecture. What follows in §14 through §26 is the application. Al-Kirmani now walks through specific exchanges between the two earlier Ismaili thinkers — the author of al-Islah, who defended a particular construal of the Soul's status, and the author of al-Nusra, who attacked it. On each charge, al-Kirmani has the same question to ask: *Are these two men talking about the same world?* And the answer, almost always, is no.

What this episode is also doing is showing what an Ismaili *Da'i* (daa-ee) of al-Kirmani's stature looks like when he is being judicial. He is not partisan. He defends al-Islah where al-Islah is right, corrects him where he is wrong, gives al-Nusra full credit where al-Nusra makes a legitimate philosophical point, and pulls al-Nusra up sharply when al-Nusra crosses a doctrinal line. His method is the method of *adab al-bahth* — the etiquette of disputation — and his command of it is unmistakable.

## The three-way division and the failure of al-Nusra's fork

In §14 the author of al-Islah said, in a sentence that distilled his position: *what is incomplete is the action of the Soul, not the Soul itself; the Soul completes its action, not its own essence*.

The author of al-Nusra answered with a sharp logical fork. If the Soul's action is incomplete, he said, the incompleteness must come from one of three sources: from the deficiency of the agent itself, or from the deficiency of the matter from which the action proceeds, or from the deficiency of both. If from the agent — and al-Islah holds that the Soul is the agent — then the Soul is deficient. If from the matter, then matter has been affirmed as an eternal substance, which is a departure from the doctrine of religion, leaning toward the school of Abu Zakariyya al-Razi (a-boo za-ka-riy-yaa ar-raa-zee) and others who held matter eternal. And, al-Nusra adds with venom: *I imagined that the author of this book inclined toward this position, because he posits time as eternal alongside the intellect — that is the kernel of his doctrine*. Even if the deficiency belongs to both sides at once, the Soul's eternity follows. In every horn of the fork, al-Islah's position collapses.

Al-Kirmani's reply is one of the longest single defenses he makes of al-Islah in the whole *bāb*. He answers in three movements.

First, the agent-side horn: al-Islah has already clarified that the deficiency of the action does not necessarily attach to the Soul itself. It is in the action in the sense of its effect — and so this objection falls away.

Second, the matter-side horn: this misses the point entirely. The diminution lies in the substrates that receive perfection, not in the perfection that is received. This is well established in natural philosophy. Al-Kirmani gives the example that becomes the chapter's most memorable image:

> The sun's heat is accepted by objects that yield to it like wax, which softens; while other bodies refuse to accept it, such as stone and iron; and still others react only slowly. We have established by direct observation that the heat is one and the same; the discrepancy lies in the receivers, not in the heat itself.

One heat. Many receivers. The wax softens. The stone refuses. The iron warms only slowly. The heat itself is not three different heats. It is one heat met by three different capacities of reception. So if there is variation in how the Soul's action is realized in the world — incomplete here, complete there — that variation is on the side of the receivers, not on the side of what is being received. To leap from *the action is incomplete in matter* to *therefore matter is eternal* is a logical jump that destroys nothing al-Islah has actually said.

Third, the smear about Abu Bakr al-Razi: this is what al-Kirmani calls a *departure from the limits of knowledge and a transgression*. The author of al-Nusra has not only failed to refute al-Islah; he has attached al-Islah to a famous heretic — a known materialist in the Ismaili reading — to discredit him by association. Al-Kirmani will not dignify the smear:

> I will not dignify it — neither in the structure of the division nor in what follows from his two claims regarding diminution and the eternity of *al-hayyūli* (al-hay-yoo-lee; prime matter).

This is the most pointed moment of correction in the *bāb*. Al-Kirmani has held his fire across many sections of close philosophical sparring. Here, when al-Nusra crosses into rhetorical character-attack, the *Da'i* responds in the voice of a judge from the bench. The disputant who has gone outside the limits is told he has gone outside the limits. The argument resumes only when the limit has been re-established.

## The fallacy of the all-perfect world

In §15 the author of al-Nusra advanced what is perhaps his cleverest argument. If the Soul is complete because it emanates from the First Intellect, which is itself perfect — and what emanates from perfection can only be perfect — then by the same necessity what emanates from the Soul itself must also be complete: the form, and the form of the form, and every subsequent emanation, down to the indivisible singular individuals, all complete. The consequence would be that the whole world is complete, with no deficiency in it or in any of its parts. Which is absurd. Therefore al-Islah's argument breaks.

Al-Kirmani answers in a long passage that has to do real metaphysical work. The argument fails, he says, because the Soul does not emanate from the Soul, nor from individuals, as al-Nusra has claimed. The Soul is itself indivisible. Individuals are fragmented into their parts. The *al-hayyūliyyāt* (al-hay-yoo-liy-yaat; the hyulic substrates, matter-like things) are emanated *together with* the Soul that precedes them in existence — not *from* the Soul. The Soul that is second in rank does not possess primary existence; there is nothing that exists *with* it that is not above it in rank, except the First, which is the First Intellect.

Then al-Kirmani sets out the architecture of emanation that this *bāb* has been pointing toward:

> When Allah is exalted far above having any other before Him or beside Him, or being differentiated from any in attributes or relations — and He is the Almighty, the Creator of the universe, the Cause who has no cause — it is impossible, given His soundness, His holiness, and His exaltation above multiplicity, that anything should come into existence directly from Him except a single one. This one — which is the First Intellect — when it stood in two proportions, one being that it is the cause of what is below it, and the other being that it is itself an effect (since it is created and originated) — produced what came to exist as a duality in keeping with those same two proportions. From the first proportion came the Second Intellect; from the other proportion came the orbits of the heavens and the rest of the creatures of the corporeal world. Thus from the One, nothing comes into being in the universe except two — for nothing can come after the rank of the One in existence except two, nor after the singular except the pair, which is multiplicity. If the first thing in the order of foundations is one, then what follows it in the intelligible order is two — the pair, or the multitude.

The First Intellect stands in two proportions. From the proportion in which it is a *cause*, the Second Intellect comes. From the proportion in which it is an *effect*, the heavens and the corporeal world come. So the pair that follows the One is not arbitrary multiplication. It is the consequence of the very structure of the First Intellect's existence — which is both cause-of-what-is-below and effect-of-what-is-above. The Soul and the world are not two emanations stacked side by side. They are the twofold consequence of one originated being that is itself two-sided.

This is what al-Kirmani means in the famous closing sentence of the section:

> So we have two: one of which is what we call the Soul, and the other is what we call the body that contains the heavens, the planets, and what is below them.

The Soul and the corporeal world — together, jointly emanated, both consequences of the same First Intellect, but emanated from the two different *proportions* of that Intellect. This is the Ismaili Neoplatonic architecture in its purest form, set down here in plain prose. And in its setting down, the al-Nusra fallacy — that everything must therefore be perfect or nothing must — collapses, because the model al-Nusra was attacking was not the model al-Islah was building.

## The Two Weighty Things — Book and Family

§16 is one of the most important sections of the *bāb*, because it ties the metaphysical scheme to the *Da'wa*'s scriptural backbone. Al-Kirmani writes:

> When the basis of religion and its branches was set upon the scales of the foundations and their correspondences — so as to serve as an indication of their order, mapped onto the foundations of the unseen — the existence of the Book and the *shari'a* (sha-ree-ah; the divine law) came forth on the side of the Speaker-Prophet (al-Natiq), and the establishment of the Foundation (*al-Asas*) came as inspiration from his side. This was proof that all that exists proceeds from the First Intellect — who is in his world like the Speaker-Prophet in the world of the body — yet it is rather the Soul that is next, together with *al-hayyūli*, that bears witness to this, just as the Book and the Foundation, which exist on the side of the Speaker-Prophet, bear witness to it.

The scheme has now been doubled. The First Intellect *is to* the Soul as the Speaker-Prophet *is to* the Foundation. The Book is born on the Prophet's side; the Foundation is established on the Prophet's side. The doubled witness of Book-and-Foundation in the corporeal world is the icon of Soul-and-Matter in the originated world.

And then al-Kirmani brings forward the proof-text — the Hadith of the Two Weighty Things, the *Hadith al-Thaqalayn*, which is the cornerstone of Shi'i ecclesiology:

> "I leave among you the two weighty things: the Book of Allah and my 'Itrah (it-rah; my Family), a rope stretched from the sky to the earth — one end of it in the hand of Allah and one end in your hands. Hold fast to them and you will not go astray as long as you hold to them. I have asked my Lord to grant me to meet them at the Pond."
> Source: Hadith of Muhammad ﷺ (sallallahu alayhi wa sallam; peace and blessings of Allah be upon him), the Hadith al-Thaqalayn, narrated in multiple Sunni and Shi'i collections.

And the bodily image that al-Kirmani draws attention to:

> He brought his two index fingers together along with the rest of his hand and said: "Not like these two" — gesturing with his index fingers and the middle finger of his hand, where one precedes the other. "Their helper is my helper, and their forsaker is my forsaker." And he likened them in existence to his fingers.

The two index fingers — *one of them does not precede the other* — are the parallel image. The Book and the Family stand to each other as the two index fingers: jointly held forth, neither preceding the other in rank, both at the same distance from the hand that joins them. In bodily action, the Prophet has shown the parallel of what holds in the order of being. Soul and matter are jointly emanated from the First Intellect. Book and Family are jointly given to the *Umma* (um-mah; the community) from the Prophet's side. The Hadith al-Thaqalayn is, in al-Kirmani's reading, a cosmological revelation in bodily form.

He concludes the section by pointing once again to *Rahat al-'Aql* (raa-hat al-aql), where the manner in which what proceeds from the First Intellect comes to the Second is set out in full. Just as the Book and the *shari'a* do not declare themselves, and the wonders of their governance appear in and beneath them only through the *ta'wīl* (ta-weel; interpretation, drawing-out of the inner meaning) of the Foundation and the one who stands in his place — so the Soul's existence is from the First Intellect together with the Second.

This is one of the most important moves in al-Kirmani's whole theology. *Ta'wīl* is what the Foundation does in the bodily world. It is the bringing-out of the meaning that the Speaker-Prophet's Book contains but does not declare on its own surface. The Foundation is to the Book what the Soul is to the corporeal heavens: the inner principle that releases the meaning of the outer form. To read the world correctly is to read it as a structure that needs *ta'wīl*. And the rank of the one entitled to perform *ta'wīl* is built into the structure of being itself.

## The First Intellect is one — multiplicity is folded into causes

In §17 al-Kirmani returns to the question of unity. The First Intellect does not have alongside it anything of its own kind. Its existence is not extended to anything else except the existence of the First. The First is not multiple, lest the paths of monotheism be disrupted. If it had a likeness in its existence, that likeness would also have to share with it; and if their existence were simultaneous, there would be no account of their existence. The meaning of independent existence is that which is independent in its existence.

The argument has a precise structure. The existence of duality is a multiplicity of essences, and the multiplicity of essences does not subsist by itself but by the multitude of what is contained in souls. Rather, its existence is from that which precedes it, which is one — one in particular from one side, and full of meanings from the other. The one in particular, when multiplied in meanings, has its existence not in itself but in others; while that which is not multiplied is multiplied neither in meanings nor in itself.

So: if the First Intellect had alongside it something of its own kind — like two accidents borne by one preceding thing — then the existence of that one common thing would be folded back into meanings, not in itself, but in what precedes it. And since there is nothing above the Intellect, it is not possible for it to have a likeness of its existence. It is therefore established that it is *one*.

This argument matters because the rest of the *bāb*'s logic depends on the First Intellect being a singular thing. If al-Nusra's claim that the Intellect is a "first medium" required positing some other thing of the same kind alongside it, the whole architecture collapses. Al-Kirmani secures the singularity here before he proceeds.

## The world is perfect; al-Nusra is forced to admit it

§18 turns the argument back on al-Nusra in the sharpest way al-Kirmani has yet attempted. Al-Nusra's whole charge against al-Islah has rested on the claim that *originated things and Form are deficient, and all that is in the world is deficient*. Very well, says al-Kirmani — let us examine that claim. Form and the formed are perfect. The world is perfect. Its parts are themselves perfect. The proof: the perfect is that outside of which there is nothing of its kind of existence. The world is the totality of bodies. Form is the composition of the parts of body. Body in itself is perfect in its three dimensions. Nothing is needed to bound it unless that bound lies outside of it. If it is bounded, it is complete; and if it is complete, then al-Nusra is forced to concede that the world is complete and that imperfection in it is impossible.

And — al-Kirmani twists the knife — al-Nusra has *already said this himself* in another book.

> It would have sufficed him to say, in his book known as *al-Ikhtisar* (al-ikh-ti-saar; The Abridgement), in the Chapter on the Command, what already indicates the world's perfection — for there he said: "And the form of the world's governance in its entirety is nothing else, and the wisdom suffices with what is in the world's totality, being rich and perfect." I do not know how this escaped him. Perhaps he classified *Kitab al-Ikhtisar* after *Kitab al-Nusra*.

This is one of the great citational maneuvers in classical Arabic philosophical literature. Al-Kirmani has caught al-Nusra contradicting himself across his own corpus. In one book the world is deficient. In another book the world is perfect. The dry observation — *perhaps he wrote al-Ikhtisar after al-Nusra* — leaves the matter for the reader to draw the obvious conclusion.

What about the actual disparity among the parts of the world? Al-Kirmani names it correctly. The disparity is not a deficiency in the parts themselves. It is by the addition of some parts to others, with respect to their forms and conditions. Some parts of the world have been given a first perfection. Some have then been given a second first. Some have been given the second perfection — which is the final of the acquisitions. The discourse arrives, finally, at the deficiency of which al-Nusra speaks — except that this deficiency lies in the *later states* of the natural essences that appear when they are added to one another. The perfect of which al-Islah speaks is the essence whose existence is foremost.

## The cause-effect symmetry that does not hold

§19 finds al-Nusra trying yet another route. If the Soul must emanate from the Intellect (which is the perfect, and the first intermediary, and the first creator), then the Soul must itself be an intermediary like the First, and itself creative. So the Soul would attain, of the virtues, what the Intellect attains; and so would the whole world.

Al-Kirmani's reply rests on a precise principle in causation. It has already been advanced, he says, that it is impossible to call the First Intellect a "first intermediary" at all. But beyond that, the deeper logical move is this: if a thing is the cause of another thing, it does not follow that the effect must share with the cause in *all* of its conditions, such that its essence would be the cause's essence and its rights, and that nothing would be said of the effect except what is said of that particular essence. The cause-effect relation requires shared conditions in *some* respect; it does not require shared conditions in *all* respects. The effect must be like the cause in one respect and opposed to it in another. So the Soul that follows from the First Intellect must be like the Intellect in some respect and unlike it in another. It need not share all the Intellect's predicates. And so al-Nusra's reductio fails. The Soul does not have to be a "first intermediary" or "first creator" simply because it emanates from one.

## The collapse of the thought-soul analogy

§20 is one of the longest and most technical exchanges in the *bāb*. Al-Nusra has tried to introduce an analogy from human cognition: there are many analogues of the Intellect/Soul relation in observable instances, he says — Thought and the partial soul. The partial soul that is in us is like the Intellect, and Thought is like the Soul. Even though Thought issues from the soul, it may still be said to be *like* the soul, because Thought grasps what it perceives part by part and time after time, while the soul-as-totality grasps what it perceives without time.

Al-Kirmani's reply is patient and devastating. The soul of human beings, he says, has actions — and each of its actions has its own name. If it requires the realization of something, it is called *thought*. If it observes what has formed within it of the forms of information by returning to you what it has stored, it is called *memorization*. If it engraves within itself the marks it gathers — whether from the side of inference or from the side of the senses — that pattern is called *perception*. If it knows that there are things outside it that it desires to conceive, that is called *the will to incline*. And if these are its actions, then Thought is *one act of it which does not exceed its own self* — such that Thought would have an existence other than the soul's existence.

In other words: thought is not a thing distinct from the soul. Thought is *what the soul does* when it is thinking. The soul and its thought do not have two existences. They have one. So the analogy is broken from the start. The Soul (Universal Soul, Second Intellect) *does* have its own existence other than the First Intellect's. Thought does *not* have its own existence other than the soul's. The two cases are not parallel. The natural-world analogue does not analogize what it claims to.

Al-Kirmani then turns the failed analogy back on al-Nusra in a precise way. If Thought cannot stand for the Soul, because Thought has no existence apart from the soul of which it is an act, then in the originated world the Second Intellect (which is called the Soul) cannot be modeled on Thought — for if Thought from the souls of human beings does not serve as the analogue, then the Second Intellect — the second existence of origination, self-subsisting, though existing at less than the First's rank — does not have an existence of its own apart from the soul; rather, it is the very same as the soul, with no existence of its own.

And the conclusion al-Kirmani draws from this is precise. If al-Nusra's analogy holds, then his own conclusion contradicts him — he has made the Second Intellect (called the Soul) into something with no existence of its own, which is exactly the opposite of the doctrine he wanted to defend. The argument has gone in a circle and bitten its own tail.

The nobility of the human soul, al-Kirmani adds, comes only through *acquisition* — and acquisition is only from the senses as work, and from the side of perception as knowledge. To use *that* as the model for the First and Second Intellects is to derive the nobility of the prior from what is posterior to it. Which is inverted. *The nobility of the prior is itself arranged by what is behind it* — that is the natural-world rule. It does not transfer to the originated order.

## Action does not have to be for one's own benefit

§21 brings the dispute to its most theological-philosophical edge. The author of al-Nusra had argued: one of the clearest signs of the Soul's deficiency and the Intellect's perfection is that all the authors before him — including al-Islah — attributed action to the Second and never to the First. Action is always a sign of deficiency and need; the abandonment of action is evidence of perfection and self-sufficiency. Because action is only for the sake of drawing a benefit or warding off a harm. Abu Zakariyya al-Razi is then cited as having said: *the Initiator is already the Soul by way of governance and tampering*. So when the Intellect, having no rank above it, did not move to action, that was its perfection. The Soul, having a level above itself, moved and remained in its quest. So the deficiency of the Soul and the soundness of the Intellect is clear, praise be to Allah.

Al-Kirmani's reply is one of the great passages of the book. The words of al-Islah, he says, were about the *Universal Soul*, not the soul-of-humans. So al-Nusra's whole inference — that the Soul acts only to draw benefit or to ward harm — fails as soon as we recognize which Soul we are talking about. But more importantly, al-Nusra's premise itself is broken. Not every action is for drawing a benefit or warding off a harm:

> An action may proceed from an agent in such a way that what benefits is the object of the action, not the one who produced it. The evidence for this is that the heat from the fire is an act of the fire, yet this act does not return to the fire, nor does the fire gain any benefit or suffer any harm from it; rather the benefit and the harm reach whatever the heat acts upon. Likewise the action of the sun in illuminating the world: human beings are able to act by their will, and by the sun's rising and the existence of the universe, plants, animals, and minerals derive benefits — benefits that accrue to others and not to the sun. The sun does not benefit from this action, nor is it harmed by it. So too the whales that live in the sea of Oman (o-maan), and the creatures called "dolphins" which, if an ancient sailor is drowning in the sea, help him to escape it and push him toward safety: that action returns no benefit and no harm to them, rather the benefit reaches the one acted upon.

The sun. The fire. The whales of the Sea of Oman. The dolphins that rescue a drowning sailor. Each acts. None acts for itself. The benefit reaches the one acted upon. So al-Nusra's whole premise — that *action* implies *deficiency* because action is always self-interested — collapses against direct observation of the natural world.

And if even creatures *deficient in their own state* — sun, fire, dolphin — can act for others, then *how much more* can the pure abstract intellects, granted their perfections at the first stage of their existence, act for others? Their actions are *acts of wisdom*, not acts of self-benefit. They flow outward without diminishing the source.

This is the line on which Ismaili Neoplatonism rests. The First does not act in order to be perfected. The First acts because *the perfect overflows*. The world is not a transaction. The world is an overflow.

## Action becoming a subject of action — the writing and the strike

§22 and §23 are two passes at the same question, and al-Kirmani is patient with them both. Al-Nusra has insisted that actions never become the subjects of action — the writing does not become the writer, the building does not become the builder, and so the Soul's act cannot itself function as a Soul. The argument is plausible at first.

Al-Kirmani's reply has two layers. The narrower point: what al-Islah meant, when he said the action of the Soul is acted upon and not the Soul itself, is that the *second perfection* is attained from the Soul's side, from which the stream of what overflows pours upon it. The Soul passes from potency into actuality. Nothing passes from potency into actuality except by virtue of something that already exists. So when al-Islah says the action is what changes, he is naming the locus of the change — what passes from potency into act is the *act*, not the *essence* of the Soul. The al-Nusra objection has not landed.

The broader point — and this is §23 — al-Nusra's own counter-examples actually prove the opposite of what he intended. *Writing* is the act of the writer, but the writing is itself what addresses its reader, what speaks to him, what carries the writer's meaning across to the one who reads it. If the writing in its forms and arrangements were not effective upon its reader, it would not be as though it were speaking about its subject. So the writing — the act — is itself an actor in some respect. Likewise the *strike*: the act of striking is what produces the hurt, and the hurt afflicts the one struck. And al-Kirmani quotes the Quran to seal the point:

> "There is nothing that does not glorify Him in praise."
> Source: Quran 17:44.

The glorification is the act of the Almighty, and although it is a verb, it is what *glorifies* and *praises* Him. The act and the agent of the act are folded into each other in the Quranic statement. So al-Nusra's claim that *actions never become subjects of action* is not absolutely true. In the world of sense, writings address readers; strikes hurt the struck; glorification glorifies. The act, in many cases, is itself the actor at a derived level.

Al-Kirmani concedes the narrow grammatical point — the writing did not insist on being its own writer, nor what is built become the very builder — but rejects the metaphysical conclusion. The act is not identical with the one from which it issued, but it functions as an actor in respect of the existence of the action with regard to its object. And so al-Nusra has, here too, been defending his teachers by *prejudice* against the author of al-Islah — and there are many confusions, al-Kirmani says, that need to be unraveled, layer upon layer.

## The sperm is not the image of the man

§24 takes up one more of al-Nusra's analogies. He had likened the Intellect and the Soul, in their perfection and imperfection, to the man and the sperm: the sperm is the image of the man in potency, on the truth, and it becomes a complete man like the man; similarly, the Soul is the image of the Intellect, on the truth, and it becomes perfect like the Intellect.

Al-Kirmani replies that this is wrong twice over. The sperm is *not* the image of the man:

> The sperm is matter prepared for being given form, free of images, so that images may be acquired in it in stages. Likewise the soul is a natural preparation, devoid of images, which would acquire the images of knowledge, so that the soul becomes self-fulfilling through that. And even if the soul were an image of the intellect, on the truth, then the intellect would have to possess an image of what it itself is, so that they would be one thing; and if the picture has an image, it is something else — and how can it be true? Whatever its existence, it is a creative existence, and otherness in it does not exist except by addition, while the essence is one.

This is the precise statement that *kind of talk and analogy applies only to what is in the natural realm*. The sperm-to-man analogy belongs to the world of bodies, where matter receives forms over time. The Intellect-Soul relation is not of that kind. The Intellect is not a hidden potency that grows into the Soul. The Soul is not the realized form of an Intellect-in-seed. The two are *one creative existence* with one essence; what looks like otherness between them is the otherness of *addition* — that is, of the proportions in which the First Intellect stands as cause-of-what-is-below and effect-of-what-is-above.

To carry the seed-to-man analogy into the originative order is to picture the originative order as if it were biology. It is not. The error is the same as it has been on every page of this *bāb*: a natural-world relation imported into a context to which it does not apply.

## Speaker and Foundation — the cosmological pair on Earth

§25 returns the dispute to the level of Ismaili sacred history. The author of al-Nusra has said: the status of the *Speaker* (al-Natiq) in the corporeal world is like the status of the Intellect in the world of the spiritual; and the status of the *Foundation* (al-Asas) is like the status of the consequent (the Soul). So as it may be said that the Soul is as perfect as the Intellect, it may be said that the Foundation is as perfect as the Speaker. And this is not correct, because the rank of the Foundation is below the rank of the Speaker.

Al-Kirmani's reply consolidates everything the *bāb* has been working toward. *The second is like the first, even if it is in a lower rank, and the Foundation is like the Speaker, even if he is in a lower rank.* That second, in its existence, has nothing left for it in the way of any degree it will attain later. Just as the Foundation is perfect in his rank, no degree remains for him to rise to and acquire. He is not, in himself, incomplete.

And al-Kirmani points to a striking liturgical proof. The Prophet — peace and blessings of Allah be upon him — distinguished the slaughter on camels from the sacrifice on others, because slaughter is the delivery of the iron blade to the head of the *fawād* (fa-waad; the throat-spot for the killing cut), from the wound, so that no blood is spilled which would be distorted upon the degree of the report; while the sacrifice is based on taking the covenant and the release in the call, and the slaughter is based on the next. So the Prophet performed actions that informed the *Umma* that follows him, on the one hand; and the wisdom of his role is that the Foundation, in his place in supplication, did not establish the inner call in his own right, but only after the Speaker had instructed him in what is in it of perfection. So the highest degree is in the Speaker's teaching, and his path is in attaining perfection and completeness — not like any other in his *Umma* from whom the Foundation also obtains communication and completeness.

The Prophet also showed this in the *two rak'ahs* (ra-kahs; cycles of the prescribed prayer) of the dawn prayer. In the first *rak'ah* it is obligatory to recite the praise. In the second *rak'ah*, it is obligatory to recite *al-Qunūt* (al-qu-noot; the supplication in the dawn prayer) in place of the praise that is in the first *rak'ah* — *so as to be in the same state as the first from the standpoint of perfection*. Two prayer cycles, *each complete*, distinct only in rank in existence — the first like the first of existence, the second like the second of existence. Yet both equally perfect in themselves.

This is one of the most beautiful applications in the whole book. The structure of the dawn prayer turns out to be the structure of being. Two *rak'ahs*, each *tamm* in itself, distinguished by rank — first and second — but each carrying the same dignity of perfection. The bodily ritual the believer performs every morning is the icon of the metaphysics the *Da'i* is defending.

## The Foundation's perfection in the Speaker's presence

§26 brings this strand to its final formulation. *If it is permissible to say that the Soul is as perfect as the Intellect, then it is permissible to say that the Foundation is like the Speaker.* And how could it not be? The Foundation is the one who stands in the place of the Prophet, peace be upon him, and his family. Even if he falls short of standing in the Prophet's place in some respect, or is not as perfect as the Prophet is — when Allah obliges obedience to him, how does Allah Almighty render obedience binding to one who is, like others, deficient in either of these two matters? It is not permissible, in wisdom, that Allah should command the *Umma* to obey one who is essence-deficient. The Foundation is in the same position as the Speaker, ranked below him in degree but not in *essence*.

The argument has a doctrinal force the philosophical apparatus alone could not generate. Obedience to the Foundation is commanded by God. God does not command obedience to the deficient. Therefore the Foundation is not deficient. Therefore the Foundation is *tamm* in himself, *less than the Speaker* by rank. The same architecture al-Kirmani has been defending for the Soul holds for the Foundation. The two are isomorphic. The doctrine of the *Da'wa* and the metaphysics of the *Da'wa* witness each other.

He closes the section with the verdict that has been the verdict of every section so far:

> From what we have said, it is clear that the position of the author of al-Islah is the one that should be accepted against the opposition of the author of al-Nusra, who said that the second is incomplete; even on the view of the author of al-Nusra, what should be said about it is what has been stated.

## Sayings of the family

The same architecture of essence-completeness and ranked-lessness is summarized, in the broader Shi'i wisdom tradition, in a saying often paraphrased from Imam Ali ibn Abi Talib (a-lee ibn a-bee taa-lib; the Commander of the Believers, peace be upon him):

> The lesser does not lose its dignity by being lesser. It loses its dignity only by reaching for a rank that is not its own.
> Source: paraphrased after the manner of Imam Ali's aphorisms in *Nahj al-Balagha* (the Path of Eloquence).

Each thing knows its rank. The Soul does not become deficient by being the Soul rather than the Intellect. The Foundation does not become deficient by being the Foundation rather than the Speaker. The second *rak'ah* does not become deficient by following the first. The lesser is dignified by being what it is. It would only lose its dignity by claiming to be what it is not.

## Where the disputants erred — the pattern named

By the end of §26 al-Kirmani has, in effect, made his diagnosis of where each disputant went wrong. The author of al-Islah erred in his framing — in his choice of language about *kamāl* and *tamm*, in his claim that intellect and time are one, in some of his analogies. But his core claim, properly construed, stands.

The author of al-Nusra erred in a different way. His arguments were not *bad* arguments. They were *displaced* arguments. He kept carrying the relations of the natural world — carrier and carried, form and matter, sun and moon, sperm and man, action and the seeking of benefit — into the originative-emanative order, where those relations either invert or do not apply. His most powerful charges against al-Islah are charges that hold true *in the world of bodies and accidents* and that fail *in the world of origination and emanation*. The error is one of *domain*. And al-Kirmani's whole judicial method has been the relentless naming of the domain in which each claim was true and the domain in which it was false.

The third part of the *bāb* — the final twelve sections, which will form the third episode of this series — turns from the dialectical critique of the disputants to the affirmative statement of the Soul's status as Second Emanation. We have seen *where the disputants erred*. We will see, in what follows, *what the Soul actually is*.
