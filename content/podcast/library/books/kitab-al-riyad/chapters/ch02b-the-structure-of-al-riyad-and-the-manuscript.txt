# The Structure of al-Riyad and the Manuscript

*This chapter covers Aref Tamer's chapter-by-chapter walk through* Kitab al-Riyad *itself: al-Kirmani's opening statement of method, the ten substantive chapters in which he adjudicates between* al-Islah *and* al-Nusra *(perfection of the soul; the relation of Intellect and Soul; soul and spirit; souls as parts of the First Truths; man as the fruit of the material world; motion, stillness, and image; the sections of the world;* qada *and* qadar*; the law of Adam and the great resurrection; and the nine-point critique of* al-Mahsul*), and finally the editorial history of the two Indian manuscripts on which the modern critical edition rests.*

## Al-Kirmani's opening and stated method

*Al-Kirmani* opens his book *al-Riyad* by praising and glorifying God — the customary opening of all scholars in those eras — and then points to the Imam of his ruling time, ruling by the command of Allah, of the Fatimids. He then states the situation he is addressing:

> I saw that *Abu Hatim al-Razi* had taken up the errors in the book *al-Mahsul* — *The Harvest*. Then *Abu Ya'qub al-Sijistani* came and defended the author of *al-Mahsul*, insisting on the soundness of his argument and the strength of his theory, and that the matters he discussed were not Ismaili philosophical side-issues or branches that could be passed over — for to pass them over would be an affront to the creed and a disturbance of the basic principles and foundations.

*Al-Kirmani* sees the trouble clearly. *Abu Ya'qub* backed certain texts against *Abu Hatim al-Razi*, attacking him, and in some places speaking in ways that bore no relation to the topic at all. Worse, certain passages in *al-Mahsul* dealing with monotheism had made it obligatory for *Abu Hatim* to correct and discuss them, and he did not. *Abu Hatim* preferred instead to indulge in long stories on subjects of lesser importance — the historical material contained in the Torah — with which he filled his book, leaving *al-Mahsul* uncorrected and neglected on the points that mattered. He thereby produced a kind of intellectual confusion among the followers of the quiet *da'wa* by setting different and divergent opinions before them, especially once they had taken to reading *al-Mahsul*. This had an effect on the purity of the idea of the paths of monotheism, and it led to the corruption of the principles of *Shari'a* and of the arrangement of the *hudud*.

*Al-Kirmani's* method follows from the diagnosis:

> For this reason I decided to quote here the original statements contained in *al-Islah* that correct some of what is mentioned in *al-Mahsul*, along with what has been mentioned in the statements of *al-Nusra* that support *al-Mahsul*, presenting my own opinion and comments. My aim is to display the principles in their correct sense and to separate the two positions; once we have reached this point it becomes easy to expose the error, so that the correct teachings remain free of doubt and ambiguity, and far removed from every disparity and confusion.

*Al-Kirmani* divided *al-Riyad* into ten chapters, each subdivided into several sections. With his literary and scientific deftness, the strength of his exposition, and his learning, he selected what he saw as important and fundamental from *al-Islah* and *al-Nusra* and recast it in his own style — clear and concise — a kind of artistry totally different from *al-Razi's* style.

What follows is a chapter-by-chapter overview of what *al-Riyad* actually contains.

## Chapter 1: the perfection of the soul

The first chapter of *al-Riyad* is divided into thirty-eight sections, devoted to the subject of the perfection of the soul. The author of *al-Islah* says: the soul is perfect, a perfect source issuing from perfection itself, because the Intellect is perfection. The author of *al-Nusra* contradicts this, saying that the author of *al-Islah* does not realize that "the perfect" is higher and more complete than "perfection," because the second — perfection — is an attribute of the first and is therefore predicated of it. The perfect, which bears this attribute, is therefore higher, and the attribute attached to it is named after it; the perfect exists, and when perfection exists it becomes the follower, not the followed.

Here the debate between truth and fancy sharpens, and *al-Kirmani* lifts the cover from the logical error. The author of *al-Islah* did not mean perfection in general, but the highest perfection — the Intellect — which is the cause of the existence of the perfect soul that has come to be characterized by it. The effect cannot stand higher than the cause. *Al-Kirmani's* view thus leads us to hold that *al-Sijistani's* attack on *al-Razi* was out of place and incorrect.

*Al-Kirmani* then takes up *al-Razi's* opinion. *Al-Razi* says: although the soul is perfect, its actions are not, since these actions depend on time, whereas the soul is the emanation of the Intellect, and the Intellect is a being composed in itself of origination, time, and perfection — unlike the soul, which is emanated within time and depends on the supply that comes from the Intellect. This dependence does not impair the soul's substance, nor render it base, just as the Intellect's dependence on the divine will does not make it inferior. The substance is not the inferior of matter on account of this perfection or imperfection, which are born of the soul and are not its substance.

What is mentioned in the first chapter of *al-Riyad* — both quotations are drawn from *al-Islah* — *al-Kirmani* strongly criticizes the opinions of *al-Nusra*, holding that *al-Sijistani* misunderstood these theories. He likens him to a newcomer who entered the world of the ideals with no goal but material vanity.

The author of *al-Nusra* cited the saying of Empedocles in protest: "The Intellect became as it is only because it was not subject to anything but the Almighty; the soul became deficient because it has been subject to nature." We say: the philosophers who preceded them, even if they were virtuous in their time, remained the authority of error in many of the matters they spoke of from their own minds. Had they been in our time, and had they seen the springs of grace and what overflows from the House of Revelation with the facts of things as they are — namely the 'Alawi *da'wa* — they would have used their diligence to do themselves justice, and would have expressed many of their beliefs in the absence of the senses. They would have been among those for whom Allah set a light in the mysteries of things. In their own time, however, they followed their own opinions and their own minds, each going in his belief in the direction his reason pointed him.

(Empedocles, the great philosopher who lived around 440 BC and was a contemporary of Parmenides though younger than him, is described in the editor's footnote in some detail. His doctrine is connected with that of Heraclitus with a slight modification. He was from the city of Akragas on the southern coast of Sicily, a democratic politician who claimed himself to be a god. Legends recount that he performed miracles — sometimes by magic, sometimes by his scientific knowledge. He held that air is a self-contained element, a claim he supported with solid scientific evidence; he recognized that plants reproduce sexually; he held a doctrine of evolution and survival of the fittest; in astronomy he said that the moon is illuminated by rays reflected onto it from the sun. He held that earth, air, fire, and water are the four elements, that the material world is spherical in shape, and he upheld some Pythagorean views and refused to adopt monotheism. He held that the course of nature is governed in its system by chance and necessity more than by any directed end, and in these respects his philosophy is closer to science than the philosophies of Parmenides, Plato, and Aristotle.)

Empedocles and the rest stand to the *al-Riyad's* argument as the *al-Zahir* school stands inside the Hanafi sect — relying on their own minds in walking the paths of religion. Their opinions diverged, and among them there was a distinction: some closer to right, others closer to wrong. They relied on what the balance of religion testifies to — the horizons and the souls. In light of *al-Kirmani's* discussion, the matter of belief in Empedocles's protest is shown to be false, free of exaggeration and prolixity. *Al-Kirmani* closes the chapter: "We ask God to enlighten our minds and to close our affairs and our acts of goodness with happiness, and to aid us through His kindness and generosity in good works."

## Chapter 2: the relation between Intellect and Soul

The second chapter of *al-Riyad* is divided into nine sections and is devoted to the discussion of the relationship between the Intellect and the Soul. *Al-Sijistani* objects to *al-Razi* because *al-Razi* raised the topic of motion, repose, and stillness — the central topic that so often caused difficulty for the ancient philosophers.

Here motion and stillness are taken as two effects united in the First and the Second. This supports what *Abu Hatim al-Razi* said: that the First was made originative and has become attached to and responsible for origination, since the truth of His station is a duality (the originator and the originated), and these two effects are present in the First. When the Second was brought forth from the First, the bringing-forth itself became dualized (the same emanation and the same emanated), and so the two effects are linked to the two basic principles. Motion is the *fayd* — the benefaction — that the First releases upon the Second and which acts upon all assets and entities. Stillness is the acceptance of the First's command throughout all the basic entities.

*Al-Razi* says: "I do not say that motion and stillness are generated from the soul by a power received from the Intellect; rather, I maintain that both are two effects of the soul, generated by the power produced by the Intellect within the form and the image. For this reason, form and image have become the basis of this world."

*Al-Sijistani*, in *al-Nusra*, calls this argument delirious. He holds that motion is generated from a specific intention — "to seek something" — and the motion of physical bodies is intended either to fill the void above through the upward movement of flame, or through the downward movement of earth, and so on. From this, *al-Kirmani* concludes that *al-Razi* was correct, while *al-Sijistani* was wrong.

The author of *al-Islah* gives the fullest version of the position: "We hold that motion and stillness are two effects united in the First and the Second. For when the First was creative, it united with creativity, and for that reason the union was affected by power. We called them two effects because creativity and the creative are two: one is the creativity itself and the other is the creator. Yet creativity and the creator, though two, are a single essence, namely the All. These two effects belong first to the First by power, and then are emitted from the First to the Second. Through that emission, two actual effects arose in the Second: one is identical with the resurrection, the other identical with the origin. The two origins are united in matter, and one of the two effects, motion, is higher than the other, stillness, since it is the higher of the two originals — just as the First is higher than the Second."

## Chapter 3: are the soul and the spirit one?

The third chapter is divided into six sub-chapters and opens with phrases drawn from *al-Nusra*, set among the texts cited from *al-Islah*, arranged in a way that clarifies their original meaning. *Al-Kirmani* says: *al-Sijistani* criticizes *al-Razi* for something *al-Razi* never said.

The author of *al-Islah*, in reply to what the author of *al-Nusra* had advanced, said: "We do not say that nature and the dark skeletons are born from the Second in order to confirm that the Second derives from the First. That higher world is the world of honor and virtue, while this world is the world of darkness and gloom. The Second, though born of the First, resembles Him, and is unlike the case of *al-Hayuli*." The author of *al-Nusra* replies that this is a pure mistake, for if that world were illuminated, there would be no darkness in it, and this world is dark with no light in it, so no resemblance could fall between them.

## Chapter 4: are souls parts of the First Truths?

The fourth chapter, in eight sub-chapters, treats the question of whether souls are parts of the First Truths. *Al-Kirmani* notes that *al-Sijistani* alters the meaning of what is stated in *al-Islah* by rearranging its phrases. *Al-Kirmani* agrees with him on the subject of partial souls, granting that the aggregate of these souls does not constitute the universal soul.

*Al-Sijistani* then returns to speak about this world, alluding to the complete Truths, and says that the human soul belongs to the region between the world of nature and the world of rebirth; it is the compound, known by natural force, and the soul may be raised to the highest world by practicing piety and then by uniting with the Second. *Al-Sijistani* does not stop there. He says: "The Intellect is the First Resurrection, while the speaking souls are the Second Resurrection. Whatever occurred in the first rebirth is resurrected in the second as well."

*Al-Kirmani* criticizes this opinion and refers the reader to his own writings, especially to the epistles *al-Wa'iz* — *The Preacher* — and *al-Risala al-Mudi'a* — *The Illuminating* — in which he says: "The Intellect is nothing but the Word itself, and the Word is nothing but the Intellect itself." He confirms this by citing the saying of Imam *al-Mu'izz li-Din Allah* (may God's prayers be upon him) in *Ta'wil al-Shari'a* — *Interpretation of the Shari'a* — where he speaks of the intellects and indicates that the Word is the Intellect and that these names are properly applied to it. *Al-Kirmani* adds: "We have already explained and discussed the Creator, the Intellect, the Word, and what may be said of the world of creativity and the First Rebirth in *al-Risala al-Mudi'a* and others. These treatises suffice the researcher."

*Al-Kirmani* does not stop here. He cites the words of *al-Razi* as recorded in *al-Islah*: "Motion and stillness are to the soul as the body and the image are to it; and these last two are like the body of the first two, because the soul is gentler than the body, and the body is denser than the soul. So motion and stillness are gentler than the substance and the image."

*Al-Sijistani* rejects this analysis, supporting instead the theory of the author of *al-Mahsul*. *Al-Kirmani* says that both men erred, especially *al-Razi*, regarding motion. Motion is the action of the mover, which we call the soul. Stillness is the cessation of the mover's action. Therefore, if the mover is the soul, it cannot resemble motion, which is its own action; the same applies to stillness. Moreover, motion and stillness cannot be combined into a single thing, because they alternate as opposites, while the structure and the image are inseparable on the mixed plane and form one thing. This topic has been fully explained in *Rahat al-'Aql* — *The Repose of the Intellect*.

## Chapter 5: the human as the fruit of the material world

The fifth chapter, in seven sub-chapters, treats the theme that the human being is the fruit of the material world. *Al-Razi* says so, and "we see him as belonging to the whole world, its foundation, and its roots." *Al-Hayuli* is also the basis of the substrate formed in it by motion, stillness, and certain other factors that lead to the formation of different souls — such as the region and others.

*Al-Sijistani* calls this philosophy the "world philosophy," which does not acknowledge the existence of the soul after its separation from the body. The Orientalist Goldziher, in the Islamic Encyclopedia, gives a long explanation of these beliefs of this category, but without giving a fully decisive answer. It is held that this group follows the school of Democritus and Epicurus, as further pursued by Lucretius.

*Al-Kirmani* says that both schools are right on one side and wrong on another. Man is the result of the interaction of natural-planetary and physical action, and man is the purpose of the Second and the purpose of the work of nature. The meaning is this: man is created and divided into two parts — one part belonging to lifeless nature, and another part belonging to God — and therefore he is the highest of creatures.

But how can what *al-Sijistani* has said be true, when order and arrangement come only from the Intellect? Or how is it correct that universals are made preferable only by the Intellect, while the soul is unaffected by its effects, being on a par with the animals? It is for this reason that God has lifted the pen from the soul until the effects of the Intellect take hold in it. As *al-Kirmani* says, the matter lies in the agents, not in totalities; not every agent seeks, through its action, to achieve a goal or purpose decreed for it. By the same token, the First Intellect itself is not a compound, so it is in order and arrangement, but the Intellect only perceives true perception.

## Chapter 6: motion, stillness, and the image

The sixth chapter, in six sub-chapters, rehearses the theme of motion, stillness, and the image. It maintains that what is reported in *al-Mahsul*, and what *Abu Hatim al-Razi* and *al-Sijistani* all transmitted on this score, is incorrect. *Al-Kirmani* refers the reader to consult his *al-Masabih* — *The Lamps* — for the full treatment.

The author of *al-Nusra* proceeds to define body and soul. He defines body by its acceptance of the three dimensions, and then defines the soul as the similarity of the parts, making the soul of the same course as water, fire, and air — each of which is similar in its parts. With this premise, it becomes impossible to say that the soul is not embodied. The soul (*al-nafs*) and the spirit (*al-ruh*) are synonymous names of one thing, and that thing is not embodied. Were it a body, it would necessarily possess the nine qualities and accidents that the body requires, and could be apprehended by a sense; nor would the imagination remain in it as to the nine accidents, or one of them. And when different physical norms hold in it, it must not be a body. Our well-known *al-Masabih* contains what fully addresses this.

## Chapter 7: the sections of the world

The seventh chapter, divided into seven sub-chapters, deals with the classification of the composite materials of the world. In truth it is dedicated entirely to explaining the status of the human being, who forms a class of his own, and his relationship to all else. For this reason *al-Razi* says: "Man is a plant that sleeps upon the substances of the world." *Al-Sijistani* agrees with him on one side, but disagrees on another: the human body disintegrates or dissolves into such substances, and *al-Razi* classes the human being and likens him to an animal because he possesses the senses and attributes. *Al-Sijistani* replies that this feeling belongs to the growing vegetative soul. *Al-Razi* argues that the very speaking human composes a point of difference from the animal, while *al-Sijistani* counters that the animal too possesses the senses. When *al-Kirmani* weighs the two opinions, he rules that *al-Razi* is closer to the truth than *al-Sijistani*.

Finally, *al-Kirmani* says: "The resemblance is not only between man and the material world, but also between man and the spiritual world. The one who is united in himself is such that everything preceded him in the scale of existence, and he displays the analogies between himself and everything in the material and spiritual worlds."

In the world there are different motions, and in the human being there are also six motions corresponding to the six. In the world there is a mover that moves it, and in the human being likewise there is a mover that moves him — namely the soul. In the world there are the four investigations (the elements), and in the human being likewise there are the four natures. In the world there are rivers, valleys of different tastes, mountains, and minerals, and in the human being likewise — as the author of *al-Islah* has shown — there are bones, cartilage, flesh, blood, and what flows from him and issues out of him. In the world there are things that grow and increase without sensation, and in the human being likewise there are things that grow and increase without sensation, such as hair and nails; and his body itself grows and increases from his youth to his old age. In the world there is the sentient animal as well, and in the human being there are the sentient faculties. The whole world is structures and compositions; it is complete in what exists. From all this comes the analogy with the world — though what *al-Kirmani* has mentioned is only a summary, which his other writings detail under the title "Repose of the Mind."

## Chapter 8: qada and qadar

The eighth chapter is divided into twenty-four sub-chapters, and its subject is *qada* (judgment) and *qadar* (destiny). These two expressions are mentioned in the Holy *Qur'an* and evidence specific synonyms, but both mean judgment. This topic has had its impact on the long scientific debate that took place in the Islamic era and never reached a decisive result. Thousands of works in Islamic theology have been written on it, but in fact it is a wrong and time-wasting matter to revisit.

*Al-Razi*, the author of *al-Islah*, says: "In view of the statement of *al-Mahsul* that *qada* (judgment) agrees with the First and the Preceding, and *qadar* (destiny) with the Second, this is all wrong, because destiny precedes judgment just as the First precedes the Second. The meaning of *qadar* is estimation — that He made the thing possible — and the meaning of *qada* is detail, the separation of the thing or its parts. And the Second, i.e., the manifest *qada*, cannot be presented over the First." *Al-Razi* cites several verses from the *Qur'an* in support of his interpretation and likens destiny, in detail, to the cloth that is drawn in the tailor's imagination before and after, before being cut. When he has put it into its form — that is, judges it — it is no longer possible to change it.

*Al-Sijistani* objects to this explanation in defense of his basic theory, which agrees with *al-Mahsul*. He says that *qada* does not mean detail, although *qadar* means estimation; the first describes and obliges, while the second is the judgment, a void and an end. He adds that the tailor analogy does not fit the case, because *qada* is the idea of the dress that results from the art of tailoring, which is sewing, and the Second is based on the soul as the estimation, and so it became destiny.

Then *al-Kirmani* comes and discusses the ideas and opinions. He says: "If *qada* agrees with the First, and the First with the Preceding, and the Following with the Second, then we must admit that the Speaker is suited to the Following, as shown in the book. Although the Speaker is responsible for defining the *Shari'a*, it is wrong to combine the Speaker and the Following, because the Speaker is concerned with the material world, while the Following — which is the Foundation — is concerned with the spiritual world. The likeness of the Speaker and the Foundation is like the sun and the moon." And here there is a point worth attention: some preachers tended to set the rank of the Foundation above the rank of the Speaker, saying that spiritual worship is higher than legal-natural worship.

The author of *al-Nusra* reports a story from the scholars: When God Almighty created the Intellect, He emptied the two worlds into it, so that it might be the sum of the images of the two worlds. *Al-Kirmani* responds that the Intellect that is the sum of the images of the two worlds is not the First Creative Intellect, nor the Second Intellect, which is the First Emanator; rather, it is the Intellect that ascends from the world of nature by acquisition. In order to parallel the First Emission, a Second Emission was emerging, and thus it became a collection of the images of the worlds, despite their multitude, and it can imprint itself with the images of created things.

## Chapter 9: the law of Adam and the great resurrection

The ninth chapter is divided into thirty-three sub-chapters by *al-Kirmani*. It treats the basis of the principle and the law of Adam, and the laws that came after him, and then proceeds further. In this connection *al-Kirmani* speaks of the Great Resurrection, saying: The Great Resurrection will come when the doors of teaching are closed, the call that has been perfected ceases, and the *Qa'im* sets aside the *hudud* in the administration of the *da'wa*. Then it is no longer necessary, wherever a new creation has come.

There are also sub-chapters devoted to the question whether Adam, mentioned in the *Qur'an* as one of God's interlocutors conveying revelation to humanity and the Speaker — in Ismaili idiom, the *natiq* who holds the role — has truly preached the law of God. To whom is it given, and how? In this chapter we find no attachment to purely philosophical conjecture — only an effort to collect and explain the few symbols and signs scattered through the Scriptures, and to combine and reconcile those often contradictory references into a convincing theory.

The Christians turned the Adam of the Old Testament — the simple man named in the Torah of the Bible — into a towering figure unlike any other. Islam, alongside a great variety of other views, has inherited this new story of Adam; and of the various groups, the Ismailis came closer to the truth than the rest in defining his role, because they considered him to stand at the very beginning of the world — it being enough that the angels were commanded to prostrate to him in honor. Within the Ismaili system it is acknowledged that the seven *natiqs* — the speaker-prophets — are the first and not the last, so Adam, the first, and the others who follow differ only slightly in their work and their duties: Adam did not bring the closing decree, and the *Qa'im* will not come with a new law — which is why Muhammad (peace and blessings be upon him) is taken as the seal of the apostles, and his law as the last.

*Al-Kirmani* mentions the Great Resurrection because at that time the call (*da'wa*) will have been completed, the activity of the *hudud* (the ranks of the call) will have ceased, the task of education will have ended, and a new creation will have begun. This idea seems to reverse what is expected from the Ismaili call when it reaches its summit — when the whole world becomes united under their authority and follows a single religious doctrine, namely the true Islamic law that they preach.

The author of *al-Nusra*, after his interpretation of God's words in the story of the son of Noah — "I will take refuge on a mountain that will protect me from the water" — goes on to say: "I will seek refuge in him." Then Noah replies: "There is no protector today from the command of God" — meaning that the matter is now received only through the revelation given through those who bear it, for Noah's son did not know the rank of revelation, even if it was a subordinate rank. Adam already had a revelation for his son so that he might know this.

## Chapter 10: the critique of al-Mahsul

The tenth and last chapter is a critique of all the passages cited from *al-Mahsul* — *The Harvest* — which *Abu Hatim al-Razi* left uncorrected before. There are roughly nine passages and fifteen chapters in this section. It is necessary to provide a translation of the basic passages contained in *al-Mahsul* to give an idea of its style. *Al-Kirmani's* response in the second section is presented as an example of his method. He says:

> In truth, when the author of *al-Mahsul* undertook the propagation of the call and began explaining the foundations and rules of the *da'wa*, he opened the doors of new departures by writing a number of books for those within it; and the majority accepted his judgment on *al-Mahsul* despite the apology of the author of *al-Islah*. This came as proof of the error and complexity of the subject — and unfortunately the reality of the situation is the opposite of what they saw and imagined. *Al-Razi's* corrections demonstrate that he did not challenge the value of *al-Mahsul* or its author, neither by exposing faults nor by issuing judgments, because *al-Razi* was among the men particularly concerned with repairing and elevating religious knowledge, explaining the truth of the faith, and affirming the duties of man toward God.

From the opinions of *al-Kirmani* recorded in his books, one judges him to be a great author and a writer trained in the manners and the principles. One of his epistles is *al-Mabasim* — *The Preacher* — and from it we see that he could make an important note and set it down with restraint, where another man would have sent insults and abuse. Here are some of the pieces he corrects.

**First.** The author of *al-Islah* says, on the subject of monotheism, that God is the Creator of things and other-than-things: of the intelligible, the intellectual, and the rational — meaning that such materials or topics may fall under the rule of such elements or may not. *Al-Kirmani* replies: this contradicts the theory that reason — *al-'aql* — is the foundation of creation, behind which there is no other deity, and that nothing can be prior to the Intellect in its existence or alongside the Intellect in its existence. For this theory entails *shirk*, association; it contradicts the law of monotheism. And to speak of creating things and other-than-things, material and non-material, unless one has confused himself and imagined illusions that there is no world other than this one and nothing that negates creation — how then is it possible to say that God is the creative Creator and at the same time deny this?

**Second.** In the fourth chapter, the author of *al-Mahsul* says: "The Creator made things from nothing alone; there is nothing with Him and He has no end; and if we said only 'He and nothing,' we would have denied things and other-than-things, and made both ourselves and Him creative. We have said that His Self, His identity, whatever idea it may be, is a simple vehicle, and that He made everything continuous and non-continuous, bringing it into this existence by virtue of the infinite and finite effect — because nothing comes after 'the thing,' and because the negation of a name is itself a name that comes into being once that name exists, and because the word 'no' is not an idea but is itself one of the ideas, a negation of every idea." This is partly *shirk*: he has refuted the theory of monotheism. *Al-Kirmani* refers the student to his own thesis *al-Rawda* and to *Ta'wil al-Shari'a* by Imam *al-Mu'izz li-Din Allah*.

**Third.** In the eighth chapter, the author of *al-Mahsul* says, in the section investigating the First Creator, that God Almighty created the world all at once — meaning that He created the Intellect with a single utterance, and by His power and design displayed images within it of existence, the ideas of the world, and everything it contains, without anything else having priority over it; the Intellect has seniority. *Al-Kirmani* protests against this statement because it implies and decides that the Intellect operates independently, relying on its own thinking — and this is a grave error, because the Intellect is only a mediator in the divine work and not another creator.

**Fourth.** The author of *al-Mahsul* says, in the ninth chapter: "Eternity and its causality are the unity of God; and the unity of God is eternal." *Al-Kirmani* refuses the idea of "eternal unity," regarding it instead as a mediator between God and reason — the second being itself the substance of unity for one primary cause, and at the same time being the first cause of the act of creating and the creature, the perfect and the perfecting, the eternal and the everlasting, the existing and what exists.

**Fifth.** In chapter eleven the author of *al-Mahsul* says: "The image is visible in the Intellect by way of the unity of God, so the abstract image becomes eternal in it, because the Intellect itself becomes wholly eternal and not partial." *Al-Kirmani* tends to speak about the basic facts even while thinking about the manifestations of the physical world, and suggests that this applies more to the speaker's mind — to the base or the forward — than to the underlying matter. This is the Nearest Angel, or the Hidden Pen that writes upon the Tablet of existence.

**Sixth.** The author of *al-Mahsul* says, in chapter twelve: "Since the Intellect is caused by the eternity which is called 'the Word of God,' and since there is no third between them, the Intellect becomes similar to it." *Al-Kirmani* objects, saying that the Intellect and the Word are one and the same.

**Seventh.** In the thirteenth chapter the author of *al-Mahsul* says that the Intellect received the name *Tam* — the Complete — because it became the creator, and because creativity was done by the perfect Creator, and that the creator is thereby perfect, which is itself created — meaning that "creativity" can only be complete. *Al-Kirmani* objects: God has no attributes whereby the creator or createdness imposes its superiority over the creature.

**Eighth.** In the fourteenth chapter the author of *al-Mahsul* says: "The Intellect carries the ideas, and the source from which it draws is the Word exactly — like the sun, which sends out light by its own brilliance and not by any natural friction or material cause. If the work of the Intellect is the result of the power of the Word and of the Word's ability to convey it, then it becomes clear that the Word is the cause of everything that proceeds from the Intellect, and not the Intellect itself; and the second — which is the Intellect — is only an intermediary between the Word and the *hudud* beneath it." *Al-Kirmani* objects: such an arrangement applies only to the minds of the speakers.

**Ninth.** Finally, in the fifteenth chapter the author of *al-Mahsul* says: "The act of creativity is a medium between the Creator and the created, and the same is true of the agent who is entrusted with it. The idea of creativity depends on the agent who influences the image, which is transmitted from the creature to the Creator." The closing section contains scattered sentences that refer to the same topic drawn from different clues. As one would expect, *al-Kirmani* rejects these statements: "This does not agree with the belief of the monotheists, because the act of the creature here is taken to be independent of the Creator."

*Al-Kirmani's* refrain across these nine corrections is the same one: the idea of monotheism and the understanding of the knowledge of the *hudud* is hard, indeed very hard. It is by reaching this knowledge that souls survive, grow, and find stability, and that they endure beyond the world of matter. The author of *al-Mahsul* repeats his closing claim: "Creativity is a mediator between the Creator and the created; it is an effect of the influencer upon the influenced. The image of creativity is intermediate between the doer and the effect, its existence proceeding from the side of the creative doer. Therefore the effect of the image is on the side of the creative in the effect, and this image — I mean creativity — has come into the Creative One from the Creative Almighty." Even this restatement does not save it: it still hands the creature an independence of action that monotheism cannot allow.

This is the book *al-Riyad* of the great preacher *Hamid al-Din Ahmad al-Kirmani* — the sum of the philosophical opinions that were the subject of controversy in his era. *Al-Kirmani* brought them into the light, drew them with care, refined them, and produced one of the finest philosophical texts in their exposition. The author of *al-Riyad* deserves the gratitude of generations and the appreciation of the Islamic world, because his book is a fruit of thought, and a tall tree that grew up in the shadow of this vast world.

## Manuscript and method of investigation

When I was seconded by the Ismaili Scientific Society in India under the great Orientalist Prof. W. Ivanow — its secretary — to edit *al-Riyad* and supervise its production, he sent me the first copy, the original, designated by the letter (A) and used as the base copy for the edition. After examining it and finding it full of grammatical errors as well as missing and corrupted passages, I was prompted to write back, apologizing that it would certainly not be possible to realize the project and reach the goal of producing a correct text unless it could be checked against at least one other copy. Almost a month passed, and then he sent me another copy, photographed on microfilm from Tehran, designated by the letter (B). It served me greatly, because it came to me well-set and reaching its target, and is more correct than the first — although the first has the more beautiful handwriting and layout, as the photographs show.

## Copy history

At the end of copy (A) the following is recorded:

> The first day of the month of *Rabi' al-Thani* of the year 1354 of the *Hijra*, corresponding to the second of August 1935 — in the hand of Adam ibn *Sheikh* al-Majid *Muhammad* Ali *al-Surati* of India.

Copy (B) is likewise Indian, and at its end it says:

> The branches of its copies, on Wednesday the twenty-eighth of the month of *Shawwal* in the year 1355 of the Prophet's *Hijra*.

The name of the scribe is not mentioned in copy (B), regrettably.

## Final word

This is now added to the series of historical and philosophical books that I have edited — in another work, *al-Riyad* of *al-Kirmani*. I can do no more than record here a word of thanks and gratitude to Professor W. Ivanow, in recognition of what he has done and continues to do in service of scholarship, striving to make as many Ismaili manuscripts as possible available, correctly edited, to the world of being — all for one specific purpose that does not go beyond introducing the Islamic world to this cosmological philosophy, which has occupied minds from time immemorial, and has been the focus of the attention of scholars and the orbit of their researches in the East and the West.
