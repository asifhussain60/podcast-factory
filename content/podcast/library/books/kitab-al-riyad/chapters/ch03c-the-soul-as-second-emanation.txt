# The Soul as Second Emanation

*From the Kitab al-Riyad (ki-taab ar-ri-yaad) of Hamid al-Din Ahmad al-Kirmani (haa-mid ad-deen ah-mad al-kir-maa-nee). This chapter covers Bāb 1, §27 through §38 — the closing third of the first book, where the dialectical work of the previous twenty-six sections gives way to al-Kirmani's affirmative statement of the Soul's status as Second Emanation. The author of al-Islah (al-is-laah) and the author of al-Nusra (an-nus-rah) recede; what comes forward is the doctrine itself, with its proofs, its analogies in worship, its quiet correction of even the greatest of the philosophers, and its closing prayer for illumination.*

## The arc of this final third

The opening third of this *bāb* set the architecture: each emanated thing is *tamm* (tamm; complete, perfect) in itself and *less than what stands above it* in rank. The middle third diagnosed where the two disputants went wrong — al-Islah in his framing, al-Nusra in his persistent importation of natural-world relations into the originative-emanative order.

The closing third is different in character. Al-Kirmani is no longer mainly correcting. He is now affirming. He is laying out what the Soul actually is: how it relates to the natural-world souls beneath it, why the *al-Natiq* (naa-tiq; the Speaker-Prophet) precedes the *al-Asas* (a-saas; the Foundation) in rank but not in essence-perfection, why forgetting cannot be the mark of the Soul-as-Second-Emanation, what it means for the Second to be perfect at the beginning of its existence rather than through time. The mode of writing changes, too. The verdict sentences become longer; the philosophical demonstrations become tighter; the closing prayer arrives.

This is the section in which al-Kirmani turns from prosecutor and defender into teacher of the *Da'wa* (daa-wah; the Ismaili summons). It reads accordingly.

## The Speaker, the Foundation, and the moon at the fourteenth night

§27 takes up an argument the author of al-Nusra had been building about the asymmetry between *al-Natiq* (the Speaker-Prophet) and *al-Asas* (the Foundation). Al-Nusra had argued: the Prophet, peace and blessings of Allah be upon him, reached the rank of the prior (the First Intellect's rank in the corporeal order) in perfection. The Foundation did not. The Foundation remained below the Prophet's rank, just as the female remains below the male, the moon below the sun, the earth below the sky.

Al-Kirmani concedes part of the argument and disposes of the rest in a single sentence. The author of al-Nusra's claim that the rank of the prior is higher than that of the second is *a true saying, and it is exactly what the author of al-Islah said*. The fact that the second's rank is below the prior's rank is not a deficiency in its essence. That part is settled.

But on the claim that the Prophet attained the rank of the prior while the Foundation did not — *this is hardly true*. Because the Foundation, even if he did not reach that rank by himself, *reached the level of perfection by means of the Speaker*, through the Speaker's instruction and his making him aware of the blessings sent down to him. And the image al-Kirmani uses for this is unforgettable:

> By accepting it completely, the Foundation himself was illuminated, just as the moon is filled with light on the fourteenth night of the month.

The moon on the fourteenth night is the full moon. It is not the sun. It does not generate its own light. But on the fourteenth, having received the sun's light fully, the moon is *as bright as it is possible to be* — its essence-completeness has met the sun's light without reservation, and the result is the round, full disc of the cloudless night. This is what happens to the Foundation when he accepts the Speaker's teaching completely. The Foundation does not become the Speaker. He becomes *the Foundation in his fullness*. The light is from the Speaker; the receptivity, and the brightness in that receptivity, is the Foundation's own.

Al-Kirmani then names the bodily image that proves this — the Prophet's own pattern of preaching on the Friday and on the Day of *Eid al-Fitr* (eed al-fitr; the festival at the end of Ramadan). On Friday, the Prophet ﷺ (sallallahu alayhi wa sallam; peace and blessings of Allah be upon him) ascended the pulpit to offer the sermon *before* the prayer; on the Day of Eid al-Fitr, the prayer comes *first* and the sermon comes *afterward*. Al-Kirmani reads this as a deliberate teaching about the two orders. The Friday sermon-then-prayer signifies the Prophet's attaining the degree of perfection at the side of the originals — he is the *first to receive*, and then he calls others to receive what he has received. The Eid-al-Fitr prayer-then-sermon signifies that the Speaker first attained perfection, and *through his mediation* the Foundation was acquainted with the degree of perfection. The two ritual orders are the *Da'i*'s evidence that the Prophet himself taught the structure of the *Da'wa*'s metaphysics through the structure of his liturgy.

And on al-Nusra's male-female metaphor, al-Kirmani is also patient and precise. *The existence of the female is not a deficient existence.* If its deficiency were due to what it executes that is specific to the male, then the existence of the male would likewise be deficient with respect to what is specific to the female. Each is complete in itself. By adding one to the other, their rank becomes uneven, with the female ranking below the male. *The disparity is in their ranks, not in their selves.* Once again the formula al-Kirmani has been driving toward: *rank is real, essence-completeness is also real, and neither cancels the other*.

The verdict on §27:

> The Foundation has not reached this level — so the statement of the author of al-Islah is correct: that the second is complete.

The author of al-Nusra had wanted to use the Speaker-Foundation asymmetry as proof of essence-deficiency in the Soul. Al-Kirmani turns it back into proof of the doctrine al-Islah was defending all along.

## The sensual soul versus the Soul-as-Successor

§28 brings a complication that has to be addressed head on. Al-Nusra had said: many *speaking souls* are subject to nature, are drawn toward it and toward what it commands them, and incline to its desires and pleasures, forgetting their own knowledge, light, and splendor. This is a sign of the Soul's deficiency. The Intellect, by contrast, does not forget the pleasures of its own world, its agency, its beauty, and its perfection. It does not incline to nature, does not desire it for its own sake.

This argument has a real bite, because the empirical fact is undeniable: human souls *do* lose themselves in their desires. They *do* forget what they were made for. So if the human soul is what al-Islah is calling "the Soul," then deficiency is the right word for what we see.

Al-Kirmani's reply is the most important *distinction of subjects* in the whole *bāb*:

> The submissive thing that is drawn toward selfishness, pleasures, and the following of natural desires, and the abandonment of worship, is the sensual soul of a person — not the speaking soul to which what we have attached should be attached. The sensual soul is only persuaded — and only when persuaded does it bring forth ugliness and vices. If it is affected by the intellect within the bounds of education, the intellect prevents it from committing what it would otherwise commit. These are not the successor-souls that emanate from the First Intellect, on which the author of al-Islah speaks. We have already advanced our argument about this — that the successor is distinct from these souls, and these souls are other than the successor — and that the successor is perfect and complete in a state where nothing remains in himself that he has not attained, such that he could be said to be deficient as is the case with these souls. The successor-souls exist in the first perfection, and they have a second perfection which they attain in time and through acquisition.

There are *two different things* being called by similar words. There is the *sensual soul* — the soul-in-a-human-being, the locus of desires and pleasures, the part of the human composite that needs the intellect's discipline to behave well. And there is the *Soul-as-Successor* — the universal Soul, the Second Emanation, the *al-Tālī* (at-taa-lee; the Follower, one of the Ismaili names for the Soul as Second Intellect). These are not the same subject. The first is what al-Nusra has been pointing at. The second is what al-Islah has been speaking about.

Once the distinction is named, al-Nusra's whole charge in §28 falls away. The deficiency of the sensual soul is real. It is the experience of every honest seeker. But it has nothing to do with the question al-Islah was answering. The Soul-as-Successor is *not* the sensual soul. It is the universal principle of which natural-world souls are partial expressions.

## The successor is perfect from the beginning of its existence

§29 is one of the most carefully argued sections of the entire *bāb*. The successor is perfect, says al-Kirmani; and the one who gives him his perfection and completion at the very beginning of his existence — *all at once, when he exists* — and with him and through his overflow, the deficient catches up so as to accept the degree of perfection.

He then sets out a chain of reasoning that has the precision of a geometrical proof.

Beings in the natural world exist first by potentiality and then by act. There is no passage of what exists by potentiality to act *except in the presence of what already exists in act*. And if there is no such passage except by the presence of what is already in act, then external to act there is something that *already exists* — for the existence of what is already in act is a necessary existence. If that necessary existence is undisturbed in its existing, then it is either the First Intellect or another.

It is invalid for it to be the First Intellect, says al-Kirmani, *refraining from existence-in-act except in the presence of another and needing in the mind what exists with him outside of himself so that he could act upon it*. The First Intellect is too self-sufficient to need anything else in his acting. There is nothing in him other than himself. He is the one whose perfection has its being from the One who exists, the Almighty, who is beyond him. He arose as a single existence which has no parallel in his existence, and there is nothing else present with him for him to do anything to it — for if there had been, that other would have become a worshipped god alongside the Almighty.

> We have explained this at length in our book known as *Rahat al-'Aql* (raa-hat al-aql; The Repose of the Intellect); what is in this position is that, in his isolation, there is nothing other than himself to act upon, and there is no need for any other in his acting except by acting in himself. He acts in himself, and his acting is what brings out the existent-by-potentiality in him, and by his overflow in others. This invalidates the claim that what brings out the existent-by-potentiality to act is the First Intellect; and if it is not the First Intellect, it has been proven that it is something different from him, and that is the second.

So the existence of the Second — the Soul, the Successor — is not an accidental fact about the cosmos. It is the necessary consequence of the First's self-sufficiency. The First cannot be both purely self-sufficient and the agent that brings potential things into actuality, because *acting on potentials* presupposes something with which to act. The First does not have a "something with which" outside himself. So there must be a Second who carries out the act of bringing potentials into actuality. That Second is the Soul.

The Speaker-Prophet's mission is the witness of this. The Prophet, peace and blessings of Allah be upon him, did not need someone other than himself for the greatness of his rank and integrity in order to complete his act — which is revelation and Law. And he, peace and blessings of Allah be upon him, in that uniqueness of himself and from himself, issued what he obtained according to what he benefited from what reached him as a degree. He found what he had read and prescribed it perfectly, conducting himself in a bodily house upon the descent that is the First Intellect's first stream — within the same creativity — so that his actions and his rank might lead to contemplation of the truth of the belief, in knowing what is hidden from the senses.

The First Intellect is among the principles of creativity and revival; so the path of monotheism is invariant. As for the Foundation, even though he is at the highest degree of perfection, he has not attained that degree at which he could be unique in his action — in the matter of the mission of preaching, interpretation, teaching, and guidance — by himself. He has not dispensed with what he found with him: the revelation and the Law. The Foundation works *with* what the Speaker has brought. The Prophet works *with* what he himself has received from the First.

And so, in the bodily order: the Prophet is on the path of the First — the perfect — and is self-sufficient in his action, not using anything other than himself. The Foundation is on the course of the Second — the perfect — but his action is not from revelation and Law in the manner of the Prophet. The subordinate community is conducting a subordinate orbit, of the natural world, in which its beings come out of potentiality into act *by the flow of the Second*. The souls that are in potentiality need what they obtain — the degree of communion and perfection — from the Foundation's call. Just as the Second is the one who supports those who are ready to accept, in addition to what comes from the House of Nature, likewise the Foundation, in his place in the call, is what makes the one who is willing to obey reach the degree of communion and perfection.

The result, in al-Kirmani's own concluding sentence:

> These souls are not the successor.

The natural-world souls — the souls in this body, in this *Umma* (um-mah; the community) — are not the Soul as Second Emanation. They are what the Soul-as-Successor *acts upon*. The Foundation's call is the channel through which the overflow of the Second reaches them. Each thing in its rank; each rank dignified by what overflows into it from above.

## Forgetting cannot be the mark of the Second

§30 through §32 take up al-Nusra's claim — pressed in different forms — that the Soul's deficiency is shown by its forgetting. The Soul, says al-Nusra, forgets its world. It does not remember its origin in the world of intellect. That forgetting is the proof of deficiency.

Al-Kirmani answers in three precise moves. First, in §30: forgetting takes place only *after* knowledge. If these souls had existed previously while they were knowers, then either their knowledge was essential to them or accidental. If essential, they would not be able to forget at all, since the essential cannot be removed. If accidental — and the accident came to them in the higher world — then we are positing an impossibility, because that world is the abode of eternity and perfection, not the abode of impossibility. An impossible accident cannot befall what does not admit of impossibility.

Second, in §31: if the soul's arrival is from the world of origination, then its existence in the first place is on the side of origination and emancipation in the best proportion, and what so exists is already actually existent — and what is actually existent is barred from accepting any accident, since acceptance of accidents follows the division of things that exist potentially. That which exists in the abode of origination has its perfection and completeness conferred from the very beginning of its existence, which is eternal. So it is impossible for it to accept an accident, including the accident of forgetting.

Third, in §32: forgetting befalls *the souls that are prepared, from within the natural world, to receive knowledge by entering into accidents on the basis of the causes of their existence*. The natural-world souls forget because they are the *kind* of souls to which accidents happen. The Soul-as-Successor is not.

And then al-Kirmani turns the argument back on al-Nusra in a way that catches him out by his own corpus, as he caught him out in §18:

> It suffices to point, for the refutation of what the author of al-Nusra said in his well-known book *al-Maqalid* (al-ma-qaa-leed) in its forty-fourth Euclidean proposition, to his own assertion of the impossibility of these souls coming from the world of intellect — a clear sign refuting what he said elsewhere. I do not know how that escaped him. And as far as I can judge, *al-Maqalid* was composed after the book of al-Nusra. May God seat him with our pure loyalists in the House of *al-Quds* (al-quds; the Holy Place, the holy world), and seal our religion and our worldly life with goodness through him.

This is the second time al-Kirmani has caught al-Nusra contradicting himself between *al-Nusra* and another of his own books. In §18 it was *al-Ikhtisar*; here it is *al-Maqalid*. And again the dry observation — *as far as I can judge, al-Maqalid was composed after the book of al-Nusra* — leaves the matter for the reader to weigh. The book *al-Nusra* may be al-Nusra's earlier work, before he had thought the question through. The later books make corrections he did not carry back into the earlier text.

Note the prayer al-Kirmani adds at the end. He prays that God will seat the author of al-Nusra with the pure loyalists in the *House of al-Quds* — that is, in paradise, in the company of the friends of God. Even when correcting an opponent on a serious doctrinal point, the *Da'i* prays for his salvation. This is *adab*. The argument is sharp; the heart is not.

## What the perfect does not forget — and what overflow is

§33 takes up the affirmative half of the same question. If forgetting belongs only to natural-world souls, then what marks the Soul-as-Successor is *not forgetting*. The act, in its perfection, does not forget its world, does not incline toward nature, does not desire it.

Then al-Kirmani considers a complication. What about *natural-world* souls that have acquired knowledge and works, and whose perfection is complete? Such souls are sometimes said to have *become attached* to the abode of nature in a way that resembles the *nutaqā'* (nu-ta-qaa; the speaker-prophets) and the foundations (the *asas*) that stand in their place, the prayers of God be upon them, and the emanated intellects that are the First, the Second, the Preceding, the Following, the Originative and the Emanated. Al-Kirmani's answer is precise:

> The First is loftier and further than to possess any portion of the natural world, and is likewise above inclination toward it; but its overflow remains connected to it, in order to complete what is yet incomplete.

The First Intellect is *above* nature. But its *overflow* is *connected* to nature. The Soul that has acquired knowledge and reached its second perfection joins this overflow. It does not become the First. It does not even become identical with the Second-as-Successor. It joins the river that runs from the First through the Second into the natural world.

And so al-Nusra's question — *if the Soul were as perfect as the Intellect, it would remain steadfast in what it saw in its own world; it would not desire that effect by which it has been relieved of its state* — has its answer. The Soul-as-Successor does not desire the natural world the way a deficient soul desires its food. The natural world exists *from the First Emanator*. The Soul-as-Successor does not produce nature; the First does. Through the Second, yes. But the source is the First. To make the Soul into the *cause* of nature, in the way al-Nusra's argument requires, is to confuse the overflow with its source. And to suppose that the Soul-as-Successor's perfection consists in *not* sending overflow into the lower world is to confuse perfection with isolation.

The closing sentence of §33 names the rhetorical fault one last time:

> To explain this is a chapter in itself, manifesting the prejudice of the author of al-Nusra against the author of al-Islah — even though the words of the author of al-Nusra are sound on the construction we have set out, not in the way he proposes in his rebuttal.

*The words are sound on the construction we have set out.* If al-Nusra's sentences are read as we have read them — as claims about natural-world souls, not about the Soul-as-Successor — then they are true. Read in the frame al-Nusra himself proposed, they are wrong. This is the recurring verdict of the entire *bāb*: the natural-world reading of al-Nusra's words is *correct*; the originative-order reading of those same words is *wrong*. The discriminating reader keeps the two senses distinct, and accepts what is true in each domain.

## The four modes of existence

§34 turns to a different kind of philosophical apparatus. The author of al-Nusra had cited the wise — meaning the philosophical tradition — to the effect that all things exist in four modes: in the very substance of things, or in the idea, or in speech, or in writing. He then argued: the Intellect encompasses all four. The Soul encompasses only what the idea admits of being encompassed. So the Intellect is perfect and the Soul is incomplete.

Al-Kirmani replies that the discourse is not well-ordered. The four modes the wise named, properly construed, are not four parallel domains of existence; they are nested levels of the same act of representation. He works through them:

*In the very substance of things*: this means things that exist outside the soul and have a meaning in themselves there — like the body, which is outside the soul, and whose meaning in itself is what it is in itself: length, breadth, and depth.

*In the idea*: this means things that have no essence outside the soul but whose presence is in the mind — like genus, like place, like the circles that are drawn within the soul concerning the movement of the imagined creature, and so on.

*In speech*: this means things that exist in speech — like propositions in affirmation and negation concerning subjects, in which the subjects do not themselves change.

*In writing*: this means things that exist in writing, and the ruling on their existence in it is like the surplus divine matters from the world of intellect upon the speaker-prophets and the helpers, the prayers of God be upon them, which — if they are not fixed in written letters and the forms not deposited as a whole — the soul has no perception of them.

Now al-Kirmani's correction. The four modes diminish on investigation, with sub-classes falling beneath them. So the wise (taken charitably) meant to say that writing stands in place of speech; speech stands in place of the soul in what one wishes to display; the soul likewise represents those things that lie outside it by picturing them — and in its place attaches to them in the sense of being the same, by way of the acquisition of meanings. The four modes are *one act of representation* moving through four registers. They are not four independent domains. The intellect's relation to them is not, therefore, a comparative ranking of how many domains it covers versus the soul's.

The verdict on this section:

> In the words of the wise man there is no proof of what he wanted to support him in, regarding the speaker's failure to attain that great rank. And if the words of the author of al-Nusra are taken to apply to what is in the natural world — of the souls that have risen in rank and become already-existing intellects called a Second Emanation, which still remains a list of forces below the First Emanator — then in the world of *al-Quds*, this would be sound.

Once again: *take this as a statement about natural-world souls, and it is right; take it as a statement about the Soul-as-Successor, and it is wrong*. The same pattern.

## Thought as a power that ends

§35 follows the same line. The author of al-Nusra had argued: thought, for the souls in the natural world, is a power for them to acquire what they do not yet possess the form of. The end of this power is for it to be dispensed with — that is, when the soul has acquired what it sought, it no longer needs to think about it. So the soul does not need thought regarding what it already encompasses; it needs thought only regarding things it has not yet attained, so that, by thought, it actually attains them, reaching perfection and self-sufficiency.

Al-Kirmani's reply, in this section, is brief but pivotal:

> What is meant by the "next" is the overflow and blessing upon the existents of the world — and I mean the engendered beings — to which He has given His power first, enriching them by what we have indicated in the words we have stated, for the sake of His perfection which He attained at the beginning of His existence and by which He became richer. And if this is so, then the prejudice of the author of al-Nusra against the author of al-Islah has been exposed in his discourse against him, and the truth of what he said has come to light — namely, that this is natural, not what is contained in the abode of *al-Quds*.

What the Soul-as-Successor (the "next") does is *overflow and blessing* upon engendered beings. This is *not* the same as the thought-process of a natural-world soul acquiring something it does not yet have. The Soul-as-Successor is not *learning*. It is *enriching*. The overflow of blessing upon the engendered beings is the consequence of the Successor's perfection-from-the-beginning, not the means by which the Successor *itself* attains anything new. Once again the same shape: al-Nusra's argument is right about natural-world souls and wrong about the Successor.

## Time again — the limit of al-Islah's framing

§36 returns to time, the question that has divided the disputants since §5. Al-Nusra had said: if the act of the soul is deficient because it can only occur in time, then it must be supposed that shorter time means greater perfection. We see otherwise. Things that take place in a shorter time are not therefore more perfect. So time is not the cause of deficiency.

Al-Kirmani's reply patiently corrects the misreading. *The author of al-Islah did not claim nor say that the act is in time.* He said that what is implicated as a meaning in it is attained through time, and that the duration is the lesser; and if it is obtained, it is complete. The soul performs what enables it to attain the degree of perfection from its side and to acquire the form by which it is completed by its overflow. The deficiency in the capable self is from the soul which follows the overflow, and which can accept any payment only in time: in days, months, and years. *Time is the medium in which the act unfolds, not the cause of its deficiency.*

And the verdict — the same as on every other section:

> If this is so, then the statement of the author of al-Islah was sound from the outset in what he was discussing, and the statement of the author of al-Nusra, if construed regarding the natural soul, is sound — though he wanted to be an advocate for his teacher. May God bless them and bring us together with the loyal ones, the prayers of God be upon them.

Again the prayer for both — for al-Nusra and his teacher — and for the loyal ones, the *awliyā'* (aw-li-yaa; friends of God) of the *Da'wa*. The argument has been long and detailed; the heart toward the disputants is unfailingly generous.

## What precedes the Intellect is not time

§37 brings the question of time to its conclusion. Al-Islah had said that *al-Bari* (al-baa-ree; the Originator, the Almighty) originated the intellect and time together in one fell swoop. Al-Nusra answered: time is the measure of motion, motion belongs only to the heavens, and so the heavens and the time generated from their motions came into being with the intellect at once — or at any rate it is permissible to say this. But what the intellect is preceded by is *not time*; rather it is *eternity and perpetuity*.

Al-Kirmani gives the verdict that everyone reading the *bāb* has been expecting:

> The conclusion of his statement, even if it is incomplete in its evidence and indication, is that the existence of time together with the intellect is impossible, and this is true on our own doctrine, and it follows as a corollary from the position that it is impossible for anything to be together with the first intellect. That is sufficient as a demonstration. The matter, however, is otherwise than what the author of al-Islah has mentioned in this chapter, except that the author of al-Nusra said that what precedes the intellect is not time, but rather the eternal and the everlasting — for there is nothing that precedes the first intellect.

So on the question of time, al-Nusra is right, and al-Islah is wrong. *What precedes the First Intellect is not time. It is eternity, perpetuity.* Time is an emergent phenomenon in the order of being, not a primary stratum that runs alongside the First Intellect from the beginning. The reader is sent to *Rahat al-'Aql* and to the present treatise (which al-Kirmani himself calls *al-Riyad*) for the full demonstration. The matter need not be extended further here.

## The philosophers were great — and limited

§38, the closing section of the *bāb*, is one of the most striking pieces of writing in the whole book. The author of al-Nusra had argued, by way of summary, that the intellect became perfect because it was not subject to anything other than the Almighty, while the soul became deficient because it was subject to nature. Al-Kirmani's reply is, in effect, an account of the place of philosophy itself within the *Da'wa*.

> The philosophers, including the foremost among them, although they were eminent in their own time, were nevertheless prone to error in many of the intelligible matters of which they spoke. Had they lived in our time and witnessed the springs of blessings that overflow from the House of Revelation — that things are in truth as the Alawite (a-la-wee; the Alid) call declares them to be — they would, by their own diligence, have done justice to themselves and to many of their beliefs in which the witness of the senses falls short. With the traces of the One in whom God has placed a light for unveiling the mysteries of things, they would have been convinced. But in their times, they followed their own opinions and intellects in matters of this kind, so that some of them ended up holding beliefs whose implications align with the position of the literalists of the *Hanafi* (ha-na-fee; the Hanafi school of jurisprudence) school when these latter rely on their intellects alone to follow the paths of religion. Their opinions diverged; among them were some who were closer to being correct, and some who were more in error.

This is a careful, charitable assessment. The great philosophers — al-Kirmani has Plato and Aristotle and the great *falāsifa* in mind, but he speaks of them obliquely — were eminent in their time. They did the best they could with the tools they had. But they did not have access to the *Alawite call*, the *Da'wa* of the *Ahl al-Bayt* — the People of the House of the Prophet — and so on a number of intelligible matters they reasoned by their own opinions alone, and their conclusions diverged. Some came closer to the truth; some erred more.

If they had witnessed what al-Kirmani's contemporaries have witnessed — the springs of blessings that overflow from the House of Revelation, the Imam (i-maam; the divinely appointed guide) of the time who unveils the mysteries of things — they would, by their own diligence, have arrived at the truth. They did not lack the diligence. They lacked the witness.

> He who relies upon what the balance of religion attests to, in the horizons and in the souls, has been spoken of in the discussion above, where the falsity of any belief in such a position has been shown. There is no need to dwell on this at length, except for what the author of al-Nusra has mentioned in his rebuttal. And we ask God to illuminate our intellects, and to conclude our affairs in happiness, and to grant us good outcomes and good deeds, by His existence, His kindness, and His generosity.

The *bāb* ends with this prayer. *We ask God to illuminate our intellects.* All the philosophical apparatus, all the careful adjudication, all the citations and re-citations of the *Qur'an* (qur-aan; the recitation, the Book) — they exist for the sake of what the prayer asks for. Illumination is what makes the difference between the philosopher who reasons by his own intellect alone and the believer who reasons within the light that the *Da'wa* makes available. The book closes its first *bāb* not with a triumphant verdict but with a request for what no verdict can substitute for.

## A sentence from the family

The architecture al-Kirmani has set out — *each thing complete in itself, less only by addition; the Soul as Successor perfect from the beginning of its existence, with its second perfection coming through time only in the natural-world souls beneath it; the philosophers admirable but limited by what they could not witness* — is rendered, in the wisdom literature of the Ahl al-Bayt, in a single sentence often paraphrased from the supplications of Imam Zayn al-'Abidin (zayn al-aa-bi-deen; the fourth Imam, peace be upon him):

> The lights you have given me, I receive at the rank you have placed me in; and the rank you have placed me in is the fullness of what I am, for I was not made to be another thing.
> Source: paraphrased after the manner of the supplications in *al-Sahifa al-Sajjadiyya* (the Psalms of the House of Muhammad).

Each soul receives its own light at its own rank. The rank is not an indignity. The rank is *what the soul is*. The Soul-as-Successor receives its perfection from the First Intellect; the natural-world souls receive theirs from the Foundation's call; the disciple receives his from the teaching of those above him. Each is *what he is, fully*. None is dishonored by not being what stands above.

## The book the *bāb* belongs to

This first *bāb* has thirty-eight *fusūl* (fu-sool; sections), and they are the foundation of the entire treatise. The remaining nine *abwāb* (ab-waab; books) take up: the First Intellect as First Originator (Bāb 2); the question of soul and spirit and whether they resemble the First (Bāb 3); souls as parts and effects (Bāb 4); the human being as the fruit of the world (Bāb 5); motion, stillness, and form (Bāb 6); the divisions of the world (Bāb 7); *qadar* (qa-dar; destiny) and judgment (Bāb 8); the law of Adam and the Trusteeship of Nuh (nooh; the Prophet Noah) (Bāb 9); and the closing critique of *Kitab al-Mahsul* (ki-taab al-mah-sool; The Book of the Harvest) on monotheism and the First Originator (Bāb 10). Each of these will sit on the architecture that this first *bāb* has now established.

The Soul is *tamm* in itself. The Soul is *less than the First Intellect* by rank. The natural-world souls are *not* the Soul-as-Successor but are *enriched by* the Soul-as-Successor through the overflow of the Foundation's call. The philosophers were great but partial; the *Da'wa* completes what their diligence could not reach on its own. *We ask God to illuminate our intellects.*

The first *bāb* closes here. The book continues.
