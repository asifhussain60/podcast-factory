# Al-Mahsul Corrected — Tawhid and the First Originator

*From the Kitab al-Riyad (ki-taab ar-ri-yaad) of Hamid al-Din Ahmad al-Kirmani (haa-mid ad-deen ah-mad al-kir-maa-nee). This chapter covers Bāb 10 (baab) — *On What Is More Befitting in the Treatment of the Book of al-Mahsul (al-mah-sool) in the Chapter on Monotheism and Origination* — comprising fifteen fusūl (fu-sool; sections). The bāb is the book's closing and its hinge. The nine prior bābs adjudicated the dispute al-Islah (al-is-laah) and al-Nusra (an-nus-rah) carried forward from the lost Kitab al-Mahsul (ki-taab al-mah-sool) on the *originative-emanative chain* and *the architecture of the call's unfolding through history*. Bāb 10 turns from that adjudication back to its source. The lost Mahsul itself — composed by an earlier Da'i (daa-ee) of the Da'wa (daa-wah), now read only through the second-order voices of al-Islah and al-Nusra — had erred on the most foundational doctrine of all: *tawhid* (taw-heed; the unity of God) and the *First Originator* (al-Bāriʾ, al-baa-ri). Sheikh Abu Hatim (a-boo haa-tim; the Da'i of Rayy in the prior generation, the disputant who composed *al-Islah* against Abu Bakr al-Razi (a-boo bakr ar-raa-zee) — not the figure al-Kirmani is correcting here, but the figure al-Kirmani says *did not* correct what here needed correcting) passed by the errors. Al-Kirmani names this as no fault on Abu Hatim's part: *the imams cooperate with one another, like the senses; if one sense misses something in the world, another perceives it*. Yet the duty of religion makes the correction obligatory. Across the bāb's fifteen fusūl al-Kirmani refutes seven distinct moves in *al-Mahsul*'s tawhid chapter: (1) the positing of *al-shayʾ* (ash-shay; "the thing") and *la-shayʾ* (laa shay; "nothing") as creatures of God that fall under His ibdāʿ (ib-daa; origination, innovation); (2) the formulation *"He, and nothing with Him"* — which al-Kirmani exposes as covert shirk (shirk) by smuggling *nothing* in as a *positive*; (3) the claim that the intellect in which the images of the two worlds emerge "all at once" is the First Intellect (al-ʿaql al-awwal, al-aql al-aw-wal); (4) the claim that *unity* (al-wahda, al-wah-dah) is the *cause* of the First Intellect's eternity, as if a *disease* of unity preceded the intellect; (5) the claim that the eternity of the *images* in the intellect follows from the intellect's own eternity, in the same register as the First Intellect itself; (6) the claim that the First Intellect "becomes like the Word" (al-Kalima, al-ka-li-mah) through some affecting eternity, with no intermediary; (7) the claim that the First Intellect is called *Creator* (al-Khāliq, al-khaa-liq) on account of the completeness of its creativity, and that *creativity* (al-ibdāʿ) is an *intermediary* between Creator and created. Across the seven critiques al-Kirmani returns again and again to the same principle: *what al-Mahsul has predicated of the First Intellect, the First Originator, and the act of origination is true only of the intellects that emerge from the natural world — the intellects of the speaker-prophets (al-nutaqā', an-nu-ta-qaa), of the foundations (al-asās, al-a-saas), of the Imams (i-maam; peace be upon them), and of those who follow them upon the truth*. The First Intellect cannot be confused with the second emanation; the First Originator cannot be predicated as a *creative agent in possession of creativity*; *tawhid* requires that *creativity is the Creator*, not a separable image between Him and what He brings forth. The bāb — and with it the book — closes on al-Kirmani's most extended closing reflection in al-Riyad: *Tawhid and knowledge of the limits is difficult; one man, perfect and chosen, the Imam, attains it in every age; salvation is in the two acts of worship; we ask Allah to gather us with our Imam* — naming, beneath the protocol of "[the Ruler by the Command of Allah]," the Fatimid Imam-Caliph al-Hakim (al-haa-kim) under whose reign al-Kirmani composed the book.*

## What this bāb is really asking

For nine bābs al-Kirmani has stood as a third voice between al-Islah and al-Nusra, the two later commentators on the lost Mahsul. He has corrected each of them in turn, refusing to flatten what is right in either, refusing the partial readings each had settled on. He has done this on the architecture (Bāb 1 through Bāb 7), on the call's unfolding (Bāb 8), on the historical sequence of speaker-prophets (Bāb 9). In Bāb 10 he turns from *the disputants* back to *the source they disputed*. The Mahsul itself, on the doctrine that founds every other — *tawhid* and the *Originator before all things* — had erred.

The bāb opens on an apologia. Why must al-Kirmani write this when Sheikh Abu Hatim, his predecessor in the Da'wa, *did not*? Two answers. First, the imams and the Da'is who follow them are *like the senses* — if one sense passes by something in the world of nature, another perceives it, *without that second perception being a defect for the one that missed it or for the others*. The Da'wa is not a chain of replacement; it is a chain of *cooperation*. Second: the obligation to set the doctrine right is *not optional*. The chapter on tawhid is *the first of what comes first*; whatever errs in that chapter ramifies forward into every doctrine downstream. To leave the error standing for the sake of charity toward the predecessor would be to fail what the predecessor himself was *charged with*. So al-Kirmani writes — in the name of cooperation, not in the name of correction.

The bāb's structural concern is not the *content* of *tawhid* as such (al-Riyad has been doing that for nine bābs already) but the *grammar* of how *tawhid* may be spoken. Al-Mahsul had reached for vocabulary — *al-shayʾ*, *la-shayʾ*, *al-ibdāʿ*, *al-Khāliq*, *al-wahda* — and had positioned the First Originator and the First Intellect within a grammar of *predication*: *He is the Creator of the thing*; *He, and nothing with Him*; *the intellect became eternal by virtue of unity*; *creativity is an intermediary between Creator and created*. Al-Kirmani's reply, across all fifteen fusūl, is that *each of these formulations smuggles a positive existence under a name that should have been used only as an effacement*. *The thing* becomes a *creature*. *Nothing* becomes a *creature*. *Unity* becomes a *cause*. *Creativity* becomes an *intermediary*. Each move multiplies what tawhid required be effaced.

His diagnostic instrument is simple and almost ruthless: every time al-Mahsul ascribes a predicate to *the First Intellect* or *the First Originator*, al-Kirmani asks: *Is this true of the First Intellect — the one that exists from Allah Almighty, with nothing prior — or is it true only of the intellects that emerge from the natural world, the intellects of the speaker-prophets and their followers?* In almost every case the answer is the second. Al-Mahsul has been speaking about *the second emanation* and *naming it* as the *first*. The corrections, one by one, restore the distinction.

The bāb's payoff — and the book's last argument — is the equation al-Kirmani finally writes at Fasl 15: *creativity is the Creator*. The Da'wa's tawhid does not say that *Allah possesses creativity*, nor that *creativity is an intermediary by which Allah creates*. It says that the act and the agent and the Originator are *not three*; they are the *one* whose existence is itself *the cause of every existent thing*. Beyond that one, there is *only what tawhid is itself the refusal to name*.

## The architecture of the seven corrections

Bāb 10 corrects al-Mahsul on seven distinct moves. The structure repays attention before the per-fasl walkthrough, because the moves are not parallel — they ascend.

The first three (Fasl 2 through Fasl 7) are corrections in the *grammar of being*. Al-Mahsul reached for *al-shayʾ* (the thing) and *la-shayʾ* (nothing) as if these were *names that could apply* — names of creatures, names of what God brings forth. Al-Kirmani replies that *al-shayʾ* is not a name of any specific creature; it is *the common term* under which every essence and accident falls. To make *the thing* a creature is to predicate a *single image* upon what only exists *across* every image, distorting language at its root. To make *la-shayʾ* a creature — even by negation — is worse, because it presupposes that *nothing* has some positive footing from which it can be *denied*. The whole machinery of *al-Mahsul*'s opening formulation collapses on the discovery that *the Originator does not need His act named in terms that, when named, smuggle a second creature in*.

The next two (Fasl 8 through Fasl 10) are corrections in the *identification of the intellect*. Al-Mahsul had written that the First Intellect contains the images of the two worlds "all at once" *in potency*, and that the intellect "became eternal" by virtue of *unity* — as if unity were the cause and the intellect the caused. Al-Kirmani refuses on the ground that *potency does not belong to the First Intellect at all*. The First Intellect *is the cause of the existence of every existent*; nothing precedes it; it is therefore *pure act with no admixture of potency*. The intellect *in which* the images emerge in potency, and *which* is preceded by something to bring it into act, is *the second emanation* — the intellect of the natural world, the intellect that emerges in *the speaker-prophets and the foundations and the Imams and their followers*. The First Intellect *cannot* be that. The structural diagnostic — *did al-Mahsul mean "First" but describe "second"?* — fires in every fasl from here onward.

The next two (Fasl 11 through Fasl 12) are corrections in the *eternity claim*. Al-Mahsul had argued that if the intellect is eternal, the *images within* the intellect are *equally* eternal, on the ground that what is in an eternal cause shares its eternity. Al-Kirmani's reply distinguishes two kinds of eternity. The eternity of *the First Intellect* is *the eternity of its existence as cause*; what is eternal of it is *that it is*. The eternity of *images* in the *natural-world intellects* is *the eternity of permanence-through-the-cause*: the souls of the speaker-prophets and the foundations, having attained the perfection of receiving the eternal cause, become *permanent* in a derivative register. To say of *both* registers what is true *only* of the second is to make the First Intellect *receive* what it can only *give*, and to make the second a *first-order eternal* alongside the Originator. Both errors collapse the tawhid that the predication was supposed to defend.

The last two (Fasl 13 through Fasl 15) are corrections in the *naming of the act*. Al-Mahsul had called the First Intellect *Creator* on account of the *completeness* of its creativity; had then described *creativity* as an *intermediary* between Creator and created; and had located the *image of creativity* on the side of the Creator while the *effect of the image* lay on the side of the created. Al-Kirmani's reply is the bāb's most rigorous: to name *anything other than Allah* with the name *Creator* is *shirk*, *plainly so*. To posit *creativity* as an *intermediary* — an image whose existence is *not in itself, not in the agent, not in the patient, but between them* — is to posit an *uncreated something*, since the intermediary's existence cannot itself be *originated* by the Originator (one cannot use the intermediary to originate the intermediary). And to posit an *uncreated something* alongside Allah is, by the architecture of every prior bāb, *impossible*. The bāb's conclusion is the equation: *creativity is the Creator*. There is no intermediary; the act and the Actor are *one*; *tawhid* speaks itself by the refusal of every grammar that would interpose a second.

## Fasl 1 — The apologia: why this bāb must be written

Al-Kirmani opens the bāb on a note unusual for the book. He has not — across nine prior bābs — paused to *justify the writing*. The structure of al-Riyad is dialectical: the disputants speak; he replies; the truth emerges from the seam. Bāb 10 disturbs this. The error to be corrected is not in al-Islah or in al-Nusra; it is in the source they both drew from. To correct *al-Mahsul* itself — composed by a predecessor in the Da'wa — is to speak past Sheikh Abu Hatim, whose *al-Islah* set itself to *defending* Mahsul against al-Razi's attacks. *Abu Hatim did not correct this*, al-Kirmani concedes. *He was capable, and he did not*.

What follows is a remarkable refusal of two readings of that fact. The first reading (which al-Kirmani says *most people supposed*) is that Abu Hatim's silence amounts to *endorsement* — that what Abu Hatim did not correct must therefore be *correct*. The second reading (which al-Kirmani also refuses) is that Abu Hatim's silence amounts to *failure* — that the predecessor *fell short* of his obligation. Both readings, al-Kirmani says, are *corrupt estimation*. They suppose that *one Da'i is the sufficient cover for the science of religion*. But the science of religion is *too vast* to be encompassed in a single breath, or arranged by *any number of persons short of the imamate itself*.

The image is Hamid al-Din's own, and it carries the bāb's whole frame. The imams *cooperate with one another* on the sciences of religion — *like the twelve sources (al-ḥujaj, al-hu-jaj)*, each of whom *takes benefit from those whom Allah has appointed*. They are *like one human being whose parts come together*, and *like the senses, which perceive things in such a way that if one sense misses something, another perceives it without that second perception being a defect for the one that missed it or for the others*.

So the obligation of correction does not fall on al-Kirmani *despite* Abu Hatim having not corrected, but *because* he has not. The cooperation of the Da'wa is *what makes Abu Hatim's silence non-defective and al-Kirmani's correction obligatory at once*. To leave the error standing in the name of charity toward Abu Hatim would be to mistake *what the Da'wa is for*.

The fasl closes on the principle that anchors the bāb: *the souls of religion are all on the same footing*. The duty to set right the tawhid chapter is *no man's* and *every man's*. Al-Kirmani writes because writing has become his to do, and because *the chapter on tawhid is the first of what comes first*.

## Fasl 2 — "The Creator of the thing": the first smuggling

Al-Mahsul's opening sentence in its tawhid chapter, as al-Islah and al-Nusra both quote it, reads:

> In the chapter on monotheism: He is the Creator of the thing — the rational thing, the imaginal thing, the intellectual thing, the logical thing — I mean, that which is beneath these elements, and that which is not beneath them.

The sentence sounds innocent. It can be read as saying: *Allah created the rational creature, the imaginal creature, the intellectual creature, the logical creature; whatever is beneath the elements, whatever is not.* On that reading it is unobjectionable. Al-Kirmani forces a different reading. *Al-shayʾ* — "the thing" — is not a name of *any* particular creature. It is, as the philosophers have it and as the Da'wa accepts, *a common term* under which *every essence and intention*, *every sensed and intelligible*, falls. To say *He is the Creator of the thing* is — once *the thing* is taken seriously as a name — to say that *the thing* is *one creature among His creatures*, *one image among His images*.

But if *the thing* is a creature, then it is *either prior to, with, or after the First Intellect*. It cannot be prior, because the First Intellect *is the first act behind which there is nothing but Allah Almighty*. It cannot be with, because the First Intellect *when it occurs is a single essence, not two*. And it cannot be after, because — if it followed the First Intellect — *the intellects would not be devoid of knowing it*; it would be *known and defined, like every other asset*, but it is not, and *to speak of what is not known and not defined* is *misguidance*.

The correction is small in its move and large in its register. Al-Mahsul has used *al-shayʾ* in a way that, *taken as a name*, conjures a creature that *cannot exist*. The doctrine of tawhid is the doctrine of *what cannot be predicated*. Al-Mahsul's first sentence, taken literally, predicates.

## Fasl 3 — Al-Mahsul's escape hatch closed

Al-Kirmani concedes the obvious objection. *Perhaps al-Mahsul did not mean "the thing" as a creature*; perhaps he meant the term *generically*, in the way a teacher uses *creature* without listing every creature. Al-Kirmani replies with two steps. First: *if* al-Mahsul means the term generically, then it conveys nothing — *we have already known* that Allah is the Creator of whatever falls under *the thing*; the sentence is a *tautology of religion* and contains no doctrine. Second — and this is the move that closes the escape hatch — al-Mahsul *cannot* have meant the term generically, because *he goes on to use it as a name* in what follows: he says *the Creator of the thing — and of nothing*. The opposition of *al-shayʾ* and *la-shayʾ* requires that *al-shayʾ* be *one specific something* against which *la-shayʾ* is the contrast. The generic reading collapses; the smuggled-creature reading is what al-Mahsul has committed to.

The two together produce the doctrine al-Kirmani must refute: that *something* has being to which God's creativity *extends*, and that *nothing*, too, has being to which God's creativity *extends*. Both are *creatures* in al-Mahsul's grammar. Both are *impossible* in al-Kirmani's.

## Fasl 4 — "He, and nothing with Him": the language objection

Al-Mahsul's next move makes the difficulty exact. He writes:

> The Creator of things is not of anything; only He, with nothing alongside Him, has always been — He and nothing with Him. We have denied the thing and the nothing, and we have made them both creative.

The reader might suppose this is *tawhid's classical formula*. Al-Kirmani shows what is wrong with it as a *formula*.

The first wrong is grammatical. *He, and nothing with Him* — if *nothing* is *a name*, then *nothing* is *something*, and the formula posits *something with Him*. The very act of *naming* the absence of any-thing creates a verbal counterpart to a *thing*, which the formula was supposed to deny. If *nothing* is *not a name*, then the formula is *nonsense* — words pointing to a void to which nothing in language can point. Either reading subverts the doctrine.

The second wrong is theological. *The words and letters of the composer, simple and compound alike, are all temporally generated*. Words *exist*; they are *originated*; they are *creatures*. To use words to speak of Allah *as Allah* — to suppose that *the meaning of "with Him"* can apply to the Originator who is *behind a veil from understandings and thought* — is to put *creatures* in the place where *only the Creator* stands. Al-Kirmani's invocation of his own earlier epistle *al-Rawda* anchors the point: Allah is *beyond minds and perceptions, behind a veil from understandings and thought — and without that veil, the paths of inquiry would be cut off*.

The exception that proves the rule is the Qur'an's own grammar: *Allah bears witness that there is no god but He* (Quran 3:18) — *but He*, not *but Allah*. Even the *name* "Allah" cannot stand at the place where *the affirmation* is finally lodged; only the pronoun *He*, drained of every predicate. *He* is what remains when every name has been refused.

## Fasl 5 — "Nothing" cannot be the negation of creativity

Al-Mahsul's third move is a tightening. *And if we say "He, and nothing with Him," we have denied the thing and the nothing, and we have made everything creative.* Al-Kirmani replies that this is not what the formula achieves. *Nothing*, on al-Mahsul's grammar, has now been pressed into double service: it is *the negation of being* in the formula's surface; and it is *what falls under creativity* in the formula's depth. The two cannot both hold. If *nothing* is the *negation of being*, it cannot *fall under* anything, because to fall under creativity is to *be*. If *nothing* is *what falls under creativity*, then it *is*, and the negation collapses.

The diagnosis is that al-Mahsul has been *thinking of nothing as a placeholder* — a term whose function is to mark the *limit* of being and the *beginning* of speech about being. Al-Kirmani's reply is that no such placeholder is *needed*. *Nothing is the negation of the eye of creativity* — and the negation does not *call for a name*. Tawhid does not need the term *nothing* to do its work; it needs only the *refusal* of every grammar that supposes a *with*.

## Fasl 6 — The brief reply on identity

Al-Mahsul's fourth move is more elliptical and al-Kirmani's reply is shorter. *We have denied every simple and complex image of His identity, and we have made everything of what comes to Him to say, or not to say, that He is creative — and is influenced by the truth.* The sentence is incomplete in transmission and al-Kirmani's reply registers as a marginal note: *The saying about which the findings were found — which does not fall upon Him — Yasir Bag: all of them are creative; that which is caused is wrong.* The point — visible through the textual difficulty — is that *every image*, simple or compound, is *of His creatures*, not *of Him*; to deny *His* images is the *first move* of tawhid, but to suppose that *the denial itself* yields a *positive characterization* of Him as *creative-and-influenced* is to repeat the error in a softer key.

## Fasl 7 — The closing of the verbal game

Al-Mahsul's fifth move closes the verbal play. *Nothing comes after the thing, because the occurrence of its name only happened when the thing appeared.* Al-Kirmani's reply: *nothing is not a name that falls upon an eye that has any share in existence; the thing is a name, and the one who is the pure letter has not been denied — and the negation of the name does not become a noun, except for that which the name has as a name.* The verbal scaffolding of *al-shayʾ* and *la-shayʾ* — which Mahsul had used to *do tawhid's work* — collapses into itself. The two terms, paired and opposed, *cannot together carry a doctrine of the Originator*. They can carry, at most, a *taxonomy of creatures*. The taxonomy is *legitimate*; the *use* to which Mahsul puts it — to *define* tawhid by their opposition — is not.

## Fasl 8 — The intellect "all at once" is the second emanation, not the First

Mahsul's sixth move is the most consequential. *In the first chapter on the Creator, the Almighty said: He created the world all at once. This is because He created the intellect in a single sentence, and within it the images of the two worlds emerged, along with what is in them — without those images being indicated by their identities at the level of intellect.* The picture is one al-Riyad has used itself in earlier bābs: the First Intellect contains the world *seminally*, in a single creative pulse, the images neither *severally* nor *individually* but as a *collected potency*.

Al-Kirmani's reply is the bāb's central diagnostic. *This intellect — within which the images of the worlds emerged along with what is in them — is not permissible to be the First Intellect that exists from God in the first place.* Why not? Because what is *advanced in existence* is *dispensed* from the *perception of what exists within it*; its existence is *related to its Creator who created it*; it *does not need to know more than its knowledge of itself*, namely, *that it is creative, and that its existence is not by itself but by another*.

The First Intellect, in other words, is *not a container*. It does not *hold* the images of the worlds *within it as potencies*. To say so is to make it a *vessel* that has *contents*, and contents imply *quasi-things waiting to come out*, which imply *potency*, which implies *something prior that brings potency into act*, and *nothing is prior to the First Intellect except Allah*. The intellect Mahsul describes — with the images *all at once* in *potency* — is the *second emanation*: the natural-world intellect, the intellect of the speaker-prophet who *has the images of the worlds and from whom the harvest of these images has already come to exist*. That intellect is a container; the First is not.

The correction relocates the entire grammar of *world-in-potency-in-intellect* from cosmology into anthropology — from the architecture of the chain into the structure of the prophet's soul. The chain itself is *not* the unfolding of potencies within the First Intellect; it is the *successive originations* by which what is *eternal in act* gives existence to what is *latent in nature*. Mahsul has put the unfolding *inside* the First Intellect; al-Kirmani returns it to *between* the First Intellect and the second emanation.

## Fasl 9 — Unity is the intellect, not its cause

Mahsul's seventh move tries to *explain* the First Intellect's eternity. *When the intellect's illness was the unity of the Almighty and the unity of eternity, the intellect became eternal.* The Arabic *ʿillat* — translated here as "illness" but meaning *cause* — is the technical term: *the cause of the intellect's eternity was unity*.

Al-Kirmani's reply is bracingly short. *You have made obligatory by saying this that something precedes the First Intellect in its existence — namely, the disease which you called unity. But we have already said that the intellect is not preceded in existence by anything in the first place; so it is the first of the eternal, because of the impossibility of something existing before it.* The Mahsul has made *unity* into a *predicate* the intellect *receives* — making *unity* prior, the intellect posterior, and *receiving-of-unity* the cause of *being eternal*. But *unity is not a cause of intellect, and is not other than it so as to advance upon intellect; rather, it is intellect itself, and the intellect is the unity, which is the one*.

The equation matters. Tawhid is not *the intellect's eternal possession of unity*; it is *the intellect's identity with unity*. Where al-Mahsul wrote *X became eternal by virtue of Y*, al-Kirmani writes *X is Y*. The substitution closes the door on any grammar that would interpose between the First Intellect and what it *is*.

## Fasl 10 — The cascade of identity

Fasl 10 walks the equation outward. *It is the cause, it is the effect, it is the creativity, it is the creator, and it is the Perfect — the Perfect, which is the Eternal, the Eternal, the Existence, and the Essence. One.* The cascade is not a list of *separate predicates* applying to a single subject; it is the *refusal of separation* — *cause* and *effect*, *creativity* and *creator*, *perfect* and *eternal*, *existence* and *essence* — are *not three or seven or twelve* but *one*. The refusal closes the door against every later move al-Mahsul will try.

The structural point: where Mahsul reaches for *intermediaries* (unity intermediates between Allah and intellect; creativity intermediates between Creator and created; image intermediates between actor and acted-upon), al-Kirmani replies that *intermediation* is exactly the move that *tawhid* refuses. *The cause is the effect*, in the sense that the cause's *act of causing* is *what the effect is*; *the creativity is the creator*, in the sense that *the act of originating* is *what the Originator is*. The doctrine has no room for *between*.

## Fasl 11 — Eternity of images: the second-emanation register

Mahsul's eighth move: *Since the image was made manifest in the intellect from the One, the image too is eternal in the intellect, because the intellect has become eternal in its entirety, not in part only.* The move is to *extend* the intellect's eternity *into* the images contained in it. Al-Kirmani replies that the move is *legitimate*, but *only in the register of the natural-world intellects*: the intellects of the speaker-prophets, of the foundations, of the Imams, and of their followers who *came forth into intellect through their own words*.

The diagnosis is the bāb's structural one again: Mahsul has predicated of *the First* what is true *only of the second*. The First Intellect *is* eternal; but it does not *contain images that become eternal in it*. The natural-world intellects — the intellects of the speakers and their inheritors — *do* contain images that, through the prophet's perfection of receiving the eternal cause, *attain* eternity in the register of *permanence*. The two registers cannot be collapsed. To collapse them is to dissolve the distinction between *Originator* and *originated*.

The fasl closes on the soteriological cost. If the eternity Mahsul describes belonged to the First Intellect, *the images would have no permanence, and there would be no return to the source from which they emerged*. *They would have perished and faded, and there would be no hope, no fear, no reward, no recompense, and no punishment.* The Da'wa's *salvation architecture* — by which souls return to their cause through the inheritors of the eternal — requires that the *eternity-of-images* belong to the *second emanation*, not the first. To put it in the first is to *lose* the second, and with it the *return*.

## Fasl 12 — "The intellect like the Word": no intermediary

Mahsul's ninth move tightens. *And for us, the intellect was affected by the eternity which we have explained in the compound saying — the Word of Allah, with no third intermediary between them, and so the intellect became like it.* Al-Kirmani's reply locates two errors. First, the *language of becoming*: the First Intellect is not something *that becomes* like the Word; it *is* the Word. There is no temporal or logical *interval* across which *being-like-the-Word* is acquired. Second, the *language of affecting*: the First Intellect is not *affected by* eternity from outside, as a vessel receives a stamp; *its existence is first and foremost, beyond which descriptions break off — it grows and is exalted*.

The fasl closes on what is, again, a register-error. The whole passage Mahsul wrote — *the intellect was affected by eternity, became like the Word, has no third intermediary* — is *valid speech* but only about the *intellects passing from potentiality into actuality in the realm of nature*. The speaker-prophet's intellect *does* receive the effects of the Word, *does* become *like* the Word in the register of receiving-and-becoming. *That* sentence is sound. To say it of the *First* is to *put the speaker-prophet's intellect in Allah's place*.

## Fasl 13 — "Creator" of intellect: the naming that is shirk

Mahsul's tenth move names what Hamid al-Din will not allow. *Since this is so, it is called creativity, because creativity comes from the Creator. With complete creativity, it is only complete.* The Mahsul has now called *the First Intellect* the *Creator*. Al-Kirmani's reply is unhesitating. *His saying is mistaken on two counts. The first is that he names the intellect Creator and makes it like its Creator, of whom he said it is perfect — this is shirk, and it is not permissible to say.* The second: *the Creator, in the completeness of creativity, is anything but the Complete in order that creativity be complete* — the syntax is tangled but the meaning is plain: the *Creator* is *not* the term that applies to *anything within the natural world*. The Creator is *prior to all things*; the *intellect* is *among things*; to name the intellect *Creator* is to *invert the ranks*.

The shirk-diagnosis is the bāb's most unambiguous moment. *Plainly so*, al-Kirmani says, *it is not permissible to believe what he said except in the way that is correct* — that is, by *reserving the name Creator for Allah alone*.

## Fasl 14 — Intermediary: the impossibility extended

Mahsul's eleventh move walks back from *Creator* to *intermediary*. *The intellect only derives images from its cause, which is the Word, just as the two plugs derive benefit from the sun's light, not from its substance. If it is to bring forth, this is by virtue of what is within it from the power of its cause, which is the Word — and the Word also benefits from it. It is thus clear that the Word is what has become the cause of the things issuing from the intellect.* The grammar has retreated: the *Creator* of fasl 13 is now *the Word*; the intellect is now *intermediary* between the Word and what issues from it.

Al-Kirmani replies that *the intellect Mahsul describes here is again the intellects of the speaker-prophets and their followers* — intellects deriving from their cause (the prior speakers, the prior foundations), not from the First Intellect *who is the First Creator and needs nothing else for the existence of whatever exists through it*. The diagnostic fires; the relocation is the same.

The fasl then extends with what al-Kirmani calls a *fitting addition*. *How is it permissible for anything to precede the First Intellect, so as to be a means of action between it and what is below?* The reductio proceeds by trichotomy. The hypothetical antecedent is either *of the same kind* as the First Intellect (in which case its *priority* is invalidated — same kind implies same rank), or it is *something* (in which case it is *an object for the perception of the intellects*, and the intellect is *prior to* what it perceives), or it is *nothing* (in which case it is *not the Word* but *That which transcends every predicate* — Allah Almighty, exalted above all that). *Given the impossibility of anything being prior to the First Intellect, there is nothing above the thing except the Thing of the Almighty. And if that is also the Word, it is the Cause, it is Oneness, and it is the Intellect.* The chain of identities recurs. *Allah suffices us, and He is the best Trustee.*

## Fasl 15 — Creativity is the Creator: the closing equation

Mahsul's twelfth and final move is the bāb's culmination. *Creativity is an intermediary between Creator and created, an effect of the influencer upon the influenced. The image of creativity is intermediate between the doer and the object, and its existence is on the side of the creative agent, while the effect of the image is found on the side of the created in the thing affected. This image — I mean creativity — has thus come to be in the Creator, the Almighty.*

The passage is, in its way, refined. Mahsul has retreated all the way: *creativity is not the Creator*; *creativity is an image*; the image is *between* doer and object; its *existence* is *on the side of the agent*; its *effect* is *on the side of the patient*. The grammar is the grammar of *causal mediation*: cause emits image; image affects effect.

Al-Kirmani's reply is the bāb's most rigorous and the book's final argument. *This statement contains the positing of something that Allah has not created, that He has not invented, nor brought into being. This is contrary to what the monotheists are bound to hold.* The diagnosis: an *intermediary image* between Creator and created is — by Mahsul's own grammar — *not* itself a creature (otherwise it would *itself* need an intermediary to be created, regressing to infinity). But if it is not a creature, then it is *uncreated*, and *the only uncreated is Allah*. So Mahsul has either (a) created a second uncreated, which is *shirk*; or (b) accepted that the *intermediary* is itself *Allah's act*, in which case it is *not an intermediary* but *Allah's own creating*.

The argument extends through a long disjunction. If the intermediary creativity were *similar* to Allah, it would be a *second example* of Him — two examples positing competence in each, *which arises only from a positive cause* — and the duplication regresses. If it were *not similar*, then *the existence of two opposites occurs only in the presence of another that preserves their existence*, and that *other* must be *prior* to both — again regressing. If its existence is *from Allah*, then *creativity has its existence from Allah Almighty* — and is *not* an *intermediary* but *the Creator's act itself*.

The equation al-Kirmani then writes is the book's final formula. *It is proven that creativity is the Creator; and if creativity is the Creator, then what the author of al-Mahsul said is in error, and let him avoid it, if Allah wills. We have thus arrived at what we promised at the beginning of the book.*

*Creativity is the Creator.* The Da'wa's tawhid is the refusal of the *between*. There is no image that mediates between Allah and what He brings forth. There is no creativity that *Allah possesses* and then *exercises*. There is no intermediary that *exists on the side of the agent* and *acts on the side of the patient*. *Allah is what He does, and what He does is what He is.* Every grammar that interposes is, by that interposition, the smuggling of a second creature into the place that tawhid reserves for *He, and Him alone*.

## The book's closing — the Imam, the prayer, and the gathering

The bāb does not close where the argument ends. After *We have thus arrived at what we promised at the beginning of the book* al-Kirmani turns, in a single unbroken passage, from refutation into supplication. The shift is the book's most striking moment of register.

*Tawhid and knowledge of the limits is difficult.* The book has now demonstrated the difficulty across ten bābs. *Souls' salvation from the world of nature and its impossibilities* depends on tawhid; tawhid requires what most servants cannot attain. But — *Allah Almighty knows that all His servants do not attain the degree by which they reach perfection in knowledge of the limits of Allah and in His tawhid* — *among them is in every age one man, perfect and chosen, like the Imam, may Allah's prayers be upon him*.

The Da'wa's anthropology lands here. *Perfection* is *to be complete in accepting the overflow taking place in the natural world from the side of the angels who are close to him* — the *angels* meaning the prophets, the foundations, the Imams, and their followers. The Imam is *the perfect, the chosen* in every age; *Allah makes obligatory upon them obedience to him, and His love so that they may become part of him, and His compassion for them*. The salvation architecture of every prior bāb gathers itself here: *the two acts of worship* — knowledge and deed — *fulfilling the truth from them in knowledge and action, both outward and inward* — *being keen to preserve them*.

The closing then names what Bāb 8 had named (*remembering death*), what Bāb 9 had named (*the surplus of obedience*), and what Bāb 6 had named (*the perception of the realities of things*): *its opportunities are an obligation*. The book's last full theological sentence is the supplicatory anchor: *fulfilling its right, doing good deeds, and knowledge of the realities of things*.

Then the prayer itself. *May Allah place us and the body of believers in the east of the earth and its west — those who conclude their lives with goodness — and may our sustenance and their intercession be the intercession of Muhammad, Ali, and the pure nation, and our Imam [the Ruler by the Command of Allah] and his fathers, peace be upon them, in the land of Jerusalem with His mercy and compassion.*

The honorifics are exact and load-bearing. *Muhammad* — the Prophet ﷺ. *Ali* — Imam Ali ibn Abi Talib (a-lee ibn a-bee taa-lib), the foundation of the Imam-line. *The pure nation* (al-ʿitra al-ṭāhira, al-it-rah at-taa-hi-rah) — Fatima (faa-ti-mah) and the Imams of her line. *Our Imam [the Ruler by the Command of Allah]* — the convention by which the Fatimid Imam of the Cairo court was named in formal Da'wa writing in al-Kirmani's lifetime, the Imam-Caliph al-Hakim bi-Amr Allah (al-haa-kim bi-amr al-laah), under whose reign al-Kirmani wrote and to whose court he was attached. *His fathers* — the Imams of the Fatimid line back through al-ʿAziz (al-a-zeez), al-Muʿizz (al-mu-izz; who established Cairo and to whom al-Kirmani earlier referred when invoking *Imam al-Muʿizz's well-known book*), and back through the *concealed* Imams to Imam Jaʿfar al-Sadiq (jaa-far as-saa-diq) and ʿAli (peace be upon them). *In the land of Jerusalem* — naming the eschatological gathering at *bayt al-maqdis* (bayt al-maq-dis) where the Da'wa's tradition placed the return.

The book closes on the doxology. *Then we conclude the book with praise to Allah, Lord of the Worlds, and prayers upon the best of the first and the last, Muhammad, the family of the Imams, and the guiding Imams. And we say: there is no power but from Allah the Almighty, what Allah wills; I ask Allah for forgiveness, and I delegate my affair to Allah, sufficient is Allah, yes the Trustee, yes Allah, yes the Supporter. Praise be to Allah unto the praise of Him, and prayers upon Muhammad and his good, pure family, all of them.*

The argument of al-Riyad has been a chain of corrections — al-Islah corrected, al-Nusra corrected, al-Mahsul itself corrected at its foundation. What the chain of corrections has uncovered is not a *novel doctrine* but the *grammar* under which the Da'wa's doctrine of tawhid was always being held. *Creativity is the Creator*; the Originator does not stand on intermediaries; the First Intellect is not a vessel; the *between* of doer-and-object collapses into the *one* of *He, and Him alone*.

And under that doctrine — across ten bābs and one hundred and fifty-seven fusūl — the architecture of the call returns, in the closing passage, to its proper destination. *Tawhid is difficult; one Imam attains it in every age; we ask to be gathered with him; we ask to receive the intercession of Muhammad, Ali, the pure nation, and our Imam*. The book of *judgment between the two reformers* closes with the supplication that judgment itself was always for.

*Praise be to Allah unto the praise of Him.*
