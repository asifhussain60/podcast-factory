# Soul and Spirit: One Substance or Two?

*This chapter covers all six sub-chapters of* Chapter Three *of the book at hand — the judgment offered by the author, a Da'i and master among the limits of the call, on a question the previous chapter set up but did not answer: the Second (the universal Soul) and the lower soul (prime matter), are they similar to the First or not? The first reformer had argued that this world contains darkness because the Second is born from the First and resembles him, while prime matter is only a trace of the Second and so does not resemble it. The second reformer had refused: if light and darkness are absolute and untouching, there is no contact between worlds. The master settles the dispute. The First and Second are counterparts, "the mineral of honor and perfection"; prime matter is the trace of the Second, the essence of darkness manifested by the power learned from the First. Resemblance is not the condition of contact. The chapter then works the same doctrine through the architecture of cause and effect, through the question of whether the Second has parts or sides, through the claim that the Second "fell" by distance from the First, and through the closing problem of whether nature is born of soul as soul is born of intellect. The whole chapter is a defense of one settled formula: the Second is the like-counterpart of the First; prime matter is the trace beneath, brought forth not by resemblance but by the power of the First operating through the Second.*

## Where this chapter picks up

*Chapter Two* held the line on what the First Intellect is not — not a body, not in motion, not joined to a co-present "creativity" alongside it. The categorical mismatch had been the master-error: bodies and intellects do not share predicates. Now *Chapter Three* takes the matching step downward. If the First Intellect is what it is, and the Second is the Soul that the First emits, then what stands one further step down? The first reformer had a name for it from the philosophers — prime matter, the substrate of the natural world. The second reformer called the same matter "the lower soul" or simply "matter," and pressed a different question: how can the higher and lower worlds touch at all if one is light and the other is darkness?

That, more than any other question, is what *Chapter Three* exists to settle. The chapter's title records it bluntly: *On the judgment among the limits of the call concerning what was discussed about the soul and the lower soul: are they similar to the First or not?* In the perceiving language of the author's day, "soul" meant the universal Soul, the Second, the rank emitted from the First Intellect. "Lower soul" meant either prime matter itself, the essence of the lower world, or, sometimes, the rational soul as it inhabits the natural composite. The question across the chapter is the same: are these substances continuous with the First, or categorically separated from it?

The answer the chapter builds, sub-chapter by sub-chapter, is neither. The Second is the like-counterpart of the First. Prime matter is the trace of the Second, the essence of darkness in this world. The two worlds do not need to resemble each other to be in contact. Contact happens by emission and by the power learned from the One who emits. The doctrine is precise because the architecture of the call rests on it. If the Second cannot be linked to the First by resemblance, and prime matter cannot be linked to the Second by birth, then the speaker-prophet, the Imam, and the ranks of the call must be linked to their principles by something other than likeness. They are linked by the same chain of emission our author has been describing since *Chapter One*.

## Sub-chapter one — the trace, not the birth

A chief preacher, the first of the two reformers, opened *Chapter Three* with a defense of his earlier position in the book *The Correction*. A master of the school, the second of the reformers, had proved, on his own reading in the book *The Defense*, that nature and darkness are born of the Second the way the Second is born of the First. The chief preacher refused. We do not say that, he wrote. The Second is born of the First because that world is the world of honor and virtue, and the Second resembles the First even in being one rank lower. This world, by contrast, is the world of darkness and ignorance. Prime matter is not born of the Second; it is only a trace of it. The Second resembles its principle; the substrate does not.

The rebutter counter-pressed. This is wrong, he said. If that world were light with no darkness in it, and this world were dark with no light in it, then there would be no connection between this world and that world. The two would be sealed off from each other. Rather we say, the master of the school proposed, that the Soul has two extremes. One toward the intellect, and on its horizon is the luminous, noble side. The other toward nature, in which there is some darkness, as it were a circle, because nature is on its horizon. And nature itself has two ends. One toward the partial soul, in which there is some light because it is on the horizon of the Soul; the other facing the elements, which are the darkness in which there is no light at all. So both worlds are mixed at their edges. We do not say that this whole world is the world of light with no darkness in it; nor that this whole world is a world of darkness with no light in it. If it were as the chief preacher described, there would be no contact between them at all.

The rebutter, the second reformer, pressed further. It is true, he said, that the celestial spheres are born of the Soul although they do not resemble it, just as the Soul is born of the First although it does not resemble it. The Soul has fallen into a state of frailty and darkness because of its distance from the First Cause, and this is what made it possible to be born from the Soul. Likewise the diminution that fell upon the Soul, because it is beyond the First Cause, is what enables the further chain of generation. *This is what he said.*

The master's answer is careful, and consequential.

*The wording of what the first reformer said in his book is different from what the second reformer said.* The gist of the rebutter's saying, our author records, is that the Second is born of the First and is not like the First. The chief preacher refused that formulation. Rather, he said, the Second is born of the First, is not like the First, and yet *resembles* him, though the First is of higher rank and the more honorable station. Like the male and the female in every race and species. The male, even though he is higher and more honorable than the female, is nonetheless her counterpart and her likeness. The Second is the counterpart of the First in the rank below.

Then he gives the doctrine that the whole chapter will lean on. The First and the Second are two counterparts, *the mineral of honor, perfection, and elevation*. There is no honor above them. The Almighty has made them the mineral of perfect honor and perfect light, complete elevation, complete knowledge, with no ignorance there, no cycle, no darkness whatsoever. They founded the higher world, which is the mineral of purity, of the sanctified, and of life. Prime matter is a trace of the Second, which is the essence of this world; it is the essence of darkness, which cannot be described by mercy or power, yet the Second has manifested it. By means of the power learned from the First, the Second then brought forth from the substrate the image, which is nature.

The settlement is not that the rebutter is wrong on every word. The chief preacher was not obliged to claim that the entire higher world is light without any darkness in it as an axiomatic premise; the proof that prime matter is not born from the Second is enough on its own. There is no need to belabor the discussion with matters that are not the question. The question is the relation of the substrate to the Second, and the answer is that the substrate is the trace of the Second, not its birth.

The author puts it in his own formula: *the substrate is not like the Second, nor like the First, because the existence of the Second and the First is by their emission* from the First, at the lower proportion that I have shown in our earlier words and in our well-known book, the master's other treatise on the cosmology. That is enough.

The reference to the master's other treatise is precise. There our author had laid out the cosmology in full: the First Intellect emerges from the act of the Almighty Creator in the realm of origination in a single complete eternal existence. The Second is emitted from the First in the same act. From the Second's overflow, the spheres of the natural world, the realm of emanation, are generated, and the substrate receives the impression of the Second's power, not as a child receives the form of a parent, but as a mirror catches a shape that does not resemble the light that casts it. The earlier treatise maps the seven seas through which the overflow chain runs; this book assumes that map and uses it to adjudicate where the two reformers misplaced the categories.

The image he is reaching for, and that the rebutter missed, is something like this. Two adjacent regions of being do not need to share a color to share a border. The border is the work of the power that joins them. The First and Second share honor because they are like-counterparts. The substrate shares no honor with the Second; the substrate is darkness. But the Second meets the substrate through the power learned from the First, and from that meeting nature comes forth. The rebutter's worry that light and dark cannot touch is the worry of someone who has imagined the two worlds as two opaque substances. They are not. The higher world is the agent of contact. The lower world is the patient. There is no need for the two to resemble each other for the work to be done.

The Commander of the Faithful, the cousin of the Prophet (peace be upon him), in one of the aphorisms preserved in the book *The Brilliant Aphorisms* — the collected sayings — set the principle that anchors the master's whole picture of mediation:

> *The intellect is the messenger of the truth.* — (from the book *The Brilliant Aphorisms*, compiled from the sayings of the Commander of the Faithful)

The messenger is not the truth, nor is the messenger like what he carries. The messenger is the means by which the truth reaches the one who is to receive it. The messenger does not need to share the form of what is being delivered. He needs only to be able to deliver. That is precisely the function our author assigns to the Second in its relation to the substrate: the Second is the messenger of the First's power into the world of darkness, and the trace that the substrate carries is not the Second's likeness but the impression of the power that was delivered through it.

## Sub-chapter two — the Soul has two aspects, neither of them like the First

The second reformer repeats his objection, that the chief preacher's picture leaves no contact between the worlds, and the master works through the same ground again, but now from a different angle. The Soul, he says, is from the world of creativity and renewal. What is common to the Soul suits what is above it by being emitted, and what is below it suits being a body. The Soul has two aspects. Neither of them is like the First from which the Soul exists.

The Second resembles the First on one side and does not resemble the First on the other. The side from which the Second resembles the First is that it is emitted, emission being the principle of the higher world, and the Second shares emission with the First. The side from which the Second does not resemble the First is that the Second is also that which receives the natural body, the form of nature that was added to the world of creativity and renewal, becoming a life that is sound from that world without its direct presence, and gives nature what nature has the capacity to accept of backwardness and perception. *And if it is added to this world, it would be the principle of movement, and would be in the thing that is in it itself, not by way of presentation, and it would become nature, even if it is emitted in its existence, or a medium between the two emissions, the First and the Second.*

The Soul, in the master's careful formulation, is the medium between the two emissions. Above it stands the First, pure emission, complete actuality, no admixture of body or nature. Below it stands what the Soul gives rise to through the power learned from the First — the natural world, the substrate receiving the image, the spheres rolling in their orbits, the bodies of the elemental world coming to be. The Soul is what holds these two together. It is the rank at which emission meets receivership. From above it receives; below it gives. Its two aspects are not "two halves" of a single substance, the way the philosophers of antiquity imagined the soul as a divided thing. They are the two relations of one rank: the relation upward to its principle, and the relation downward to what its overflow generates.

The image that the court treatises of the Fatimid caliph make explicit — the Imam of the chain being the source the master repeatedly reaches for when settling questions of the doctrine of one — is that of a single light that is one in itself but lights two different regions of what stands below it. The light does not divide. The regions differ in their capacity. The higher region receives the light as light. The lower region receives the light as warmth, as movement, as the patterned change we call nature. To name the Soul as having two aspects is not to bifurcate the Soul; it is to name the two ways the one Soul stands in relation to the ranks above and below.

The Quranic figure of the soul fashioned with twin inspirations — one inclination upward, one downward, yet held within a single created unity — captures at the rank of the particular soul something of the same picture the author is reaching for at the rank of the universal:

> *By the soul and Him Who fashioned it, then inspired it with its corruption and its piety. Indeed he succeeds who purifies it.* — (the Quran, chapter al-Shams, verses seven through nine; translation rendered after Yusuf Ali)

The verse speaks of the human soul and the two inclinations placed in it at the moment of its fashioning. The tradition of the call reads the figure upward as well as inward. The same logic that gives the human soul two inclinations from a single fashioning gives the universal Soul two relations from a single emission. The Soul is one in itself; its twoness is the twoness of what stands above and below it, not the twoness of a substance cut in half. The Soul has corruption and piety in the precise sense that it has a side that meets nature and a side that meets the Intellect, and as in the verse, *the one who succeeds is the one who purifies* — not the one who imagines the soul as divided property to be split.

This formulation refuses the master of the school's picture of light and darkness as opposed substances meeting at a border. There is no border in the picture our author is drawing. There is one chain of existence. What looks like a border to the rebutter is in fact the difference of capacity at different ranks. The Soul is the rank at which the chain bends from emission into receivership, but the Soul itself is still wholly within the world of creativity. It does not "fall" into darkness. The substrate falls; the Soul does not.

## Sub-chapter three — no parts, no sides, no diameters

The rebutter now presses a more specific version of his earlier claim. By "the Soul" we mean the Second, he says, and the Second has two sides. One side toward the intellect, on its horizon, the noble luminous limit. Another part turned toward nature, lying upon nature's horizon, the side in which there is some darkness.

The master refuses. *This is not correct.*

The proof he gives is sharp. The one possessing parts is the one possessing diameters estimated by area. Parts are the diameters of bodies. The rebutter has called the Second a Soul, and on the basis already explained, the Second is not an embodiment. If the Second is not an embodiment, then it cannot have parts in the sense the master of the school requires. To say the Second has "a luminous limb that lies upon the horizon of the intellect" and "a limb in which there is some darkness lying within the interpretation of nature that faces the intellect" is to talk as though the Second were a body with two sides. The rebutter has imported the geometry of bodies into a rank where bodies cannot reach.

The doctrine our author substitutes is one he has been building since *Chapter One*. The Second's existence is derived from a noble proportion in the First, the proportion of being a cause. The Second is also itself an effect — the caused — and being an effect adds another proportion below the cause-proportion. *And I have already said that the first is that which exists and is not in itself a cause.* The First is what exists pure. The cause-proportion in the First is what gives rise to the Second. The effect-proportion in the First — that is, the First considered as itself standing in relation to what is below it — is what determines that the Second's existence is from the lower proportion.

The effect, by being a cause in addition to what stands below it (which is the lower proportion in the First), and the effect, by being added to itself (which is the lower proportion in the First), is from its own intellect. It is a cause from which something like itself emanates, for the cause has its effect upon it. From its own intellect, the effect is a non-being in the technical sense, from which emanates that which is not like it, by being an emanation from the lower proportion in the First.

This is dense and the master knows it. The point that matters most for the chapter is the next one. The honor of the effect's proportion is greater than that of the cause's proportion. *The cause receives what is higher than what comes about from the cause, except for that which is below the effect.* The relation runs both ways. The cause receives more than it emits, in one direction. The effect emits less than it receives, in the other.

What this denies to the rebutter is the picture of the Second as a body-like thing with luminous and dark sides. The Second has no sides. It has proportions — a cause-proportion and an effect-proportion — and those proportions are not parts in space. They are differences in rank within the same single self. The Second is *one* self that acquires two proportions in its additions. To call those proportions "limbs" is to confuse the language of relation with the language of geometry.

The image the author reaches for to make this concrete is the relation of *the speaker and the foundation*. He has used this image before in the earlier chapters. The "foundation" is the rank that bears existence; the "speaker" is the one whose presence in the foundation indicates the existence of what stands above. Being invisible to the speaker, all that was originally there is present in the speaker in whom the existence of the foundation lay. And likewise, not all that the speaker has is present in the foundation, because the speaker has what the foundation does not share — the visual in which the foundation does not participate. The foundation in which the one within it is a participant of the speaker, and there is nothing in the speaker that he does not indicate. If he does not indicate it, then it is the likeness by which the existence of the Second is inferred according to its rank.

The point of the image is to refuse the language of "sides." The Second's existence is inferred from its rank in the chain, not from a survey of its dimensions. To imagine it as having a "luminous side" and a "dark side" is to make exactly the categorical mistake *Chapter Two* exposed: applying body-categories to a rank that is not embodied. *And its rank was the second. It is not made manifest to the first, and if it is not indicated, then its existence is what was mentioned, namely that it lies upon the horizon of nature, which is impossible.*

What the master of the school needed in order for his picture to work was a Second that could be split. There is no such Second.

## Sub-chapter four — distance from the First does not make a thing

The rebutter now offers a different reason for why the substrate fell into darkness: because of its distance from the First Cause, in order that the Soul might be born from it.

The reformulation tries to lean the explanation on *distance*, as though the substrate's darkness, and its capacity to give birth to the Soul, both flow from the simple fact of being far from the First. The master refuses both the distance-talk and the birth-talk.

The fact that prime matter is not like the First, and that the Second also is not like the First, is not because either is "far" from the First by the proportion that is the case of the first one — namely its being the effect. The Second is the effect because it is a combination of two ratios relative to being a cause. The proportion of being the effect, and the existence of something that exists from the proportion that is the effect, is not like the existence of something that exists from the proportion that is a cause. The cause is more honorable than the effect in every respect, and precedes it in rank and nature. From the beginning, what is said to be a cause and what is said to be an effect is one single self that acquires the proportion in additions.

The doctrine cuts the ground out from under the rebutter's image. There is no "distance" in the chain in the sense of spatial removal. There are proportions added to one self at one rank, which then determine what stands at the next rank by their character. The Second is what it is because of the proportions added to the First, not because the Second has "traveled away" from the First. And the substrate is what it is because of the proportions added to the Second, not because the substrate has "fallen further" from the First.

The same correction given in *Chapter Two* to the language of "motion" — that motion is for bodies and not for the ranks of origination — now applies to the language of "distance." Distance is for bodies that occupy space. The ranks of origination do not occupy space. To speak of the Second as "far from the First" by way of being the effect, and of the substrate as "further still" — to imagine the chain of existence as a line stretching outward from the First into greater and greater remove — is to import a body-category into a rank where bodies cannot reach. The rebutter did exactly that.

The more accurate image is one the master's other treatise gives in the language of the seven seas of the universal Intellect. The ranks of the Intellect are not separated by intervening distance. They are concentric proportions of one single light, each one determined in its character by the proportion at which it stands within the originating act. The First Intellect is the act of origination itself. The Second is the same act considered under the proportion of being an effect, and so is the medium through which what stands below the Second comes to be. Prime matter is the trace impressed on what stands below the Second when the Second exercises the power learned from the First. Nothing has traveled. The ranks have not unfolded across a distance. The same single act has been seen under the proportions that determine each rank.

When the chain is read this way, the rebutter's "distance" language collapses. The substrate is not far from the First because it has gone far. The substrate is what it is because the proportion at which it stands is that of the trace impressed by the lower proportion in the First. There is no "further" for it to fall to. There is the rank it occupies, and there are the ranks above it.

The Quran itself refuses spatial language for the most intimate of relations between the ranks. The verse speaks of the human soul, but the grammar is general:

> *And We have created the human being and We know what his soul whispers to him, and We are closer to him than his jugular vein.* — (the Quran, chapter Qaf, verse sixteen; translation rendered after Yusuf Ali)

Closer than the jugular vein is not a measured nearness; nothing is measurable on the scale the verse names. The verse names the kind of closeness that is not closeness-by-distance, the closeness of the One whose act is the act by which the soul whispers in the first place. If even between the Almighty and the human soul the language of distance fails, then the master of the school's picture of the substrate as "further from the First by the proportion of being twice-effected" fails on the same grammar. The chain runs by rank, not by removal. To stand at a lower rank is not to have been moved to a more distant address; it is to occupy a different proportion in the one originating act.

## Sub-chapter five — no deficiency in the Second

The rebutter presses one more variation of the same picture. The diminution, he says, occurred in the Soul because the Soul is beyond the First Cause, *I mean the previous one.* The Soul is *less than* the First in a way that admits of being called a diminution.

The master answers carefully. There is no deficiency in the Second, which the rebutter called a deficiency, as already mentioned; nor is the Second far from the First. Rather, the Second is *like* the First, like its counterpart, as the earlier sub-chapters established. And the diminution that the rebutter speaks of is necessary, but not from the very nature of the Second. It came about not from the side of the Second's own selves, which is the side of the Second's perfection, but from the side of which its second perfection is acquired by way of benefit.

The distinction is delicate and load-bearing. The Second has, considered from one side, no deficiency at all. From the side of its own self, the Second is perfect. This is the formula that has carried this book from the opening pages of *Chapter One*: *perfect in self; deficient only in addition to what stands above.* The Second is perfect in itself the way the First is perfect in itself. The two are like-counterparts. The Second's "diminution" is only the formal fact that, considered in relation to the First, it stands at a lower proportion, and that lower proportion is what makes possible the Second's second perfection — the perfection it acquires by receiving the benefit that the First emits to it.

The trap the rebutter fell into was to imagine the Second's lower proportion as if it were a defect *within* the Second's own being. A defect is a lack inside something. The Second has no lack inside it. What the Second has, on one side, is a relation of being-emitted-from above, and the lower-ness of that relation is not a defect in the Second; it is the form of the gift the First gives. To call this "diminution" in the ordinary sense of the word — to imagine the Second as a damaged or incomplete version of the First — is the same body-category mistake the master has been correcting all along. Bodies can be diminished. The Second cannot.

The Fatimid caliph, in his interpretive treatise on the law, returns again and again to a kindred formula about the Imam in his time. The Imam is not the Imam by being less than the Prophet who founded the path, but the Imam by virtue of the rank at which he stands in the chain of guidance, where his perfection in self is intact and his benefit comes from his relation to the rank above him. The structural form is the same: each rank is perfect in itself; each rank receives benefit, not defect, from what stands above. There is no diminution in the rank itself; there is only the form of the giving by which the rank stands where it stands.

The image the author's own corpus reinforces is that of light. The light that fills a higher chamber is not diminished when it also illumines a lower one. The lower chamber receives less light than the higher because the lower chamber's capacity is less. But the light has no defect. Whatever is in the higher chamber's light is in the lower chamber's light too, in the measure the lower chamber can hold. *The receiver, not the giver, sets the limit.* This is the doctrine sub-chapter seven of *Chapter Two* had already framed in the language of overflow and capacity; *Chapter Three* now applies the same doctrine to the Second's relation to the First.

The fourth Imam in the line of the Commander of the Faithful — whose supplications were preserved by his sons and gathered as the book *The Psalms of Islam* — opens his prayer for himself and his followers with a formula that names the same structural truth at the rank where it matters most for the worshipper:

> *O You who give to the one who asks of You; O You who give to the one who has not asked of You and the one who has not known You, out of Your tender mercy.* — (from the book *The Psalms of Islam*, the fifth supplication; the prayer for himself and the people of his fold)

The supplication makes the same structural point being made about the Second. The Giver is undiminished by the giving. The asker and the non-asker, the knower and the non-knower, receive in the measure of their capacity, but the capacity is set by them, not by any withholding on the part of the Giver. To imagine the Giver as "less" because some receive less is the same category-mistake the rebutter makes about the Second. The Second is not diminished because the substrate below it receives only a trace. The receiver sets the measure of what passes through. The Giver remains what the Giver is.

## Sub-chapter six — nature is born of soul; the chain holds

The rebutter makes one last argument, and on this one, the master concedes more than he refuses. The rebutter sets up a careful parallel. The proportion of the birth of nature from the Soul is no greater, he says, than the generation of the Soul from the Intellect. The Soul is less than nature in their capacity to be generated from what stands above them. So as it is permissible for the Soul to be generated from the Intellect, with the Soul's perfection and honor and its closeness to the First Cause of the Soul, it is equally permissible for nature to be born from the Soul. The reason: nature is more like the Soul in all its states, in its actions and powers, than the Soul is like the Intellect in *its* actions and powers.

Nature, among the early teachers, is labeled as the principle of motion. Some of them said it is the active force. If one looks at nature within these two limits, the master of the school observes, it is the best fortune and the best portion, because the Soul, among the first, is the enduring moving essence, and is nevertheless labeled with the Intellect, though not in the way that the Intellect is one of the determinations of the Soul. The Soul also seriously challenges the Intellect, since the definition of the Intellect is that it is the first of things, that it is the creator, that it is the first effect, and that it is the first middle. It is not permissible to say that the Soul is the first thing, nor that it is creative, nor that it is the first effect. So it is permissible for the Soul to be born from the Intellect with its perfection and honor and its closeness to the word, like the Soul that does not bear the limitation of the Intellect. And it is not denied that it is permissible for nature to be born from the Soul like nature, which resembles in its limits the limits of the Soul.

The rebutter then makes the move that, in his own judgment, settles the case. Even if one looks at the matter from the perspective of consideration, this amount of knowledge would not be hidden. Even if the chief preacher — his master — stood upon the wonders and wisdom of nature, and the splendor and honor in nature's actions, and the beauty from which the partial soul desires to incline toward here and to follow it, the rebutter would still hold his ground. *For you know and are certain that nature is born of the Soul*, even though it does not resemble it, because effects are never desirable in order to regulate their causes. From this fact, the partial soul desires to follow nature, and that all goodness, splendor, honor, and adornment is from the higher noble world. If nature were not born of the Soul, the Soul would not be able to speak of beauty, splendor, and adornment at all. Rather, in order for nature to be generated from the Soul, the Soul said: what she saw in her world of beauty and honor was an incomplete story, incomplete without the story of the Soul. *So understand what he said.*

The argument has real weight. The partial soul, the human soul in the natural composite, is drawn to nature's beauty. The beauty is real. The drawing is real. If nature were not in some way the offspring of the Soul, then nothing the partial soul finds beautiful in nature could connect back to the higher world. But what the partial soul actually does — every act of perceiving beauty in nature and tracing it back to the splendor of the higher rank — is testimony that nature is, in fact, born of the Soul. The rebutter takes this as decisive.

The master's response is terse, and it is the chapter's closing word: *If there is any precedence in the discussion over the intellect, the soul, nature, and the existence of the above — its existence over others — and otherwise, it implies the correct statement, then I quote it; and we do not need to elaborate with certainty upon what continued the mistake in perception. And in that is enough.*

The reading is concessive but careful. The rebutter is, in this sub-chapter, recovering the doctrinal order — Intellect, Soul, nature, in that order of priority — and is right to say that the chain is what makes the partial soul's longing for beauty intelligible. To the extent his statement carries that correct chain, the master lets it stand without further argument. *I quote it.* The matters where the rebutter continued the mistakes earlier in the chapter — the talk of sides, the talk of distance, the talk of diminution — do not need to be revisited. The chain itself, when stated cleanly, holds.

But the word he uses for "born" is to be read in the precise sense the chapter has established. Nature is born of the Soul only in the sense that the Soul, by the power learned from the First, brings forth the image in the substrate, and that image is what we call nature. Nature is prime matter informed by the Soul's power. The "birth" is not a likeness-birth. It is the impression of the higher rank's power on the lower rank's substrate. Prime matter is still the trace, not the offspring. Nature is still the image of the trace, not the image of the Soul. The chain holds because the power runs through it, not because the lower ranks resemble the higher.

The Quranic anchor that runs beneath this whole closing sub-chapter, though the author does not quote the verse here, is the verse the tradition of the call routinely reaches for when explaining the Soul's office in the natural composite: *Indeed We offered the trust to the heavens and the earth and the mountains, and they declined to bear it, and were afraid of it; but the human being undertook to bear it. Indeed he was unjust to himself and ignorant.* (the Quran, chapter al-Ahzab, verse seventy-two). The trust is, in the inner interpretation of the Fatimid caliph and the master's own earlier treatise, the deposit of the rational soul in the natural composite, the rank at which the higher world entrusts its light to a substrate that does not resemble it. The natural composite carries the trust without being the trust. The partial soul's desire for beauty in nature is its dim awareness that the substrate it inhabits is the bearer of a deposit that does not match the substrate.

That is also the sense in which the verse of the spirit applies. The Quran refuses to give a substrate-account of the spirit when the question is pressed:

> *And they ask you about the spirit. Say: the spirit is from the affair of my Lord, and you have not been given of knowledge except a little.* — (the Quran, verse eighty-five of the chapter on the night journey; translation rendered after Yusuf Ali)

The verse's occasion of revelation is preserved in the canonical hadith collection. A companion of the Prophet, a transmitter of the Quran, recalled the moment in the cultivated land outside the city of Medina:

> *I was walking with the Prophet (peace and blessings of Allah be upon him and his family) through the cultivated land of Medina, and he was leaning on a palm-leaf stalk. He passed by a group of the people of the Book. Some of them said to others: ask him about the spirit. They rose to him and asked, and he stood still and did not answer. I knew revelation was descending on him, and I stood in my place. When the revelation had come down, he recited the verse.* — (preserved in the canonical hadith collection of the traditions narrated through the Companions)

The narration does two things for the chapter. It anchors the verse in the moment of its first speaking — the questioners being the people of the Book in Medina, the answer coming by descent of revelation, not by ratiocination. And it preserves the silence that precedes the answer. The Prophet does not answer at once; he waits until the descent. The form of the revelation is itself the form of what the revelation says. The spirit is from the command — the divine word — of the Lord, and what reaches the messenger about it reaches him by the same chain of command the verse names. The questioners are looking for the spirit as if it were a substance to be inventoried. The answer is that the spirit is a rank in the chain through which the command runs.

The spirit's "affair," the divine word, is the affair of the First whose power reaches the Second and through the Second reaches the natural composite. The spirit is not the natural substrate; the spirit is the power borne by the rank above the substrate. To ask whether the Soul and the spirit are one substance is to ask the wrong question if one is asking about substrate-likeness. The Soul is the rank in the chain of origination; the spirit is the affair carried by that rank; prime matter is the trace impressed by the affair in the substrate. They are not three substances in a row. They are three names for what happens at one continuous overflow of power from the First.

## The chapter's settled formula

The six sub-chapters land on one settled formula. The First and the Second are like-counterparts — *the mineral of honor, perfection, and elevation* — without ignorance, without darkness, without diminution, in the world of the sanctified. Prime matter is the trace of the Second, not its child; the essence of this world; the substrate impressed by the power learned from the First. Nature is the image of the substrate informed by the Soul's power. The chain holds not by likeness from rank to rank but by the running of the same originating power through the proportions of cause and effect. The rebutter's questions across the chapter — how can light touch darkness? does the Second have parts? has the Second fallen by distance? is there a diminution in the Second? is nature born of soul as soul is born of intellect? — each receive the same kind of answer. The category is wrong. The picture has imported body-talk into a rank where bodies cannot reach. The right answer comes through the chain of proportions, not through the image of resemblance.

The political stake of this is real and it sits one layer below the metaphysics. If the ranks of the call are linked to their principles by *likeness*, then any believer who happens to resemble the Imam in some quality could claim equivalent rank. If the limits are linked to their principles by the *chain of proportions*, then no amount of resemblance closes the gap; the gap is closed only by the office that the rank carries. This is why the author has fought every step of *Chapters One through Three* to refuse the talk of sides, parts, distances, diminutions. The talk of sides is the talk of a substance one could imitate. The talk of proportions is the talk of an office one cannot. The speaker-prophet is the speaker-prophet by the office; the friend of God is the friend of God by the office; the Imam is the Imam by the office. None of these are similarities one could acquire. They are the proportions through which the originating power runs.

The closing prayer of *Chapter Two* — *that the ranks of the limits be kept clear, so that there be no confusion about the matter of perceiving things in their realities; for such confusion would lead to the corruption of the essence of souls* — applies again here. To confuse the Soul with the spirit, the Second with the First, the trace with the birth, is not an academic mistake. It corrupts the souls that hold the confusion, because it leaves them unable to distinguish what they can and cannot receive from each rank.

## What comes next

*Chapter Four* of this book will take the next step in the same architecture. If the Soul is what stands between the First and the substrate, and if the natural composite is prime matter informed by the Soul's power, then the partial souls — the human souls that inhabit the natural composite — what are they? Are they parts of the First Truths, as some of the older masters supposed? Or are they only traces of the Second, the way prime matter is the trace of the Second? The eight sub-chapters of *Chapter Four* settle that question with the same kind of move our author has used here. The categories of part-and-whole are body-categories; the partial souls are not parts of any higher rank; they are something subtler — a trace of a trace, an impression at the lowest rank carrying the deposit of the chain that runs above it. The formula remains exact. Perfect in self; deficient only in addition. Each rank is what it is in its place. The chain holds because the power runs through it, not because the ranks resemble each other.

And before that next chapter opens, *Chapter Three* leaves the reader with the image it has earned: the First and the Second standing together as the mineral of honor, with no ignorance or darkness between them, and below them, the substrate, the trace, the essence of this world, receiving the impression of a power that does not resemble it. The light and the darkness do not need to look alike to be in contact. The contact is the work of the power that runs from one through the other to what stands below. That is the doctrine. That is what the chapter exists to settle. And it is settled.
