## The place of this section in the letter

This is the last stretch of *Ayyuhal Walad*. Ghazali has spent the bulk of the epistle on diagnosis, then on the discipline of the spiritual path, then on warnings against four corruptions. He now closes the letter by answering the two practical requests the student made at the very beginning. The student had asked, in addition to the question about which knowledge would save him, two specific things. First, a supplication he could always recite. Second, a piece of advice he could always act on. Most of the letter has been the larger answer to the larger question. The final movement of the letter is the answer to the two small requests.

This matters for the hosts. The four matters and the closing du'a are not a tacked-on appendix. They are the redemption of a promise. The student gets, in the closing pages, exactly what he asked for at the opening.

## What du'a actually is

*Du'a* (du-AAH) is most often translated as *supplication*. The translation is true as far as it goes. But the word in classical Islamic vocabulary carries a freight the English word does not. The root *d-a-w* means *to call*, *to invoke*, *to address*. *Du'a* is the act of standing before Allah and speaking to Him. It is not, in its primary sense, a list of wants delivered to a vending-machine deity. It is communion, expressed in the form of address.

The closing du'a Ghazali offers the student reflects this clearly. The supplication does begin with petition: *I desire from You the completion of Your blessings, the most beneficial knowledge, the most plentiful sustenance*. But it shifts. It moves from asking for things into asking for protection. Then it moves from protection into refusal: *liberate us from the fire of Hell, from loans, from oppression*. Then it dissolves into pure address: *O You the Victorious One, O You the Forgiving One, O You the Generous One*. Eight divine names in sequence. *O Allah, O Allah, O Allah*. The petition has emptied out into invocation.

This is what du'a does when it is allowed to run its full length. It begins as request and ends as recognition. The hosts should not flatten this into *prayer of asking*.

## How this differs from Western supplicatory prayer

A Western listener may have a picture of supplicatory prayer formed by hymns, by the Lord's Prayer, by the petition prayers of liturgical Christianity. The picture is not wrong, but it is partial. In much of the Western devotional tradition, the supplication is directed to a Father, and the relationship being expressed is that of child to parent. *Our Father, who art in heaven*. The prayer reaches up.

In Islamic supplication, the relationship is structurally different. The supplicant is *abd* (servant or slave). Allah is *Rabb* (Lord, owner, sustainer). The names Ghazali stacks at the end of the du'a (*Ya Aziz, Ya Ghaffar, Ya Kareem, Ya Sattar, Ya Haleem, Ya Jabbar, Ya Azeem, Ya Qahhar*) are not familial. They are the names of sovereignty, of mercy, of judgment, of power. The du'a does not climb up toward a parent. It stands beside the slave and faces the Owner. The intimacy is real but the form is different. This is part of why the supplication can shift from petition into pure naming without losing itself; the act of naming the Sovereign is itself a form of worship.

For listeners who come from Christian or Jewish backgrounds, the comparison can be useful as long as it is held lightly. The Psalms also shift from petition into pure address; the structure is not unique to Islam. But the relationship the prayer expresses, and the names invoked, are distinctively Islamic, and the hosts should not collapse them into a generic *prayer of asking*.

## The genre of advice-for-daily-life in Ghazali

The four matters belong to a recognizable form in Ghazali's writing. He repeatedly compresses the demands of religious life into short numbered lists of practical injunctions. The four conditions of the seeker, opened earlier in this letter. The eight benefits of Hatim ibn Ism, narrated in the middle of the letter. The four cautions, in the previous section. Now the four matters.

The reason for the form is pedagogical. Ghazali knows the student is at the edge of paralysis. He has too much knowledge, not enough action. A long discursive treatise will make this worse. A short numbered list gives the student something he can carry in his pocket. *Today, four things to do.* The lists are scaffolding. The reader is not meant to absorb the lists as theory; he is meant to live them.

The four matters of this section have an additional feature. They are not arranged from the inward to the outward. They include both. *Relating to God* and *not stockpiling food* sit in the same list because, in Ghazali's vision, the heart and the pantry are not separate provinces. The same posture of *Tawakkul* (ta-WAK-kul; reliance on Allah) governs both.

## The food rule and Tawakkul

The fourth matter, *do not accumulate more than one year's provision for your family*, will read as eccentric to a modern listener. Two things to know.

First, the rule is grounded in the Prophet's own practice. Ghazali is not improvising. He is reporting that the Prophet stockpiled one year's provision for some of his wives, and used the du'a *Allahumma Aj'al Quta Aale Muhammadin Kafafun* (O my Allah, make this provision sufficient for the family of Muhammad) over them. The cap of one year is the maximum Ghazali finds in the Prophet's practice and so he transmits it as the maximum the student should observe.

Second, the cap is graded by spiritual condition. Ghazali notes that the Prophet did not stockpile for all his wives. He stockpiled for those whose *Tawakkul* was weak. The wives of strong *Tawakkul* (the example named is Aisha Siddiqa, the youngest wife of the Prophet, a major scholar of the early community in her own right) had nothing stored, not even a single day's worth. The ceiling for the weak is the floor for the strong. The rule is not arbitrary. It is calibrated to where the soul actually stands. The point is *Tawakkul*; the food rule is the visible measure.

Hosts should not laugh this off. Ghazali takes the pantry as seriously as the prayer mat.

## The connection to the student's original ask

The student wrote to Ghazali asking, in part, for *a supplication he could always recite, and a piece of advice he could always act on*. (Episode 1 of this series, *The Frame and the First Counsel*, covers the original letter in detail.)

The architecture of *Ayyuhal Walad* is such that the larger answer to the larger question (which of all my learning will matter at death) unfolds across the bulk of the letter. But the two small requests are kept open until the end and answered together at the close. The four matters are *advice he could always act on*. They are short, numbered, and operative on any given day. The closing du'a is *a supplication he could always recite*. Ghazali says explicitly: recite it after every *Salaat* (formal prayer). The two halves of the student's request are answered in the same final stretch of the epistle.

The hosts should make this explicit at some point. The episode is not just *the closing material*. It is the redemption of a request the student made at the opening and that the letter has carried, unanswered, for thousands of words.

## What it means that the letter ends

Ghazali writes: *In this book, I have answered all of your questions. Now you should gather courage and act on all of them and do not forget me in your supplications.* The teacher closes the conversation. He does not promise another letter. He does not invite further correspondence. He says: act. The closing line of the letter is praise, not instruction.

For the student, this is the end of the relationship as it has existed. The teacher has spoken. What remains is the doing. The episode (and the series) ends in the same posture. The hosts should not produce a synthesis. They should let the letter end.
