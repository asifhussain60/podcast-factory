What I Wish Babu Taught Me
Last updated: April 14, 2026
Purpose: Clinical sign synthesis for ADHD, Dyslexia, Depression, Anxiety, and Sleeplessness,
mapped to memoir characters and incidents for narrative weaving into chapters.

============================================================
HOW TO USE THIS LIBRARY
============================================================

This library has THREE sections:

  SECTION A — CONDITION PROFILES
    Clinical signs for each of the five conditions, synthesized from CDC, NIMH,
    Mayo Clinic, Cleveland Clinic, Yale Dyslexia Center, Sleep Foundation, and
    peer-reviewed research (PMC/PNAS). Each profile lists observable signs that
    a memoirist can describe WITHOUT clinical jargon (memoir rule: no therapy
    language). The signs are grouped by age bracket to match the memoir's timeline.

  SECTION B — CHARACTER PSYCHOLOGICAL MAPS
    Every named character in the memoir, mapped against the five conditions.
    Each mapping cites specific memoir incidents as evidence and assigns a
    confidence tag: STRONG (multiple converging signs), MODERATE (clear but
    fewer data points), or SPECULATIVE (plausible inference, needs Asif's
    confirmation). These are NOT diagnoses. They are narrative lenses.

  SECTION C — WEAVING GUIDE
    Condition-to-chapter mapping showing where each psychological thread
    could deepen an existing scene or Babu's Advice section.

RULES:
  - This library is a NARRATIVE TOOL, not a clinical record.
  - Never use clinical labels in chapter prose (memoir voice rules apply).
  - Describe the BEHAVIOR and its EFFECT, not the diagnosis.
  - Conditions are lenses for deepening character, not accusations.
  - Cross-reference using INC-### and chapter tags from incident-bank.md.
  - Once-only placement: each psychological insight woven into ONE chapter.

[COND-001] ADHD (Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder)
Source: CDC, NIMH, Mayo Clinic, Cleveland Clinic

  CHILDHOOD SIGNS:
    - Difficulty sustaining attention on tasks not intrinsically stimulating
    - Easily distracted by extraneous stimuli or unrelated thoughts
    - Frequently loses things needed for tasks (books, tools, assignments)
    - Difficulty organizing tasks and activities; avoids sustained mental effort
    - Fidgeting, squirming, inability to stay seated when expected to
    - Talks excessively; blurts out answers before questions are finished
    - Difficulty waiting turn; interrupts or intrudes on others
    - Acts without thinking through consequences (impulsivity)
    - Shifts from one uncompleted activity to another
    - Appears not to listen when spoken to directly

  ADOLESCENT/ADULT SIGNS:
    - Restlessness rather than overt hyperactivity (internal motor)
    - Low frustration tolerance; irritability under stress
    - Difficulty following through on instructions or finishing tasks
    - Chronic procrastination paired with bursts of hyperfocus
    - Impulsive decisions in relationships, spending, career
    - Frequent or intense mood changes; emotional dysregulation
    - Difficulty with time management and meeting deadlines
    - Sensation-seeking or risk-taking behavior
    - Pattern of starting many projects, completing few
    - Poor working memory (forgets conversations, appointments)

  COMORBID PATTERNS:
    - Often co-occurs with anxiety, depression, sleep disorders
    - Emotional dysregulation frequently mistaken for mood disorder
    - Academic underperformance relative to intelligence (common overlap with dyslexia)

---

[COND-002] DYSLEXIA
Source: Yale Dyslexia Center, Mayo Clinic, Cleveland Clinic, Reading Rockets

  CHILDHOOD SIGNS:
    - Trouble learning nursery rhymes or playing rhyming games
    - Difficulty learning and remembering letters of the alphabet
    - Confuses letters that look similar (b/d, p/q)
    - Very slow in acquiring reading skills; reading errors disconnected from letters on page
    - Difficulty sounding out unfamiliar words (phonological processing deficit)
    - Persistent "baby talk"; mispronounces familiar words
    - Trouble remembering sequences (days of week, alphabet in order)
    - Avoids reading aloud; becomes anxious when asked to read
    - Discrepancy between verbal ability and written performance

  ADOLESCENT/ADULT SIGNS:
    - Slow, laborious reading; avoids reading for pleasure
    - Frequent "tip of the tongue" moments; imprecise vocabulary
    - Mispronounces names of people and places
    - Difficulty with written expression despite strong verbal ideas
    - Relies on memorization rather than comprehension strategies
    - General anxiety around reading, writing, or timed academic tasks
    - High IQ yet tests poorly in academic settings
    - Poor self-esteem related to academic struggles
    - Often pronounces words incorrectly or uses "um" as filler
    - Strong in visual-spatial reasoning, creativity, or oral storytelling

  COMORBID PATTERNS:
    - Frequently co-occurs with ADHD (estimated 30-50% overlap)
    - Shame and anxiety from academic struggles can trigger depression
    - Compensatory behaviors (memorization, avoidance) mask the condition

---

[COND-003] DEPRESSION (including Trauma-Linked Chronic Depression)
Source: NIMH, PMC studies on childhood trauma and depression, Psychology Today

  CHILDHOOD/ADOLESCENT SIGNS:
    - Persistent sadness, irritability, or emotional flatness
    - Withdrawal from friends, family, or activities previously enjoyed
    - Changes in appetite (over- or under-eating)
    - Sleep disturbances (insomnia or hypersomnia)
    - Difficulty concentrating; decline in academic performance
    - Feelings of worthlessness or excessive guilt
    - Frequent physical complaints (headaches, stomachaches) without clear cause
    - Self-harm behaviors or preoccupation with death
    - Anger outbursts disproportionate to the situation

  ADULT SIGNS (especially trauma-linked):
    - Persistent low mood or emotional numbness for weeks or longer
    - Loss of interest or pleasure in most activities (anhedonia)
    - Fatigue or low energy despite adequate rest
    - Difficulty making decisions; cognitive fog
    - Social withdrawal; isolation even from trusted people
    - Negative self-talk; belief of being fundamentally broken or unlovable
    - Emotional dysregulation: sudden anger, crying spells, irritability
    - Chronic feelings of emptiness or meaninglessness
    - Weight gain or loss unrelated to intentional changes
    - Suicidal ideation or passive death wish ("I wish I didn't exist")

  TRAUMA-SPECIFIC PATTERNS:
    - 75.6% of chronically depressed patients report significant childhood trauma
    - Emotional abuse especially linked to cognitive distortions (self-blame, worthlessness)
    - Sexual abuse linked to higher severity and treatment resistance
    - Multiple trauma types = significantly more severe depressive symptoms
    - Childhood emotional abuse uniquely associated with cognitive depression
    - Sleepwalking, sleep talking, nightmares, and night terrors common

[COND-004] ANXIETY (including Generalized, Social, and Trauma-Related)
Source: NIMH, PMC studies on childhood maltreatment and anxiety, Psych Central

  CHILDHOOD SIGNS:
    - Excessive worry about school performance, family, or safety
    - Physical symptoms: stomachaches, headaches, muscle tension before events
    - Avoidance of social situations or new experiences
    - Clinginess; difficulty separating from caregivers
    - Perfectionism driven by fear of punishment or failure
    - Hypervigilance: constantly scanning for signs of adult displeasure
    - People-pleasing behavior to prevent conflict or punishment
    - Difficulty sleeping due to racing or intrusive thoughts
    - Freezing or going blank when confronted or challenged
    - Need for constant reassurance

  ADULT SIGNS:
    - Persistent, excessive worry difficult to control
    - Restlessness; feeling keyed up or on edge
    - Fatigue from chronic mental tension
    - Difficulty concentrating; mind going blank
    - Muscle tension, jaw clenching, headaches
    - Sleep disturbance (difficulty falling or staying asleep)
    - Irritability, especially under perceived scrutiny
    - Avoidance of situations that trigger fear of judgment
    - Overanalyzing others' reactions and moods (hypervigilance)
    - Catastrophic thinking: small events spiral into worst-case scenarios

  TRAUMA-SPECIFIC PATTERNS:
    - Childhood emotional abuse and neglect strongly linked to social anxiety
    - Hyper-vigilance toward parent's moods in childhood becomes generalized scanning in adulthood
    - Child trained by volatile parent becomes adult who overanalyzes every social cue
    - Childhood maltreatment linked to greater severity, reduced quality of life
    - Emotional abuse uniquely differentiates anxiety from other conditions
    - Physical abuse linked to panic disorder and specific phobias

---

[COND-005] SLEEPLESSNESS (Insomnia and Trauma-Related Sleep Disorders)
Source: Sleep Foundation, PMC, VA PTSD Center, Frontiers in Sleep

  GENERAL SIGNS:
    - Difficulty falling asleep (onset insomnia)
    - Difficulty staying asleep; frequent waking (maintenance insomnia)
    - Waking earlier than intended and unable to return to sleep
    - Non-restorative sleep: feeling unrefreshed despite hours in bed
    - Daytime fatigue, irritability, difficulty concentrating
    - Increased arm and leg movements during sleep (restless sleep)
    - Sleep talking, sleepwalking, or other parasomnias
    - Reliance on substances (alcohol, medication) to fall asleep

  TRAUMA-RELATED SIGNS:
    - Hyperarousal: feeling the need to stay alert even during sleep
    - Nightmares, often replaying or echoing traumatic events
    - Night sweats; dysphoric dreams without clear narrative
    - Startling easily at noise during sleep
    - Fear of sleep itself (associating bed with vulnerability)
    - Disruptive nocturnal behaviors: kicking, screaming, dream enactment
    - 80-90% of PTSD patients experience insomnia symptoms
    - 50-70% of PTSD patients experience nightmares
    - Sleep disturbance often FIRST symptom to appear after trauma
    - Chronic insomnia worsens depression, anxiety, and emotional regulation

  COMORBID PATTERNS:
    - Bidirectional with depression and anxiety (each worsens the other)
    - ADHD-related racing thoughts intensify onset insomnia
    - Sleepwalking specifically linked to childhood trauma and stress
