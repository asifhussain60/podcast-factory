
Babu, Tell Me What Love Really Is

I did not grow up understanding what love was. I grew up understanding what it cost. In our home, what passed for love was built out of obedience, duty, fear, and performance. "This is for your own good." My mother could stretch that sentence over the damage that took decades to measure. For me, love arrived in the same voice as control. I did not learn tenderness. I learned to brace.

It was not all bad. My first memories of closeness came from my Afifa Khala, the second-youngest of my mother's sisters. She was the closest mother figure I had growing up. She showered me with love, care, and tenderness. She read me stories. She played games with me. When I was sick, she put my head in her lap and massaged my head until I fell asleep. I remember I used to enjoy that experience so much that I would deliberately not take my medicine and remain sick so I could enjoy her love longer. Her love flowed freely. She was always present, but most importantly, I felt safe around her in a way I never did at home.

Annu Khala, the youngest of my mother's sisters, lived in the US and often visited Karachi every few years, and each visit felt like a small season of warmth arriving. She came bearing gifts for everyone, but what I remember most is not what she brought. It is the way she was with me. The same love and affection Afifa Khala gave, she gave just as freely, without condition, without performance. Even though she was visiting the whole family, she always made me feel special. Took the time to listen to me and always wished me the best. Between them, I had two mother figures who showed me what tenderness was supposed to feel like before life taught me how rare it actually was.

The rest of my education on love came from home, and over time, I built a little damaging story in my head that went something like this. Confusion leads to guilt. Curiosity is an offense. Affection can be withdrawn at any moment without warning and restored only after panic, apology, and surrender. I learned to read moods and facial expressions long before I learned to trust words.
One of my earliest memories is one I still cannot fully explain. I was very young, maybe seven. Something happened. I do not remember the details, but I remember the frustration. I remember walking back into my parents' bedroom, pressing a pillow over my face, and screaming into it with everything I had. Now I understand what rage feels like when it has nowhere to go. When it cannot find a target below, it goes upward. I remember looking at the ceiling, crying and cursing God for creating me. If He exists and He created me, was He not supposed to protect me?

Years later, when I began studying faith seriously, suffering was finally reframed in a way that made sense. God tests not to destroy, but to shape what needs shaping. Not punishment disguised as love, but love expressed through difficulty, the way gold is not ruined by fire but purified by it. I do not say that to excuse what was done to me, or what I did to my mother. I say it because without that reframe, the pillow and the ceiling and the screaming would have nowhere to land except bitterness. And bitterness, as I eventually learned, is a dead-end road with no view and terrible company.
I must have been in fourth or fifth grade when a boy at school told a dirty joke that everyone else seemed to understand. I did not. The joke went something like this: a servant boy was told to wash a woman's clothes, and while doing it, he accidentally tore her bra. She had also told him not to drink the milk in the fridge she had left for herself, but he drank it anyway. When her husband came home and asked why the boy was crying, he said, "Because I ripped your wife's bra and drank her milk."

Everyone laughed. I did not get it. I think I knew what a bra was, but I did not make that connection. In my defense, I was nine in a highly conservative country like Kuwait.

Anyway, I brought the joke home and repeated it to my mother verbatim. Before I could even finish, she slapped me across the face. Then she called my father, and together they turned my confusion into an offense. I remember standing there, getting hit and yelled at, trying to work out what crime I had committed. Was I in trouble because the joke was dirty, or because I did not understand it? In our house, those were not mutually exclusive.

Then something else happened. Around the same age, we were returning from a Moharram majlis when Amma saw a fat cat and said, Lagta hai, hamila hai. Looks like she is pregnant. I was absurdly proud of myself for knowing what hamila meant. Pregnant. I expected praise. Instead, she said it was because of the bad company I kept. In my head, the equation probably landed like this:

Fat Cat + Hamila + Pregnant = Bad Company ==> WTF?

That was the education I was getting. Innocence was suspicious. Curiosity was dangerous. Not knowing was bad, but knowing was somehow worse. Ali (AS, peace be upon him) said people are enemies of what they do not know. My parents, apparently, took that as a daily operating manual.
But curiosity was only one of the weapons turned against me. The deeper arsenal was emotional.

My mother dominated through her voice. When yelling failed, she turned to tears and guilt. "Look what you are doing to me." She had phrases she kept loaded and ready, and she used them in rotation. "You are the punishment of my sins." "One day, you will have a horrible child like yourself to teach you a lesson." These were not rare outbursts. They were regular weapons, pulled out the way other mothers might pull out a wooden spoon. Except that a spoon leaves a mark that fades. These settled into my heart and mind and quietly redecorated the whole place.

Then came the lessons that still live in my body. I must have been ten or twelve. All I remember is that I was giving my mother a hard time over homework. My mother cared about grades and coming first, never about learning or education. In those days, we were expected to memorize textbooks like parrots and regurgitate them on the exam paper. Silly things like comprehension, curiosity, or expanding one's mind were apparently unacceptable luxuries. The point was not to understand the material. The point was to become a photocopier with anxiety.

Anyway, I must have done something that pushed Amma into one of her rages - I was giving her a hard time doing my homework, I think. She locked herself in the bedroom and left me outside. Her room had two entrances, and I remember running between both doors, banging, crying, begging her to forgive me, promising I would never do it again. She eventually opened the door hours later and claimed she had fainted.

What stays with me is not just the punishment. It is the panic, followed by the guilt of disturbing my mother so badly that she had apparently passed out. What a horrible son I am. That feeling took hold early: love had gone missing, and I was expected to earn it back by collapsing first.

The silent treatment became one of her favorite tools after that. Sometimes it went on for days. She would not speak to me directly. She spoke through my father, who, as usual, supported her and did nothing to interrupt the whole exercise. All this was supposed to teach me a lesson. But what lesson? Was it that authority could shut you out without explanation? Or was it that affection could be suspended until you crawled back into line? That if the person holding power over you was displeased, your job was not to understand but to beg?
Around that same time, I had gotten my hands on a book on how to build a magic set and put on a magic show. I do remember I spent weeks building it. It was meticulous. It was mine. I was proud of it!

Then I brought home bad grades. Amma went into her usual rage, and Baba, right on cue, picked up my weeks of work and ripped it apart in front of me. This is why you get bad grades, he said. It felt like he ripped my heart out of my chest and tore that up, too.

I burst into tears. Not the quiet kind. The loud, uncontrollable kind that comes from a place deeper than the thing that was just destroyed. I remember my mother turning to my father and saying, why did you have to tear his work. As if she had not lit the match herself. As if the rage that put the kit in his hands had come from nowhere. That was the other lesson: the person who starts the fire and the person who puts it out can be the same person, and somehow, both versions expect your gratitude.
I also remember somewhere around that same period, she took me to a therapist, presumably to fix me, the way you take a clock to a repairman because the ticking annoys you. I remember the therapist was a kind man. He asked me questions in a warm, playful way, and I was only too happy to answer. I do not remember what he asked or what I said, but I must have said something I was not supposed to say.

When my parents came back in, I was made to sit on a red sofa facing them while the therapist sat across from us at a table. One sentence has stayed with me all these years.

"Dr. Sahab, is ke saath kya problem hai?" (Doctor, what is the problem with him?)

Then came his reply, which landed in the room like a slap before the real ones ever came.

"You," he said to my mother. "You are the problem."

What I felt was not relief. Not vindication. Fear. My mother was angry, offended, and probably embarrassed.

"Is this why we are paying you so much money? To hear this?"

My heart stopped. I could see clearly what was about to happen, not as a vision, but as a feeling in my bones.

She did not disappoint. The journey home and the days afterward became yet another sermon on all they had done for me, all the feeding and clothing and raising, and yet here I was, the ungrateful one, continuing the familiar cycle of begging and pleading for forgiveness without having the faintest idea what I had done wrong. This was one of the deepest lessons of my childhood: even truth, if spoken aloud in the wrong room, could become a crime.
By ninth grade, the pattern had become almost elegant in its cruelty. Waiting for my school bus, I found a pack of Marlboro cigarettes outside our Kuwait apartment building and, in one of my brief and idiotic attempts at moral heroism, started breaking them one by one because I had been told smoking was bad. My father came home from work, saw me with the pack, assumed they were mine, and started beating me without asking a single question. I kept telling him they were not mine. He did not believe me. Or perhaps belief was never the point. Authority had seen enough. Verdict delivered. Sentence immediate.

And hanging over all of it was something he said as he was disciplining me: "If I ever catch you smoking, I'll break your hands."

I think that threat snapped whatever restraint was left inside me. When I got to school that day, I bummed a cigarette off a friend and had my first smoke. What a twisted curriculum fear produces. I had already been punished for the crime, so I may as well commit it. If innocence and guilt are to receive the same treatment, the distinction loses its practical value.

By then, I was older, stronger, and no longer as easy to break in the old ways. I think I was in my first year of college when Amma stopped talking to me as part of one of her usual mind games, and this time, I did not chase. I ignored her, too. Not casually. Deliberately. We went on like that for nearly two months. Then one day, my father, probably on the instigation of my mother, took time off work, came home early with a cane, and beat the hell out of me for ignoring my mother. I stood there in my uniform and took it, staring him in the eye the whole time without flinching, without reacting. "Do your best," I think I said in my head. I stood like a stone, never breaking gaze. I was not going to flinch, beg, or ask him to stop. He finally did.

At that moment, I saw something shift in his eyes. The anger left them, and grief entered. He had not broken me. I broke him. And we both knew it. I did not feel proud. I felt sad for him. I still do not fully understand why. Maybe it was the first time I understood that what I had always taken for authority was really just fear holding a cane.

Afterward, I still went to college. On the way back, I stopped at Afifa Khala's house and showed her what he had done. I was black and blue. She cried. She took care of me. She put balm on my wounds and went off on my mother for beating me like an animal. And then, the very next day, she turned on me. Suddenly, I was the ungrateful one.

Years later, she told me what had happened. Khala herself had long been one of my mother's victims, always under threat of losing support or being abandoned if she did not fall in line. After she defended me that night, my mother called her and did what she did best. She accused Khala of turning me against her. Guilt. Pressure. The old weapons, now aimed at the one person who had dared to take my side. By the next morning, Khala had been brought to heel. So when I came back and complained again, Khala turned on me. Not because she wanted to. Because she had been made to.

That was the price of softness in my mother's orbit. It could exist, but only as long as it did not threaten her authority. Then it was either crushed or recruited.
That was the emotional grammar. Some of those moments I remember in perfect detail. Others I have tried to bury. But my mind keeps returning to what the heart has never been able to let go of. The two locked doors. The red sofa. The cane. The cigarettes on the ground. These are not memories I chose to keep. They are the ones who chose to stay.

I did not grow up asking, "Am I safe here?" I grew up learning to read moods, anticipate punishment, and survive the emotional weather of whoever held power. Truth was no longer the incident or the fact. It was whatever got me out of a beating. My younger brother grew up in the same weather, the same grammar. But where I learned to brace against it, Atif stopped bracing altogether. He just let it in.

I felt empty, but maybe that was the design. A person who feels full does not go searching. A person who feels rich does not beg. Maybe the emptiness I carried was not some flaw in the system. It was the thing that eventually sent me looking for what I did not even have names for yet.

Years later, I understood its mechanics more clearly. A starving person clings to any sign of food. A prisoner sees any bond not just as love, but as refuge. The intensity of our clinging depends on how empty or trapped we feel inside. That explained a great deal about me and the patterns I repeated for years before I understood them. It also explained Atif. I clung. He stopped reaching. A starving person who gives up on food does not become free. He just becomes quieter about the hunger.

The need for softness went deeper than I could name. Somewhere during my college years in Karachi, I very selectively started telling people I had cancer. I did not think about where the lie would lead. I did not plan it. I just knew that when I said it, people softened toward me, and that softness was the closest thing to love I could manufacture on my own.

Eventually, of course, I got caught. And the ridicule that followed was exactly the kind of curriculum I deserved. Not because lying is uncommon, but because the lie itself exposed the size of the hole I was trying to fill with other people's pity. A starving person will eat what is not food. I had become the kind of person who manufactures the very thing he is starving for, and then tries to pass it off as real.

I did not understand it then, but that humiliation was one of my better teachers. My failures taught me what no amount of borrowed sympathy ever could. The moment I stopped trying to be pitied was the moment I started, slowly, to become a student of my own damage instead of a victim of it.
But some people broke through the grammar without even knowing it existed.

I have a female cousin, a few years older than me, who showed me a kind of love I had no framework for. She was warm, nurturing, gentle in her touch, soft-spoken, easy in her laughter. I watched the way she loved her brothers, and something in me burned. Not anger. Jealousy. The way she held them, spoke to them, laughed with them, and played with them was everything I had wanted and never received.

At the time, I thought I wanted a sister. What I really wanted was tenderness, love, and care. So I tried to take their place. I mimicked the way they acted around her, hoping she would return the same tenderness to me. She did, but it never worked the same way. What came naturally to them required me to perform, and she could probably feel the difference even if I could not.

Whenever my mother spoke against her, I rose to her defense with a fierceness that confused everyone in the room, including her. I did not fully understand it myself. I only knew that something in me had decided she was worth protecting, and I did not care who disagreed. Maybe this was Bollywood scenarios playing in the head.

I must have been around twenty when she fell sick during one of our visits to Pakistan. It was just a common flu, the sort that passes through every household like a bored relative who overstays two days and leaves behind a cough. But it disturbed me in a way I could not explain. I do not remember exactly what I did, but I remember trying to make it better. Sounds funny now. A man rattled by a flu that was not even his business to worry about. That should have told me something.

Then came her engagement ceremony. Her brother pulled me aside during a conversation I do not remember and said she had warned him to be cautious around me, claiming I was dangerous. I did not know the context of their conversation, or what she meant, or what had triggered it. To me, it felt like another betrayal. After all the love, affection, and care I have for her, after fighting with my parents for her, oh, I also stole money from my dad to buy her a perfume she wanted. After all that, how could she say that? How could she call her protector dangerous?

And that is the thing about the stories we tell ourselves. We are all the stars of our own insane films. Everyone else is a supporting actor whose job is to keep our plot moving. What we forget is that we are the only ones writing, directing, and rewatching this thing on repeat. Nobody else bought a ticket, but we behave as if they did. That is why we become angry while they stand there confused. They never auditioned for the role we cast them in.

What I did not have a word for then, but found one for later, is this: mercy. Mercy in its truest sense is not simply refraining from cruelty. It shelters. It nourishes. It carries what is too young or too damaged to carry itself. That is what both Khalas gave me in fragments when I was small, and what I never found in full at home. That is what I felt in the tenderness of my cousin's affections. She did not know what she meant to me. She was simply being herself, loving her brothers, living her life, and I was the one who turned it into something it was never meant to be. That was never her fault. I am grateful for those very precious memories at a time when I badly needed them. They were among the few who taught me what tenderness looked like before I had another name for it.
Sadly, there was one thing left that could have been a refuge. They turned that into a weapon, too: religion.

As far as I can remember, religion in my home was used not as guidance but as leverage. The Quran and hadith were conveniently selected to serve my mother's authority. Paradise lies under the feet of your mother. If your parents are unhappy with you, you will not see paradise. These were not offered as invitations to mercy. They were used as weapons of guilt and shame, pulled out whenever obedience was questioned, whenever I pushed back, whenever I tried to say that something felt wrong. The message was clear: to disobey your parents is to disobey God. To question them is to question Him. Convenient arrangement, really, if your goal is absolute control with divine customer support.

That framing twisted my already broken understanding of love. Love became obedience. Obedience became survival. And God became the enforcer standing behind my parents' anger. No wonder I gave up on Him. It took me decades to untangle that. I had to learn the hard way that growing in religion is supposed to make a person more compassionate, not more judgmental. A heart truly filled with God's love and mercy softens. It does not harden. And if someone's faith is making them harsher, more self-righteous, and more eager to condemn, then perhaps it is not God filling that heart at all. Perhaps it is the ego, dressed in piety, congratulating itself.

What took me even longer to untangle was the difference between remorse and shame. They wear the same face but lead in opposite directions. Remorse pulls a person back toward God. It says, I can do better, and it moves. Shame says, I am too broken to try, and it paralyzes. My mother's arsenal was shame. Every word she loaded and fired was designed (unintentionally, I’m sure) to make me feel defective, not temporarily wrong. Self-hatred dressed as guilt is not devotion. It is not piety. It is the opposite of it. It took me years to learn that God does not ask us to crawl. He asks us to get back up.

Years later, when I finally read the full ayah about parents, I found something very different from what I had been taught. The Quran says to speak to them with a noble word, lower the wing of humility before them out of mercy, and pray for them. It says mercy. It says humility. It says prayer. It does not say surrender your mind. It does not say accept cruelty and call it love. They used the verse to silence me. But the verse itself was asking for something far more beautiful, and far more difficult, than blind submission.
To understand my mother honestly, though, I also have to say what came before me. She came from poverty, overcrowding, pressure, and early responsibility. My Nana (her dad) was a deeply religious, traditional, strict man, rigid in faith and temperament. My mother was one of nine siblings. Apparently, he had wanted twelve, but my Nani had three miscarriages and then suffered a stroke. Between the two grandfathers in this family, having children appears to have been treated like a male hobby with female consequences.

After my Nani's stroke, the older children had to become adults before they had finished being children. My mother was one of them. She was highly intelligent and could have excelled in any field she wanted. She had the mind for it. She was good with words, tried her hand at poetry, cared about beauty, and had a gift for home decor. I remember watching her play Scrabble with Khala, and even as a child, I could tell she was not a small mind. But a sharp mind trapped in duty does not always become wise. In her case, it became bitter. She had to become useful early. Responsible. Dependable. Hard. That would do something to anyone.

I have one memory of her that I sometimes return to. Baba had bought me a paint-by-number kit, and somewhere in the middle of it, I ran out of colors. I did not know how to fix that. She sat with me and rebuilt all the shades from what was there. She did not make a production of it. She just worked alongside me quietly until the palette was whole again. I do not have many memories like that.

It is not that I did not have good moments growing up. I did not get to where I am without their raising me, sacrificing for me. Now that I have children of my own, I see things I could not see then. The way she stayed up when I was sick, not because she expected thanks, but because that is what mothers do. The way she stretched a budget across a household without ever letting us feel the strain. The speed with which she could read a room and know exactly what someone needed. These were not small things. I did not appreciate them then because I was too busy surviving the other parts. But I see them now, and I owe her the honesty of saying so.

So she did love, but unevenly. She took pride in my intelligence and achievements, though often in ways tangled up with her own social mileage. She wrote my speeches and essays so I could win, and so she could enjoy the reflected praise of having a brilliant son. I was loved, but often in a way that felt half-claimed. She was strong, competent, perceptive, and also deeply afraid of disrespect, loss of control, social shame, insecurity, and impatience. Understanding that helps me tell the truth about her with some mercy. It does not require me to lie.
Years later, when I came to the United States, the story took a turn I would not have predicted. By the time I reached America, I had become a complete atheist. The goal, as far as I could tell then, was freedom. Freedom to do whatever I wanted. Good, bad, ugly, halal, haram, all of it. No guilt. No religious noise. No one is telling me what to do. One of the reasons I married my ex was precisely that she was not religious. She was a Christian at heart with a Muslim name, which made the whole arrangement feel, in my mind at least, like a safe little loophole. If I wanted distance from religion, she seemed like a sure bet.

Allah, clearly, has a sense of humor. I ran from religion in a fanatically religious home, crossed an ocean to be free of it, only to be taken deep into it. If irony ever needed a passport, that would have been the trip.

I started studying religion after 9/11. But that was not my only obsession. I was also diving into psychology with a seriousness that probably looked obsessive from the outside. I used to ask Anees, my psychotherapist friend, endless questions about the mind, and she was always generous with what she knew. I studied literature. I took courses like The 7 Habits of Highly Effective People and Landmark Education. I read my way through a small mountain of self-help books, trying to understand the work that needed to be done in me. I was trying to make sense of faith, religion, love, sex, marriage, childhood, damage, all of it. And if I am being honest, the religious studies were probably also an escape from life, a way of climbing into meaning, so I would not have to sit too nakedly with the mess underneath.

And then there was my beloved spiritual teacher, Javeed bhai. I had known of him since Kuwait, but it was not until after 9/11 and my religious journey that I truly came to know him. In an ocean of religious noise and confusion, he was the only voice of reason I trusted. I came at him with questions that were relentless, challenging, and often outright blasphemous. The kind that would have made most religious men flinch, lecture, or show me the nearest exit. He did none of that. He answered every one of them with knowledge, wisdom, and a patience I had not known a human being could carry.
What made Javeed bhai different from every religious figure I had known was that he never once used faith as a weapon. He never shamed me for doubting. He never made me feel defective for asking. Where my childhood had taught me that questioning God was a sin punishable by silence and guilt, Javeed bhai treated my questions as signs of a mind waking up. He met my anger with calm. My sarcasm with substance. My pain with something I can only describe as mercy in human form.

I did not understand it then, but he was slowly undoing the damage my parents had done to my relationship with God. Every question he answered without flinching loosened one more bolt in the cage my childhood had built around faith. Religion, for the first time, stopped feeling like a courtroom and started feeling like a conversation. That shift did not happen overnight. It happened across years of relentless back-and-forth, during which I tried every possible angle to break his patience, and he refused to give me the satisfaction.

He was, in a way I had never experienced from anyone, the mother I never had. That sentence sounds dramatic, but I mean it plainly. The kind of patience he carried, the kind that does not keep score, does not withdraw when tested, does not punish me for being difficult, that is the love I had searched for my entire life without knowing what to call it. It was maternal in its steadiness. Not soft in the weak sense. Soft in the way water is soft, the kind that reshapes stone over time without once raising its voice.

Part of what kept me searching in both directions was that religion and human psychology never felt separate to me. They seemed to illuminate each other. I was investigating religion, theology, and the human mind at the same time, and somewhere in that combined search, something in me began to soften. Javeed bhai's influence was central to that softening. He did not just give me answers. He gave me permission to search honestly, and that permission changed the quality of everything I found.

My faith, or at least the words that came from that faith, had once been rigid in the way frightened people often mistake for devotion. But real devotion, I was beginning to see, does something to a person that fear never could. It makes room. When mercy enters the picture, it does not weaken anything. It clarifies everything.

I remember the first time the verse truly landed in me: Allah's mercy encompasses all things. All things. Not just the obedient. Not just the correct. All things. Even the confused. Even the angry. Even the ones who ran. Once mercy started making sense, a great deal else followed. Even the meaning of the Quranic term "ABD" (slave) began to shift inside me. What had once felt like force, fear, and imposed submission began turning into something else: love, willingness, and conscious surrender. Not slavery as humiliation, but devotion as chosen nearness. That changed more than my faith. It changed how I began to understand love itself.

I once read that God shows us seasons on the earth so we can understand the seasons of our own hearts. If we think our hearts are dead, remember that the One who gives life to dead land can give life to dead hearts. Mine had been winter for a very long time. The thaw, when it came, was not dramatic. It was slow, uneven, and mostly invisible to anyone watching from the outside. But I could feel it. There is pain in this world. But there is also beauty. And sometimes pain is what prepares you to finally see it.

What I was also beginning to understand was that the God I had been shown as a child was missing a dimension. The God of that household was all judgment and no forgiveness, all punishment and no tenderness. What the fuller picture showed me was something more complete: justice and mercy are not opposites in God. They are both intrinsic to Him. A God of only mercy has no weight. A God of only justice has no warmth. Both together meant that neither my failures nor my injuries were the last word on me.
What I was really chasing, though I would not have phrased it so neatly at the time, was a clean definition of love that did not smell like control. Real love, the kind that holds its shape under pressure, is the most powerful force I have ever encountered. Not because it overwhelms everything else. Because it outlasts it. Love built on fear can be intimidated into retreat. Love built on need collapses the moment the need is not met. But love that comes from somewhere deeper than appetite, from choice and conviction and something close to grace, does not flinch. It holds.

The women who corrected my understanding were not the ones who lectured most. They were the ones whose presence made other people feel safe. Afifa Khala and Annu Khala had done that for me in fragments when I was a child, and so did my cousin in her unknowing ways.

Much later, I met my true soulmate, Ishrat, who showed me the fuller picture. She was my closest friend at the time, and long before she stood in the foreground of my life, she was already helping me quietly behind the scenes, like the wind beneath my wings, except less cheesy and much more useful. Some people enter your life like gems. No ulterior motives, no need to prove anything, no masks. They know your flaws and love you anyway. They remind you of God without once preaching. Ishrat was that person. She understood me without me having to explain myself, and after a lifetime of speaking a thousand words and still being misread, that was more healing than anything she could have said.

I want that said plainly because too many accomplishments in a man's life get narrated as if he climbed there alone. I did not. I attribute my present life to the gifts of all those who helped me along the way, especially my parents, who gave me life; I would not have existed without them. Even with the best case of cruelty I could possibly make against them, the fact my mother did not abort me in her womb; the fact that she carried me for nine months and went through childbirth to bring me into this world; for her sleepless nights I will never know of; for her prayers I will never hear; for her worry when I was reckless and did not care, I would certainly and deservedly lose the case in court as I should. For that, I do understand the value the Quran puts on parents. And yet, even with all of that, the scales are not mine to hold. They belong to the One who sees everything I cannot.

There is a thought I keep returning to: He withheld what I loved to teach me about the nature of this life. Then he gave me what I loved, to teach me about the nature of the next. I cannot prove that. But I feel it in my bones. Every good thing that arrived came late and heavy with meaning precisely because of what had been withheld before it. I could not have appreciated the light without having lived so long in the dark.

At some point, I started holding my own Islamic sessions. Not to preach. To reach youngsters like me, people who had grown up in communities where God and Islam had been presented as something cruel, controlling, and punitive. I wanted to show them what I had experienced on the other side of that wall. That faith could be a source of mercy, not fear. I

It was in one of those sessions that I connected with Ishrat. She was going through a very difficult emotional time, dealing with someone's controlling patterns that echoed things I knew too well. In a strange way, we met each other in the rubble. But what she showed me was more important than companionship. She introduced me to a completely different side of love than anything I had known before.

I saw it in the way she dealt with difficult, demanding in-laws. I saw it in the way she carried herself despite having very little emotional support from her husband. And I saw it most clearly in the way she loved her children. Watching her with them taught me something I should have learned much earlier in life: what a mother is supposed to feel like. Not feared. Not unpredictable. Not emotionally punishing. Not someone whose love has to be begged back from behind a closed door. She was patient, warm, emotionally present, and deeply giving in a way that did not feel strategic. It was simply who she was.

I think this is what love does to a person when it is no longer being driven by hunger or fear. When someone loves from a real place, from something they have worked out in themselves rather than something they are trying to fill in themselves, they become safer for everyone around them. Not just for the people they choose. For anyone who passes through. That kind of love is a mercy in the world, not just a feeling inside it.

And she did not keep that love reserved for her children. She turned that same tenderness toward me, too. First as a friend, then as a companion, and eventually as something deeper, my soulmate. She gave me care without humiliation. Warmth without mind games. Closeness without panic.

Her patience with me deserves its own place, because without it, the truth becomes dishonest. When she met me, I was pushing 265 pounds and rushing toward a heart attack with the stubbornness of a man who had half given up on himself. Not dramatically. Just quietly, the way a person stops trying without sending out a memo.

She helped me lose the weight. Not once. Not cleanly. I failed numerous times along the way, and she watched each failure without using it against me. Part of me was waiting for her to get tired of me, give up on me, and walk away, just as everyone else had. That was the pattern I knew. Love has a limit. Patience has an expiry date. But she never did. She stayed patient. Constant. Consistent.

She kept helping me until I became ashamed of myself and finally did what needed to be done. She saved my life in more ways than one. Without her, I honestly do not know where I would be today. Nothing worth having has come to me without a fight. Every good thing in my life has required walking through something painful first. And Ishrat, the best thing, required the longest walk of all.

That changed me. Not all at once, but day by day, piece by piece. Her love proved to me that fear and control were not normal forms of love. Once someone kept showing up, kept helping, kept refusing to weaponize my weakness against me, the old lies began to lose their authority.

One pattern that struck me was that Ishrat's children absolutely adore her, just as Afifa Khala's daughters do. Forget adore, I did not even like my parents. So then what did these women do? They obviously did something right. Children do not give that kind of love by accident. They give it to the person in whose presence they feel safe, seen, soothed, and emotionally at ease. That fact taught me more than a thousand moral speeches ever did.

So what did they do right that my mother did wrong? What kind of man would I have become if I had been raised by that kind of mother, or anything close to it? I think about that more than I care to admit. Not because I enjoy grieving imaginary childhoods, but because the contrast is too sharp to ignore.

But the answer did not come from imagining a different childhood. It came from the people who showed up in the one I actually had.

It was also during that season that I met Stephanie at CKO, the kickboxing gym I had joined as part of the fight against my own weight. I do not remember all the details anymore, but I always noticed she had a kind face with a smile that arrived before she spoke. I think something small happened where I ended up sharing the sun breeze oil I had in my bag. Soon, I learned she was a respiratory therapist, and we got to talking about my CPAP, which broke the ice in the way only two people bonding over medical equipment can.

Over the next few sessions, we happened to take the same classes and ended up as partners, working side by side, punching and kicking that bag as if it owed us money. Besides thinking that God save her husband if he ever steps out of line, because this girl can punch, I learned she was a kind-hearted, soft-spoken, gentle person with very kind eyes and a protective nature. She was gentle but possessed a strength that felt like a force. I wondered what fueled her punches, but never dared to ask.

But at the same time, the alarms went off in my mind. Her nature was too familiar. The kindness, the softness, the protective instinct. What if I mess this up again? What if I fall into my past patterns? Should I stop and give in to my fears and lose a possibly beautiful friendship, or move forward trusting myself?

I remember asking Ishrat about it. As expected, she encouraged me to face it because she did not want me to miss out on a friendship. That is who Ishrat is. Where my mother and my ex would have stopped me, possessed me, kept me to themselves out of insecurity disguised as love, Ishrat did the opposite. She opened the door wider. She trusted me enough to let someone else in, and she trusted herself enough not to feel threatened by it. That was not generosity alone. It was the confidence of a woman whose love does not operate from fear. It was the kind of freedom I had never been given by anyone who claimed to love me before her.

Stephanie's power punches did make sense later, as I learned about the walls she had walked through herself. But that is her story to tell. What I can say is that whatever had tested her had not made her bitter. It had made her fiercer in her kindness, the way pressure either crushes coal or makes diamonds. What I did not expect was that Stephanie would become one of my dear friends and a steady presence in my life.

That was how I began to understand love. Not through slogans. Not through fear. Not through lectures about duty. Through contrast. Through Ishrat, Afifa Khala, Stephanie, and the other mentioned and unmentioned women in my life whose love I watched in motion, in kitchens, in sickrooms, in gyms, in family gatherings, in the way they handled husbands, children, relatives, pressure, disappointment, and ordinary days. Not as poetry alone, but as endurance without cruelty, service without humiliation, correction without contempt, and warmth without games.

And I came to understand, through religion, literature, and experience, what love is. Love is hard work done in willing submission to the beloved. The work begins, not ends, with commitment, and like the daily prayers, it must be practiced daily with the people entrusted to me. Just as there are five prayers in a day, people, too, have their own different ways of receiving and expressing love. I had to learn to recognize that. To speak it. To practice it. To stop treating love like some grand feeling that proves itself once, and start seeing it as a discipline of mercy, attention, service, restraint, truth, and daily return.
If the hopefully wiser version of me could go back and sit across from the boy I was, this is what I would say to him about love.

Asif, love is not noise. It is not volume, drama, constant correction, or making people walk on eggshells around you. If the people closest to you are always bracing themselves, something is already wrong. Love should make a home feel breathable, not tense.

With parents, honor them without lying to yourself. Be respectful. Be patient. Try to understand what made them hard. But do not confuse respect with surrender. Even if the confusion comes from your mother or me, confusion is still confusion. Love and respect your mother, but refuse her mind games. Be gentle and still be firm. Firmness without cruelty is not rebellion. It is maturity.

And learn this: forgiveness and justice are both essential. You cannot have peace in a family without both. A home that insists on justice but withholds forgiveness becomes a prison. A home that offers forgiveness but never names the wrong becomes a performance. Hold them together in yourself, not as contradictions, but as a pair.

With siblings and loved ones, be loyal without competing. Stay close without turning everything into a contest, correction, or quiet little war. Do not keep score. Do not enjoy putting them down. Speak truth without contempt. The world will wound them enough. Do not become one more injury they have to survive.

With friends, be sincere, steady, and trustworthy. Do not be the man who is charming in public and absent the moment it costs something. Laugh with them, yes, but also tell them the truth, guard their dignity, keep their secrets, and stay when life becomes inconvenient. Friendship is one of the purest forms of love because it is chosen, not assigned.

With a wife, become safe for her before you become demanding. Do not treat marriage as ownership dressed up as leadership and then ask for applause. Love her with protection, tenderness, patience, and the daily discipline of making her feel cherished and at ease in your presence. The Messenger said the best of you are those who are best to their families, and that he himself was best to his family. Let that be your standard, not the broken examples you grew up watching.

And learn the difference between love and hunger. Love gives. Hunger takes. If all you are doing is reaching, clutching, needing, that is not love. That is starvation dressed up as devotion. A starving person does not choose wisely, Asif. He takes whatever is closest and calls it love. Asif, if you confuse attachment with love, you will keep holding on to what should be released and keep running from what should be held close. Love freely, but love from fullness, not from the fear of being empty.

Do not measure love by how much pain you can endure in silence, or how much of yourself you can abandon to keep others comfortable. That is not love. That is fear, guilt, or hunger wearing noble clothes. Real love does not ask you to disappear so someone else can feel large.

And learn the difference between remorse and shame. They will wear the same face in your life, but they lead in opposite directions. Remorse will pull you back toward God. It will say, I can do better, and it will move you. Shame will say, I am too broken to try, and it will paralyze you. You will meet people who use shame like a weapon. Do not let them convince you that self-hatred is devotion. It is the opposite of it. God does not ask you to crawl. He asks you to get back up.

And be careful with expectations. If there is one sure way to make yourself miserable, it is expecting people to be what they were never built to be. Your parents were not the parents you needed. Your first wife was not the wife you imagined. Even the people who loved you best could not always love you the way you wanted. That is not betrayal. That is the limitation of being human. Grieve it if you must, but do not punish people for it.

Ali (AS) taught that patience is of two kinds: patience over what pains you, and patience against what you crave. Asif, you will need both. Patience to endure what your childhood put in you, and patience to resist the shortcuts your hunger will offer. The easy love. The familiar pain. The quick escape. None of those will take you where you need to go.

And about that emptiness you carry, the one that makes you think something is wrong with you, listen to me carefully. That emptiness is not a flaw in you. It is the very thing that will send you searching for what matters. A person who feels full does not ask. A person who feels rich does not beg. You will beg, and you will search, and it will hurt. But the searching is the point. Do not resent the void. It is the engine.

Treat love as chosen work. Treat it as a verb, not a noun. Give attention. Offer service. Build discipline. Return again and again with mercy in your heart and protection in your arms. Learn how each person receives care. Honor that. Speak to that. Love is not proven once. It is practiced daily.

And about that night with the pillow and the ceiling, when you cursed God, and no one came, I need you to hear this: you were not abandoned. The Prophets themselves were surrounded by fire, flood, armies, and seas, and Allah kept them safe inside the belly of calamity, not outside it. It was not that they were spared the storm. They were carried through it. You were a child screaming into a pillow. He did not leave the room. He was the only one who stayed. Do not fear the storm, Asif. Fear the distance from Shelter. That is the only real danger there ever was.

And do not resent the people who used faith as a weapon against you. They were wrong. But resentment will chain you to their version of God longer than their words ever could. Widen your view. The people who hurt you, the ones who left you, the ones who misread you, they are all part of the story. But the story has a Designer. And in the end, all that remains is the heart inside your chest, free or shackled, beautiful or ugly. That is the only thing you take with you. So do not let anyone else's cruelty determine its condition when you arrive.

And about what was withheld from you, the love you wanted, the childhood you deserved, everything that arrived late in your life arrived heavier with meaning than it would have if it had come on time. The delay was not a mistake. It was preparation. Do not be angry at the waiting. Be grateful that what finally came was worth it.

A wise friend once told me to treat the ones you love better than your most important customer. If strangers are reaping the fruits of your patience, manners, and softness while the people closest to you suffer at your hands, then whatever that is, it is not love.

And know this: you spent so much of your life looking for love that you nearly missed the larger truth. It was not in receiving it that you found anything lasting. It was in giving it. The moment love stopped being something you chased and became something you offered, everything changed.

So remember this, Asif: you grew up understanding what love costs. You learned that long before anyone showed you what it gives. Do not let that be the house you build. Build the one where a boy does not have to brace.

In the end, love is not what people say around you, demand from you, or claim in your name. Love is what the people closest to you feel in your presence, and whether they thank Allah for your nearness or recover from it after you leave.
