What I Wish Babu Taught Me

Kids,

I want you to know how this started, because it did not start with a plan or a purpose. It started with a sentence I said to Ishrat one day, in the middle of an ordinary conversation about life. I said, "I wish I had a dad who would have taught me that." And the sentence would not leave me alone.

Not because my father was a bad man. He was not. And not because this is a complaint against him. It is not. My father did the best he could, as most fathers do. And if you are honest with yourselves one day, you may say the same thing about me, with the same mix of love and frustration. I would deserve it. No father is perfect, and no man is handed a manual before he becomes one. I certainly was not handed one either, and I doubt I wrote one for you.

What stayed with me was not the accusation inside the sentence, but the absence. The shape of something I had needed and did not have. Not luxury. Not perfection. Just a few clear words at the right time. A few truths spoken plainly enough to save me from learning everything by collision, friction, and disaster.

Part of what I owe you is the truth about where I came from. Not to excuse anything, but because a man who does not explain his origins leaves his children guessing. And guessing, in my experience, is almost always worse than knowing. My parents came from a world that did not teach them how to speak about the things that mattered most. That silence shaped them, and it shaped me, and I am certain that some version of it shaped you, too. I am writing this so that silence is not the only thing I pass on, and so that you do not spend your years wishing someone had broken it sooner.

This is not a memoir written for its own sake. I am not trying to turn my life into literature or build a monument out of memory. That does not help anyone, least of all you. The point is simpler than that, and harder. I want to track the road I have walked, make sense of what it cost me, and leave it behind for you. Not as a performance. Not as a complaint. As a record. As a conversation I am having with you now, so you do not have to wish, the way I did, that someone had said these things sooner.

Because this is not really about the father I wish I had. That would be too easy and too dishonest. Life did not leave me untouched because my father failed some impossible standard. Life taught me what it teaches most men eventually, only I learned too much of it late, through disappointment, bad decisions, wasted years, avoidable pain, and the stubborn arrogance that mistakes confidence for understanding. These pages are not an inventory of my parents' failures. They are an account of my lessons. And they belong to you now.

I also know that some of my lessons became your wounds. The things I learned late, I could not teach you on time. The damage I carried, I did not always keep to myself. I cannot undo that. But I can at least be honest about the road that brought me here, so that the parts of me you did not understand begin to make sense.

There are things I need to talk to you about. Manhood, Love, Anger. Money. Marriage. Pride. Discipline. Faith. Work. Respect. The price of foolish decisions. The quiet damage caused by not knowing what matters until after you have already paid for not knowing it. These are the chapters that follow, and every one of them cost me something to learn.

Life has a brutal way of teaching. Sometimes it explains itself gently. More often, it takes payment first and gives the lesson afterward. I do not want that for you. I do not want you to wish one day that someone had said these things sooner, only to write the same lessons to your own children. Not if I can help it. Not so that you avoid mistakes. You will make them, and you should. Mistakes have been my wisest teachers. But I want you to make the corrections early enough to enjoy a fuller life than I did, and to leave something better behind.

Each chapter tells the story of how I learned these lessons, and ends with the advice I wish my father had given me when I was young. I wrote that advice as if I were sitting across from my younger self, saying the things no one said to me when I needed to hear them. But the lessons are not locked inside my past. They are not about me. My experiences were the classroom, but the lessons belong to anyone willing to listen. If the advice I give to the boy I was sounds like something you needed to hear too, that is not a coincidence. It was written with both of us in mind.

The truth is, even if my father had sat me down as a teenager and explained these things properly, there is a good chance I would not have listened. At that age, I believed I knew everything, and that my parents were simply not of these modern times. Youth has its own religion, and one of its articles of faith is that experience is overrated until your own mistakes begin to corner and shape you.

You may feel the same way reading this. You may even feel that way about me. That is your right. I was not the father I should have been in every season of your lives, and I know it. But I am writing this anyway. Not because I earned the authority to teach, but because I would rather you hear it from me, however imperfectly, than spend years wishing you had heard it from someone.

Because once a man has paid dearly for certain lessons, he should at least have the decency to leave a map behind. And if that man has children, it is not decency. It is a duty. Even if the map is drawn by a hand that made some of the wrong turns itself.

I cannot go back and change what I learned late. I cannot rewrite my own beginnings, and I cannot rewrite yours. But I can leave you something honest. Not polished wisdom from a safe distance, but hard-earned truth from a life that had to find out the long way around. Every chapter that follows is a question I wish my father had answered, and my attempt to answer it for you with whatever clarity fifty-four years of mistakes, searching, and stubborn survival have given me.

My father never had this conversation with me. I wish he had. I am having it with you now, so that sentence does not become yours.

Maybe you will not listen now. Maybe you will read this and think your old man is being dramatic. But I know this: the lessons were worth learning, even when they came late. And if they are worth learning, they are worth saying out loud to the people I love most, before wishing becomes the only option left.

Your Baba
