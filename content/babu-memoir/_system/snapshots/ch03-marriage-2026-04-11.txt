Babu, Tell Me What Marriage Really Is

By the time marriage was put in front of me, sex was already a broken subject in my mind. Not because I had encountered it too early or too much, but because I had never encountered it honestly. Everything I knew about the subject had arrived through fear, secrecy, humiliation, and silence. There had been the bus stop in Kuwait, the rooftop in Karachi, the years of not telling my parents because fear rose before trust did, and the whole censored madness of a world where everything natural was hidden while everything distorted leaked through. Not a single honest sentence from anyone who was supposed to protect me. So by the time marriage entered the room, it was not meeting a clear mind. It was meeting a man already split inside.

I was a little older when the bus stop incident happened in Kuwait. It was quiet, almost empty. An older Arab boy appeared out of nowhere, threatened me, and made me do something humiliating before walking away as if nothing had happened. What stayed with me was not only the act but also the terror within it. Not drama. Terror. And almost as disturbing as the memory itself is this: I did not tell my parents. That is still one of the hardest parts to explain. Not only what happened, but what happened after. Why was I afraid to tell the people who were supposed to protect me? The real question is not whether my father would have wanted to kill that boy. He probably would have. The real question is why fear rose in me before trust did. Why, after something ugly and frightening, my instinct was not to run home and speak, but to go quiet and carry it alone.

I think the earlier years explain more than I want them to. By then I already knew what it meant to be punished before being understood. I already knew that truth could cost me. I already knew that innocence and guilt were sometimes treated exactly the same, depending on whose mood had the microphone. So when something ugly happened, my instinct was not confession. It was concealment. I never felt filthy in the theatrical way films like to show. I felt terrified. Those are not the same thing. Filth belongs to shame. Terror belongs to danger. What I carried was danger with no safe place to set it down.

By thirteen, the ignorance was still there, only now it had panic inside it. On one of our trips to Karachi, I asked my cousin, just a year older than me, how babies are born. He explained sex to me, and I remember recoiling in horror. I argued with him, genuinely upset. So you mean to tell me our Prophet, peace be upon him, was also born that way? He said yes, confidently, almost proudly, as though that settled the matter. It settled nothing. It made the contradiction worse. In my mind, sex was already contaminated. Not because it is dirty in itself, but because by then I had only encountered the subject through secrecy, threat, punishment, and half-understood obscenity. A clean thing explained through shame starts to feel diseased.

On that same trip, I think, there was another incident. Someone took me to the roof of his apartment building and crossed a line I was too young to understand and too afraid to defend. I hesitated. I resisted in that weak, uncertain way frightened boys resist when they have never been taught that their no is enough. Then he raised his voice, and I folded. That part makes terrible sense to me now. Fear and submission through yelling already had a home in me. My body had been trained long before my mind caught up. That is what people miss. A child does not need to be persuaded from scratch when fear has already been installed.

Then came images. Not explanation. Not guidance. Images.
Magazines first, or at least visual nudity before anything more explicit. Then, in seventh grade, one of my classmates showed me a picture of a nude woman. I still remember the feeling of it. Strange, disorienting, like falling out of a plane. Something in me jolted awake without context, without language, without protection. The curiosity did not go away. It grew. Then one day, I think in ninth grade, a friend gave me a VHS cassette of actual porn.

Kuwait television was so heavily censored that even the beginning of a light kiss would flash briefly before being cut off. That kind of censorship does not create innocence. It creates distortion. Everything natural was hidden. Everything distorted leaked through. So what reached me was not a sane understanding of bodies, intimacy, desire, or tenderness. What reached me was fragments, shocks, secrecy, dirty jokes, schoolyard rubbish, porn tapes passed hand to hand, and a household where simple questions were treated like moral crimes. No one taught me what belonged to love, what belonged to lust, what belonged to violence, what belonged to tenderness. So all of it bled together.

That is what I mean when I say my understanding of sex got damaged early. It was never just about seeing the wrong things. The more serious damage was having no honest framework to place any of it in. No calm explanation. No adult who could say, without violence or disgust, this is what sex is, this is what it is not, this is what makes it human, this is what makes it degrading, this is what belongs in love, and this is what belongs nowhere near it. Without that, curiosity becomes infected. Desire becomes confused. A boy reaches for knowledge and finds noise instead.

Years passed. I grew older, earned a degree, and looked, from the outside, like a man who had moved on. But damage that has not been understood does not disappear. It just waits for the next relationship to surface in.

In my late twenties, I was engaged to my cousin, Afifa Khala's (Aunt Afifa's) eldest daughter. She was a wonderful girl, exceptionally intelligent, kind, balanced, the kind of girl any decent man would be lucky to marry. She was the pride and joy of my father. That made sense. He never had the fortune of a daughter of his own, so some part of him poured that longing into her, and he wanted her to be my wife. He asked me in the kind of way that is technically a choice while leaving very little room for one. Rejecting her would have meant breaking my parents' hearts, being a bad Muslim, and apparently qualifying as the dumbest man alive for turning down such a girl. But yes, the choice was mine. Talk about impossible choices.

I agreed, not because I could not see her value. I could. But she was too close for comfort. She was the girl I had always been compared to and never quite measured up against. Even so, with all the uncompromising family expectations about marrying within the community, the dating pool was less of a pool and more of a puddle.

By then, I had completed my Master's in Software Engineering. I used to go over to her apartment often to tutor her and her best friend. It was also completely normal for me to sleep in the living room. We had all grown up in the same house. There was nothing unusual about that.

Then one morning I woke up and the whole climate in the apartment felt cold as hell. Everyone seemed upset with me. Anger shot through Afifa Khala's eyes. I had no idea what was going on. Confused, I left. Later, when I returned, I found everyone gathered in the living room. A proper intervention. My parents looked furious. My mother was crying. As the dust settled, I finally understood the accusation.

Apparently, I had woken up in the middle of the night, walked into my fiancee's room, and tried to wake her up with some evil, undignified intent. She said she told me to leave, and when she got louder, I ran out.

What had just happened?

I did not remember any of it. Not a second. Did I sleepwalk? Did something else happen? I had absolutely no recollection, but for the life of me, nobody would believe me. I cried. I begged. I swore on the Qur'an, on the Prophet (peace be upon him), on everything I could think of, but no one believed me. I had no defense except that Allah was my witness that I had not done what they thought I had done.

It turns out there is a God.

The following night, while I was at my own house, someone climbed through the balcony and came in again. The noise woke someone up, and he jumped out and ran away. Whoever he was, he had my gratitude for coming again the next night, because otherwise I might never have been proven innocent beyond a speck of doubt.

But by then, a great deal had already broken in me.

The engagement eventually broke, but the scars remained. Not because people reacted to what they thought they saw. The circumstances were stacked against me. I can understand that. My pain came from something more personal than that. Nobody believed me. Not my parents. Not even Afifa Khala, who had always been my last quiet hope. That is what stayed. Not only the accusation, but the loneliness of having no witness except Allah and still not being given the benefit of the doubt. Ali (AS, peace be upon him) once said that it is in the darkest nights that stars shine more brightly. I did not know that then. All I knew was the dark. The Quran promises, twice over as if once were not enough, that with hardship comes ease. In that room, ease was not a word I had any use for. But there is something I have come to understand about not getting what you want. When something is withheld, you discover a need you did not know you had. When you discover a need, you begin to ask. And when asking goes unanswered long enough, something deeper takes over. You start reaching toward something larger than the thing you originally wanted. Sometimes He does not give you what you ask for because He wants to give you something more. Sometimes He deprives to give. I did not understand that then. I understand it now.

That was the foundation I was standing on when marriage arrived.

Still a virgin, I got married to my ex, who herself came from a broken, messed-up home. What do you get when you mix one hot mess with another? Exactly what you would expect, except less entertaining and with far worse consequences. I did not go into that marriage with any real understanding of love, intimacy, or emotional steadiness. She did not come from anything whole either. Two damaged people do not magically become healthy just because someone recites a nikah and serves biryani. A contract can make something legal. It cannot make it loving. A ceremony can make something official. It cannot make two people safe for each other.

My experience of marital sex with my ex was no help. If anything, it pushed the confusion deeper into the ground. She would get drunk and demand sex, and I would comply mostly out of fear of what she might do if I refused. I do not even like writing that sentence, because people have a very neat script in their heads for who gets violated and how, and I do not fit the casting. But I know what I felt. Used. Helpless. Disgusted. Trapped inside something I wanted to end while it was still happening. More than once, I faked orgasm just to bring the whole thing to a stop. There are forms of violation that leave no bruise and still make your skin feel like it no longer belongs to you.

And the sex was only one part of it. There was no emotional connection. No support. No rest. Her hygiene was unbearable, the house was filthy, there were constant complaints, and I was not allowed to do anything by myself. I had no social circle left. If I took her anywhere, I risked being embarrassed. The easiest way to survive the drama was to submit to it. To shrink. To stop resisting. I had turned into my father. The man I had looked down upon. The man I had told myself, with all the confidence ignorance gives a young man, I would never become. Yet there I was, not leading, not loving, not protecting anything, just becoming smaller to keep the peace. Or what passed for peace. The Quran says that Allah will not change the condition of a people until they change what is within themselves. I had not changed anything. I had only gotten quieter.

By then, all that madness had done its work. I found myself married to a woman I did not love, and who did not love me, with two children and a life that felt less like a life than a sentence I was serving. There was no tenderness. No sense of being known, wanted, or even particularly cared for. Just duty, noise, exhaustion, and the slow death of whatever hope I had left.

Somewhere in all that, I arrived at the inversion I had been raised on without knowing it was a lesson. People who should have been loved were managed, measured, and found wanting. Things that should have been used were accumulated and prized. I had absorbed this without knowing it was a lesson: that we are supposed to love people and use objects, and that most of us have it exactly backwards. A wife treated like an inconvenient fixture, strangers treated with warmth while family bore the cost of your exhaustion. I had done all of it without noticing.

I had gone up to 270 pounds and thrown myself into religion with the desperation people usually reserve for lifeboats and last prayers. It was not peace, not really. It was more like I was trying to bury myself under piety before I had to look too closely at the wreckage underneath.

What I did not understand then, but gradually came to believe, is that the door of God's mercy does not close based on how broken a person is. Not because of the house you grew up in. Not because you ran from faith for years and then came back a little frantic and not very clean. Not because the state of your heart at any particular moment was worse than you knew. The door was open. The only barrier was my own idea of myself: that I was too far gone to be the kind of person it was meant for. That turned out to be the only barrier there was.

And part of me, the part I am least proud of, had found a strange kind of safety in the misery. The pain was familiar. It was all I had known. Stepping out of it meant risking something unknown, and the unknown felt more dangerous than the prison I was already sitting in. That is perhaps the greatest ingratitude a person can commit: when God hands you a chance at something better, and you refuse to take it because suffering has become your home.

And in the middle of that loveless marriage, I fell in love with a cousin a few years older than me. Or perhaps the more honest way to say it is that I felt love from her for the first time and did not know what was happening inside me. She was in an unhappy marriage too, though of a different kind. A simple, naive woman, loyal to the core, with a husband who was harsh-hearted, disconnected, and socially clumsy in that deadening sort of way some men can be. She found a friend in me. My mind turned it into something else.

Had she given me an opening, I am not sure what would have happened. That is not a noble sentence, but it is an honest one. I think one of the reasons we are so afraid to admit our mistakes is because we believe our weaknesses make us less worthy of love. The truth is the opposite. Admitting what is broken in us does not diminish us. It is one of the few things that actually makes a person worth trusting. Despite whatever I felt, she remained loyal. I, on the other hand, had a mind trained in confusion and a nervous system built like faulty wiring. What I experienced as intensity may have been part longing, part damage, part hunger, and part emotional stupidity. Usually all at once. I was confused, unsettled, possessive, jealous in ways I could not explain, and too emotionally illiterate to understand that these were not random storms. They were signs. Or warnings. Maybe both.

What made it worse was how familiar she felt. We had grown up in the same culture. There were similarities to my mother in her, but softened by tenderness, care, and a great deal of TLC. Was that what attracted me to her? Was I searching for my mother's love through her? If so, that raises its own ugly question: then why the taboo thoughts? That is the kind of contradiction that makes a man stare at himself a little longer than he wants to. What I called love had more of attachment in it than I understood at the time. Attachment is what happens when you are starving. It is rooted in fear and dependency and has more to do with love of self than love of another. Love without attachment is something else entirely, something that comes from fullness, not emptiness. But I was not full. I was running on fumes and calling it devotion. The two feel identical for a long while, and then something distinguishes them, usually at the worst possible moment. My fight-or-flight, shame-soaked, half-feral brain caused her a lot of grief. I became jealous, possessive, and demanding. Worse than that, I used the same tactics my mother used on me. Silent treatment. Mental games. Turning the tables on her when I was the one in the wrong. The tongue is like a lion. If you let it loose, it will wound someone. I let mine loose on her regularly. I can dress it up in better language now, but the truth is plain enough. I turned into my mother.

That was one of the hardest recognitions of my life. I had already become my father in marriage, growing smaller to survive conflict. Now I was becoming my mother too, using emotional pressure when fear and need rose in me. That is what unresolved damage does. It does not simply make you suffer. It recruits you. It starts borrowing your face. Atif had found his own version of the same inheritance. Where I repeated her tactics outward, he had absorbed them inward. The same house, the same blueprints, two different directions of collapse.

And then, in the middle of all that, Ishrat came into the picture.

There is a saying that some women like men they can work on, men who arrive as projects. If that is true, then when Ishrat met me, I was every woman's dream. Not the romantic kind, obviously. More like a salvage operation with a pulse. I was overweight, emotionally scrambled, spiritually overcompensating, tied to a dead life, and carrying enough unresolved confusion to qualify as a public hazard. And yet, somehow, she stepped into that mess.

I met Ishrat when she herself was going through a very difficult emotional time. So in a strange way, we met each other in the rubble. Neither of us arrived untouched. Neither of us arrived shining. We were both carrying hurt, both trying to keep moving, both in need of something steadier than what life had given us so far. And I think, in some real way, we helped heal each other.

But what Ishrat showed me went beyond companionship. She introduced me to a completely different understanding of what partnership could be. Not performance. Not negotiation. Not two damaged people managing each other's moods across a dining table. She carried herself with a steadiness I had never seen up close in a marriage, despite in-laws who were difficult and a husband who gave her very little emotional support. That alone taught me something. She did not need the conditions to be perfect before she could be good. She was good regardless, and that goodness was not naive. It was chosen.

She gave me care without humiliation. Warmth without mind games. Closeness without panic. And she did it with a patience that defied every pattern I had known. I failed her numerous times. Part of me was probably hoping she would finally get tired of me and walk away, the way frightened people keep trying to prove their own worst expectations correct. But she never did. She stayed. She saved my life in more ways than one, and without her, I honestly do not know where I would be today.

That changed me. Not all at once. Life is usually less efficient than that. But once I had been loved with that kind of steadiness, I could no longer pretend that fear, shame, and confusion were normal forms of marriage. Once someone keeps showing up, keeps refusing to weaponize your weakness against you, the old lies start losing their authority.

What I also had to learn, and learned only by exhaustion if I am being honest, is that carrying my own verdict against the people who had hurt me did not protect me. It just added weight. Something only shifted when I was willing to set my own judgment down and trust something larger to sort out what I could not. Not pretending the wrongs did not happen. Not resignation. Something more like clearing the obstruction so grace had room to come through. I could not have arrived at Ishrat, or at anything honest, with my hands still clenched. There is a saying in Islam that the best way to let go of bitterness is to stop seeing the creation and start seeing the Creator. Whatever befell you was not meant to pass you. Whatever passed you was not meant to befall you. And everything that came or did not come can be used to bring you closer to Him. I did not arrive at that understanding gracefully. I arrived at it because I ran out of other places to put the weight.

It also forced me to rethink what marriage and marital sex are actually for.

Marriage is not there to decorate loneliness, legalize appetite, or hand a man a socially approved body and call that fulfillment. Allah tells us in the Quran that He created spouses so that we may find tranquility in them, and that He placed between them affection and mercy. He calls husband and wife garments for one another. A garment may or may not fit perfectly, but either way, it covers what is exposed, protects what is vulnerable, and makes the one wearing it feel less bare in the world. That is not the language of extraction. That is the language of covering, nearness, protection, and rest.

And once I understood human beings a little better, that began making deeper sense to me. Marriage is not just about protecting fun from being forbidden. It protects the human psyche from fragmentation. Human beings are not built to walk untouched through endless emotional entanglements, bodily comparisons, broken attachments, secret histories, and the psychic debris of multiple partners as though none of it leaves a mark. The body may move on faster than the heart does. The ego may brag about freedom while the nervous system is quietly collecting wreckage. Marriage, at its best, gives desire a moral home and gives intimacy a container sturdy enough to protect dignity, attachment, conscience, and children yet to be born. It does not make people pure by magic. It gives them a place where purity has a fighting chance.

That helped me understand why marital sex is not the same thing as sex in the abstract. Marital sex is not supposed to be two bodies using each other under halal lighting. It belongs inside trust, friendship, affection, emotional safety, and mutual dignity. For many women, desire is often tied not only to the body, but to context, tone, emotional ease, partner responsiveness, and feeling cherished rather than used. I had no sisters, no emotionally safe mother, and no sane early education in these matters, so I learned it late and awkwardly. But once I saw it, a great many ugly male assumptions started collapsing.

That is when the whole thing began to rearrange itself in my mind. Marriage is not sex with furniture. Marital sex is not legal access to another person's body. A wife is not a pressure valve, and a husband is not a mule for provision plus silent endurance. Marriage is a trust. A covenant. A friendship that must be fed with truth, patience, gentleness, and restraint. If friendship is not there, marital sex will not save it. If safety is not there, duty will not beautify it. If mercy is not there, the house may continue standing, but love has already packed its bags.

I think the father I needed would have answered this one more cleanly than life did.

Asif, do not think a nikah makes you ready. A ceremony can make something official. It cannot make two people wise, kind, or safe for each other. Readiness is not proved by age, desire, income, or family pressure. Readiness is shown in character. Can you tell the truth? Can you carry frustration without becoming cruel? Can you be faithful without becoming cold? Can you be trusted with another person's vulnerability? That is what matters.

Marriage is not permission. It is responsibility. It is not a place where your rights go to feed and her heart goes hungry. It is not a room where two lonely people act out their damage and call it destiny. If there is no friendship, no safety, no emotional ease, no mercy, then the walls are still up but the marriage is already empty.

And marital sex is not some separate department where character stops mattering. It is not legal access to another person's body. It is not a husband collecting what is "owed" to him, and it is not a wife surrendering herself just to keep the house quiet. Where trust and tenderness are absent, the body may still show up, but the person inside it has already left the room.

Learn this before marriage, not after it breaks somebody. A woman is not ready because a man is ready. Her body will not arrive where her heart has not been welcomed first. You cannot frighten a woman and then ask why warmth has left the room. You cannot neglect her heart and expect her body to feel welcomed by you. Do not reduce her to appetite management. Approach her as a whole person.

Do not carry the confusion of your wounds into a woman's life and call it manhood. A wife is not your mother, not your therapist, not your punishment, and not the keeper of your self-respect. Do not make her carry the fear, hunger, or chaos you refused to name in yourself. If she gives you trust, handle it carefully. If she gives you softness, do not answer with control. If she gives you loyalty, do not repay it with games. The Prophet, peace be upon him, reminded us to show mercy to those on earth so that the One above the heavens will show mercy to us. That begins at home. It begins with the woman sleeping next to you.

And one more thing. Marriage and marital love must also learn to break old oppressive patterns. Do not inherit passivity and call it patience. Do not inherit domination and call it leadership. Do not inherit emotional cruelty and call it honesty. If pain shaped your elders, understand them. But if their patterns are false, break them. Let mercy interrupt what fear handed down. Let truth end what shame kept alive. Let your marriage be the place where an old family wound finally loses the right to keep speaking.

And about the faith that holds any of this together: the obligation is the floor, Asif. What you choose to build above it is where you start to be loved back. Allah says, in His own words carried to us through the Prophet, peace be upon him, that His servant continues to draw near through voluntary acts of devotion until He loves him. Not accepts him. Not tolerates him. Loves him. That is what the nawafil are for. That is what patience and daily return are training you toward. Not compliance. Nearness. Let your faith be that, and not merely the minimum you have to show up for.

The Prophet, peace be upon him, told us that the affairs of the believer are always good: if something good comes, he is grateful, and that is good for him, and if something hard comes, he is patient, and that is good for him too. Marriage will hand you both. You will not always receive them well. But receiving them with something other than rage and self-pity is what you are practicing toward.

The Prophet, peace be upon him, said: make things easy, and do not make them difficult. Give glad tidings, and do not drive people away. Marriage is one of the places where that instruction is most needed and most often ignored. So many men make marriage harder than it has to be, heavier than it was designed to be, and then wonder why the woman beside them has gone quiet.

And about what was withheld from you before Ishrat arrived, the broken engagement, the dead marriage, the years of confusion. Sometimes what is withheld is the setup for what is given. He deprives to give. He breaks to rebuild. Do not look at the years of loss as wasted time. They were the reason you recognized what was real when it finally stood in front of you. Learn to let go of what was never meant to be yours. Nothing lasts forever. Not even broken hearts. The night is given so you can recognize the light that follows. And it always follows.

So remember this, Asif: sex was a broken subject long before marriage ever found you. You walked into it already split inside. But what was broken was never the subject itself. It was the silence around it, the fear inside it, and the absence of anyone willing to explain it with honesty and care. Marriage is not where you finally get to be obeyed. It is where two people should find more rest, more honesty, more mercy, and more room to become fully human before God. And when the sea in front of you looks impossible to cross, do not stare at the water. Look at the One who can split it in two. Because long after the arguments are forgotten and the rights are no longer being counted, the truest measure of your marriage will not be what you built together. It will be whether the woman who shared your life felt safe enough to be herself inside it.
