# Monotheism and the Critique of The Harvest

*The visual companion to Chapter Fourteen. The audio chapter is sustained polemic across sixteen sub-chapters of negation; this file is its argument made structural. Same concepts, same depth, restructured for slide-deck rendering.*

## Where this chapter sits in the book

A position on the architecture. Chapter Ten of the book is the polemical climax that the previous nine chapters have been climbing toward, and the audio chapter expounds all sixteen of its sub-chapters.

| Chapters 1 through 9 | Chapter 10 (this chapter) |
|---|---|
| Mode: Referee's report | Mode: Author in his own voice |
| Voices: Jonathan, Samuel, author | Voices: The Harvest, author |
| Object: branches where disagreement was permissible | Object: foundations where disagreement is not permissible |
| Subject matter: soul, intellect, emanation, time, motion, *qadr*, the law of Adam and Noah | Subject matter: monotheism (*divine oneness*) and the First Creator (*al-Mubdi al-awwal*) |
| Rank addressed: from First Intellect downward | Rank addressed: the rank above the First Intellect, the rank of God Himself |

The architecture of the chain at this rank, top to bottom:

```
Allah (al-Mubdi, the Originator, the rank above all rank)
  | issues
First Intellect (the Pen, al-qalam)
  | inscribes
Universal Soul (the Preserved Tablet, al-lawh al-mahfuz)
  | continues
The ranks below, down through the natural world
```

What Chapter Ten asks: what is the rank of *al-Mubdi*, Allah Himself, the rank that admits no language by which a creature might describe it? The Harvest wrote on this question and erred; Jonathan, in *The Correction*, passed over the errors; the author enters at the close of his treatise to *discharge the right of religion* by stating what is *not permissible to believe*.

## Why the author writes where Jonathan was silent

A contrast pair on the meaning of Jonathan's silence.

Column A: Common reading

- The author of The Harvest, may Allah have mercy on him, did not exert himself in the right of the *the mission*, and Sheikh Jonathan, may God have mercy on him, did not correct him though he was able.
- Most readers conclude: Jonathan stabbed at The Harvest, then offered apology; the silence amounts to passing the errors as doctrine.
- Verdict on the silence: *a corrupt judgment*.

Column B: The author's reading

- The Speaker, when he laid down things, knew that the sciences of religion are too many for one person to encompass.
- He arranged them through many people, like the twelve *hujjas* (twelve proofs), that they might cooperate over all the sciences and memorize them.
- They are like one body whose limbs together gather the whole; one limb does not shame another for what it does not perceive.
- Jonathan missed certain corrections; that is not a fault in him, nor proof that The Harvest was right; it is the right of another limb to tie the knot the first passed over.

Shared row: both readings agree Jonathan was silent on certain points in The Harvest's monotheism chapter. They disagree on what the silence means.

> *We are limbs of a single body, the body of the mission. The hand does not shame the foot, nor the eye the ear. When one limb sees a knot the other passed over, it is its right to tie it; that is the very meaning of being one body.*
> The author, *the author's other treatise* (*The Repose of the Intellect*), on the cooperation of *the limits* in the sciences of religion.

The framing is essential. The chapter is not denunciation. It is *cooperation in discharging the right of religion, because the souls of religion are all one*. The author closes the framing with the prayer to be gathered *with the former, the reciprocal, the preceding, and the latecomer among our pure loyalists in the Abode of Holiness, Jerusalem*.

## Sub-chapter two unpacked, the threefold impossibility

An annotated structure dissecting The Harvest's opening sentence. Each piece of the sentence is positioned and its consequence is named.

The whole: The Harvest's opening formulation, *He is the creator of the thing, the rational, the illusory, the intellectual, the logical, I mean what is beneath these elements, and what is not beneath them*.

```
                    [the thing]
                         |
        ----------------------------------
        |          |          |          |
   rational   illusory   intellectual  logical
        |
   [what is beneath these elements]
        |
   [what is NOT beneath them]   <-- the trap
                |
        the rational, illusory, intellectual, logical
        do NOT perceive it;
        logic does NOT speak of it;
        yet it is *under His creativity*.
```

The trap (the clause *what is not beneath them*) entails three positions for the supposed entity. Each must be ruled out.

| Position of the supposed entity | Why it must fall |
|---|---|
| **Precedes the First Intellect in existence** | Reason is the first existing thing from Allah; nothing precedes the First Intellect but Allah Himself. |
| **Is with the First Intellect** | The Intellect, when it occurs, is one self and not two. Either no second is added (identity), or a second exists alongside (multiplicity at the head of the chain, which is associating partners with God). |
| **Follows the First Intellect** | What follows the Intellect is by definition within the field where the Intellect knows and defines. A *known-by-the-Intellect* that is *not-perceived-by-reason* is a square circle. |

*Since he was not devoid of three sections, each of which obliged that his existence be impossible, it is proven that his existence is impossible, and to say it is misguidance.* The Harvest, in his opening sentence, has made obligatory the believing-in of something whose existence is impossible. This is the first error.

## Thing and nothing, the contrast at the heart of the sophistry

A contrast pair. The word *nothing* is doing two different jobs, and the chapter turns on the difference.

Column A: *Nothing* as the ordinary sense

- Meaning: the absence of a thing
- Grammar: *not the human* denies the human to a certain place; no entity called *non-human* is brought into being
- Worked example: *a letter intended not to be a letter has no reality removing it from existence*
- In the formula *He and nothing with Him*: only Allah, and the absence of any other thing
- Verdict: orthodox, the natural reading
- Quranic anchor: *Kana Allahu wa lam yakun shay'un ghayruhu*, *Allah was, and there was nothing else with Him*

Column B: *Nothing* as the philosophers' positive predicate

- Meaning: *an affirmative for what is other than something; affirmatively stripped of what the thing is confined to in all its parts of essence and accidents, and elevated to the corrected state*
- Grammar: *not the human* becomes a positive predicate denoting *the non-human*, a positive entity that is everything except the human
- Worked example: *the human being who is an affirmative for everything that is not human*
- In the formula *He and nothing with Him*: Allah and the No-Thing alongside Him, two things
- Verdict: associating partners with God in language-form
- The Harvest's actual usage: this one, throughout the chapter

Shared row: both senses use the same word, *nothing* (*la shay'*). The Harvest's formulas read piously in column A; the same formulas commit associating partners with God in column B; The Harvest uses the word in column B's sense.

The author's diagnosis: *if he says "nothing with Him," it is as if he said "it and what is not with Him, He is still," and that is associating partners with God*. The grammar betrays the metaphysics: *with* requires two things, and column B has supplied the second.

## The Creator of His Creator, the reductio walked through

A process flow. The doctrine *He is the Creator of the thing and of nothing* is followed step by step until it forces an absurdity.

```
Start: "He is the Creator of the thing and of nothing"
   |
   v
Step 1: "the thing" = creativity, the eye of existence
   |
   v
Step 2: "nothing" = the negation of creativity, the nullification of existence
   |
   v
Step 3: substituting equals: "He is the Creator of creativity and of the-negation-of-creativity"
   |
   v
Step 4: but creativity is the Creator's own act, which issues from Him
   |
   v
Step 5: therefore He is the Creator of His own act of creation
   |
   v
End: "He is the Creator of His Creator", absurd
```

The doctrinal lock. The Almighty does not *create His own act*; His act *issues from Him*. The act *just is* the First Created Thing. To say *He creates His act* is to interpose the Creator between Himself and His own creativity, splitting the rank at the head of the chain in two by a sentence.

The verse that anchors the alternative grammar:

> *Allah bears witness that there is no god but He, and the angels and those of knowledge, upholding justice. There is no god but He, the Almighty, the Wise.*
> Quran 3:18, author's rendering.

Allah witnesses to His own unicity *by His own name*. The grammar of *la ilaha illa huwa* contains *only His name*; the exception clause has collapsed into the very name being excepted. This is the only kind of language that points to Him without thereby creating-and-originating a second alongside Him.

## The bare pronoun *huwa*, the threshold of language

An annotated structure. The chapter identifies *huwa* (He) as the only borrowed word that may stand in Allah's place. Its grammatical privileges are dissected.

The whole: the pronoun *huwa* in *la ilaha illa huwa*.

| Element | Property | Why this matters |
|---|---|---|
| *Huwa* (He), bare third-person singular pronoun | Carries no attribute | Cannot add anything to Him by being predicated |
| Carries no plurality | Cannot multiply Him by being a noun-class | |
| Carries no comparison | Cannot likeness Him to another | |
| Is *positive for what is outside of it* | Points past every predicate without naming what is pointed-to | |
| Placed after the negation of every god | Names Him in the place where one would expect an exception clause | |

> *Imam the Fatimid caliph Maad ibn Ismail indicated this in his well-known book. He said that "it" (huwa) is only Him, even if it is a borrowed word, for it is among the minds positive for what is outside of it, without any attribute or plurality.*
> Imam the Fatimid caliph, *The inner interpretation the law'i'* (*Interpretation of the Laws*), on the unique grammatical privilege of *huwa*.

*Huwa* is the threshold at which language stops short of Him without falling into associating partners with God. The Prophet's own teaching grounds the doctrine in the language of the first witness.

> *Allah was, and there was nothing else with Him; and His Throne was upon the water; then He created the heavens and the earth.*
> Sahih the canonical hadith compiler, Book 59, Hadith 3191; narrated by Imran ibn Husayn.

The Prophet does not say *He and the no-thing*; he says *Allah was, and there was nothing else with Him*. The negation is of *every other thing*, not the affirmation of *a No-Thing alongside Him*.

## The First Intellect vs the acquired intellect, the chapter's most repeated distinction

A contrast pair sustained across sub-chapters nine, twelve, thirteen, fourteen, and fifteen. The chapter returns to this distinction every time The Harvest's formula confuses the two.

Column A: The First Intellect

- Origin: the *first existing thing from Allah Almighty*
- Position in the chain: directly below Allah; nothing precedes it but Him
- Knowledge: knows only itself and the One above it; does not know the multitude below it as multitude
- Image-content: does not contain the images of the two worlds; *does not need the perception of what is within it*
- Eternity: eternal in itself, by its own being
- Relation to the Word: *is* the Word (identity, not likeness)
- Relation to creativity: *is* creativity, *is* the Creator at this rank
- Name in the *the mission*'s vocabulary: the Pen (*al-qalam*), the proximate sovereign
- Number: one self, not two

Column B: The acquired intellect

- Origin: emerges *from the natural world by acquiring work and knowledge in parallel with the first emanation*
- Position in the chain: the second emanation; the intellect of the speakers (*the speaker-Prophets*), foundations (*al-asas*), imams, their followers
- Knowledge: gathers all the images of both worlds in its full receivership
- Image-content: contains all the images of the two worlds, by acquisition over time
- Eternity: attains timelessness *by uniting with the paradoxical intellect called the proximate sovereign*
- Relation to the Word: becomes *like* the Word by receiving its effects
- Relation to creativity: not the Creator simply; the medium-sense applies
- Name in the *the mission*'s vocabulary: the chain of *the limits of the mission*
- Number: many, across the cycles of speakers and imams

Shared row: both are called *intellect* in The Harvest's chapter; the equivocation is the source of the errors at sub-chapters nine, twelve, thirteen, fourteen, and fifteen.

The author's recurring move: when The Harvest says *the Intellect contains all the images, became eternal, became like the Word, is the Creator-from-the-Creator, is intermediary between the Word and what is below it*, the formula is *true of the acquired intellect* and *false of the First Intellect*. Most of The Harvest's chapter is an importation of *acquired-intellect* attributes into the First Intellect's rank, and the importation collapses the architecture.

## The relation diagram at sub-chapter fifteen, no third intermediary

A process flow showing the architecture the chapter defends versus the architecture The Harvest's formula requires.

```
The architecture the chapter defends (the *the mission*'s chain):

   Allah
     |
     v
   First Intellect (= Word = unity = cause = creativity = Creator at this rank)
     |
     v
   Universal Soul (the Preserved Tablet)
     |
     v
   ...the ranks below...
     |
     v
   the natural world
```

```
The architecture The Harvest's formula requires (intermediary inserted):

   Allah
     |
     v
   the Word                              <-- inserted rank
     |
     v
   First Intellect (as intermediary)
     |
     v
   ...the ranks below...
```

Why the intermediary doctrine fails, two branches:

```
Is the Word above the First Intellect a thing or a nothing?
   |
   |---> if a thing: the First Intellect perceives it, so the Intellect is above it.
   |     but the doctrine put the Word above. Contradiction.
   |
   |---> if a nothing: then it is not the Word. What then is it?
         it is "transcendent above the qualities a thing deserves", but then it has
         no role; it cannot mediate; it adds no rank.
```

*And given the impossibility of anything being advanced over the First Intellect, there is nothing above the thing except the Thing of the Almighty. And if this is so, then it is the Word, it is the cause, it is the unity, and it is the intellect, according to what we have explained above.* The chain has no *between*. *God is sufficient for us, and He is the best Trustee.*

## The chain of identities at sub-chapter ten

A hierarchy by collapse. The chapter's central doctrinal move is to identify a string of names that The Harvest's chapter treats as distinct ranks.

At the rank of the First Emanation, the following are one name for one rank:

- unity (*al-divine oneness*)
- the One (*al-wahid*)
- the First Intellect (*al-'aql al-awwal*)
- the Word (*al-kalima*)
- the cause (*al-'illa*)
- creativity (*al-origination*)
- the Creator (*al-Mubdi*) at this rank, not the rank above
- the Perfect (*al-tamm*)
- the Eternal (*al-azali*)
- Existence (*al-wujud*)
- the Essence (*al-dhat*)

What the identity does and does not do:

- It does *not* add to the rank by naming it from many angles
- It does *not* make Allah identical with the First Intellect; Allah is *al-Mubdi*, *above* this rank; the *Creator* listed above is *the creator-at-this-rank*, the medium-sense
- It *does* refuse the doctrine that posits *cause* as a rank above *effect* at this point in the chain
- It *does* settle the chapter's later sub-chapters: when The Harvest says *unity is the cause of the Intellect*, the answer is *they are the same*; when he says *the Intellect became like the Word*, the answer is *the Intellect is the Word*; when he says *creativity is intermediary*, the answer is *creativity is the Creator*

## Sub-chapter sixteen, creativity IS the Creator

A process flow culminating in the chapter's final identity statement. This is the closing critique and the closing settlement.

The Harvest's doctrine, walked through:

```
"Creativity is intermediary between the Creator and the created."
   |
   v
Creativity sits on the Creator's side as form, on the created's side as effect.
   |
   v
Creativity is a form IN the Creator, uncreated.
   |
   v
Creativity is eternal-with-God-not-from-God.
```

The author's reductio:

```
Suppose creativity is something OTHER than the Creator. Two options:
   |
   |---> Option A: creativity exists for itself
   |       (self-sufficient ground)
   |       Fails: creativity is fragmented in itself; cannot have its own ground;
   |              and yet is held to be "with God Almighty."
   |
   |---> Option B: creativity exists WITH God Almighty
           Fails by the two-examples proof:
              creativity-with-God must either be (a) similar to God, or (b) an opposite of God;
                 (a) two examples sharing one rank require a prior cause that produced them
                     together; the cause would be PRIOR to God, impossible
                 (b) opposites require something else to preserve their co-existence;
                     God would be preserved by another, impossible
```

The closing identity:

> *Creativity is the Creator.*

At the rank where the chain begins, *creativity* and *the Creator* are one name for one rank. To say *creativity is intermediary between the Creator and the created* is to introduce a *third entity* between Allah and His act, the very thing the architecture refuses at the head of the chain.

The Quranic anchor of the settlement:

> *Huwa al-awwalu wa al-akhiru, wa al-the apparentu wa al-the hiddenu; wa huwa bi-kulli shay'in 'alim.*
> *He is the First and the Last, and the Outward and the Inward; and He has knowledge of all things.*
> Quran 57:3.

The verse settles it from the Qur'anic side. *He*, the bare pronoun, *is the First*; there is no *intermediary* between Him and what issues from Him. The act of creation issues *from* Him; the act *is* Him in *the rank of being-the-cause*; the act is not a third entity *between* Him and the created.

> *Vision perceives Him not, but He perceives all vision; and He is the Subtle, the Aware.*
> Quran 6:103.

The exhaustion of language at the threshold of Him is the only honesty.

## the chapter on sincerity, the doctrine in Quranic form

An annotated structure. Four lines of Quran 112 enact the entire chapter's doctrine line by line.

> *Say: He is Allah, One. Allah, the Eternal Refuge. He neither begets nor is begotten. Nor is there to Him any equivalent.*
> Quran 112:1-4, Sahih International.

| Line | What it enacts |
|---|---|
| *Qul huwa Allahu ahad* | Opens with the bare pronoun *huwa*, the threshold named in sub-chapter five; *Allah* is the name placed after the negation of every god; *ahad* means *one-without-a-second*, not *one-among-many*. |
| *Allahu al-samad* | *The eternal refuge*; the rank that admits no addition, no plurality, no other. |
| *Lam yalid wa lam yulad* | The refusal of every relation of generation; the mode by which the chain runs *from Him* without making anything be *of His essence*. |
| *Wa lam yakun lahu kufuwan ahad* | The closing negation of every *likeness*; parallel to *nothing is like Him* in Quran 42:11. |

The Prophet named this surah *equal to a third of the Quran by its weight*. Four lines, and the architecture of the head of the chain is settled in the Quran's own language; the surah carries no positive predicate that requires unpacking and no negation that introduces a No-Thing alongside Him.

## The Commander of the Believers' formula

A verbatim anchor, preserved with attribution.

> *Whoever describes Him has limited Him; whoever limits Him has counted Him; whoever counts Him has denied His pre-eternity. He is not described by direction, nor compared to a likeness, nor confined to a measure; He is the First whose firstness no firstness preceded, and the Last whose lastness no lastness will follow.*
> Imam the Commander of the Faithful, the book *The Path of Eloquence*, Sermon 91 (*the sermon the figures*), on the impossibility of describing the Lord without falling into limit.

The chapter's whole polemical labor has been to refuse every formulation that *describes Him, limits Him, counts Him*, and to land at the only honest language: the bare *huwa* in the verse of self-witness, and the negation *nothing is like Him* in the verse of incomparability.

## The Du'a of Arafa, the doctrine in devotional form

A verbatim anchor. The chapter's deepest devotional voice is the supplication of Imam Hussain ibn Ali on the Day of Arafa, prayed in the year before his martyrdom at Karbala.

> *Blind be the eye that does not see You watching over it. How can a proof be sought from what is itself in need of You in its very existence? Is there for any other than You a manifestation that is not Yours, that it might become the indicator of You? When were You absent that You should need an indicator to show You? And when were You far that traces should bring people to You?*
> *the Supplication of Arafa* of Imam Hussain, the supplication on the Day of Arafa, the closing apophatic section.

How the supplication settles the doctrine from the devotional side:

| The supplication's question | The chapter's parallel refusal |
|---|---|
| *How can a proof be sought from what is itself in need of You in its very existence?* | The philosopher's *signs, creativity, the Word, unity-as-cause* are each themselves creatures in need of Him; they cannot indicate Him. |
| *Is there for any other than You a manifestation that is not Yours, that it might become the indicator of You?* | The rank of His being-witness-to-Himself is more present than anything that might point to Him. The *self-witness* of Quran 3:18 *is* the manifestation. |
| *When were You absent that You should need an indicator to show You?* | The very *needing-an-indicator* is what The Harvest's chapter unwittingly affirmed when it set *creativity* between the Creator and the created. |

The Imam's *I know what you do not know* is here in another voice: the *one perfected man* prays what only the perfected man can pray, and by his praying, the *path to the Almighty* opens to those who *hold to him*.

## The closing settlement, the Imam and the two worships

A hierarchy and process flow. The chapter's salvific consequence: monotheism is difficult, and Allah selects *one man* with the perfection of reception.

The architecture of the settlement:

```
Allah
  |
  v
the angels who are near Him (al-malaika al-muqarrabun)
  |
  | overflow descends to
  v
the one perfected man, the Imam, peace be upon him
  |
  | the believers hold to him by
  v
the two worships (al-'ibadatan)
  |
  v
the apparent worship (the apparent)        the hidden worship (the hidden)
  - the sacred law                       - the inner interpretation (the interpretation)
  - the actions                         - the inner knowledge
  - the body of religion                - the spirit of religion
                  |             |
                  +-------+-----+
                          |
                          v
                  the path to the Abode of Holiness, Jerusalem
                  (the Abode of Holiness, Jerusalem)
                  a noble station
```

The terms of the perfection:

| Term | Author's meaning |
|---|---|
| *Perfection* (in the Imam) | *Being complete in accepting the overflow taking place in the natural world from the side of the angels who are near to Him* |
| *Dominance over the angels* | He will be promoted to *His whole*; God makes it obligatory upon them to obey and love him |
| *The believers' share* | They become *a part of him*; they share in his compassion for them |
| *The two worships* | *The apparent* and *the hidden*; *body and spirit of the religion*; neither dispensable; both held |
| *The path's requirement* | *Remembering death and what follows from it*, *the paradox of parents, children, relatives, and homeland*, *the certainty that nothing brings one to the promised time except through surplus* |
| *The surplus* | *The obligation that fulfills its right*, *good deeds*, *good actions that one performs*, *knowledge of the realities of things* |

## The book's closing prayer

A verbatim anchor. The chapter's closing is also the book's closing.

> *May God place us and the company of believers in the east of the earth and its west, those who conclude with goodness, and our provision and their intercession is the intercession of Muhammad, Ali, and the pure nation, and our Imam [the Ruler by the Command of God] and his fathers, peace be upon them, in the land of Jerusalem (the Abode of Holiness, Jerusalem) with His mercy and mercy.*
> *Kitab al-Riyad*, the closing prayer of Chapter Ten and the book.

> *Then we conclude the book with praise to God, Lord of the Worlds, and prayers upon the best of the first and the last, Muhammad, the family of the Imams, and the rightly guiding Imams. And we say: there is no power but from God Almighty; what God wills. And I ask God for forgiveness, and I delegate my matter to God; God is sufficient for me, yes the agent, yes the Lord, and yes the supporter. Praise be to God, until He is praised as is due; and prayers upon Muhammad and his good family, all of them, the pure.*
> *Kitab al-Riyad*, the very final lines.

The architecture that began at the Editor's Introduction with the four *dais* and the debate between *the two felicities* (Jonathan and the author of The Harvest), and ran joint-by-joint through nine chapters of cosmology, anthropology, and history, finds its rest at the rank above which there is no rank. *the Abode of Holiness, Jerusalem, a noble station*. The chain runs; the chain comes home; the speaker stops speaking, and gives the last word to the Imam in whose person the Command runs and to the prayer with which a treatise honestly ends.
