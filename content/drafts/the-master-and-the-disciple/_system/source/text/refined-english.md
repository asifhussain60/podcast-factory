The Book of the Master and the Boy

Author: Sayyidina Ja'far ibn Mansur al-Yaman

In the name of God, the Most Gracious, the Most Merciful.

(1) We have been informed that some groups of believers and some preachers of religion said to a Master among them: "You have set us at liberty by reminding us of what you have made us aware of, which we are obliged to fulfill — and that on three counts: thanks for that to which you have called us, thanks for the knowledge you have shown us, and thanks for the work you have ordered us to do. So show us, from each of these, what one who wills may do to render that thanks. We have understood, from the limits of religion, what is obligatory upon us and what we must do — the student in his question and what is required in his answer — and we have understood, from the doctrines of the righteous and the manners of the seekers, what is easy to say."

(2) The Master said to them: "The matter to which I have called you is the one I honor. May Allah be with His servants, and may He complete it for them and ennoble those who answer Him. He has made for the first of every thing a last, and for the last of every thing an end, and for every end a further end — and these are the houses of the hearts. Their keys are remembrance: the first of which is affliction, the middle of which is guidance, and the last of which is piety. So whoever has sought to know the structure of things and stood upon the limits of obligation that is within his reach, has intended guidance along the features of the request, and walked in it in the best way. As for thanking the Master, it is to obey him;

<!-- page 2 -->
as for thanking the knowledge, it is to act upon it and call to it; and as for thanking the work, it is to be patient with it and call to it.

(3) As for the doctrines of the righteous and the manners of the seekers, we have heard that a man from the people of Persia was struck by the calamity of ignorance — though he possessed a heart of [keen] retention, a sound intellect, and a culture he had acquired. Yet ignorance prevailed over him with regard to the knowledge we have mentioned, and he grew up in that state, wavering in the heat of thirst, reckoning the glittering mirage to be the whiteness of water — so that when he came to it, he found nothing of it, and he found Allah there [waiting] for him, and his reckoning was settled. Then He honored his resting-place and uncovered him from his cover, and found him in a light, and so guided him and found him out. He became his sustainer and enriched him with knowledge — so that he became one of the scholars of [Mount] Tur and one of the dwellers of the Bayt al-Ma'mur.

(4) When his guidance was completed and he had reached the end of his hope, it became incumbent upon him to give thanks to his Maker and to toil for his Lord. He had heard a saying from his father, one that was with him as the soul is with the body: "The best of good things is to raise the dead." So he said within himself: "I was dead and He revived me, ignorant and He taught me. I was not the first to be ignorant — that I might be the one to precede people to knowledge — and I am not the last to be ignorant — that teaching might end with me. In truth, I must be one who gives thanks for this blessing and who conveys this trust to those after me, just as it was conveyed to me by those before me. This matter is the first of God's commandments, and it has reached me only through many causes — the first reaching the second, the second the third — even if those who liked it [most] were the highest of God's creatures in the world. Then the veil was breached, and the doors stormed,

<!-- page 3 -->
until it reached me. So am I its endpoint and its end? No — those who transmitted it and were faithful to it had the prior claim in respect of gains and virtues; and if their gains are not mine, then I rest on the fact that I am the one who gained from them through their work. And if their bounty does not exempt me from work, then I remain as poor as I was poor when I first sought knowledge." He said this within himself, and he knew that his duty is the very thing that is obligatory upon him, and that his last command is, in judgment, the same as his first.

(5) So he left his family and his wealth for the sake of his Lord, answering Him and praying for the good, calling toward the obligation, [yielding to Him] the same thanksgiving that is His due. He passed through the peoples of the lands and through the tribes of the Arabs, scanning faces and casting questions — yet he found no answer and met no [fellow] seeker — until he ended at the far reaches of the island [al-Jazira]. He entered a city of it at a time when its people were heedless. Recalling the signs and verifying the marks, he saw a group of the people of that land quarreling aimlessly over religion and wading in the depths of caprice. So he sat down at a little distance, in seclusion from them, listening to their words and watching to see which of them had the best method.

(6) He said to himself: "These are the foremost in three qualities in living debate: the first is the conveying of the argument; the second is that they are of the people of the Umma; and the third is that they are closer in their awareness of religion, in their observation of it together, in their searching for it together — and the seeker is on the verge of meeting [what he seeks]."

(7) When they had finished their speech, they turned to him and said: "Who are you, and from where, boy?"

(8) He said: "I am a servant of Allah, and I am one of the dwellers of His sanctuary [Haram]."

(9) They said: "What is your need, and what is your work?"

<!-- page 4 -->
(10) He said: "My need has passed me by, and I am at work in seeking it."

(11) They said: "Did anything of what we said please you?"

(12) He said: "All of it is strange — to one who marvels."

(13) They said: "Wonder is in two senses — praiseworthy and reprehensible."

(14) He said: "Yes, I have learned that. So too, words are praiseworthy and reprehensible."

(15) They said: "What is the praiseworthy kind, in your view?"

(16) He said: "It is what is correct."

(17) They said: "And what is the correct?"

(18) He said: "That whose origin was from Allah, and which calls to Allah."

(19) They said: "And what is the reprehensible?"

(20) He said: "That whose origin was caprice, and which calls to something other than guidance."

(21) They said: "We believe you. Have you nothing of your own words for us to hear?"

(22) He said: "I have nothing to say — but I am a follower of [the right] saying."

(23) They said: "Then let us hear from your words what you may show us of your doctrine. We have wearied you of your condition, and may your brotherhood be a blessing to us. We hope that you will be a door of mercy that Allah has opened to us."

(24) He said: "Yes." So he listened to the people, and he received them with his face.

(25) Then he said: "He who created the creation by His power has made each thing equal to what He has decreed for it. He is the One who has brought forth the highways of the pasture, and made his outward and his inward free of caprices, [He] who is not ambiguous and is not [reached by] the study of mere traces — if imaginings are bewildered by the demand of Him, and minds neglect [the search for] His [essence], yet He does not appear in being so as to be possessed by His attributes or so that the intention of His path can be altered. Rather, He is generous: His knowledge is hidden by its very grace, and His secrets are protected by their [proper] family. His honor is the plumage of guidance; he is completed by the garment of piety, marked with the light of lights — a deposit in the breasts of the righteous, the keepers of [the Word], the honor of the scribes,

<!-- page 5 -->
a trust which they discharge and a limit which they do not exceed — it is the rule of judgment and the bounties of the Most Merciful. He honored him and shielded him, protected him and concealed him, persuaded him of his rights and entrusted to him His covenants. So the thoughts of the thinkers do not reach him, and the schemes of the schemers do not arrive at him. His outward is a remembrance and an evidence for those who are guided, and his inward is a sure drink, whose sky is brought low to the beholders and whose land is paved for the seekers — and [it has] witnessed stations for the students. He has a guide for those who walk to Him, and signs whose wonders follow one another — she points to Him by her parables. His path is preferred, his method laid out in detail, his rulings binding, his Sunna [are as] laws, and truthfulness has ennobled their doctrines, and justice has refined their gains. For every good there is a leader and a way, and He has made it a paradise and a guide to its inner [chamber]. Then minds were compelled, by ignorance and by want, to seek knowledge and capacity. Blessed is He who has made night and day a succession for those who would be reminded or would give thanks; and may Allah bless those whom He chose from among them to worship Him and made a warning to the worlds.

(26) Then he said: "Fear Allah, and fear those who fear Him, and fear Him with the fear of one who would please Him. Seek knowledge, learn, work, and have mercy."

(27) His eyes overflowed with tears, and so he broke off his speech, took leave of the people, rose, and set out on his journey. The people wept at the warmth and the eloquence of his sermon, and they wished to rise with him; but their reply, in order to preserve courtesy, was to celebrate with him in all this. They did not know what they had agreed to do, so they went off to their houses. He was followed by a youth from the group — the youngest and the most thoughtful of them — whose discipline had perfected his intellect and whose reflection had filled his heart, and this came on top of what he had heard from him

<!-- page 6 -->
of the Master's words, and of what had been laid before him and granted to him. The boy had a father who was a great sheikh of the Arabs — the most honorable and the most influential of them over the people of religion. So he kept the company of the Sheikh-Master, with his permission, until [the Master] reached the end of his journey. [The Master] then went in and brought the boy in with him. Then he called for food, and when they had finished their conversation and were settled in talk:

(28) The boy said: "O wise one! I have spoken and I have heard and I have preached; I was informed, and I sweetened the religion with the finest ornament and desired it with the best desire many times over, and I called to it by the noblest of its trusts. The minds yearned for your question, by the cracks your words made in the hearts. This excellence could only have appeared from one in whom the inward [batin] has been integrated as a teacher, and the outward [zahir] has expanded his dream — for he is the seventh fountain of wisdom from a firm origin, his blessings upon the worlds, and the branches of his branches are fruits for the seekers. I hope you may be one of those blessed branches, for what I see of [your] noble opinion, sound core, and perfect intellect. You have said that minds are compelled by ignorance to seek knowledge and capacity, and my mind is among the most needy and the most destitute. So may I have life from the way?"

(29) The Master said: "He who created the creatures by His power did not create them in vain, nor [as] scholars [from the start], nor does He abandon them in the misguidance of ignorance, nor is He content for them with stupidity in culture, nor with the garments of ignorance. He has made for them, to the exit out of all this, the way by which He composed their constitution and completed their creation. He made hearing, the eyes, and the hearts full of lights, as a sign of His dignity to them and as an argument against them. Then

<!-- page 7 -->
He made the treasures of His wisdom and the marks of His will [reside] in doctrines that satisfy Him in His elect from among His creation. He entrusted them with religion and called them to it, and imposed upon them the answering [of Him], by the instrument we have mentioned, so that God might distinguish the evil from the good. So the seeker is able, by his demand, to see — and the one ignorant of his own backwardness is [made known] — for there is no argument left to anyone against Allah after His Messengers and [no excuse for] going astray."

(30) The boy said: "The lack of any excuse has frightened me, and the existence of the evidence has made me desire [it]. I implore you for a proof, and I will be a servant to you."

(31) The Master said: "God did not create men [already] scholars. Rather, He created them as little children and ignorant ones who knew nothing. There is no great except after small, and no scholar except after ignorance. Smallness is the prelude to greatness, and ignorance is a guide to knowledge. So too, knowledge is not bearable except with the introduction of [some] knowledge prior to it — [knowledge] quicker than itself to the hearts and smoother to the understanding — so that he may be steadied in his first imaginings, the trials may be lightened for his obligations, the wings may shiver [in him] for it, and minds may bear its purpose. This is the Sunna of Allah in His servants, so let us not deviate, in religion, by our own opinion, nor follow with us anything other than the Sunna."

(32) The boy said: "I am too small for what I want, so raise me; I am ignorant of what I have asked for, so teach me; and I am bound by Your favor, so have mercy on me. You were [once] in this very situation, so God forbid that you should withhold the blessing — through my hands — of the greatness of His affliction upon you, for which He is to be thanked through you. I have learned the condition of the one who suffers the distress of ignorance and the need for the weakness of resourcefulness."


<!-- page 8 -->
(33) The scholar said: "The state you have described is indeed as you say, but I will not show you mercy by doing for you what should be done with your own effort. Every claim is accepted only when it is truthful; knowledge does not benefit unless it is acted upon, and action does not benefit unless it is accepted. The truthfulness of speech is its justice, and the proof of knowledge lies in the public ways and documented exemplars that have endured throughout the lands and through which night and day have differed. Do not pursue an argument carelessly, do not dismiss one carelessly, and do not accept one on imitation alone — lest the first doubt that rises against it tear it out of your heart. Do not reject the proof of an argument either, lest falsehood find an opening; and do not let your own opinion render inquiry and consensus pointless. Think well of me without reservation, strive in your questioning without suspicion, seek the religion, seek one like yourself, and seek God's help in your affair. God does not let the seeker's reward go to waste, and does not deprive of reward anyone He sees seeking the good."

(34) The boy said: "You have dealt justly with yourself in a way that is both fitting and effective, and you have set forth the truth in the best of articulations. I ask God's help, and I ask you about this matter you are calling me to: what is it, whose is it, and to whom does it belong?"

(35) The scholar said: "It is the religion of Allah that He has accepted, and from whom it can be accepted is no one but Him. He delivered it first to the elite among His Messengers, and then in general to all His creation. The one specially favored owes thanks for it, action upon it, and a call to others to take it up; the one deprived of it owes the seeking after it so that he may come to know it, and obedience to the one who guides him to it."

(36) The boy said: "This favor that Allah has singled out for His friends — are all such favors equal in their excellence, or do some surpass others?"

(37) The scholar said: "Have we not said that every form of knowledge has its predecessor who taught it, and that each predecessor carried his own portion, so that they stand at graded ranks before their Lord, each one charged with —

<!-- page 9 -->
— what he has acquired of knowledge? Then every one of them is set right in his creation by the merit of his bestowal, and it becomes a mercy upon His servants."

(38) The boy said: "This thing you are calling me to — is it that favor itself, or is it something other than it?"

(39) The scholar said: "It is neither identical with it nor wholly other than it — it is from it, like a single chamber of water drawn from the breadth of the Euphrates. I call you to what Allah has accepted for His servants and honored. It is the day on which I complete it — precious, secure, generous, hidden. Its honor with its reward is dignity for the one who lifts it up; its retribution is debasement for the one who loses it. Its outward form is figures, and its inner reality is knowledge. Its wisdom is a yoke, and its justice is plain. This is what I call you to."

(40) The boy said: "What has made you more exclusively entitled to this matter than others? If you claim it is from God for creation in general, then everyone has the same claim as you. What has made you more deserving of the name of truth than they — has this matter come to you bypassing them?"

(41) The scholar said: "There is no scholar who speaks any word of truth except that he has taken it from the very origins from which we ourselves have taken it; in this they agree with us, by acknowledging it from us and among themselves. What then separated us from them was their abandonment of those principles while we held fast to them: we obeyed where they disobeyed, we preserved what we were entrusted with where they squandered it, we did what we were commanded where they neglected. So they agree with us in acknowledgement and disagree with us in fact. Their acknowledgement is a witness for us; their loss is the sign of their poverty toward us and of their need for what is in our hands. They are like brothers who inherited great wealth from their father and divided it between themselves: some squandered it until they fell into poverty, while others kept it, preserved it, traded with it, and the wealth in their hands multiplied. Then the poor among the brothers turned to the wealthier ones, to serve them and seek their bounty. So too those who have lost the way of their Messengers turn, in their ignorance, to seek knowledge from the people of the original sources, the generous ones. The proof that we are more deserving of the truth than they is their need of us and their dependence on what is in our hands —

<!-- page 10 -->
— so that our knowledge has become the call that answers their ignorance, our garment covers their nakedness, and our abundance satisfies their hunger. So the truth is binding upon those who would be virtuous, the credit goes to the one who confers favor, and thanks is owed to the one who bestows kindness."

(42) The boy said: "As for your superiority over them, that has become clear; and as for the argument of truth, it has been established for every honest seeker. But what is your argument against me, when I already agree with what you have said, stand by what you have described, deny what you have denied, and recognize the truth and its excellence as well as falsehood and its people?"

(43) The scholar said: "My argument against you is the same as my argument against them — through my disagreement with them in description and my agreement with them in meaning."

(44) The boy said: "How is that, when I acknowledge the truth to its owner, and they deny it?"

(45) The scholar said: "If their denial is what keeps you back from joining them, they too have refrained from what is forbidden to them. So what is the benefit of acknowledgement without an agent to put it into effect?"

(46) The boy said: "Then take me to the people of truth that I may know them — or to an agent who can prove it to them. Show me which of the two you can do."

(47) The scholar said: "Do you not see that you have been made to need the proof of the rightful owner of the truth, or of his agent, just as the public has been compelled to need a proof of the truth? Action has separated you from them, even as ignorance has joined you with them."

(48) The boy said: "I have believed, and the matter has become clear to me."

(49) The scholar said: "Have I not firmly established my argument against you and against the public?"

(50) He said: "Yes."

(51) The scholar said: "Can anyone prove to others a truth he holds for himself?"

<!-- page 11 -->
(52) He said: "No one is able to do that."

(53) The scholar said: "Then the argument has been established against you and against the public. Nothing remains but submission, and the appointment of the one whom you cannot do without and who can do without you."

(54) The boy said: "As for my poverty, I acknowledge it. As for the One who is rich while I am in need — I ask of Him, and I ask Him to have mercy on me."

(55) The scholar said: "Have you ever seen someone driven by poverty to seek aid from a man of bounty obtain anything from him except by acquisition or by favor?"

(56) The boy said: "Yes — nothing can be obtained except by acquisition or by favor."

(57) The scholar said: "And does the people of favor reach the people of grace by way of acquisition?"

(58) He said: "No."

(59) The scholar said: "Then seek this matter by work, and you will be granted it as a merit. Do not seek it by flattery, or you will be deprived of it."

(60) The boy said: "You are the key to goodness and its guide. Show me the most meaningful of the two paths and the nearest to guidance. I am the most destitute of those who follow you."

(61) The scholar said: "O my son, there is nothing more for you, and nothing more is expected of me. What you have hoped from me has reached you — but there are limits not to be transgressed and conditions not to be forgotten."

(62) The boy said: "Set the limits you love, and lay down the conditions you approve. I hope you will find me patient under them, and grateful for your kindness."

<!-- page 12 -->
(63) The scholar said: "The first of the limits is to uphold the outward meaning of the Book and its laws, and to act upon what you know of it. Whoever does for God what he knows, God will guide him to what he does not know."

(64) The boy said: "There are many Books, and all of them are from Allah. For every Book there is a scholar from among creation who adheres to it, and they are all united in safeguarding it and in the religion it contains."

(65) The scholar said: "We have returned to our earlier words, and we now need to settle what should not have needed settling."

(66) The boy said: "How is that?"

(67) The scholar said: "By your saying that the Books are many, and that all of them are from Allah, and that each has a scholar of its own. Have they then issued the verdict that the words of God and His Books cancel one another out — that the first of them gives the lie to the last, and the last gives the lie to the first?"

(68) The boy said: "I do not think so. But what is the proof of it?"

(69) The scholar said: "If created beings had been able to do what is in the first Book, then it would have pointed them to the second; and if they did what is in the second, it would have pointed them to the third — so that the work culminates in the last of them, because the last is what the earlier ones were pointing toward. Even though all of them are from Allah, the later is nearer to the present age and clearer in doctrine, since it copies and confirms what came before it, and nothing came after it to copy or supersede it."

(70) The boy said: "I have believed; the knowledge of the truth and the sign of truthfulness have been shown to me. I am holding to the limits you have ordered me to keep — so what are your conditions for me?"

(71) The scholar said: "My conditions for you are five: do not be absent if I summon you; do not conceal anything from me if I ask you; do not question me until I answer you; do not request anything until I begin with you; and do not mention what passes between us to your father."

(72) The boy said: "I will do all that you have mentioned — it is easy for me set against what I have hoped from you. But how can I manage the matter of my father? He is one of the heaviest trials upon me, and one of the heaviest I have to bear — so will you permit me to remove myself from him?"

<!-- page 13 -->
(73) The scholar said: "Withdrawing from him would be a sign to others. Rather, stand with him and his estate with some outward agreement, and keep all this your secret and conceal it. God will be enough for us against his evil and the evil of others."

(74) The narrator said: Then the scholar and the boy took leave of each other, and they were apart for a long time. The boy remained perplexed about his affair: he knew nothing but what the law had brought him to stop at, did not know where the scholar was settled, and was unable to seek him out under the condition imposed on him, even as the absence lengthened — while the scholar showed him his own patience and endurance for what it cost him. At last, on a quiet night when creation was still and the informers had grown forgetful, when they had settled together in their assembly, the boy chanced upon him as his need pressed close and he was able to be alone with his companion. He rejoiced over him; and when he raised his head —

(75) The scholar said to him: "I saw you prostrating in thanksgiving. Thanksgiving before the work inherits the obligation; thanksgiving cut short after the work makes the works themselves vain."

(76) The boy said: "All your words are truthful, and all your works are mastery. I thank you for your kindness, and I hope from you for yet more of it."

(77) The scholar said: "Thanksgiving is owed because gratitude is what makes thanksgiving increase. Your right has been fulfilled — so come to me with your heart, open your understanding, and listen to what is presented to you."

(78) The boy said: "I have brought the instrument of understanding and I have taken up the instrument of hearing. Say what seems good to you."

(79) The scholar said: "Religion has a key that opens it and closes it, like that which distinguishes between forbidden union and lawful marriage."

(80) The boy said: "This key you have mentioned is great in the sight of Allah, since it is what distinguishes between the lawful and the unlawful, between truth and falsehood. So what is it?"

<!-- page 14 -->
(81) The scholar said: "It is the covenant of Allah which establishes His rights, gathers His obligations, makes Paradise binding for His friends — the rope of God upon His earth, and the safe haven among His servants. I will recite it to you, and bind you to it."

(82) The boy said: "Yes — take from me whatever you love. I do not refuse your opinion, and I do not transgress your command."

(83) The narrator said: Then the scholar set about reciting the covenant to the boy, conveying it to him and binding him fast by it, while the boy could not contain himself for awe, and his tears poured down from the intensity of the instruction, until he came to the end of the covenant. He praised God and glorified Him, and thanked Him for what He had granted him; and he understood that, by accepting their pledge, he had become part of the Party of God (Hizb Allah) and of the company of His friends. Then he fell silent in his composure — and the scholar began to expound and explain.

(84) The first thing the scholar said to him by way of explanation, regarding the first of the causes of the manifest order and the beginning of its creation, was this: God is the Creator of things and the One who composes them. He created them and originated them — He is not the first of every first created thing, He having taken that as an evidence for what was created, but rather the First of all firsts and the Inheritor of every thing. He began the creation of what was created out of light, from which three words branched. The first of them is the will; from the will branched the command; and the command is divided into "saying" — that He says "Be" to what He wills, and it is. So the first creation is the will, then the command, then the saying.

(85) These are three words, from which "Kun" — two letters — is derived, and "fa-yakun" — five letters; that is seven letters, from which seven things branch. From the light He created —

<!-- page 15 -->
— air, which is the first of them: singular without consort, unique among things, a mixture surrounding them but not surrounded by any of them. It is the proof of the One concerning whom "there is nothing like Him." Then He created out of these three words: water, darkness, and light. Then He created the heavens and the earth. He created smoke out of water, He created condensation out of the heat of darkness, and He created fire out of the light. These are three things branching from three things before them: each became consort to its origin because it was created from it. And the seventh, air, encompasses all of them. This was the beginning of creation and the beginning of things.

(86) When creation had been established on seven principles, and the path of forms had been laid down on the basis of likeness and analogy, He created out of smoke seven heavens and their signs, and He created out of condensation seven earths and their powers. These seven branch from those seven origins.

(87) Then we look to the signs of the heavens and the powers of the earth — what are they, and what is the proof of them? We return to the three words and their letters which created the seven, namely "the will to command by saying." That is twelve letters; and its signs in heaven are the twelve constellations, its witnesses are seven kingdoms, its signs upon the earth are twelve, ten of seven islands, and its proofs are seventeen seas. These are the signs of the heavens and the earth — through which we pass, and which are presented to us.


<!-- page 16 -->
(88) Then the heavens and the earth were veiled in darkness from the black heat, which is the authority of the earth. Then He pierced it with His lights, and light and darkness were intermingled — the two distinct realities. He distinguished between them by night and day, with two signs. He made the night, and its sign a way (minhāj) of darkness, and apportioned it; and He made the day, and its sign a way of light, and apportioned it. He adorned the night with its darkness — by the moon and the stars — and the day shines with its light, gleaming through the sign of the sun. Then the days produced states between night and day, which are the seven days. Their signs: each day has twelve hours, and each night has twelve hours.

(89) When the creation of things was combined with these principles, He made them in pairs — male and female. I do not mean male and female in the literal sense, but in the sense of the active and the receptive. Then the sunna (custom) of creation in pairs went forth, and He created all its essences: animals — humans, beasts, birds, and crawling creatures. Then this sunna extended into speech, and these too became pairs: adjectives and descriptions, names and meanings. Glory be to Him who created all pairs, and made them manifestations of His command, exemplars of His religion, and signs of His power — pointing by things to their origins, and by parables to their interpretations.

(90) Then He pierced it with His light, opened it with His command, created it by His knowledge, and sealed it among His names with the letters of "In the name of Allah, the Most Gracious, the Most Merciful." When He completed all of them by His making and composed them in pairs by His measure, He selected for Himself, from the choicest of His will and the secrets of His unseen, great knowledge and clear light. He made these a religion by which God draws His creation to Himself. Then He honored it with laws and books, made it manifest by parables and the sunna, and appointed from the choicest of His creation banners, signs, and rulers. He made them the houses of His prophethood, the treasuries of His knowledge and wisdom, His interpreters and the bearers of His revelation. Their succession in His earth is evidence of the path of salvation, both in the apparent (ẓāhir) and in the inner (bāṭin). Some of them are imams and speakers (nāṭiqs); some are arguments (ḥujaj) and chiefs (nuqabā'); among them are the summoners (du'āt) to the good and the learned. In their dwellings they are like the stars of heaven —

<!-- page 17 -->
— they were called the works, and the parables were called by them. Their imam is like the great sun among the lights, surpassing all sight; he is their apparent and their likeness. The argument and the gate of their imam is like the shining moon — its appearance and its likeness. Their summoners are like the manifest stars (zawāhir) — their appearances and their likenesses.

(91) All the pairs I have described to you are apparent and inward, and likewise figure and likeness. Every man's saying falls away, and the Creator is foremost over creation and over the command, and every day He is engaged in some affair, and His action is not to be denied — even if He sends a warner every day."

<!-- page 85 -->
(510) Abu Malik said: "This community has gone far astray."

(511) Salih said to him: "Most of the first two went astray before them — for they cast off the guardians of Allah, abandoned their rights, and allowed the foolish among them to prevail in their command, so they made lawful what Allah had forbidden, deliberately, lest they have to imitate prophethood — so people might lose track of the prophets and not kill them on that account. Yet they distracted them with kingship and largesse and led them to indulge their whims. Those known for honesty and piety on the surface helped them in this — until the community was stripped of those who would command good, and led toward those who would forbid it; and they called the partisans of falsehood the guardians of Allah, so that truth became falsehood — like light and darkness in their difference. Were it not for the remnants of Allah's guardians and His justice in the earth, and the revelation that comes to them, only a few of the honest would have remained against the many of the false. Otherwise, by what could Allah distinguish the evil from the good — except by His servants? And how could Allah's argument be conveyed to all creation, except through them?"

(512) Abu Malik said: "There is no escape and no avoidance of the justice of Allah and His command. With the multiplicity of opposites, none but the just servants of Allah remain — and by them the argument of Allah against His creation is upheld."

(513) Salih said to him: "Then it is established that the company is one of the prophets of Allah — namely, the just ones — and is the one among them called 'the just'?"

(514) He said: "Yes, he is the just one, as it is said: 'So-and-so testified, and he was just among the just.'"

<!-- page 86 -->
(515) Salih said to him: "It has been established for you that the just one is the guardian of Allah — to the extent that you say it as a description, and the description holds?"

(516) He said: "Yes, this is true as a saying."

(517) Salih said to him: "Whoever does not understand the one being described, will the description benefit him?"

(518) He said: "The description does not benefit him except by understanding the one being described."

(519) Salih said: "Then whoever does not know the just one of Allah and His guardian, will his speech be just?"

(520) The narrator said: Abu Malik smiled and said: "You have brought me out of my chapter on justice and monotheism, and what has brought me out of it is the truth itself, in the clearest of arguments and the kindest of speech. After this, what chapter remains to be proved?"

(521) Salih said to him: "From here, O Abu Malik, the camouflaged glass falls away from those who deal in the colors of jewels — they could not recognize its virtue, and when it is set before me, the essentialists are exposed, and those who are deceived by them are exposed."

(522) Abu Malik said: "I have believed and acted accordingly. But how shall we be safe from the [seducers of the] nations, and know the causes of Allah, or the cause among them, when we do not perceive them in their time — and in our time there are neither prophets nor messengers, as there were in the nations before us?"

(523) Salih said to him: "Glory be to Allah, O Abu Malik — you keep speaking of the justice of Allah at every moment of your life, but as soon as We have proved it to you in your own presence, where it is obligatory upon you, you turn back and say that you would be deceived about Allah, and attribute injustice to Him."


<!-- page 87 -->
(524) Abu Malik said: "God forbid — whoever says such a thing has transgressed. He has lied against Allah. So where did you take me as committed to that?"

(525) Salih said: "You say the age of the prophets has passed and that in our own time there is neither prophet nor messenger. Was the sending of those earlier two ranks simply for the sake of being worshipped, or was it in vain?"

(526) He said: "May Allah forbid — only what is just issues from Him."

(527) Salih said: "Then what has excluded us from that justice? Are we not worshippers as they were? They were created as we were created, and we are bound in service as they were bound. Has the obligation been lifted from us after it was made binding on them? Or has the giving run out in our time, so that we cling to Him only by virtue of what was given to them? Or has the Sunnah of Allah changed in our case after being firmly established in those who came before us — so that we should judge that God has changed? Or is justice a duty upon us while its instrument is left lawful for us to neglect, when it was forbidden to those before us — when there is no substitute for the words of Allah and no overruling of His judgment? And who deserves the name 'just' except the guardians of Allah? Justice is not found among those God created, except in those whom God Himself chose — they became just by His command. That is why a judge is called 'just' for the sake of the one who appointed him, and a true word is called 'truthful' because of the truthfulness of the one who speaks it."

(528) Abu Malik said: "It is indeed so, and we are not permitted to say anything of God except the truth. Were we to follow our own opinion in speech and judgment in the absence of the rightly-guided among us, we would have abandoned His judgment. This is part of Allah's ruling and His Sunnah with us, just as it was with those before us during the interregna between one messenger and the next — like the period between Abraham and the time of Moses and the Torah, and the period between the Torah and
<!-- page 88 -->
the time of Jesus and the Gospel. We have been on that same footing, working through the interregnum by relying on the traditions and the Sunnah."

(529) Salih said to him: "The religion of Allah is too precious to Him for that. Was there really an interregnum after the religion of Abraham, when Allah sent after him Ishmael, Isaac, Jacob, Joseph, Jonah, and Shu'ayb, leading up to Moses? Then He inspired them with righteous deeds and the call to the religion of Abraham, the establishment of prayer by its Sunnah, and the giving of zakat by its obligation, until the Torah was given to Moses. So what interregnum stood between Moses and the Gospel, when Allah sent Joshua son of Nun after Moses, and Elias, Talut, David, Solomon, Zechariah, and John, leading up to Jesus? They stood by the ruling of the Torah in its religion, calling to it and reciting its Psalms, until the Gospel was revealed to Jesus. The same applies to those who passed before them and those who came after them. We have seen great evidence among them — only Allah knows their full count — and their messengers brought them proofs. Allah did not leave them; He set parables for them, and He was the one calling them to the truth. So where is this 'interregnum,' and when was it? Allah Himself said, 'After a succession of messengers,' and so confirmed the messengers. The period after the rising of the call came after fear, so they prayed in secret and concealment. Why then would they cut themselves off from the earth? Allah has made it so that, in the blink of an eye, they are either openly present or hidden in fear, veiled."

(530) Abu Malik said: "This is an irrefutable statement, a proven word in which there is no doubt. As for the proof of Allah's argument through justice and the succession of messengers — that has been established and confirmed. What then is the meaning of their absence in our own time?"
<!-- page 89 -->
(531) Salih said to him: "What was the reason for Moses' absence when he went out of Pharaoh's city and passed in fear, waiting until he reached the land of Shu'ayb?"

(532) Abu Malik said: "He fled from Pharaoh's people, hiding from them, because he feared for himself when they had agreed to kill him."

(533) Salih said to him: "Have you heard that any of the just — the just ones of Allah and the sons of His prophets — was killed while patiently bearing the burdens of the religion?"

(534) Abu Malik said: "Yes — many of them have been killed."

(535) Salih said to him: "Then what is the justice of this age that appears to you and to the people of your time? Is there one whom God sends down to you after death? Or one fleeing from among you whom you would force back to the killing he escaped, when he has followed Allah's example in His prophets and messengers — patient under fear and concealment until Allah judges between him and us, and who is the best of judges?"

(536) Abu Malik said: "There is no shame on the one who follows the Sunnah of the righteous by fearing for himself and fleeing from his people. The shame is on the wicked nations and the helpers of the darknesses for killing the prophets of Allah and His just ones, and for their unanimous agreement upon that act."
<!-- page 90 -->
(537) Salih said: "What do you say about one who kills the just of Allah and His proof — where is his end? And what of the one who helped him?"

(538) Abu Malik said: "Both are in the Fire — and so too is anyone who raised a banner or carried a flag in their cause."

(539) Salih said to him: "What do your jurists say about this?"

(540) Abu Malik said: "They show mercy to the slain, but see no disobedience in the killer."

(541) Salih said to him: "Whoever obeys the killer voluntarily — is he not a partner with him in the blood of the slain?"

(542) Abu Malik said: "Yes, by Allah — he is his partner, and even more wicked than he."

(543) Salih said to him: "His companionship is acknowledged in his helping the killer — so how is he not worse than him?"

(544) Abu Malik said: "Were it not for the narrations of the jurists, no tyrannical king could ever be established."

(545) Salih said to him: "You have spoken the truth, O Abu Malik. I have laid this on you yourself: with their king, their house, their land, and their soldiers stationed as your neighbors, while we have been taken as their slaves. And you are their just ones — you judge them by your knowledge, you uphold their kingship, you have called them by your questioning, and you have thanked them for what you needed from them. You placed on their stockings the crown of your justice, and they grew proud;
<!-- page 91 -->
you covered their clay with the robes of the sacred law, and they went too far; you lit up their falsehood with the idleness of your truth. So the weak were enraged by your conduct — those who were scholars like you — and they said, 'These are the jurists of the umma; we have in them an example.' You went astray by your own reasoning, and you led others astray by the reasoning of the mighty. You supposed the one really in office was someone other than yourself, and you forgot the words of Allah Almighty: 'And do not incline toward those who have done wrong, lest the Fire touch you.' So you severed what Allah commanded to be joined, while seeing it, and you spoke about the outward forms of justice while being confused by it. You came across His saying: 'Obey Allah and obey the Messenger and those in authority among you,' and you made these darknesses your authorities, even when they disbelieved. And you forgot the words of Allah Almighty: 'Fight those who disbelieve in you' — so you disobeyed those whom you were commanded to obey, and obeyed those whom you were commanded to fight, all the while thinking you were doing well. So you have nullified and gone too far. Your opinion has reached the point of claiming that Allah will not send a messenger or a warner in your age. Is it that the goodness of your deeds has put you beyond need of the guardians of Allah for the argument? Or has the ugliness of your deeds cut you off from hope in Allah's acceptance of repentance? Or is it just to judge God without justice — and to be satisfied for your Messenger with what you would not be satisfied with for yourselves in his place? Would any one of you accept that

his fifteen-year-old son be killed, or that his house and money be taken from him unjustly?"

(546) Abu Malik said: "No one is satisfied with that. Even when one of us has a child killed, or is driven out from his property by the anger and rage of his people, we do not consider ourselves content."

(547) Salih said to him: "Allah and His Messenger have a right that you be angry on their behalf, if His vicegerent is killed and His guardian is driven out of the land."

(548) Abu Malik said: "It is dear to us that the guardian of Allah be killed among us or driven from his land. But they have deliberately absented themselves from us, and the mighty have appeared in their place."

(549) Salih said to him: "Had you supported the friends of Allah, they would have appeared; had you failed His enemies, they would not have prevailed. But the verdict has bound you to the guardians of Allah in three ways: the killer of the ruler does not tire of seeking and is not healed even when his case is nailed down — he is content with the stinging bite at his fingertips, out of anger; they do not stir up their enemy and do not turn from them even in their heedlessness; and out of hatred — because they believe in love for the guardians of Allah — they have no insight into faith and no trustworthiness in confidentiality. For this reason the guardians of Allah have absented themselves from you by Taqiyya, and for this reason the great creatures who took their own desires as their God, and seized the goods of this world, have done what is on a par with killing the prophets. They were ignorant of the fault of their conduct, so they did not hold themselves accountable for the injustice they committed, and were not humbled into repenting for what they had done; instead they returned to attributing the injustice of their own deeds to Allah, and they said: 'Allah will not send messengers after these thirteen messengers, nor will He send
<!-- page 93 -->
a warner.' So they made the killing of the guardians of Allah lawful unjustly — they were indeed unjust, and they wronged Him by that saying and by promising a lie."

(550) Abu Malik said: "Yes — this is the description of this nation of ours, and this is exactly what they say. So where is the salvation from this, and where is the escape from the punishment of Allah?"

(551) Salih said to him: "By Allah, take refuge in Him and repent to Him — He accepts repentance from you. Ask Him for the way of His guardians, and He will save you by your works from the deeds of those who have done wrong. So it is with this creation: if they repent to Allah, He accepts their repentance — and what does Allah do with His punishment of them then? Allah does not oppress them by the weight of an atom, even when they are unjust and lie; and He does not hasten them with punishment, even when they hasten to commit disobedience."

(552) Abu Malik said: "I seek refuge from the punishment of Allah by repenting to Him and by seeking the means to His satisfaction. That belongs to the student in you and to the guide in his guidance — your Lord will reward you."

(553) Salih said to him: "If you do that, you will find Allah pardoning of you. He loves repentance, and you will find me careful in guiding you, watchful over you."

(554) Then Salih's eyes welled with tears at the mention of repentance, and he interrupted his speech. He encouraged them and ordered them to go to his father's house. And he went in to his father — the scholar who had first sent him out — and consulted him about the matter of Abu Malik and his companions.

(555) The scholar said: "You know your companions. If you know what is best for them, guide them with your guidance; and when you warn them, do not let it move you to inflict

The one who desires good is not hidden, and Allah will not abandon you behind your back; He will open for you, from the light of His success, the gates of guidance that He has appointed for him, and will show you the deeds of those who turn back to Him."
