Chapter 00 — Before the Door Opens

Before entering Kitaab al-Aa-lim wal-Ghulaam, The Master and the Disciple, it helps to know what kind of book this is.

It is not a simple tale of a teacher giving answers to a student. It is a story of seeking, testing, speech, silence, obedience, doubt, recognition, and the gradual opening of meaning. The Aa-lim, the master of knowledge, does not teach as a lecturer filling time. The Ghulaam, the young disciple, does not learn as a listener collecting interesting ideas. Their relationship is more serious than that. It is a path in which knowledge must be earned, carried, tested, and lived.

The book belongs to the early Ismaili Shi-i tradition and is associated with Ja-far ibn Mansoor al-Yaman, one of the important early Ismaili authors. Its form is dramatic and conversational. The teaching unfolds through meetings, questions, challenges, journeys, and changes in the lives of the people involved. The reader is not handed a neat summary of doctrine at the beginning. Instead, the reader is asked to walk with the characters as they learn how difficult true seeking can be.

At the center of the book is the question of how a person comes to know. The answer is not treated as merely intellectual. The seeker must learn how to ask, how to listen, how to obey, how to wait, and how to recognize when speech is true and when it is only attractive noise. The book is concerned with religion, but it is just as concerned with the condition of the person who approaches religion. A confused heart can turn even noble words into confusion. A prepared heart can hear guidance in a single sentence.

The story begins among people who are already close to religious concern. They are not careless people. They speak about truth, guidance, duty, gratitude, and the proper conduct of seekers. Yet the book shows that concern is not the same as understanding. Wanting the truth is only the beginning. The seeker must also become capable of receiving it.

This is why the early conversations move carefully. The master does not rush to satisfy curiosity. He pays attention to readiness. He distinguishes between speech that leads toward Allaah and speech that follows passion. He shows that learning is not simply the transfer of information from one mind to another. It is the formation of a person.

For a modern listener, this matters immediately. Today, knowledge is often treated as something available on demand. One searches, clicks, listens, saves, and moves on. But this book comes from a world in which knowledge is a trust. The question is not only, “Can I access it?” The question is, “Am I prepared to carry it?” A person may gather many religious words, philosophical ideas, or spiritual impressions, and still remain unchanged. The book does not admire that kind of learning.

As the story continues, the reader enters the relationship between the outer form and the inner meaning. The Ismaili language of zaahir and baatin is important here. Zaahir is the outward form, what appears and can be seen. Baatin is the inner meaning, what gives the outward form its depth. The book does not reject the outward form. It asks the seeker to understand why the form exists and what it points toward. This movement from outward form to inner meaning is called taa-weel.

A helpful modern comparison is the difference between using an application and understanding the system beneath it. A user sees buttons, screens, menus, and messages. That is the visible layer. Beneath it are rules, permissions, code, and architecture. The visible layer is real, but it is not the whole reality. In the same way, the book treats religious practice, speech, symbols, and events as doors into deeper order. The listener should pay attention to how often the story moves from what is seen to what is meant.

The book also gives great weight to rightful guidance. The seeker does not appoint himself as his own final authority. He encounters teachers, tests, ranks, and responsibilities. This is not presented as blind submission to personality. It is presented as the discipline required for a person who wants more than scattered opinions. The book asks whether guidance is a living relationship or only a set of inherited words.

That question becomes sharper as the story widens beyond the first seeker. Family, community, religious loyalty, debate, and public reputation all enter the narrative. The search for truth does not remain private. It disturbs assumptions. It forces people to examine what they have inherited, what they defend, and what they truly understand. The book is careful here. It does not praise rash rebellion, but it also does not praise comfortable imitation. It asks for justice, patience, courage, and examination.

Several names will recur. Saa-lih is the young seeker whose path becomes central to the story. Al-Bakh-ta-ree is his father. Aboo Maa-lik is a learned figure who enters the discussion with caution and seriousness. The Shaykh and the greater Shaykh represent levels of guidance that shape the seeker’s path. These names should be pronounced clearly and consistently: Saa-lih, Al-Bakh-ta-ree, Aboo Maa-lik, and Shaykh.

Some key terms should also be heard carefully. Allaah means God. Sharee-ah refers to the revealed path of religious practice. Taa-weel is the disciplined interpretation that returns outward symbols to their inner meaning. Zaahir is the outward aspect. Baatin is the inner aspect. Wilaayah refers to spiritual allegiance and rightful devotion. Hujjah means proof or authoritative argument. Daa-ee means one who summons or calls others toward guidance. Imaam refers to the rightful spiritual guide.

The listener should not expect the book to behave like a modern self-help chapter, academic lecture, or ordinary novel. It moves by dialogue, symbol, challenge, and recognition. Some passages may feel dense because they are meant to be contemplated, not skimmed. The best way to hear the book is to follow the movement of the conversation: who is asking, why the question is being asked, what the answer tests, and how the person changes afterward.

This preface should not explain the whole journey before it begins. The book itself must do that work. It is enough to enter with a few questions in mind. What makes a seeker ready? What separates true speech from impressive speech? Why does the outward form need an inner meaning? How does gratitude become action? What happens when inherited certainty is placed under examination? And what kind of person must one become in order to receive knowledge without reducing it to another possession?

With those questions in mind, the listener can now enter the first chapter. The door opens not with spectacle, but with gratitude, humility, and the first signs of a search that will demand far more than curiosity.

# Chapter 1 — Scholar and Seeker

It has been narrated to us that a number of the truly faithful and a group among those who call people to true religion once said to one of their scholars: ‘You have liberated us by helping us learn about an affair of great importance, for which we feel obligated to show our gratitude for three reasons:

1. Our gratitude for you having called us to the true religion
2. Our gratitude for the knowledge that you directed us to
3. Our gratitude for the religious practices you ordered us to perform.

So inform us how we should express our gratitude for these three reasons. Then inform us about:
- the rights and duties that are obligatory on us as directed by the ordinances of religion;
- and the obligations of a seeker in his mode of questioning,
- and the obligations on the responses of the scholar being sought.

And inform us, to the best of your ability, about the ways of the righteous and the proper conduct of the seekers.’

The scholar replied: ‘The matter to which I invite all of you is the religion that Allaah has bestowed as an honor on His servants; which He has perfected for them and through which He honors whoever responds to Him. So for each beginning, He has placed in it an end, and for each end, a goal; and each goal has a limit whose full extent cannot be attained. These are the way-stations of the people of true understanding. Their key is constantly remembering Allaah, their initial levels are trials; the middle ones are of right guidance and the final ones are piety. So whoever is seeking to learn the basics of Allaah's affairs, and then discovers the ranks of the divinely determined (hudood ad-deen al-maqdoor), he has sought guidance from the right sources and has set upon it in the best possible manner.
As for showing gratitude to the scholar (who guided you), that is through his obedience;
and the gratitude for the knowledge (he gave you), is putting it into action and calling others to it;
and the gratitude for the right practice (he taught you), is striving patiently in it and in calling others to it.

Gratitude here is not treated as a feeling. It is treated as a responsibility. In ordinary life, this is like being trained by a master craftsperson: thanking the teacher does not end with praise. It shows itself when the apprentice practices the craft correctly, preserves its standards, and teaches it responsibly to someone else. In the same spirit, Ali, peace be upon him, is reported to have taught that knowledge is better than wealth because knowledge guards the one who carries it. That line fits this passage exactly: knowledge becomes a trust that protects the seeker only when it is put into action.

Scholar: Obedience
Knowledge: Putting knowledge into action
Practice: Remaining steadfast

As for teaching you the ways of the righteous and the proper conduct of the seekers, the following story has been narrated to us about a man from Persia who was among those subject to the trial of spiritual ignorance. Although he had a rich heart and a brilliant mind, and had acquired a refined culture and education, nonetheless his ignorance outweighed his knowledge, because of his earlier experiences and the environment in which he grew up. So he was restless in the burning heat of his thirst, assuming the glimmering of mirages to be the reflection of water, until he reached it, and found it to be nothing. However, there he found Allaah, and paid Him his account in full! Then Allaah honored his abode and removed the veil in front of his intellect. He found him straying, so He guided him; He found him in need, so He satisfied him with knowledge. Thus by His graces he eventually became one of the scholars of Sinai and the angelic dwellers in the well-populated temple.

Finally when his guidance had been completed and he reached the goals of his aspiration, he was duty-bound to show gratitude to his Master and to exert himself for his Lord. He used to hear his spiritual father repeat a proverb, which held the rank of soul to his body: ‘The most excellent of deeds is giving life to the dead (inna afdhal al-hasanaat ihyaa al-amwaat).’ So he said to himself: ‘I used to be dead, and he gave me life; I was ignorant, and he gave me knowledge. I am not the first person to ever be ignorant, such that I attained knowledge before everyone else; nor am I the last one to be ignorant, such that the process of spiritual teaching has come to an end on me. Therefore out of gratitude for this blessing, it is only appropriate for me to pass on this divine trust to those who come after me; just as those who preceded me have handed it down to me. For the origin of this affair is from Allaah, and it only reached me through its many intermediaries, the first passing it on to the second, the second passing it on to the third, until it descended from the heavenly host to the creatures of this lower world. After that, did it tear asunder the veil between Allaah and His creations, and did the ‘gateways’ pass it onward until it reached me, only so that I could be its final goal and ultimate end? Not at all. For those who have transmitted it and handed on this trust before me are more deserving of precedence and spiritual gains! What they have earned does not belong to me, so that I could rely on it. No. I (myself) am part of what they have earned through their acts (of teaching me). Nor does their precedence relieve me from having to act! So now I need to strive to spread knowledge, just as I needed to strive to seek knowledge in the beginning.’ He realized that he is now obligated to pass on his spiritual understanding to others, just as his master was obligated to teach him, and that his final duty was similar to his initial duty (wa anna al-aakhir amruhu fil-hukm mithl al-awwal).

In other words, in the beginning, the scholar's task was to strive to acquire knowledge, and now in the end, his task is to strive to teach it.

So he travelled towards his Lord leaving his people and possessions behind, calling people to the good, in order to deserve a gratitude, similar to what was incumbent upon him for his master. He started traveling through countries, passing among the non-Arab and the Arab tribes, scrutinizing their faces for signs of spiritual aptitude and posing religious questions. But he received no response, nor did he meet anyone seeking knowledge, until he ended up in the furthest part of the jazeerah. There he entered a city where no one knew him; and while he was recalling Allaah’s blessings and searching various gatherings, he noticed a group of people from the town who were discussing religion without any guidance, and recklessly following the inclinations of their passions. He sat nearby, out of sight, listening carefully to their discussion and analyzing them carefully to determine which of them was closer to the right path.

Then he said to himself: ‘These people are more deserving of life (spiritual knowledge) for three reasons:
First, because the true divine argument has reached them;
Second, because they belong to the people of my community;
and third, because they are closer to the right path shown by their keen interest in religion and the fact that they are inquiring about it and discussing it —for the person who is already searching for something is much closer to finding it.’

This is like how someone actively searching the internet to learn about a specific health condition is far more likely to absorb medical guidance than someone who has no interest in their health at all. The presence of the search indicates a readiness for the truth.

The scholar recognizes readiness before he speaks openly. A person already searching for truth is like a patient who finally admits the symptoms and seeks a real diagnosis; such a person is much closer to healing than one who denies the illness. This is why the Prophet Muhammad, peace be upon him, is remembered in the tradition as the city of knowledge, with Ali, peace be upon him, as its gate. The seeker is not merely collecting ideas; he is looking for the right entrance into knowledge.

The narrator continued: When those people completed their discussion, they turned to the scholar and asked: "Who are you, and where are you from, O youth?’

‘I am Abdullaah and I am among the residents of His sanctuary,’ he replied.

‘What is your business here and what is your work?’ they asked.

‘My business has completed,’ he replied, ‘and as for work, I am looking for it.’

In other words, "Business" implies acquiring knowledge, while "work" implies spreading the acquired knowledge.

‘Well then,’ they said, "did anything in our discussion catch your attention?”

‘All of it was interesting for the person who would be interested,’ he answered.

‘But the word “interesting” has two meanings,’ they said, ‘a commendable one and a reprehensible one....’

‘Of course!’ he said, ‘and similarly all "speech" can be commendable or reprehensible.’

They asked: ‘Then according to you, what is commendable speech?’

‘Speech that is true,’ he replied.

‘And what kind is that?’ they asked.

‘Speech, whose origin comes from Allaah and through which people are called towards Allaah,’ he answered.

‘Then what,’ they asked, ‘is the reprehensible speech?’

He replied: ‘Speech that which is based on passion, and is used to call people to something other than true guidance.’

‘You have spoken truly.’ they said. ‘So won’t you share some of your words with us?’

‘I do not claim words of my own,’ he replied. ‘For I am following the words of another!’

‘Then let us hear you say something that will inform us about your particular religious ways’ they said. ‘For you have made a good impression on us, and we were pleased by your brotherly concern, so now we ask you to become a gateway for us, through which Allaah may send His loving kindness for us.'

‘All right,’ he replied. He stood up, facing the attentive group and began...

‘Verily He created with His power, then arranged everything perfectly! Who determined a path through His generosity, then guided! It is He who brought forth paths of the pastureland, then made its outer form a dried-up brush and its inner aspect broad in scope and content! whose ways are not obscure (laa multabisa as-subul) and whose traces will never be obliterated (wa laa mundarisa al-aathaar)—for if it were so, then intellects would fail to seek it, and would despair reaching it. His knowledge does not exist outwardly, so that the unworthy could not possess and alter it. Instead it is benevolent and well-protected! Because of its excellence, it is well concealed, and its secrets are well-guarded for those worthy of them! Its dignity is the splendid robe of guidance, and its perfection is the garment of piety! It is marked out by the light of lights, entrusted safely in the hearts of the best and chosen ones - the truly pious, guardians, protectors, generous and recorders! This knowledge is a trust which they safely pass on, and do not transgress the divine ordinances. It is the law of the Supreme Judge containing all the benefits of the All-Merciful! He honored it by making it inward, and He protected it by keeping it secret. He veiled it with His obligations, and crowned it with His covenants. For it cannot be reached by the aspirations of those who rely on the strength of their intellects (fa laa tanaaluhu himam al-mutafakkireen), nor can it be attained by those who are scheming for it (wa laa tablughu fikr al-muhtaleen). Its outer aspect is a reminder and a sign for those who are rightly guided, while its inner aspect is a sweet drink gushing forth.

His lofty heavens come down close, and his earth is arranged for those who inquire about Him and His guiding stars are arranged for those who seek Him. He has watering-places for those who move towards Him, and signs, whose wonders follow each other in succession, pointing to Him through their symbols. His accustomed way is preferment, and He is approached through distinguished ranks. His duties are obligatory and His accustomed ways are paths to be walked on. Sincerity ennobles his obligations, and justice purifies the efforts made for him. For He has made his ways a guide and a path leading to good, with its outer aspect a protective shell, and a sign pointing toward its inner dimensions. Intellects are forced by their neediness and ignorance to seek knowledge and open abundance. So blessed be the One Who placed the night and the day in succession, for those who reflect and those who wish to be grateful! May Allaah’s blessings be on the one who He has specially chosen from His servants, and made a warner for all the worlds!’

Then he said: ‘Be mindful of Allaah like the one who fears Him; and fear Him like the one who is hoping to please Him! Seek knowledge and you will learn; Act according to it and you will receive Allaah’s loving mercy (wa-tlubul-‘ilm, ta‘allamoo, wa‘maloo turhamoo)!’

Then his eyes overflowed with tears and he stopped talking and bid the group farewell. Still weeping, he got up and walked toward his travel baggage. All in the group were overcome with emotions by the intensity of his speech and the effectiveness of his sermon. They wanted to accompany him, but he prevented them from doing so. Since they didn’t know what else to do, they all returned to their homes. But a young man from the group, who was the youngest of them, yet had the best character, accompanied him. He had completed the education of his intellect, and reflection had awakened his heart. His thoughts coincided with, and were reinforced by what he heard in the scholar’s speech - both in what he had presented and what he had alluded to. This young man's father was a great Shaykh among the most distinguished people of Arab by lineage and was one of the most devoted supportees of the people of his religion. With the scholar's permission, the young man accompanied him until they reached his (the scholar's) inn. The scholar entered and invited the young man in. He then ordered food, and when they finished eating and were comfortable enough to talk,

The young man said: ‘O wise man! You spoke and we heard! You preached with eloquence and adorned religion with the most beautiful of ornaments, and motivated us with the most beautiful of motivations and the noblest of preparations. You called us through the most eminent of responsibilities, enticing our minds to enquire from you about all that has struck our hearts as a result of your speech. Such excellence could only manifest through one whose knowledge has perfected internally and whose character has completely developed externally, making him a source of wisdom! You are like a tree... its roots firm, its splendor towering over the worlds, its fruits hanging low from its branches for those who seek them. So I ask you to be one of those blessed branches for me, from what I can see of your distinguished judgment, your native aptitude and your perfect intellect. You mentioned that our minds are forced by their ignorance to seek knowledge and abundance. Now my mind is the most needy and indignent of all, so is there any way to life for me?’

The scholar replied: ‘Surely the One Who created by His power, did not create without purpose, nor did He create them entirely knowledgeable. He did not leave them wandering in ignorance without end, nor did He want them to adopt foolishness as their behavior, or wear the garments of ignorance. So He established a way out for them by means of an "instrument" (intellect) around which He shaped their constitution (alif bihaa tarkeeb) and by which He perfected their creation (wa akmala bihaa khalqahum). He made their hearing, eyes and hearts, an opening to accept the light of His knowledge as an act of generosity toward them, and by it completed His argument for them. He then placed the treasures of His wisdom and the specific characteristics of His wishes that pleased Him in the pure, chosen, elite of His creation. He gave them the responsibility of worshipping Him according to those ways, and calling others to those ways, while imposing on people the duty of responding to their call by using their instrument (intellect), so that He may distinguish the wicked from the good. Hence the scholar will reveal himself plainly through his seeking of that knowledge, while the ignorant will be distinguished by his falling behind. Therefore there is no argument against Allaah after His messengers, nor is there any excuse for straying after the right path has been clearly pointed out.’

The young man replied: ‘My lack of excuse has frightened me, and the existence of signs pointing to the right path makes me desire setting out on that search; so I ask you to be a guide, and I'll be your humble servant.’

The scholar answered: ‘Surely Allaah did not create His creatures as fully grown scholars. He created them as infants, small and ignorant, not knowing anything, so that they grow big after having been small, and come to know after having been ignorant. Thus their being small is a precondition for their becoming big, and their ignorance is a sign pointing toward knowledge. The same is true of spiritual knowledge. One can only attain its essence on the basis of preceding, lower forms of knowledge, which hearts can quickly assimilate and intellect can easily grasp. And so that the first stage of spiritual growth is established in their minds, and they are humbled enough to carry out the required duties. This opens up their hearts to the essence of that knowledge and enable their intellects to bear its goal. That is Allaah's accustomed way regarding His servants! So we neither need to depart from the opinions of religious communities, nor do we need to follow anything other than the accustomed way to be guided.’

‘But I am an infant in relation to what I wish to achieve on this path,’ the young man said, ‘so raise and educate me! I am ignorant with regard to what I have been seeking, so teach me and help me learn. I am forced to rely on your gracious favor, so have mercy on me! Surely you were once in this state, and Allaah brought His bounties to you through the hands of the one who became so important for you, and to whom you owe such gratitude! So you already understand the state of someone like myself, caught in the vice of ignorance and so needy, that he will desperately resort to any way out.’

The scholar said: ‘I myself was once in the state you’ve described, and I surely wish to spare you the desperation. But my compassion towards you will not bring you closer to what you hope for without your "working" for it! For just as every word has a proper employment that can only be accepted if it is sincere and true, similarly knowledge is of no benefit until it has been put into action; and action is of no benefit until it is accepted by Allaah. Now the true and sincere form of speech is that, which is just. And the signs pointing to knowledge are the revealed paths of righteous acts, and faithfully conforming to the right models throughout all that is encompassed by the furthermost regions and the variation of day and night. So do not seek knowledge impatiently, and do not dismiss it out of negligence! Nor should you accept it out of blind imitation, without any convincing argument — for in such a case it would slip out of your heart at the first onset of doubt. But do not deny the argument once it has been firmly established, or you will become the pleader for all that is false. And do not stop inquiring about that knowledge and reflecting upon it, or your judgment will become sterile. You must trust me without prejudice, and make every effort to understand without confusion, seeking the truth and asking Allaah’s help for it — for Allaah does not neglect a recompense, nor does He forbid reward if He sees you seeking for the good!’

Blindly imitating without understanding is like navigating a busy modern city using a GPS map from a hundred years ago. If you trust the outdated map so blindly that you refuse to look at the actual roads in front of you, you will eventually crash into a wall rather than verifying the reality with your own eyes and intellect.

‘Surely you have spoken most justly, accomplishing great good, and you have pointed the way to the truth with the most beautiful of words,’ said the young man. ‘But now I am seeking Allaah’s help, and I am asking you about that affair to which you are calling and specifically referring to. What is it? Who does it come from? and to Who does it lead?’

‘That is Allaah’s religion which He has approved!’ replied the scholar. ‘It is from Him, so He will accept none other! Specifically, it leads to the pure elite among His messengers, and generally to all of His creation. So the person who has been singled out for His favor should be grateful for it, and put it into practice, and call others to it. But the person who has been excluded (from that knowledge) should keep seeking it until he comes to know of it, and should willingly obey whoever is guiding him.’

‘Do you think,’ asked the young man, ‘that this favor by which Allaah has singled out His friends is at the same level, such that they are all equal in its excellence? Or do some of them have a rank of excellence over others?’

‘Haven’t I already mentioned,’ said the scholar, ‘that every knowledge has a preceding stage of knowledge below it, and that for every favor there are “bearers”? So they have ranks of honor with their Lord; each entrusted with what he has earned through his knowledge. Thus He gives of His bounty to every meritorious person, and out of His justice towards His creatures, and His loving mercy towards His servants!’

‘Then what you are calling to,’ asked the young man, ‘is it this infinitely divine favor towards all creatures, or is it something else?’

The scholar responded: ‘It is not exactly the same as that favor, nor is it something other than that. Rather, what I'm calling you to, is related to this favor, as a handful of water is related to the vast extent of the Euphrates! I am calling you to the religion which Allaah approved for His servants and which He ennobled on the day He made it complete:

(Al-yawma akmaltu lakum deenakum wa atmamtu 'alaykum ni'mati wa radeetu lakumu al-islama deena)
"This day, I have perfected your religion for you, completed My Favour upon you, and have chosen for you Islam as your religion."

It is sublime, protected, noble, hidden. He has ennobled it with His rewards in honor of those who uphold it, and rebuked those with painful punishments who neglect it, making them a sign of dishonor. Its outward dimension is formal prescriptions, and its inner aspect is the various levels of knowledge. Its authority is illuminating and its justice most clear. This is what I am calling you to!’

The young man asked: ‘But what singled you out for this affair instead of others, and made you more deserving of it than others, since you just claimed that it comes from Allaah to all creation in general? Indeed everyone lays claim to it (for himself or his own group), as opposed to others. So what has made you all more deserving of the name “the truth” than those others, when this affair only came to you in the same way it came to them?’

The scholar replied: ‘There is not a single person who speaks a word of truth, that has not been taken from the same sources from which we have taken it. So they are in agreement with us about that word of truth, since we both affirm it. But then they separated from us by abandoning the sources of that very truth, while we persevered in our allegiance to them by obeying them. Through our perseverance we held on to it, while they let go of it and abandoned it. Hence they agreed with us in their affirmation, but differed from us in their actions. Thus, what they affirm bears witness against them, but bears witness in our favor, and their abandonment caused their need for us and the need for what we possess. They are like the brothers who inherited enormous wealth from their father and divided it equally among themselves. Then some of them lost their wealth and fell into poverty, while the others economized and traded with it, and multiplied their wealth many times over. The impoverished brothers were then forced to come to the rich ones and serve them and ask for their help. Likewise those who have lost the path of their messengers, have been forced by their ignorance to seek knowledge from those of noble sources. So the sign that indicates that we are more deserving of the truth than them, is their need for us and our self-sufficiency without them. That is how our scholar has come to invite the ignorant among them; and how our clothed ones have come to cover up the naked; and how our rich have come to feed the hungry. For the true right belongs to the one who has precedence and true merit (wajaba al-haqq al-faadhil), while precedence and true merit belongs to the one who acts kindly (wal-fadlu li-kulli dhee ni‘mah); and gratitude is due to the one who favors (wash-shukru li-kulli dhee ihsaan).’

‘Their greater merit and precedence over others has become clear,’ said the young man. ‘And as for your argument for the truth, that has been verified against the common people who have neglected the truth. But what is your argument against me? For I am affirming what you have mentioned, upholding what you have described, denying what you have denied, acknowledging the truth and its precedence, and loathing falsehood and its people!’

‘My argument against you,’ replied the scholar, ‘is similar to my argument against them, since even though you disagreed with them about the description of the truth, yet you actually agreed with them about its inner meaning.’

‘But how can that be?’ asked the young man, ‘since I am affirming that you hold the truth while others are denying it!’

‘Even if you disagreed with them by your affirmation of the truth,' the scholar continued, 'still you were in agreement with them in preventing access to it, when they prohibited it. For merely verbal affirmation is of no benefit without any repentance and reverting back to the truth; and denial does no harm to the person being denied when he has clear evidence.’

The young man exclaimed: ‘Then if I recognize them, I will turn to the people of the truth, or to a confirmed representative from them, and seek their truth! So tell me which of those (options) is possible for me.’

‘Can’t you see,’ replied the scholar, ‘that you’ve been forced to affirm the need for recognizing the possessor of truth or his representative, just as common people have been forced to affirm their need for the truth? So your actions may have been different from theirs, but you shared their ignorance!’

‘You’ve spoken accurately,’ said the young man, ‘so please explain that to me.’

‘Didn’t you just say,’ asked the scholar, ‘that you were confirming "my" argument against yourself and against the common people?’

‘Yes,’ he said.

‘Then how can anyone confirm a “truth” for themselves, which actually belongs to someone else?’ asked the Scholar.

‘Nobody is able to do that,’ he said.

‘So now that you have confirmed "my" argument against you and against the common people,’ demanded the scholar, ‘nothing else remains but to surrender to Allaah and turn back to the One you need, and who is self-sufficient without you.’

‘I do admit my need,’ said the young man, ‘But as for the one who is self-sufficient without me, I am still searching for him, so have mercy on me!’

The scholar asked: ‘Have you ever noticed that when the poor helplessly turns to a generous man, he receives a favor from him in only two ways. Either "earning" through work, or through that wealthy man’s gracious "generosity"?’

‘Yes,’ replied the young man, ‘he receives favors either by earning it or through an act of generosity.’

The scholar asked: ‘And is the merit of those who received, through an act of charity anything like the merit of those who actually earned it?’

‘No,’ he admitted, ‘it couldn’t be!’

‘So seek this distinction through action!’ said the scholar, ‘and you will reach it because you will have earned it. But don’t seek it through flattery, for if you try to obtain it as some undeserved favor, you will be excluded from its merit.’

The young man responded: ‘You are the key to the good and the one who is pointing to it. So show me the way to whichever path is superior by its essence and closer to guiding me rightly. For my great need for your generosity has captivated me!’

‘My dear son,’ said the scholar, ‘May Allaah not estrange you from the good (laa bu‘da lak), and there is no blame for you (wa laa tathreeba ‘alayk). What you hope from me is coming to you. But it has limits you must not overstep, and conditions you must not forget to follow.’

‘Set whatever limits you wish and impose whatever conditions you think best,’ declared the young man, ‘for I hope that you will find me patient and grateful for your kindness.’

‘The first of these limits,’ said the scholar, ‘is to fulfill the outward aspect of the Book and its revealed paths, acting upon it in accordance with your knowledge. For who acts for Allaah’s sake based on their knowledge will be guided by Allaah to that which he does not know.’

‘But the revealed books are many,’ the young man asked, ‘and they all are from Allaah. Each book has a large group of people adhering to it, so they all are in agreement about upholding them and worshipping in accordance with them.’

‘But now we’ve returned to what we were saying before,’ the scholar remarked, ‘and we are forcing ourselves to argue in confirmation of what we don’t really need to confirm!’

‘And how is that?’ asked the young man.

‘Because of what you said about there being many books, all coming from Allaah, and each having a large group of followers,’ explained the scholar. ‘So then do you believe that Allaah’s words and His books invalidate each other, or that the first book denies the last, or the last one denies the first?’

‘No, I don’t believe that!’ exclaimed the young man. ‘But then what is the argument regarding this?’

‘If people acted in accordance with what was revealed in the first book,’ replied the scholar, ‘it would lead them to the second one. And if they acted in accordance with the second, it would lead them to the third; until in the end, they would have arrived at the latest of the books. For the latest book is more deserving of being followed than the ones that came before it— even though they all are from Allaah — because the latest one is more recent in time and clearer in its way of proceeding, since it has replaced what was before it, and nothing has come after it to replace it.’

‘You have spoken truly,’ said the young man, ‘and you’ve made clear how we should acknowledge the truth and the signs of truthfulness. Now I will uphold the limits you’ve ordered me to keep, but what are your conditions for me?’

‘I have five conditions for you’ replied the scholar:
1. Don’t neglect anything I’ve entrusted you with.
2. Don’t conceal anything from me if I ask you about it.
3. Don’t come looking for me to give you an answer whenever you have a question.
4. Don't ask me about anything until I bring it up with you.
5. Don’t speak about me to your father.’

‘I shall do everything that you have mentioned,’ the young man declared. ‘Indeed your conditions seem easy to me, in comparison to all that I am hoping to receive from you. But why the subterfuge with my father? Surely that is the greatest test and the most painful trial for me! Would you allow me to move away from my father?’

‘Moving away from him,’ explained the scholar, ‘would create a reason to point fingers at someone else (i.e., the scholar and his mission). Instead you must stay with him, properly respect him, try to please him by being agreeable with him when you can. And you must protect your innermost self and keep your (spiritual) concerns secret from him. For surely Allaah will make up for his, and other people's hostility.’

# Chapter 2 — Oath and Cosmic Origins

The narrator continued: The scholar and the young man occasionally met and then departed for a period of time. The young man was perplexed because he kept performing everything that had been assigned to him from the Sharee-ah, but he did not have the underlying knowledge behind those actions. He also did not know the scholar’s place of residence, nor was he able to seek him out, because of the conditions he had set for him. This continued for a long time until one night, the young man’s period of trial ended and his merit and perseverance in fulfilling what had been prescribed for, became clear to the scholar. So when their next meeting was arranged, the young man sensed within himself that the moment of the fulfillment of his needs was close. And since they were alone, the young man prostrated himself humbly before the scholar. Then when he raised his head...

The scholar said to him: ‘I saw you bow down before me out of gratitude. But being thankful before the deed gives rise to suspicion of insincerity, and it puts an end to being grateful after the deed is done.’

‘All your words are true,’ replied the young man, ‘and all your actions are good deeds. My gratitude is only for all your gracious favors that you have bestowed on me before this day! Yet, in displaying my gratitude to you, I did hope for even more acts of kindness from you.’

The scholar continued: ‘It is certainly true that gratitude merits an increase in good deeds for those who are grateful, and what you deserve is an obligation on me. So turn your heart toward me, pay close attention and listen carefully to what I say to you.’

‘I have already prepared the instrument of my understanding and made myself ready for listening,’ the young man said, ‘so say what seems right to you.’

The oath is not just a sentence repeated by the tongue. It is closer to receiving authorized access into a protected system. A person may know the system exists, admire its design, and even describe its functions, but without proper authorization he remains outside it. The oath admits the seeker into responsibility, loyalty, and protection. This also explains why faith cannot remain only inward. The Prophet Muhammad, peace be upon him, is reported to have described faith as acceptance in the heart, expression by the tongue, and action by the limbs.

The scholar began: ‘There is a key to religion, which renders it either sacred or profane, like the difference between marriage and fornication.’

The young man remarked: ‘Surely that key you have mentioned must be extraordinary in Allaah’s eyes if it is what distinguishes between the sacred and the profane, between truth and falsehood. So what is it?’

‘That is the pact of Allaah which confirms His rights, encompasses His obligations and safeguards His friends. It is the rope of Allaah upon His earth and His guarantee of security among His creation,’ said the scholar. ‘I shall repeat that oath to you and impose its obligation on you.’

‘Yes!’ said the young man. ‘Impose whatever obligation that you like! I am not hesitant about your opinions, nor will I violate your tradition.’

The narrator continued: The scholar began to recite the oath to the young man, and he kept repeating it and bound himself by it. The young man couldn’t control his emotions, and his eyes started streaming with tears, because of the intensity of that moment, until the scholar brought him to the end of the oath. So he praised Allaah and glorified Him and gave thanks for all that had reached him, knowing with certainty that he was henceforth part of the party of Allaah, and the party of Allaah’s friends, because he had accepted the obligations of their oath. Then he finished his praises and became silent, and the scholar began his explanation and clarification.

The first thing the scholar said to him by way of setting forth the first principles of the manifest intermediaries and the beginning of their creation. He said:

‘Allaah is the Originator of things and the Giver of their existence (anna Allaaha mubdi‘ al-ashyaa’ wa mukawwinahaa). He created them, and then initiated them without precedence, such that no prior entity served as a model for the later. On the contrary, He is the First of every first (huwa awwalu kulli awwal) and the inheritor of every existence. He initiated His creation from a Light, with three words branching from that light.
1. The first of those words was His will
2. From the will derived His command
3. From the command came His saying to whatever He wishes “Be!,” so it became.

So the beginning of all creation was by Allaah’s will of a command, executed through His words. (iraadah, amr bil-qawl)

The threefold pattern of will, command, and speech is difficult because it is metaphysical. A modern analogy is software deployment. First there is intention: the decision that something should exist. Then there is command: the instruction issued to make it happen. Finally there is execution: the code runs and the system appears. The text uses this pattern to show that creation is not random. It unfolds through order, command, and meaningful expression.

This is why Wilaayah is divided into three components: affirmation with the heart, declaration with the tongue, and action with the limbs. Just as the Creator brought entities into existence through His Will, Command, and Word, we shape our noble existence through these three actions.

‘Now from those initial three words (iraadah, amr bil-qawl) there came the command, “Be!,” which is made up of two letters and “so it came to be”, which is made up of five more letters. Hence those seven letters became the root-principles from which seven things were derived. From the Light was created the air (khuliqa min an-noor al-hawaa’), which was the first of those, singular and without any pair, unique among all things in its mixing and encompassing them, but not being encompassed by any of them. Thus it symbolically points to the One who has no like unto Him (laysa kamithlihi shay’). Next from those three words came the water, the darkness and the light—the light of the heavens and the earth. Then He created from the water smoke; from the darkness He created the thickness of the mud and from the light He created fire.

Thus these three things were derived from the preceding three things, so they became pairs, because they were created from them. But the air, the seventh of these principles of creation, governs them altogether. This was the beginning of creation and the commencement of all things.

‘So once the creation was established on these seven principles, He proceeded with the process of shaping the outward forms and likenesses of things. He created from smoke, the seven heavens and their signs, and from the thickness of mud He created the seven earths and their sustenance. Thus these seven branches of the heavens, and the seven branches of the earths came from those seven root-principles.

Next we should examine the signs of the heaven and the sustenance of the earth - what are they, and what are their visible witnesses? Now if we return to what we were saying about the three words and their letters, from which the seven (root-principles) were created, those words are Allaah’s “willing a command through (His) speaking” (iraadah, amr bi-qawl)—which makes 12 letters. Hence their signs in the heaven are the 12 mansions of the Zodiac, and their witnesses are seven rulers And their signs on the earth are the 12 regions, while their witnesses are the seven seas. So those are the signs in the heavens and the earth, which most people pass right by, turning away.

Next, the heavens and the earths were one single piece of that darkness of “black mud,” which is the ruler of the earth. Then He split it with His light, so that the various lights and darknesses mixed together. So He separated both (the mixture of light and darkness) through the day and night as two signs. He made the night, a symbol of darkness and its portion, and made the day, a symbol of light and its portion. So He darkened the night and made the moon and planets its signs; and He brightened the daylight to shine forth, and made the sight-giving rays of the sun as its sign. Then from both, resulted the days revolving in turn between day and night. Now there are seven days in a week, and their signs are that each day has twelve hours, and each night has twelve hours.

‘Now, when the creations from these seven root-principles completed, He created pairs between male and female — and by that I don’t mean masculine and feminine, but rather in terms of greater and lesser excellence and merit. Thus Allaah’s accustomed way proceeded with the creation of the pairs, and from the essential realities of those pairs He created all animals, including people, beasts, birds and the vermin. Then He proceeded with language, and it also became pairs: attributes and things qualified by them, names and the essential meanings. So glory to the One Who created all the pairs and then made them the outward aspects of His command/affair, symbols for His religion, and signs pointing to His power. Thus through all symbols He pointed to their Source (fa-dalla bil-ashyaa’i ‘alaa munshi’ihaa), and through his creations He pointed to their Creator (wa-dalla bil-bariyyah ‘alaa baari’ihaa).

‘Thus to summarize: He separated them with His Light, He originated them through His command, He created them with His knowledge, and He sealed them by their names, with the letters “In the Name of God, the All-Merciful, the All-Compassionate” (bismillaah ir-rahmaan ir-raheem). Until finally, when He had perfected their shape and harmoniously arranged them in pairs, from the quintessence of His wish, and the innermost depths of His mystery, He selected and Purified for Himself abundant knowledge and an illuminating light. He made that the religion through which He is worshipped, and by which one can draw closer to Him. Then He ennobled it with the revealed paths (thumma akramahu bish-sharaa’i‘) and the books, and He pointed to it through the symbols and recurring patterns of all creation. Then from among the very best of His creations He raised for Himself, certain distinguished personalities, guiding figures and Wise judges. These, He placed in the houses of his prophethood, the treasuries of his knowledge and wisdom, and made them the inspired translators of His revelation; and He made them his viceroys on His earth (wa-stakhlafahum fil-ard), guideposts to the path of salvation in both the outer and the inner spiritual aspects. Among them are the Imaams and the Naa-tiqs, the Hujjas (proofs) and the Naqeebs (chiefs), and those who are summoning people to the good and the scholars. In their various ranks, they are like the stars of the heaven! Actions of those they guide, rise up to them, and the symbols of religion come to an end in them (i.e. they are the goal of all good symbols). Their Imaam is similar to the immensity of the sun in comparison to the other heavenly lights - impossible for it to be veiled from sight. Indeed the sun is his (Imaam’s) symbol and an outer aspect, and the Imaam’s Hujjah, a gateway, is like the illuminated moon, for the moon is his (Hujjah’s) outer aspect and his symbol. And the shining stars are the symbols and the outer aspect, representing the Du‘aat's.

Now, all of these pairs I have described, have an inner and outer aspect, a symbol and what it symbolizes. Thus the outer aspect of the seven heavens are symbols, while their inner aspect are the seven Naa-tiqs - the possessors of determination among the divine messengers. The outer aspect of the seven earths is likewise a symbol, while its inner aspect is the Imaam of right guidance. Thus each Naa-tiq among them has twelve Naqeebs, and their symbols in the seven heavens are the twelve mansions. Likewise each Imaam among the imams of right guidance has twelve Hujjas, who he uses to bring His argument to his people; and their symbols on earth are the twelve regions, which are also their dwelling-places, with one Hujjah assigned to each region. Then each Hujjah has a greater or lesser number of Daa‘ees - for God increases among His creatures whatever He wills — who communicate God’s argument among His creatures, so that the people will not have any argument against God after sending down His messengers.

‘So it is through these intermediaries of Allaah that His argument has reached His creatures, and it is through them that His justice has manifested, both outwardly and inwardly. For the inner aspect is the true religion of Allaah through which His friends worship Him appropriately, while the outer aspect is the revealed paths of religion and its symbols. So religion is the "soul" and the living spirit of those revealed paths, and they are the body for religion and signs pointing to it. The body can only subsist through the spirit because it is its life; and the spirit can only subsist through the body, because that is its outer covering. The same rule applies to all outer aspects of the religious paths and other commandments. They only subsist through their inner spiritual aspects, because it is their light, and their essential meaning. Nor can the inner aspect subsist without the outer aspect, because that is its covering and a sign pointing to it. Now the outer aspect is the distinctive mark of this lower world which can only be seen through that (body); and the inner aspect is the distinctive mark of the other world, which can only be seen through that (spirit). Hence there is not a single letter among the “letters” of the inner aspect, nor a friend among the friends of Allaah, who does not have many visible signs in the outer aspect of this world, because of the multiplicity of the symbols and the great extents of the revealed paths.

‘Now my speech about this could go on and be greatly expanded. But due to the preciousness and purity of the words of wisdom, the longer one speaks about them, the more the actual point gets hidden; the later part makes you forget the beginning. For one part of the light of wisdom can obscure another part, just as the light of the sun veils and weakens the light of the moon and the stars. It is in this manner words of wisdom should embellish the tongues of the wise men.’

‘But don’t you see,’ asked the young man, ‘that regarding this lower world — in which He created these creatures whose essence is so marvelous, so immense in their sheer numbers, from the first to the last—that people differ about it? For some people are very intent on this lower world, plunging into its pursuits and seeking excellence by accumulating more of it. While others condemn it and carefully avoid it, even though they can’t do without it—and if a great deal of something is forbidden, then surely a little of it is also forbidden? Now I haven’t heard you mention this lower world or discuss it at all, so what do you have to say about it?’

‘As for this lower world,’ replied the Knower, ‘neither the scholar nor the ignorant has ever condemned it. The ignorant person is forced to recognize the merits of this lower world since he is ignorant of any thing else, while a scholar recognizes its value because he understands the merit of its essential meaning. So no one ever really condemns this lower world except for the totally ignorant. And as for my opinion on this subject, it is dear to whoever can really see that this lower world was established on the basis of seven things, which are derived from the seven root-principles. Those derivatives are the heaven, the earth, darkness, light, the “vehicles,” the divine favors and the adversities. That makes seven groups, and from each of those seven are derived seven further divisions:

Thus from the heaven are derived the seven heavens (Saturn, Jupiter, Mars, Sun, Venus, Mercury, Moon).
And from that earth are derived the seven earths which are the seven continents (Asia, Africa, N-America, S-America, Antarctica, Europe, Australia).

The light gives rise to seven days:
- Sunday: The “Sabbath” which is the first of God’s days is for resting and repose according to God’s limits and accustomed ways.
- Monday: The second day is for the seven doubled ones. (Surah Al-Hijr: (Wa laqad ataynaka sab'an mina al-mathani wal-qur'ana al-'atheem) - And We have certainly given you seven of the often repeated verses and the great Qur’aan. And Surah Az-Zumar: (Allahu nazzala ahsana al-hadeethi kitaban mutashabihan mathaniya) - Allaah has sent down the best statement: a consistent Book wherein is reiteration).
- Tuesday: The third day is for the three levels (of reality): Outer aspect, Inner aspect, Innermost aspect (batin al-bain).
- Wednesday: The fourth day is for the four sacred months. (Surah At-Tawbah: (Inna 'iddata ash-shuhoori 'inda Allahi ithna 'ashara shahran fee kitabi Allahi yawma khalaqa as-samawati wal-arda minha arba'atun hurum) - Indeed, the number of months with Allaah is twelve months in the register of Allaah from the day He created the heavens and the earth; of these, four are sacred).
- Thursday: The fifth day is for the fifth of God’s intermediaries.
- Friday.
- Saturday: The final day is for the final day of gathering (Qayamah) which is God’s greatest law.

The darkness gives rise to seven nights as a protection for the seven days, each day having a night.

Following are the seven “vehicles” used to cross the great expanses of the earth:
- Camels, for the sand
- Horses, for the plains
- Mules, for the mountains
- Donkeys, for short distances. (Surah An-Nahl: (Wal-khayla wal-bighala wal-hameera litarkabuha wa zeenatan) - And He created the horses, mules and donkeys for you to ride and as adornment).
- Elephants, for wastelands
- Cattle for wilderness
- Ships for the seas.

The “favors” of this lower world come to seven:
- Air, which provides an opening between the heaven and the earth in which the winds arise and where the breaths can come and go.
- Water, enables life for all living things.
- Light, is the basis for sight and vision.
- Darkness, allows the growth of plants.
- Food, provides sustenance for living bodies.
- Clothing is protection and covers the private parts. (Surah Al-A'raf: (Yaa Banee Aadama qad anzalnaa ‘alaykum libaasan yuwaaree saw’aatikum wa reeshan, wa libaasut-taqwaa dhaalika khayr) - O children of Aadam, We have bestowed upon you clothing to conceal your private parts and as adornment. But the clothing of righteousness - that is best).
- Marriage, provides for the means of propagation and raising offspring.

So among them are the keys to what is good and the wellsprings of Allaah's blessings. Then, which of these is to be condemned when they are all among Allaah’s kindnesses and His favors for which His servants should all be grateful!

The opposites among the “adversities” (of this lower world) are also seven:
- Heat
- Cold
- Hunger
- Thirst
- Fear
- Sickness
- Death

That makes seven things about which we must exhibit faithful perseverance after we have come to recognize their essential meanings.’

‘Those are undoubtedly the attributes of this lower world,’ the young man responded, ‘and nothing exists that is not part of the Creators handiwork and design. So why is it some learned criticize this lower world, even though they cannot do without it? And why do the ignorant praise it? For it seems to me that they are right in this matter, even though they are ignorant—and their being right necessarily means the learned must be mistaken, since they have opposed the ignorant in this belief.’

The scholar replied: ‘The learned were not mistaken in criticizing this lower world, but people did not necessarily understand their true intention in doing so. Nor did the ignorant really grasp the truth when they praised this lower world, since they failed to understand its essential meaning. And the person who is ignorant of something, naturally opposes it.’

‘Then show me,’ asked the young man, ‘what is accurate in the words of the learned who criticize this world, so that I can understand why they are right; and clarify for me what is in the saying of the ignorant that will reveal their falsehood, since appearances would seem to indicate the opposite. For my heart has been constricted by my ignorance, and I desire the relief that comes from knowing the truth.’

‘Now this lower world and everything I have described to you,’ the Knower continued, ‘is only the outer aspect of the inner dimension. So this lower world has nothing to sustain it except by its inner dimension, because it stands in relation to the material world, like the spirit exists in relation to the body. Therefore, whoever knows the spirit, finds the body pleasing. But for that person who is ignorant of the spirit and only recognizes the body, understands that it is dead and decaying flesh, which is forbidden. This is the reason why scholars have forbidden this lower world, because Allaah has forbidden eating the dead, unless it is approached in the way Allaah wishes (sacrifice). ‘Now it has become clear why the ignorant were wrong in praising this lower world, since they didn’t recognize its inner dimension and what Allaah intended by it. They imagined that He had created it without any essential meaning—but Allaah did not create things pointlessly nor in jest. For if this lower world and all it contains had only been created for this lower world alone, bringing forth in it creatures returning to nothingness, there would have been no ultimate outcome for it, and it would have had no essential meaning. And every action without any ultimate outcome is vain amusement, and every speech without any essential meaning is falsehood—but Allaah is exalted above that!’

‘You have certainly established the truth of the scholars,’ the young man admitted, ‘and you have shown their excellence and what distinguishes them from the ignorant, as well as what their mistake is in the way their argument initially appeared. And now we need to know about the inner dimension of this lower world and what it contains. For I already feel the constriction of my heart, that I was complaining about, being lifted!’

‘Then what is the reward,’ asked the Scholar, ‘for the person who liberates you from the constraints of neediness and brings you to freedom and abundance?’

‘Gratitude and praise,’ he replied.

‘Then what is the reward,’ he continued, ‘for the person who has freed you from the shackles of ignorance and brought you to the open space of true knowledge?’

‘Obedience and Action in accordance to the knowledge gained,’ he answered.

The scholar said, ‘So this physical world and all that it contains are symbols for the outer aspects of the spiritual world. This physical world is a body while the spiritual world is its soul, bringing it to life. Therefore whoever strives in and for this physical world without any awareness of the spiritual world, his efforts are nothing more than aimless wandering, for it is without any essential purpose or result. But who strives in this world with the goal of acquiring the rewards of the spiritual world, his effort does have a purpose and a result, and is met with Allaah's acceptance.’

Imagine a person who spends all their time grinding in a virtual reality video game, accumulating digital gold and building virtual empires. They sacrifice sleep and health for this game. But the moment the power goes out or the game server shuts down, all that effort vanishes because it had no anchor in reality. Striving solely for this physical world without preparing for the eternal spiritual world is the exact same aimless wandering.

‘You have spoken truly,’ said the young man. ‘So explain to me the distinctive signs of this physical world and what they contain, and lay out for me the various symbols of this world, connecting them to their inner representations.’

The scholar began: ‘Allaah, may He be praised and exalted, did not create a single creature among the creations of this physical world—not an animal crawling upon the earth; nor a creature flying with its wings; nothing wet nor dry; neither inanimate objects assembled like the mountains, stones, or trees; nor minerals like gold, silver and gems; neither things great nor small - but as a "symbol" established by Him. Don’t you see that even the tiniest dust mote, despite its tiny size and essential meaning, is bound from all its sides? It has a front, a back, a right and a left, a top and a bottom! Those are its six boundaries, while the dust mote itself is the seventh of them. The same holds true of everything that Allaah has created — from the dust motes or the gnat to whatever species exist above them; from the heavens to the earths and everything in between them, from the biggest to the smallest. Every creation is surrounded by six boundaries, and the creature itself is the seventh. These six boundaries are a sign pointing to the six root-principles from which everything was created, while the “Air” which encompasses them all is their seventh and their king! So now, if your heart has opened up to these questions, and your intellect is able to compare these symbols with their spiritual partners from the inner dimension, then ask of me about whatever seems appropriate to you.’

The 'Wilaayah' of the Commander of the Faithful and the pure Imaams corresponds to the Air, which encompasses all other pillars of Islam. Every entity is a seventh dimension encompassed by six. Without the seventh, the six cannot exist. Similarly, without Wilaayah, all other actions yield no lasting spiritual benefit.

"Then what is symbolized by the Heaven," asked the young man, "with it towering above all creation and the vast extent of its regions, such that it totally encompasses everything?’

‘It symbolizes the Naa-tiq, replied the scholar, who is towering above all spiritual creations by virtue of his excellence, and the loftiness of his elevated rank. By the vast extent of his knowledge, he knows all the inner meanings behind the actions of the revealed path.’

‘Then what is symbolized by the mansions of the 12 Zodiacs, and the rest of their stars?’ the young man inquired.

‘They symbolize the 12 Nuqaba,’ the Scholar explained, ‘who are the chiefs of the Naa-tiq, and the stars are their dua'at who call people to good, with the permission of their Lord.’

‘Then what is symbolized by the Earth and its great extent, asked the young man, ‘since it has been made a resting place and a refuge for all creatures?’

‘It is a symbol of the Asaas of the Naa-tiq during his life time,’ the Scholar responded, ‘and of his designated Wasi (trustee) after his death. For he is indeed a refuge for the people and a resting place for whoever is seeking right guidance. He knows the various symbols and the inspired interpretation of their inner aspects.’

‘Then what is symbolized by the 12 Regions on the earth?’ asked the young man.

‘They symbolize the 12 Hujjah, who are the proofs, assisting the Wasi,’ replied the scholar.

‘And what are symbolized by the earth’s Rivers and its Springs?’ asked the young man.

‘They are the symbols of the dua'at, those who call people to good,’ the scholar answered.

‘Then what is symbolized by the spiritual entities who are the Rulers of the Seven Heavens and of their spheres?’ asked the young man.

‘Those are the intermediaries between Allaah and the Nutaqa,’ explained the Scholar. ‘They represent the seven heavenly spheres (baa, jeem, laam, daal, meem, laam-haa, meem), plus the two outermost spheres, which are the greatest of Allaah’s intermediaries.’

‘Then what is symbolized by the Seven seas?’ asked the young man.

‘They are the Intermediaries between Allaah and the Imaams following the Naa-tiq, and are beneath the previously mentioned intermediaries’ the Scholar replied.

‘Then what is symbolized by the Salty Water and the Sweet, Fresh Water,’ asked the young man, ‘and by the difference between them here on earth?’

‘The salty water,’ explained the Scholar, ‘represents the knowledge of the Outward aspect of things, which does not contain any spiritual calling and contains no good, just as salty water has no support for growth or anything useful. But the sweet, fresh water that comes either from springs flowing in the earth, or rain water coming down from above the earth, symbolizes the knowledge of the Inner, spiritual aspect of things which is conveyed by the dua'at.'

‘How did water become a symbol for knowledge?’ asked the young man.

‘Because water is life for every living being,’ the Scholar responded, just as knowledge is life for every scholar.’

‘So what, then, is symbolized by the Air which encompasses everything?’ asked the young man.

‘The Air is too exalted to be a symbol for any creation’ replied the scholar.

‘Is Air greater or are the heavens and the earth greater’ asked the young man,‘since they are both symbols?'

‘May Allaah be glorified beyond all limitations!’ exclaimed the Scholar. "Indeed it is greater than both of them. For the extent of the earth in relation to the Air is like a seal-ring (mahr kee angoothee) in relation to a great plain. Similarly, the extent of the earth’s atmosphere in relation to the heavens is like a seal-ring in relation to a vast desert. But the expanse of the heaven and its regions, in comparison to the full extent of the Air is still like an egg in relation to an immense plain. Therefore what possibly could symbolize Air and point to it? There is none like Air!’

‘Then what is its inner and essential meaning?’ inquired the young man.

The scholar replied: ‘It is a sign pointing toward the One who has nothing as His likeness—may He be glorified and exalted above whatever they associate with Him!’

‘But didn’t the Creator say: (Fala tadriboo lillahi al-amthala inna Allaha ya'lamu wa antum la ta'lamoon) “So do not make any likenesses for Allaah, for He knows and you don't”?’ the young man objected.

(haadhaa mathal ad-Daa‘ee)” For their symbols have become signs pointing to them and to their respective dignities. And that unique symbol is the Air, which is higher than the heavens and the earth, encompassing everything and not being encompassed by any thing. Being the loftiest symbol among all, it points to the One who has no likeness. Allaah did not say “Nothing is like Him,” since He obviously has no “like.” Rather, He meant that the sign pointing to him is not like all the other signs—for His is the loftiest symbol — and that none of the other signs pointing to his other creations are at all like His sign. So blessed be Allaah, Lord of the majestic throne!’

He is not saying that 'Air' is like Allaah, but rather that nothing among the creations is like Air, just as nothing among the creations is like Allaah.

‘Your meaning has certainly become immense and its scope has become vast!’ exclaimed the young man. ‘So was there anything before the Air, between it and Allaah, given what you have described of its limitlessness?’

‘The Instance, replied the scholar.

‘Now you’ve carried me into the depths of the sea of this loftiest assembly the young man remarked. ‘But return with me to the knowledge of this physical world and its symbols. Perhaps I’ll be able to find help in that knowledge and be able to use it to uphold what is expected of me based on my strength. For in this moment I fear for my soul and am worried that my intellect might have led me away toward something whose essence I am unable to grasp.’

‘You did climb high in your questioning, the Scholar replied, ‘and my answers climbed with you to the very peak of these "inner aspects". Therein you lost your vision and your intellect got perplexed. So then tell me what state would you have been in, had I started to unveil the inner meanings of these inner aspects for you? You and I would have been like Moosaa and the scholar Khidr when patience was tested over hidden realities. (Fawajada 'abdan min 'ibadina ataynahu rahmatan min 'indina wa 'allamnahu min ladunna 'ilma) (And they found a servant from among Our servants to whom we had given mercy from us and had taught him from Us a certain knowledge).’

So that makes three levels of revealed knowledge:
1. The clothing which is the outer aspect.
2. Its inner purpose which is to cover your private parts and adorn yourself.

‘Now, as for the intellectual proof of this,’ the Scholar continued, ‘everything can only be known by means of either its name, its specific characteristics or its meaning. But meaning is the real essence of the thing, while the name "belongs" to it and the characteristic only "points" to it. The same is true of Allaah’s religion. It has its outer aspect - the name; its inner dimension - the characteristics, which points to it’s essential reality; and the innermost level which is its true meaning, which can subsist only through both the previous layers (name and characteristics).

‘Now we can only point to something through either a name or a specific characteristic of that thing, as when someone says “The moon rose.” Now the essential meaning is the actual round disk in the sky. “Moon” is its name, and “Rising” is its specific characteristic. But if someone were to say “the disk” and then stop, that would have no real meaning. Or if they were to say “the moon” and then stop, that would also have no meaning; and if they were to say “it rose,” that would likewise be without meaning. It is only when the name and the specific characteristic are combined, such that one says “The moon rose,” that the real meaning appears and what was said about it is actually understood. ‘Likewise, if you were to say “the outer aspect,” then someone would have to ask in response: “the outer aspect of what?” And if you were to say “the inner aspect,” again it would be necessary to ask in response: “the inner aspect of what?” But if you said “the outer aspect of religion and its inner aspect,” then what you said would be understandable. The revealed paths are its outer aspect, while the inner spiritual dimensions are its inner aspect that subsists by itself. Therefore in order to understand its essential meaning and reality, there are many symbols pointing to it, one of which is the "Egg.". An egg has its outer aspect - the shell, which hides the inner contents; its inner aspect is the white of the egg; and the inner dimension of that inner aspect is its yellow yoke, which is its true substance and essential meaning.’

Consider the architecture of a modern digital application. The outer shell (Zaahir) is the graphical user interface—the buttons and screens that everyone interacts with. The inner white (Baatin) is the backend source code that dictates how it behaves. But the innermost yolk (Baatin al-Baatin) is the core machine code and database architecture that serves as the absolute foundation for everything else. Without the core, the code is useless; without the code, the interface is dead.

This section echoes a teaching attributed to Ali, peace be upon him: people are of three kinds — the divine scholar, the learner on the path of salvation, and the confused masses who follow every call. As for that knowledge through which Allaah raised the ranks of His friends, you are now at the beginning of that stage. So if you put that knowledge into action, you can reach its culmination. Now the imam is called hawl because he “transforms and conveys” the Creator’s speech (hawl kalaam al-khaaliq), with all the subtlety of its essential meaning, converting them into words of speech for human beings, so that they can understand it. Thus Allaah’s meanings of wisdom get veiled behind the words, such that the words of wisdom become the body and form, while the Creator’s speech becomes the true spirit of life and light of salvation (rooh al-hayaat wa noor an-najaat). For in the same way, an outward religious action without any inner spiritual dimension will not be accepted at all, no matter how often it is repeated. Likewise a vision seen in a dream does not give any lasting result at all, even if there are many. Thus the king said, according to the transmission of the common people, “Verily, I see seven things revolving around seven others.” ‘But as for the true meaning of the seven fat cows, they are Allaah’s Seven heavenly intermediaries (angels) who are between Him and the Nutaqa. As for the seven thin cows, they are the seven Nutaqa, who are compelled by their hunger and need for what comes from Allaah by means of those seven divine intermediaries. And as for the seven green ears of wheat, they are the seven spiritual intermediaries between Allaah and the Wasi (of the imams), while the seven dry ones are the Awsiya themselves, who are compelled by their hunger and need for what comes to them from what is with Allaah, by means of those seven intermediaries. So blessed be Allaah, Lord of the worlds!’

‘Then what do they symbolize for us?’ asked the young man.

‘These symbols, with respect to the Asaas and his sons,’ explained the Scholar, ‘are like a seed which has grown into seven ears of grain — I mean, from whose seed there have come seven imams.’

The young man continued: ‘But then how is it that Allaah has prohibited this physical world, since he said: (Fala taghurrannakumu al-hayatu ad-dunya) "Then do not let the life of this physical world deceive you"?’

‘Allaah did speak truly,’ replied the scholar. ‘He didn’t say “Don’t let this physical world deceive you.” Instead He said: “Don't let the life of this physical world deceive you.” Because “life” has four meanings:
1. The outward life in this physical world and its ultimate outcome, which is passing away
2. The life of the spiritual world and its ultimate outcome, which is eternally lasting.

Hence He said that you shouldn’t act for this passing, transient life, but you should act for the lasting and eternal life. For that reason He said: (Yaqoolu ya laytanee qaddamtu lihayatee) “He will say, I wish I had prepared for my life!—which is to say, “If only I had prepared during this passing life for the lasting life.” And the meaning of these two kinds of life is,
1. Life through knowledge of its outward physical aspects (hayaat bi-‘ilm az-zaahirah), and
2. Life through knowledge of its inner spiritual aspect (hayaat bi-‘ilm al-baatinah).

‘For knowledge of the outward aspect of this physical world is of the lowest form. But knowledge of the inner aspect is indeed the cause for true life in the eternal world. Because of this He said: Don't let yourselves be deceived by the "life" of this physical world!’—that is, don’t be deluded by the outward aspect of knowledge and by actions according to that alone. For the outcome of your actions will only be accepted from you through their inner aspect and through your upholding and accomplishing that along with the outward aspect. The outward aspect of "action" is not accepted without the inner aspect of "knowledge".

‘What do you think,’ asked the young man, ‘about someone who knows the knowledge of the inner aspect, but who doesn’t know the knowledge of the outer aspect and doesn’t uphold it? What would his rank be, according to the people of religion?’

‘What a terrible position!’ the Scholar replied. ‘For in that case the inner aspect of religion couldn't subsist and be sound, since that person would have neglected something that has been commanded by Allaah and has been established as a protection for the inner aspect. This is similar to the outer aspect of fruits! If their outer covering is peeled off before they ripen, they rot and are useless after that. The body works just the same way. If its limbs are cut off, the spirit won’t remain in it for even a moment. And that is how the outward aspects of religion function. If its basic obligations aren’t carried out, then its inner aspect won’t be realized for that person. But in fact, such a person’s neglect of the outward aspect of religion, without any permission from the One who made it obligatory for them, can only be for one of two reasons.
1. If they neglected it because of some incapacity, then they are even more incapable of realizing the inner aspect.
2. Or if they neglected the outward aspect intentionally, even though they were able to uphold it, then they are willfully disobedient to the one Who commanded them to uphold it.

Now the disobedient person is an evildoer, and the evildoer cannot be a companion of the Friends of Allaah. Indeed, the evildoer is the enemy of the friends of Allaah, and they are his enemies, because of their cutting off what Allaah has commanded should be joined.’

So this physical world can only be religiously licit for someone who truly knows the other world, which is its life and its inner aspect. Likewise religion is only complete for its people once they uphold both its outward and its inner aspects.’

‘Yes,’ the scholar answered, ‘that is the true meaning of religion, and that is the way "actions" should be, because upholding the totality of what Allaah has commanded leads you to deserve His satisfaction; but neglecting some of what Allaah has commanded exposes you to His wrath.’

‘Then isn’t the person who knows the outer aspect through the inner dimension of religion and who upholds and accomplishes both, the person of faith?’ asked the young man.

‘Yes,’ said the scholar, ‘such a person is in reality a person of faith.’

‘Then what about the person who has recognized the true religion and acted for the sake of the spiritual world,’ continued the young man: ‘Doesn’t this physical world become good for such a person, and aren’t its good things licit for him, along with enjoying its fruits?’

‘Yes,’ replied the scholar, ‘this physical world is not for anyone but them; they have the best right to it and they are most deserving of it.’

‘But then why is it,’ the young man responded, ‘that I see the people of this description, those who are the true knowers among the people of this lofty rank, rejecting this physical world, looking askance at it, as though their souls were weighed down by it? Indeed they have rejected this physical world and what it contains to such a degree that someone who didn’t know them, would think that they considered it forbidden. Moreover, I see that their skin has become pale from nightly prayers and vigil without them being sick, and that their bodies are so weak from fasting, and their skin sticks so tightly to their bones that it seems they are not eating at all, even when they are eating.’

When the Scholar heard him mentioning his spiritual brothers and their description, he was overcome with emotion, and his eyes overflowed with tears, choking him to the point where he wasn’t able to speak anymore. Seeing him cry, the young man too started weeping, until the Scholar became worried that some of their enemies might surprise them. So he controlled himself, and calmed his heart, and then approached the young man.

And the Scholar said: ‘O my dear son, the people you have described, have looked at themselves with the true light of life, and they have devoted themselves to perfecting their affair with the certainty of salvation. They were not being harsh on themselves. O my dear son, truly this physical world is viewed in terms of possessions and progeny. But the spiritual world is seen through true knowledge and certainty. O my dear son, those people were right in having faith in the unseen, for they were certain about the divine promises and threats. Their hearts were longing for what they had been promised, and the fear of Allaah’s threats has filled their hearts with fear, and so they struggled fiercely! They were in dread of the pangs of death, so they hastened to "ACT" while they had the time. During their struggle their bodies became weak from their persistent fasting; and their complexions became pale from their long night-vigils of praying. They did not sleep with the heedless sleepers out of anxiety for their soul’s fate; and they did not eat except with repugnance. They were distracted from the pleasures of life by the attractions of reflection, and they were so busy tasting the sweetness of remembrance of Allaah, that they forgot to sleep. Their souls turned away from all carnal attractions, such that this physical world did not seem agreeable to them. They were so busy remembering death, that the pleasures of this life did not seem pleasant anymore; and because they were constantly contemplating the blessings of the eternal world with certainty, they could only look askance at this physical world. Hence they only rejected this physical world because they were distracted from it by their concern for the next world, and not because they considered it forbidden.’

‘This is the true tiding!’ exclaimed the young man, ‘and this is the reason why the true scholars were rejecting this physical world. But most of the people are heedless of this reality assuming that they have somehow forbidden for themselves what Allaah had made licit for them. So describe for me the illusions of the people of this physical world concerning this world of theirs, and their heedlessness of the other world.’

‘If those who are attached to this physical world were to reflect upon their creation,’ explained the Scholar, ‘and the transformation of their states—from weakness to strength, then from strength back to weakness, from pleasure to pain, and from life to death; or if they were to closely observe the state of those who have gone before them from past centuries, and realize how the good and the evil among them passed away on the same level, without their seeing a reward for the good ones nor any retribution for the evil in this physical world, then they would realize that they are not created for this physical world. But instead they are so weighed down by what they are busy acquiring of this world, that they have not taken upon themselves the burden of seeking understanding. Therefore they grasped the goods of this lowest life, and became satisfied with the enjoyments of this physical world instead of the eternal world. This is their preoccupation and their essential reality, so they have no argument against Allaah, nor against His friends regarding the shame that will soon overcome them; and there has already come to them the notifications from Allaah containing details of what should restrain them.’

The young man said: "Praise be to Allaah Who guided us to His religion, for we would not have been rightly guided without His generosity. So inform me about the origin of these creatures and their outer and inner make-up. Were they a single creation, all alike? Or were they different, judging from the many differences in their opinions and passions? "

‘As for the creation of their bodies,’ the scholar began, 'they vary, as you can see from the difference in their languages and colors. That is a sign of Allaah’s power, in order for them to recognize each other. Through these distinctions a child recognizes his parents, and parents recognize their own children; a husband identifies his wife from other women, and the wife recognizes her husband from among other men— all because of Allaah’s justice toward His creatures. But as for the creation of their internal and external instruments and faculties, they are all created in an identical fashion. The similarity of their outer aspects should indicate to you that they are similar in their inner faculties as well. For did you ever see a scholar with three eyes, so that his extra knowledge was clearly visible? Or did you ever see an ignorant person with just one eye, so that his deficiency was visible? If that were the case, people would say “the scholar sees more with his additional instrument of sight, but the ignorant sees less due to his deficiency”! Did you ever really see any increase in certain physical instruments of one in comparison to others? ‘No. you will not see anything but equality in the creation of their physical instruments, and the essential reality of their genetic make-up will always be the same.

Therefore by logic we deduce that the inner instruments (of will and understanding) also follow this same pattern of divine justice. For Allaah would not treat His creatures justly in their outer aspect, and unjustly in their inner dimensions. He has not placed two hearts within the scholar's chest, nor has He forbidden any means of understanding for the ignorant. For the instrument of knowledge is one, and that internal make up extends to everyone. Now, it is by this instrument of knowledge, Allaah has established His argument against them, because He gave them the necessary instruments which they failed to employ.

Or do you think that Allaah, despite all His bounties and generosity, would impose on them a task which they are unable to perform or bear when he clearly said:

(La yukallifu Allahu nafsan illa wus'aha laha ma kasabat wa 'alayha ma iktasabat)
"Allaah does not burden anyone beyond his capabilities. He gets reward for that which he has earned, and he is punished for that which he has earned."

Of course not! Surely He is more just than that!’

‘That was not my intention, and neither do I believe that!’ exclaimed the young man. ‘I was merely asking you about the difference in people’s passions/factions. Where did that difference come from when their instrument of knowledge is the same and the root of their physical and intellectual make-up is one?’

‘The root of their essence is the same,’ replied the scholar. ‘They all come from Aadam, and Aadam came from dust. His Lord created him, and then breathed His spirit into him. But then through marriage, they left behind the divine way. Their progeny abandoned the divine creed and followed different paths that led them astray. Indeed I do not doubt that among them were illegitimate children claiming as their fathers those who were not their real parents. Once the root is corrupted, the branches are soon ruined. Such people changed for the worse! Their envy and pride towards Allaah's friends kept them from Allaah's obedience; and their laziness prevented them from worshiping Him, and they were unable to return to the truth. They did not restrict themselves to what was licit, and did not avoid what was forbidden.’

‘But if their origin and their faculty of intelligence was the same,’ continued the young man, ‘then where did the difference in their passions/factions come from? Because the essential reality of what they were seeking was one, and each of them (under the guidance of their intellect) was seeking what was good for him.’

‘By my life!’ responded the scholar. ‘The essential reality of what they were seeking was indeed one. But they all differed in actually reaching that unified goal! This is similar to people trying to shoot arrows at a particular target. Even though they were alike in their intention and their instruments for shooting, they all still differed in actually hitting the target. This same pattern applies to the hearts of human beings which are in agreement with their minds, and their minds are in agreement about preserving and respecting their souls. Yet when they were all called to the truth, they differed among themselves. Some of them concluded that responding to the call of divine messengers, and surrendering their will to them was part of respecting their souls and preserving it from corruption. While others among them believed that denying the messengers and refusing to obey them was likewise part of respecting their souls and preserving it from the constraints of servitude. So the root in both cases was the same. They were both seeking ease for their bodies and proper respect for their souls. However one group accepted what the messengers had promised them of Allaah’s eternal recompense, even though it was small and rare in this lower world; while the other group chose the enjoyments of this lower world, because even though its pleasures were fleeting, it was readily available, easy to seek and quick in its rewards. Therefore the first group chose the blessings of the other world with their hearts, while the other sought the enjoyments of this lower world with their hearts. The fact is, they were all concerned with the salvation of their souls and chose the action that they considered to be the best for them. They were all alike in their quest, yet differed in what they actually obtained. All that is from Allaah’s justice and generosity and His bounty. Each one receives from Him what he sought.’

‘Now the people of this lower world are in different levels,’ replied the young man. ‘Some of them are so rich that they never need to work. By the abundance of their possessions, they live like kings. While others are poor, and out of their need for those possessions, and to obtain their sustenance, they end up serving those kings. Now since they are all together on the same level in striving to accumulate the pleasures of this lower world, then is their torment equally unpleasant when they suffer in hell, or do some of them have a lower position than others?’

‘It is only part of Allaah’s justice,’ the scholar answered, ‘to join them together and not keep them separate from their comrades and close companions. So they are all alike in receiving their punishment from Him, just as they were all in agreement when disobeying Him!’

The young man continued: ‘Then what about this religion that Allaah has honored and perfected? The one He accepted as his preferred religion and imposed as a duty on His servants? Do you think that He will accept less (in terms of actions) from some of his bondsmen in comparison to others, such that they will have different levels in heaven, just like the people of this lower world you mentioned earlier? Or will He accept only the actions of those who accomplishes them perfectly?’

‘If the people of this lower world have their levels,’ the scholar replied, ‘then surely the people of religion should also have their ranks. Certainly it is a necessary aspect of Allaah’s justice that the bondsmen should worship the Creator with the perfect devotion of their will. But His self-sufficiency is so great without them, and their need for Him is so great, that He extended His loving mercy toward them. He knew that some were weak among them, so He accepts from them whatever (actions) they are able to perform in good faith, and wholehearted devotion, no matter how great or small those acts may be. Therefore, He imposed the duty of knowledge and action in accordance with it on every person according to their capacity, and He gave of His bounty to everyone of merit, so they have many ranks with their Lord. Then in His justice, and as the ultimate outcome of their obedience, He joined them with people of the most complete faith and the noblest actions among His servants. This is a reward for their wholehearted obedience - not because of their superlative actions, but as a loving mercy from Allaah towards them even though, they and their actions were insignificant.’

‘Praise be to Allaah Who did that for them!’ exclaimed the young man. ‘Should anyone fear being punished for something other than his own disobedience, even if his good actions were few?’

‘Allaah is more just than that,’ the scholar answered.

‘And can infants and the insane hope to enter heaven without any deserving actions?’ asked the young man.

‘How difficult is that action for Allaah in comparison to the vast extent of His bounty and generosity and His all-encompassing loving mercy?’ replied the scholar.

The young man continued, 'And what level of effort must we make in striving to obey Allaah and in wholeheartedly performing righteous acts, such that Allaah will have to accept our excuses for not being able to accomplish the rest?’

‘Attainment of knowledge and action to the furthest extent of your capabilities!’ replied the scholar.

‘Then describe to me the ultimate limits of that knowledge and action that are required of me,’ asked the young man, ‘so that I can test myself to see if I am able to attain the furthest extent of what pleases Allaah. If not, I will at least know that I have stopped at the limits of my capacity—and it is certainly more fitting for Allaah to forgive what I am unable to do. For I am sure of Allaah’s justice and have faith that He will not do wrong. I only fear the harmful influence of my own carnal self.’

‘Have no fear regarding that in which you have faith,’ replied the scholar, ‘but neither feel too confident regarding that which you should fear. And strive vigorously for Allaah in your acts with your soul and your possessions. For Allaah is not pleased with that which He abhors, nor does He abhor that which pleases Him. He Certainly abhors ingratitude in His creatures, and He is pleased by their gratitude and justice towards Him, therefore He will accept nothing less . Sincerity and faithfulness are His two Gardens, so none will be saved except the sincere and the faithful!’

The young man asked, 'Then show me the goal of my actions with respect to my soul, and what Allaah has made obligatory for me in regard to that goal.'

‘Surely obedience to Allaah ennobles all those who practice it,’ the scholar began, ‘even if their roots (in understanding it) are weak. But righteous acts are the crown of those who strive purely for Allaah. So it is obligatory for you to carry out all your religious obligations and to abstain from all, that has been prohibited. That is the very foundation of religion and the offerings of our bodies. And in general, the rule that will make your actions deserving of Allaah’s satisfaction and acceptance, is that you interact with his creation in a manner that you would want for them to behave with you; and that you refrain from interacting with them in a manner that you would not want them to behave with you. Justice lies in exactly that, and Allaah’s satisfaction is with whatever is just.’

‘Yes, that is indeed the obligatory truth, and the one who fails to do that has nothing to do with Allaah!’ the young man responded. ‘But in the event that someone is forced by necessity to act in a contrary manner, is there any allowance?’

The scholar responded: ‘Certainly Allaah has made allowances for His servants as a mercy and loving kindness from Him, since He said:

(Famani idturra ghayra baghin wa la 'adin fala ithma 'alayh)
"So whoever is forced by necessity, neither desiring the forbidden nor willfully disobeying, there is no sin for that person."

‘Praises be to Allaah, who did not place any undue burden on His servants in religion!’ exclaimed the young man. ‘I have now resolved to uphold all that I have been commanded to do, and I ask Allaah’s help in all that He has commanded. So what are my obligations with regard to my possessions, since you mentioned those along with me when said to act with myself and my possessions?‘

‘As for your possessions,’ replied the scholar, ‘that is what Allaah has bestowed on you from what you have earned. So only take from that what was lawfully acquired, and only spend it on what is rightfully deserving!’

‘But what is that licit portion that I should take from my income,’ asked the young man, ‘and what is the rightfully deserving cause for which it should be spent?’

‘As for the licit portion of your possessions,’ began the scholar, ‘those are the acquisitions, in the course of which no one was wronged, and which are not tainted by any falsehood. You should then purify them by taking out one-fifth from its finest portion that you love the most, and spend it on those who have a right on it and are most deserving of it. In doing so, you will purify your possessions through charity, just as you purified your body through your righteous acts. For right action is a protection, and charity is a purification and whoever purifies himself has prospered.’

‘I will do that,’ responded the young man, ‘and I will turn to Allaah in repentance for what has gone before. Then what should I do with the remainder of my possessions?’

‘Spend it on yourself and your family in the accustomed manner,’ replied the scholar. ‘And if its surplus continues to grow, then give it in charity to the needy among your brothers and spend on what you think is appropriate on those who are pleading for your help, and are forced to ask for your generosity. So race towards every good act, and be the first in reaching what is excellent! For that is the true goal of your actions, if you persevere in them!’

‘Yes, I will persevere, and continue to persevere,’ the young man answered, ‘for how can I not persevere when I am immensely in need of the One Who is self-sufficient without my actions? For the one who is needy must be patient, and the one who bestows favors is to be thanked.’

The narrator continued: Now when the scholar was pleased with the words of the young man, he said: ‘Praise be to Allaah who has brought you to speak so accurately! I implore Him to help you fulfill your (required) deeds, for that is the perfection of excellence and true merit.’

‘I have always been seeking generosity for my soul from the hand of others,’ replied the young man, ‘but when my soul tried to be generous towards me, I forbade it, for I have not found anyone more disloyal to me than my own (carnal) self. So now, here before you are all my possessions, divided into five portions. One portion is for its purification; One is for my atonement for whatever harm I've committed against my soul by my misdeeds; One portion is in gratitude to Allaah for the knowledge I have acquired (through you); One portion is for the poor among my brothers; and one portion is to support you during your travels, which you must kindly accept.’

The narrator continued: Hearing that, the scholar’s heart was filled with emotions for the young man, by witnessing the purity of his essence, the praiseworthy depth of his experience that was revealed by his test; and by the manifestation of his generosity and the purity of his actions.

This shows that the test begins as soon as knowledge is acquired. The moment the scholar imparted the knowledge of the divisions of wealth, the young man immediately acted upon it and distributed his belongings.

Then with his eyes overflowing with tears, the scholar said: ‘O my son, it is for the likes of you that (true) scholars under take their difficult journey. It is for the likes of you this earth carries its burden and the sky expands its shade. It is by the likes of you that the intimate ties of spiritual kinship are manifested. And it is upon the likes of you that the showers of divine loving mercy descend through the split in the clouds! Congratulations my son! for all that Allaah has bestowed upon you, and opening your heart to true Islam. Now I am bringing you the good news of a light from your Lord and a victory that is near, and I am offering you a gift from Allaah and the benefits of His gracious blessings. So your obligation is to practice abundantly and be grateful with nobility.

As for my portion of your wealth, I have no need of it. Indeed, I have left behind possessions and fled from the conflicts and temptations of wealth. Therefore my son, it would certainly not be respectful piety for you to force me in returning to what I dislike. And as for the portion of your brothers, they are already here with you. So if they ask you, give it to them with your own hands so that they recognize your generosity and merit, and be thankful for your acts of kindness. As for the portion for atonement, keep it with you so that you keep remembering your sins. As for the portion of thankfulness and gratitude, consider that a trust to you by Allaah, until you attain your full maturity and have reached the ultimate goal of what you have come to know. And as for the portion for the purification of your wealth, that is also entrusted to you by its rightful master. So keep it all with you until you meet him. For he is the most rightful judge for you, and against you!

The young man asked, ‘What kind of authority does that noble person have over you, such that you do not oppose his judgment or go against his command?’

‘That person,’ replied the scholar, ‘is the one whose right Allaah has made obligatory to me and to all people of true faith; the one in whose hands are the keys of the heavenly Gardens; the one who is the guide for angels; the one whose palms open up to reveal the light of Sinai, so that he becomes the reason for the divine signs; the one who ascends to the peaks of the ultimate goals through his knowledge. It is through him that your light will be completed! and it is by him that Allaah will perfect your affair (yukmil Allaahu amrak), and it is through his hands, blessings will pour down on you (tajree laka al-barakaat).’

‘That is indeed true generosity!’ exclaimed the young man. 'So what is he for you, and what are you for him?’

‘I am his son, replied the scholar, ‘and one of his good works (hasanah min hasanaatihi). I am constantly seeking his satisfaction, and fearing his wrath.’

‘I suspect that I am far removed from pleasing him and much closer to incurring his wrath,’ remarked the young man, ‘since I do not even know him! Do I have any excuse for that?’

‘The excuse (you mentioned) only exists for you before you are offered the chance to know him,’ replied the scholar.

The excuse of not acting upon this principle is only valid until one gains knowledge about it.

‘Then should I speak or stay silent?’ asked the young man.

‘Say whatever seems right to you!,’ responded the scholar.

‘Now, at this time, isn’t it my obligation to seek your help in reaching the gate of repentance so that I can enter it (baab al-hittah hattaa adkhul), and to seek your help in reaching the house of light so that I may be illuminated by it (wa bayt an-noor hattaa aqtabis)?’ the young man enquired. ‘Surely that must be part of perfecting that trust which you must pass on?’

‘Indeed that is my obligation,’ the scholar answered, ‘but it is subject to his permission and his good judgment.’

# Chapter 4 — The Greater Shaykh

The narrator continued: Then the scholar left the young man. The young man wanted him to take some of his wealth as provisions for his journey, but he was unwilling to accept any of that. The scholar travelled until he reached his own spiritual father. He then narrated the story of this young man. His (spiritual) father recognized the young man’s description and knew from his qualities that he was among the good and the wellsprings of excellence and bounty, and so he was pleased.

He said: 'Hurry in returning back to me with this young man! For I would like for him to become a gateway of loving kindness that Allaah may open for the people of his time.’

The narrator continued: The scholar then returned and spent some time away, until one day he approached the young man and found him in a very different physical state from the one he had left him in.

So the scholar said: ‘How is it that I see you in such a different state from what I left you in?’

‘May my father and mother be your ransom! What don’t you recognize about me?’ the young man asked

‘The weakness of your body and the paleness of your complexion,’ replied the scholar.

‘That is from the loneliness and grief that overcame me after you left,’ said the young man.

‘But why this loneliness?’ asked the scholar, ‘and what is the cause of this sickness?’

Your separation made me lonely,’ replied the young man, ‘and the fear of what might happen to me before I met you again made me sick.’

‘Allaah already knew what was in your heart,’ the scholar responded, ‘so He made your period of testing easier, and He placed a special loving mercy for you in the heart of His friend. So start preparing for your journey. For I am about to help you reach the object of your hope! I am taking you to the gateway of your ultimate goal—and there is no power but through Allaah!’

The narrator continued: Hearing that, the young man sighed so heavily with joy and awe that his spirit almost left his body—until Allaah returned it to him, because of what He knew about him. Then when he regained consciousness,

He said: ‘Praise be to Allaah Who is eternally compassionate and merciful with those who have faith! (alhamdu lillaah alladhee lam yazal bil-mu’mineen raheeman)’

The narrator continued: He thanked his (spiritual) father profusely, and started kissing his hands and feet. Then he stood up hurriedly and began preparing for the journey. They both left his town and travelled on until they reached the greater scholar. There they asked permission to see him. They were admitted in his house where they greeted him, and sat down with his permission.

The young man said to the scholar. ‘Teach me how I should speak and how I should request what I need.’

‘I am not permitted to teach you anything while you are in this place,’ the scholar said to him, out of respect for my master. ‘So be quiet, because you are in the hands of someone who is aware of what you need, and who knows what it is that you seek.’

The narrator continued: The young man remained silent and they both stayed with the Shaykh for a while, until the young man’s affair was completed and his guidance was perfected and he attained the goal of his hopes and came to know his rights and obligations. Then they both took leave and left together, cautiously and in secret. Neither of them uttered a word during their visit, except for their greetings and expressions of friendship. The young man had become so pale from his powerful feeling of awe, that he wasn’t able to speak a single word. The Shaykh ordered the master of the inn to treat them with proper respect, and dignity and to take care of all their needs. So the innkeeper accompanied them back to his inn, brought them inside, greeted them both properly and expressed his great happiness at seeing them.

Then the innkeeper asked the scholar, who was also his close friend with whom he shared a longstanding sense of brotherhood and mutual understanding: ‘My brother, is this the young man we’ve all been hearing about?’

‘Yes,’ he replied, ‘this is the one whose news has gladdened so many hearts, and who is being mentioned by all the faithful.’

‘Has my news come to such a point that the friends of Allaah are talking about me in their gatherings?’ asked the young man.

‘Yes, my brother,’ said the scholar. ‘That’s exactly what's happening!’

The young man exclaimed, 'Allaah’s graces and blessings are flowing over me from all directions, and His favors are coming to me from every side! So I don’t know who to thank or how to repay!’

The narrator continued: Then they all sat together and talked late into the night. And when they got up in the morning, they asked permission to see the Shaykh. They entered his room and after greeting him and with his permission sat down. Then the Shaykh began speaking:

The meeting with the greater Shaykh is not presented as a social visit. It is a controlled entry into a higher level of responsibility. In modern terms, it resembles entering a secure institution where credentials, conduct, timing, and readiness all matter. Access is not denied out of cruelty; it is delayed until the seeker can bear what he is asking to receive.

‘Praises be to Allaah who split the dark obscurity of hearts with His light, and who unlocked the chambers of what their hearts sought with His justice! Who makes remembrance easy by fashioning everything as a subtle grace for His servants, and a loving mercy for all those who are longing for Him. Creatures cannot bear the essence of what He deserves in return, for all His blessings. Yet He is not pleased by them when they are ungrateful. He therefore, in His justice, imposed upon them what they were able to bear. His command was therefore harmoniously balanced between those two points, such that it was called "Justice." And out of His bounty, He has given everyone abundantly from His favors. Thus neither the one who prays to Him is left disappointed, nor is the silent excluded from His bounty. On the contrary, His blessings are all-encompassing and his favors are marvelous! They are not limited to those who are grateful, even though the rewards of the grateful are overwhelming; nor are His blessings removed from those who are ungrateful, even though their ultimate torment is immense! For He has ennobled His blessings above all sorts of stinginess, and He has firmly established His arguments beyond all possibility of denial. He has extended His all-inclusiveness bounty without any form of constraints (fa-akrama ni‘amahu ‘an al-iqtaar), yet He has so many signs that He cannot possibly be denied (wa-stawthaqat hujajuhu ‘an al-inkaar). So He exists everywhere, but not within boundaries (fa-huwa mawjooodun ghayru mahdoood); and He is well known, but cannot be described (wa ma‘roofun ghayru mawsoof). Praise be to Him Who is so immense, yet so subtle with His creation (subhaanahu min ‘azheem maa altafahu bi-khalqihi)! Who is all-powerful, yet so merciful and loving in his dealings with His creatures (wa min jabbaar maa arhamahu bi-‘ibaadihi)! Who is solitary and supreme, yet just and wise in His judgments (wa min ‘azeez maa a‘dalahu fee hukmihi)! Who is infinitely exalted, yet so close to humankind (wa min ‘aalin maa aqrabahu min al-anaam)! Who is so near, yet infinitely removed from people’s imaginations (wa min qareeb maa ab‘adahu ‘an al-awhaam)! So worship and serve Him, and be grateful to Him, for to Him we are all going to return (Innaa lil-laahi wa innaa ilayhi raaji‘oon) (Indeed we belong to Allaah, and indeed to Him we will return).’

Then he said: ‘As for what comes next, as a sign of honor for people's intellect is their use in seeking of Allaah; and among the fruits of seeking Him, is finding Him; and among the distinctive signs of having found Him is its sweetness. For every fresh, sweet water has its outward aspect of drinking it, and its inner dimension of peace (when thirst is quenched).’’ The one who is truly seeking Him, never gets tired of thinking about Him, since creatures cannot grasp His ultimate ends. Its distinctive sign is the "truth", and the duties that flow from that truth are obligatory. By its nature of necessary verification, and realization of its obligatory duties, it is called the "truth". Therefore, whoever prevents people from carrying out the obligations of truth is unjust. For surely among the obligations flowing from thinking about Him is the purification of our hearts through the proper modes of behavior (min al-haqq al-fikr, zakaat al-quloob bil-aadaab); and among the obligations of that proper conduct, is the seeking of spiritual knowledge (wa min al-haqq al-aadaab, talab al-‘ilm); and among the obligations of that knowledge, is acting in accordance with it (wa min al-haqq al-‘ilm, al-‘amal); and among the obligations of those right actions, is its purification through willing obedience to the masters of true authority (wa min al-haqq al-‘amal bihi tahaarah bit-taa‘ah li-oolil-amr)’; and among the obligations of that willing obedience, is perfecting it both in ease and in hardship (wa min al-haqq at-taa‘ah kamaaluhaa fil-‘usr wal-yusr), and being tested in what is good, and in trials and temptations. So it shall only be granted to those who persevere, and it shall only be granted to the person who has immense good fortune!'

The narrator continued: Then he turned his gaze toward the young man and said: ‘O brave youth,’ you have been honored by a new intimate friend, and greeted by a visitor seeking (a soul like yours), so what is your name?’

U'bayd Allaah Ibn Abdullaah (Ubaydullaah ibn Abdullaah) (Servant of Allaah, son of the servant of Allaah),’ he answered.

The discussion about names is really a discussion about spiritual identity. A username is not the same as ownership of an account, and a title is not the same as authority. The Shaykh forces the young man to see that a name can describe him, but it does not automatically free him, authorize him, or establish his true station. This is close to the discipline of Ali, peace be upon him, who repeatedly links worth to knowledge, conduct, and rightful measure rather than empty claims.

‘That is your description,’ replied the Shaykh, ‘but we already know that about you.’

The young man said: "Then, I am Hurr (freed man), son of the servant of Allaah (Hurr ibn Abdullaah)’

‘Who set you free from being owned?’ inquired the shaykh.

‘This scholar set me free,’ said the young man, pointing with his hand toward the scholar who had called him (to the way).

The Shaykh continued, 'But if he too is owned by someone else, and not your owner, then does he have any right to set you free?’

'No, he doesn’t,' replied the young man.

‘Then what is your name?’ repeated the Shaykh.

The narrator continued: Then the young man hung his head low, bewildered and unable to respond.

‘O brave youth,’ pursued the Shaykh, ‘how can we recognize something that has no name, even if it has been born?’

‘I have been born to you,” replied the young man, 'so please give me a name!’

‘That can only happen after the fulfillment of seven days of waiting,’ the Shaykh responded.

‘But why must this task be delayed by seven more days?’ asked the young man.

‘In honor of the new-born infant,’ replied the Shaykh.

‘But what if the new-born should die before the seven days have passed?’ the young man continued.

'Nothing can harm him,’ replied the Shaykh, ‘and he will be named after that.’

‘Then will I own this name that you give me?’ asked the young man.

‘If you were to own that name, then you would become the one that is being worshipped (idhaa takoonu ma‘boodan)' replied the Shaykh.

‘But then how should we speak of it?’ asked the young Man

‘The name is your owner (al-ismu laka al-maalik),’ answered the Shaykh, ‘and you are owned by it (wa anta lil-ismi mamlook). For you cannot rise beyond your own spiritual rank (fa-laa talij illaa fee haddik). Now depart until your period of wait is complete.’

The narrator continued: Then the young man got up quickly, eager to obey, without replying back, because of his grasp on proper conduct. So he and his spiritual father left and returned to their residence. There the master of the inn greeted them and said:

'What is the state of our brother in regard to his needs?’

The scholar answered, ‘‘A promise was made to the one who is thirsty, and the appointed time is near.’

The narrator continued: They remained in the inn until the seventh day. Then they asked for permission to visit the Shaykh. The Shaykh gave them permission and ordered the young man to perform a complete ablution and dress in his finest clothing. The young man's heart was filled with joy in anticipation of what he was about to receive that very day. He knew that the preparations he had been ordered to undertake was a sign pointing to a good that would subsequently come to him. After performing what he had been ordered to do, as they both walked towards the Shaykh, the young man kept repeating to himself "What a lucky day, and what an auspicious master! (yawm sa‘d wa waliyy sa‘d)". It was the abundance of his joy and the intensity of his desire for the knowledge that would bring him closer to attaining Allaah’s satisfaction, that brought him to such a state of mind. When they both entered the Shaykh’s room, they found him in the midst of preparing for the ceremony. Then he greeted them with blessings of peace, and they returned his greetings.

The Shaykh ordered the young man to come closer to him, so he obeyed’ Then the shaykh came even closer and began to speak of realities that cannot be comprehended by imagination, that pens cannot write, and that has not occurred to the heart of any mere mortal.” That knowledge was among those precious jewels that are not mentioned in the schools of sermons and are not to be openly expressed in the contents of books. They are only unveiled to those who are worthy of it. The shaykh fulfilled his duty and led the young man out of his wandering (in the wilderness of doubts) and brought him to the end of his guidance. His way became pure, his drinking place became sweet,' and he came to know his Lord (wa ‘arafa Rabbah). Finally his goal was accomplished by glorifying what Allaah made sacred (fa-halla ihraamahu bi-ta‘zeem hurumaat Allaah). He held firmly to the solid connection (wa‘tasama bi-hablihi al-wathee) , circled around the House of Allaah (wa taafa bil-bayt al-‘ateeq), fulfilled his devotions through remembrance of Allaah (wa qadaa manaasikahu bidh-dhikraa), and completed his pilgrimage with Allaah’s greatest sign (wa tamma hajjuhu bil-aayaat al-kubraa).

After that, the young man and his spiritual father frequently visited the Shaykh in order to study what he had bestowed upon them. By his noble character, He was always patient with them, never getting tired by their questioning, and out of his agreeable nature he never grew weary of replying. So they continued their education for a certain period of time until the shaykh knew that the young man’s roots have been well watered and that his branches have grown tall.

Then (the young man) said to the shaykh, thanking Allaah and him: ‘Praise be to Allaah, Who made you worthy of this station, Who brought religion to life and destroyed ignorance through you! Who made you a refuge and a sanctuary for the people! For you are the gateway for the people of heaven and the ascending pathway for the people of the earth. From the light of your glory appear the lights of right guidance, and from the abundant rains of your palms, flows the rivers of Allaah’s grace and blessings. May you continue to do so such that the One who was pleased with you may gratify you!'

‘As for you,’ said the shaykh, ‘may Allaah bless you in all that He has bestowed upon you, and may He help grant you success in preserving what He has granted you. And may He use you properly to accomplish His will and may He make the way easy for you through the light of His right guidance. May He open up your heart with the keys of wisdom, and may He ennoble your affair through the path of the call, and perfect your rank and worth by the sincere signs of proper response to His call. And may He help you reach the most excellent and deserving degree of your hopes, for His satisfaction in your obedience to Him. I am now bidding you farewell, my son, and giving you permission to depart. Being separated from you is hard for me, but three things make it necessary for us to part. First is because we are about to travel to another country. Second, you have been separated from your family for a great length of time, despite the hardships you faced in leaving without your (physical) father’s permission. For in that is a terrible pain and a great sorrow for him that can only be removed by your return, although I do have hopes for your father to enter this path of guidance if you are clever and gentle with him. And third, it is your obligation to pass on what is rightfully required by this divine favor which you have received. It is now obligatory for you is to worship and serve in accordance with it and to call others to it.

‘Leaving you is sad for me and not having you is a great loss for me,’ replied the young man, ‘but following your advice is indeed the better path, and obeying you is more obligatory, and seeking your judgment is most accurate. So do give me some parting advice concerning what you judge to be right, and then send me away if you will. And please let my spiritual father (the scholar) travel with me to my country, so that I can rely upon him in my work and can look up to him for support and guidance in all my affairs.’

‘I shall do that,’ said the shaykh. ‘Now I advise you to be mindful of Allaah Who created you, to uphold the oath that you have taken upon yourself, and to safeguard and sustain your (spiritual) father, who has raised you when you were an infant and who has worked so hard and long on your behalf, with you and with your possessions. So neither dress him up in the garments of pride, nor drag him into situations that would destroy him. Do not allude to him by name and do not mention him in your conversation, nor visit him in your city, although at the beginning you may have some flexibility.’

The young man responded: ‘May Allaah reward you with good from an abundant source of blessings that He has stored for every grateful person. And may the Bountiful compensate you on behalf of those who you watch over, since their own efforts and favors cannot possibly do so. Now I shall observe your parting advice, and please do not stop my share in the blessings of your prayers!’

The narrator continued: Then they both got up and shook hands with the shaykh and embraced him, while he said goodbye to both. Unable to keep himself from crying, the young man could only speak with gestures. After that they departed, and travelled until they reached the out skirts of the young man’s city where his father lived.

Then the scholar said to the young man: ‘O my son, you've heard the Shaykh’s parting advice and you can only be rightly guided by his words. We have reached the outskirts of your city, so sit with me for a moment, so I can give you some instructions and final advice about how you should act.’

The scholar then said: ‘O my son! you are well aware of your father’s condition and his enmity toward the people of our cause. That has only been compounded by your leaving with me without his permission and blessing. Now I wish to carry out my (spiritual) father’s advice by concealing myself somewhere in this surrounding country. And as for you, you should return to your father and try to calm him down and pacify his anger. For I do hope that if you are clever and subtle with him, he will prove to be closer to what you desire - especially since he has been disturbed by your absence and saddened by his loneliness. O my son! You should know that with little effort on your part, Allaah has opened up a door for you. This is something that I don’t think you will fully appreciate until later. Allaah has been very good to you, so do good by your soul, and honor the one through whom Allaah has honored you. We have a very high opinion of you, and great hopes for you. You must safeguard the trust that your (spiritual) father has entrusted upon you and you must remain steadfast in what you have been commanded! Mindfulness of Allaah and careful precaution are the foundation of your religion and your religious practice, and gratitude and perseverance will increase your light. So I ask Allaah to provide you with the means, and to grant you the most beautiful success.’

Then the scholar continued: ‘My parting advice to you is of six things. (1) That once you have resolved (upon something), you entrust yourself completely to Allaah. (2) That you speak with words of wisdom when you call others to good. (3) That you not become angry when you are wronged. (4) That you not become upset when you are called a liar. (5) That you remain patient and perseverant with anyone who responds to your call. (6) And that you make sure that you become the best example of that, to which you are calling others. Allaah will surely help you and open up His support for you.’

‘May Allaah reward you rightly for three things,’ replied the young man: ‘(1) For the Imaam whose argument you have upheld; (2) The Hujjah whose trust you have earnestly fulfilled; (3) and the corpse, that you have so graciously given life to. By the immensity of what you have opened up for me, my heart and my soul are filled with light and joy. I don’t know whether the outer aspect of your words - that you have so eloquently arranged in its proper order are finer, or whether it is the inner dimension of those words that you have explained with all their essential meanings are finer!’

The scholar responded: 'Praise be to Allaah! who has made His way easy for you and has brought you closer to what was remote in Him, and who has helped you to bear His burden. Now I am going to bid you farewell and leave. So do you have any (special) need?’

'I have three fundamental needs’ the young man replied: ‘(1) A need which you have already fulfilled; (2) A need which I am now hoping to fulfill myself; (3) And a need for which I ask you to generously help me with.’

‘I have already understood your three needs,’ the scholar answered, ‘and I will respond to what you have requested. (1) As for your need that has already been fulfilled, it is your being given life through spiritual knowledge after being dead (for so long). May Allaah bless you with this new life and may He help you reach the heights of your aspirations! (2) As for the need of what you are still hoping for, it is to put your knowledge into practice in a way that may be acceptable to Allaah. (3) And as for that need for which you have asked my help, it is that you would love for me to add even more to what you have already learnt!’

‘Nothing you might add could be more remarkable than your knowledge of what was in my soul!’ exclaimed the young man. ‘But please do add whatever you think is best.’

‘As for my knowing what was in your soul,’ the scholar began, ‘no one but Allaah knows the unseen (fa-laa ya‘lamu al-ghayba illaa Allaah) and yet “the true believer does see with the light of Allaah. (wa laakin al-mu’min yanzuru bi-noor Allaah)” As for adding to it, I am setting out the basic principles for you, which should guide your actions. You should keep in mind the outward aspects of all the symbols I have mentioned to you, and you should use them as examples with the people you encounter. Thus whoever is praiseworthy among those your encounter, are similar to the symbols of truth and its people! Therefore apply the noblest of those symbols to the noblest of those men, just as their visible signs are pointing to them. And those who are similar to the reprehensible symbols, they are the enemies of truth and its people, therefore apply the greater symbols to the bigger enemies, and the lesser symbols to the lesser enemies, and you will find their visible signs are also pointing to them. These are the fundamental principles of knowledge on which righteous actions are based. If you follow these principles you will not need anything else. My parting advice to you is to be mindful of the One Who created you when you were nothing, and to safeguard His covenant, and to uphold His rights, which He has made an obligation for you and for all His creatures.’

Then when the scholar had finished speaking, and informed the young man about his refuge and where he could be found, and completed his spiritual training, he said: ‘O my son, take care of your soul! For now I have business to attend with others, so this is the moment of our separation’

The narrator continued: At the mention of their separation, the young man gave a great sigh as though his soul had taken flight, and his spirit had separated from his body. Then he came up to the scholar and kissed his feet, rubbing their dust on his cheeks. After that the scholar said farewell and left. The young man returned home, but he was unable to control his heart because of his sorrow, and was unable to hold back his tears because of his sadness at being separated from his greater father (the shaykh) and his mother (the scholar) who was the immediate cause of his blessings.

# Chapter 5 — Father and Community

He stayed depressed day and night from that grief to the point where his family and his neighbors started getting upset. Then one day his own (physical) father came in angrily, and said:

‘O my son, is this how sons repay their fathers? Now I have never known you to be anything but the best of sons. I have never disapproved of your state and your ways never seemed objectionable to me, until after that stranger came along. Then you chose him over me, and you went over to him and supported his cause instead of the path of your forefathers. So if what you recognized in him was the truth, then you have betrayed me by keeping that truth hidden from me. And if it was a false and vain cause, then you have betrayed and destroyed yourself, because whatever happens to you as a result of that also happens to me.’

‘O my dear father,’ the young man replied, 'You have never been anything but compassionate and kind towards me! You have raised me with loving kindness and nurtured me, and preferred me over other people and wealth. I have never needed that compassion more from you, than this very day! I need you to please do one of two things for me!’

‘So explain to me, what are those two things that you need of me,’ said the Shaykh (his father).

‘Either you leave me to my beliefs’, the young man began, ‘in which case I will continue to remain your obedient son who you raised when he was little, and leave the final judgment regarding our affair to Allaah. Or else you should allow me to have a religious debate’ with you, which requires that you use your intellect and grant me assurance that you will not get angry with me.’

‘To be outwardly submissive and agreeable, despite the opposition in our hearts would be iniquitous and unjust’ replied the shaykh. 'How long do you think such an outward agreement could possibly hold up in the face of such inner differences? I don’t like that option at all. I therefore agree to debate with you and give you my word that I will not be angry with you! So go ahead and say what seems right to you.’

The son’s argument is forceful but not disrespectful. He gives his father only two honest possibilities: either the father knew the truth and withheld it, or he did not know it and now needs it too. A modern parallel would be a family business discovering that its old safety standards are outdated. The younger person is not insulting the elders by asking for a better standard; he is asking whether the family will protect pride or protect life.

‘When you consider my position in relation to yours over the past forty years,’ the young man began, ‘could it have been anything other than one of these two possibilities? Either you were a scholar of the truth, and yet you stopped me from acquiring my share in your knowledge. If that is true then you cannot prevent or criticize me for acquiring that knowledge from someone else in an attempt to seek salvation for my soul. Now if that's not the case, and you were ignorant of the truth which I have now learnt, then you are innocent towards me, but at the same time you are even more in need of that stranger than I am. For you should have preceded me in learning from him, just as you have preceded me in birth and age!’

The narrator continued: The heart of the old man was touched by the words of his son, and he knew that there was no way out of both arguments, and his eyes overflowed with tears from what he recognized of the truth.

And he said: ‘O my son, the argument which you have just made against me will also apply to you in the same manner if you continue to remain silent about your cause. So state it to me! If it is correct, then I will accept it from you out of respect for my own soul. And if it is false, then I will dissuade you from it, out of respect and consideration for your soul.’

The narrator continued: They continued their discussion for a while, and went on revealing things to each other for a long time. The ultimate outcome of their discussion was right guidance and divine direction, upholding and safeguarding the religion of Allaah, worshipping according to it, calling people to it, and helping each other in it. Through them, Allaah gave life to a great number of people. The young man sent a message to the scholar telling him the good news about his father’s readiness and asked him to come and visit them. So he came to visit them and became a support for both. And Allaah brought down blessings upon the people of that country. Through them, and by their hands, Allaah’s command was fulfilled, while those who made up lies against Allaah failed completely. The new name of the young man was Saa-lih, and the name of his father was Al-Bakh-ta-ree. They enjoyed great respect among their people because of the favors and assistance they had bestowed, and they formed a great reputation among them by the excellence of their culture, and the knowledge they had acquired. So their reports and their religious position spread throughout the region until it reached a group among the followers of their former beliefs. Al-Bakh-ta-ree had been very generous with them in the past, and they feared that those favors would be taken away from them, now that he had left their sect. So they went out and sought the help of a scholar of their sect named "Abdul Jabbar bin Aboo Maa-lik." Because of his scrupulous piety, his great understanding and his passionate devotion to his religion, he was known among them as Ka‘b Al-Ahbaar.’ He was greatly renowned among them because of his knowledge, his understanding of theology and his discernment regarding dogmatic (official system of principles or tenets concerning faith) opinions. Now when they entered his presence, they said,

‘O Aboo Maa-lik, haven’t you noticed that the young man has left our sect and gone over to a different sect?“

‘What young man?’ he asked.

‘Saa-lih, the son of Al-Bakh-ta-ree,’ they replied.

‘But what (sect) has he gone over to?’ he asked.

‘Yes indeed,’ they began, ‘we want to inform you that a stranger has come to our country, who has an impressive way of speaking, and he calls to a religious school of thought that we have not heard of. The young man got infatuated and left with him without even asking his father’s permission. Then he stayed with the stranger for a while, and after returning to his father, started speaking the same way and began calling people to the same religious beliefs. We just found out that his father has also accepted the young man’s faith and is now helping him with his cause. We are very apprehensive about this and have been avoiding them, for three reasons. Firstly, due to the abundant favors his father has bestowed upon us out of the special brotherhood in religion that we've shared. So now we are afraid of getting separated from him after being so close with him. Secondly, if those people are following something true, then we are being remiss in not following and assisting them in it. And finally, if they have fallen into error and strayed from the true path, then we are being remiss in not preventing them from following that belief, and in not supporting the truth and its people. Now you are the leader in our religion and our foremost learned dignitary; so we have come to you to inform you and to consult with you, and follow your opinion.’

Aboo Maa-lik’s response shows the ethics of disagreement. He does not condemn before listening, and he does not blindly follow before testing. This matches a report from Imaam Ja‘far as-Saadiq, peace be upon him, that the charity most loved by Allaah is reconciliation between quarrelling parties. Here, reconciliation does not mean pretending differences do not exist. It means creating the conditions where truth can be examined without rage, pride, or mob pressure.

Aboo Maa-lik exclaimed, 'Indeed, I am deeply troubled by your words. For we have always known that Al Bakhtari and his son are among people of sound intelligence’ and scrupulous piety. Moreover, we have incurred many obligations toward them, out of their many favors and generosity over the years. Despite your concerns, you are not among those who would desert their sincerity and faithfulness for others—may Allaah protect you from that! ‘Now this news (from the stranger) has come down to them and its argument has convinced them to the point, where they have started following it with pleasure. Therefore it is not possible for us to judge them and convict them of being at fault without first listening to them, since we would be doing them an injustice. Nor can we assume that they are following true guidance, since in that case we would be doing our souls an injustice by blindly imitating them. Therefore we cannot say that they have been deceived, since we would be condemning them without any real knowledge about them—and we know that they are certainly more noble than that!

Instead, we need to look more closely at this news, examining it with an eye of impartiality, in the light of justice, and with the firmness of our intellects. Now it is said that the authority of the one who stays firmly grounded in his affairs, will continue to be protected; but whoever is hasty in his affair might slip up. (man tathabbata fil-umoor lam yazal sultaanuhu mahroozan, wa man ‘ajila fee amrihi lam ya’man zallata qadamih)”. Therefore in most matters a foolish error is something that can be excused and whose damage can be readily compensated, such as an error with food. Its harm is limited and its remedy is easy. On the other hand, a foolish error regarding religion entails great damage and enormous loss, since such a fault can’t be excused, and it can’t be easily exchanged. It is like a foolish error made in prescribing a medicine: the doctor cannot undo its effects, nor can he exonerate himself!

‘It has been said that the most excellent of treasures are the treasures of enlightened hearts and the acquisition of proper modes of behavior (inna afdhal adh-dhakhaa’ir kunooz al-albaab wa iktisaab al-aadaab).” For surely the fruits they provide are praiseworthy! Among their fruits is the pursuit of sound wisdom. Indeed, sound wisdom is the crown of the learned (inna al-hilm taaj al-‘ulamaa), and all their noblest aspirations ascend to the heights of its crowning glories, and their preeminence manifests every where they go, through what they have attained of those noble treasures, and by the familiarity of the subject they are seeking. We too have gathered such treasures of sound intelligence and proper conduct over a long period of time despite our trying circumstances. We have been persevering in that because of its excellence, despite the vicissitudes of fate and the changing nature of our fortunes.’ Thus we cannot throw away all that acquired wisdom . Now we truly need those treasures in this auspicious moment and use it to deal with this affair that has overcome our brothers!

‘So there is no sense in abandoning them, and what they are following, without first interrogating and debating with them about it, relying on the sound intelligence of those who are self-controlled, and the insight of those who are seeking! We cannot fall in doubt over our beliefs, nor assume they have deserted the truth heedlessly, because we know that they are wiser than that. I don't see how they could have chosen to abandon the truth for error, when error is far smaller than truth. Neither is falsehood so eminent that they would abandon the plain truth to follow it! ‘Indeed, if you carefully think over this, their choice of this new belief can only be for three reasons:

(1) Either these people are still following our religion, and this new change is only an additional element drawn out from the traditions of proper conduct, stories, or poetry - because there is an abundance of such sayings (from various people).
(2) Or, the stranger has brought to them something else in addition to their religion which they found very pleasing, and in that case “knowledge is the lost camel of the person of true faith (al-‘ilmu daallatu kulli mu’min),” and an increase in knowledge is certainly better than a lack of it!
(3) Or perhaps their guidance stands completely apart from what they knew before, and it comes from a true source of prophethood, in which case they may have followed him with clear guidance. For indeed no one can be denied of Allaah’s command, and the creatures will never attain the understanding of Allaah’s wise judgments and His will regarding them, no matter how hard they strive. For surely Allaah is involved daily in work."

‘O Aboo Maa-lik,’ they answered, ‘we came to you with our hearts full of wrath against these people, ready to claim them as enemies of faith, and to condemn their opinions so that we might consider licit the shedding of their blood and the seizing of their property.’ But now we find our hearts inclining towards humility based on what you have said in arguing for them and in defending them.’

‘My argument is not for them,’ explained Aboo Maa-lik, ‘but for the truth, so that I may not unknowingly betray it. That is why I upheld the argument for truth on their behalf because the ones who should be arguing for it are not present (at the moment). I was not trying to defend them either. On the contrary, I was only defending us against saying what is false and making assumptions about the unseen, and imagining from what our knowledge does not encompass yet. For in that case we would be acting like the false idols of earlier religious communities, who were pleased by their own ways of sound intelligence and who rejoiced in whatever knowledge they possessed, assuming that they had fully encompassed everything from Allaah's command, His knowledge and His intentions for His creatures. For they said Allaah will never send a messenger after His messenger, and they did not realize that there was a way of proceeding beyond the knowledge they had attained. Hence they concluded against Allaah, that He had ceased His argument against His creatures and stopped maintaining His viceroys on His earth. Thus they limited Allaah’s commands towards His servants to the extent of their own limited understanding. They considered anything beyond that to be sheer error. But they themselves were in error in their opinions and they fell in deeply into it. By their false judgments, they considered licit what Allaah had forbidden, and through their misguided opinions they killed Allaah’s true prophets. So I implore Allaah’s help lest you follow the same passions of a group that went astray. For passion inclines people of intelligence in every direction, so that they get deceived in every regard (fa-inna ahl al-‘uqool qad yasghoo bihim al-hawaa ilaa kulli ma‘naa wa yukhda‘oona ‘an kulli tareeq). But that is not the path of religion: seeking it is most proper, and its excellence is not hidden from anyone!’

‘O Aboo Maa-lik,’ they replied, ‘we don’t mean to disagree with your command and your good advice is not hidden from us. We haven’t been obeying you in our affairs for so long, in order to start contradicting you at this point. Now you’ve just been alluding to “considered opinion” and dropping hints about the “search,” so what is your opinion about accompanying us to these people so that we can interrogate and inquire about their new ways? Then if it is divinely directed we shall follow it too, but if it’s a delusion we shall stay away from it.’

‘Now this is a sign of your doubts in what you follow’ Aboo Maa-lik responded, ‘and a person in doubt doesn’t really know the truth of his own view from the falsehood of someone else's view.’

‘We strongly affirm that what we believe is the truth and we hold everything else to be false, whatever it may be!’ they exclaimed.

‘The search for the essential meaning of religion is not reached like that at all,’ Aboo Maa-lik replied, ‘and you cannot find Allaah’s right guidance by holding everything else to be false.’

‘But then what is this “search” like,’ they asked, ‘if we don’t deny what is false and affirm what is true?’

Aboo Maa-lik replied, ‘If you already really knew the truth and how to receive it, and recognized what is false and how to expose it, then you wouldn’t be numbered among the seekers. Rather you’d be numbered among the true scholars of prophecy and rightly guided, and among those who judge the people of this lower world through divine revelation.’

‘But we are quite content with the truth we already have' they objected, ‘and we have no doubt about it. So we’d like to be excused from the trouble of seeking it.’

‘Aren’t you satisfied with the superiority they hold over you in one respect?’ Aboo Maa-lik responded, ‘Must you make them superior to you in other aspects as well? And aren’t you satisfied with being among the ranks of seekers out of your neediness? Must you ignorantly fall into the ranks of those staying behind?’

‘But how could that happen to us?’ they asked, ‘and in what way are they superior to us?’

‘Their superiority over you is established by their knowing what you know, but having more knowledge of that, which you don’t know,’ Aboo Maa-lik replied. ‘Now this is something that has already happened to these people. And if you don’t seek them out, then they will seek you to it. And if you don’t pay any heed to it, they’ll strive with you in order to convince you of it. So in that case they will become superior to you, not only by their precedence over you, but also in their seeking and their striving before you. But as for your staying behind and avoiding the search, that is total ignorance! It is negligence and the shortcoming of a scholar to not share his knowledge, and it is a negligence for the ignorant to not seek and learn.’

‘O Aboo Maa-lik! your argument leaves us no way out, and everything you've said is correct,’ they acknowledged. ‘So open a way for us with your opinion that we can follow in action.’

‘Pride in one’s own opinion is blindness and neglecting to seek Allaah, is a sin!’ Aboo Maa-lik responded. ‘But there is no fault for those who continue searching. However the seeker does need to know the proper ways of searching in order to find the truth with a real understanding of it. For someone who seeks the truth without really knowing the proper way to look for it will be quick in denying it, since the distinctive signs of falsehood manifest in hypocritical appearances and in conformity to accepted opinions. But the clear signs of truth only manifests themselves through tests and afflictions, and through opposition of passions. Only those with a sound heart can renounce their passions and can persevere despite the onset of tests and afflictions.’

‘Now you’ve already opened up a proper gateway for us by alluding to real understanding of the search,’ they replied, ‘So explain to us what that understanding is, and what is a sound heart?'

‘As for the true understanding of the search,’ Aboo Maa-lik began, ‘that’s when you know that you are in need of what you're searching. For the person who is in need seeks what they lack with the humility that comes from their neediness of what they’re seeking. And a sound heart does not insist on denying what occurs to it, no matter what it may be, until the truth reveals itself and manifests its superiority, and until falsehood unveils its defects. At that point the person is able to distinguish what is false with their intellect and to receive the truth in the way that it should be received.’

‘But we have been told’ they objected, ‘that whoever does not confirm the truth and expose falsehood is acting in ignorance, because that means they cannot recognize the truth so that they can follow it, nor can they identify falsehood so that they can avoid it.’

‘Indeed, what you've heard is accurate!’ said Aboo Maa-lik. ‘But according to you, hasn’t that person already moved out of the ranks of those who deny the truth, and entered the ranks of those who are ignorant?’

‘Yes,’ they admitted, ‘In our opinion, such a person is ignorant and not actually denying the truth.’

‘But surely,’ Aboo Maa-lik continued, ‘the ignorant (who at least recognizes that he doesn’t know the truth) is more actively seeking the truth hoping to find it. So if he seeks that knowledge out of his own accord, then that’s fine; and if he doesn’t do so, then the one who knows, will seek him out. For ignorance is the original state of creatures, so there’s no fault in those who seek knowledge, being fully aware of their ignorance. But denial of the truth is “the worst trade of all”, because the person who denies it doesn’t gain any right guidance by his denial, nor does he ever reach any assurance about that which he was denying.’

‘That is true,’ they concurred, ‘for you have made us realize that denying the truth was wrong. So we will never deny anything again until we know it. Now you were helping us understand about the gateway of seeking truth with humility, which comes from one’s need for that which he's searching. So then does that humility and modesty come before, or after its truthfulness has become clear?’

‘No,’ said Aboo Maa-lik. ‘Humility and modesty comes before the truth becomes clear, because only by that humility can one actually witness the manifestation of truth’

‘But what if despite our humility what was made clear to us was in error?', they asked. 'What good would that humility be then? Wouldn’t it have been wasted?’

‘No,’ said Aboo Maa-lik, ‘on the contrary, that humility is for Allaah, and we must be submissive to His Command. You receive the reward for that humility, even if the person you displayed it to does not deserve it! That is because the seeker carried the requirements of his search, even when the one who was sought did not fulfill his obligations. He is like the beggar who comes to a king seeking his favors. All he can do is be humble, beseech himself, implore the king and complain of his neediness. Now if the king gives him something, then he carried out his obligation of being bountiful, and therefore he gets the reward for what he gave to the beggar. But if the king does not give the beggar anything, still the beggar at least carried out his obligation of seeking, by politely asking, and in doing so brought out the avarice of the one who turned him down. The one who is seeking knowledge should be just like that. It is obligatory for him to be modest and humble, and to persevere in the face of trials and adversity until he carries out what is required by that search. Then the course of time will bring to him whatever it brings for him.’

‘That is plain and illuminating truth!’ they exclaimed. ‘So will you give us permission to go to those people, and act in accordance with the duty you have pointed on us? For we will not disobey whatever you command.’

‘I would not suggest anything to you,’ replied Aboo Maa-lik, ‘that I have spared myself from doing! Indeed if I did so, I fear I would do you wrong and be the one preventing you from the path of Allaah. I would also not suggest that you should stay behind while I go to them alone, taking precedence over you in seeking the good, out of respect for my own soul and giving it preference over every other soul. But instead, let us all go together, and out of respect and gratitude for your having singled me out especially for this good, I will assist!’

‘You sound as though you’ve been waiting for this chance even before this day,’ they remarked.

‘Well not exactly for this particular occasion,’ he replied, ‘but I have long been waiting for an opening to come to me from Allaah. I have been looking in every direction with hope, listening attentively with my spiritual ears to every speaker, seeking an increase in my knowledge from his words, whether they are confirming the truth or revealing falsehood.’

‘The increase in knowledge that comes from words that confirm the truth is obvious,’ they answered, ‘But why are you hoping for such an increase from words that reveal falsehood?’

He replied: ‘The addition of truth to truth is a light, but the awareness of falsehood that comes from words revealing falsehood is an increase in certainty (ziyaadat al-haqq ilal-haqq noor, wa ma‘rifat al-baatil minal-mubtil ziyaadah fil-yaqeen)’. So the more our certainty increases, the greater the light in our hearts grow! And none shall be saved by Allaah but the people of certainty! ‘Now I am getting up to visit those people. So if you have all resolved to get up and go with me, then consider this. Whoever knows of any sin in himself, let him turn to Allaah in repentance; and whoever has any debts, let him repay those to the people he owes them to; And let us purify ourselves by ablution (wa-ghtasiloo bit-tahaarah ‘an ad-danas) ; and adorn ourselves in the purest robes of determination (wa-lbasoo lit-talab anqaa jalaabeeb al-hazm) and prepare for what we are seeking with the intention being truly in need (wa-ahdiroo lil-matloob niyyat al-muhtaaj). For I am doing that now, since surely our efforts are for Allaah. And if He knows that we are truly sincere, He will help us succeed in what we deserve. But if He knows of any mockery and scorn for this religious community, He will turn us away from His signs in humiliation, in order to protect His close friend from our mockery.’

‘Yes' they replied, 'we'll do what you have ordered in three days.’

‘No,’ he said.

‘In two days?’ they asked.

‘No,’ he responded, ‘today is the only day for us! So if you all are doing that, then fine; otherwise I will go alone!’

‘Then we are turning to Allaah in repentance from every sin,’ they responded, ‘and we will pay off all of our debts and do what you have ordered us to do. And there is no power except through Allaah.’

# Chapter 6 — The Ultimate Truth

The narrator continued: They completed all those tasks on the very same day, and they all went out together until they reached Al-Bakh-ta-ree’s residence. Then when they had greeted him and taken their places,

Aboo Maa-lik said to Al-Bakh-ta-ree: ‘O father of Saa-lih, how is your son, and where is he?’

‘Today Saa-lih is my father,’ Al-Bakh-ta-ree replied, ‘and I am Saa-lih’s son, and Saa-lih is with his Lord.’

Aboo Maa-lik, assuming that Saa-lih had died, responded: ‘(Innaa lil-laahi wa innaa ilayhi raaji‘oon) (Indeed we belong to Allaah, and indeed to Him we will return). Has Saa-lih died?’

‘No,’ replied Al-Bakh-ta-ree, ‘Saa-lih is not dead, nor will he ever die; indeed he is abiding with Allaah for all eternity!’

‘I find three things in your statement whose meanings have been turned upside down!’ exclaimed Aboo Maa-lik. ‘The son has become the father; the Lord has come to exist on earth with a servant who is receiving grace from Him; and one who was created to die, is abiding forever! Woe unto those who seek their own ends through perplexing speech! Indeed there is something very strange behind those words!’

‘O Aboo Maa-lik,’ responded Al-Bakh-ta-ree, ‘seek what you need, and be determined in your questions, for you are at the onset of your test.’

‘You are right,’ replied Aboo Maa-lik, ‘for the first stage of the truth is testing. And what I need, is to meet the righteous servant.’

‘There is no way to do that,’ replied Al-Bakh-ta-ree. ‘But perhaps you mean my son Saa-lih?'

‘Yes,’ he said.

‘That is only with his permission,’ he replied.

The narrator continued: Then the Shaykh got up and left, and informed his son Saa-lih about Aboo Maa-lik and his companion's visit. Saa-lih was very happy to hear the news because he knew they were of excellent intellects.

Then with raised hands he prayed: ‘O my Allaah, open up for Your servants the ears of their hearts and guide them with Your loving kindness to the signs of what they seek. And open their breasts with the light of Your veiled light.'

The narrator continued: Then he passed his hand over his face and made the room ready for them and he asked his father to bring them in. After they all came in and greeted him and were seated, Saa-lih had Aboo Maa-lik come closer to him, drew him near and...

He said: ‘O Aboo Maa-lik, you have visited us most generously, and out of your nobility you have preceded us in good, for it is I who should have come to visit you, out of respect for your friendship and out of all the obligations I have accumulated towards you in the past.’

‘But when has your generosity ever stopped, O source of good?’ Aboo Maa-lik replied. ‘You were lenient and understanding toward us even when you were little, and you have always been generous towards us once you grew up. And now you are calling us toward good and have become a warner of Allaah’s accustomed ways. So may your root be nourished, may your branches grow high and may your actions be purified, and congratulations to you on what you are doing!’

‘Now did you come here to accuse me, O glory of the Yearned” Saa-lih asked, ‘or to follow me blindly? And what has become of your perfect intellect and that noble practical intelligence which we are all familiar with?’

‘Your capabilities and rank are far above any accusations,’ Aboo Maa-lik replied, ‘and religion is far above blind obedience.’

‘Then how is it,’ Saa-lih asked him, ‘that you’ve termed me a “warner,” when the warner is a prophet and the prophet is a proof between Allaah and His creatures, both for them and against them? And how can someone be a proof for you who has not yet established the divine argument against you?’

‘You are right,’ replied Aboo Maa-lik. ‘That is truly what is required for someone to be a divine proof! Now we are not acting based on rash words. No. we have come to you with that which is better. And I thought that direct sincerity was more appropriate with someone who has established himself as a guardian for religion and a firm proponent for the truth; who has opened his doors to those who are seeking the truth. Now that we have recognized the excellence of the search for truth, we have sought you out and we humbly ask you regarding what we’re seeking. So do be attentive to our needs!’

‘O Aboo Maa-lik,’ Saa-lih responded, ‘if you have recognized someone’s excellence, isn’t it also obligatory for you to acknowledge what is due to them?’

‘Yes,’ he answered, ‘that is obligatory.’

‘So when you recognized the excellence of the search for truth,’ he continued, ‘then shouldn't you also acknowledge its due?’

‘And what is its due?’ he asked.

‘To understand the ways in which to carry out your search,’ Saa-lih explained to him.’

‘Then what are the different ways of searching,’ asked Aboo Maa-lik, ‘and how many are there?’

‘There are three ways of seeking,’ Saa-lih explained to him. (1) There is the seeker who already knows; (2) the seeker who is beginning to know; (3) and the seeker who desires to know.’ So which of these three types are you?’

‘I need to learn all three ways,’ said Aboo Maa-lik, ‘so how can I choose when I don’t really know?’

Saa-lih asked: ‘But then how did you claim to be a seeker when you don’t even know the ways of seeking?’

‘I don’t really know them,’ Aboo Maa-lik admitted, ‘so make them clear to me.’

Saa-lih began: ‘Possessions are the treasures of the people of this lower world, and spiritual knowledge is the treasure of the people of the other world.’ Now the search for possessions takes place in three ways: (1) there is the seeker who searches for more of what he already has; (2) the seeker who searches until a determined limit; (3) and the seeker who owns nothing and is searching to obtain charity. Likewise there are three ways to seek for religion and the treasures of the other world. (1) There is the seeker who fully understands! He is the sanctified scholar who is seeking those that are dead (ignorant) in order to bring them back to life (through his knowledge). (2) Then there is the seeker who is a student! He has already understood some levels of knowledge and is now seeking the final stage of those levels. (3) And finally, there is the seeker who is just beginning to understand: That is the ignorant, who at the very least has recognized his own ignorance and he really knows nothing more than the awareness of his need. So he seeks out scholars in order to learn. Those are the three ways to seek the truth and the descriptions of those who are seeking.’

‘Yes', replied Aboo Maa-lik, ‘Indeed those are the ways of seeking both religion and this lower world. Now I am the seeker who is just beginning to understand, the one who doesn’t know anything except the fact that he is in need (of knowledge). So please teach me!’

‘Do you just “know” that,’ he asked, ‘or do you really "understand"?

‘What’s the difference between them?’ asked Aboo Maa-lik.

Saa-lih explained: 'Knowledge is a report, while real understanding is immediate vision.’

‘Then this is also something that I need to learn’ Aboo Maa-lik admitted.

‘As for the “report”,’ Saa-lih continued, ‘that is the case of an ignorant person who doesn’t know that he is ignorant, until he encounters a learned person. Then the scholar informs him that he is ignorant, so he realizes that he is in need of that knowledge by words of that scholar. That knowledge of his ignorance is based on a report. ‘As for truly understanding one’s ignorance and need, that is when a man knows with his own intellect that he has no capability of finding out the answer he seeks by himself. Such a person’s heart becomes constricted by his ignorance, and his only hope for freedom is through learning whatever he is ignorant of. Such a person is made aware of his ignorance by his own intellect (and not by some external source). Ignorance is a need, which is constricting, and that constriction, in itself is an immense intense need, that forces people to seek release; and that release comes from the openness of knowledge, since knowledge is a wide-open expanse. So that is what makes you really understand that you are in need!’

‘I find everything that you have described in my own soul!’ admitted Aboo Maa-lik. ‘Indeed I have truly understood that I was in need, and that my neediness was intense. So cure my neediness with your generosity!

‘It seems to me,’ Saa-lih replied, ‘that you are rushing ahead in your search, before finding any solid ground.’

‘My neediness from my heart's constriction forced me to hurry’ admitted Aboo Maa-lik. ‘I recognized the excellence of that open expanse, so I came seeking for it.’

‘But if your neediness forced you to hurry, and you did recognize the excellence of that open expanse and sought it,’ Saa-lih inquired, ‘then how did you identify what you were seeking?’

‘I became sure of your excellence, so I came straight to you!’ Aboo Maa-lik exclaimed. ‘You hinted the real meaning of religion, so I sought you out.’

Saa-lih asked: ‘But just how did you know for sure that I, and not you, really understood that open expanse of true knowledge? And how did you know that I was the one you were searching for? For no true scholar of prophecy has guided you to me, so that you may know for sure that I am the one you seek. Nor have you seen in me the confirming signs of such divine inspiration so that you could truly identify me! Don’t you see, that in your search, what you are really aspiring for is the effortlessness of blind imitation? And by doing so, you are performing an injustice to wisdom and to yourself, just like the merchant who measures from the bottom of the barrel! If he doesn’t fill up his measure, he does injustice to his customer; and if he overfills it, he does injustice to himself. The same is true of someone who simply accepts knowledge on the basis of blind imitation. If he puts it into practice, the person who granted him that knowledge won’t receive any gratitude (because the knowledge did not benefit); and if he abandons it, he will get accused of infidelity. But in my opinion you are more intelligent than that.’

‘I am certainly aware of that,’ admitted Aboo Maa-lik, ‘but I wanted it to be the truth that would confirm itself, so that I could absolve you of having to confirm it for me. Because disputation gives rise to disagreement that leads to discord and controversy, and that gives rise to malice and rancor, as each antagonist seeks to overcome his opponent. But once one has perceived the truth, who would persist in defending falsehood?’

Saa-lih exclaimed: ‘How accurately you have spoken since the truth confirms itself by its own excellence and nobility. Whereas disputes, once they have proliferated, will surely distract the hearts from what is more important than them. Yet there’s no getting around what the learned men have set down as a simple rule, when they said that every seed for which the ground has not been properly prepared beforehand, will not grow and flourish,’ and every knowledge for which the hearts of the students have not first been purified by proper argument will not flourish.’

‘Then mention as many arguments as you wish,’ replied Aboo Maa-lik. ‘For words of wisdom are like jewels! If you turn it around and scrutinize it, its light sparkles and shimmers, but if you leave it alone, its value is still not diminished.’

‘You have spoken truly,’ Saa-lih answered, ‘but that is only if there isn’t any confusion between the jewel and a forged piece of glass! The case of the jewel is quite obvious. But as for when the false idols of this lower world multiply their pretensions and disputes about religion, and deceive people by mixing it with other beliefs, and counterfeit and distort it for those who are seeking the truth, then indeed there is an obligation on the seeker to have the jewel tested, in order to reveal its true value and expose the counterfeit piece of glass and its dealers.’

‘You have spoken justly and have upheld what is required by the truth,’ said Aboo Maa-lik, ‘and as for the deceptions of such people, we are well aware of them and their shortcomings. That is why we have rejected such deceptions and have clung firmly to our faith and confirmed that our religion is indeed a genuine jewel. We have supported this belief, and you and your father have agreed with us on this subject, and together we have held tightly to the cord of this belief. So if you have now found a meaning which is a more precious jewel than mine, because by my life! some jewels are more precious than others, then you must show us the value of your jewel through a test by which its real worth can be determined. As for our jewel, we already recognize its value, just as you do. So say what seems best to you about it.’

Saa-lih replied:
The jewel analogy is about verification. In modern terms, it is the difference between a fake certificate and a credential verified by the issuing authority. Something may look impressive, use the right language, and be accepted by a crowd, but the question is whether those qualified to recognize the truth accept it. This is why Ali, peace be upon him, is reported to have said that knowledge guards you while you must guard wealth. True knowledge protects the seeker from counterfeit certainty.

‘As for the “jewel” you and your companions possess, we all held it dear, until we presented it to the jewelers, and those experts in gems. But they told us that it was a counterfeit and they would not even recognize it as a precious stone! Instead they said “This is a forgery made of glass!” and they showed us its flaws, so that we too could no longer consider it a jewel. For a jewel is only held in high esteem by its rarity and preciousness, and by the opinions that people of discerning insight hold about its value and its competition. As a result its price becomes lofty. But when the jewelers reject it, and deem it a forgery, then who else will acknowledge it as a gem. Who would purchase or sell it after that except the deluded? Similarly, spiritual knowledge can’t be accepted as genuine until it has been presented to those who know with inspired knowledge from heaven. Only if they accept it, then it is genuine knowledge. But if they reject it, then it is merely empty talk which cannot be accepted as wisdom, nor will it be accepted by the wise.’

Aboo Maa-lik is asking the central question: after prophecy, where does authoritative interpretation live? This is why the chapter turns repeatedly to the idea of a continuous rope from heaven to earth. Ismaili tradition treats guidance not as an archived document alone, but as a living chain of interpretation through the Imaam and the rightful hierarchy of knowledge.

Aboo Maa-lik asked, 'And who are these people who have this divine “knowledge from heaven,” after the prophets and the messengers? For I myself don’t know a single one of them in our time, nor do the ordinary people have any acquaintance of such a person, since the divine revelation of the Book has ended, and all religious communities are only worshipping according to transmitted reports. So what do you have to say about that?'

Saa-lih said: ‘To begin with, every religion in which worship is based solely on transmitted reports shouldn’t be called a religion at all, since religion is "one", purely upholding what is due to Allaah, without any differences in it. While transmitted reports, as you already know, all differ from and deny each other. But Allaah’s religion is clearer and more unique than that!'

‘But even if they all differ in their process of transmission,’ Aboo Maa-lik continued, ‘still they all refer back to the same books. Therefore they accept whatever agrees with those Books, and they deny whatever differs from them.’

‘Then for what is only found in general terms in the books,’ Saa-lih answered, ‘who is going to explain its particular applications for them? And as for what is detailed in it, who is going to explain its actual connection to them? And who among them should they trust to learn what Allaah has permitted and forbidden? For we have heard the story when an upset man approached you and said: "O Aboo Maa-lik, I formally swore to divorce my wife, and then later I broke my oath!" And you replied: "Some people say that she is divorced from you and that you cannot remarry her until she has first married another man. Other people say that she is still your wife and not forbidden to you by a single oath of divorce". Then when the person asked: “But which of those two opinions should I follow?” you said: “Act according to whichever of the two you like.” So he left you, not knowing whether he would be doing something that was religiously permitted or forbidden. Now is that how wise judgment should be in Allaah’s religion? Or is that really what Allaah has permitted and forbidden?’

'By my life!’ replied Aboo Maa-lik, 'certainly Sharee-ah has become corrupted and ineffectual, and we are only clinging to the name of religion! So what is the state of religion in your opinion?’

'But what is religion, O Aboo Maa-lik?’ Saa-lih asked him.

'Commands and prohibitions regarding what is permitted and what is forbidden, and the Prophetic traditions and the religious obligations,’ he replied.

‘If so, then what is "Sharee-ah" in your opinion?’ Saa-lih asked him.

‘I don’t know of anything else other than what I just described to you,’ answered Aboo Maa-lik.

‘So then have you placed religion and the Sharee-ah at the same level?’ Saa-lih asked.

'Yes', he replied.

‘So then you're saying, "religion" has become corrupted and ineffectual because of the corruption in "Sharee-ah"? Saa-lih asked. ‘If so, then what remains with you?’

The narrator continued: Then Aboo Maa-lik stopped and bowed his head, unable to reply for a while, thinking about the state of his religion. After that he lifted up his head,

and he said: ‘I don’t know if anything remains with me now. But what is religion for you?’

Here Saa-lih separates religion from mere inherited procedure. Sharee-ah is the visible order of practice, but religion is the living rope that gives those practices their direction and life. A modern comparison would be the difference between a company’s checklists and its mission. Checklists matter, but they are not the mission itself. They protect the mission only when they remain connected to it.

‘The way I see it,’ Saa-lih answered, ‘religion is the veil Allaah has strengthened against the corruptors, and whose gates have been opened up to those who are seeking it (wa futihat abwaabuhu lit-taalibeen). So the person who attains it, is ennobled with precedence, and the one who truly seeks it joins with the good. Religious actions are ennobled with its excellence (fa sharufatil-a‘maalu bifadlih), and the revealed paths are shining bright with its justice (wa zaharatish-sharaa’i‘u bi‘adlih). For it is a rope extending from heaven to the earth, continuous and without any breakage (wa huwa sababun minas-samaa’i ilal-ard, mawsoolun ghayru mafsool); the firm handhold of those who cling to Allaah and the rope of those who hold it firmly; the ark of the divine presence; the ship of salvation, and the light of life. Everything it contains is noble, its knowledge is well-protected, and its jewels have their distinctive signs so that their excellence is not hidden from anyone who looks closely at them.’

Aboo Maa-lik replied: ‘If Allaah does have a religion by which He is to be worshipped, and if there is a day for his rewards and punishments, then indeed this would be its essential meaning! Now its description has become clear, but what about the reality of what you've described?’

‘What is good can only come from good (laa yakoonu at-tayyibu illaa min tayyib), O Aboo Maa-lik!’ Saa-lih answered. 'So put your light in the niche of your intellect and understand what is required of you! For the rights of what has been determined by Allaah, are now disputing with you, and the bulky robes of self-deception have fallen off you, and you have taken upon yourself a responsibility which only you can uphold. You have also shouldered the affairs of these companions who have accompanied you in good; since they listen to what you say, follow your guidance and agree with your opinions. Therefore, it is obligatory for you to protect yourself, and them from entering a fire, from which none of you have refuge! So seek the path of salvation, because you, among all your companions, have had the greatest share of Allaah’s blessings, and therefore you should be the most grateful to Him. Indeed I know how much you desire the good and hope for its reward! And as for confirming that which I am calling you to, I am obligated to confirm it for you in whatever way you prefer.'

‘Everything you have said is correct and firmly established,’ Aboo Maa-lik began. ‘And as for the transmitted reports of the common people,’ religion cannot be appropriated by relying on them, and neither are they appropriate for it. So the only thing that remains for us is to learn about what you are calling us to.’

Saa-lih answered: ‘I am calling you to Allaah’s justice and to the assertion of His unity.”

‘Then what you were calling us to, is just wonderful,’ replied Aboo Maa-lik, ‘because we already say exactly that, and we know its superiority over all the other (competing theological) doctrines!’

‘But I do not say it in the way you imagine it, O Aboo Maa-lik!’ Saa-lih answered. 'The school of thought that you subscribe to describes Allaah’s justice without any clear understanding of who is actually being described. And they strayed regarding His Names, without any real understanding of His unity. What I'm calling you to is the real understanding of Allaah’s justice and the distinctive traits of His unity. With your permission may I explain to you what I mean, by beginning with the deception of your original doctrine?’

‘If the doctrine of the people of justice is erroneous, and their declaration of Allaah’s unity has gone astray,’ declared Aboo Maa-lik, ‘then there is no firm truth on earth! Unless there is another meaning, different from all the existing doctrines. So show us just how our assertion of Allaah’s justice and unity are erroneous.’

‘The assertion of Allaah’s justice and His unity are the noblest of all doctrines (al-‘adl wat-tawheed min ashraf al-maqaalaat),' Saa-lih answered. 'Allaah’s unity is far exalted from error, since it has no contrary measure; and His justice cannot be impaired since it is a firm decree established between Allaah and His creatures. But all existing doctrines are erroneous because of the way they undermine the assertion of His justice and other traits. They are empty pretensions that cannot ground any doctrine. For they do not encompass any knowledge of what they assert, such that they could return to Allaah’s command. So take it upon yourself to defend whichever of those two points you wish, so that I can put an end to it for you using Allaah’s argument.’

‘I shall never uphold against you a doctrine which I myself don’t assert,’ Aboo Maa-lik began, ‘nor has the proponent (of some other doctrine) asked me to do so and appointed me as the defender of his doctrine. For I am not the protector or supporter of other doctrines, and I will only defend my own doctrine against you. Now I do maintain that Allaah is One, there is nothing like Him; and that He is just in His judgment, not imposing on His servants what they cannot bear. Thus He has commanded them to do certain things and He has forbidden them from others, but He does not command by force, nor does He forbid by compulsion. Rather, His command is by giving them the choice of the better, and His prohibition is by way of warning of the consequences of disobedience. Therefore He is not obeyed involuntarily, nor is He disobeyed against the will of the disobedient. That is the gist of my meaning and what I maintain.’

‘Didn’t I just tell you that this doctrine was among the most noble of all doctrines?’ Saa-lih answered. 'But those are only names and descriptions which are of no benefit without the existence of their essential realities. Your approach is like saying “fire is hot.” You’ve certainly spoken the truth, but that truth is of no benefit to you because your saying “fire is hot” is only a noun and an adjective. Simply mentioning them is of no benefit, since you can’t cook food with those words, or brighten darkness without the essential reality of the existence of fire. For if mere nouns and adjectives could take the place of realities, then your tongue would start burning at the mere mention of fire and your hunger would be satiated at the mention of food!’

‘You have spoken truly in what you’ve just said about these things,’ Aboo Maa-lik replied. 'Names and attributes are only of any benefit through the actual existence of their essential realities. So show me how my assertion of Allaah’s unity and justice is only in names and attributes, and how I can reach the reality of the one Who is named and described.’

Saa-lih replied: 'You’ve said that Allaah is one, which is written with four letters. Of those two letters are connected and two are separate. So which of those letters is "one", or do you believe that together they are one? And "Allaah " is also written with four letters: so which of those letters is “Allaah,” or are they together “Allaah,” or are they signs pointing to Allaah—or do you believe something else entirely?’

The narrator continued: Then Aboo Maa-lik hung his head for a while,

and then he said "I don't see any way out of this except to say that they are signs pointing to Allaah."

‘But then which of those letters points to Allaah?’ Saa-lih asked. "Or with what do you point to Allaah? Do you point to Him with those letters separately, or by joining them together? Or by separating some of them, and joining others, as when you say “Allaah”? For then you’ve separated the initial ‘alif’ by itself and joined the next three letters together. So which of these point to Allaah? The one by itself, or the others that are joined together?’

‘I don’t know what to say to you about that,’ Aboo Maa-lik replied. ‘Help us find some other way to approach this matter.’

Saa-lih replied: ‘O Aboo Maa-lik! Whoever speaks (about this matter) in this way, has already closed the doors to a real understanding of Allaah’s unity.’

‘Yes,’ he said.

‘Now I’m going to ask you about your understanding of Allaah,’ Saa-lih continued. ‘Where did that come from, and from whom did you take it? From an adjective that pointed you toward the reality so described?’ (‘an sifatin dallatka ‘alaa mawsoof?)

‘No,’ he answered.

‘Then from a name that pointed you toward (the Reality so) named (fa-‘an ismin dallaka ‘alaa musamman) ?’ he continued.

‘Yes,’ he replied.

‘And what is that name which pointed you (toward Him)?’ he asked.

‘Allaah,’ he replied.

‘Is “Allaah” a name or something named?’ Saa-lih inquired.

‘Something named,’ he replied.

‘Then what is its name?’ he asked.

‘Allaah,’ he answered.

‘“Allaah” is a name for Allaah?’ asked Saa-lih.

‘Yes,’ he said.

‘Then what is the difference between the name and the named?’ asked Saa-lih. ‘And which of the two do you worship—or do you worship them both?’

The narrator continued: Then Aboo Maa-lik was completely bewildered for a while, reflecting how he should answer.

Then he said: ‘I don’t have any other way of answering this except that when I looked at everything that was made, they all had a maker. Based on that I concluded that this immense universe also had a Creator, Who does not resemble anything among His creations, just as the makers (of things) do not resemble what they produce.’

‘O Aboo Maa-lik,’ Saa-lih answered, "You have now taken us away from the original meaning of your assertion! ‘But what do all your legal scholars say about that?’ Saa-lih asked him.

‘They all pray for Allaah’s mercy for the murdered one,’ said Aboo Maa-lik, ‘but they don’t see the sin of the murderer!’

‘Now, as for the person who willingly obeys the murderer,’ Saa-lih asked him, ‘isn’t he his accomplice in the blood of the murdered victim?’

‘By Allaah, of course he is!’ exclaimed Aboo Maa-lik. ‘For he is the murderer’s associate, rather even worse than him.’

‘His association with the murderer is obvious, because of his helping him. But how does he become worse than him?’ Saa-lih asked him.

‘Were it not for the opinions passed down and circulated by the legal scholars, Aboo Maa-lik responded, ‘the oppressors could not have maintained their domination over the foolish ones.’

‘You have spoken truly, O Aboo Maa-lik,’ Saa-lih said to him. ‘Indeed would you yourself be satisfied to have their kingdom as your home, their land as your sanctuary, their armies as your neighbors, and their guardians as your best friends? Yet you are a witness for them, judging in their favor with your “knowledge” and affirming that they fulfill your needs. For you have prayed for them in asking them for money, and you have thanked them for their help with your needs. And you have dressed their wrongdoing with the crown of your ‘justice,” so they filled with pride. And you adorned their proud boasting with the outer garments of your “revealed path,” so they went to even greater extremes. You have illuminated their falsehood with indifference to your truth. And the despised ones were deceived by your actions and those who resemble you among the so-called “scholars.” For they said: “We have a model to imitate in them, since they are the legal scholars of the community.” So they have gone astray by all you scholars, just as you all went astray due to the oppressors. Thus you imagined that the person responsible for this atrocity was someone other than yourselves, since you forgot Allaah’s saying—may He be praised:

(Wa la tarkanoo ila al-latheena thalamoo fatamassakumu an-nar)
“And do not incline toward those who do wrong, lest you be touched by the Fire, and you would not have other than Allaah any protectors”

So you all have cut off what Allaah ordered to be connected, while you were all looking on! And you have proclaimed the outer appearances of “justice” while you strayed far away from its path. You turned away from his saying:

(Ya ayyuha al-latheena amanoo atee'oo Allaha wa atee'oo ar-rasoola wa olee al-amri minkum)
“O you who have believed, obey Allaah and obey the Messenger and those in authority among you.”

You claimed that these wrong doers were the “possessors of authority over you,” even though they openly rejected Allaah. In doing so you forgot Allaah’s saying—may He be exalted:

(Ya ayyuha al-latheena amanoo qatiloo al-latheena yaloonakum mina al-kuffar)
“O you who have believed, fight those adjacent to you of the disbelievers”

Thus you all willfully disobeyed the person you had been commanded to obey, and you willingly obeyed those whom you had been ordered to struggle against. And all the while, you assumed that you were those who are performing good deeds! ‘So the self-delusion of error carried you all so far in the end, and your own opinions overpowered you to such an extent that you all said that Allaah will never send a messenger or a warner in your time! Is it that as a result of this imagined “goodness” of your actions, you felt you had no need for the friends of Allaah to carry out His argument? Or was it the ugliness and evil of your actions, such that you gave up all hope for forgiveness from Allaah through repentance? Or is it part of your “justice” that you should pass judgment against Allaah, and that you should be satisfied and pleased with the sort of systematic persecution and rejection of your Messenger that you would never be satisfied with for yourselves? For would any one of you be satisfied to see his own child killed or expelled from their home and possessions without any right?’

‘None of us would be satisfied with that!’ Aboo Maa-lik exclaimed. ‘If any one of us had his child killed or was expelled from his property, he would become outraged, and his people would be outraged for him, so they would not be satisfied until he was satisfied.’

‘Surely Allaah and His Messenger are more deserving that you all should be outraged and be angry for him,’ Saa-lih answered him, ‘when His viceroy (khalifa) was killed and His friend (wali) was removed from the earth?’

‘Indeed, it is hard for us that they should kill Allaah’s friend among us or remove him from his earth,’ said Aboo Maa-lik. ‘But (His friends) have decided to hide themselves from us, while the oppressors are unjustly dominating us.’

‘If you were all to help the friends of Allaah, then they would win out,’ Saa-lih answered him. ‘And if you were to abandon His enemies, then they would not be victorious. But (in reality), as far as your relation to the friends of Allaah, you can all be judged to belong to one of these three levels. There is the relentless murderer, who never tires of hunting them down and whose blood thirst is not quenched even when he wins out. And there is their inveterate enemy, who bites his nails in rage, who will go to any length to incite enmity against them, and who never forgets his hatred of them, even if they overlook (him). Finally, there are those who do dislike those enemies’ actions, who are sympathetic to the friends of Allaah but are really abandoning them, who have no spiritual insight in regard to faith, and who cannot be relied upon to protect the secret of the imams. So it is because of that that the friends of Allaah have concealed themselves from you, out of precaution against you and this immense number of people who have taken their passions as their lord, who fight like dogs over the stuff of this lower world, and who conspire together about killing the prophets. Since such people are unaware of the wrongfulness of their actions, they do not trouble their souls with the wrongfulness of their evil-doing and they do not humble themselves in repentance for what they have done. Indeed they go so far as to attribute the wrongfulness of their actions to Allaah and to ascribe them to Him. So they say that Allaah will never send a prophet after their prophet, nor any warner. Thus they sought to legitimize the slaughtering of the friends of Allaah without any right. And in so doing they did wrong with their actions, they turned away from (the truth) in their speech, and they transgressed by calling (them) liars.’

‘Yes,’ said Aboo Maa-lik. That is the description of this community of ours, and that is what they say. So how can we be saved from that, and where is the refuge from Allaah’s punishment?’

‘With Allaah!’ Saa-lih answered him. ‘Seek protection with Him and He will give you refuge. Turn to Him in repentance and He will accept that repentance from you. And ask Him for the path of His friends and He will rescue you through your own right action from the deeds of those who do injustice. The same is true for all these other people: if they were to turn to Allaah, He would accept their repentance. And Allaah would not punish them, for Allaah does not do a dust-speck’s weight of wrong, even if they themselves have done wrong and denied Allaah’s friends. For He does not hasten to punish them, even when they have hastened to disobey (Him).’

‘Then I am seeking protection from Allaah’s punishment,’ Aboo Maa-lik replied, ‘through repentance and turning to Him. And I am searching for the connection to what pleases Him. So be generous to the one who comes to you seeking (help), and help to guide rightly the one who is seeking your guidance—may Allaah reward you for that!’

‘If you do that,’ Saa-lih answered him, ‘you will find that Allaah, in His forgiveness, is closer to you than your repentance and returning to Him, for surely He loves those who turn to Him in repentance. And you will find me full of concern for you in regard to guiding you.’

The narrator continued: Then Saa-lih's eyes filled with tears at the mention of repentance, so he drew his speaking to an end. Since he was in his own rooms, he asked them to go away to his father’s house. Then he went in to see his (spiritual) father, the scholar who had called him, and he consulted with him about the matter of Aboo Maa-lik and his companions.

Then the scholar said: ‘You know your companions better (than I do), so if you recognize some good in them, then guide them rightly as you were guided. But if you are apprehensive about them, then don’t let your desire for them lead you to approach them while putting yourself at risk. And test them by ignoring them, but not harshly. Order your father to treat them kindly and to be respectful toward them for a while. For the person who is truly seeking the good will not remain hidden. Surely Allaah will not leave you behind and He will open up for you, from the light of His providential arrangement of things, that through which He manages the affair of His creatures. And He will open up for you from the gateways of right guidance what will show you the (appropriate) actions of those who are rightly guided.’

The narrator continued: Aboo Maa-lik and his companions continued to go through their different levels of testing, until their affair was complete and they recognized their right guidance. And it was Allaah’s friend among them who took care of their guidance, and they thanked Allaah for that. Then they returned to their people warning them, so that through them Allaah guided a great many of His servants to His religion.

Nor was this a made-up story, but rather the confirmation of what Allaah has commanded. For in it is the confirmation of the (divine) messengers, the signs of their trustees (the imams), and the proper conduct of those who are seeking.

So praise be to Allaah, in the beginning and at the end! May Allaah’s blessings be upon His Messenger, our master Muhammad, who was sent by Him to His creatures as a bearer of good news and as a warner. And (may His blessings be) upon his trustee, the imam of those who are mindful (of Allaah), ‘the best of the best,’ the beloved of the Lord of the worlds; and upon the imams from the people of His house upon whom Allaah has bestowed His favors! —may He take away from them all impurity and purify them totally. And Allaah is sufficient for us, the best of trustees, the best of protectors and the best of supporters. And there is no strength and no power except through Allaah, the Exalted, the Tremendous.

Chapter 7 — The Living Rope

It has been narrated to us that when the account of the scholar, the young man, Saa-lih, Al-Bakh-ta-ree, and Aboo Maa-lik had been set forth, some among the people of sincere reflection asked: “What, then, is the whole meaning of this affair? We have heard of the seeker, the oath, the worlds of symbols, the outer and inner dimensions, the greater Shaykh, the trial of family and community, and the testing of the jewel against the glass. But how should a person carry these teachings into daily life, so that the story does not remain only a story, and wisdom does not remain only beautiful speech?”

So it was said to them: “The beginning of the path is not information, but awakening. A person may possess many reports, remember many sayings, and repeat many terms, yet still remain asleep in the soul. The first gift in this book is the discovery of need. The young man does not become noble because he already knows. He becomes noble because he recognizes that he does not know, and because his ignorance wounds him enough to make him seek healing. That wound is mercy when it leads to guidance.”

For this reason, the first chapter teaches that gratitude is not a feeling that passes over the heart and disappears. Gratitude to the teacher is obedience to what is true. Gratitude for knowledge is putting knowledge into action. Gratitude for practice is steadfastness in carrying it forward. A person who thanks a physician but refuses the medicine has not truly shown gratitude. A person who praises a master craftsperson but never practices the craft has not honored the teacher. In the same way, the one who praises spiritual knowledge but does not allow it to reshape conduct has only admired the lamp while refusing its light.

This is why Ali, peace be upon him, is remembered as teaching that knowledge is better than wealth, because knowledge guards the one who carries it, while wealth must be guarded by its owner. The saying fits the path of the seeker: knowledge is not an ornament worn before people, but a protection placed within the soul. When knowledge guards a person, it guards his speech from arrogance, his desire from haste, his loyalty from blindness, and his worship from becoming an empty habit.

The book then brings the seeker to the oath. The oath is not a ceremony added to religion from outside. It is the door through which responsibility enters the heart. Without covenant, religion becomes opinion. Without obligation, love becomes mood. Without discipline, seeking becomes wandering. The oath teaches that truth is not merely something to be admired; it is something to be entered, carried, and answered for. In daily life, this means that a person should not treat faith like a preference, changing it whenever it becomes inconvenient. A covenant is not like clicking “I agree” beneath words one has not read. It is like receiving entrusted access to a sacred house: one enters with reverence, protects what has been entrusted, and does not pretend ownership over what was given as a trust.

The teachings of the Ismaili tradition have long preserved this balance between the outer path and the inner meaning. The Sharee-ah is not discarded because one has heard of the baatin, and the baatin is not denied because one performs the zaahir. The outer form is the body, and the inner meaning is the spirit. A body without spirit is lifeless, and a spirit without a body is not visible in the world of action. The one who performs outward practice without seeking meaning becomes dry, harsh, and mechanical. The one who claims inward meaning while neglecting outward discipline becomes unmoored, private, and self-deceived. The path requires both: practice that becomes intelligent, and understanding that becomes embodied.

This is the meaning of taa-weel when it is properly understood. Taa-weel is not guessing. It is not the freedom to make every symbol mean whatever the ego prefers. It is the disciplined return of a sign to its rightful meaning, under the guidance of those authorized to unveil it. The world is full of signs, but signs can be misread. A road sign can guide the traveler only if he understands the language of the road and trusts the proper authority that placed it there. If every person invents his own meaning for every sign, then guidance becomes traffic without lanes, voices without order, and motion without arrival.

In the second and third chapters, the scholar teaches the young man how creation itself becomes a book. Heaven and earth, water and air, light and darkness, body and spirit, name and meaning, shell and yolk, all become signs. The young man learns that the world is not mute. It speaks through pattern. It teaches through structure. Yet it does not reveal its deepest meanings to impatience. The surface is real, but it is not the whole. The inner meaning is real, but it must not be torn away from the surface that protects it. The innermost meaning is the goal, but it is approached through stages.

A modern person should understand this well, though he often fails to apply it. He uses devices whose screens are simple, whose code is hidden, and whose architecture is deeper still. He knows that a screen alone is not the app, that code alone is not the purpose, and that infrastructure without wise design can become dangerous. Yet in religion he may demand the surface only, or reject the surface entirely, or claim the deepest meaning without submitting to the discipline that leads there. This is the failure of an age rich in information and poor in formation. It has many search bars, but few seekers; many opinions, but little purification; many voices, but few gates.

The book does not condemn intellect. It honors intellect by placing it in its rightful service. Intellect is the instrument by which the seeker recognizes need, examines claims, avoids blind imitation, and receives proof. But intellect is not made to enthrone itself as lord over every mystery. It is a lamp, not the sun. It sees by the light given to it. When intellect becomes proud, it mistakes its horizon for the limit of truth. When intellect becomes humble, it becomes capable of guidance. The Ismaili tradition has consistently honored the life of the mind, not as a rebellion against faith, but as a requirement of faith. The seeker must think, question, test, and understand, but he must also know that not every locked door opens by force.

The greater Shaykh appears in the fourth chapter to teach this discipline of rank. Even the scholar has a master. Even the guide is guided. Even one who has brought life to another was himself brought to life through a chain of mercy. This is difficult for the modern ego because it wants mentorship without authority, knowledge without hierarchy, and correction without discomfort. But the young man’s silence before the greater Shaykh is not humiliation. It is readiness. He learns that some truths are not seized by clever speech; they are received through proper adab, the refined conduct of the seeker.

The lesson is severe but merciful: not everything should be spoken immediately, and not everyone is ready for everything. A seed is not wrong because it is not yet a tree. A child is not deficient because he must be raised. A patient is not insulted because the physician gives treatment in stages. In the same way, the seeker should not demand the end of the path before he has learned how to walk. The one who wants everything unveiled at once may lose even what he was able to bear. Therefore, the wise teacher does not merely answer questions. He prepares the questioner to survive the answer.

This preparation is not only mystical. It is practical. In daily life, a person should ask: Am I seeking truth, or am I seeking relief from uncertainty? Am I asking in order to understand, or asking in order to win? Am I ready to act on what I learn, or do I merely want another idea to store in my mind? The book repeatedly warns that knowledge without action is incomplete, and action without acceptance is uncertain. This means the seeker must become accountable in ordinary moments: in speech, work, family, money, loyalty, disagreement, and worship.

The fifth chapter brings the matter into the house, where truth often becomes hardest. Saa-lih’s father confronts him not as an enemy, but as a father wounded by what appears to be abandonment. Saa-lih does not answer with rebellion. He answers with reason, reverence, and courage. He does not flatter his father by hiding the truth, and he does not insult him by using truth as a weapon. He gives him two paths: either allow him to follow what he has recognized, or debate with him by intellect and without anger. Here the book teaches that family loyalty is noble, but it cannot become a prison for the soul. Tradition is honorable, but it must not become an excuse for refusing guidance.

This chapter is urgently needed in daily life. Many people today either submit outwardly while resentment grows inwardly, or else they break relationships as soon as disagreement appears. The book offers a harder path: speak truth with adab, remain loyal without lying, and reason without rage. Saa-lih does not despise his father’s past kindness. He builds his argument upon it. The father, for his part, does not cling to authority after truth touches him. His eyes overflow when he recognizes that his son’s argument is just. This is what makes him noble: he is not defeated by truth; he is freed by it.

Then comes the community, and with it the trial of collective opinion. Aboo Maa-lik is approached by people who are ready to accuse, condemn, and perhaps harm those whom they do not understand. Yet he refuses the intoxication of group anger. He says, in effect, that one must not condemn without listening, nor imitate without examining. He defends not a faction, but truth itself. This is among the clearest practical teachings of the book. When people speak from fear, loyalty, or inherited hostility, they may imagine that they are defending religion while actually betraying justice.

In modern life, this failure is everywhere. People read headlines and judge souls. They receive fragments through algorithms and call them knowledge. They condemn before asking, repeat before verifying, and gather around anger because anger feels like certainty. Aboo Maa-lik shows another way. He teaches that the first obligation in a serious matter is not reaction, but examination. A person should ask: What do I actually know? What have I only heard? What am I assuming? What would justice require if the people I distrust are closer to truth than I am?

The sixth chapter gathers these lessons into their sharpest form. Saa-lih and Aboo Maa-lik speak of seeking, knowledge, blind imitation, the jewel and the glass, Sharee-ah and religion, report and direct understanding. The chapter is not against transmitted reports, but it refuses to reduce religion to reports alone. Reports may preserve traces, but living guidance gives them order, meaning, and application. Without rightful interpretation, inherited material can become a pile of fragments. One person takes one report, another takes its opposite, and both claim certainty while the confused seeker is told to choose whichever opinion he likes. That is not wisdom. That is abandonment dressed as tolerance.

Saa-lih therefore distinguishes the jewel from the glass. The jewel is tested by those who know jewels. Spiritual knowledge is tested by those who possess rightful knowledge. A counterfeit may shine under poor light, but it cannot endure examination. This matters in daily life because every age manufactures false jewels. Some are made of slogans. Some are made of charisma. Some are made of inherited prestige. Some are made of clever language. Some are made of outrage. Some are made of convenience. The seeker must learn to ask not only, “Does this sound beautiful?” but, “Who has tested it? What authority confirms it? What fruit does it bear? Does it lead to humility, justice, service, and nearness to Allaah, or does it inflate the self?”

Here the teachings of the Ismaili Daa-ees and scholars become especially helpful. Al-Qaadi an-Numaan preserved the relation between Wilaayah, obedience, and practice, showing that allegiance is not merely affection but a structure of religious life. Al-Muayyad fid-Deen ash-Sheeraazee represents the teacher whose wisdom is not private brilliance but service within the ordered da-wah. Naasir-i Khusraw, who journeyed outwardly through lands and inwardly through knowledge, speaks to the seeker who discovers that the road to truth may require exile from comfort, reputation, and inherited assumptions. Such figures do not decorate the story. They show that the book’s path is not fantasy. It has been lived by people who treated knowledge as liberation, not as entertainment.

The Prophet Muhammad, peace be upon him, is remembered as the city of knowledge, and Ali, peace be upon him, as its gate. The image gathers the whole book. A city is not entered from every direction according to personal whim. It has a gate. The gate does not imprison the traveler; it admits him properly. The one who rejects the gate may circle the walls forever and call his movement progress. The one who enters through the gate finds streets, order, shelter, and a path to the center.

The first seven Imaams in the Shia understanding of guidance embody this continuity of light: Ali, peace be upon him; Hasan, peace be upon him; Husayn, peace be upon him; Ali Zayn al-Aabideen, peace be upon him; Muhammad al-Baaqir, peace be upon him; Ja'far as-Saadiq, peace be upon him; and Ismaa-eel ibn Ja'far, peace be upon him. Their remembrance in this chapter is not merely historical. It reminds the seeker that guidance is not an isolated opinion appearing in one age and vanishing in the next. It is a living inheritance, carried through rightful authority, recognized by knowledge, and proven through the transformation of those who receive it.

The practical application of all this begins before the tongue speaks. When a matter arises, the seeker should pause and ask whether his speech is coming from truth or from passion. If from truth, it should carry justice, restraint, and benefit. If from passion, even a correct sentence may become a weapon of the ego. The seeker should ask whether his knowledge has produced action. If not, he should fear that he has become a collector of lamps while sitting in darkness. He should ask whether his worship has meaning. If not, he should seek the baatin without abandoning the zaahir. He should ask whether his inward claims have discipline. If not, he should return to the Sharee-ah and to adab.

In work, this teaching becomes integrity. Policies and procedures are like outer forms; mission and ethical purpose are their inner meaning. When institutions keep procedures but lose purpose, they become heavy machines that produce exhaustion instead of justice. When individuals claim noble purpose but ignore procedure, they create chaos and call it authenticity. The book teaches balance: the body and spirit must remain together.

In family, this teaching becomes truthful mercy. The seeker should not weaponize growth against those who raised him. Nor should he allow love to become a chain that prevents obedience to truth. Saa-lih shows how to speak with firmness without contempt. Al-Bakh-ta-ree shows how to receive correction without losing dignity. Both are needed.

In community, this teaching becomes fairness before judgment. Aboo Maa-lik teaches that anger is not evidence, numbers are not proof, and inherited certainty is not the same as understanding. The seeker must resist the ease of belonging to a crowd that condemns before it listens. He must become the kind of person who can defend justice even when justice temporarily benefits those he does not yet understand.

In learning, this teaching becomes patience. Not every difficult idea is false, and not every attractive idea is true. Some truths are bitter at first because they expose the seeker’s poverty. Some falsehoods are sweet at first because they flatter the seeker’s self-image. The young man grows because he accepts stages. Aboo Maa-lik grows because he allows his certainty to be examined. The father grows because he lets truth defeat pride. The scholar grows because he gives what he received. Thus every person in the book is judged by movement: from ignorance to seeking, from seeking to covenant, from covenant to knowledge, from knowledge to action, from action to service.

The whole book may therefore be gathered in one image: the living rope. It descends from what is higher than the seeker, yet it reaches him where he stands. It is not broken by time, because its purpose is not trapped in one moment. It does not abolish the path beneath the feet, because the rope must be held while the traveler walks. It does not remove responsibility, because the one who holds it must still climb. It does not flatter the seeker, because it first reveals his need. Yet it does not abandon him, because once he holds it with sincerity, it becomes a means of ascent.

So whoever reads these chapters should not say, “I have understood,” until he has asked what the understanding now requires. Let him begin with gratitude, and make that gratitude action. Let him seek knowledge, but not as a collector of curiosities. Let him honor the zaahir, but not imprison religion in the outer form. Let him seek the baatin, but not claim inward meaning without discipline. Let him use intellect, but not worship it. Let him love his family, but not prefer comfort over truth. Let him belong to a community, but not surrender justice to the crowd. Let him test every jewel, including the one he already holds. Let him remember that living guidance is mercy, and that the path is not completed by hearing about it.

And if he asks how to begin tomorrow morning, let him begin simply. Let him guard one word from falsehood. Let him turn one piece of knowledge into action. Let him perform one duty with presence. Let him seek one clarification instead of pretending certainty. Let him repair one relationship without surrendering truth. Let him examine one inherited assumption without arrogance. Let him serve one person without announcing it. Let him remember Allaah in one moment where he would otherwise remember only himself.

For the path of the seeker is not separate from daily life. Daily life is where the seeker is tested. The market, the home, the screen, the meeting, the argument, the prayer, the silence after being corrected, the hesitation before judging, the loneliness after choosing truth: these are all places where the chapter continues. The book ends, but the rope remains. The words fall silent, but the obligation speaks. The seeker closes the page, but the path opens before him.
