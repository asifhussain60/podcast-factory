# Show notes — EP06

**Title:** Justice, Monotheism, and the Guardians of Allah

**Blurb:** The book's doctrinal climax and final chapter. Chapter five ended on Abu Malik (a-BOO MAA-lik), the senior scholar of the old creed, brought to a *zero point* — the *Sharia* (sha-REE-ah) of his age confessed to be *lost, corrupted, and disrupted*, and his identification of *religion* with *Sharia* collapsed by his own admission. He had asked: *the attribute is preferable to him — so how is it described?* Chapter six is the *answer*. Salih (SAA-lih) calls Abu Malik to *God's justice and His unity* — and immediately refuses the *monotheism-without-meaning* (*the use of God's names without knowing His Oneness*) that Abu Malik's school holds in common with the *people of justice and monotheism*. The opening figure: *as when you say, 'fire is hot' — if names and attributes stand in place of meanings, then let your tongue burn at the mere mention of fire, and let the mention of food satisfy you*. Names require their meanings. Salih then leads Abu Malik through three failed approaches to monotheism. The *four letters of Allah*: *which letter indicates God — the letters by their separation, or by their joining?* Abu Malik *was struck silent*; *take us on to another chapter*. *The door to the knowledge of monotheism has been closed before you*. The *name-vs-named-which-is-worshipped* trap: *Allah is a name for Allah; what is the difference between the name and the named? Which is worshipped — or both?* Either word-worship or two-worship; monotheism fails. The *analogy from human makers*: *everything made is made of three things, and God did not create things from anything that existed before them*. The likeness fails at the root. And the grammatical correction: *had you meant monotheism, you would have said, 'He is not like anything,' and you would not have said, 'nothing is like Him'* — for the second form still posits a *Him* whose likeness nothing matches. *Glory be to the One whom no determination of thought can grasp*. From the closure of sight and speech, and the obligation that *God does not charge worshippers with what they cannot bear*, Salih derives the positive doctrine: *there must be causes for this knowledge between Him and His creation — intermediaries who stand as the just ones between Him and His servants, conveying His command and prohibition*. Abu Malik's grand concession: *this, for me, is from the foundations of justice*. The structure follows: single justice → single judgment → succession of messengers → ranks among the just → equal reward for obedience regardless of rank → the *virtuous one* (in the *da'wa* (DAH-wah) register the *nāṭiq*, NAA-tiq, the speaker- prophet, bearer of revelation) and the *curious one* (the *waṣī*, /waˈsˤiː/, the successor, bearer of *ta'wīl*, /taʔˈwiːl/, esoteric interpretation) — *both* inspired, *both* yielding obedience-to-Allah. *The Book bears witness to the interpreter*. Abu Malik then raises his community's great objection: *they all agree that after their prophet there is no prophet and no warner in their time*. Salih's answer is the chapter's most devastating piece of historical-doctrinal indictment. *The originators of this were three, in every age: a wayward devil, a stubborn tyrant, and a hypocrite*. The same lie was told to *the nation of the Magi*, *the nation of the Torah* (*no messenger after Moses*), *the nation of the Gospel* (*its prophet is its God* — *no messenger after Jesus*), and now *your community* (*no messenger or warner after their Messenger*) — *four nations, one error*. The community's *three qualities* are exposed: the prior nations have *earliness in religion*, *consensus among themselves and with you*, and the *testimony-asymmetry* (*your own testimony to them that their prophets are truthful, and none of them has testified for any prophet of yours*). The *just one is the guardian of Allah* (*awliyāʾ*, /ʔawliˈjaːʔ/). Abu Malik smiles his first breakthrough — *you have brought me out of my chapter on justice and monotheism, and what has brought me out of it is the truth itself*. The *temporal-question*: *how shall we know the causes in our time?* Salih's *interregnum-defense* collapse: *was there really an interregnum after the religion of Abraham, when Allah sent Ishmael, Isaac, Jacob, Joseph, Jonah, and Shu'ayb (shoo-AYB), leading up to Moses?* Six prophets named. *So what interregnum stood between Moses and the Gospel, when Allah sent Joshua son of Nun, Elias, Talut (taa-LOOT), David, Solomon, Zechariah, and John, leading up to Jesus?* Seven more. *There was no interregnum*. The just ones are *openly present or hidden in fear, veiled* — *Moses' flight from Pharaoh's city to the land of Shu'ayb* is the paradigm. The *veil* is the *form of their presence in a hostile age*, and is named *Taqiyya* (ta-KEE-yah, protective concealment). The indictment of the jurists who shelter the killers — *they show mercy to the slain, but see no disobedience in the killer* — leads to the chapter's most pointed single line: *were it not for the narrations of the jurists, no tyrannical king could ever be established*. Salih turns the indictment on Abu Malik himself: *you placed on their stockings the crown of your justice, and they grew proud; you covered their clay with the robes of the sacred law, and they went too far; you lit up their falsehood with the idleness of your truth*; *do not incline toward those who have done wrong*; *obey those in authority among you* (the guardians, not the king); *fight those who disbelieve* — *you disobeyed those whom you were commanded to obey, and obeyed those whom you were commanded to fight, all the while thinking you were doing well*. The community's *no-messenger-after-these-thirteen-messengers* claim is exposed: *they made the killing of the guardians of Allah lawful unjustly — they wronged Him by that saying and by promising a lie*. Salih names the structural diagnosis: *had you supported the friends of Allah, they would have appeared; had you failed His enemies, they would not have prevailed*. The guardians' *Taqiyya* is the community's responsibility, not God's deficit. Abu Malik repents; Salih opens the way back (*take refuge in Him, repent to Him, ask Him for the way of His guardians*). Salih's eyes well with tears at the mention of repentance and he interrupts his speech. Al-Bakhtari (al-bakh-tah-REE) — Salih's father, the scholar of chapter five — counsels gentleness: *do not let it move you to inflict ta'zir (tah-ZEER, discretionary religious chastisement) upon them. Test them by ignoring them without ignorance*. Abu Malik and his companions are guided and return to their people *as warners*. The author's closing: *whatever in this account took the form of a narrative was no idle telling, but a confirmation of what Allah has commanded — for in it is the steadfastness of the messengers, the signs of the guardians, and the manners of the seekers*. The doxology blesses the Messenger Muhammad, peace and blessings of Allah be upon him; *his guardian, the Imam of the pious, the leader of the Muhajirun (moo-haa-jih-ROON), the beloved of the Lord of the Worlds* (named *only by attribute*, preserving the book's discipline of naming-by-attribute); and *the Imams of the people of his house, from whom Allah has removed every abomination*. The closing formula — *there is no power and no strength except by Allah the Almighty* — is the same formula whose interpretation (the *ḥawl* as the Imam who turns the Creator's words; the *quwwa* as his *ḥujja* and *bāb*) the book has spent six chapters unfolding. The book is done.
