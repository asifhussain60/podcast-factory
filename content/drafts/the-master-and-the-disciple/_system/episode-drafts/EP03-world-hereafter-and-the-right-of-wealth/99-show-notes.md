# Show notes — EP03

**Title:** World, Hereafter, and the Right of Wealth

**Blurb:** The ethical-practical bridge of *Kitab al-'Alim wa-l-Ghulam* (*The Book of the Master and the Boy*). After the cosmology of chapter two — the three words, the seven principles, the seven *nāṭiqs*, the parable of the egg — the boy turns to *application*. He names the *pairs* the cosmology has put in place (world and Hereafter, soul and body, apparent and subtle knowledge), and the Master walks them outward into ethics: the three houses of worship (angels, humans, beasts) and their three knowledges; the two kinds of people (the teacher whose soul has been animated by the spirit of certainty, and the learner still seeking for his survival); the great station of God's saints that no one can give to another, and the figure of the sower who prepares and waters but cannot bring out the plant. The Master names the *rope* — not the *rope of Allah* directly, but *your rope*, *your hujja*, *the door of your Imam* — and halts at the *rope of the rope of Allah* ("the end of your guidance and the end of the worlds"). He drops the *ta'wīl* of *la hawla wa la quwwata illa billah*: the *hawl* is the Imam who *turns* the Creator's words into the people's; the *quwwa* is his *hujja* and *bāb*; the *ʿuṣba* in the Joseph story is the ten arguments; the eleven planets, sun, and moon are the twelve captains, the Imam, and his *hujja*. He gives the *batin* of Pharaoh's dream — seven fat cows = causes of God; seven lean = the seven *nāṭiqs*; seven green ears = al-Khidr as cause between God and the *awliyāʾ*; seven dry = the guardians — and the four meanings of *the life of this world* (perishing vs lasting; outward knowledge vs inward knowledge). He gives the verdict on the one who has the inward without the outward (a *prophet* in level, but the *outward* does not rise if the *inward* does not) and on the one who has the outward without the inward (*a body raised up but into which no soul has been breathed; counted among the dead*). The narrator records the Master *beaming* and *weeping* when the boy describes his brethren — the scholars yellow with vigil and weak with fasting, who *refused to work the world even from what is not forbidden to them*. The Master pivots to the question of why *passions* differ when the origin (Adam from dust) and the *instrument* (the senses, outer and inner) are the same; the answer is *justice* — same archers, same target, same instrument, *differing in striking*. He gives the doctrine of *classes in the world* (equal in punishment because joined in disobedience) and *degrees in religion* (graded in acceptance because charged according to capacity) with the Qur'anic warrant of Q 4:69 (*whoever obeys Allah and His Messenger — those will be with the ones upon whom Allah has bestowed favor: the prophets, the truthful, the martyrs, and the righteous*), the limit of effort (*the full measure of one's knowledge and the utmost reach of one's capacity*), the duty of the body (the golden rule restated as *justice*), and the duty of wealth: *take it only from a lawful source, and spend it only in its rightful place*, with the *zakat* measured as *a fifth of the best of it* — *what you yourself would love to receive*. And then the chapter's structural climax: the boy *enacts* the doctrine, placing *all* his wealth in the Master's hands in *five shares* (zakat, expiation, thanksgiving, brethren, the Master's share). The Master, *tears flowing*, refuses his own share (*I have come out from wealth and from its temptations, fleeing from it*) and reapportions the rest as *trusts held for their rightful owners*. The boy asks who this *generous one* is — and the chapter, for the first time, speaks of the *Imam* directly: *the one whose right Allah has made binding upon me and upon every believer, in whose hand are the keys of the heavens and the landmarks of the kingdom, whose palms are opened with the light of Sinai*. The Master places himself as *his son and one of his servants*. The chapter ends on the boy's request to be taken *by the hand to the door of his chamber* and the Master's careful answer: *This is your duty — but only with his permission and his decision*.
