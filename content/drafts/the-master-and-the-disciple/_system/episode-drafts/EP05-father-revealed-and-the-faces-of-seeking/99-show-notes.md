# Show notes — EP05

**Title:** The Father Revealed and the Three Faces of Seeking

**Blurb:** The chapter where the book at last names them. Chapter four ended on the father's anger. The boy — *re-born* into the chain on the *seventh day* by the *greater Shaykh* (SHAYKH), given the *highest transmission* the source itself veils, and walked back to the edge of his city by the *scholar* — returned home with his heart absent in fear, and his father came to him, angry. This chapter is the *answer*. The father reproves; the boy offers *two strands of gratitude* (outward courtesy with divided hearts, or open debate with confidence between them); the father rejects the *taqiyya*-strand (*how long can outward agreement stand when its underpinnings differ?*) and chooses *debate*; the boy lays his bind (*either you are a scholar who withheld the teaching, or you are ignorant and needed the stranger; either way, I will precede you*); the father weeps, concedes, *they debated at intervals and disclosed things to each other at length, until they were guided to the right path*. *Through them, God revived many of His creation*. *The boy was sent out into the world to preach, with his father's blessing*. Then — for the first time in the book — the source names them: *the boy's name was Salih (SAA-lih), and his father's name was al-Bakhtari (al-bakh-tah-REE)*. News of them reaches a group who held to the old creed; the community goes to *Abu Malik* (a-BOO MAA-lik) — *the heel of the inks*, their senior scholar — and asks him what to do about the *blood and property* of these *crossers-over*. Abu Malik refuses to judge by anger. He gives the figure of the *breach* (*a breach in religion is gravely damaging, like a breach in medicine — its flaw cannot be repaired*) and lays out *three states* the *new community* could be in: *either they are upon what we know of religion (only with additions of literature, hadiths, poetry); or they have been given an increase in what they hold — and knowledge is a lost thing of the believer; or the people have given them guidance other than what they have, by the prophecy of a prophet who has been brought to them. This is not denied by the command of God … God is at work every day*. The *senior scholar of the old creed* voices the *three-states* doctrine that includes *prophecy after the books* as a *live possibility*. He warns the community against becoming *like the tyrants of the previous nations, who said God would not send messengers after their messengers … and even killed the prophets of God*. He humbles their hearts, teaches them the *door of seeking* (*the needy person must humble himself before what he needs*), the *sound heart* (*the heart that does not persist in denying what is said to it*), the *reverence- before-the-statement* doctrine, and the *petitioner-before- the-king* parable. He collapses the timetable from three days to two to *only this day, if you will do it; otherwise I go on alone*. They all repent, pay debts, bathe, put on the purest garments, and go out to al-Bakhtari's gate. Abu Malik calls al-Bakhtari *Abu Salih* (a-BOO SAA-lih, *father- of-Salih*); the father plays the title back open — *today my father is good, and I am the son of Salih; and he is good with his Lord*. Abu Malik names the *transpositions* (*the child has become the father, the Lord is in the earth, the Lord has a servant come to Him from him, the one who created annihilation has become eternal*) and asks for the *righteous slave*. Al-Bakhtari grants it under chain- discipline. Salih clears the council and welcomes them. The chapter's central exchange is the long Salih-Abu Malik dialogue. Salih's opening rebuke — *O Ka'b al-Ahbar* (KAB al-AH-bar, the paradigm of imitation-without-direct- knowledge) — *is it imitation, then?* Abu Malik's concession: *religion stands above imitation*. The *prophet- warner* doctrine: *the warner is a prophet, and the prophet is an argument from Allah, both for-them and against-them*. The *three faces of seeking*: *a seeker who knows, a seeker who does not know, a seeker who knows that he does not know* — with the parallel between *money-seeking* and *Hereafter-seeking* (the *divine scholar* who seeks out the dead; the *learned man* seeking the full end; the *ignorant who knows his ignorance*). Abu Malik places himself in the third — *the knowing seeker who knows nothing but knows that he is in need*. Salih probes: *knowledge by report* or *knowledge by direct sight*? — and lays out the *poverty- distress-capacity* doctrine: *ignorance is a need, poverty is the narrowness of need, distress is a great need that compels the creature to seek capacity; and capacity is the breadth of knowledge, because knowledge is wide*. Abu Malik confesses dire need. Salih: *how do you know me, without having seen me beside a scholar of prophecy?* The *essence- traders* parable: the *old creed's essence*, presented to the *people of metals*, was found to be *glass in camouflage*. *Knowledge is not knowledge until it is presented to those who know the science of heaven*. Abu Malik raises the question that frames the closing movement: *we know of no prophets and messengers in our time; nor have the people had any covenant with such men since the revelation was cut off by the books*. Salih: *any religion that is established only on narration is not called a religion*. The *books-themselves* fail because they give only the general. The *Maqrub* (mak-ROOB) divorce-case: Abu Malik, asked for a ruling, gave *two opposed rulings* and said *act on whichever of the two you wish* — *Maqrub goes home not knowing whether the woman in his house is his wife or another man's*. Abu Malik's confession: *the Sharia* (sha-REE-ah) *of my age has been lost, corrupted, and disrupted, and we hold on only to the name of religion*. Salih: *what is religion?* Abu Malik: *command, prohibition, lawful, forbidden, Sunnah* (SOON-nah)*, obligations*. Salih: *so you have combined religion and Sharia; then religion has been disrupted by the suspension of the Sharia — what is left with you?* Abu Malik sits a long while thinking, raises his head: *I do not see that anything is left with me. So what religion do you have?* Salih's positive doctrine — *religion is with me, and I am not veiled from it … the cause that connects heaven to earth, unbroken — the loop of the firm handhold, the rope of those who hold fast, the ark of tranquility and the ship of life and the light of life, generous in its content, safeguarded in its sciences, marked in its jewels, its virtue hidden from no one who looks upon it*. The chapter ends on Abu Malik's seam-question into chapter six: *if God has a religion by which He will judge on the Day … but now, since it has been lost, the attribute is preferable to him — so how is it described?*
