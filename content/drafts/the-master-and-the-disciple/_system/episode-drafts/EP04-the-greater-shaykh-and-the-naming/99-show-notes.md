# Show notes — EP04

**Title:** The Greater Shaykh and the Seventh-Day Naming

**Length:** 50 to 60 minutes (extended tier)

**NotebookLM format:** Deep Dive. Length: Long.

## Blurb

The initiation chapter of *The Master and the Disciple* — the chapter where the disciple is re-born into the chain of the call by a seventh-day naming at the hand of the greater elder, receives the highest transmission (the innermost inward the source deliberately does not record), and is sent as a caller in his own right.

After the chapter-three deferral on the Imam's permission, the Master goes to the disciple's father with the report; the father marvels and instructs "make haste." The Master returns and finds the disciple in "a different state of body than the one he had left him in" — yellowed by longing in a deliberate echo of the chapter-three brethren yellowed by the rigor of fasting.

The announcement comes: "I cast mercy upon you into the heart of the one who has charge of you. So take up your traveling provisions, for I have high hopes for you, and I will accompany you to the door of your goal." The disciple nearly dies of joy; "God took hold of him, knowing his condition; then He returned him."

They set out and reach the greater elder. In the elder's council the disciple turns yellow from the intensity of his awe and cannot speak; the elder recognizes him to the Master as "the disciple we used to hear about," and the disciple learns that his story is studied in the gatherings of remembrance of the brethren themselves.

The next morning the elder delivers two sustained discourses: the justice of God as "the middle between two extremes" (with the figure of spread-out hands and the evidence against ingratitude itself unlimited), and the dignity of minds with the chain of rights — thought to good manners to knowledge to action to obedience perfected in hardship and ease.

Then the chapter's central ritual: the seventh-day naming. The elder asks the disciple his name; the disciple gives a name meaning "little servant, son of the servant"; the elder receives it as "your description"; the disciple withdraws to "I am free, son of the servant"; the elder probes ("who freed you from a master until you became free?"); the disciple gives the manumission-by-five-shares account ("this world possessed me, and I have handed it back to the One who called it"); the elder tightens to the impossibility of self-naming; the disciple falls silent. The elder names the impasse — "how can a thing know that which has no name?" — and defers until the completion of seven days, "for the dignity of the newborn," "the name belongs to you, and you belong to the name. So it does not appear except within your limit, and it travels with your duration."

On the seventh day the disciple bathes, puts on his purest garments, relieves himself ("a sign of the good that was to come after it"), and goes out repeating "the day of the auspicious, and the guardian of the auspicious." The elder receives him with the implements of his work already taken up and delivers, in a veil the source itself never lifts, the highest transmission: "matters upon which no fancy had ever descended… not to be mentioned in the assemblies of preachers, and not permitted in mosques. These books are among the greatest of His bounties, and are unveiled only to His own people."

The moment is sealed in the figure of pilgrimage-by-the-great-sign: "he removed his pilgrimage garments by glorifying the sanctities of God, took hold of the rope, circumambulated the ancient House, spent his rites in remembrance, and performed pilgrimage by the great sign." Then attendance — "the root was watered and the branch raised" — in deliberate reversal of the chapter-three sower-figure: what the Master alone could not do, the chain has now done together.

Then the farewell. The disciple praises the elder as "the gate of the people of heaven and the ascent of the people of the earth"; the elder blesses him in eight clauses, with the method of the call named directly; the elder gives three reasons for parting (the next journey to another country, the family awaiting the disciple's return, the duty of fulfilling the right of this grace — to worship by it and to call others to it); and the elder enjoins the safeguarding of the father in five negatives ("do not clothe him in the garment of contempt, do not board him on the ships of harm, do not omit his name, do not become distracted from his remembrance, and do not deceive him in your city — for there is a sting at the very outset").

The scholar walks the disciple back toward his city, and on the edge of the city gives the disciple the six qualities and the interpretation key — "whatever in it is praiseworthy is like the truth and its people; whatever was a reprehensible parable, I spent it upon the enemies of the truth and its people… these are the foundations of the science that works by them: if she preserves them, she has no need of any others."

The final parting — the disciple's gasp at the mention of parting was such that he almost killed himself; then he kissed the scholar's feet and wiped their dust upon his face — closes the chapter. The disciple returns home with his heart absent from him in fear, unable to return any utterance of his grief over the parting from his elder father and the father who had been the cause of his grace. His family becomes afraid for him. Then his father came to him, angry. The chapter ends there. The naming has happened; the name in the world will now happen. The chapter is the re-birth; the next chapter is the re-entry.
