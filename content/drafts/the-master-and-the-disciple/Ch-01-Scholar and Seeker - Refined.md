# Chapter 1 — Scholar and Seeker

It has been narrated to us that a number of the truly faithful and a group among those who call people to true religion once said to one of their scholars: ‘You have liberated us by helping us learn about an affair of great importance, for which we feel obligated to show our gratitude for three reasons:

1. Our gratitude for you having called us to the true religion
2. Our gratitude for the knowledge that you directed us to
3. Our gratitude for the religious practices you ordered us to perform.

So inform us how we should express our gratitude for these three reasons. Then inform us about:
- the rights and duties that are obligatory on us as directed by the ordinances of religion;
- and the obligations of a seeker in his mode of questioning,
- and the obligations on the responses of the scholar being sought.

And inform us, to the best of your ability, about the ways of the righteous and the proper conduct of the seekers.’

The scholar replied: ‘The matter to which I invite all of you is the religion that Allaah has bestowed as an honor on His servants; which He has perfected for them and through which He honors whoever responds to Him. So for each beginning, He has placed in it an end, and for each end, a goal; and each goal has a limit whose full extent cannot be attained. These are the way-stations of the people of true understanding. Their key is constantly remembering Allaah, their initial levels are trials; the middle ones are of right guidance and the final ones are piety. So whoever is seeking to learn the basics of Allaah's affairs, and then discovers the ranks of the divinely determined (hudood ad-deen al-maqdoor), he has sought guidance from the right sources and has set upon it in the best possible manner.
As for showing gratitude to the scholar (who guided you), that is through his obedience;
and the gratitude for the knowledge (he gave you), is putting it into action and calling others to it;
and the gratitude for the right practice (he taught you), is striving patiently in it and in calling others to it.

Gratitude here is not treated as a feeling. It is treated as a responsibility. In ordinary life, this is like being trained by a master craftsperson: thanking the teacher does not end with praise. It shows itself when the apprentice practices the craft correctly, preserves its standards, and teaches it responsibly to someone else. In the same spirit, Ali, peace be upon him, is reported to have taught that knowledge is better than wealth because knowledge guards the one who carries it. That line fits this passage exactly: knowledge becomes a trust that protects the seeker only when it is put into action.

Scholar: Obedience
Knowledge: Putting knowledge into action
Practice: Remaining steadfast

As for teaching you the ways of the righteous and the proper conduct of the seekers, the following story has been narrated to us about a man from Persia who was among those subject to the trial of spiritual ignorance. Although he had a rich heart and a brilliant mind, and had acquired a refined culture and education, nonetheless his ignorance outweighed his knowledge, because of his earlier experiences and the environment in which he grew up. So he was restless in the burning heat of his thirst, assuming the glimmering of mirages to be the reflection of water, until he reached it, and found it to be nothing. However, there he found Allaah, and paid Him his account in full! Then Allaah honored his abode and removed the veil in front of his intellect. He found him straying, so He guided him; He found him in need, so He satisfied him with knowledge. Thus by His graces he eventually became one of the scholars of Sinai and the angelic dwellers in the well-populated temple.

Finally when his guidance had been completed and he reached the goals of his aspiration, he was duty-bound to show gratitude to his Master and to exert himself for his Lord. He used to hear his spiritual father repeat a proverb, which held the rank of soul to his body: ‘The most excellent of deeds is giving life to the dead (inna afdhal al-hasanaat ihyaa al-amwaat).’ So he said to himself: ‘I used to be dead, and he gave me life; I was ignorant, and he gave me knowledge. I am not the first person to ever be ignorant, such that I attained knowledge before everyone else; nor am I the last one to be ignorant, such that the process of spiritual teaching has come to an end on me. Therefore out of gratitude for this blessing, it is only appropriate for me to pass on this divine trust to those who come after me; just as those who preceded me have handed it down to me. For the origin of this affair is from Allaah, and it only reached me through its many intermediaries, the first passing it on to the second, the second passing it on to the third, until it descended from the heavenly host to the creatures of this lower world. After that, did it tear asunder the veil between Allaah and His creations, and did the ‘gateways’ pass it onward until it reached me, only so that I could be its final goal and ultimate end? Not at all. For those who have transmitted it and handed on this trust before me are more deserving of precedence and spiritual gains! What they have earned does not belong to me, so that I could rely on it. No. I (myself) am part of what they have earned through their acts (of teaching me). Nor does their precedence relieve me from having to act! So now I need to strive to spread knowledge, just as I needed to strive to seek knowledge in the beginning.’ He realized that he is now obligated to pass on his spiritual understanding to others, just as his master was obligated to teach him, and that his final duty was similar to his initial duty (wa anna al-aakhir amruhu fil-hukm mithl al-awwal).

In other words, in the beginning, the scholar's task was to strive to acquire knowledge, and now in the end, his task is to strive to teach it.

So he travelled towards his Lord leaving his people and possessions behind, calling people to the good, in order to deserve a gratitude, similar to what was incumbent upon him for his master. He started traveling through countries, passing among the non-Arab and the Arab tribes, scrutinizing their faces for signs of spiritual aptitude and posing religious questions. But he received no response, nor did he meet anyone seeking knowledge, until he ended up in the furthest part of the jazeerah. There he entered a city where no one knew him; and while he was recalling Allaah’s blessings and searching various gatherings, he noticed a group of people from the town who were discussing religion without any guidance, and recklessly following the inclinations of their passions. He sat nearby, out of sight, listening carefully to their discussion and analyzing them carefully to determine which of them was closer to the right path.

Then he said to himself: ‘These people are more deserving of life (spiritual knowledge) for three reasons:
First, because the true divine argument has reached them;
Second, because they belong to the people of my community;
and third, because they are closer to the right path shown by their keen interest in religion and the fact that they are inquiring about it and discussing it —for the person who is already searching for something is much closer to finding it.’

This is like how someone actively searching the internet to learn about a specific health condition is far more likely to absorb medical guidance than someone who has no interest in their health at all. The presence of the search indicates a readiness for the truth.

The scholar recognizes readiness before he speaks openly. A person already searching for truth is like a patient who finally admits the symptoms and seeks a real diagnosis; such a person is much closer to healing than one who denies the illness. This is why the Prophet Muhammad, peace be upon him, is remembered in the tradition as the city of knowledge, with Ali, peace be upon him, as its gate. The seeker is not merely collecting ideas; he is looking for the right entrance into knowledge.

The narrator continued: When those people completed their discussion, they turned to the scholar and asked: "Who are you, and where are you from, O youth?’

‘I am Abdullaah and I am among the residents of His sanctuary,’ he replied.

‘What is your business here and what is your work?’ they asked.

‘My business has completed,’ he replied, ‘and as for work, I am looking for it.’

In other words, "Business" implies acquiring knowledge, while "work" implies spreading the acquired knowledge.

‘Well then,’ they said, "did anything in our discussion catch your attention?”

‘All of it was interesting for the person who would be interested,’ he answered.

‘But the word “interesting” has two meanings,’ they said, ‘a commendable one and a reprehensible one....’

‘Of course!’ he said, ‘and similarly all "speech" can be commendable or reprehensible.’

They asked: ‘Then according to you, what is commendable speech?’

‘Speech that is true,’ he replied.

‘And what kind is that?’ they asked.

‘Speech, whose origin comes from Allaah and through which people are called towards Allaah,’ he answered.

‘Then what,’ they asked, ‘is the reprehensible speech?’

He replied: ‘Speech that which is based on passion, and is used to call people to something other than true guidance.’

‘You have spoken truly.’ they said. ‘So won’t you share some of your words with us?’

‘I do not claim words of my own,’ he replied. ‘For I am following the words of another!’

‘Then let us hear you say something that will inform us about your particular religious ways’ they said. ‘For you have made a good impression on us, and we were pleased by your brotherly concern, so now we ask you to become a gateway for us, through which Allaah may send His loving kindness for us.'

‘All right,’ he replied. He stood up, facing the attentive group and began...

‘Verily He created with His power, then arranged everything perfectly! Who determined a path through His generosity, then guided! It is He who brought forth paths of the pastureland, then made its outer form a dried-up brush and its inner aspect broad in scope and content! whose ways are not obscure (laa multabisa as-subul) and whose traces will never be obliterated (wa laa mundarisa al-aathaar)—for if it were so, then intellects would fail to seek it, and would despair reaching it. His knowledge does not exist outwardly, so that the unworthy could not possess and alter it. Instead it is benevolent and well-protected! Because of its excellence, it is well concealed, and its secrets are well-guarded for those worthy of them! Its dignity is the splendid robe of guidance, and its perfection is the garment of piety! It is marked out by the light of lights, entrusted safely in the hearts of the best and chosen ones - the truly pious, guardians, protectors, generous and recorders! This knowledge is a trust which they safely pass on, and do not transgress the divine ordinances. It is the law of the Supreme Judge containing all the benefits of the All-Merciful! He honored it by making it inward, and He protected it by keeping it secret. He veiled it with His obligations, and crowned it with His covenants. For it cannot be reached by the aspirations of those who rely on the strength of their intellects (fa laa tanaaluhu himam al-mutafakkireen), nor can it be attained by those who are scheming for it (wa laa tablughu fikr al-muhtaleen). Its outer aspect is a reminder and a sign for those who are rightly guided, while its inner aspect is a sweet drink gushing forth.

His lofty heavens come down close, and his earth is arranged for those who inquire about Him and His guiding stars are arranged for those who seek Him. He has watering-places for those who move towards Him, and signs, whose wonders follow each other in succession, pointing to Him through their symbols. His accustomed way is preferment, and He is approached through distinguished ranks. His duties are obligatory and His accustomed ways are paths to be walked on. Sincerity ennobles his obligations, and justice purifies the efforts made for him. For He has made his ways a guide and a path leading to good, with its outer aspect a protective shell, and a sign pointing toward its inner dimensions. Intellects are forced by their neediness and ignorance to seek knowledge and open abundance. So blessed be the One Who placed the night and the day in succession, for those who reflect and those who wish to be grateful! May Allaah’s blessings be on the one who He has specially chosen from His servants, and made a warner for all the worlds!’

Then he said: ‘Be mindful of Allaah like the one who fears Him; and fear Him like the one who is hoping to please Him! Seek knowledge and you will learn; Act according to it and you will receive Allaah’s loving mercy (wa-tlubul-‘ilm, ta‘allamoo, wa‘maloo turhamoo)!’

Then his eyes overflowed with tears and he stopped talking and bid the group farewell. Still weeping, he got up and walked toward his travel baggage. All in the group were overcome with emotions by the intensity of his speech and the effectiveness of his sermon. They wanted to accompany him, but he prevented them from doing so. Since they didn’t know what else to do, they all returned to their homes. But a young man from the group, who was the youngest of them, yet had the best character, accompanied him. He had completed the education of his intellect, and reflection had awakened his heart. His thoughts coincided with, and were reinforced by what he heard in the scholar’s speech - both in what he had presented and what he had alluded to. This young man's father was a great Shaykh among the most distinguished people of Arab by lineage and was one of the most devoted supportees of the people of his religion. With the scholar's permission, the young man accompanied him until they reached his (the scholar's) inn. The scholar entered and invited the young man in. He then ordered food, and when they finished eating and were comfortable enough to talk,

The young man said: ‘O wise man! You spoke and we heard! You preached with eloquence and adorned religion with the most beautiful of ornaments, and motivated us with the most beautiful of motivations and the noblest of preparations. You called us through the most eminent of responsibilities, enticing our minds to enquire from you about all that has struck our hearts as a result of your speech. Such excellence could only manifest through one whose knowledge has perfected internally and whose character has completely developed externally, making him a source of wisdom! You are like a tree... its roots firm, its splendor towering over the worlds, its fruits hanging low from its branches for those who seek them. So I ask you to be one of those blessed branches for me, from what I can see of your distinguished judgment, your native aptitude and your perfect intellect. You mentioned that our minds are forced by their ignorance to seek knowledge and abundance. Now my mind is the most needy and indignent of all, so is there any way to life for me?’

The scholar replied: ‘Surely the One Who created by His power, did not create without purpose, nor did He create them entirely knowledgeable. He did not leave them wandering in ignorance without end, nor did He want them to adopt foolishness as their behavior, or wear the garments of ignorance. So He established a way out for them by means of an "instrument" (intellect) around which He shaped their constitution (alif bihaa tarkeeb) and by which He perfected their creation (wa akmala bihaa khalqahum). He made their hearing, eyes and hearts, an opening to accept the light of His knowledge as an act of generosity toward them, and by it completed His argument for them. He then placed the treasures of His wisdom and the specific characteristics of His wishes that pleased Him in the pure, chosen, elite of His creation. He gave them the responsibility of worshipping Him according to those ways, and calling others to those ways, while imposing on people the duty of responding to their call by using their instrument (intellect), so that He may distinguish the wicked from the good. Hence the scholar will reveal himself plainly through his seeking of that knowledge, while the ignorant will be distinguished by his falling behind. Therefore there is no argument against Allaah after His messengers, nor is there any excuse for straying after the right path has been clearly pointed out.’

The young man replied: ‘My lack of excuse has frightened me, and the existence of signs pointing to the right path makes me desire setting out on that search; so I ask you to be a guide, and I'll be your humble servant.’

The scholar answered: ‘Surely Allaah did not create His creatures as fully grown scholars. He created them as infants, small and ignorant, not knowing anything, so that they grow big after having been small, and come to know after having been ignorant. Thus their being small is a precondition for their becoming big, and their ignorance is a sign pointing toward knowledge. The same is true of spiritual knowledge. One can only attain its essence on the basis of preceding, lower forms of knowledge, which hearts can quickly assimilate and intellect can easily grasp. And so that the first stage of spiritual growth is established in their minds, and they are humbled enough to carry out the required duties. This opens up their hearts to the essence of that knowledge and enable their intellects to bear its goal. That is Allaah's accustomed way regarding His servants! So we neither need to depart from the opinions of religious communities, nor do we need to follow anything other than the accustomed way to be guided.’

‘But I am an infant in relation to what I wish to achieve on this path,’ the young man said, ‘so raise and educate me! I am ignorant with regard to what I have been seeking, so teach me and help me learn. I am forced to rely on your gracious favor, so have mercy on me! Surely you were once in this state, and Allaah brought His bounties to you through the hands of the one who became so important for you, and to whom you owe such gratitude! So you already understand the state of someone like myself, caught in the vice of ignorance and so needy, that he will desperately resort to any way out.’

The scholar said: ‘I myself was once in the state you’ve described, and I surely wish to spare you the desperation. But my compassion towards you will not bring you closer to what you hope for without your "working" for it! For just as every word has a proper employment that can only be accepted if it is sincere and true, similarly knowledge is of no benefit until it has been put into action; and action is of no benefit until it is accepted by Allaah. Now the true and sincere form of speech is that, which is just. And the signs pointing to knowledge are the revealed paths of righteous acts, and faithfully conforming to the right models throughout all that is encompassed by the furthermost regions and the variation of day and night. So do not seek knowledge impatiently, and do not dismiss it out of negligence! Nor should you accept it out of blind imitation, without any convincing argument — for in such a case it would slip out of your heart at the first onset of doubt. But do not deny the argument once it has been firmly established, or you will become the pleader for all that is false. And do not stop inquiring about that knowledge and reflecting upon it, or your judgment will become sterile. You must trust me without prejudice, and make every effort to understand without confusion, seeking the truth and asking Allaah’s help for it — for Allaah does not neglect a recompense, nor does He forbid reward if He sees you seeking for the good!’

Blindly imitating without understanding is like navigating a busy modern city using a GPS map from a hundred years ago. If you trust the outdated map so blindly that you refuse to look at the actual roads in front of you, you will eventually crash into a wall rather than verifying the reality with your own eyes and intellect.

‘Surely you have spoken most justly, accomplishing great good, and you have pointed the way to the truth with the most beautiful of words,’ said the young man. ‘But now I am seeking Allaah’s help, and I am asking you about that affair to which you are calling and specifically referring to. What is it? Who does it come from? and to Who does it lead?’

‘That is Allaah’s religion which He has approved!’ replied the scholar. ‘It is from Him, so He will accept none other! Specifically, it leads to the pure elite among His messengers, and generally to all of His creation. So the person who has been singled out for His favor should be grateful for it, and put it into practice, and call others to it. But the person who has been excluded (from that knowledge) should keep seeking it until he comes to know of it, and should willingly obey whoever is guiding him.’

‘Do you think,’ asked the young man, ‘that this favor by which Allaah has singled out His friends is at the same level, such that they are all equal in its excellence? Or do some of them have a rank of excellence over others?’

‘Haven’t I already mentioned,’ said the scholar, ‘that every knowledge has a preceding stage of knowledge below it, and that for every favor there are “bearers”? So they have ranks of honor with their Lord; each entrusted with what he has earned through his knowledge. Thus He gives of His bounty to every meritorious person, and out of His justice towards His creatures, and His loving mercy towards His servants!’

‘Then what you are calling to,’ asked the young man, ‘is it this infinitely divine favor towards all creatures, or is it something else?’

The scholar responded: ‘It is not exactly the same as that favor, nor is it something other than that. Rather, what I'm calling you to, is related to this favor, as a handful of water is related to the vast extent of the Euphrates! I am calling you to the religion which Allaah approved for His servants and which He ennobled on the day He made it complete:

(Al-yawma akmaltu lakum deenakum wa atmamtu 'alaykum ni'mati wa radeetu lakumu al-islama deena)
"This day, I have perfected your religion for you, completed My Favour upon you, and have chosen for you Islam as your religion."

It is sublime, protected, noble, hidden. He has ennobled it with His rewards in honor of those who uphold it, and rebuked those with painful punishments who neglect it, making them a sign of dishonor. Its outward dimension is formal prescriptions, and its inner aspect is the various levels of knowledge. Its authority is illuminating and its justice most clear. This is what I am calling you to!’

The young man asked: ‘But what singled you out for this affair instead of others, and made you more deserving of it than others, since you just claimed that it comes from Allaah to all creation in general? Indeed everyone lays claim to it (for himself or his own group), as opposed to others. So what has made you all more deserving of the name “the truth” than those others, when this affair only came to you in the same way it came to them?’

The scholar replied: ‘There is not a single person who speaks a word of truth, that has not been taken from the same sources from which we have taken it. So they are in agreement with us about that word of truth, since we both affirm it. But then they separated from us by abandoning the sources of that very truth, while we persevered in our allegiance to them by obeying them. Through our perseverance we held on to it, while they let go of it and abandoned it. Hence they agreed with us in their affirmation, but differed from us in their actions. Thus, what they affirm bears witness against them, but bears witness in our favor, and their abandonment caused their need for us and the need for what we possess. They are like the brothers who inherited enormous wealth from their father and divided it equally among themselves. Then some of them lost their wealth and fell into poverty, while the others economized and traded with it, and multiplied their wealth many times over. The impoverished brothers were then forced to come to the rich ones and serve them and ask for their help. Likewise those who have lost the path of their messengers, have been forced by their ignorance to seek knowledge from those of noble sources. So the sign that indicates that we are more deserving of the truth than them, is their need for us and our self-sufficiency without them. That is how our scholar has come to invite the ignorant among them; and how our clothed ones have come to cover up the naked; and how our rich have come to feed the hungry. For the true right belongs to the one who has precedence and true merit (wajaba al-haqq al-faadhil), while precedence and true merit belongs to the one who acts kindly (wal-fadlu li-kulli dhee ni‘mah); and gratitude is due to the one who favors (wash-shukru li-kulli dhee ihsaan).’

‘Their greater merit and precedence over others has become clear,’ said the young man. ‘And as for your argument for the truth, that has been verified against the common people who have neglected the truth. But what is your argument against me? For I am affirming what you have mentioned, upholding what you have described, denying what you have denied, acknowledging the truth and its precedence, and loathing falsehood and its people!’

‘My argument against you,’ replied the scholar, ‘is similar to my argument against them, since even though you disagreed with them about the description of the truth, yet you actually agreed with them about its inner meaning.’

‘But how can that be?’ asked the young man, ‘since I am affirming that you hold the truth while others are denying it!’

‘Even if you disagreed with them by your affirmation of the truth,' the scholar continued, 'still you were in agreement with them in preventing access to it, when they prohibited it. For merely verbal affirmation is of no benefit without any repentance and reverting back to the truth; and denial does no harm to the person being denied when he has clear evidence.’

The young man exclaimed: ‘Then if I recognize them, I will turn to the people of the truth, or to a confirmed representative from them, and seek their truth! So tell me which of those (options) is possible for me.’

‘Can’t you see,’ replied the scholar, ‘that you’ve been forced to affirm the need for recognizing the possessor of truth or his representative, just as common people have been forced to affirm their need for the truth? So your actions may have been different from theirs, but you shared their ignorance!’

‘You’ve spoken accurately,’ said the young man, ‘so please explain that to me.’

‘Didn’t you just say,’ asked the scholar, ‘that you were confirming "my" argument against yourself and against the common people?’

‘Yes,’ he said.

‘Then how can anyone confirm a “truth” for themselves, which actually belongs to someone else?’ asked the Scholar.

‘Nobody is able to do that,’ he said.

‘So now that you have confirmed "my" argument against you and against the common people,’ demanded the scholar, ‘nothing else remains but to surrender to Allaah and turn back to the One you need, and who is self-sufficient without you.’

‘I do admit my need,’ said the young man, ‘But as for the one who is self-sufficient without me, I am still searching for him, so have mercy on me!’

The scholar asked: ‘Have you ever noticed that when the poor helplessly turns to a generous man, he receives a favor from him in only two ways. Either "earning" through work, or through that wealthy man’s gracious "generosity"?’

‘Yes,’ replied the young man, ‘he receives favors either by earning it or through an act of generosity.’

The scholar asked: ‘And is the merit of those who received, through an act of charity anything like the merit of those who actually earned it?’

‘No,’ he admitted, ‘it couldn’t be!’

‘So seek this distinction through action!’ said the scholar, ‘and you will reach it because you will have earned it. But don’t seek it through flattery, for if you try to obtain it as some undeserved favor, you will be excluded from its merit.’

The young man responded: ‘You are the key to the good and the one who is pointing to it. So show me the way to whichever path is superior by its essence and closer to guiding me rightly. For my great need for your generosity has captivated me!’

‘My dear son,’ said the scholar, ‘May Allaah not estrange you from the good (laa bu‘da lak), and there is no blame for you (wa laa tathreeba ‘alayk). What you hope from me is coming to you. But it has limits you must not overstep, and conditions you must not forget to follow.’

‘Set whatever limits you wish and impose whatever conditions you think best,’ declared the young man, ‘for I hope that you will find me patient and grateful for your kindness.’

‘The first of these limits,’ said the scholar, ‘is to fulfill the outward aspect of the Book and its revealed paths, acting upon it in accordance with your knowledge. For who acts for Allaah’s sake based on their knowledge will be guided by Allaah to that which he does not know.’

‘But the revealed books are many,’ the young man asked, ‘and they all are from Allaah. Each book has a large group of people adhering to it, so they all are in agreement about upholding them and worshipping in accordance with them.’

‘But now we’ve returned to what we were saying before,’ the scholar remarked, ‘and we are forcing ourselves to argue in confirmation of what we don’t really need to confirm!’

‘And how is that?’ asked the young man.

‘Because of what you said about there being many books, all coming from Allaah, and each having a large group of followers,’ explained the scholar. ‘So then do you believe that Allaah’s words and His books invalidate each other, or that the first book denies the last, or the last one denies the first?’

‘No, I don’t believe that!’ exclaimed the young man. ‘But then what is the argument regarding this?’

‘If people acted in accordance with what was revealed in the first book,’ replied the scholar, ‘it would lead them to the second one. And if they acted in accordance with the second, it would lead them to the third; until in the end, they would have arrived at the latest of the books. For the latest book is more deserving of being followed than the ones that came before it— even though they all are from Allaah — because the latest one is more recent in time and clearer in its way of proceeding, since it has replaced what was before it, and nothing has come after it to replace it.’

‘You have spoken truly,’ said the young man, ‘and you’ve made clear how we should acknowledge the truth and the signs of truthfulness. Now I will uphold the limits you’ve ordered me to keep, but what are your conditions for me?’

‘I have five conditions for you’ replied the scholar:
1. Don’t neglect anything I’ve entrusted you with.
2. Don’t conceal anything from me if I ask you about it.
3. Don’t come looking for me to give you an answer whenever you have a question.
4. Don't ask me about anything until I bring it up with you.
5. Don’t speak about me to your father.’

‘I shall do everything that you have mentioned,’ the young man declared. ‘Indeed your conditions seem easy to me, in comparison to all that I am hoping to receive from you. But why the subterfuge with my father? Surely that is the greatest test and the most painful trial for me! Would you allow me to move away from my father?’

‘Moving away from him,’ explained the scholar, ‘would create a reason to point fingers at someone else (i.e., the scholar and his mission). Instead you must stay with him, properly respect him, try to please him by being agreeable with him when you can. And you must protect your innermost self and keep your (spiritual) concerns secret from him. For surely Allaah will make up for his, and other people's hostility.’
