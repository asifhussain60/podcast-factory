## The two strands of gratitude

The boy answers the father's reproach not by denying it but by offering him a choice. *Contrast pair*, the boy's two strands of gratitude attribute by attribute.

The father's reproach in the boy's own framing: *I have no need to be so unkind to you in our day that I should make you the object of one of two strands of gratitude due to you*.

Column A: Outward courtesy

- The boy's posture: the obedient son
- What the boy gives: the courtesies and favors due from a young son to the elder who raised him
- The doctrinal name in the school: *taqiyya*, the protective concealment of chapters one through four
- The doctrinal form: outward agreement while hearts differ within
- What it costs the boy: the inward speech is kept secret from the father; the chain's teaching is not opened
- What it preserves: the household's peace; the elder's expectations of the son

Column B: Open debate

- The boy's posture: the truth-bearer
- What the boy gives: confidence and candor; the right to be drawn upon for what he knows
- The doctrinal name in the school: the lifting of *taqiyya* in the moment a partner can carry it
- The doctrinal form: outward agreement matched by inward agreement; the underpinnings made the same
- What it costs the boy: nothing of the truth; the *taqiyya* is opened by his own gift
- What it preserves: the father's mind, given full access to the boy's

Shared row: both strands are gratitude, both are owed, both are offered. The boy is not refusing the protective concealment the school has built; he is making the concealment itself a gift the partner may decline. The father is given the choice no one had given the boy in chapter one.

The father's reply, verbatim:

> As for your courtesy on the outside, while hearts differ within, this is a reproach between me and you. How long can outward agreement stand when its underpinnings differ? I do not want that. As for your debate and your candor, you have that, so say what seems best to you.
> Chapter prose, line 19.

The father has read the *taqiyya* the boy is offering and rejected it; the chapter five conversation opens by the father's own consent.

Anchor: chapter prose, lines 15 through 21.

## The boy's counter-bind on the father

A *contrast pair* that has the structural shape of a binary trap. The boy poses two readings of his father's forty years of withholding; in *both* cases, the boy's crossing to the stranger is defensible.

Column A: Either you are a scholar

- What this implies the father has been: a possessor of the teaching the boy is owed as a son
- What this implies the father did: withheld it from the boy
- The boy's standing in this case: vindicated; *do not blame me if you forbid me from seeking my own salvation through another*
- The cost to the father: the failure of his teaching duty, registered by his own admission

Column B: Or you are ignorant

- What this implies the father has been: not a possessor of the teaching
- What this implies the father did: also needed the stranger, since the teaching was nowhere else available
- The boy's standing in this case: ahead of the father; the boy has already crossed over to what the father, too, would have to seek
- The cost to the father: he is now behind, and the boy will *precede* him on the path because of the father's age

Shared row: in either case the boy's crossing was defensible; the only question is whether the father follows him or condemns him. There is no third case; the boy's enumeration is exhaustive.

The father's break in the source's own voice:

> The shaykh's heart was rent by the boy's words, and he knew there was no escape for him from all of these arguments, and his eyes overflowed with tears.
> Chapter prose, line 29.

The father then concedes doctrinally, binding himself by the boy's own logic to accept the truth if true and reject the falsehood if false, *out of compassion*. He has crossed over by his own consent.

## The community's three reasons for hesitation

The community arrives before Abu Malik with anger ready, but it names three reasons that hold them back from acting on the anger alone. A *contrast pair* between what the anger demanded and what the three reasons require: the same community, two postures pulled against each other.

Column A: What the anger demanded

- Standing of the boy and the father: defectors who have crossed to a stranger's call
- Posture toward them: estrangement, accusation, refusal
- Action contemplated: *to call them unbelievers, refute their opinion, and decide what we should do with their blood and their property*
- The community's own stance toward truth: settled in advance; no inspection needed
- The figure the community would become if it acted on the anger: the *tyrants of the previous nations* Abu Malik names next

Column B: What the three reasons require

- Reason one: al-Bakhtari's long-standing rights of brotherhood over the community; *we fear estrangement after intimacy, and we hate division after brotherhood*
- Reason two: if the new community is on the truth, the community has failed to follow them and help them in it
- Reason three: if the new community has gone astray, the community has failed to push them away and support the truth and its people
- Posture the three reasons require: inspection, fair-eye reading, consultation with the head of their religion
- The figure the community would become if it acted on the three reasons: seekers in the third face, by the chapter's later naming

Shared row: the community has come *prepared for blood* and is asking, by its own three reasons, to be turned. Abu Malik's *cooling* answers exactly that request. The lethal turn is averted before any meeting.

Anchor: chapter prose, lines 49 through 53.

## The figure of the breach

Abu Malik's first long speech contains the *comparison matrix* on which his refusal to rule by anger rests. Three domains of breach, four attribute columns. Cells are concrete; no hedges.

| Domain of the breach | Repairability | Stake to the bearer | What haste in judgment costs |
|---|---|---|---|
| Food | Repairable; *its harm is easy to remedy and its cure is near at hand* | Limited to the meal | Little; the breach is fixed by the next correction |
| Medicine | Not repairable; the breach in medicine *does not dismiss its owner nor leave him to himself* | The bearer's body | The breach cannot be exchanged for another; the consequence stays with the body |
| Religion | Gravely damaging; *its flaw cannot be repaired, nor exchanged for another* | The bearer's soul; the soul of the community | The gravest cost of all: haste in religion is graver than haste in anything else |

The figure does the work Abu Malik needs: the community has come *with anger ready*; Abu Malik holds them back by naming the asymmetry across the three domains. A medical breach already does not admit exchange; a religious breach is *of that kind*, only graver. *We cannot judge them by error and so wrong them, nor can we judge them by guidance and so wrong ourselves by imitating them; nor can we say they have been deceived and refuted without knowledge of them*.

## Abu Malik's three states of the new community

A *hierarchy tree* with three named branches from a root decision. Each branch is differentiated by a named warrant and a named doctrinal consequence.

- Root: *reflect on the affairs of these people, for they cannot be free of one of three states*
  - State one: same religion, new literature
    - What the new community holds: what the old creed already knows
    - What is added: *literature, hadiths, poetry, and the abundant words of people*
    - Doctrinal consequence: no rupture; the new community is the old creed with new wordings
    - Warrant: the canonical content is unchanged
  - State two: an addition that is good
    - What the new community holds: what the old creed already knows, *plus an increase in what they hold in their religion*
    - What is added: knowledge itself, in which the holders have rejoiced
    - Doctrinal consequence: the addition is to be welcomed; *knowledge with every believer is a lost thing, and an increase of knowledge is better than a decrease*
    - Warrant: knowledge is the believer's lost property; recovering it is gain, not threat
  - State three: a new prophecy, brought to them
    - What the new community holds: *guidance other than what they have, by the prophecy of a prophet who has been brought to them*
    - What is added: a new speaking source, called for clearly, followed clearly
    - Doctrinal consequence: *this is not denied by the command of God*; the door of new prophecy is not foreclosed in this age
    - Warrant: *God is at work every day*; the creation has not exhausted the divine decree

The striking move: the senior scholar of the old creed himself voices, as a live possibility, the doctrinal space the new community's call has always claimed in its own right. The three-states is *granted in advance* by the man who has come to judge them.

Anchor: chapter prose, lines 63 through 67.

## The tyrants of the previous nations

Abu Malik names the figure that holds the *blood and property* line back. An *annotated structure* on a single named pattern: what the tyrants of the previous nations did, what they said, what their judgment cost the world.

The whole, in Abu Malik's voice:

> We would then be like the tyrants of the previous nations, who fancied their doctrines in their judgment and rejoiced in their knowledge, until they thought they had encompassed all that God had commanded, taught, and intended to create. They said God would not send messengers after their messengers, and they did not consider that beyond what they had attained there was knowledge or doctrine. So they passed judgment that God should cut off His argument against His creation and His viceregents in His land.
> Chapter prose, line 75.

The parts of the pattern, named in order:

| Element of the pattern | What it names | What it costs |
|---|---|---|
| *They fancied their doctrines in their judgment* | The settled interior; doctrine held as already complete | The interior closes against any further teaching |
| *They rejoiced in their knowledge* | The pride that takes the present holding as the limit of the field | The seeker disposition is foreclosed |
| *They thought they had encompassed all* | The claim of doctrinal totality | The field of possible knowledge is collapsed to what they already hold |
| *They said God would not send messengers after their messengers* | The doctrinal denial of any new prophecy | The door of new prophecy, kept open by the three states, is shut from below |
| *They passed judgment that God should cut off His argument* | The community arrogates to itself the closure of the divine argument | The tyrants killed the prophets of God |

The doctrinal punch in Abu Malik's mouth: he has just held the *blood and property* line *back* from *exactly* the same logic. *I ask you, by God: do not follow the whims of a people who have gone astray before you*.

## The door of seeking and the sound heart

Abu Malik tightens the community's request. They have asked to be *taken* to the new community to *look into* its doctrine. Abu Malik refuses the half-measures and lays out the discipline by which seeking actually happens. A *comparison matrix* on the three postures the community tries, in order, and what each one is named.

Frame: *how then is the request to be made, if we do not deny falsehood and believe the truth?*

| Posture the community tries | Abu Malik's name for it | Why it fails | What it forecloses |
|---|---|---|---|
| *Look at it, accept the reasonable, avoid the rest* | *Doubt on your part about what you hold* | Doubt does not know its own right; it cannot know what is wrong about another | The seeker has no standing-place from which to discern |
| *Stand firm on our truth, lie about whatever falsehood* | Not how meaning is sought; *there is no guidance through lying* | Lying about the field cuts the field's signs from the seeker's discernment | The signs of truth and falsehood cannot self-disclose |
| *We are convinced and we exempt ourselves from the request* | The refusal to seek; *worse than humility-in-seeking* | *If you will not consent to be in need in the houses of the seekers, then you will sit in ignorance in the houses of the seekers* | The third face is foreclosed entirely; the community is left in the second |

The chapter's positive doctrine of *the sound heart*, named verbatim by Abu Malik:

> The sound heart is the heart that does not persist in denying what is said to it, nor in being, even, the truth is what unveils itself and shows its merit, and falsehood is what reveals its own nakedness. Then the sound heart discerns falsehood by its intellect and accepts the truth by acceptance.
> Chapter prose, line 91.

The doctrine: both truth and falsehood self-disclose; the sound heart is *not the heart that knows in advance*; it is the *heart that does not persist in denying*. The sound heart is the discipline that lets the disclosure land.

Anchor: chapter prose, lines 79 through 91.

## Reverence before the statement; the petitioner before the king

The community asks a fine question. Is reverence due *after* the teaching has shown its right, or *before*? Abu Malik answers with the chapter's most beautiful piece of teacher-doctrine. A *contrast pair* on the two possible orderings, with Abu Malik's verdict.

Frame: *has this reverence and humility for what is sought to be shown after it has shown its right, or before the statement is given?*

Column A: Reverence after the statement (the community's reflex)

- When the reverence is paid: after the teaching has proven itself
- What the reverence is paid to: the verified teacher
- What it protects against: the risk of revering an unworthy teacher
- What it loses if the teaching turns out unworthy: nothing; the reverence never landed
- What it costs the seeker: the *statement* itself cannot be grasped without reverence; the reverence comes too late
- The petitioner-before-the-king image: the petitioner refuses to bow until the king has given; the king will not give to one who has not bowed

Column B: Reverence before the statement (Abu Malik's verdict)

- When the reverence is paid: before the teaching is given
- What the reverence is paid to: God's command that the seeker humble; not the teacher's deserving
- What it protects: the seeker's standing as seeker; the disposition without which knowledge will not enter
- What it loses if the teaching turns out unworthy: nothing; *it was reverence to God and humility before His command, and you will have its reward, even if the one before whom you humbled yourself did not deserve it*
- What it costs the seeker: the formal posture; humility, persistence, patience in adversity
- The petitioner-before-the-king image: the petitioner asks only with reverence; if the king gives, the king has fulfilled his grace; if the king gives nothing, the petitioner has still discharged the duty of asking

Shared row: in both columns the verdict on the teacher is what it is. The difference is the seeker's standing. *The seeker has fulfilled the duty of the request, even if he has not fulfilled the duty of the truth*. The doctrine *frees the seeker* from *needing to know in advance that the teacher is true* before humbling himself.

Anchor: chapter prose, lines 97 through 103.

## Only this day

A small *process flow* on the chapter's pacing of the community's surrender. The community offers Abu Malik *three days* to act on what he has commanded; Abu Malik tightens to *two*; then to *one*. The collapse is doctrinal, not theatrical.

```
Community: "We will do what you have commanded, over three days."
   ↓ Abu Malik: "No."
Community: "Two days, then?"
   ↓ Abu Malik: "Only this day, if you will do it; otherwise I go on alone."
Community: "We repent to Allah from every sin, and we put aside what is owed; and we do what you have commanded, there is no power except in Allah."
   ↓
They went out together to the gate of al-Bakhtari that same day.
```

The doctrinal force: *seeking is not scheduling; seeking is now*. The community, *without knowing the inward meaning*, has invoked the closing formula *there is no power except in Allah*, the same formula whose inner meaning the chain has carried since chapter three. They have *crossed by their own mouth*.

Anchor: chapter prose, lines 107 through 111.

## The three faces of seeking

The chapter's central *comparison matrix*. Three rows, four named-attribute columns. Cells filled with the chapter's own commitments.

The frame Salih puts to Abu Malik: *the seeking is of three faces: a seeker who knows, a seeker who does not know, and a seeker who knows that he does not know. Which of the three are you?*

| Face | Knowledge state | Awareness of lack | Action the seeker takes | Object the seeker pursues |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| The seeker who knows | Already possesses the knowledge | Not applicable; there is no lack | Goes out into the world | The dead-in-their-ignorance: students to be revived |
| The seeker who does not know | Lacks the knowledge | Unaware of the lack | None; the unawareness forecloses motion | Nothing; the face is stalled at the root |
| The seeker who knows that he does not know | Lacks the knowledge | Aware of the lack | Asks the scholars; humbles himself before what he needs | Knowledge from those who carry it |

Salih's own naming of the first face is the chapter's strongest editorial move: *the seeker who knows* is *the divine scholar*. The first face is not the *student*; the first face is the *teacher who seeks his students*. The third face is where Abu Malik places himself: *I am the knowing seeker who knows nothing but knows that he is in need*.

Anchor: chapter prose, lines 145 through 157.

## Money-seeking and knowledge-seeking, the source's own parallel

The chapter delivers the three-faces taxonomy a second time, in a second register, as a *comparison matrix* of two domains. The source's own pedagogical move; the cells are named in the prose.

Frame from Salih: *money is the treasure of the people of this world, and knowledge is the treasure of the people of the Hereafter*.

| Mode of seeking | In money-seeking | In knowledge-seeking |
|---|---|---|
| First, by right | The seeker who *asks for what is his by right*; the debt is owed without terms | *The divine scholar*, the *seeker-who-knows*, who goes out to *revive the dead in their ignorance* |
| Second, on terms | The seeker who *asks for what is owed on terms*; the debt admits of an agreed condition | *The learned man*, the *knowing seeker* who *has mastered certain degrees and seeks the full end and limit* |
| Third, as alms | The seeker who *asks for alms*; the request is grounded in *acknowledged need* | *The ignorant who knows he is in need*; he *knows nothing beyond knowing that he is in need*; so he *asks the scholars in order to know* |

Shared row: both domains move from claim-of-right at the top, through claim-with-condition in the middle, to claim-from-need at the bottom. The doctrinal weight is concentrated at the third row in *both* domains: alms-seeking and need-driven asking are not lesser; they are the only mode the third face can use, and the chapter's whole pedagogical settlement is that this third mode is *the door of seeking* itself.

Anchor: chapter prose, lines 151 through 155.

## Knowledge by report; knowledge by direct sight

A *contrast pair* embedded inside the third face. The chapter sub-divides the *seeker who knows that he does not know* by *how* he came to know his ignorance.

Frame from Salih: *did you come to know, or did you come to know-by-sight? What is the difference between the two?*

Column A: Knowledge of ignorance by report

- How the seeker learns of his lack: a scholar tells him
- Source of the knowledge: another's word; news; testimony
- Standing of the knowledge: derivative; held by report
- The seeker's interior: not yet straitened by his own lack
- Example in the chapter: the ignorant man who *does not know that he is ignorant until he meets a scholar*, and learns of his ignorance *by the scholar's word*

Column B: Knowledge of ignorance by direct sight

- How the seeker learns of his lack: he sees it in himself
- Source of the knowledge: the seeker's own mind; the *straitening of the chest* by ignorance
- Standing of the knowledge: first-hand; *his ignorance has itself become a knowledge of his mind*
- The seeker's interior: distressed; hopes for no relief except by knowledge of what he is ignorant of
- Example in the chapter: the man who *already knows in his own mind that he is asking about something but does not know the face of the answer*

Shared row: in both cases the seeker arrives at the same place, the third face. But only Column B carries the body's witness to the mind's ignorance. Salih reads Abu Malik's interior by *the signs of distress*, then names the verdict: *by this, I know that you are in need*. Abu Malik's confession lands: *I find within myself everything you have just described*.

The chapter's positive doctrine of knowledge, named verbatim by Salih:

> Ignorance is a need, poverty is the narrowness of need, and distress is a great need that compels the creature to seek capacity; and capacity is the breadth of knowledge, because knowledge is wide.
> Chapter prose, line 163.

Anchor: chapter prose, lines 159 through 167.

## The transpositions at the gate

An *annotated structure* on the figures Abu Malik names as he steps onto the threshold. The whole is a single sentence the source delivers verbatim; the parts are four named transpositions; the axes are the two registers the call has always carried.

Frame the source uses: *these are three qualities whose meanings have been transposed*. The source names *three* and enumerates *four*; the count and the contents are both preserved.

The two axes of every transposition:

- *zahir*, the apparent; what the words say on their face in the world
- *batin*, the inward; what the words mean inside the chain's reading

The four transpositions named at the gate:

| Transposition | The *zahir* reading | The *batin* reading |
|---|---|---|
| *the child has become the father* | The biological son and father of the household | The son, by his crossing, has become the spiritual father of the man who raised him |
| *the Lord is in the earth* | A claim about location | The generous one of chapter three, the one whose right is binding, is *present* in the world through the chain |
| *the Lord has a servant come to Him from him* | Two unrelated agents | The cause comes from the generous one and returns to him; the chain's both-ways doctrine |
| *the one who created annihilation has become eternal* | A paradox at face value | The mortal boy is, as *the righteous one*, eternal in his rank; mortality has been carried *into* the eternal |

The chapter's discipline at this moment: the figures' *axes* (the *zahir* and *batin* registers) are named in the deck; the *content* the chain reads through those axes, the names and ranks of the generous one and his servants, is not laid out as a hierarchy on the slide. The chapter announces these *as a whiff of perfume to perplexed students*; the deck honors that register. The structure is the *pair of axes*, not a chart of the divine names.

Al-Bakhtari's verbatim closing line on the figure of *Salih*:

> Salih does not die and will never die. It is no figure of speech, the eternity remains.
> Chapter prose, line 119.

## The essence-traders and the jewel-or-glass

A *contrast pair* on what knowledge is, with Abu Malik's *jewel-figure* taken up by Salih and *reversed*.

Frame from Abu Malik: *the words of wisdom are like the jewel, if you turn it over, its light shines, and if you set it aside, its bounty does not fade*.

Frame from Salih: *I believed you, but that is only if the jewel is indeed an essence, and not a camouflaged glass that has no essence hidden in it*.

Column A: Knowledge as essence (true jewel)

- What it is: the genuine article; the substance the figure names
- How it certifies itself: by direct sight to the people of insight; *the people of insight give thanks for it and compete for it*
- What it survives: testing in the forge; *the essence-traders and the people of metals* test it and do not refuse it
- Its price-behavior: rises with its own preciousness; *reaches every sum*
- Who recognizes it: *those who know the science of heaven*

Column B: Knowledge as camouflage (glass dressed as jewel)

- What it is: a likeness; a surface that has *no essence hidden in it*
- How it presents itself: as essence to the unaided eye; *the world is full of camouflage*
- What it fails: testing in the forge; *the essence-traders point out its defects and the camouflage falls from the limits of essence*
- Its price-behavior: collapses under inspection; *it falls from the limits of essence*
- Who recognizes the failure: the same people who recognize the true essence, *the people of the science of heaven*

Shared row: the test is not internal to either column. *Knowledge is not knowledge until it is presented to those who know the science of heaven. If they accept it, it is true knowledge; and if they do not accept it, it is nonsense*. The applicant cannot certify the essence himself; he must carry it before the testers in the forge.

Anchor: chapter prose, lines 181 through 191.

## The Maqrub case; the lost code of the age

The chapter's most concrete legal indictment. A man named Maqrub came to Abu Malik with a single concrete question about a broken oath of divorce. A *comparison matrix* on what Abu Malik gave him, what each ruling implied, and what the man went home with.

The frame: Maqrub said *I swore an oath of divorce and broke it*. He asked Abu Malik for the ruling.

| Ruling Abu Malik gave | The ruling's implication | What Maqrub goes home to |
|---|---|---|
| *Some people say she is forbidden to you and not lawful for you until she marries another husband* | The marriage is dissolved by the broken oath; remarriage is required after an intervening husband | The household is *not lawful*; the woman is *another man's wife* until the procedure is followed |
| *Some people say she is still your wife and is not forbidden to you by a single divorce* | The oath is a single divorce; the marriage stands | The household is *lawful*; the woman is *his wife* |
| *Act on whichever of the two you wish* | The petitioner chooses, on no doctrinal basis | The petitioner does *not know* whether the woman in his house is his wife or another man's |

Salih's verbatim indictment:

> Yet he does not know whether what he goes home to is lawful or forbidden. Is this how rulings work in the religion of God? Is this how God's lawful and forbidden are determined?
> Chapter prose, line 211.

Abu Malik's confession is the chapter's pivot, given verbatim:

> The inherited code of my age has been lost, corrupted, and disrupted, and we hold on only to the name of religion. What is the state of your religion?
> Chapter prose, line 215.

The senior scholar of the old creed, by his own most respected scholar's mouth, has reduced the inherited tradition of his age to its name.

Anchor: chapter prose, lines 209 through 217.

## What is religion? Salih's collapse argument

A *hierarchy tree* on Abu Malik's definition of religion, and the collapse Salih draws from it once the Maqrub case has been confessed.

Abu Malik's definition, given verbatim: *command and prohibition, the lawful and the forbidden, the prophetic norm and the obligations*. Six elements; all legal.

The structure of the argument, branch by branch:

- Root claim from Salih: *what is religion, O Abu Malik?*
  - Abu Malik's enumeration: command and prohibition, the lawful and the forbidden, the prophetic norm and the obligations
    - Salih's follow-up: *what is the inherited legal code, in your view, other than this?*
    - Abu Malik's reply: *I do not know of anything beyond what I have described to you*
  - Salih's conclusion drawn from the enumeration: *so you have combined religion and the inherited legal code into a single thing?*
    - Abu Malik: *yes*
  - The collapse, premise by premise:
    - Premise A: religion equals the inherited legal code (Abu Malik's own definition)
    - Premise B: the inherited legal code is lost, corrupted, disrupted (Abu Malik's own confession on the Maqrub case)
    - Conclusion: religion, *as defined by Abu Malik*, is lost
  - Salih's verbatim closing question: *then religion has been disrupted by the suspension of the inherited code, and corrupted by its corruption, what is left with you?*

The chapter records Abu Malik's silence. *For a while he sat thinking about the matter of his religion. Then he raised his head, and said: I do not see that anything is left with me. So what religion do you have?*

The *long silence* and the *naked confession* are the chapter's *zero point*. The senior scholar of the old creed has arrived at *nothing-is-left*, and *now asks Salih* what Salih has. The deck's next slide answers him.

Anchor: chapter prose, lines 221 through 231.

## Religion as the unbroken cause

The chapter's *positive doctrine*, delivered after Abu Malik's *I do not see that anything is left with me*. An *annotated structure*: a single whole, *the unbroken cause that connects heaven to earth*, dissected into named figures, each anchored in revelation.

The whole, named by Salih in the chapter's recurring thesis:

> Religion is the cause that connects heaven to earth, unbroken, the loop of the firm handhold, the rope of those who hold fast, the ark of tranquility and the ship of life and the light of life, generous in its content, safeguarded in its sciences, marked in its jewels, its virtue hidden from no one who looks upon it.
> Chapter prose, lines 235 through 247.

The whole and its parts:

| Figure | What the figure names | Anchor in revelation |
|---|---|---|
| The cause that connects heaven to earth, unbroken | Religion as the chain that runs between the two, with no broken link | The chain doctrine carried across the chapter |
| The loop of the firm handhold | The unbreakable grasp by which the believer holds fast | The verse of the chapter on the Cow: *whoever rejects the false gods and believes in God has grasped the firmest handhold, which never breaks* |
| The rope of those who hold fast | The shared cord stretched out by God, held together by the community | The verse of the chapter on the Family of Imran: *hold fast, all together, to the rope which God stretches out for you, and do not be divided* |
| The ark of tranquility | The vessel of calm; the saving ship of the household | The Prophet's saying that the people of his house are as the ark of Noah: *whoever boards it is saved, and whoever stays back drowns* |
| The ship of life | The vessel that carries the believer across the sea of the world | The figure of the ark carried into the believer's living passage |
| The light of life | The light that illumines the seeker's living | The verse of the chapter on Light: *God is the light of the heavens and the earth, the parable of His light is as a niche within which is a lamp, the lamp enclosed in glass* |
| Generous in its content | The content is given, not stinted | The chapter three doctrine of the generous one, carried into the chain's giving |

The figures are not free invention. Each carries an anchor in revelation that the chain has read as a *named figure for the chain itself*. The four central names (*handhold*, *rope*, *ark*, *light*) are four names of the one thing the chain has always claimed to be.

## The chapter's closing seam

A single hanging question, drawn from the chapter's last line. *Not* a summary; *not* a takeaway; the chapter's own ending discipline carried into the deck.

Abu Malik has granted the doctrine. He has also held to its lostness in this age. He now poses the conjunction as a single question, addressed to Salih:

> If God has a religion by which He will judge on the Day, with which He is pleased, and for which He rewards and punishes, then in this sense it exists. But now, since it has been lost, the attribute is preferable to him, so how is it described?
> Chapter prose, lines 251 through 253.

The deck ends on the question. The next chapter answers it; this chapter does not. The reader carries the question.

Anchor: chapter prose, lines 251 through 257.
