# World, Hereafter, and the Right of Wealth

*This chapter covers the third long teaching of* The Book of the Master and the Boy *(*Kitāb al-ʿĀlim wa-l-Ghulām*, ki-TAAB al-AA-lim wal-ghu-LAAM), attributed to Sayyidina Ja'far ibn Mansur al-Yaman (sai-yi-DEE-nah JAH-far ibn man-SOOR al-YAH-man). After the cosmological architecture of chapter two — the three words and the seven principles, the seven heavens and the seven* nāṭiqs *(NAA-tiq — speaking prophets), the doctrine of* zahir *(ZAA-hir — the apparent) and* batin *(BAA-tin — the inward), the parable of the egg with its shell, white, and yolk — the boy turns to* application*. He names the pairs the cosmology has put in place — world and Hereafter, soul and body, knowledge of the apparent and knowledge of the subtle — and the Master walks them outward into ethics: the three houses of worship (angels, humans, beasts) and their three knowledges; the two kinds of people; the great station of God's saints that no one can give to another; the figure of the sower who prepares and waters but cannot bring out the plant. The Master names, for the first time, the* rope of God *as the* hujja *(/ˈħudʒːa/) and the* bāb *(/baːb/) of the Imam, then halts at the* rope of the rope of Allah *— "the end of your guidance and the end of the worlds." He drops the* ta'wīl *(/taʔˈwiːl/) of* la hawla wa la quwwata illa billah *("there is no power except in Allah"): the* hawl *(/ħawl/) is the Imam who* turns *(*yuḥawwil*, /juˈħawwil/) the Creator's words into the people's words; the* quwwa *(/ˈquwːa/) is his* hujja *and* bāb*; the* ʿuṣba *(/al ˈʕusˤba/) in the Joseph-story is the ten arguments; Joseph's eleven planets, sun, and moon are the twelve captains, the Imam, and his* hujja*. He gives the* batin *of Pharaoh's dream — seven fat cows = the causes of God; seven lean = the seven* nāṭiqs*; seven green ears = al-Khidr (/al ˈxidˤr/), a cause between God and the* awliyāʾ *(/ʔawliˈjaːʔ/); seven dry = the guardians — and the four meanings of "the life of this world" (perishing vs lasting; outward knowledge vs inward knowledge). He gives the verdict on the one who has the inward without the outward, and on the one who has the outward without the inward — both standings are bad; only the one who establishes both is a true believer. The narrator records the Master beaming and weeping when the boy describes his brethren — the scholars yellow with vigil and weak with fasting, who refuse the world even from what is not forbidden. The Master pivots to the question of why* passions *differ when origin (Adam from dust) and instrument (the senses) are the same; the answer is justice — each archer aims at the same target with the same instrument, but they differ in* striking*. He gives the doctrine of* classes in this world *and* degrees in religion *with the Qur'anic warrant of* Q 4:69 *("And whoever obeys Allah and His Messenger — those will be with the ones upon whom Allah has bestowed favor: the prophets, the truthful, the martyrs, and the righteous"); the limit of effort ("the full measure of one's knowledge and the utmost reach of one's capacity"); the duty of the body (the golden rule restated as* justice*); and the duty of wealth: take from a lawful source, spend in its rightful place, pay the* zakat *(za-KAAT) as* a fifth of the best of it*. The chapter climaxes when the boy enacts the doctrine: he places all his wealth in the Master's hands in* five shares *— zakat, expiation, thanksgiving, the share of his brethren, and the share the Master should take — and asks the Master to join every one of them to himself. The Master, in tears, refuses his own share and reapportions the rest as* trusts held for their rightful owners. *The boy asks who this generous one is, and the chapter, for the first time, speaks of the Imam directly: "the one whose right Allah has made binding upon me and upon every believer, in whose hand are the keys of the heavens, whose palms are opened with the light of Sinai (SEE-nigh)." The Master names himself as "his son and one of his servants." The chapter closes on the boy's request to be taken to the Imam's door, and the Master's careful answer: "This is your duty — but only with his permission and his decision."*

## Where this chapter picks up

Chapter two ended on the parable of the egg: shell (apparent), white (inward), yolk (its essence and meaning). The boy had been brought to the *shores of the seas of the Most High* and had asked to be returned to the sciences of this world, lest his mind wander into what it could not bear.

This chapter is the *return*. The boy does not ask for more cosmology. He asks for the *application* of the cosmology to his own life — to the world he must live in, the Hereafter he must work for, the body he must care for, and the wealth he must use. The shape of the chapter is the shape of his question. He opens by naming the *pairs* the Master has just put in place; the Master accepts the question and runs it outward through *ethics*; the boy receives each teaching and presses for the next application; and at the end the boy *enacts* the teaching — he disposes of his wealth in five shares, in front of the Master, and asks to be taken to the door of the one whose right has been named.

Two registers run through the chapter. The *ethical*: a long catechism answering *what then must I do?* across body, knowledge, work, wealth, gratitude. The *revelatory*: while teaching ethics, the chapter is also *unveiling* — for the first time in the book — the doctrine of the *Imam* as the rightful owner of the *zakat*, the one whose *hujja* is the *rope* the boy has been called to hold, the one whose *permission* the Master must seek before bringing the boy to his door. Chapter three is the *threshold* of the great revelation that the seventh-day naming of chapter four will complete.

## Pairs that point: world, Hereafter, soul, body

The boy opens with what has by now become his way of asking. He does not pose a single question; he names a *pattern* the Master has been showing him, and asks whether the pattern holds.

> Are not this world and the Hereafter a pair, and the soul and the body a pair, and the knowledge of the apparent and of the subtle a pair, and so on — many pairs, all of them?

The Master accepts the pattern but tightens it. *I accept. But are they pairs of a single kind?* The pairs are not all of *one* kind; they *point*, but they point in different ways.

The boy gives the rule the chapter rests on. *The pairs indicate the religion of Allah.* The pairs are *signs*; what they signify is the *religion*.

The Master receives the rule and gives the structural reading. *The pair: the outward is a name, and the inward is an attribute, which signifies the knowledge of God and the religion of God — which is the inner.* Every pair is a *name-and-attribute* pair whose inner term — the *attribute* — is what the *name* points to. And what every name and every attribute ultimately points to is *the knowledge of God and the religion of God*. Religion *is* the inner. This is the small but decisive move that opens the chapter.

## Three houses of worship; three knowledges; two kinds of people

The Master walks the doctrine outward.

> So too is the creation of the three houses of worship: first the creation of the angels, second the creation of human beings, and third the creation of the beasts. The apparent is the knowledge of the limit of beasts; whoever knows it without the secret is like the beasts. The knowledge of the inward is the knowledge of the children of Adam alone; whoever knows it is a believer, like a true human being. The knowledge of the inner of the inner is the knowledge of the angels; whoever knows it has become the spiritual counterpart of the physical knowledge of the body, and he is a prophet sent by God in His land, by whom He argues against His creation. He is the veil of the angels, the symbol of revelation and its interpreter for the children of Adam. With him are the keys of Paradise, which only those who obey him will enter; and with him is the permission for the fire, which none enter except by his staff.

The three orders of creature correspond to three kinds of knowledge. The *apparent* without the secret is the knowledge of *beasts*; the *inward* is the knowledge of the *children of Adam alone* — and *only at this rank is one truly a believer*; the *inner of the inner* is the knowledge of the *angels*, and the one who knows it is *a prophet sent by God in His land*. The figure is the figure of the *nāṭiq*. The keys of Paradise and the permission for the fire are not unmediated; the chain of bearers stands at the gates.

The Master then divides humanity in two. *The people are but two men.* On one side, *one who has taught the purpose of knowledge, whose soul has been animated by the spirit of certainty — he is a scholar by his knowledge and a master by his work*. On the other, *a learner, for his own survival*. The rest are *savages and a rabble*, *followers of every false guide*, *deceived* and *seduced* — and *they will not be left behind for what Allah has promised them*. Two ranks, and a remainder who do not count among the ranks at all.

## Can the boy aspire to the great station?

The boy asks the question of his life. *Will I attain this great station — the example of houses that are above them?* The Master steers carefully. *O my son, this is a station that God has honored above all houses and degrees, so that you can attain it only by virtuous work. If the soul does its work, and you seek it with honest certainty and a clear and conscious heart, I hope that you will realize from it what the saints of God, the righteous, have realized.*

The boy presses. *And who can aspire to attain the likeness of this station to which God raises His guardians?* The Master rebuffs the implication. *Their God is your God, and between God and anyone else there is closeness only by meeting Him and doing good deeds. The knowledge by which Allah has raised His guardians — you are at its beginning, and if you act upon it, you will realize its end.*

The boy makes the natural request. *Can you help me to that?* The Master refuses. *This is a place in which nothing avails except the work of its own people.* The station cannot be *received* from another. The boy presses again, and the Master gives the chapter's first great figure.

> The sower can prepare the land well, sow it, and water it — but he cannot bring out the plant and the leaves. A man can be a craftsman whenever he wills — but he cannot create from his work what he wills.

The Master can prepare the land, sow the seed, water — but the *growth* is not in his power. The figure walks the boy past the seductive thought that the Master can give him the station. The Master closes with a blessing and a directive. *So fear Allah and do what is best, for Allah does not waste the reward of the best of deeds. You are on the path of salvation … Hold fast to the rope to which you have been called, until you are guided by a rope from God to the rope of God, for it is the goal of the seekers.* The boy will press on the rope immediately.

## The rope of God and the rope of the rope of God

The boy hears the figure and asks the natural Qur'anic question. *Is it not the rope of Allah to which you have called me?* The Master makes the precise *da'wa* (DAH-wah — the call, the missionary apparatus of the Ismaili tradition) distinction.

> It is apparent to you. It is your rope and the most trustworthy link, your hujja (argument), and the door of your Imam.

The rope the boy has been called to is not the *rope of Allah* directly; it is the *rope* by which the boy is bound to *his hujja* and *the door of his Imam*. The boy presses for the next step. *Then what is the rope of Allah, and what is the rope of the rope of Allah?* The Master halts. *This is the end of your guidance and the end of the worlds.* The halt is doctrinal: the *batin-of-batin* that chapter two named as the *yolk*.

The boy humbles himself. *There is no power except in Allah. He has drowned in the depths of ignorance who has no evidence, just as he drowned in the seas of the world who has no ship.* The Master picks up the formula and turns it into the next teaching.

## No power except in Allah: the dropped *ta'wīl*

The Master takes *la hawla wa la quwwata illa billah* and reads it the way the *da'wa* reads everything — by going behind the surface to the meaning. He probes. *Why is it that you say, "There is no power except in Allah," and you do not say, "There is no stratagem except in Allah"?*

The boy gives the folk-etymology he has heard. *We have it narrated in our sciences that the ḥawl (year) is the same ḥawl as in Allah's words "two complete years" — meaning two years. And we have seen, and the public has shown, that "quwwa" (power) means mules — because Allah says, "And We brought him out from the treasures, since the keys to them would have been carried by a band of strong men (al-ʿuṣba)." They said: the ʿuṣba is forty men, and the quwwa is the mules; and it was that the keys of his treasures filled forty mules.* On this folk-reading, *there is no power except in Allah* would mean — comically — *there is no year and no mule except by Allah*. The Master frames the absurdity, and the boy admits the reading is *for the weak and the lowly* but has no better.

The Master opens with a structural rule. *Creation has not uttered any true word except that it has an origin in the subtle, and there is no false word except that it has a meaning. So I refer speech back to its meaning.* The *zahir* of speech is its surface; the *batin* of speech is its *origin in the subtle*. Then the *ta'wīl*.

> As for al-ḥawl, he is the Imam of Allah and His truthful Caliph, the lord of the twelve captains — just as the ḥawl, which is the year, is completed only in twelve months. But the Imam was called ḥawl because he turns (yuḥawwil) the words of the Creator gently into the words of the people, until they are understood. They then become apparent speech, and the words of wisdom acquire a body, while the words of the Creator carry within them the spirit of life and the light of salvation. The Imam was called al-ḥawl because of this converting of words, and he does not change it except with the permission of Allah.

The *hawl* is the *Imam*, *lord of the twelve captains* (the twelve *nuqabā'*, noo-qa-BAA — chiefs); the figure: the year completes in twelve months as the *da'wa* completes in twelve *nuqabā'* around each *nāṭiq*. The Imam is called *ḥawl* because *he turns* the words of the Creator into the words of the people — *gentle turning*, not word-for-word translation, so that what the Creator said in His mode may be understood in the mode of those addressed. He does not change the words *except with the permission of Allah*.

> And quwwa (power) is his hujja (argument) and his door, whom God has strengthened to bear the heavy burden of utterance. He takes the speech from the Imam as a whole and distributes it among the dā'īs (preachers) in detail, according to their houses. So he called the hujja "quwwa," for it is strengthened only by God. Therefore, whoever says "there is no power except in Allah" means: there is no Imam and no hujja except by Allah, and from Allah comes their light and their sustenance.

The *quwwa* is the *hujja* and his *door* (the *bāb*). Speech flows down the chain — God to Imam, Imam to *hujja*, *hujja* to *du'āt* (doo-AAT — summoners), *du'āt* to the people. The *ta'wīl* of the formula every Muslim utters daily: *there is no Imam and no hujja except by Allah, and from Allah comes their light and their sustenance*. The Master then completes the figure with the *ʿuṣba* the boy brought in from the Joseph-story.

> As for the ʿuṣba (the band), it is the first power: they are the ten arguments. Joseph too said to his father: "O my father, I saw eleven planets, and the sun and the moon — I saw them prostrating to me," meaning by the sun the Imam, by the moon his hujja and his door, and by the planets the twelve captains. Likewise, Joseph's brothers said: "Truly Joseph and his brother are dearer to our father than we are, though we are a band (ʿuṣba)" — meaning the twelve captains; let him choose a man from among us. … Yet the command of God was fulfilled in Yusuf (Yūsuf, /ˈjuːsuf/), and the outcome of their plot was remorse and repentance before him.

Joseph's dream in the *batin*: sun = Imam, moon = *hujja* and *bāb*, eleven planets (with Joseph the twelfth) = twelve captains. A Qur'anic dream the boy has heard since childhood turns out, in its *batin*, to be the same architecture the Master has been teaching him. The brothers' *remorse and repentance* is the figure's vindication.

## Pharaoh's dream: cosmology in a vision

The boy asks for another reading. *What is the meaning of the king's saying, "I see seven fat cows being eaten by seven lean ones, and seven green ears of grain and others dry"? What are they? And what is a dream-vision?*

The Master first frames the *dream-vision* itself. *As for the dream-vision, it is like that which appears as outward work — like a mirage that, when one comes upon it, one finds nothing in it. So too is the work of the outward without the inward: it is not accepted of him, however much it may be.* The *dream* has its *batin* the same way the *work* has its *batin*. Then the four elements.

> The seven fat cows are the causes of God between Him and His creation. The seven lean ones are the seven appointed speakers (nāṭiqs) who seek what comes from God through His seven causes. The seven green ears are al-Khidr, a cause between God and the guardians (awliyāʾ). The seven dry ones are the guardians, obliged to receive from God at the hands of these seven causes.

The dream's elements, *batin*-read: *fat cows* — the *causes* between God and creation, *generous and sustaining*; *lean cows* — the *seven nāṭiqs*, *lean* because they are *receivers* dependent on what they are given; *green ears* — *al-Khidr* as a cause between God and the *awliyāʾ*; *dry ears* — the *guardians* who receive *through* these causes. The dream Pharaoh saw is, on this reading, the architecture the boy now knows: *causes*, *nāṭiqs*, *al-Khidr*, *guardians*, *seven-and-seven*.

The boy presses for the *like of this among us*. The Master gives a brief answer. *This is the foundation of Islam, and its child is like a grain that sprouted seven ears — meaning that seven or eight imams came out of his loins.* The figure of the *single grain* sprouting *seven ears* is the figure of the *imam* from whose loins the succession comes — *seven or eight*, the *structural depth* of the cycle without enumeration.

## The four meanings of "the life of this world"

The boy now asks the question that has been waiting since chapter two's settlement of the world-and-Hereafter distinction. *How can God forbid this world and say, "Be not tempted by the life of this world"?*

The Master's answer hinges on precise reading. *God has spoken truly. He did not say that the world should not tempt you; rather He said, "Be not tempted by the life of this world."* God did not forbid the *world*; He forbade the *temptation by the life of this world*. Then the doctrine: *life* has *four meanings*, in two pairs.

The first pair: *a life that is apparent in this world, whose consequence is annihilation, and the life of the Hereafter, whose consequence is permanence*. *Do not work for a perishing life — work for the lasting life.* The Qur'anic warrant: *"Oh, would that I had sent forward something for my life" — meaning, would that one had sent forward, in the perishing life, something for the lasting life.*

The second pair: *a life of outward knowledge and a life of inward knowledge*. The first is *the life of this world* (the *lower* knowledge); the second is *the life of the Hereafter*. *For this reason He said, "Be not tempted by the life of this world" — meaning that you are not tempted by the outward of knowledge and acting upon it alone. He will only accept from you the inward together with the outward, and He does not accept the outward without the inward.* The *zahir*-*batin* doctrine of chapter two is now read into the very *verb* of the Qur'an.

## Inward without outward; outward without inward

The boy tests the doctrine in two directions. *What of one who knows the inward knowledge but does not know the outward, and does not act upon it — what is his standing among the people of religion?*

The Master's verdict is sharp. *He is at the level of the prophet — but if his inward does not rise, neither does his outward rise up.* He gives two figures: *if the rind is peeled off before being harvested, it is spoiled and not repaired afterward; and if the body is severed, the soul is not sustained in it for an hour*. The *outward* is the *protection* of the *inward*; remove the *outward*, and the *inward* itself is spoiled or unsustained.

The doctrinal consequence: *his leaving the outward without the permission of the one who imposed it upon him cannot escape one of two characteristics: either he abandoned it from incapacity — and if he was incapable, he was so to the one who commanded him — or he abandoned it in disobedience. The disobedient is a sinner, and the disobedient is not near to the guardians of God; rather he is their enemy, and they are his enemies, because he severs what God commanded him to maintain.* The outward — even when one has reached the inward — is not *droppable* at one's own discretion. Permission to drop must come from *the one who imposed*.

The boy presses the other direction. *And of one who knows the outward knowledge and acts upon it but does not know the inward — what is his standing among the people of religion?* The Master's verdict here is even sharper. *An evil standing — for he is like a body that has been raised up but into which no soul has been breathed; he is counted among the dead, and the dead are the abodes of the disbelievers. As one who disbelieves in the signs of God, he is an enemy of religion and its people.*

The boy synthesizes. *I see that the outward is not valid except by the inward, and the inward is not made good except by the outward; that the world is not lawful except for those who know the Hereafter, which is its true life and its inward. Likewise religion is not granted to its people unless they establish both its outward and its inward.* The Master confirms, and the boy seals the verdict. *Then whoever knows the outward together with the inward and establishes them — is he a true believer?* The Master: *Yes, he is a true believer.* The true believer is the one who *establishes both*.

## The scholars who weighed yellow

The boy reaches for the practical corollary. *Is there not one who learns and then works for the Hereafter, and along with that takes the world and its good and what swings to him of its gains?* The Master: *Yes — the world is not made good except for them; they are the most righteous in this world and the foremost of its people.*

The boy has seen them. *I do not see the people of this character — those who are the scholars among the people — except that these things of the world and what is in it have been rejected by them, so that the ignorant among them think it is forbidden to them. … Then I see their colors yellowed without cause, their bodies weak, their skins clinging to their bones so that they do not eat their food while they are eating.* The boy is describing the scholars he knows: *yellow*, *thin*, *not eating their food while eating*. He does not know what to do with the picture.

The narrator enters for the first time since chapter one. *When the scholar heard the mention of his brethren and the description of them, he beamed and his eyes overflowed with tears; at times he could not speak from the intensity of the lesson. The boy wept at his weeping, until the scholar feared that some of their enemies might prevail over them. He took hold of himself and his heart settled within him, and then the scholar turned to the boy.* The Master, recognized in the boy's description, *beams*, *weeps*, *cannot speak from the intensity of the lesson*; the boy *weeps at his weeping*; the Master *fears* and *takes hold of himself*. The narrator is showing the listener, in passing, that the Master himself is one of the brethren the boy has been describing.

The Master gives the *batin* of the picture. *O my son, the people whom you have described in this way looked upon themselves by the light of life and worked in their affairs with the certainty of salvation, and they did not become arrogant over themselves.* Then the doctrinal aphorism.

> O my son, this world is seen by the eye of wealth and children, and the Hereafter is seen by the eye of knowledge and certainty.

*Wealth and children* are the *eye* of the world; *knowledge and certainty* are the *eye* of the Hereafter. The scholar the boy described looks *with* the Hereafter's eye.

> O my son, these people's faith in the unseen is sound; they believed the promise and the warning and were satisfied; they were terrified by the fear of the threat, so they laboured; they feared the drunkenness of death, so they hastened to good deeds. Their bodies were weakened by the rigor of fasting, and their colors yellowed by the length of vigil. … The desires of this world's spectacle have been emptied for them; they remember death often, so that life does not torment them; they have seen the Hereafter as certain, and they have seen this world only as a fragment. So they refused to work the world even from what is not forbidden to them.

The yellow of the scholars is the *vigil's* yellow. The verdict — the line that closes the picture, and in the Master's own life is his form of life: *they refused to work the world even from what is not forbidden to them*.

The boy receives the picture and corrects his earlier ignorance. *This is the true news. In this sense the scholars hated this world; and the people were heedless of them, supposing that they had forbidden to themselves what God had made lawful for them. You have described to me the arrogance of the people of the world in their bodies, and their heedlessness of the Hereafter.*

The Master closes the section. *If people would only think about the world, their states would change — from weakness to strength, from strength to weakness, from bliss to demise, and from life to death. They would then look at those who passed before them out of the vanished centuries: the good and the wicked among them went to the same level; they did not see a reward for the good in this world, nor a punishment for the wicked in this world, so that they would know that they had been created for other than this world.* The empirical fact that *should* turn the eye to the other world: *the good and the wicked both went to the same level*.

## One creation, one instrument, many passions

The boy asks the question waiting since the doctrine of the two kinds of people opened the chapter. *Tell me about the beginning of this creation and their composition: is it a single creation or different? And why do I see so much difference in opinions and passions?*

The Master separates *bodies* from *instrument*. *As for the creation of bodies, it is different, as you see, in the variety of tongues and colors — and that is a proof of God's power, made clear by the signs, so that the created among them may know one another.* Bodily difference is *functional* — child-and-father, husband-and-wife.

> As for the creation of the instrument — namely the senses, what is apparent of them and what is hidden — it is a single creation. … Have you seen a scholar with three eyes as an increase in him, or an ignorant man with one eye showing a deficiency in him? … The instrument is one, and the composition belongs to the gatherer.

The *instrument* — outer and inner senses — is *one*. The *scholar* does not have three eyes; the *ignorant* is not deprived. *God would not have been just in what is apparent and unjust in what is hidden, nor would He have made for the scholar two hearts in one cavity.* The Qur'anic argument (Q 7:179): *They have hearts wherewith they do not understand, and they have eyes wherewith they do not see, and they have ears wherewith they do not hear.* The instrument has been given; the creatures did not use it.

The boy presses. *I am asking you about the difference of passions: from where did they come, when the instrument is whole and the origin in the composition is the same?* The Master gives the genealogical answer first. *As for the origin, it is one; all of them are from Adam (Ādam, /ˈʔaːdam/), and Adam is from dust, created by his Lord, who breathed into him of His Spirit. But they deviated from the Sunna by marriage: they were born on a non-religious basis and grew up upon another way. … When the origins were corrupted, the branches were corrupted; the way back was lost to them, and it was difficult for them to return to the truth — with their arrogance in obedience, their laziness in worship, and their envy of the guardians of God — so they did not hold to the limit of the lawful and the forbidden.*

But the genealogical answer does not explain *why*, with one instrument, the *aim* should differ. The boy presses again, and the Master gives the deeper figure.

> By my life — their meaning in seeking is one, but they differ in hitting the mark, like archers aiming at a target: they are unanimous in shooting it with courage and with the instrument, and they differ in striking it.

The archer-figure. The *aim* is one; the *courage* and *instrument* are one; the *striking* differs. *When the call of truth appeared, they differed.*

> Some of them held that answering the Messengers and humbling themselves before them was a part of one's dignity toward oneself and one's protection of it from folly; others held that denying them and lifting himself above their obedience was a part of his dignity toward himself and his protection of it from the narrowness of worship. The origin was the same: seeking ease for the body and dignity for souls. So some of them chose what the Messengers promised them of the reward of God, even if it was, in this world, dear to behold, for its permanence; and others chose from the bliss of this world, even if it was perishing, because of its presentness, the ease of its asking, and the swiftness of its reward.

The same *dignity*-impulse, two opposite readings. The first group can *wait* for the *permanence*; the second cannot. The chapter's settlement: *This is the justice of God, His generosity, and the extending of His giving: each seeker is given what he sought of them; whoever sought the reward of this world is given from it, and whoever sought the reward of the Hereafter is given from it.*

## Classes in the world and degrees in religion

The boy now extends the question. *The people of this world have classes within this world. … Then the bliss of this world brings them together on one side: are they all alike in evil consequences, or is it for some of them and not for the others?* The Master answers: *It is from the justice of God that He joins them to one another and does not separate them in their forsaking Him and in their affection; they are equal in His punishment as they were, in His disobedience, joined together.* The wealthy king and the poor servant are equal in the consequence because both were of one class in the forsaking.

The boy puts the symmetrical question about religion. *What of this religion … will He accept it from some without the others, so that they will be in their classes like the people of this world, or will only those who bring it in full be accepted from?* The Master's answer is the chapter's settlement on religion.

> If the people of this world have classes within this world, then the people of religion have degrees within their religion. In justice it is incumbent that the Creator be worshipped to the measure of His will, the magnitude of His sovereignty, and the magnitude of their need of Him. His mercy expanded toward them: He knew that there were weak among them, so He accepted from them what was easy of striving in work, and of striving in obedience, whether little or much. Then He charged the whole of His creation according to its capacity and its work; all those upon whom His bounty has flowed of His bounty are at degrees with their Lord.

The symmetry holds — but the *degrees* of religion are not, in their consequence, like the *classes* of the world. The world's classes are equal in judgment; religion's degrees are graded in acceptance. Each is charged according to capacity. Then the Qur'anic warrant.

> And whoever obeys Allah and His Messenger — those will be with the ones upon whom Allah has bestowed favor: the prophets, the truthful, the martyrs, and the righteous. What excellent companions they are! That is the bounty from Allah.

The crucial gloss: *This is by exerting effort in obedience, not strictly by the merit of their own deeds. It is the righteous who reach this rank, but only as a mercy from Allah to them, even if they themselves are weak and their works are slight.* The *effort* is enough; the *deeds' merit* is not the basis.

The boy presses the implications quickly. *Is it to be feared that anyone should be punished for something other than disobedience, even when his work is little?* The Master: *Allah is more just than that.* *Is it possible for anyone to enter Paradise without works — such as the insane and small children?* The Master: *That falls under the grace of Allah, His generosity, and the breadth of His mercy.* The doctrine of *capacity* protects the *incapable*.

The boy asks the threshold-question. *What, then, is the limit of effort in obedience and the standard of sincere work, such that Allah is bound to accept one's excuse in all else?* The Master gives the standard the rest of the chapter operationalizes.

> The full measure of one's knowledge, and the utmost reach of one's capacity.

The standard is *internal*. Beyond one's knowledge, the excuse is accepted; beyond one's capacity, the excuse is accepted. Within them, the *full measure* is required.

## The duty of the body: the golden rule as justice

The boy puts himself in the test. *Then describe to me the duty of knowledge and the duty of action, and the goal of each — so that I may test myself. … I do not trust my own soul: Allah is just, and I am safe from injustice on His part, but I am afraid of the evil within myself.*

The Master gives the posture first. *Do not fear where you have come to believe, and do not feel secure where you should still fear. Strive for the sake of Allah with both your person and your wealth.* The *and* will become the structural seam of the rest of the chapter. *For Allah does not accept what He hates, nor does He hate what pleases Him. He hates disbelief in His creation, and He is pleased when His servants give thanks and act with justice in His judgment. He accepts only from those who come to Him with truthfulness and faithfulness — these are the gardens of His Paradise — so that only the people of truthfulness and faithfulness are saved.*

The boy asks the operational question. *Show me the goal of my work, and what Allah has made binding upon me in it.*

> Obedience to Allah ennobles its people, even when their origins are humble; and good work is the crown of those who do it. So what is incumbent upon you in your own person is to perform every obligation and to avoid every forbidden thing — for this is the very religion of your soul and the purification of your body. The sum of what is obligatory upon you, so that Allah may be pleased with you and accept you, is this: extend to others what you would wish extended to yourself, and refrain from doing to others what you would wish refrained from being done to you. This is justice, and Allah is pleased with justice.

The golden rule as the chapter formulates it — *extend* and *refrain* in symmetry with what one would wish for oneself. The Master names what this *is*: *justice*. The boy receives the rule. *This is the binding truth, and whoever does not act by it has nothing with Allah. Then is there any further latitude beyond what you have mentioned?* The Master: *Allah has made things easy for His servants, out of His compassion and mercy. He has said: "Whoever is compelled — without willful transgression — there is no sin upon him."*

## The duty of wealth: lawful source, rightful place, the fifth

The boy picks up the second term. *What is the duty regarding my wealth — for you joined wealth to my own person when you said I must act "with your person and your wealth"?* The Master gives the binding rule. *As for your wealth: it is whatever Allah has granted you of every kind of gain. Take it only from a lawful source, and spend it only in its rightful place.* The rule is paired — both ends of the wealth-pipe must be right.

The boy asks the operational question. *And what is the rightful claim by which it is taken, and in what place is it to be spent?*

> Its lawful source is acquisition in which you have wronged no one and which you have not mixed with falsehood. As for the rightful claim upon it, that is its zakat (zakāt, /zaˈkaːt/): look at all that has come into your possession, whether little or much, and take from it a fifth of the best of it — what you yourself would love to receive — and pay it to those who have a binding claim upon you. In this way wealth gives its alms, just as your body gives its alms through work. Work is the gateway to Paradise, and zakat is purification, and whoever pays the zakat has succeeded.

The *measure*: *a fifth of the best of it* — *what you yourself would love to receive*. The *parallel*: *wealth gives its alms* as *the body gives its alms through work*. The *fifth of the best* is the *da'wa* measure (distinct from the *forty-of-one* standard of the *fiqh* schools, echoing the Qur'anic *khums* of war-spoils — here generalized to *every kind of gain*); the chapter does not unpack the difference, it gives the *fifth* as the binding measure for the boy.

The boy receives the rule. *I shall do so, and I repent to Allah for what has passed. Then what shall I do with the remainder of my wealth?* The Master gives the spending rule. *You shall spend upon yourself and your family with kindness. If your bounty exceeds your need, single out the poor among your brethren and extend your favor to all who are willing to receive it — for one who has no merit of his own needs the favor of another. Hasten to every good and rush ahead to every act of bounty, for this is the goal of work — if only you are patient in it.*

The boy seals the rule with the patience-and-thanks doublet. *Yes — patience and then more patience. And how can a great man fail to be patient in the face of the poverty of those who cannot do without his help? Every needy one practises patience, and every one who has been favored should give thanks.* The boy now speaks the doctrine in his own voice. The Master, *pleased with the boy's words*, gives the blessing. *Praise be to Allah, who has guided you to speak rightly. I ask Him to help you bring the work to completion, for that is the perfection of virtue.*

## The five shares: the boy's enacted ethics

The boy now does what the chapter has been preparing him for. He does not *say* he will follow the rule; he *enacts* it.

> I had been asking another for the credit of a favor I should have done for myself; and when at last that credit fell to me, I deprived myself of it, finding no one able to cheat me out of it on my own behalf. All this wealth lies in your hands now, in five shares: one share is its zakat; one share is the expiation for what I have wasted; one share is thanksgiving to Allah for what I have come to know; one share for the poor among my brethren; and one share that you accept by your grace — and it will return to you increased. Join everyone of them to yourself, and Allah will repay you in their kind.

The *five shares*: *zakat* (the fifth, the purification); *expiation* for what he has wasted (correction of past wrong); *thanksgiving* for what he has come to know (response to the gift of knowledge); *the poor among his brethren* (binding claim of the brotherhood); *the share for the Master himself* (gift to the teacher). The boy has performed the doctrine: lawful source, rightful place, zakat paid, expiation made, thanks given, brethren singled out, every good hastened to.

The narrator enters again. *The scholar's heart was moved on the boy's behalf when he saw how the jewel within him had revealed itself, and how the ordeal had brought forth a praiseworthy result, and that the boy had truly made his bounty and his zakat his work.* The Master, *tears flowing*, gives a benediction in the cosmological register of chapter two.

> O my son, may scholars travel the road for the sake of one like you; may the earth rise and the heavens remain stable for the sake of one like you; may the bonds of kinship be made manifest, and the rain come down from the bellies of the clouds, for the sake of one like you.

The boy is now, in the Master's mouth, the *occasion* for which scholars travel and the earth and heavens hold their order. Then the Master reapportions the five shares, refusing his own.

> So here, build with what Allah has given you and with the breast He has opened to Islam (Islām, iss-LAAM). Take the glad tidings of a light from your Lord and a swift opening. … As for my share of this wealth — I have no need of it. I have come out from wealth and from its temptations, fleeing from it. It would not be right, my son, that you should return me to what I have detested.

The Master's refusal names the form of life the boy described at the start of the chapter. *Returning him to wealth* would *contradict* the very form of life that drew the boy to the catechism. The refusal is doctrinal, not polite.

> As for your brothers' share, that you will keep for them: they will turn to you for the things you have done for them by your own hand, so that they may know your kindness and thank Him for your benevolence. As for the share of expiation, I will show you how to use it, so you may know your duty. As for the share of thanksgiving, that is a trust kept for Allah until you understand what you do not yet know and rise to a goal beyond your present sight. As for the share of zakat, that too is a trust held for the rightful owner of zakat — every one of them is your concern until he receives what is his, for Allah is the Ruler over you and on your behalf.

The reapportionment: *expiation* — *I will show you how*; *thanksgiving* — *a trust kept for Allah*; *zakat* — *a trust held for the rightful owner*. The phrase *the rightful owner of zakat* is the chapter's pivot. The boy has not yet been told *who* the rightful owner is. He will ask in the next exchange.

## Who is the generous one? — the Imam, first named

> Who is this generous one among you, who does not overreach his own judgment and does not transgress his own command?

The boy asks for the name. The Master gives the description before the name.

> He is the one whose right Allah has made binding upon me and upon every believer, in whose hand are the keys of the heavens and the landmarks of the kingdom, whose palms are opened with the light of Sinai (Sīnāʾ, SEE-nigh) — he is the cause behind the signs, and the one whose knowledge calls to the loftiest of ends. Through him your light is completed, through him Allah perfects your affair, and at his hand you are blessed.

The chapter's first *direct* speaking of the *Imam* in the unmistakable register. *The one whose right Allah has made binding upon every believer.* *In whose hand are the keys of the heavens* — the keys of Paradise from earlier in the chapter, now in *his* hands. *Whose palms are opened with the light of Sinai* — the Mosaic light, the *theophanic* light, opened in *his* palms. *The cause behind the signs* — the *batin* of the *zahir*. *Through him your light is completed.*

The boy asks the second question. *Such kindness as this — to whom does it belong, and who are you yourself?* The Master places his own person. *I am his son and one of his servants in good works, seeking his pleasure and shunning his displeasure.* The Master is *his son* — not biologically; the *da'wa* figure of *spiritual sonship*, the *son* of the Imam being the one bound to him by the rope. The Master is placed *in* the chain; the Master is *not* the *generous one*; he is *one of his servants*.

## Take me to his door

The boy makes the move that closes the chapter. He has received the doctrine, *enacted* the wealth-disposition, heard the *name* of the *generous one*, and heard the Master place himself as the Imam's *son and servant*. He asks for the next step.

*I think I am far from his good pleasure and yet close enough that — had I not known of him — I would have an excuse. Do I have an excuse, given what I now know?* The Master answers without softening. *An excuse is granted only before exposure to such knowledge.* *No.* Knowledge removes excuse.

The boy hesitates. *Shall I speak, or shall I hold back?* The Master gives permission. *Say what seems best to you.* The boy speaks.

> Should you not, at this moment, take me by the hand to the door of his chamber until I enter, and to the house of light until I draw from it? This is part of the full measure of truthfulness which is binding upon you.

The boy invokes the *truthfulness*-rule the Master has been teaching him: *this is part of the full measure of truthfulness which is binding upon you*. The boy has learned the rule and is now applying it to the Master. The Master answers in the register of the chain itself.

> This is your duty — but only with his permission and his decision.

The boy's *duty* — *yes*. The *bringing* — *only with his permission and his decision*. The Master cannot bring the boy to the Imam of his own initiative. The *Imam* must permit the meeting; the *Imam* must decide whether the boy is ready.

The chapter ends here. The boy has reached the *door of the chamber*; he has *asked* to be brought in; the Master has *promised* that this is his duty but has *deferred* the bringing to the Imam's permission. The arc has been a single line: the boy who in chapter one was bound to the rope, and in chapter two received the architecture of creation, has in this chapter received the ethics of the rope and *enacted* them in his own wealth. He has moved from *receiving doctrine* to *performing it*. The *generous one*, named only by his attributes here, has been *placed* in the chain, and the boy has *asked to be brought to his door*. The next chapter will *answer* his asking — but only after the *Imam's permission* has been sought, and only after the *boy himself* has been named.
