# The Architecture of Creation: Will, Command, and the Seven

*This chapter covers the second long teaching of* The Book of the Master and the Boy *(*Kitāb al-ʿĀlim wa-l-Ghulām*, ki-TAAB al-AA-lim wal-ghu-LAAM), attributed to Sayyidina Ja'far ibn Mansur al-Yaman (sai-yi-DEE-nah JAH-far ibn man-SOOR al-YAH-man) — the unbroken exposition the Master begins immediately after the binding of the covenant at the end of chapter one, with the boy now seated, silent in his composure, and the* da'wa *(DAH-wah — the call, the missionary apparatus of the Ismaili tradition) opened for him for the first time. The Master starts at the origin: God as Creator and Composer, the* First of all firsts and the Inheritor of every thing*. From the original light He brings forth three words — the* will*, the* command*, and the* saying*. From these three branches the word* Kun *(KOON, "Be") and its fulfilment* fa-yakun *(fa-ya-KOON, "and it is") — seven letters from which the seven things branch: air (singular, surrounding all), then water, darkness, and light, then smoke, condensation, and fire. From these seven principles He builds the seven heavens, the seven earths, the twelve constellations, the seven kingdoms, the twelve islands (*jazā'ir*, ja-ZAA-ir), and the seventeen seas. He distinguishes day from night, ordains the pairs of all creation — animal and speech, names and meanings — and from the choicest of His creation draws out a* religion *and a chain of bearers:* imams *and* speakers *(*nāṭiqs*, NAA-tiq),* arguments *(*ḥujaj*, HOO-jaj) and* chiefs *(*nuqabā'*, noo-qa-BAA), and* summoners *(*du'āt*, doo-AAT). The Master then maps the apparent onto the inward: the seven heavens are the seven nāṭiqs, the seven earths are the imams of guidance, the twelve constellations are the twelve nuqabā', the twelve islands are the twelve ḥujaj, the rivers and springs are the du'āt. He turns to the world and its seven attributes — heavens, earths, lights and darknesses, vessels, blessings, miseries — and to the* zahir *(ZAA-hir, the apparent) and* batin *(BAA-tin, the inward) of every created thing, ending with the egg as the parable of three layers of knowledge: the* shell*, the* white*, and the* yolk *which is its essence and meaning.*

## Where this chapter picks up

The boy has just been bound. At the end of chapter one the Master recited the covenant of God to him, the boy wept until his tears poured down, he understood that he had become part of the Party of God (*Hizb Allah*, HIZB ahl-LAH) and of the company of His friends, and *he fell silent in his composure*. The Master *began to expound and explain*. This chapter is the first long expounding. Where the first chapter walked the boy through the *outer* of the call — *who* may receive it, *how* it is given, *under what conditions* — this chapter walks him through the *architecture of creation itself*. The bond has been struck; now the architecture inside the bond is shown.

The chapter is, in form, a sustained Master-Boy dialogue, but the proportion has shifted. In chapter one the boy spoke half the lines; here the Master speaks most of them. The boy is a prompter rather than an interlocutor — he asks short, structurally placed questions, and the Master replies in long architectural answers. The substance is what later Ismaili philosophical literature will call the *cosmology* of the *da'wa*: the doctrine of *will, command, and saying* (*irāda*, *amr*, *qawl*); the doctrine of the *seven principles* and the *twelve constellations*; the doctrine of the *parallel* between the seven heavens above and the seven *nāṭiqs* below, the twelve constellations above and the twelve *nuqabā'* below, the twelve islands above and the twelve *ḥujaj* below; the doctrine of *zahir* and *batin*; and the doctrine of the *three layers of knowledge*. The doctrines are not separate teachings; they are *one architecture*, and the chapter's force is in the way the Master walks the boy down through each layer in order, never skipping a step.

There are two registers the listener must keep in mind. The first is *numerical*: the chapter is dense with numbers — three words, seven letters, seven principles, seven heavens, seven earths, twelve constellations, twelve islands, seven kingdoms, seventeen seas, six limits and a seventh, three layers. The numbers are not decoration; they are the *grammar* by which the cosmology is read. The second is *figurative*: the Master gives every cosmological fact a *likeness* (*mathal*) in the apparent world and a *meaning* (*ma'nā*) in the inward. The heavens are *like* the *nāṭiqs*; the constellations are *like* the *nuqabā'*; the islands are *like* the *ḥujaj*; the rivers are *like* the *du'āt*. The figure-and-meaning method is itself a teaching, and toward the end of the chapter the Master will defend it explicitly against the Qur'anic verse that seems to forbid it.

## The three words: will, command, saying

The Master begins exactly where the *da'wa* literature insists every teaching must begin — at the origin. *God is the Creator of things and the One who composes them.* He is not *the first of every first created thing*, the first of a series that begins with Him as the first member. He is *the First of all firsts and the Inheritor of every thing* — outside the series altogether, the One who *inherits* every thing because everything returns to Him. The negative theology of chapter one is now stated as a doctrine of origin: God's relation to the creation is not as the *first member* of any sequence; it is as the *First-before-firsts* and the *Inheritor-after-lasts*. He is not located in the time of the creation; the creation is located in His will.

Then the structural claim. *He began the creation of what was created out of light, from which three words branched.* The doctrine of the *primordial light* is the doctrine the rest of the architecture rests on. Before the seven, before the heavens, before the earth, there is *light* — and from that light *three words* branch. The order is precise:

> The first of them is the will; from the will branched the command; and the command is divided into "saying" — that He says "Be" to what He wills, and it is. So the first creation is the will, then the command, then the saying.

The three words are *iradat-amr-qawl*: *will*, *command*, *saying*. The will is the first; from the will the command branches; from the command the saying is *divided*. The saying is *Kun fa-yakun* — *Be, and it is* — the Qur'anic formula by which God creates whatever He wishes. The Master is reading the Qur'anic *Kun fa-yakun* against a more fundamental structural order: *the saying is the last of the three*; before the saying there is the *command*; before the command there is the *will*. What looks, in the Qur'an, like a single fiat — *Be* — is in fact the *third step* of a sequence whose first two steps are *the will* and *the command*. The cosmology will run on this triple all the way down.

## Kun fa-yakun: seven letters, seven things

The Master now performs the operation that the rest of the chapter rests on. *These are three words, from which "Kun" — two letters — is derived, and "fa-yakun" — five letters; that is seven letters, from which seven things branch.* The Arabic *Kun* (KOON) has two letters: *kāf*-*nūn*. The Arabic *fa-yakun* (fa-ya-KOON) has five: *fā'-yā'-kāf-wāw-nūn*. Together: *seven*. The seven letters of the divine fiat are the *cipher* of the seven principles that emerge from it.

The seven principles are not all of one rank. The first principle — *air* — has a unique status. *From the light He created air, which is the first of them: singular without consort, unique among things, a mixture surrounding them but not surrounded by any of them. It is the proof of the One concerning whom "there is nothing like Him."* Air alone among the seven is *singular without consort* — without a pair. It *surrounds* everything but is not surrounded by anything. It is the *proof of the One* — the cosmological argument for the unity of God built into the structure of creation itself. The Qur'anic formula *there is nothing like Him* (*laysa ka-mithlihi shay'*) is given a structural reading here: in the same way that *air* surrounds everything and is not itself surrounded, *God* surrounds everything and is not contained. The Master will return to this figure at the end of the chapter as the great image of the *highest similitude*.

The other six are paired: *Then He created out of these three words: water, darkness, and light. Then He created the heavens and the earth. He created smoke out of water, He created condensation out of the heat of darkness, and He created fire out of the light.* The three words branch into three substances — *water*, *darkness*, *light* — and from these three substances three further substances are drawn: *smoke* from water, *condensation* from darkness, *fire* from light. *Each became consort to its origin because it was created from it.* The *consort* pairing is the structural law of creation: every derivative is the consort of its origin, because the derivative was *drawn from* the origin. Water and smoke are consorts; darkness and condensation are consorts; light and fire are consorts. *And the seventh, air, encompasses all of them.* The seventh stands outside the pairing and *encompasses* the other six. *This was the beginning of creation and the beginning of things.* The seven principles are the *grammar* of everything that follows.

## The cipher unfolded: heavens, earths, constellations, kingdoms, islands, seas

The Master now applies the cipher. *When creation had been established on seven principles, and the path of forms had been laid down on the basis of likeness and analogy, He created out of smoke seven heavens and their signs, and He created out of condensation seven earths and their powers. These seven branch from those seven origins.* The cosmology is *fractal*: the seven principles unfold into seven heavens and seven earths; the seven heavens and seven earths unfold further still. The Master is teaching the boy how to *read* the structure: the heavens *branch from* smoke; the earths *branch from* condensation; smoke *branches from* water; condensation *branches from* darkness; water and darkness *branch from* the three words. Every level of the cosmology is *generated* from the level above by the same rule of *consort-pairing* and *fractal unfolding*.

Then the next operation. *We return to the three words and their letters which created the seven, namely "the will to command by saying."* The phrase *al-irāda li-l-amr bi-l-qawl* — *the will to command by saying* — is a twelve-letter cipher. *That is twelve letters; and its signs in heaven are the twelve constellations, its witnesses are seven kingdoms, its signs upon the earth are twelve, ten of seven islands, and its proofs are seventeen seas.* The cosmology unfolds into a more elaborate set of correspondences:

- *Twelve letters* in the formula *the will to command by saying*.
- *Twelve constellations* in the heaven (the signs of the zodiac).
- *Seven kingdoms* in the heaven (the seven heavens themselves, as the kingdoms of the seven *nāṭiqs* to come).
- *Twelve signs* upon the earth.
- *Seven islands* (the *jazā'ir*), of which ten share something — the source's wording is compressed here, and the figure will return when the Master draws the parable between the twelve islands and the twelve *ḥujaj* of the *imams of guidance*.
- *Seventeen seas* — the proofs.

The Master does not pause to expound the number-correspondences. He delivers them as the *structural skeleton* and moves on. The boy is being trained, here, to *receive* a cipher rather than to *unpack* it on first hearing. The cipher will be unpacked passage by passage in the rest of the book — and in the long Ismaili philosophical tradition that takes this dialogue as foundational.

## The piercing: night, day, and the pairs

The cosmology is so far symmetrical and self-similar. Now the Master introduces the *first difference*. *Then the heavens and the earth were veiled in darkness from the black heat, which is the authority of the earth. Then He pierced it with His lights, and light and darkness were intermingled — the two distinct realities. He distinguished between them by night and day, with two signs.* The original veil over creation was *darkness from black heat*; God *pierced* the veil with light; light and darkness were *intermingled* but remained *two distinct realities*. The intermingling is not a confusion; it is the *condition* under which the two can be distinguished. Light and darkness *meet* in creation, but they *remain themselves*.

The Master draws the rest of the day-and-night doctrine. *He made the night, and its sign a way (minhāj, min-HAAJ) of darkness, and apportioned it; and He made the day, and its sign a way of light, and apportioned it. He adorned the night with its darkness — by the moon and the stars — and the day shines with its light, gleaming through the sign of the sun. Then the days produced states between night and day, which are the seven days. Their signs: each day has twelve hours, and each night has twelve hours.* The day-and-night cipher is *twelve-and-twelve* (the hours), repeated *seven* times (the days of the week). The numerical grammar continues to unfold: seven, twelve, twelve.

Then the doctrine of *pairs*. *When the creation of things was combined with these principles, He made them in pairs — male and female. I do not mean male and female in the literal sense, but in the sense of the active and the receptive.* The pair-doctrine is not a doctrine of *sex* but of *form*: every created thing is paired *active-and-receptive*, *agent-and-patient*. The pairs run all the way down: *Then the sunna (SOON-nah — custom) of creation in pairs went forth, and He created all its essences: animals — humans, beasts, birds, and crawling creatures.* And the pair-doctrine extends past biology into *speech*. *Then this sunna extended into speech, and these too became pairs: adjectives and descriptions, names and meanings.* Names and meanings are *pairs*; descriptions and adjectives are *pairs*. The grammar of language is itself a grammar of pairs.

The doxology closes the move. *Glory be to Him who created all pairs, and made them manifestations of His command, exemplars of His religion, and signs of His power — pointing by things to their origins, and by parables to their interpretations.* The pairs are not just structural; they are *pedagogical*. They *point*: things point to their *origins* by being *consorts* of their origins; parables point to their *interpretations* by carrying *meanings*. The whole doctrine of *figure-and-meaning* (*mathal-and-ma'nā*) that the chapter will later defend is grounded here.

## The religion drawn out: imams, nāṭiqs, ḥujaj, nuqabā', duʿāt

Having laid down the cosmology, the Master now draws out the doctrine of the *religion* that crowns it. *Then He pierced it with His light, opened it with His command, created it by His knowledge, and sealed it among His names with the letters of "In the name of Allah, the Most Gracious, the Most Merciful."* The *Bismillāh* itself is given a structural reading: it is the *seal* by which God closes the creation under the cover of His names. *When He completed all of them by His making and composed them in pairs by His measure, He selected for Himself, from the choicest of His will and the secrets of His unseen, great knowledge and clear light.*

The *great knowledge* and the *clear light* are the doctrine of *religion* itself, given as a divine selection from *the choicest of His will* and *the secrets of His unseen*. The boy is being told: *religion* is not a code of practices appended to creation; it is a *substance* drawn from the *innermost* of the divine will. *He made these a religion by which God draws His creation to Himself. Then He honored it with laws and books, made it manifest by parables and the sunna, and appointed from the choicest of His creation banners, signs, and rulers.*

Then the chain that runs the rest of the book. *He made them the houses of His prophethood, the treasuries of His knowledge and wisdom, His interpreters and the bearers of His revelation. Their succession in His earth is evidence of the path of salvation, both in the apparent (ẓāhir) and in the inner (bāṭin).* The *houses of prophethood*, the *treasuries of knowledge*, the *interpreters*, the *bearers* — and their *succession* on the earth is *evidence* of the *path of salvation*. The salvific significance of the chain is not internal to the chain itself; it is *evidence* — the chain *points*. And the chain has both an outer expression (apparent) and an inner expression (inner). Both are required.

Then the ranks. *Some of them are imams and speakers (nāṭiqs); some are arguments (ḥujaj) and chiefs (nuqabā'); among them are the summoners (du'āt) to the good and the learned.* The four ranks of the *da'wa* are named. The *nāṭiq* is the *speaker* — the prophet at the head of a cycle, the bearer of a new law. The *ḥujja* is the *argument* — the proof of God in the time, the figure who stands as evidence for the *nāṭiq*. The *nuqabā'* are the *chiefs* — the lieutenants of the *ḥujja* on the twelve islands. The *du'āt* are the *summoners* — those who *call* on the *ḥujja*'s authority. The chain runs *from* the *nāṭiq* *through* the *ḥujja* *through* the *nuqabā'* *to* the *du'āt* and out to the rest of creation. *In their dwellings they are like the stars of heaven — they were called the works, and the parables were called by them.* Their habitation is heavenly; their function is to be *called by* the *parables* of the apparent.

The Master then draws the astronomical figure. *Their imam is like the great sun among the lights, surpassing all sight; he is their apparent and their likeness. The argument and the gate of their imam is like the shining moon — its appearance and its likeness. Their summoners are like the manifest stars (zawāhir, za-WAA-hir) — their appearances and their likenesses.* The cosmology folds back on itself: the *sun* is the *imam*; the *moon* is the *ḥujja* or the *gate* (*bāb*) of the imam; the *stars* are the *du'āt*. The heaven the boy looks up at and the religion he has just bound himself to are the *same architecture* read at two scales.

## The seven heavens and the seven nāṭiqs

The Master now states the *parallel* in full. *All the pairs I have described to you are apparent and inward, and likewise figure and likeness.* The pairs are not just male-female and active-receptive; they are also *apparent-and-inward*. Every thing in the cosmos has a *zahir* and a *batin*, a *mathal* (figure) and a *ma'nā* (meaning). *The apparent are the seven heavens; their inner counterparts are the seven speakers (nāṭiqs) — and the first of the speakers are the messengers.* The seven heavens above are the *apparent*; the seven *nāṭiqs* on the earth are the *inward*. The seven *nāṭiqs* of the *da'wa* are the seven prophets who bear new laws in the great cycles of the religion — and *the first of the speakers are the messengers* (*rasul*). The seven heavens are not a cosmic fact set apart from history; they are the *figure* of the seven prophetic cycles by which the religion has been borne.

The boy must hold the figure carefully. The seven *nāṭiqs* of the Ismaili tradition — Adam, Noah, Abraham, Moses, Jesus, Muhammad ﷺ, and (in some Ismaili readings) the Qā'im — are not named here. The Master does not enumerate them. He gives the *form* of the figure (seven heavens, seven *nāṭiqs*) and leaves the *enumeration* to other places in the literature. What the boy needs at this point in his formation is the *structural claim*: the *cosmology* and the *religion* are *parallel structures*. The seven heavens parallel the seven *nāṭiqs*. Every figure in the apparent has a counterpart in the inward.

Then the lower-rank parallel. *The seven earths are outwardly parables; their inner counterparts are the imams of guidance.* The seven earths are *parables*; their *inner* counterparts are the *imams of guidance*. The *imams* are the rank below the *nāṭiqs* — the successors of each *nāṭiq*, the chain that runs across the time between *nāṭiqs*. Each *nāṭiq* has his successor and his successor's successors, and that line of imams *guides* the people of the cycle between one *nāṭiq* and the next.

Then the constellations. *For each of them there are twelve chiefs (nuqabā'), and their like from the seven heavens are the twelve constellations.* The twelve constellations *parallel* the twelve *nuqabā'* of each *nāṭiq*. The *zodiac* is the *figure* of the *nuqabā'*. *Likewise, each of the imams of guidance has twelve arguments (ḥujaj) by which God is invoked upon his people; their like in the earth are the twelve islands (jazā'ir), the dwellings of the chiefs.* The twelve *islands* parallel the twelve *ḥujaj*. *On every island stands an argument.* Each *jazā'ir* is the dwelling of one *ḥujja*. And *for every argument there are many summoners and few — He increases in creation what He wills, to convey the argument, the argument of God to His creation, so that people may have no argument against God after the messengers.* The *du'āt* multiply according to need; their number is not fixed. The structural claim closes on the Qur'anic formula: *no argument against God after the messengers*.

## The inner is the religion; the outer is its body

The Master now moves from architecture to function. *Through these causes — the causes (asbāb, as-BAAB) of God I have described to you — the argument of God reaches His creation, and His justice appears among them, both outwardly and inwardly.* The *causes of God* (*asbāb Allāh*) are the *chain of bearers*: through this chain the *argument* reaches the creation and the *justice* of God *appears*. The chain is the *medium* of both *argument* and *justice*.

Then the doctrine of *zahir* and *batin* given in its sharpest formulation in the chapter. *The inner (bāṭin) is the religion of God by which He has bound the saints of God; the outer is the laws of religion and their likenesses.* The *inner* is the *religion* itself — the *substance* by which God has *bound* His friends; the *outer* is the *laws* (the *Sharī'a*) and their *parables*. The two together are one. *Thus the religion of the laws became one and the same, and the laws of religion became a body and a guide.*

The Master draws the body-and-soul figure. *There is no body without a soul, for the soul is life; and there is no power for the soul except in the body, for the body is its instrument.* The principle is the principle of every later Ismaili philosophical defense of the *batin*: the *soul* and the *body* are *mutually necessary*. The body without a soul is a corpse; the soul without a body has no instrument. *Likewise, the apparent laws and their likes have no foundation except in the inner religion, because that is their light and their meaning — it is the spirit of life within them. The inner has no strength except in the outer, because the outer is its body and its proof.* The outer is the *body* of the religion; the inner is its *soul*. Neither stands without the other.

Then the consequence for *knowledge*. *The knowledge of this world cannot be perceived except through the outer; the knowledge of the hereafter cannot be perceived except through the inner.* The two knowledges are *located*: knowledge of this world *is* knowledge of the outer; knowledge of the hereafter *is* knowledge of the inner. *Not a single letter of the letters of the subordinate, and not a single saint of the saints, can be perceived through the outer alone in any abundance — because of the abundance of parables and the breadth of the laws.* The outer alone cannot deliver the *saint*; the saint must be perceived through the inner, because the *parables* are too many and the *laws* too broad for the outer to fix the saint by itself.

The Master closes with a warning about discourse. *The more one speaks of this, the more there is yet to be said — but wisdom hides its truths and conceals its last from its first, because of its preciousness and the purity of its essence. The bright lights of wisdom cover one another as the light of the sun covers the light of the moon and the stars, weakening them. Likewise, the words of wisdom adorn the tongue of the wise.* Wisdom *conceals its last from its first* — what is last in the order of wisdom is concealed by what is first. The lights of wisdom *overshadow* one another the way the sun overshadows the moon and the stars. The teaching the boy is receiving is not exhaustive; it is the *first* of the wisdom, and the *last* is concealed by it.

## The boy's question about the world

The boy, who has been silent through the long architectural exposition, now speaks. His question marks the chapter's pivot from *cosmology* to *ethics* — from the architecture of creation to the moral character of the world the cosmology has produced.

*This world, in which this great creation was set, has many people — both the first and the last — who differ concerning it. Some have entrusted themselves to the world and sought it, and saw no merit except in multiplying it. Others reproach it and practice asceticism within it, neither dispensing with it entirely, even though all that is forbidden is much, while only a little of it is forbidden. I have heard no mention or report from you concerning it. What do you say of it?* The boy is naming the standing controversy: some praise the world and accumulate within it; others reproach it and renounce within it; the Master has not yet given his verdict. *What do you say of it?*

The Master's answer dismantles the question. *As for this world, no one will ever truly reproach it — neither the scholar nor the ignorant. For the ignorant knows its virtue by his very ignorance of what lies beyond it, and the scholar knows its virtue through the virtue of its meaning.* Neither the *scholar* nor the *ignorant* truly reproaches the world. The ignorant cannot reproach it because his ignorance of what lies *beyond* it makes the world the *all* of his horizon — and you cannot reproach the *all*. The scholar cannot reproach it because he knows it *through the virtue of its meaning* — and you cannot reproach a thing whose meaning is virtuous. *No one ever reproaches this world except an ignorant man — and how can it be permissible for the creation to reproach the action of the Creator?* The world is *the action of the Creator*; to reproach it is to reproach the Creator. *But say rather: the world is set up for those who would see seven things, branching from seven origins — heaven, earth, darkness, light, vessels, prosperity, and misery.*

The cipher returns. *These are seven qualities, and from each of these seven attributes branch seven further qualities.* The same seven-and-seven grammar that opened the cosmology now reads the *ethical* world. The Master enumerates the seven attributes and their further sevens:

- *(a) The heavens are seven.*
- *(b) The earths are seven.*
- *(c) The light has seven days and seven nights: Saturday is for slumber and rest; Sunday is for the limits and the sunna; Monday is for the bladder, in the great meaning, al-Kabīr (al-ka-BEER); Tuesday has three houses — apparent, inward, and the inward of the subtle; Wednesday is for the four, which is the sanctuary; Thursday is the fifth of the causes of God; and Friday is the greatest law of God.* The seven days of the week are given a hidden meaning in the *da'wa* — each day is a *cipher* for an aspect of the religion. Saturday is *rest*; Sunday is *limits and sunna*; Monday names the *great meaning* (*al-Kabīr*); Tuesday names the *three houses* (*zahir*, *batin*, and *batin-of-batin* — the three layers of knowledge that the chapter will name explicitly at its close); Wednesday names the *four*; Thursday names the *fifth of the causes*; Friday names the *greatest law*. The Master does not unpack each day; he gives the boy the *cipher* and moves on. The unpacking, again, is for the rest of the book and the rest of the literature.
- *(d) The darkness is the seven nights of paradise for the seven days — for each day and each night.* The *nights* parallel the days; the seven-and-seven holds.
- *(e) The vessels are seven: camels, horses, mules, donkeys, elephants, oxen, and ships — for by these seven vessels the regions of the earth are traversed. Camels for the sands, horses for the plains, mules for the mountains, donkeys for short destinations, elephants for marshes and wasteland, oxen for salt-flats and thickets, and ships for the seas. Seven vessels for seven domains.* The vessels of travel are themselves a *seven*. The Master does not give the *batin* of the seven vessels here; the *zahir* alone is offered. The boy is expected to *recognize the cipher*: any *seven* in this cosmology has an *inward* that will be unpacked elsewhere.
- *(f) The blessings of this world rest upon seven: air, water, light, darkness, food, clothing, and marriage. Air is the medium between sky and earth, and the life of the animal. Light is the substance of sight; darkness is the increase of vegetation; food is the sustenance of the body; clothing in paradise wards off harm; marriage is the seed of reproduction and the cause of offspring. Some of these are the keys of the good, and some are the mines of blessings.* The seven blessings repeat the seven principles of creation — *air, water, light, darkness* are the same four that opened the cosmology — and add *food, clothing, marriage* for human life specifically. The Master draws the moral: *Which of them is blameworthy, when all of them are from the gifts of God and His blessings, for which His servants should give thanks?* The blessings of the world *are* the world; to reproach the world is to reproach the blessings.
- *(g) Their opposites in misery are also seven: heat, cold, hunger, thirst, fear, sickness, and death. These are seven things that must be borne with patience — know their meaning.* The seven *miseries* are the *consorts* of the seven *blessings*. The pair-doctrine holds even here: blessing and misery are consorts; *patience* is the response to misery as *thanks* is the response to blessing.

## The scholars' censure and the ignorant's praise

The boy now poses the hardest theoretical question of the section. *These are the attributes of this world; I do not doubt that anything of it is other than what the Creator has made and composed. So what do the scholars mean by censuring the world, or singing its praise, and what do the ignorant mean by praising it? I see that the ignorant have arrived at the meaning, even if they are ignorant — and if so, the rightness of their case obliges the scholars to err, because the scholars contradict the people of ignorance in every sense.* The boy has caught a paradox: the world is *good* (made by God); the *scholars censure it*; the *ignorant praise it*. If the ignorant praise what is good, and the scholars contradict the ignorant, do the scholars not *err*?

The Master's answer is the same kind of move he made in chapter one, when he distinguished *acknowledgment* from *action*. He does it here by distinguishing *the word* from *the meaning*. *The scholars did not err in censuring this world, but no creature knows what they mean by it. The ignorant did not perceive the meaning of what they were praising in the world — they are correct in praising it, but ignorant of its meaning. The one who is ignorant of a thing is merely accustomed to it.*

The verdict cuts cleanly. *No creature knows what they (the scholars) mean by it.* When the scholars censure the world, the *meaning* of their censure is not the surface meaning. *The ignorant are correct in praising it, but ignorant of its meaning.* When the ignorant praise the world, the *meaning* of their praise is also not the surface meaning. Both speak truly at the *outer* level; both *miss the inner*. The verdict is a *batin* verdict. The boy, who has just received the doctrine of *zahir* and *batin*, is being shown that the same doctrine cuts through the most ordinary speech of his society. The scholars and the ignorant *appear* to disagree; *in meaning* they are both speaking past one another.

The boy presses. *Show me the truth of what the scholars say, so that I may rightly believe them. Distinguish the speech of the people of ignorance by what shows their falsehood, since the apparent indicates otherwise. If my chest is weary of this ignorance, I do not hope for relief except by His knowledge.*

The Master gives him the *inner* of the censure. *This world, and all I have described to you of it, has an apparent and an inner. It has no strength except in Him, because He is like the soul of the body.* The world is the *body*; God is the *soul*. *Whoever knows the soul, the body is graced for him; whoever does not know the soul and confesses only the body is upon a corpse, and corpses are forbidden.* This is the verdict the scholars actually speak when they reproach the world. *Hence the scholars forbade this world because it is dead — they fled from the dead which God has forbidden, until it comes from where God wills.* The scholars do not reproach the world as a *thing*; they reproach the world *as a corpse* — the world held *without* knowledge of the soul that animates it. *And the people of ignorance lie when they praise this world, because they did not know its inner meaning and what God intended by it. They thought God created the world for some other meaning — but God did not create things in vain. If the world and what is in it had been created only for the world, He would have created in it a fading creation that had no consequence and no meaning. Every action without consequence is play, and every meaningless speech is falsehood — and God is more exalted than that.*

The structural claim is now closed. The world is *not* what the ignorant praise (a self-sufficient thing); the world is *not* what the scholars *seem to* reproach (an evil); the world is *what the scholars truly reproach* (a corpse without its soul) and *what the ignorant would praise if they knew its meaning* (the body of the soul of God). *Zahir* and *batin* run all the way down through ordinary speech.

The boy receives the verdict. *The truthfulness of the scholars is established, and their virtue and excellence are proven — as is the falsity and the lying of the people of ignorance, in what has appeared from the argument. And we have an inner knowledge of the world and what is in it. I see that the distress I complained of to you has been lifted.*

The Master draws the *thanks*-rule that crowned chapter one back into the picture, and tightens it. *What is the reward of the one who brought you out of the distress of poverty into the capacity of welcome?* *Thanks and praise.* *What is the reward of the one who brought you out of the distress of ignorance into the capacity of knowledge?* *Obedience and action.* The doubled *thanks*-rule: thanks-and-praise for the relief of *poverty*; obedience-and-action for the relief of *ignorance*. The relief of ignorance is the *greater* gift, and it requires the *greater* response. The *law of thanks* from chapter one is now refined: the thanks owed for *knowledge* is *obedience and action*, not merely thanks and praise.

The Master closes the world-section with a one-line rule. *This world and all its like are an apparent for the hereafter, which is within it as its soul and its life. Whoever seeks this world without knowledge of the hereafter, his quest is misguidance — for his quest has no meaning and no consequence. But whoever seeks in this world for the hereafter, while believing in it, his quest has meaning and consequence, and his quest is praiseworthy.* The verdict on the world: *quest*-with-knowledge-of-the-hereafter is *praiseworthy*; *quest*-without-knowledge-of-the-hereafter is *misguidance*. The world is not what is sought; the *hereafter through the world* is what is sought.

## Every thing has six limits and a seventh

The boy asks for *parables*. *I have believed. So show me the features of this world and what is in it, and grant me parables of it from within.* The boy has accepted the doctrine; he now wants the *application*.

The Master gives him the master-parable of the chapter. *God has not created anything in the world — no animal upon the earth, no bird that flies with its wings, no wet thing or dry thing, no inanimate object such as mountains, stones, trees, and minerals, nor gold, silver, and gemstone — and nothing whatsoever, great or small, that is not made by parable.* Every created thing *is itself a parable*. The doctrine of *figure-and-meaning* is not a hermeneutic the Master imposes on the world; it is *built into* the world. *Do you not see that even the atom, despite its smallness and its subtlety, has limits — in front of it, behind it, to its right, to its left, above it, and below it? Those are six limits, and the seventh is itself.* The atom, the smallest thing the boy can name, has *six limits* and a *seventh* (its own self). *So too is everything God has created — from the ant and the mosquito to what is above them in the heavens and the earth, and what is between them, great and small.*

The Master draws the figure. *This is the parable: each of them has six limits, and the seventh of them is the proof of this — pointing to the six origins from which things were created. And the air around them is the seventh, encompassing them all. If you open your heart to questions and consider the parables, you will meet all of them in their form from the inner pairs — so ask what seems good to you.* Six-and-a-seventh: the *six limits* point to the *six origins* (the three words and their three derivatives: water-darkness-light and smoke-condensation-fire), and the *seventh* — air — *encompasses* them all. Every thing in the world repeats the *seven principles* of the cosmology in its own shape. The *atom* is the *cosmos* in miniature.

## The likenesses: heaven, zodiac, earth, islands, rivers, kingdoms, seas

The boy is now ready for the *catechism of likenesses*. He asks, the Master answers; the dialogue runs through the chapter's central correspondences in short tight pairs.

*What is the likeness of the heaven, with its height and its breadth over things, and its diameters that surround all things?* — *It is like the speaking Imam (al-Imām al-Nāṭiq), who is exalted above all creation by his virtue and his high degrees, and contains all rulings and the rulings of the Sharia (sha-REE-ah) by reason of his knowledge.* The *heaven* is the *nāṭiq*. The figure walks the boy from the visible sky over his head to the prophet at the head of the cycle.

*What is the likeness of the zodiac and its other stars?* — *They are like the twelve chiefs (nuqabā') — the chiefs of the speaking Imam. Its stars are his summoners (du'āt), who call to good with the permission of their Lord.* The *zodiac* is the *nuqabā'*; the *stars* are the *du'āt*. The figure walks down through the visible sky.

*What is the likeness of the earth and its breadth, which has been made a foundation for this creation and is praised?* — *It is like the gate (bāb, /baːb/) of the speaking Imam during his lifetime, and his successor (waṣī, /waˈsˤiː/) after his death. It is a guide for the people and a resolve for those who seek guidance, while they are knowers of the parables and the esoteric interpretation (ta'wīl, /taʔˈwiːl/).* The *earth* is the *bāb* / *waṣī* of the *nāṭiq*. The *gate* is the figure for the *ḥujja* who stands as the gate to the *nāṭiq* in his lifetime; the *successor* is the figure for the one who carries the office after the *nāṭiq*'s death. The figure brings the *batin* doctrine of the *ta'wīl* (the esoteric interpretation) into the catechism.

*What is the likeness of the twelve islands in the earth?* — *Parables of the twelve arguments (ḥujaj) — the arguments of the guardians.* The *islands* are the *ḥujaj* of the *guardians* (*awliyāʾ*) — that is, of the *imams* who succeed the *nāṭiq*.

*What is the likeness of its rivers and its springs?* — *The likeness of those who call to the good (du'āt).* The *rivers* are the *du'āt*. The figure that the cosmology used at the top of the chapter (rivers flowing from earth into seas) is now read as the *flow* of the call from the *du'āt* through the people.

*What is the likeness of the seven kingdoms of the heavens and their heavens?* — *They are the causes between God and the speaker (nāṭiq), and they belong to Him. These are the seven and the orbits (al-falak, /al ˈfalak/) — for these are the causes of the great God.* The *seven kingdoms* are the *causes* between God and the *nāṭiq* — the cosmic *intermediaries* by which the *nāṭiq* receives what he bears.

*What is the likeness of the seven seas?* — *These are the causes between God and the imams of the speaker. Take note of those causes.* The *seven seas* parallel the *seven kingdoms* at a lower level: they are the causes between God and the *imams* (the *nāṭiq*'s successors).

*What is the likeness of the salt water and the fresh water, and of their differences, upon the earth?* — *The salt water is like the knowledge of the apparent in which there is no da'wa (DAH-wah) and no summoners — for salt water yields neither sowings nor anything else. The fresh water is like the knowledge of the esoteric. As for the springs in the earth and the water that flows above the earth — likewise, knowledge: the esoteric is the knowledge of the hidden, while the apparent is the knowledge of the summoners, and the inward is the hidden esoteric.* The water-figure is now read in three: *salt water* is the *apparent* without the call; *fresh water* is the *esoteric*; the *flowing* water is the *du'āt*. The *batin* doctrine and the *zahir* doctrine and the *call* itself are all read off the kinds of water.

*How did water come to be like knowledge?* — *Because water is the life of every animal — and likewise knowledge is the life of every world.* The structural answer: water-and-knowledge are *consorts* in the same way that body-and-soul are consorts. Water is to the animal as knowledge is to the world.

## The air, and the highest similitude

The boy now turns to the seventh principle — the one that encompasses all the rest. *What is the air that surrounds all things?* The Master answers in a single line that announces the chapter's climactic figure. *The air is too great to be set as a parable.* The boy presses: *Is it greater, or are the heavens and the earth as great as it?*

The Master gives the chapter's most striking image. *Glory be to Allah, He is greater than both. The capacity of the earth within the air is like a ring on a flat plain; the expanse of the atmosphere within the sky is like a ring on a flat plain; and the breadth and diameters of the sky within the capacity of the air are like an egg on a flat plain. Whoever has the mind to grasp this image will have proof.* The figure: *the earth* fits inside *the air* the way a ring fits on a plain; *the atmosphere* fits inside *the sky* the way a ring fits on a plain; *the sky* fits inside *the air* the way an egg fits on a plain. The *air*, in this image, is *outermost*; the *sky* with all its diameters is small *within* the air; the *atmosphere* is small *within* the sky; the *earth* is small *within* the atmosphere. The cosmology is built outward to an *outermost* — and the outermost is *air*, the *seventh* principle.

The boy asks for the meaning. *What is its meaning?* The Master: *It is proof of the One who has nothing like Him — a refutation of what they associate with Him.* The air, in being *outermost and uncontained*, is the *structural argument* for the unity of God. Nothing surrounds the air; nothing is *like* the air in the cosmology. The cosmology *itself* refutes the associationists.

The boy now poses the objection that every Qur'anic reader will pose. *But is it not the Creator who said, "Do not strike parables for God"?* The Qur'an has *forbidden* the striking of parables for God. The Master's whole method in the chapter has been to *strike parables* — the heaven *is like* the *nāṭiq*, the zodiac *is like* the *nuqabā'*, the air *is like* the One who has nothing like Him. Is the Master's method not forbidden by the Qur'an itself?

The Master parries with the Qur'an. *Did He not also say, "And Allah has the highest similitude"?* The same Qur'an that forbids parables of God also says *Allah has the highest similitude*. The two verses must be read together.

The boy receives the move. *Glory be to Him who has placed enigmas in His book that come from His own door. You are one of its gates — show us how this is.*

The Master gives the chapter's key hermeneutic teaching. *Everything has a meaning, and the meaning of parables is this: the apparent is evidence of the hidden. He has forbidden that He be measured by anything He created — so one does not say, "This is like God," as one says, "This is like the Imam," "This is like the hujja (ḥujja, /ˈħudʒːa/) (argument)," or "This is like the dā'ī (dāʿī, /ˈdaːʕiː/) (caller)." Their likenesses became signs that point to them and distinguish them. Why then should it be necessary in matters of His greatness that the Creator be measured against something of His creation? It is not said, "This is like God." Rather, parables are taken according to their meanings, for their meanings carry signifiers — the lesser indicates the greater, and the greater indicates the supreme. Therefore He said: "And Allah has the highest similitude" — meaning the highest proof, for there is no evidence higher than the proof of God. He is the air: highest above the heavens and the earth, an ocean above all things; none of them surrounds Him. He is "the highest similitude" — which is the proof of the One who has nothing like Him. He did not say that there is nothing like Him (for there is indeed nothing like Him), but rather meant that there is no evidence for Him like that evidence — He has the highest similitude — not like His evidence in any other evidence from those who, of His creation, have described Him for us. May Allah bless the Lord of the Great Throne.*

The teaching is precise. *Parables* operate at two levels. At the *measurement* level — *X is like Y* in the sense that *X has Y's properties* — parables of God are *forbidden*. One does not say *God is like the air* in the sense that God is *gas* or has *extent*. At the *signifier* level — *X points to Y because X carries Y's meaning* — parables of God are *commanded*. The air *points* to the One who has nothing like Him because the *structural property* of the air (*encompassing-and-not-being-encompassed*) carries the *meaning* (*outermost-and-uncontained*). The Qur'anic *highest similitude* (*al-mathal al-aʿlā*) means *the highest evidence* — the *highest proof*, not the *highest comparison*. *He is the air*: not in *substance* but in *structural role*. The air is the *outermost* in the cosmology; the One is the *outermost* in the order of being. The figure works *as a signifier*, not as a *measurement*.

The boy is overwhelmed by the move. *The meaning has been magnified and the scope has expanded. Is the air something that stands between God and the greatness you have described in it?* The Master: *Yes.* The boy: *What is it?* The Master: *It is time.* The boy: *And what is time?* The Master: *It is the time of the will by which He created what He willed and commanded what He wished.* The air, in the *batin*, is *time* — and *time* in the *batin-of-batin* is *the time of the will*. The chapter's cosmology now closes back on its opening: the *will* is the *first* of the three words; the *time* of the *will* is the *air* that encompasses all things; the *air* is the figure of the One who has nothing like Him. The architecture is a circle.

## Three layers of knowledge: the egg

The boy senses he has gone too far. *You have brought me to the shores of the seas of the Most High. Return me to the sciences of this world and what is like them, so that I may use them in my affairs and be stronger by them to bear what I am meant to bear. I feared for myself, and felt sorry for my mind, lest it wander into what it cannot bear.* The boy asks to be brought back. He has been taken to the *shores of the seas of the Most High* — and he fears for his mind.

The Master honors the move. *You have risen above your question, and indeed you have done well. The apparent meaning alone is something in which your opinion and your mind become confused — so how would it be if I were to disclose an inner meaning to you? You and I would be like Moses and al-Khiḍr (al-Khiḍr, /al ˈxidˤr/).* The figure of *Moses and al-Khiḍr* — the Qur'anic story of the prophet Moses, who could not bear the inner meanings of al-Khiḍr's actions and at every turn protested at the outer — is given to the boy as the very figure of his own condition. The boy who has just received the cosmology is, *at this level*, in the position of Moses. He has not yet earned al-Khiḍr's inner.

The boy asks the question that the rest of the chapter rests on. *Is there, beyond this inner, an inner still more subtle?* The Master: *By my life, the inner is the highest of dwellings. It is greater in power and more complete than the apparent, for it is the ultimate guide to the path of salvation.* The *inner* is *the highest*; the *inner* is *the ultimate guide*. And *yes*, there is an *inner-of-the-inner*.

The boy: *I see here three layers of knowledge: the apparent, the inner, and the inner of the inner. Is there proof of this?* The Master: *Where else is the evidence except in this and in what it indicates?* The doctrine is *self-attesting*: the evidence for the three layers is in the *very figure* the Master has just given. The cosmology itself is the proof of its own *batin*.

*Then show me these three layers.* The Master: *I will teach you from the Book of Allah Almighty and from the argument of the intellect. This is what the audience of parables points to.*

The boy makes the move of one who is being given a doctrine he did not know was coming. *I did not think there was any doctrine beyond what I had already attained. If only I had commanded — God is the greatest of all things, to whom the illusions of the arrogant are exalted.* The boy is humbled. He had thought, until this moment, that the *batin* was the *innermost*. There is, he now learns, an *innermost-of-the-innermost*.

The Master gives the two proofs.

The first is the Qur'an. *As for the Book of Allah Almighty, He said: "O children of Adam, We have sent down to you a garment that covers your private parts, and feathers for adornment; and the garment of piety…" This is three: the outward, the inward, and the inward of the inward.* The verse (*Q 7:26*) names *three garments*: the *covering* (the outermost), the *adornment* of feathers (the middle), and the *garment of piety* (the innermost). The three garments are the three layers of knowledge.

*Al-Ghulām said: I am convinced.*

The second proof is the argument of the intellect. *Nothing is known except by a name, an attribute, or a meaning. The meaning is what the thing is; the name is what it is called; the attribute is what guides to it. In the same way, the religion of Allah has outward names and inward proofs that can only be fulfilled by each other. It is indicated by nothing but a noun or an attribute — like the saying of one who says, "The moon has risen": the meaning is the disk, "moon" is its name, and "has risen" is its attribute. If he were to say "has risen" and then fall silent, it would convey no meaning. If he were to say "moon" and then fall silent, it would also convey no meaning. Only when the name and the attribute are combined, and he says "The moon has risen," is the meaning clear and the speech sound.* The argument from speech: *meaning*, *name*, and *attribute* are the three faces of any knowable thing. *Likewise, if you say "apparent," it is necessary to say "the apparent of what?" If you say "inward," an answer is required: "the inward of what?" But if you say, "the apparent of the religion and its inward," the words are understood, and both become its religion — its apparent and its inward.* The apparent and the inward are *correlatives*; neither is intelligible alone.

The Master gives the closing figure. *As for the thing that has the name of an attribute, He said: "And give up the appearance of iniquity and its interior." So sin became a thing that has an outward and an inward. So too is the religion of Allah the Greatest. The one who says "the laws" has an outward that is outward, and an inward that is inward — each existing on its own.*

And then the chapter's signature image. *Knowledge itself: its meaning has many parables that indicate it, such as the hidden egg, which has an outward (the shell), an inward (the white within), and an inner of the inner (the yolk), which is its essence and meaning.* The egg as the parable of three layers: the *shell* is the *zahir*; the *white* is the *batin*; the *yolk* is the *batin-of-batin* — the *essence and meaning* (*al-jawhar wa-l-maʿnā*) of the egg. What the chick is *generated* from is the yolk; what *covers* the yolk and *separates* it from the world is the white; what *holds* both and *meets the hand* of the one who picks the egg up is the shell. All three are *necessary*; the *life* is in the yolk. The chapter closes on the figure of the egg — and on the doctrine that *the life of the religion is in its innermost-of-the-innermost*, just as the life of the egg is in the yolk.

The architecture has been laid: the three words, the seven principles, the seven heavens and *nāṭiqs*, the twelve constellations and *nuqabā'*, the twelve islands and *ḥujaj*, the rivers and *du'āt*, the seven kingdoms and seven seas, day and night, the pairs of creation, the seven attributes of the world, *zahir* and *batin*, the six limits and the seventh, the air and the highest similitude, and the three layers of knowledge. The boy has been walked through them all. He is *convinced*. The egg has been named but the yolk has not been opened.

*The apparent is evidence of the hidden.* *The lesser indicates the greater, and the greater indicates the supreme.* *Allah has the highest similitude.* The cosmology of this chapter is the grammar of the rest of the book.
