# The Father Revealed and the Three Faces of Seeking

*This chapter covers the fifth long teaching of* The Book of the Master and the Boy *(*Kitāb al-ʿĀlim wa-l-Ghulām*, ki-TAAB al-AA-lim wal-ghu-LAAM), attributed to Sayyidina Ja'far ibn Mansur al-Yaman (sai-yi-DEE-nah JAH-far ibn man-SOOR al-YAH-man). Chapter four closed on the father's anger. The boy had been brought to the* greater Shaykh *(SHAYKH), named on the seventh day, given the* highest transmission *the source itself veils, and walked back to the edge of his city — and his father had come to him, angry. This chapter is the* answer *to that anger. The father reproves; the boy offers him* two strands of gratitude *— outward courtesy with divided hearts, or open debate with confidence between them; the father chooses debate; the boy lays his bind — that the father is either a scholar who withheld the teaching, or an ignorant man who needed a stranger to do the teaching; the father weeps, concedes, and they debate at intervals until they are guided to the right path. Through them,* God revived many of His creation, *and the boy is sent out to preach with his father's blessing. Then — for the first time in the book — the source names them.* The boy's name was Salih, and his father's name was al-Bakhtari *(*Ṣāliḥ*, SAA-lih; *al-Bakhtarī*, al-bakh-tah-REE). News of them reaches a group who held to the old creed; they go to a scholar they call* Abu Malik *(a-BOO MAA-lik) — known among them as* the heel of the inks. *Abu Malik refuses to judge by anger; he humbles their hearts with* three states *(their religion is the same; it has been increased; or* a prophet has come to them and they have followed*); he teaches them the door of seeking, the sound heart, reverence-before-the-statement, the petitioner-before-the-king; he collapses the timetable from three days to two to* only this day*; he commands repentance, debt-paid, bathing, the purest garments, and the intention-of-the-needy. They all go out together. They arrive at al-Bakhtari's gate. Abu Malik calls al-Bakhtari* Abu Salih *(a-BOO SAA-lih, father-of-Salih) — and al-Bakhtari plays the title back open:* today my father is good, and I am the son of Salih. *Abu Malik names four transpositions that bewilder him and asks for a meeting with the* righteous slave. *Al-Bakhtari grants permission. Salih clears the council. And the chapter's central exchange begins. Salih addresses Abu Malik as* Ka'b al-Ahbar *(KAB al-AH-bar — the figure of pure imitation). Abu Malik concedes that religion stands above imitation. Salih lays out the* three faces of seeking *— a seeker who knows, a seeker who does not know, a seeker who knows that he does not know — and walks through the parallel between* seeking money *and* seeking knowledge*, then distinguishes* knowledge by report *from* knowledge by direct sight*. Abu Malik confesses dire need. Salih probes how Abu Malik knows him to be a bearer of capacity at all, without having seen him beside a prophet; gives the* essence-traders *parable of true knowledge tested in the forge. Abu Malik raises the question that frames the closing movement:* we know of no prophets and messengers in our time; nor have the people had any covenant with such men since the revelation was cut off by the books. *Salih answers:* any religion that is established only on narration is not called a religion. *The* Maqrub *(mak-ROOB) divorce-case shows Abu Malik himself, asked for a ruling, could only say* act on whichever of the two you wish. *Abu Malik confesses:* the Sharia *(sha-REE-ah)* of my age has been lost, corrupted, and disrupted, and we hold on only to the name of religion. *Salih:* what is religion?* Abu Malik gives the standard answer — command, prohibition, lawful, forbidden,* Sunnah *(SOON-nah), obligations. Salih:* so you have combined religion and Sharia. Then religion has been disrupted by the suspension of the Sharia — what is left with you?* Abu Malik admits:* I do not see that anything is left with me. So what religion do you have?* Salih answers with the positive doctrine —* the loop of the firm handhold, the rope of those who hold fast, the ark of tranquility and the ship of life and the light of life. *The chapter ends on the seam into chapter six: Abu Malik asks* now, since it has been lost, the attribute is preferable to him — so how is it described?*

## Where this chapter picks up

Chapter four ended on a single line: *then his father came to him, angry*. The boy who had been brought to the *greater Shaykh*, named on the *seventh day*, given the *highest transmission* the source deliberately veils, and walked back by the *scholar* who had brought him from chapter one — that boy had returned to his house with his heart absent from him in fear, *unable to return any utterance of his grief over the parting from his elder father and the father who had been the cause of his grace*. The *elder father* was the *Shaykh*; the *father who had been the cause of his grace* was the *scholar*. Then *his father came to him, angry* — the *biological father*, the *third father*, the one to whom the boy had returned because the chain itself was *traveling to another country* and had asked him to *be kind to him*. The father's anger is the *seam* the chapter opens on.

Chapter five is the *resolution* of that anger — a resolution that ends, against all expectation, with the *biological father walking into the company of the brethren* and *the two of them together becoming the cause for the guidance of a country*. It is also where the source — having walked the boy through four chapters without ever speaking his name in the world — at last *names* him, and at the same moment names the *father*, and a third man into whose council the *da'wa* (DAH-wah, the call, the missionary apparatus of the Ismaili tradition) is about to step. The chapter is the *naming of the world*; the chapter four naming was the *naming of the chain*; the two namings together complete the work the *seventh day* began.

## The father's reproach; the two strands of gratitude

The father comes in angry, and the chapter immediately gives him his first long speech. *O my son, is this the reward of sons to their fathers?* The reproach has the form of a settled grievance. *I have taught you only the best that a child can be taught by a father, and you have not denied your own self nor renounced your beliefs except from the time I grew old with this strange man. You drew him in upon me, and you crossed over with him in his affair without consulting your parents*. The *strange man* is the *scholar* — the *Master* of chapters one through four — and the father identifies him not by name but by *strangeness*.

The father then puts a bind to the boy that mirrors the bind the boy is about to put to him. *If what I knew was true, then you have hindered me in my very self, because you concealed the truth from me. And if it is false, then you have disobeyed me in your own ruin, and whatever has reached him has thereby reached me*. Either way, the father has been *wronged*. The reproach is structured; it is not hot anger but cold argument.

The boy answers without denial. *O my father, I have shown you nothing but mercy and compassion. You raised me as a mercy from you, and you nourished me, choosing me over family and wealth. I have no need to be so unkind to you in our day that I should make you the object of one of two strands of gratitude due to you*. The *gratitude in two strands* is the boy's framing — and the father, for whom the figure is unfamiliar, asks the question that lets the boy lay out his position.

*What is the difference between the two strands?* the father asks. The boy: *Either you allow me to offer you my courtesies and favors — for I am your son, whom you raised young, and the one who commands us to Allah — or you grant me leave to debate with you and to give you my confidence, so that I am safe from your indignation and may draw upon your mind*. The two strands are *outward courtesy* (the boy as obedient son) and *open debate* (the boy as truth-bearer). The boy is asking the father to *choose*; he will *give* the father whichever strand the father wants, not both. The choice is doctrinally framed: *outward agreement with divided hearts* (the chapter-one *taqiyya* of *stand with him with outward agreement, keep this your secret*) or *open debate*. The boy is offering the father what the *scholar* had refused him in chapter one — and offering it as a *choice the father may decline*.

The father chooses with surprising clarity. *As for your courtesy on the outside, while hearts differ within — this is a reproach between me and you. How long can outward agreement stand when its underpinnings differ? I do not want that. As for your debate and your candor, you have that — so say what seems best to you*. The father has read the *taqiyya* the boy is offering and *rejected* it; *how long can outward agreement stand when its underpinnings differ?* is his own doctrinal grasp. The opening of the conversation is the father's own consent.

## The boy's bind: scholar withholding, or ignorant needing a stranger

The boy now lays his counter-bind. *Have you seen my standing with you for forty years to be anything other than one of two strands? Either you are a scholar, and you have withheld from me the teaching I am owed — do not blame me, then, if you forbid me from seeking my own salvation through another. Or you are ignorant — and you are excused with me — but you needed a man who was a stranger to me. I will precede you to him because of your age and the lateness of your birth, for I have heard his words just as you have heard them, and I have seen what you have seen*.

The bind is exact. Either the father is a *scholar* who *failed his teaching duty*; or the father is *ignorant* and *needed the stranger himself*. In *both* cases the boy's having gone to the stranger is *defensible* — and in the second, the boy is now *ahead of the father*. The boy is offering his father a place — *behind* the boy — on the path the boy has crossed over to.

The source then signals the father's break. *The Shaykh's heart was rent by the boy's words, and he knew there was no escape for him from all of these arguments, and his eyes overflowed with tears*. The chapter calls the father *the Shaykh* — the Arabic for *elder*; the *title of age*, not the title of *the great Shaykh* of chapter four. The *Shaykh*-father weeps; he has been *cornered* by the boy's argument.

The father then concedes — and the concession is doctrinal. *O my son, the argument that has been made against me is yours. You will share a similar fate with me if you keep your affairs secret from me. Remember it for me — if it is true, I will accept dignity from you for myself; and even if it is false, I have rejected it for your sake, out of compassion for you*. He has *granted* the argument, *bound* the boy to him by the very logic the boy used, and *committed in advance* to *accept the truth* and *reject the falsehood* — *for the boy's sake*, *out of compassion*. He has crossed over by his own consent.

The source compresses the resolution into a single sentence. *So they debated at intervals and disclosed things to each other at length, until they were guided to the right path — acting upon the religion of God, upholding it, holding fast to it, calling others to it, and cooperating in it. Through them, God revived many of His creation. And He sent the boy out into the world to preach, with his father's blessing, and to ask others to come to him. The boy came back to them and became a cause for their guidance. God blessed the people of that country through them and at their hands. God commanded, and slander was disappointed*.

The sentence does the work of years in a single arc. *They debated at intervals*: not a single sitting but a series of conversations. *Disclosed at length*: the *taqiyya* between them is fully *lifted*. *Acting upon, upholding, holding fast, calling, cooperating*: the *full ministry*. *Through them, God revived many of His creation*: the *yellowed brethren* of chapter three find their *revival* through this father-and-son. *God sent the boy out to preach, with his father's blessing*: the *scholar*'s edge-of-the-city *call others to it* has been *fulfilled* — the *biological father*, who had been *opposed*, is now the *one who blesses the sending*. *Slander was disappointed*: the *enmity toward the people of this matter* of chapter one, the *anger* of chapter four — *defeated*.

## The names revealed: Salih and al-Bakhtari

And then, in a single sentence the chapter has been *building toward for five chapters*: *the boy's name was Salih, and his father's name was al-Bakhtari*. *Salih* (SAA-lih) — *the righteous one*; the name the chapter four naming-dialogue *would not give* until the *seven days* had *completed his birth*. *Al-Bakhtari* (al-bakh-tah-REE) — the family name. The two names enter the book at the same moment, in the same sentence, *together*. The boy is not renamed at his father's house — he is *named*, for the first time in the chapter's narrative voice, *while being named alongside his father*. The chapter has waited four chapters to name him, and when it does, it names *the pair*.

*They were honored — raised up before others, and given a high standing because of the excellence of the manners they had acquired*. The doctrine of *manners* from the Shaykh's *chain of rights* in chapter four (*good manners → knowledge → action → obedience*) is now visible in *Salih and al-Bakhtari*. *News of them spread through the land, along with news of their religion, until it reached a group of people who held to the old creed*.

## The community gathers before Abu Malik

*Al-Bakhtari had a long-standing influence among them, and they feared this would be cut off from them by his departure from their religion*. The fear is not *theological* — it is *social*. *So they went out and gathered before a scholar they called Abu Malik — the mighty servant — who was called among them, for his pride, the abundance of his judgments, and his learning in their religion, "the heel of the inks." He was their authority through his knowledge, his understanding of speech, and his discernment of opinions*.

Abu Malik (a-BOO MAA-lik) is introduced with three credentials — *pride*, *abundance of judgments*, *learning in their religion* — and one title: *the heel of the inks* (in Arabic, *the heel-print of those who write*, the *one who follows in the footsteps of the great writers of the school*).

*O Abu Malik, do you not see what the boy has gone over to?* *Which boy?* *Salih ibn al-Bakhtari*. *And what has he gone over to?* *A stranger has come to the country, with a doctrine and a creed he calls people to, and we do not know what it is. The boy went to him and went out with him, without his father's permission, and was absent for some time. Then he came back to his father, speaking the stranger's words and calling to what he was calling for. We have been told that his father has answered him in his opinion and given his garments over to him*. The *handing-over of the garments* is the *visible sign* of the *crossing*.

The community names three reasons for hesitation. *We are overwhelmed by what has happened and have held back from them for three reasons. First: al-Bakhtari was as a slave to us, on account of his father, for the brotherhood in religion he had taught us. We fear estrangement after intimacy, and we hate division after brotherhood. Second: if these people are upon the truth, then we have failed to follow them in it and to help them in it. Third: if they have gone astray in it, then we have failed to push them away from it and to support the truth and its people. You are the head of our religion and one of our standards, and we have come to tell you and to consult you, and we will not turn back from your opinion*.

The community has come *with anger ready*; the *possibility-of-truth* is *acknowledged in advance*; the *possibility-of-falsehood* is *also acknowledged*; *they will follow Abu Malik's ruling*.

## Abu Malik's three states; the figure of the breach

Abu Malik's reply is the chapter's first long *doctrinal* speech from the *old-creed* side — and it is striking because he *refuses to rule by anger*. *Judge gently, for I have given thought to this account, and you know that al-Bakhtari and his son were among those whom we used to count among the people of judgment and piety, and that they have rights and virtues over us from of old*. He starts by *registering al-Bakhtari's existing rights*.

*We cannot judge them by error and so wrong them, nor can we judge them by guidance and so wrong ourselves by imitating them; nor can we say they have been deceived and refuted without knowledge of them — they are nobler than that*. Three possibilities — *condemn as error*, *follow as guidance*, *dismiss as deceived* — Abu Malik rules out *all three* without *first-hand inspection*. *We need to look at this news with a fair eye, with the light of justice, and with the steadfastness of the mind*.

He gives the figure of the *breach*. *A breach in all things admits of repair and exchange of what was corrupted — like a flaw in food, whose harm is easy to remedy and whose cure is near at hand. But a breach in religion is gravely damaging; its flaw cannot be repaired, nor exchanged for another — like a breach in medicine, which does not dismiss its owner nor leave him to himself*. *Haste* in *religion* is *graver* than *haste* in *anything else*.

Then the chapter's *three states*. *But reflect on the affairs of these people, for they cannot be free of one of three states. Either the people are upon what we know of religion, and this incident among them is only an increase in literature, hadiths* (ha-DEETHS, the prophetic-tradition reports)*, poetry, and the abundant words of people; or they have given them an increase in what they hold in their religion, and they have rejoiced in it — and knowledge with every believer is a lost thing, and an increase of knowledge is better than a decrease; or the people have given them guidance other than what they have, by the prophecy of a prophet who has been brought to them and who has called them to it, and they have followed it clearly. This is not denied by the command of God, nor will the creation reach the end of God's decree in them or of His will in them, even if they strive their utmost. God is at work every day*.

State one: *they are still on our religion, with new wordings*. State two: *they have been given an addition, and the addition is good*. State three is the striking one — *the prophecy of a prophet brought to them*. In the *old creed*'s mainstream view *prophecy has ended*; yet Abu Malik says *this is not denied by the command of God*. He keeps the third state *open*. *God is at work every day*: the *door of guidance* is *not shut* by the closure of the canon. The *scholar of the old creed* voices the *three-states* doctrine that *includes prophecy after the books* as a *live possibility*. This is the chapter's first opening; it is voiced by *the opposition*, not by Salih.

## The community humbled; the tyrants

*O Abu Malik, we came to you with hearts angry against these people, ready to call them unbelievers, refute their opinion, and decide what we should do with their blood and their property. But we have found our hearts humbled by what you have said and by what you have done in arguing on their behalf and speaking for them*. The community had come *prepared for blood*; Abu Malik's *cooling* has *averted* the lethal turn *before any meeting*.

Abu Malik *catches the praise* and *deflects it*. *This argument is not for them — rather, the argument is for the truth. Do not betray it in their absence. If you make the truth's argument depend on whether the one arguing for it is present, then I am also defending them — and so I have defended myself and you against telling lies and against pelting in the unseen what we do not know*.

He gives the lesson of the *tyrants of the previous nations*. *We would then be like the tyrants of the previous nations, who fancied their doctrines in their judgment and rejoiced in their knowledge, until they thought they had encompassed all that God had commanded, taught, and intended to create. They said God would not send messengers after their messengers, and they did not consider that beyond what they had attained there was knowledge or doctrine. So they passed judgment that God should cut off His argument against His creation and His viceregents in His land*. The *tyrants killed the prophets of God* — and Abu Malik has just held the *blood and property* line *back* from *exactly* the same logic. *I ask you, by God: do not follow the whims of a people who have gone astray before you*.

## The door of seeking; the sound heart

The community asks Abu Malik to *take them*. *If you see fit, take us to these people, so that we may have their doctrine disclosed to us and look into it. If it is reasonable, we will follow it; and if not, we will avoid it*. Abu Malik tightens. *This is doubt on your part about what you hold, and one who doubts neither knows his right nor knows what is wrong about another*. They protest: *we stand firm upon our truth, and lie about whatever falsehood there may be*. Abu Malik: *this is not how meaning is sought, nor is there guidance through lying*.

*How then is the request to be made, if we do not deny falsehood and believe the truth?* Abu Malik: *If you already know the truth and accept it, and know falsehood and reject it, then you are not in the number of seekers — you are in the number of those learned in prophecy, guidance, and authority, who rule by revelation over the people of this world*. The *people of prophecy and revelation* are *not seekers*; they are *finders-and-rulers*. The *seeker* is *the one who does not yet know which is which*.

The community tries a halfway-house. *We are convinced of what we hold of the truth, and we have no doubt about it. We exempt ourselves from the procedure of the request*. Abu Malik refuses. *Have you not agreed that they should be granted favor over you in one respect, so that they should be credited over you in many? If you will not consent to be in need in the houses of the seekers, then you will sit in ignorance in the houses of the seekers*. *Refusal-to-seek* is *worse* than *humility-in-seeking*.

The community asks how the other community has come to have favor. *They have been preferred over you in their knowledge of what you know, and more of what you do not know. This is what has come to people: if you will not ask them for it, they will ask you for it; and if you will not listen to them, strive against them — then they will be credited over you, both by being the first to ask and by their striving. As for refraining from seeking, that is utter ignorance, neglect, and a defect in the world — so that he who knows must teach, and the ignorant must learn*. The *ignorant who refuses to seek* is *defective*; the *knower who refuses to teach* is *defective*; the *world's order* is *seeking-and-teaching*.

Cornered, the community asks Abu Malik to *open a door*. *Marveling at one's own opinion is blindness, and neglecting to seek is error. There is no shame in the request, but the seeker needs to know the doors of seeking before he can rightly know what to seek. Whoever seeks the truth without knowing how to seek is the quicker to deny it — because falsehood has the signs of hypocrisy and the appearance of acceptable opinions, while the truth has the signs of calamity and the contradiction of whims. Who then refrains from accepting what passion offers, and bears patiently in the face of calamity, except one whose heart is sound?*

The *signs of falsehood* are *attractive* (hypocrisy, acceptable opinion); the *signs of truth* are *unattractive* (calamity, contradiction-of-whims). The *seeker who does not know how to seek* will *mistake the signs*.

*As for the knowledge of seeking, you must know that you are needy. The needy person must humble himself before what he needs*. *As for the sound heart: it is the heart that does not persist in denying what is said to it, nor in being, even — the truth is what unveils itself and shows its merit, and falsehood is what reveals its own nakedness. Then the sound heart discerns falsehood by its intellect and accepts the truth by acceptance*. The *sound heart* is *not the heart that knows in advance*; it is the *heart that does not persist in denying*. Both *truth* and *falsehood* *self-disclose*; the *sound heart* lets the *disclosure land*.

## Reverence before; the petitioner before the king

The community asks an unusually fine question. *Has this reverence and humility for what is sought to be shown after it has shown its right, or before the statement is given?* Abu Malik: *Rather before the statement, so that you may grasp the statement with reverence*. The community presses: *if we revere what is sought until it becomes clear what it holds, and then its statement turns out to be misleading — what then is the standing of our reverence? Has it not been wasted?*

Abu Malik's answer is the chapter's most beautiful piece of *teacher-doctrine*. *No — rather, it was reverence to God and humility before His command, and you will have its reward, even if the one before whom you humbled yourself did not deserve it. For the seeker has fulfilled the duty of the request, even if he has not fulfilled the duty of the truth — like one who seeks something and stands before some king to ask for it, and is able to ask only with reverence. If he gives the petitioner something of what he asked for, he has fulfilled his duty of grace, and he earns the reward of what he gave. If he gives nothing, the petitioner has at least discharged the duty of asking gently for the matter, while stinginess obliged the other to withhold. So too with the seeker of knowledge: he must begin with humility, persistence, and patience in the face of adversity, until he fulfills the duty of requesting — and then time will bring him what it brings*.

The *reverence* is *not paid to the teacher*; it is *paid to God's command that the seeker humble*. The *seeker's duty is fixed*; the *outcome is not the seeker's measure*. The doctrine *frees the seeker* from *needing to know in advance that the teacher is true* before humbling himself.

## Only this day

*Yes — we will do what you have commanded, over three days*, the community offers. *No*. *Two days, then?* *Only this day, if you will do it; otherwise I go on alone*. The *collapse from three-to-two-to-one* is doctrinal: *seeking is not scheduling; seeking is now*. *Otherwise I go on alone* — *Abu Malik will go without them* — completes the force. The community concedes. *We repent to Allah from every sin, and we put aside what is owed; and we do what you have commanded — there is no power except in Allah*.

The *closing-formula* — *there is no power except in Allah* — is the *same formula* the *scholar* used in chapter four and whose *ta'wīl* (/taʔˈwiːl/, the inner-meaning) the *scholar* had given in chapter three: the *hawl* (/ħawl/) as the *Imam who turns* the *Creator's words*; the *quwwa* (/ˈquwːa/) as his *hujja* (/ˈħudʒːa/) and *bāb* (/baːb/). The community, *without knowing the ta'wīl*, has invoked the *formula* that *seals* the *crossing*. They have *crossed by their own mouth*.

*So they did so from that very day, and they all went out together until they came to the gate of al-Bakhtari*.

## At al-Bakhtari's gate: the wordplay on the names

Abu Malik opens with the question of *the boy*. *O Abu Salih, how is the good son? And where is he?* Abu Malik calls al-Bakhtari *Abu Salih* (a-BOO SAA-lih) — *father-of-Salih* — the *honorific* that names a man by his eldest son.

Al-Bakhtari's reply is one of the chapter's most exquisite *batin*-plays. *Today my father is good, and I am the son of Salih; and he is good with his Lord*. Al-Bakhtari has *inverted* the relation. *Today my father is good* — the *father* of *al-Bakhtari* (in the *zahir*, ZAA-hir, the apparent, his *biological father*; in the *batin*, BAA-tin, the inward, *Salih*, his *spiritual father*, for the *son* has now *led the father*); *and I am the son of Salih* (in the *zahir*, *Abu Salih = father-of-Salih*; in the *batin*, *al-Bakhtari* has *himself* become *the son of Salih*, *re-born by the son*); *and he is good with his Lord*. The *son* is now *the father*; *the father* is now *the son*; *al-Bakhtari and Salih have switched places*.

Abu Malik supposes that *Salih has died* — *to Allah we belong and to Him we return* — because al-Bakhtari has spoken of himself in the *register of death and return*. *Salih had died?* Al-Bakhtari: *Salih does not die and will never die. It is no figure of speech — the eternity remains*. *Salih* — *the righteous one* — *does not die*. In the *zahir* he is *a mortal boy*; in the *batin* *the righteous one is eternal*.

Abu Malik names the transpositions he has just received. *These are three qualities whose meanings have been transposed: the child has become the father, the Lord is in the earth, and the Lord has a servant come to Him from him; and the one who created annihilation has become eternal. A whiff of perfume to perplexed students — and behind this lie further wonders*. Four transpositions in three (the source's count): *the child has become the father*; *the Lord is in the earth* (the *generous one* of chapter three, the *one whose right is binding*, is *here, in the earth*); *the Lord has a servant come to Him from him* (the *batin* doctrine of the *cause* who *both comes from and returns to* the *generous one*); *the one who created annihilation has become eternal*. *A whiff of perfume to perplexed students* — the *batin* has *announced itself by scent*, not by *exposition*.

Al-Bakhtari: *O Abu Malik, name your need. Your question already places you at the threshold of an ordeal*. Abu Malik: *I take it as given that the first truth is itself an ordeal. My need is a meeting with the righteous slave*. Abu Malik wants to meet *Salih* — and names him *the righteous slave* (*al-ʿabd al-ṣāliḥ*), naming his *batin* name and acknowledging he belongs to the *generous one*. Al-Bakhtari: *There is no way to that — but did you mean my son, Salih?* — *no way to the generous one directly*; only to *Salih the chain-member*, and *only with his permission*. The chain-discipline of chapter four is in force at the gate.

The Shaykh-father rises in haste, enters upon Salih, tells him about Abu Malik and his companions, and explains *what he knew of the virtue of their minds*. Salih's response is liturgical. *O Allah, open the hearts of Your servants and guide them by Your mercy to the signs of what is sought; and expand our chests with the light of Your veiled light*. He *wipes his hand over his face*, *clears the council*, and *gives word to his father*.

The visitors enter and take their places. Salih opens: *O Abu Malik, you have favored us with your visit and outstripped us to goodness with your generosity; yet you were more deserving that we should have visited you, to preserve your affection and to discharge the duty long owed to you*. He *reverses the courtesies* — *registering Abu Malik's age and standing*, even as the *doctrinal authority in the council* is *Salih's*.

Abu Malik returns the honor and names the visit. *When did you not do what is virtuous, O Abu al-Khair?* — *father of the good* (a-BOO al-KHAYR), the *batin* honorific for *Salih the caller-to-the-good*. *You have honored us when young and esteemed us when grown; you have become for us a caller and a warner to the good. Your root is good, your branch is high, your work is pure — so good fortune to you*. Abu Malik picks up the chapter-four *root-watered-branch-raised* figure without knowing he is using it.

## Ka'b al-Ahbar: the rebuke of imitation

Salih turns to Abu Malik with the chapter's sharpest opening rebuke. *Their nation has come to me, O Ka'b al-Ahbar — is it imitation, then? Where is your perfect mind and the noble understanding I have always credited to you?* He calls Abu Malik *Ka'b al-Ahbar* (KAB al-AH-bar) — the early-Islamic figure who *transmitted reports from the People of the Book* and is, in the *da'wa* register, the *paradigm of imitation-without-direct-knowledge*. *Abu Malik has been treated by his community as a Ka'b al-Ahbar*, and *the community coming-with-Abu-Malik* is *coming in imitation*.

Abu Malik does not flinch. *Your stature is too high for mere prestige, and religion stands above imitation*. He has *separated himself* from *imitation* in the *first reply*.

Salih poses the *prophet question*. *How then can I have warners, when the warner is a prophet? And the prophet is an argument from Allah between Him and His creation, for them and against them. How then can he be an argument for you when he is not an argument against you?* The doctrine: *the warner is a prophet*; *the prophet is an argument-from-Allah, both for-them and against-them*. If Abu Malik calls Salih *a warner*, he is calling him *a prophet*; and *a prophet's argument-for* a person *requires being argued-against* — full obedience.

Abu Malik's response is one of the chapter's three great concessions. *I have come to believe; this is the obligation. We do not act on what is merely easy to say — we have begun with the best. I saw the truth before all else; he who has set himself up as a veil for religion and an imam for the truth has opened his doors to seekers. We have come to know the virtue of asking, so we sought you out and asked you for what was needed, intending you for our need*.

## The three faces of seeking

Salih teaches Abu Malik in turn. *O Abu Malik, is not the one whose virtue you have come to know also one to whom you owe a debt?* *Yes — and it is obligatory*. *When you came to know the virtue of asking, did you also come to know its truth?* *And what is its truth?* *To know its faces — and then to seek what is obligatory upon you of the truth in each*.

*What are its faces, and how many are they?* *The seeking is of three faces: a seeker who knows, a seeker who does not know, and a seeker who knows that he does not know. Which of the three are you?*

The three faces: *a seeker who knows* (one who *already has the knowledge* and *seeks to apply it*); *a seeker who does not know* (one who *lacks both the knowledge and the awareness of his lack*); *a seeker who knows that he does not know* (one who *lacks the knowledge but is aware of the lack*).

Abu Malik defers. *I am in need of knowing all three — how can I lay claim to a station I do not know?* Salih sharpens. *How can you claim to be a seeker at all, if you do not know the faces of seeking?* Abu Malik concedes. *I did not know them; so explain them to me*.

Salih unfolds the parallel between *seeking money* and *seeking knowledge*. *Money is the treasure of the people of this world, and knowledge is the treasure of the people of the Hereafter. The seeking of money is of three kinds: a seeker who asks for what is his by right, a seeker who asks for what is owed on terms, and a seeker who asks for alms. So is the seeking of debt*. Three kinds of *money-seeking* — *owed-by-right*, *owed-on-terms*, *alms* — each a *legitimate* form requiring a *different stance*.

*The treasures of the Hereafter are sought in three ways as well. The first is the seeker who is himself a knower — the divine scholar — who seeks out the dead in their ignorance to revive them with his knowledge. The second is the seeker who knows — the learned man who has mastered certain degrees of knowledge and seeks their full end and limit; he is the knowing seeker. The third is the ignorant man who is known by his ignorance: he knows nothing beyond knowing that he is in need; so he asks the scholars in order to know. These three are the faces of the seekers*.

The *first* — *the divine scholar* — is the *seeker-who-knows*; what he *seeks* is *not knowledge* but *the dead-in-their-ignorance*: *students*. The *second* — the *learned man* — has *mastered some degrees* and *seeks the full end*. The *third* — *the ignorant who knows he is in need* — *knows-his-need*, *does-not-know-the-content*, *asks the scholars*.

Abu Malik gives his self-diagnosis. *Yes — these are the faces of seeking, in religion and in the world. As for me, I am the knowing seeker who knows nothing but knows that he is in need. So make me know*. He has placed himself in the *third* face.

## Knowledge by report; knowledge by direct sight

Salih probes. *Did you come to know, or did you come to know-by-sight?* *What is the difference between the two?* *Knowledge by report is news; knowledge by direct sight is eye-witness*. *This too is something one needs to know*.

*As for the way of news — it is the case of an ignorant man who does not know that he is ignorant until he meets a scholar, and the scholar tells him of his ignorance; then he knows that he is an ignorant man in need of knowledge, by the scholar's word. His knowledge of his ignorance has come to him as report. As for knowledge of need and ignorance by direct sight — that is the man who already knows in his own mind that he is asking about something, but does not know the face of the answer. His chest is straitened by ignorance, and he hopes for no relief except by knowledge of what he is ignorant of; so his ignorance has itself become a knowledge of his mind. Ignorance is a need, poverty is the narrowness of need, and distress is a great need that compels the creature to seek capacity; and capacity is the breadth of knowledge, because knowledge is wide. By this, I know that you are in need*.

The *news-knowledge of ignorance* is *given by another*; the *direct-sight knowledge* is *self-discovered* — *the straitening of the chest* is the *body's witness* to the *mind's ignorance*. *Knowledge is wide* is the chapter's positive *batin* doctrine of knowledge as *capacity, breadth, room-to-stand*. Salih has *read* Abu Malik's *interior* by the *signs* of *distress*.

Abu Malik's confession is exact. *I find within myself everything you have just described. I know that I am in dire need, and my need has been broken open by you*.

## How do you know me? — the imitation rebuke

Salih now applies the *imitation*-test to Abu Malik's own coming-here. *I see you hastening the request before it is firmly established*. Abu Malik: *Poverty hastened me with its distress, and so I hastened it; and I knew you to be the preferred bearer of capacity, and so I asked*. Salih: *If poverty has hastened you, and you know the bounty of capacity, then how do you know what is required of the request?* *I have been graced by your grace; so I have come to you, and so I have asked of you*.

Salih: *How can it be true for you that I am known for capacity unless you first know it of me yourself? And how is it that I knew I was the one sought, so as to intend it for you? You have not seen me rub elbows with a scholar of prophecy, so as to know me by that; nor have you witnessed from me the proofs of evidence, so as to know me by that. Will you, then, raise your request to the ease of imitation? In doing so you wrong wisdom and you wrong yourself. You are like the man who leans on the bottom rail of the cage: if he cannot cut off the injustice of his opponent, then even greater injustice does the same to him. Likewise, the one who takes knowledge as a tradition — if he does not give thanks for it, and then leaves it, he is called a disbeliever. And you are wiser to me than this*.

The *imitation-request* wrongs *wisdom* and *the imitator*. The *taking-knowledge-as-tradition* (without *direct verification*) is *not stable*: the *taker* will *leave* it, and *leaving an unverified tradition* is — in the *da'wa* register — *disbelief*.

Abu Malik's reply has the *humility* of one *caught*. *I know that this is so; yet I hope that the truth is what proves itself. I would absolve you from having to make the confirmation, since dispute breeds dispute, and conflict breeds grudges — until each adversary demands his own opening over his opponent. When then will the truth finally take hold of the one who is propped up by falsehood?*

## The essence-traders; the science of heaven

Salih: *Yes — and I have not denied it: the truth does prove itself by its own merit and honor. But where rivalry is many, hearts are occupied with whoever speaks first. So there is no exception from what the scholars have laid down. They have said: every sowing whose ground is not first pleased with it gives no zakat; and every knowledge whose hearts are not purified for it by argument does not grow*. The *farming* figure: *unpleased ground yields no zakat* (za-KAAT); *unpurified hearts yield no growth*. *Argument* purifies *hearts*; *plowing* purifies *ground*.

Abu Malik returns the figure of the *jewel*. *Then call upon whatever arguments you wish. The words of wisdom are like the jewel — if you turn it over, its light shines, and if you set it aside, its bounty does not fade*.

Salih accepts the *jewel*-figure and reverses it. *I believed you — but that is only if the jewel is indeed an essence, and not a camouflaged glass that has no essence hidden in it. If you let dispute multiply, and the conflicts of religion become the tyrants of the world, then they clothe the essence in something other than itself and camouflage it with a likeness; and the applicant is then obliged to test the essence for him, so that its true worth may show and the camouflage and its people may be exposed*. *Knowledge* may be *essence* (the *jewel* of true *wisdom*) — or *glass-camouflaged-as-essence* (a *likeness*, *not essence*). The *applicant* must *test the essence* because *the world is full of camouflage*.

Abu Malik claims the test has *already been done*. *I have done justice and discharged the duty of truth. As for camouflage — we have come to know this creature and its defects, so we have rejected it, and have held fast to the opinion we have learned and called the correct essence of speech. We have done so, and you have agreed with us, and we have all clung to its rope together. If you can find a meaning more inward than what lies in essence, then by my life, jewels are preferable, each in its own right. Show us where the virtue of your essence lies, by an ordeal known to reveal its virtue. As for our essence, we have known it and made it known. Say of it, then, whatever appears to you*.

Salih's counter-parable is the chapter's *essence-traders* figure. *As for your essence, it was generous of us to grant it to you as a correct essence — until we presented it to the essence-traders and the people of metals, and they tested it for us in the forge. They did not accept it from us as essence; they said it was glass in camouflage, and they pointed out its defects to us — and so it fell from the limits of essence. But true essence rises with its own preciousness and by the knowledge of the people of insight, who give thanks for it and compete for it, so that its price reaches every sum. The essence-traders who know it, or who come to recognize it afterward, do not turn it down — except the people of arrogance, who neither buy nor sell. Likewise, knowledge is not knowledge until it is presented to those who know the science of heaven. If they accept it, it is true knowledge; and if they do not accept it, it is nonsense. Idleness is not permitted in wisdom, nor accepted by the wise*.

The *essence-traders* and the *people of metals* have *tested the old creed's essence in the forge* — and the *test failed*. *The old creed* — what Abu Malik has been *holding fast to* — is *failed glass*. *Knowledge is not knowledge until the people-of-the-science-of-heaven verify it*. Their *acceptance* makes *knowledge true*; their *rejection* makes it *nonsense*.

## But where are the prophets in our time?

Abu Malik raises the *prophet question* in a *temporal-historical* register. *And of those who know the science of the heavens — the prophets and messengers? We know of none of them in our time; nor have the people had any covenant with such men since the revelation was cut off by the books; and the nations are no longer condemned except by reports. So what do you say of this?*

The standard *old-creed* defense: *prophecy has ended, we have the books, reports condemn*.

Salih answers in *three steps* — the third the chapter's seam into chapter six.

*Any religion that is established only on narration is not called a religion, because religion is what is valued by God and admits no difference in it, whereas the narrations have not been revealed [as a single body of truth] — I know this from the disagreements among them and their denial of one another, and the religion of God is dearer than that*.

*Religion admits no difference*; *the narrations are full of disagreement and mutual denial*; the *narrations* cannot *be* the *religion*.

Abu Malik retreats to the *books*. *If they differ in the narration, they refer back to the books; those who agree with the books accept it, and those who disagree with them have lied*.

## The Maqrub case; the lost Sharia

Salih's counter-move is the *books-themselves* question. *But what if there is nothing in the books except the general — who then will make it specific for them? And what is detailed in them, who will deliver it to them? Who is trusted in matters of God's lawful and forbidden?*

Then Salih *names the case*. *I heard you on the day of the quarrel, when a man came to you — Maqrub — and said to you: "O Abu Malik, I swore an oath of divorce and broke it." You said to him, "Some people say she is forbidden to you and not lawful for you until she marries another husband, and some people say she is still your wife and is not forbidden to you by a single divorce." So the man said to you: "Which of these should I act on?" You said to him: "Act on whichever of the two you wish." Yet he does not know whether what he goes home to is lawful or forbidden. Is this how rulings work in the religion of God? Is this how God's lawful and forbidden are determined?*

*Maqrub* (mak-ROOB) has come with a *concrete legal question*. Abu Malik has given *two opposed rulings* (the *strict* — *forbidden until a second marriage*; the *lenient* — *still your wife*) and then *let the petitioner choose*. *Act on whichever of the two you wish* is the chapter's most pointed indictment of the *Sharia-by-narration*. *Maqrub goes home not knowing whether the woman in his house is his wife or another man's*.

Abu Malik's confession is one of the most striking lines the chapter gives to the *old creed*. *The Sharia of my age has been lost, corrupted, and disrupted, and we hold on only to the name of religion. What is the state of your religion?* He does not *defend the Maqrub case*; he *concedes the indictment in full*. The *Sharia of my age* (sha-REE-ah, the *legal-and-ritual code* of *Islamic law*) — *lost, corrupted, disrupted*. The *old creed* — *by its own most respected scholar's mouth* — has been *reduced to its name*.

## What is religion?

*And what is religion, O Abu Malik?* *Command and prohibition, the lawful and the forbidden, the Sunnah and the obligations* (*Sunnah*, SOON-nah, the *prophetic-practice norms*). Six elements; all *legal*.

*What is Sharia, in your view, other than this?* *I do not know of anything beyond what I have described to you*. Abu Malik has *not distinguished religion from Sharia*. *So you have combined religion and Sharia into a single thing?* *Yes*.

Salih draws the conclusion that has been *implicit since Abu Malik's confession*. *Then religion has been disrupted by the suspension of the Sharia, and corrupted by its corruption — what is left with you?*

The logic is precise. *Religion = Sharia* (by Abu Malik's definition); *Sharia is lost, corrupted, disrupted* (by Abu Malik's confession); therefore *religion is lost*. *What is left with you?*

The chapter records Abu Malik's silence. *Abu Malik was unable to answer the question. For a while he sat thinking about the matter of his religion. Then he raised his head, and said: I do not see that anything is left with me. So what religion do you have?*

The *long silence* and the *naked confession* — *I do not see that anything is left with me* — is the chapter's *zero point*. The *senior scholar* of the *old creed* has *arrived* at *nothing-is-left*, and *now asks Salih* what Salih *has*.

## Salih's positive doctrine; the seam into chapter six

*Religion is with me, and I am not veiled from it. The corruptors opened its doors to the seekers, and the seeker remembered the first one who brought it and followed in good; he sought it, and through it works were honored, and through its justice the laws flourished. It is the cause that connects heaven to earth, unbroken — the loop of the firm handhold, the rope of those who hold fast, the ark of tranquility and the ship of life and the light of life, generous in its content, safeguarded in its sciences, marked in its jewels, its virtue hidden from no one who looks upon it*.

The figures stack:
- *The cause that connects heaven to earth, unbroken* — religion as the *cause* (in the *da'wa*'s technical sense, the *intermediary chain*) between *heaven* and *earth*;
- *The loop of the firm handhold* (al-ʿurwa al-wuthqā) — the *Qur'anic* (qur-AAN) figure of the *unbreakable handhold*;
- *The rope of those who hold fast* — the *rope of God* of chapter three;
- *The ark of tranquility* (sakīna) — the *vessel of calm*;
- *The ship of life* — the *vessel that carries the believer through the sea-of-the-world*;
- *The light of life* — the *light* of chapter four's *Shaykh's opening discourse*;
- *Generous in its content* — *content* is *generous*;
- *Safeguarded in its sciences* — the *sciences* are *kept* against corruption;
- *Marked in its jewels* — the *jewels* are *recognizable to the people of insight*;
- *Its virtue hidden from no one who looks upon it* — *direct sight* yields *the virtue*.

Abu Malik takes the doctrine and *poses the seam-question into chapter six*. *If God has a religion by which He will judge on the Day, with which He is pleased, and for which He rewards and punishes, then in this sense it exists. But now, since it has been lost, the attribute is preferable to him — so how is it described?*

Abu Malik has *granted* that the *religion-by-which-God-will-judge* must *exist*; he has *also* held to its *lostness-in-the-age*. The *conjunction* of *existence-in-God's-judgment* and *lostness-in-the-age* is *resolved*, he hazards, *by the attribute*: *the attribute is preferable*. *So how is it described?* — *describe it for me*.

The question is the *seam* into chapter six — the long discourse on *justice and monotheism*, the *necessity of causes and messengers*, the *unbroken succession against the no-prophet-after claim*, and the *description of religion-by-attribute* that Abu Malik has just asked for. The chapter ends with the *question hanging*.

The chapter has done its work. The *father's anger* of chapter four's close has become the *father's blessing*. *Al-Bakhtari* has become the *boy's spiritual father* in the *batin*'s inversion *today my father is good, and I am the son of Salih*; *Salih* has become the *one through whom the chain extends into the world*. The *community of the old creed* has been *brought*, by the *senior scholar of the old creed himself*, to the *gate of the new*. *The three faces of seeking* have been *laid out*; *Abu Malik has placed himself in the third*; *the rebuke of imitation* has *landed*; *the Maqrub case has unmasked the lost Sharia*; *the identity of religion-and-Sharia has been broken by Abu Malik's own concession*; *the positive doctrine of religion-as-the-rope* has been *delivered*. The chapter is the *naming of the world*; the next chapter answers the *description-by-attribute* question Abu Malik has just posed.
