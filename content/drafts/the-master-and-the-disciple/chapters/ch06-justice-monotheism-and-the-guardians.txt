# Justice, Monotheism, and the Guardians of Allah

*This chapter covers the sixth and final long teaching of* The Book of the Master and the Boy *(*Kitāb al-ʿĀlim wa-l-Ghulām*, ki-TAAB al-AA-lim wal-ghu-LAAM), attributed to Sayyidina Ja'far ibn Mansur al-Yaman (sai-yi-DEE-nah JAH-far ibn man-SOOR al-YAH-man). Chapter five closed on Abu Malik (a-BOO MAA-lik), the senior scholar of the old creed, brought to a* zero point: *the* Sharia *(sha-REE-ah) of his age confessed to be* lost, corrupted, and disrupted, *and his identification of* religion *with* Sharia *collapsed by Salih's (SAA-lih) own argument —* I do not see that anything is left with me. So what religion do you have? *Salih answered with the positive doctrine of* religion as the cause that connects heaven to earth, unbroken, *and the chapter ended on Abu Malik's seam-question:* the attribute is preferable to him — so how is it described? *Chapter six is the* answer. *Salih calls Abu Malik to* God's justice and His unity, *then strips away the* monotheism-without-meaning *by leading Abu Malik through three failed approaches — the four letters of* Allah *(ahl-LAH), the* name-vs.-named *distinction, and the* analogy from human makers *— until* the door to the knowledge of monotheism has been closed before you. *From the impossibility of perceiving God directly, Salih draws the positive doctrine: there must be* causes for this knowledge between Him and His creation *— intermediaries chosen by God,* the just ones between Him and His servants. *Abu Malik concedes; Salih builds the structure — single justice, single judgment, ranks among the just, the* virtuous one *who brings revelation (in the* da'wa *(DAH-wah) register the* nāṭiq*, NAA-tiq, the speaker-prophet) and the* curious one *who brings the interpretation (the* waṣī*, /waˈsˤiː/, the successor, bearer of* ta'wīl*, /taʔˈwiːl/, esoteric interpretation). Then Abu Malik raises his community's great objection — that after their prophet* there is no prophet and no warner. *Salih's answer is the chapter's most devastating piece of historical-doctrinal indictment: the originators are* three, in every age — a wayward devil, a stubborn tyrant, and a hypocrite *— and the same lie was told to the nations of the Magi, the Torah, and the Gospel before this community. The* three qualities *of the prior nations against Abu Malik's community are laid bare; the killing of the just and the jurists' complicity is exposed; the absence of the guardians is named as* Taqiyya *(ta-KEE-yah, protective concealment). The chapter turns to Moses' flight from Pharaoh as paradigm; the jurists* show mercy to the slain but see no disobedience in the killer; *and the chapter's most pointed line:* were it not for the narrations of the jurists, no tyrannical king could ever be established. *Abu Malik repents; Salih weeps at the mention of repentance; al-Bakhtari (al-bakh-tah-REE) — Salih's father — counsels gentleness and* do not let it move you to inflict ta'zir *(tah-ZEER, discretionary religious chastisement)* upon them. *Abu Malik and his companions are guided and return to their people as warners. The author closes:* in this is the steadfastness of the messengers, the signs of the guardians (awliyāʾ, /ʔawliˈjaːʔ/), and the manners of the seekers. *The doxology blesses the Messenger Muhammad, peace and blessings of Allah be upon him; his guardian, the leader of the Muhajirun (moo-haa-jih-ROON); and the Imams of his house. The book ends.*

## Where this chapter picks up

Chapter five ended on Abu Malik's seam-question — *how is religion described?* — after the *I do not see that anything is left with me* confession and Salih's positive doctrine of *religion as the cause that connects heaven to earth, unbroken*. Chapter six is the chapter that *describes religion-by-attribute*. The attributes named are *justice* and *monotheism*. The argument the chapter develops is that *justice* without the *guardians of Allah* (*awliyāʾ*) is *only an attribute of an empty word*, and *monotheism* without *knowledge of the named One* is *only a description of nothing*. The two attributes are *one chain* — the same *cause that connects heaven to earth, unbroken* Salih named at chapter five's close, now derived from the attributes themselves.

## Salih's call: God's justice and unity

Salih opens with kindness. *The good is only good, O Abu Malik. Place your light in the lamp-niche of your mind and grasp what is meant for you*. He names Abu Malik's standing without flattery — *you may have rights, and the robes of arrogance may have dazzled you, and you may have taken upon yourself what is not your own — what you have imitated from the ways of those who follow you in charity, so that they listened to your speech, accepted your guidance, and trusted your opinion*. The *imitation* of chapter five's *Ka'b al-Ahbar* rebuke is named again, not as Abu Malik's failing but as the dazzle of the robe. *It is your duty to protect yourself and them from a fire that you cannot avoid passing through; so seek a way to escape it. As for what you asked — confirming what I have called you to — it is incumbent on you to establish it from the aspects on which it can be founded*.

Abu Malik consents. *All that you have said is proven true. As for the narrations of the common people, they are not valid for religion and not suited to it. We are left with only the knowledge you call us to*. The Sharia-by-narration indictment of chapter five is now Abu Malik's own conclusion.

Salih names what he is calling to. *I call you to God's justice and unity*. Abu Malik gives the easy answer — *yes, what you call to, we shall affirm; we know its excellence above all doctrines*. Salih refuses it. *That is not where I am going, O Abu Malik. That doctrine which means only descriptions of justice without knowing the One described, and the use of God's names without knowing His Oneness — this is the doctrine I refuse. What I call you to is knowledge of God's justice and the milestones of His unification*. The distinction is exact: *descriptions of justice* without *the One described* is *empty justice*; *names of God* without *knowledge of His Oneness* is *empty monotheism*. Salih is refusing not *the words* but *the words without their meanings*.

Abu Malik protests — *if the view of the people of justice is corrupt and their monotheism is denial, then there is no firm truth on the earth*. Salih corrects the framing. *Justice and monotheism are among the noblest of all doctrines. Monotheism is too great to be corrupted, because there is no opposition to monotheism; and justice is not corrupted, because it is the judgment between God and His creation. But all the doctrines that have corrupted the saying of justice and the rest are spoiled — it is a claim by which no statement is proved. Defend whichever doctrine you wish and imitate it, until I strip it from you under the pretext of truth*.

Abu Malik refuses to defend anyone else's doctrine — *I will defend before you only what I myself say*. His creed in one breath: *I say that God is One, nothing is like Him, just in His ruling, who does not burden His worshippers with what they cannot bear. He commanded them and forbade them — not by absolute decree, nor by force, but by choice and warning — so the obedient did not obey by His trickery, and the disobedient did not disobey by His coercion*. Clean, classical: *unity, justice, free will, choice, warning, no decree to disbelief*. The doctrine Salih has just called *among the noblest*.

## Names and attributes need their meanings

Salih opens the dismantling. *Did I not tell you that this doctrine is among the noblest? But these are names and attributes of no use unless there is a meaning behind them*. The figure: *as when you say, "fire is hot" — you have believed the words, but the truth does not benefit you, because your saying "fire is hot" is the name of an attribute that does not benefit you if the speakers do not cook food with it and do not light it; they remain in darkness without the meaning of fire being present. And if names and attributes stand in place of meanings, then let your tongue burn at the mere mention of fire, and let the mention of food satisfy you*.

*Fire is hot* is true. But truth alone does not cook food or light a lamp — meaning does. If names had the power of meanings, the name *fire* would burn the tongue and the name *food* would satisfy hunger. They do not. *Therefore* names and attributes *require their meanings*. Abu Malik concedes: *show me that my words about justice and monotheism are nouns and attributes that require knowing what is described*.

## The four letters of "Allah" — the door of monotheism closes

Salih begins on the monotheism side. *Your saying that God is One — and "One" is four letters; we see two letters joined and two letters separate. So which of them is "one," or are they all together "one"? And you say that "Allah" is four letters: is the alif (AH-lif) alone "O God," or are all the letters together "Allah" — the evidence of God — or do you say otherwise?*

The question is grammatical-physical. *Which letter* of *Allāh* is the *evidence of God*? Is the *alif* alone? Or all four together? The spelling of the most-said name of God is being made to prove its own emptiness without meaning. *Abu Malik was struck silent*. He ventures: *I find no way out of this, except that each letter is a sign of God*. Salih presses — *which letter indicates God? Or what is it that indicates God — the letters by their separation, or by their joining?* Abu Malik gives up the door. *I do not know what to tell you about this. Take us on to another chapter*. Salih names what has just closed. *The door to the knowledge of monotheism has been closed before you. Who said this to you in this way, O Abu Malik?* No one had said it this way to Abu Malik; he had inherited the creed without ever being pressed on the spelling.

## Name vs. named: which is worshipped?

Salih turns to the next door. *I ask you about your knowledge of God — where is it from, and who took it? Is there an attribute that indicates a described one?* *No*. *Does that described one have a name?* *Yes*. *And what is the name?* *Allah*. *Is "Allah" a name, or the named?* *It is the named*. *Then what is the name?* *Allah*. *So "Allah" is a name for Allah*. *Yes*. Abu Malik has just agreed that *Allah is a name for Allah* — and the formulation has put the *named* and the *name* into the same syllable. Salih lays the trap. *What is the difference between the name and the named? Which of the two is worshipped — or both?*

The trap is grammatical-doctrinal. If *the name* is worshipped, the worshipped one is the *word*; if *the named* is worshipped, the worshipped one is *not the word*; if *both*, *two* are worshipped and monotheism has failed. There is no good way out while the name remains the only mediator. Abu Malik tries the third path — analogy.

## The analogy of the makers — God did not create from anything pre-existing

*I find no doctrine in this except that I have seen all the crafts — each of them has a maker — and so I judged that this great creation has a Creator who is like nothing He has created, just as makers are not like the things they have made*. Abu Malik has reached for the teleological argument.

Salih answers with the chapter's longest single argument. *O Abu Malik, you have departed from the meaning of the Qur'an (qur-AAN). The first issue is that the narrowness in the meaning of monotheism forced you to seek Him by analogy and to set up parables for Him — and God is dearer than that, because everything made is made of three things, and God did not create things from anything that existed before them*. Three things in human craft: a maker, a material, a form. Every human making presupposes *something already existing*. But *God's creation* is *not from anything pre-existing*. The thing being compared *to* is making-from-something; the thing being compared *is* making-from-nothing. The likeness fails at the root.

*As for these makers — you know them by watching some of them with your own eyes; so you judged about the making of something you did not see, judging a maker by what you observed of an action similar to the actions of the makers you have seen and known — by comparison. But when God created, you did not witness the creation of what He has made, nor did He set up a parable for His making in the manner of the makers — yet you have testified to a parable that is absent. What gives you the right, O Abu Malik, to be satisfied with analogy in your religion?*

Then the exact correction. *Your saying "nothing is like Him" proves, from your own words, that He has a likeness and that nothing is like that likeness. Had you meant monotheism, you would have said, "He is not like anything," and you would not have said, "nothing is like Him"*. The correct form is *He is not like anything*; *nothing is like Him* still posits a *Him* whose likeness nothing matches. The grammar of monotheism is more delicate than the words.

*Glory be to the One whom no determination of thought can grasp, and whose sign no ambitions of perception can attain; the Almighty is too exalted for the description of those who are charged*. Salih extends: *if you had intended by this question one of His servants or a king from among His angels — and you are unable to comprehend what is said about that one, how he was created — then consider: the lights of the people of the earth fall short of perceiving the light of the sun, and the sun is but one of the lights of the world; so how shall one who falls short of perceiving a creature of His perceive the Creator?*

Abu Malik *struck the ground in his confusion*. Salih asks him to raise his head. The only honest answer: *speech is gone, there is nothing to say; minds are confused about the answer, illusions are astonished by thought, the faculty is too weak — one can perceive Him only by request and inquiry*. The last clause is the door to the chapter's positive doctrine.

## Sight closed, speech closed — the necessity of causes

Salih takes Abu Malik through the implications. *The One who is of such greatness — will sight perceive Him?* *No, He is higher than that*. *Will speech be heard from Him?* *There is no speech except from a speaker, and the speaker is described by words — and God is greater than such attributes*. Sight is closed; speech is closed. The two channels by which a human being knows another human being are closed between creature and Creator. And yet — by Abu Malik's own creed — God has commanded and forbidden. How does the command and prohibition reach the creature?

Salih lays the justice-question before the epistemology-question. *Is He unjust in His creation, so that He charges them with what they cannot bear and commands them with what they cannot grasp, and then punishes them if they do not?* Abu Malik: *whoever charges the obedient one with what he cannot bear has laid a sin upon him, and his punishment is aggression — and God is dearer than that*. *Would He be content with ignorance from those who serve Him?* *Ignorance is a certain denial of thanksgiving and a continuation of disbelief, and God does not love all the unbelievers*. *Were they then created as scholars who already know what is wanted of them?* *No, but He created them ignorant — they did not know anything*.

*Created ignorant*. *God does not love ignorance in His worshippers*. The gap must therefore be bridged — and not by the worshipper's own resources. *Then from where do they have knowledge of what He wants?* *They learned this from God*.

Salih asks the delivery question. *If He is higher than sight can reach, wider than perception can grasp, and greater than the limits of speech, then how does the one whom He forbids and commands come to know — unless you accept that there are causes for this knowledge between Him and His creation? Are there intermediaries who deliver His justice and bear witness to His witnessing for them and against them — whom He has chosen by His knowledge and selected from all His creation, and made them messengers to them and proofs against them, so that they stand as the just ones between Him and His servants, conveying His command and prohibition, so that obedience to them is obedience to God and His pleasure, and disobedience to them is His wrath and punishment? Otherwise, how could the command come from God to His creation?*

The positive doctrine arrives. *Causes between Him and His creation* — intermediaries, bridges, chosen ones. The chain Salih named at chapter five's close is now *derived from* the attributes of justice and monotheism themselves.

Abu Malik concedes — and the concession is grand. *This, for me, is from the foundations of justice. As soon as creation needs God's justice to teach them what He wants from them through the hands of the righteous one whom He has created for that purpose, then each shall thank his benefactor, be rewarded by Him as He wills, be reproached by Him, and punished by Him if He wills*. Abu Malik has crossed from justice-as-attribute-of-God's-rule to justice-as-the-chain-by-which-God-rules.

## The unity of judgment; ranks among the just

Salih draws the next step. *Is it permissible to rule with justice in any way other than this?* *It is not permissible for anyone but God*. Salih puts the alternative — *it would be permissible for Him to delegate the command to some of His people — to set them up justly against their own wrongdoing, and then to assign two others to do the same thing, and so on without distinguishing them more justly than the first two*. Parallel delegations, competing chains. Abu Malik refuses. *The judgment can only be one, because justice is one. If the ruling were divided into two meanings, one of them would inevitably perish*.

Salih extends. *Has it not been established for you that the judgment of Allah is one, that there is no difference in it, and that it is one practice unchanged in His messengers? And were it not for the succession of the messengers, and the commanding and forbidding, the Truth would have no growth and no meaning toward creation*. Without the succession, the Truth is a name without a meaning — the very failure Salih opened the chapter on.

Abu Malik concedes and asks the ranks-question. *The judgment could not have been otherwise; otherwise He would have falsified His messengers and broken His promise. But tell me about these ranks: is it weightier in the sight of Allah for some of them to be superior to others, or not?* Salih: *how could they stand level in rank when they are bound to obey one another? And were they on the same plane, with no disposition of obedience among them, then by His justice He has preferred some above others, so that obedience may be rendered from the virtuous to the more virtuous, and from the virtuous to the one above him, until it culminates*.

Abu Malik asks the reward-question. *Is the one who obeys the virtuous among them, and the virtuous one himself, equal in reward?* Salih: *yes, this is the justice of Allah, which obliges every obedient one to receive his reward*. Reward attaches to the obedience, not to the rank of the one obeyed. Abu Malik: *is each of them like his counterpart?* Salih: *no, because they come from the Creator with knowledge of the unseen — the Books that He has revealed to them — and no one can produce the like of them except from Allah*.

Abu Malik asks the question the chapter has been building toward. *Then can anyone imitate the curious one among them, if no book is sent down to him in which knowledge of the unseen is revealed?* *No one can claim that station*. *And how could that be, when they did not display the seven creations of the unseen, as the virtuous one himself displayed them?*

## The virtuous one and the curious one — the nāṭiq and the waṣī

The doctrine the chapter has been circling now lands. *If the virtuous one displays himself to others with knowledge of the unseen revealed in the revelation, then the curious one likewise displays himself to others with knowledge of the unseen in the interpretation of the Qur'an — whoever has it from Allah receives it and interprets it, and he receives nothing except from Allah by revelation*.

The *virtuous one* — in the *da'wa* register the *nāṭiq* (NAA-tiq), the speaker-prophet — bears revelation (*tanzīl*) and displays himself by it. The *curious one* — the *waṣī* (/waˈsˤiː/), the successor, the foundation-of-interpretation — bears the interpretation of the Qur'an (*ta'wīl*, /taʔˈwiːl/) and displays himself by it. Both receive from Allah by revelation; both bring knowledge of the unseen.

*And the curious one is also inspired?* Salih: *yes, and therefore obedience to him became obedience to Allah*. *If you place him on a level with the virtuous one in obedience and revelation, why call him "curious"?* *Because of the obedience due to the virtuous one and the need for him*. The naming is a ranking of obedience-flow and need-of-the-other, not of inspiration-quality.

*And what is his need for the creature, if the revelation comes from the Creator?* *When revelation comes to him, he brings it with an interpretation of the hadiths, the books, and the rest of what the virtuous one left behind — as it was revealed to Lot at the hands of Abraham, and Abraham's virtue was made plain; and as it was revealed to Ishmael and Isaac, so that goodness, blessings, and credit accrued to Abraham. The revelation from Allah is bound to His justice in His land, in proportion to their ranks. He does not, in His justice, will to cut off His command and prohibition and His worship after He has blessed people with it, so that worship would be cut off and ignorance permitted*.

The succession is the form of God's not-cutting-off. *God does not, in His justice, will to cut off worship*; the chain is built into God's justice.

Abu Malik: *the author of the revelation has shown his trustworthiness with knowledge of the unseen in his book; does the author of the interpretation likewise demonstrate that he has knowledge of the unseen — such that the revelation bears witness for him and obliges obedience to him uniquely, as it does for the bearer of the revelation?* Salih: *he comes in his interpretation with knowledge from heaven and a declaration from on high, and the Book bears witness to it*. The Book — the virtuous one's revelation — bears witness to the curious one's interpretation. Inside the Book itself is the warrant for its interpreter.

Abu Malik draws the conclusion. *I accept what the Books bear witness to — an interpretation that the revelation testifies to as revelation from Allah; otherwise all the books and the messengers would have to be rejected, since their meaning is the same. If they give the lie to one another, all must be reckoned liars; and if they believe one another, all must be believed*. Either accept the chain whole, or reject it whole — there is no middle.

## The objection: "no prophet after our prophet"

Abu Malik turns to the historical objection. *If obedience is obligatory by prophethood, revelation, and interpretation, then what about those who sit in their councils, who resemble them and yet are not them? They all agree that after their prophet there is no prophet and no warner in their time, and that they are the ones who obey the command of Allah and His religion. You say otherwise — so how could the scholars of this community fail to consider this period?*

Salih's answer is the chapter's most piercing sociology of error. *What they have left unexamined is not sound. They have no one to discuss it with them, so they listen to it only with their own minds, while it has been drilled into their hearts that this is the first of the commands of Allah and His religion, and that there is no prophet after their prophet — just as the seducers of the nations before them said. Every nation has claimed that there is no prophet after its prophet*.

## The originators of the lie: devil, tyrant, hypocrite

*The originators of this were three, in every age: a wayward devil, a stubborn tyrant, and a hypocrite. By saying this, they sought to cut off the marks of the prophets from the features of prophethood and turn it into a station shared among themselves; but they could not, until they had instilled in the hearts of the nations the saying, addressed to every nation, that Allah did not send anyone better than your prophet, that there is no prophet after him, and no warner*.

Three originators: the *wayward devil* (the spiritual seducer), the *stubborn tyrant* (the king who needs the prophets gone so that he may reign), and the *hypocrite* (the jurist who legitimates the king's need). *In every age* the same three recur. Their work is to *transfer the prophets' authority into their own pockets, and close the door behind them*.

*So they whispered to them from a quarter they did not perceive, and led them off from a direction they did not know. Every nation clung to its own prophet by holding all others after him to be liars, supposing that this drew them nearer to their prophet. Thus mention of prophethood and the prophets' marks ceased among all the nations on the strength of that saying — and they did not seek what they had lost, nor would they have believed had they sought it. So the seducing devils were able to seduce those they seduced, and the powerful were able to kill the prophets, and they killed them; and the doubt-mongers among the corrupt jurists adorned themselves and were destroyed and destroyed others. All of this because no hearts inclined from creation toward the prophets when they appeared, and they did not seek them out when they were lost. So they despaired of the prophets, until they had to call to the truth aloud — and were called liars — and the prophets among them were killed in anger*.

## The Magi, the Torah, the Gospel, and this community

Salih names four nations. *This is the nation of the Magi, clinging to its tyranny, worshipping its idols upon its fire, saying — according to their claim — that Allah will not send a messenger after their messenger. And this is the nation of the Torah, clinging to its books, working at its labor, standing within the bounds of its prophets, humble before its religion, saying that Allah will not raise up a messenger after Moses. And this is the nation of the Gospel, by its analogy claiming that its prophet is its God, approaching with its crosses, glorifying its priests, saying that Allah will not send any messenger after Jesus*.

*And this is your community, following the paths of those who came before it, seduced by what seduced them: it inherited the Book from other than its people, followed the religion without its people, followed its great ones, obeyed its masters in error, feared its tyrants and its jurists, so that they established for them a religion other than the Sunnah (SOON-nah) — praying at a different time, paying zakat (za-KAAT) on something other than its proper due. They are patient with them, persistent in their need, certain in saying that Allah will not send any messenger or warner after their Messenger*.

Four nations, one error; only the names change. *So you and your peers followed the saying of the greater majority, and your patron was the abundance of consensus and the oppression of the sultans; you accepted it from them and sweetened it with the best of your doctrines, preferred it with the goodness of your words, and made the argument from justice in what no argument can be made against you. So you held forth, supported from above by the mighty for prestige, while others follow you for desire, supposing that this is the truth*.

## The three qualities of the prior nations

*If this community must believe what it has decreed against Allah — that His arguments have been cut off from His creation by a single strand of consensus — then the nations are even more entitled to ratify three qualities by which they must hold an argument against you*. Abu Malik asks for them.

*The first strand: they say they are earlier in the religion of Allah than you, and they have the merit of those who came before. The second: the consensus among themselves and with you to believe what they say — for they have the merit of being truthful. The third: your own testimony to them that their prophets are truthful and that they are the messengers of Allah; and none of them has testified for any prophet of yours, neither to prophethood nor to truthfulness. They have a standing against you by your unanimity with them in saying that what you possess of knowledge is copied from their knowledge — and theirs is the origin of your knowledge*.

The asymmetry is exact. *Earliness, consensus, testimony* — all three favor the prior nations if those criteria rule. If *consensus and earliness* close the door, *they win every argument*. The community's own logic, applied honestly, hands the world back to the prior nations.

*It is clear that justice in the rule of creation is obligatory upon the Creator, and the judgment of these people is obligatory; and if the Creator, the ruler of His creation, abrogates what He wills and confirms what He wills, then no one's ruling outranks His. Every man's saying falls away, and the Creator is foremost over creation and over the command, and every day He is engaged in some affair, and His action is not to be denied — even if He sends a warner every day*.

Abu Malik: *this community has gone far astray*.

## The just one is the guardian of Allah

Salih names the *positive identification*. *Most of the first two went astray before them — for they cast off the guardians of Allah, abandoned their rights, and allowed the foolish among them to prevail in their command, so they made lawful what Allah had forbidden, deliberately, lest they have to imitate prophethood — so people might lose track of the prophets and not kill them on that account. Yet they distracted them with kingship and largesse and led them to indulge their whims. Those known for honesty and piety on the surface helped them in this — until the community was stripped of those who would command good, and led toward those who would forbid it; and they called the partisans of falsehood the guardians of Allah, so that truth became falsehood — like light and darkness in their difference. Were it not for the remnants of Allah's guardians and His justice in the earth, and the revelation that comes to them, only a few of the honest would have remained against the many of the false. Otherwise, by what could Allah distinguish the evil from the good — except by His servants? And how could Allah's argument be conveyed to all creation, except through them?*

Without the *remnants of Allah's guardians*, the few honest would not have survived. *God's argument is conveyed through them*. *God distinguishes evil from good through them*. They are the form by which God acts in the earth.

Abu Malik: *there is no escape and no avoidance of the justice of Allah and His command. With the multiplicity of opposites, none but the just servants of Allah remain — and by them the argument of Allah against His creation is upheld*. Salih: *then it is established that the company is one of the prophets of Allah — namely, the just ones — and is the one among them called "the just"?* *Yes, he is the just one*. *It has been established for you that the just one is the guardian of Allah*. *Yes, this is true as a saying*.

Salih draws the coextensivity. *Whoever does not understand the one being described, will the description benefit him?* *The description does not benefit him except by understanding the one being described*. *Then whoever does not know the just one of Allah and His guardian, will his speech be just?*

Abu Malik's smile is the first breakthrough. *You have brought me out of my chapter on justice and monotheism, and what has brought me out of it is the truth itself, in the clearest of arguments and the kindest of speech. After this, what chapter remains to be proved?* Salih: *from here, O Abu Malik, the camouflaged glass falls away from those who deal in the colors of jewels — they could not recognize its virtue, and when it is set before me, the essentialists are exposed, and those who are deceived by them are exposed*. The chapter-five *essence-traders* parable returns: the old creed's glass-in-camouflage falls away.

## Where are the causes in our time? — the Taqiyya doctrine

Abu Malik raises the temporal question. *I have believed and acted accordingly. But how shall we be safe from the seducers of the nations, and know the causes of Allah, or the cause among them, when we do not perceive them in their time — and in our time there are neither prophets nor messengers, as there were in the nations before us?*

Salih takes the question as a slip. *Glory be to Allah, O Abu Malik — you keep speaking of the justice of Allah at every moment of your life, but as soon as We have proved it to you in your own presence, where it is obligatory upon you, you turn back and say that you would be deceived about Allah, and attribute injustice to Him*. Abu Malik defends himself — *God forbid; whoever says such a thing has transgressed*. Salih: *you say the age of the prophets has passed and that in our own time there is neither prophet nor messenger. Was the sending of those earlier two ranks simply for the sake of being worshipped, or was it in vain?* *May Allah forbid — only what is just issues from Him*.

Salih now lays the whole indictment. *Then what has excluded us from that justice? Are we not worshippers as they were? They were created as we were created, and we are bound in service as they were bound. Has the obligation been lifted from us after it was made binding on them? Or has the giving run out in our time, so that we cling to Him only by virtue of what was given to them? Or has the Sunnah of Allah changed in our case after being firmly established in those who came before us — so that we should judge that God has changed? Or is justice a duty upon us while its instrument is left lawful for us to neglect, when it was forbidden to those before us — when there is no substitute for the words of Allah and no overruling of His judgment? And who deserves the name "just" except the guardians of Allah? Justice is not found among those God created, except in those whom God Himself chose — they became just by His command. That is why a judge is called "just" for the sake of the one who appointed him, and a true word is called "truthful" because of the truthfulness of the one who speaks it*.

Every one of the five questions answers itself with *no*. God has not changed; the obligation is not lifted; the giving has not run out; the Sunnah of Allah is firm; justice still requires its instrument. The cause must therefore still be present.

Abu Malik concedes and reaches for the interregnum-defense as a fallback. *It is indeed so, and we are not permitted to say anything of God except the truth. Were we to follow our own opinion in speech and judgment in the absence of the rightly-guided among us, we would have abandoned His judgment. This is part of Allah's ruling and His Sunnah with us, just as it was with those before us during the interregna between one messenger and the next — like the period between Abraham and the time of Moses and the Torah, and the period between the Torah and the time of Jesus and the Gospel. We have been on that same footing, working through the interregnum by relying on the traditions and the Sunnah*.

## There was no interregnum

Salih dismantles the defense by counting. *The religion of Allah is too precious to Him for that. Was there really an interregnum after the religion of Abraham, when Allah sent after him Ishmael, Isaac, Jacob, Joseph, Jonah, and Shu'ayb (shoo-AYB), leading up to Moses? Then He inspired them with righteous deeds and the call to the religion of Abraham, the establishment of prayer by its Sunnah, and the giving of zakat by its obligation, until the Torah was given to Moses. So what interregnum stood between Moses and the Gospel, when Allah sent Joshua son of Nun after Moses, and Elias, Talut (taa-LOOT), David, Solomon, Zechariah, and John, leading up to Jesus? They stood by the ruling of the Torah in its religion, calling to it and reciting its Psalms, until the Gospel was revealed to Jesus. The same applies to those who passed before them and those who came after them. We have seen great evidence among them — only Allah knows their full count — and their messengers brought them proofs. Allah did not leave them; He set parables for them, and He was the one calling them to the truth. So where is this "interregnum," and when was it? Allah Himself said, "After a succession of messengers," and so confirmed the messengers. The period after the rising of the call came after fear, so they prayed in secret and concealment. Why then would they cut themselves off from the earth? Allah has made it so that, in the blink of an eye, they are either openly present or hidden in fear, veiled*.

Six prophets between Abraham and Moses, named. Seven between Moses and Jesus, named. Each of them inspired. There was no interregnum. The Sunnah of Allah is not gaps; it is *continuous succession*. The just ones in any age are present — in one of two modes. *Openly present*, or *hidden in fear, veiled*. The veil is the *form of their presence in a hostile age*. The doctrine of *Taqiyya* — protective concealment — has now been named structurally, before its name is spoken.

## The example of Moses fleeing Pharaoh

Abu Malik: *this is an irrefutable statement. As for the proof of Allah's argument through justice and the succession of messengers — that has been established. What then is the meaning of their absence in our own time?*

Salih answers by paradigm. *What was the reason for Moses' absence when he went out of Pharaoh's city and passed in fear, waiting until he reached the land of Shu'ayb?* *He fled from Pharaoh's people, hiding from them, because he feared for himself when they had agreed to kill him*. Salih: *have you heard that any of the just — the just ones of Allah and the sons of His prophets — was killed while patiently bearing the burdens of the religion?* *Yes — many of them have been killed*. *Then what is the justice of this age that appears to you and to the people of your time? Is there one whom God sends down to you after death? Or one fleeing from among you whom you would force back to the killing he escaped, when he has followed Allah's example in His prophets and messengers — patient under fear and concealment until Allah judges between him and us, and who is the best of judges?*

Either God sends back the slain, or the surviving just ones hide. Hiding is the Sunnah. The age the just one hides in is not the age God has cut off justice from; it is the age in which God's justice has been driven into hiding by the same forces that drove Moses into hiding. The absence is not God's absence; the absence is the community's banishment of the just one.

Abu Malik concedes. *There is no shame on the one who follows the Sunnah of the righteous by fearing for himself and fleeing from his people. The shame is on the wicked nations and the helpers of the darknesses for killing the prophets of Allah and His just ones, and for their unanimous agreement upon that act*.

## The killers and the jurists who shelter them

Salih turns the indictment toward the jurists. *What do you say about one who kills the just of Allah and His proof — where is his end? And what of the one who helped him?* *Both are in the Fire — and so too is anyone who raised a banner or carried a flag in their cause*. *What do your jurists say about this?* *They show mercy to the slain, but see no disobedience in the killer*. The jurists clear the killer; the blood of the guardian is unprotected by the tradition that calls itself the protector.

*Whoever obeys the killer voluntarily — is he not a partner with him in the blood of the slain?* *Yes, by Allah — he is his partner, and even more wicked than he*. *His companionship is acknowledged in his helping the killer — so how is he not worse than him?*

Abu Malik gives the line the chapter has been building toward. *Were it not for the narrations of the jurists, no tyrannical king could ever be established*. Strip the narrations, and the tyrant has no legitimation; no legitimation, and no tyrant could long stand. The jurists are the secret support of the king. The killing of the just is the jurists' act, executed through the king. Abu Malik names this against his own school.

Salih names the indictment against Abu Malik himself. *I have laid this on you yourself: with their king, their house, their land, and their soldiers stationed as your neighbors, while we have been taken as their slaves. And you are their just ones — you judge them by your knowledge, you uphold their kingship, you have called them by your questioning, and you have thanked them for what you needed from them. You placed on their stockings the crown of your justice, and they grew proud; you covered their clay with the robes of the sacred law, and they went too far; you lit up their falsehood with the idleness of your truth. So the weak were enraged by your conduct — those who were scholars like you — and they said, "These are the jurists of the umma; we have in them an example." You went astray by your own reasoning, and you led others astray by the reasoning of the mighty. You supposed the one really in office was someone other than yourself, and you forgot the words of Allah Almighty: "And do not incline toward those who have done wrong, lest the Fire touch you." So you severed what Allah commanded to be joined, while seeing it, and you spoke about the outward forms of justice while being confused by it. You came across His saying: "Obey Allah and obey the Messenger and those in authority among you," and you made these darknesses your authorities, even when they disbelieved. And you forgot the words of Allah Almighty: "Fight those who disbelieve in you" — so you disobeyed those whom you were commanded to obey, and obeyed those whom you were commanded to fight, all the while thinking you were doing well*.

The jurists' inversion is exact: *do not incline toward the wrongdoers* — they inclined; *obey those in authority* (the guardians) — they made the king's darknesses the authority; *fight those who disbelieve* — they fought the guardians, not the disbelievers. *And they think they do well*.

## The thirteen messengers and the closing of the door from below

Salih names the most painful clause. *Your opinion has reached the point of claiming that Allah will not send a messenger or a warner in your age. Is it that the goodness of your deeds has put you beyond need of the guardians of Allah for the argument? Or has the ugliness of your deeds cut you off from hope in Allah's acceptance of repentance? Or is it just to judge God without justice — and to be satisfied for your Messenger with what you would not be satisfied with for yourselves in his place? Would any one of you accept that his fifteen-year-old son be killed, or that his house and money be taken from him unjustly?*

*No one is satisfied with that. Even when one of us has a child killed, or is driven out from his property by the anger and rage of his people, we do not consider ourselves content*. Salih: *Allah and His Messenger have a right that you be angry on their behalf, if His vicegerent is killed and His guardian is driven out of the land*. Abu Malik: *it is dear to us that the guardian of Allah be killed among us or driven from his land. But they have deliberately absented themselves from us, and the mighty have appeared in their place*.

Salih answers — the response is the chapter's whole structural diagnosis. *Had you supported the friends of Allah, they would have appeared; had you failed His enemies, they would not have prevailed. But the verdict has bound you to the guardians of Allah in three ways: the killer of the ruler does not tire of seeking and is not healed even when his case is nailed down — he is content with the stinging bite at his fingertips, out of anger; they do not stir up their enemy and do not turn from them even in their heedlessness; and out of hatred — because they believe in love for the guardians of Allah — they have no insight into faith and no trustworthiness in confidentiality. For this reason the guardians of Allah have absented themselves from you by Taqiyya (ta-KEE-yah), and for this reason the great creatures who took their own desires as their God, and seized the goods of this world, have done what is on a par with killing the prophets*.

*Taqiyya* — protective concealment. *The guardians of Allah have absented themselves by Taqiyya* — not because God has closed the door, but because the community's hatred has shut it from below. *Had you supported the friends of Allah, they would have appeared*; *had you failed His enemies, they would not have prevailed*. Both failures are the community's; the guardians' absence is the community's responsibility, not God's deficit.

*They were ignorant of the fault of their conduct, so they did not hold themselves accountable for the injustice they committed, and were not humbled into repenting for what they had done; instead they returned to attributing the injustice of their own deeds to Allah, and they said: "Allah will not send messengers after these thirteen messengers, nor will He send a warner." So they made the killing of the guardians of Allah lawful unjustly — they were indeed unjust, and they wronged Him by that saying and by promising a lie*.

The *no-messenger-after-these-thirteen-messengers* doctrine — the source's own count — is the legal cover-up for the killing. If no more are sent, the killing-of-the-guardians is not the killing-of-God's-just-ones, and the killing can be cleared by the law. *They wronged Him by that saying and by promising a lie*.

## Repentance

Abu Malik: *yes — this is the description of this nation of ours, and this is exactly what they say. So where is the salvation from this, and where is the escape from the punishment of Allah?* Salih: *by Allah, take refuge in Him and repent to Him — He accepts repentance from you. Ask Him for the way of His guardians, and He will save you by your works from the deeds of those who have done wrong. So it is with this creation: if they repent to Allah, He accepts their repentance — and what does Allah do with His punishment of them then? Allah does not oppress them by the weight of an atom, even when they are unjust and lie; and He does not hasten them with punishment, even when they hasten to commit disobedience*.

The door is open; the wait is generous; the way back is named. Abu Malik: *I seek refuge from the punishment of Allah by repenting to Him and by seeking the means to His satisfaction. That belongs to the student in you and to the guide in his guidance — your Lord will reward you*. Salih: *if you do that, you will find Allah pardoning of you. He loves repentance, and you will find me careful in guiding you, watchful over you*.

## Salih's tears; the scholar's counsel; the closing

*Then Salih's eyes welled with tears at the mention of repentance, and he interrupted his speech. He encouraged them and ordered them to go to his father's house. And he went in to his father — the scholar who had first sent him out — and consulted him about the matter of Abu Malik and his companions*.

The scholar — Salih's father, al-Bakhtari — gives the closing counsel. *You know your companions. If you know what is best for them, guide them with your guidance; and when you warn them, do not let it move you to inflict ta'zir (tah-ZEER, discretionary religious chastisement) upon them. Test them by ignoring them without ignorance, and instruct your elders to be kind to them and act righteously toward them in due time. The one who desires good is not hidden, and Allah will not abandon you behind your back; He will open for you, from the light of His success, the gates of guidance that He has appointed for him, and will show you the deeds of those who turn back to Him*.

*So Abu Malik and his companions differed in their state of affairs until they were given orders, and they recognized their own guidance and the kindness shown toward their guidance — and Allah is their guardian. They thanked Allah, returned to their people as warners, and Allah guided whom He willed of His servants to His religion*.

The senior scholar of the old creed has crossed over and been sent back — *as warner*. The *prophet-warner doctrine* of chapter five (*the warner is a prophet, and the prophet is an argument from Allah*) is now realized in the senior scholar's own commission.

## The author's closing; the doxology

The author's voice closes the book. *Whatever in this account took the form of a narrative was no idle telling, but a confirmation of what Allah has commanded — for in it is the steadfastness of the messengers, the signs of the guardians (awliyāʾ), and the manners of the seekers*.

Three things are in the narrative: *the steadfastness of the messengers* (the Master, the greater Shaykh (SHAYKH), the scholar, the *one whose right is binding*); *the signs of the guardians* (the seventh-day naming, the highest transmission, the chain-discipline, the Taqiyya doctrine); *the manners of the seekers* (the humility before the statement, the door of seeking, the three faces, the zero-point confession, Abu Malik's return as warner).

*Praise be to Allah, first and last. May Allah bless His Messenger Muhammad, peace and blessings of Allah be upon him, sent forth from Him to His creation as a bearer of good news and a warner; and bless his guardian, the Imam of the pious, the leader of the Muhajirun (moo-haa-jih-ROON), the beloved of the Lord of the Worlds; and the Imams of the people of his house, those whom Allah has blessed and from whom He has removed every abomination and purified them completely. Allah is sufficient for us; blessed is the Agent, blessed is the Lord, and blessed is the Supporter. There is no power and no strength except by Allah the Almighty*.

The doxology names three honored figures. *Muhammad* ﷺ — the Messenger sent as a bearer of good news and a warner. *His guardian* — *the Imam of the pious, the leader of the Muhajirun, the beloved of the Lord of the Worlds*, named *only by attribute*, preserving the naming-by-attribute discipline the book has held from chapter one. *The Imams of the people of his house* — *from whom Allah has removed every abomination and purified them completely*. The closing-formula — *there is no power and no strength except by Allah the Almighty* — is the same formula whose interpretation the book has spent six chapters unfolding: the *hawl* as the Imam who turns the Creator's words; the *quwwa* as his *hujja* and *bāb*. The book closes on the formula whose meaning it has been all along.

The book is done. *The Master called the boy*; *the boy was bound by covenant*; *the cosmology was given*; *the world-and-hereafter were balanced*; *the boy was named at the Shaykh's hand on the seventh day*; *the boy returned, and his father turned*; *the boy — now Salih — went out as caller*; *the senior scholar of the old creed was brought, by his own concession, to the threshold of the new*; *and the senior scholar was sent back as warner*. *Justice and monotheism* are named, unfolded, and shown to require the chain of the just ones. The attribute Abu Malik asked for is given; the attribute does the work of describing the religion it had to attribute. *The chain is open; the door is open; God's argument is conveyed*.
