# Beyond Linearity — Polynomials, Splines, GAMs

*From* An Introduction to Statistical Learning *(2nd edition, Springer 2021) by Gareth James, Daniela Witten, Trevor Hastie, and Robert Tibshirani. This is Chapter 7 — the chapter where the linearity assumption itself finally bends. The MAS-I scope is the full chapter; no exclusions. It is also the seventh and last chapter on the MAS-I-relevant reading list for this series.*

## The problem the chapter exists to solve

Chapter 6 spent its energy on the variance side of the bias-variance ledger. Ridge, the lasso, principal components regression, partial least squares — four ways to constrain a linear model so it predicts better on data it has not yet seen. All of them are still *linear* models. They do not bend the line.

This chapter bends the line. The premise is that the relationship between a predictor and a response is almost never exactly linear, and that the linearity assumption — useful as it is — is the assumption with the most pricing power on the table to give up. The question is *how to give it up without losing the interpretability that made linear regression worth using in the first place*. The answer is a hierarchy: polynomial regression and step functions as the warm-ups, basis functions as the unifying abstraction that contains both, regression splines and natural cubic splines as the practitioner's workhorse, smoothing splines as the wiggle-penalty cousin of ridge regression in function space, local regression as the moving-window alternative, and generalized additive models as the framework that scales all of this to multiple predictors.

One image carries through every section. The chapter asks, over and over, the same question with the same answer. *How flexible should this fit be?* There is a knob, and cross-validation sets the knob.

## Polynomial regression — the first nonlinear move

The simplest extension of linear regression to non-linear data is to add the predictor's powers to the predictor list. Replace a single column for age with four columns — age, age-squared, age-cubed, age to the fourth — and run ordinary least squares on the expanded design. This is **polynomial regression**. The intuitive scaffold is that even though the curve in the fitted picture is bent, the *model* is still linear in the coefficients, so every Chapter 3 inference tool comes along free: standard errors, confidence intervals, F-tests, the whole apparatus.

A degree-four polynomial of wage against age on the Wage data set lands the picture: a curve that rises, plateaus in middle age, then falls off, with a confidence band that widens noticeably at both ends. The widening is the chapter's first warning. Polynomials get wild at the edges. The textbook is candid that it is unusual in practice to use a degree higher than three or four, because what flexibility you gain in the middle, you pay for many times over at the tails.

## Step functions — the discrete-bin alternative

The second simplest move is to give up smoothness entirely. Chop the predictor's range into bins — under thirty-five, thirty-five to fifty, fifty to sixty-five, over sixty-five — and fit a separate constant in each. The result is a piecewise-constant function, the regression equivalent of a histogram. The textbook calls these **step functions**; they appear throughout biostatistics and epidemiology where five-year age groups are the conventional bins.

The trade-off is the opposite of polynomial regression. Polynomials are smooth and unstable at the edges. Step functions are jumpy in the middle and unbothered at the edges. The catch is that unless the bins line up with real breakpoints in the data, step functions can *miss the action* — the Wage picture shows the first bin flat across an age range where wage is clearly rising, the rise averaged away inside the bin.

## Basis functions — the unifying abstraction

Here is the move that organizes the chapter. Polynomial regression and step functions look like two different methods. They are not. They are two instances of the same abstraction.

The abstraction is the **basis function**. Instead of fitting a linear model in the original predictor, you choose a family of functions of the predictor — call them the basis — apply each to your data, and run ordinary least squares on the resulting columns. For polynomial regression, the basis is the powers of the predictor: the predictor itself, its square, its cube. For step functions, the basis is the indicator functions for each bin: one column per bin, with a one if the observation falls in that bin and a zero otherwise. Same machinery, different basis. The choice of basis is *what kind of nonlinearity you get*.

This is the frame for everything that comes next. Regression splines, natural cubic splines, smoothing splines — every method that produces a curve does so by choosing a clever basis, applying it to the predictor, and running least squares. Once you have the basis, you have a linear model again, and everything Chapter 3 taught you about standard errors and F-tests still applies. The names change. The mechanics do not.

## Regression splines — piecewise polynomials with joins

The chapter's first serious method is the regression spline. Place a small number of points along the predictor's range — the textbook calls them **knots** — and fit a separate low-degree polynomial in each region between knots. Constrain the polynomials to *join* at the knots in such a way that the seam is invisible: same value, same first derivative, same second derivative on either side. The result is a curve that bends where the data calls for bending and stays stable where the data does not.

The standard choice is the **cubic spline** — degree-three polynomials in each region, joined with continuous first and second derivatives. There is a memorable arithmetic anchor: *a cubic spline with K knots has 4 + K coefficients*. The four comes from the cubic polynomial; the K is the bonus flexibility each knot buys. Cubic is conventional because most human eyes cannot detect the discontinuity in the third derivative at the joining points — the curve looks smooth even though there is a mathematical seam there.

Splines fix the polynomial's middle-of-range problem cleanly: flexibility comes from the *number of knots*, not from a high degree. But they inherit the same boundary problem — the variance of the fitted curve climbs near the extremes, because the polynomials at the boundaries have no data on one side to anchor them.

The fix is the **natural cubic spline**. Require the function to be linear in the region beyond the boundary knots. That single rule — really four constraints, because it forces the second and third derivatives to be zero at each of the two boundary knots — pulls the boundary variance back down. The textbook shows the picture: a cubic spline and a natural cubic spline side by side, both with three knots. The cubic's confidence band fans out wildly at the edges. The natural cubic's stays tight. That is the four-constraint trick.

How do you choose the number of knots? Cross-validation. Fit splines across a grid of effective degrees of freedom; cross-validate each; pick the one with the lowest held-out error. On the Wage data the curves flatten out around three or four degrees of freedom. Same picture as every other model-selection problem in the book.

## Smoothing splines — the wiggle penalty

Regression splines force you to choose the knots. Smoothing splines do not. They put a knot at every unique value of the predictor and then control the resulting wildness with a penalty in the objective function. That penalty is the chapter's defining move.

You want a curve that fits the data well *and* is smooth. So you minimize a sum of two things. The first is the residual sum of squares — the usual measure of how badly the curve misses the data. The second is a *wiggle penalty* — a single number that measures the total amount of bending in the curve summed across the predictor's whole range. A straight line has zero bending everywhere; its wiggle penalty is zero. A near-interpolating curve has lots of bending at lots of points; its wiggle penalty is large. The penalty *adds up all the bending*. Multiply that wiggle penalty by a tuning parameter, the Greek letter lambda, add it to the residual sum of squares, and minimize the total.

Lambda is the dial. Turn it all the way down to zero and the penalty disappears — the minimization chases the data and you get the near-interpolating wiggle. Turn it all the way up and the penalty dominates — the only acceptable curve is one with almost no bending, which in the limit is the ordinary least-squares line. In between, you get something that respects the data without overreacting to every point. Cross-validation picks the middle. The chapter notes a small gift: leave-one-out cross-validation for smoothing splines has a closed-form shortcut that delivers the answer from a single model fit.

The machinery is the same as ridge regression. Ridge penalized the size of the coefficients; small lambda gave you ordinary least squares, large lambda shrunk everything toward zero. Smoothing splines penalize the bending of the curve; small lambda gives you a near-interpolating curve, large lambda flattens everything toward a straight line. Same dial. Different space — coefficients in ridge, function shape in smoothing splines. Smoothing splines are ridge regression in function space.

## Local regression — moving the window across the data

A different route. Instead of fitting one global function, fit a small regression at every point using only the nearby observations. Pick a target point. Take the closest fraction of the data — the textbook calls this fraction the **span** — and weight those points so the very nearest one counts most and the ones at the edge of the window count least. Run a weighted least squares regression on that local neighborhood. The prediction at the target point is the fitted value of that local regression. Move the target. Refit. Move again. The result is a smooth curve traced out by a moving local model.

This is **local regression**, often called **LOESS** in software and in working practice. The walking listener may already have seen it without knowing the name: it is the gray curve that appears by default on a scatterplot when you ask a plotting library for a smoother. The default behavior of `geom_smooth()` in the ggplot ecosystem is a LOESS fit; the eye recognizes the shape long before the head learns the algorithm.

The span is LOESS's tuning dial. A small span — twenty percent of the data — makes each local fit react sharply and the curve looks wiggly. A large span — seventy percent — uses most of the data for every local fit and the curve looks nearly linear. Cross-validation picks the span. Same dial story, again. LOESS does not scale well past three or four predictors — the nearest-neighbor window becomes empty in higher dimensions, the same curse that limits k-nearest-neighbor regression.

## Generalized additive models — the workhorse for multiple predictors

Everything so far has been one predictor at a time. The chapter's last move scales all of it to many predictors at once without losing the picture.

The setup is the **generalized additive model**, the GAM. In ordinary multiple linear regression the response is modeled as a sum of linear terms, one per predictor. The GAM replaces each linear term with a smooth nonlinear function — a regression spline, a natural cubic spline, a smoothing spline, or a LOESS fit — chosen separately for each predictor. The model is still *additive*: the predictors contribute separately and their effects sum, but each predictor's contribution can bend in whatever shape the data calls for.

This is the trade that makes GAMs the workhorse. Keep additivity, drop linearity. Additivity is what makes the fit interpretable: you plot the partial effect of each predictor separately, hold the others fixed in your head, and read off what age does to wage and what year does to wage and what education does to wage as three readable curves. Linearity was what was costing you the most in fit quality. You can give it up without giving up the interpretability. The textbook is candid about the cost: GAMs assume no interactions unless you add them explicitly, and that assumption can hurt when interactions matter.

How are GAMs fit when smoothing splines are involved? With an algorithm called **backfitting**: iteratively fit each smooth function while holding the others fixed, looping until convergence. The R workhorse is the `gam()` function in Simon Wood's mgcv package — the practical handle on these fits for two decades.

The historical anchor matters. GAMs originated in a 1990 book by Trevor Hastie and Robert Tibshirani — *Generalized Additive Models*, Chapman and Hall. Same Hastie and Tibshirani as two of the four ISL authors. The chapter is, in part, those same two authors revisiting their own thirty-year-old framework in pedagogical form.

And here is the actuarial anchor — the chapter's most exam-relevant beat. **GAMs are the workhorse of insurance pricing on the property-and-casualty side.** Claim frequency as a function of policyholder age is almost never linear: a bend in the very young (inexperienced drivers crash more), a long minimum through the middle ages, an upward bend in the very old (slower reaction times). When an actuarial pricing team fits that curve in production, they almost always fit it as a smooth function inside a GAM, alongside linear or smooth terms for other rating variables. Same for severity-by-age, frequency-by-vehicle-value, claim counts by region. GAMs are not a research curiosity; they are the standard tool. The MAS-I student walking with us will see GAMs again in working actuarial life with a frequency few other methods in this book will match.

## What to leave with, and the close of the series

Three pictures.

Polynomial regression and step functions are the warm-ups, and they share an abstraction. The basis-function frame is the chapter's organizing move: choose a family of transformations of the predictor, apply them, run ordinary least squares on the result. Polynomials are the powers of the predictor as the basis. Step functions are the bin indicators as the basis. Every method that follows is another choice of basis with another way of controlling flexibility.

The spline family. Regression splines fit piecewise cubics joined smoothly at knots — flexible in the middle but wild at the boundaries; a cubic spline with K knots uses 4 + K coefficients. Natural cubic splines tame the boundary wildness with the four-constraint trick: linear beyond the boundary knots. Smoothing splines drop the knot-count question entirely by placing a knot at every observation and controlling the resulting flexibility with the wiggle penalty — the *same dial* as ridge regression's lambda, in function space rather than coefficient space. LOESS is the moving-window cousin that scales poorly in high dimensions but gives you the smoother you have been looking at on default scatterplots for years.

Generalized additive models scale everything to many predictors at once. Keep additivity for interpretability; drop linearity for flexibility. Each predictor's contribution is its own smooth curve, plottable separately. The fitting algorithm when smoothers are involved is backfitting — iteratively fit each smooth while holding the others fixed, looping until convergence. The framework originated in Hastie and Tibshirani's 1990 book — two of the four ISL authors. And GAMs are the actuarial-pricing workhorse for nonlinear effects in claim-frequency models — the spot in this book most directly continuous with the work an MAS-I-credentialed actuary will end up doing.

That is the chapter. The textbook turns next to trees, then to random forests and boosting, support-vector machines, deep learning, and unsupervised methods — none of which are on the MAS-I syllabus or on this series. Seven chapters — the framework in Chapter 1, bias-variance in Chapter 2, linear regression in Chapter 3, classification and GLMs in Chapter 4, resampling in Chapter 5, regularization and dimension reduction in Chapter 6, and the bending-the-line methods here — is what an MAS-I candidate needs to carry from this book into the exam and into the actuarial life that follows. This is the last walk.
