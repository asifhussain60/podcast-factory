# Selection and Regularization — Ridge, Lasso, PCR, PLS

*From* An Introduction to Statistical Learning *(2nd edition, Springer 2021) by Gareth James, Daniela Witten, Trevor Hastie, and Robert Tibshirani. This is Chapter 6, the chapter where the bias–variance dial of Chapter 2 finally gets a handle on it. The MAS-I scope is the full chapter; no exclusions.*

## The problem the chapter exists to solve

Chapter 3 fit a linear model with whatever predictors you handed it. That works when the right predictors are obvious and few. The moment the predictor list grows — twenty, fifty, a few hundred, half a million single-nucleotide polymorphisms — the picture changes. With too many predictors, ordinary least squares chases noise: the coefficients become wildly variable from one training set to the next, and the test error climbs. With too few, you leave information on the table. Somewhere in between is a model that predicts well and a story the analyst can actually tell.

This chapter is a catalog of disciplined ways to land in that middle. Three families. Subset selection, which considers candidate models built from subsets of the available predictors and picks among them. Shrinkage, which fits a model on all the predictors but penalizes large coefficient values, pulling the estimates toward zero. Dimension reduction, which builds a small number of new predictors as combinations of the originals and regresses on those. Three families, one job: trade a little bias for a lot of variance reduction, and let the data tell you how much.

The chapter's central image — the one the listener should carry through every section — is a tuning dial. Turn the dial one way, the model gets more flexible and tracks the training data closely. Turn it the other way, the model gets simpler, the coefficients shrink, the predictions stabilize. The whole chapter is about *where to set the dial*, and *how to let cross-validation set it for you*.

## Subset selection — the combinatorial baseline

The cleanest idea first. With p predictors there are exactly two-to-the-p possible subsets — every predictor either in or out. **Best subset selection** considers all of them, scores each, picks the winner. The algorithm is simple: for every model size from zero predictors up to p, find the best model of that size by training error, then choose among those p-plus-one candidates by a criterion that punishes complexity.

Best subset is conceptually clean and computationally hopeless. Two-to-the-thirty is already a billion; two-to-the-fifty would not finish in a human lifetime. So the chapter introduces the greedy approximations every working analyst actually reaches for. **Forward stepwise** starts with the empty model and adds the single best predictor at each step until you stop. **Backward stepwise** starts with the full model and drops the single weakest predictor at each step. Both fit on the order of p-squared models instead of two-to-the-p — practical at any p you will plausibly meet. Both are greedy: neither is guaranteed to land on the same subset best-subset would have chosen, but in practice they find good models most of the time. Forward stepwise works even when p is larger than n; backward stepwise needs n bigger than p to start with a full fit.

## Choosing the optimal model — Cp, AIC, BIC, adjusted R-squared, and cross-validation

Subset selection produces a sequence of candidate models — one per size. Now you have to choose one. The chapter offers two families of selection criteria.

The first family adjusts the training error by adding a penalty for the number of predictors used. Four names appear, all doing the same job in slightly different ways. **Mallows's Cp** adds a penalty proportional to the number of predictors, scaled by an estimate of the noise variance — the intuitive scaffold is that Cp adjusts the training error upward by exactly enough to account for the optimism of having fit those predictors. **AIC**, the Akaike Information Criterion, lands at essentially the same place for normal-error linear models; the scaffold is "training error plus a complexity penalty, derived from information theory." **BIC**, the Bayesian Information Criterion, has the same shape but penalizes complexity *harder* — the penalty grows with the logarithm of the sample size, so on any dataset of meaningful size BIC will prefer smaller models than Cp or AIC will. And **adjusted R-squared** is the friendly heuristic — ordinary R-squared with a correction that prevents it from rising automatically every time you add a predictor; pick the model with the highest adjusted R-squared.

The chapter names these by their penalty shapes, not their formulas. BIC is the strict one. Cp and AIC are the moderate ones. Adjusted R-squared is the casual one. All four answer the same question: how do you adjust the training error to estimate what the test error would have been? When they agree, the choice is easy. When they disagree, you should know that BIC will steer you toward parsimony and Cp toward retention, and you can take a position on which posture suits the problem.

The second family is the resampling route. Cross-validation, the discipline from the resampling chapter, simply estimates the test error directly: hold out folds, refit, score, average. The textbook is candid that when cross-validation is computationally feasible, it is the preferred selection criterion — it makes no assumptions about the noise distribution or the model form, and it estimates exactly the quantity you care about. On the *Hitters* dataset the textbook uses throughout the chapter, cross-validation lands on a ten-variable model. Different criteria, different but reasonable choices.

## Shrinkage — fit on everything, but pull toward zero

The second family abandons the in-or-out logic of subset selection. Fit the model on all the predictors, but add a penalty to the objective function that grows as the coefficient values grow. Minimizing the sum of squared residuals plus that penalty drives the coefficients toward zero. How aggressively depends on a single tuning parameter, the Greek letter lambda. The chapter calls this the **shrinkage** or **regularization** family.

There are two members. They differ only in the *shape* of the penalty.

**Ridge regression** adds lambda times the sum of squared coefficients to the residual sum of squares. The penalty grows quickly when coefficients get big, more gently when they are small — the intuitive scaffold is that the squared shape concentrates its discipline on large coefficients and barely notices small ones, so as lambda grows every coefficient shrinks continuously toward zero, but none of them ever reach exactly zero. Ridge keeps all the predictors in the model. It just turns down their volume. On the *Credit* dataset the textbook works through, the picture is striking: every predictor's coefficient drifts smoothly toward zero as lambda climbs, with the income, limit, rating, and student variables holding the largest values the longest. The chapter notes a small wrinkle worth mentioning — because ridge penalizes the sum of *squared* coefficient values, predictors measured on bigger scales get penalized harder for the same effect, so the standard practice is to standardize the predictors before fitting.

**The lasso** swaps the squared penalty for the *absolute-value* penalty. Lambda times the sum of absolute values of the coefficients. Everything else identical to ridge. The change of shape changes everything. The absolute-value penalty has sharp corners at zero in every dimension, and the optimization tends to land on those corners. As lambda grows, lasso coefficients shrink, and then — one by one — they snap to exactly zero. That is the lasso's signature move: it does shrinkage and variable selection in a single step. The same *Credit* picture under the lasso looks different: as lambda climbs, the model first uses only the rating predictor, then student and limit enter together, then income, then the remaining predictors one at a time. Sparsity, produced automatically. The visual argument behind this — the geometric difference between the lasso's diamond-shaped constraint region and ridge's spherical one — explains *why* the absolute-value penalty produces exact zeros while the squared one does not, and the chapter shows the picture but the picture lives on the page; the spoken takeaway is simply that the corners do the work.

The intuitive contrast is the entire story. Ridge shrinks all the dials a little. The lasso turns most of the dials all the way off. Both end at the same place when lambda is enormous — every coefficient zero, the model is just the intercept. Both reduce to ordinary least squares when lambda is zero. The interesting behavior is everywhere in between, and the chapter is clear that neither universally dominates the other. When the truth involves many predictors with modest effects, ridge wins. When the truth involves a few predictors with substantial effects and a long tail of noise, the lasso wins. The honest answer for any real dataset is to fit both and let cross-validation choose.

The historical anchor matters. The lasso came from a 1996 paper by Robert Tibshirani — one of the four authors of the textbook the listener is walking through. "Regression shrinkage and selection via the lasso." That paper is among the most-cited papers in statistics, and it defined what shrinkage methods would look like for the generation that came after. The chapter doesn't lean on this; the textbook is too restrained for that. But the lineage is worth knowing.

## Cross-validation chooses lambda

How do you pick lambda? Cross-validation. Pick a grid of candidate lambda values, run cross-validation for each, score by the average held-out error, take the lambda with the smallest score. Refit on the full dataset at that lambda, and that is your model. The chapter walks this exactly on both the *Credit* ridge fit and a simulated sparse dataset for the lasso, and the picture lands cleanly: cross-validation error against lambda traces a U-shape — too little shrinkage and the model overfits, too much and it underfits — and the bottom of the U marks the choice. On the sparse simulated example the lasso paired with cross-validation correctly identifies the two true signal predictors out of forty-five candidates, with only fifty observations. The combination of shrinkage and resampling does work the unaided eye cannot.

The dial-and-knob image lands here. Lambda is the dial. Cross-validation is the hand on the dial. Together they let the data tell you how much regularization the problem actually needs.

## Dimension reduction — summarize the predictors, then regress

The third family takes a different route. Instead of selecting or shrinking the original predictors, *build new ones* — a small number of carefully chosen linear combinations of the original p predictors — and run an ordinary least squares regression of the response on those. The dimensionality of the regression drops from p to whatever number of new predictors you choose, M. If M is much smaller than p, the variance of the coefficient estimates falls sharply, at the cost of a constraint on what the model can express.

Two methods, distinguished by *how* the new predictors are built.

**Principal components regression** — **PCR** — runs **principal components analysis** on the predictor matrix first, then regresses on the leading components. Principal components analysis is the workhorse linear technique for finding the directions in predictor space that capture the most variance — geometrically, the first principal component is the direction along which the data spread out the furthest, the second is the perpendicular direction that captures the most of what remains, and so on. The intuitive scaffold is that PCA finds the natural axes of the predictor cloud, and the leading axes summarize most of the structure. The mathematical machinery underneath is an eigendecomposition of the predictor covariance matrix — named here, not derived. PCR picks the first M of those axes, uses them as predictors, regresses on those. If most of the relevant structure in the predictors sits along the leading components, PCR with a small M can do markedly better than ordinary least squares on all p predictors.

The catch is in the assumption. PCR builds the components looking only at the predictors — the response is not consulted. The hope is that the directions of greatest variance in X are also the directions most associated with Y. Sometimes that hope holds. Sometimes it does not.

**Partial least squares** — **PLS** — is the supervised alternative. PLS builds its components the same way structurally — linear combinations of the original predictors — but at every step the component is constructed to *also be related to the response*. The order matters: PCR summarizes X, *then* regresses; PLS builds components that already aim at Y. The first PLS direction weights each predictor by its simple-regression coefficient on Y, which is proportional to that predictor's correlation with the response — so PLS leans most heavily on the variables most strongly tied to the outcome. The second PLS direction does the same thing on the residuals after removing what the first explained. And so on.

That sounds like it should always beat PCR. The textbook is candid: the supervised reduction in PLS reduces bias, but it tends to increase variance, and the overall benefit relative to PCR is usually a wash. PLS is the workhorse in chemometrics, where many variables come from digitized spectrometry signals; for general regression work, PCR and PLS perform comparably. The number of components M is chosen by cross-validation.

The labs run all four methods — ridge, lasso, PCR, PLS — on the *Hitters* dataset, predicting baseball salaries from career statistics. The numbers land close to each other. The lasso test MSE comes in near 143,000. Ridge near the same. PCR near the same. PLS lands at 151,995 — slightly higher than the others, in the same neighborhood. Four methods, four different mechanisms for controlling variance, and on this dataset they end up in striking proximity. The lesson is less "which method wins" and more "the family of disciplined regularization techniques does meaningfully better than the unregularized least-squares fit, in roughly equivalent ways."

## A note on high dimensions

The chapter closes by addressing the setting that motivated half the methods. When the number of predictors p approaches or exceeds the number of observations n — the **high-dimensional** setting that biology, genomics, and modern marketing routinely produce — ordinary least squares fails outright. With more parameters than data points, the model fits the training data perfectly, residuals are exactly zero, and the test error is catastrophic. R-squared on the training set reaches one and means nothing. The training-set fit metrics that were diagnostic in low dimensions become actively misleading in high ones.

The methods of this chapter — forward stepwise, ridge, the lasso, PCR — work in high dimensions because they constrain flexibility. They cannot achieve a zero-residual training fit, and that is what makes them useful. The cost is interpretive caution: when predictors are highly correlated, the particular subset a method selects is one of many that could have predicted nearly as well. The lasso identifying seventeen useful predictors out of half a million SNPs does not mean those seventeen are *the* causal variables; it means they are *a* set that predicts well. A different sample would likely surface a different set. The model can be useful without being unique.

## What to leave with

Three pictures.

Subset selection is the combinatorial baseline. Best subset is intractable past modest p; forward and backward stepwise are the practical approximations. Among the candidate models of different sizes, choose by Cp or AIC for moderation, BIC for parsimony, adjusted R-squared for the casual heuristic, or cross-validation for the assumption-free direct estimate.

Shrinkage is the dial-and-knob family. Ridge shrinks every coefficient toward zero by adding a squared-magnitude penalty — all the dials turn down a little, but none turn off. The lasso shrinks every coefficient toward zero by adding an absolute-value penalty — and because the absolute-value shape has corners at zero, most of the dials turn all the way off. Lambda controls how aggressive the shrinkage is. Cross-validation picks lambda. Ridge wins when many predictors contribute modestly; the lasso wins when a few predictors carry most of the signal.

Dimension reduction is the summarize-then-regress family. PCR builds the leading principal components of the predictor matrix and regresses on those — unsupervised, hoping the high-variance directions in X line up with Y. PLS builds components that aim at Y from the start — supervised, but the supervised advantage often washes out against the variance cost. Both pick the number of components by cross-validation.

The dial, in every case, is set by the data. That is what the chapter built. The textbook now turns to nonlinear models — polynomial regression, splines, local regression, generalized additive models. Methods that drop the linear assumption itself. The next walk.
