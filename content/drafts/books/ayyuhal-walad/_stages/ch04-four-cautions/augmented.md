# Augmented — ch04-four-cautions (knowledge stage: Quran refs verified from the Arabic spine)

Young person, I offer you eight pieces of advice; heed them, lest your knowledge become a burden against you on the Day of Judgment. Practice four, and avoid four.

The first thing to avoid is arguing with anyone, whenever possible, for it leads to great harm. Its drawbacks far outweigh its benefits, and it is the root of every blameworthy trait: hypocrisy, envy, pride, malice, enmity, boasting, and the like. However, if a question arises between you and another person or group, and your sole aim is to uncover the truth, then discussion is permissible. Two signs indicate such a sincere desire: first, it makes no difference whether the truth is revealed by your words or someone else's; and second, you prefer private discussion over public debate.

Consider this helpful point: asking about obscure matters is like presenting a heart disease to a physician, and the answer is the attempt to cure it. The ignorant have diseased hearts, and the learned are their doctors. The partially learned cannot complete the treatment, and the truly learned do not treat every sick person, but only those they hope will accept the treatment and cure. If the weakness is chronic, fatal, and incurable, the doctor will not labor to give medicine, as it would be a waste of time.

Ignorance, then, manifests in four types of sickness: one curable, and three incurable. Among the incurable types is the person whose questions or objections stem from envy and hatred. Every time you offer the best, clearest, or plainest answer, it only fuels their rage and envy. The wise course is not to attempt an answer.

> One hopes for the removal of every enmity
> Except enmity arising from envy.

Therefore, you must distance yourself from such a person and leave them with their ailment. Allah the Exalted said, "Withdraw from whoever turns away from Our warning and desires nothing except the present life." The envious person, in all they say and do, sows discord through their actions, as the Prophet, peace be upon him, said, "Envy consumes good deeds as fire consumes wood."

The second incurable type is one whose weakness stems from stupidity. As Jesus, peace be upon him, said, "Indeed, I did not fail in bringing the dead to life, but I failed in curing the stupid." This is the person who has briefly pursued knowledge, learning a little about intellectual and sacred sciences. In their ignorance, they question and object to a truly learned person who has dedicated their life to these same sciences. This ignorant individual fails to realize that what is obscure to them is not necessarily obscure to the highly learned. Their questions arise from sheer stupidity, and you must not attempt to answer them.

The third type is a seeker of guidance who attributes what they don't understand from the words of great scholars to their own intellectual shortcomings. Their questions aim to gain benefit, but they are dull-witted and cannot grasp the truth of matters. You must also not attempt to answer them, as the Prophet, peace be upon him, said, "We, the company of the prophets, have been commanded to speak to people according to their understanding."

The curable sickness, however, belongs to the intelligent and perceptive seeker of guidance. This person is not swayed by envy, anger, or the love of worldly vanities, wealth, and honor, but genuinely seeks the straight path. Their questions and objections do not stem from envy, a desire to cause trouble, or to test others. This person is curable, and it is not only permissible but necessary to attempt to answer them.

The second thing to avoid is becoming a preacher and admonisher, for it carries great harm unless you first practice what you preach before addressing others. Consider what was said to Jesus, peace be upon him: "O Son of Mary, preach to yourself, and when you have preached to yourself, then preach to others; otherwise, be ashamed before your Lord." If you feel compelled to undertake this work, guard against two conditions. First, avoid affectation in speech, whether in explanations, allusions, vehement cries, versification, or poetry. Allah the Exalted hates pretension, and excessive pretentiousness reveals inner confusion and a heedless heart.

The purpose of admonition is for the worshipper to remember the fire of the afterlife, prompting them to dedicate themselves to the service of the Creator. They should reflect on their past life, wasted on matters that did not truly concern them. They should contemplate the obstacles to their faith's security at life's end, their state in the grasp of the angel of death, their ability to answer Munkar and Nakir, their condition at the resurrection and its stages, and whether they will cross the bridge safely or fall into the pit. These thoughts should remain in their heart, disturbing their tranquility. Stirring up these concerns and lamenting these afflictions is called 'admonition.' Informing humanity, drawing their attention to these realities, warning them about shortcomings and omissions, and making them see their soul's defects—so that the intensity of these concerns affects the congregation, making them eager to rectify past years according to their ability, and feel regret over days spent in disobedience to Allah the Exalted—all this, carried out in this manner, is called 'preaching.'

Imagine seeing a sudden torrent rushing towards someone's house, with them and their family inside, and you cry out, "Danger! Flee from the flood!" In such circumstances, would your heart crave to deliver your message to the homeowner with pretentious explanations, witticisms, or allusions? Certainly not. Such is the situation of the preacher, who must avoid such affectations.

The second condition to avoid is making your concern in preaching not about people becoming emotionally agitated in your assembly, showing excitement, or tearing their clothes, merely so that it might be said, "What an assembly this was!" Such a focus is a worldly inclination. True preaching aims to guide people from apostasy to obedience, from greed to abstinence, from stinginess to generosity, from vanities to the fear of God. It should inspire them to love the afterlife and despise this world, and to teach them the knowledge of worship and asceticism. This is because their predominant natural tendency is to deviate from the clear path of the law, to engage in what displeases Allah the Exalted, and to occupy themselves with their evil characteristics. Instead, instill fear in their hearts, frighten them, and warn them about the terrifying things they will encounter. Perhaps then the qualities of their inner lives will change, their outer deeds will transform, and a craving for obedience and a return from apostasy will emerge. This is the true method of preaching and admonition. Any preaching that deviates from this is a blight upon both speaker and listener. Indeed, it is even likened to a ghoul or a devil that leads people astray and destroys them. One must flee from it, for the corruption this speaker inflicts upon religion is greater than what Satan himself can achieve. Whoever holds power and authority must remove such a person from the pulpits of the Muslims and forbid them from proclaiming their message, in accordance with the principle of "enjoining good and forbidding evil."

The third thing to avoid is associating with princes and sultans, and even seeing them. For seeing, sitting with, and mixing with them leads to great harm. If you are compelled to do so, avoid praising or commending them, for Allah the Exalted is angered when an oppressor and impious person is praised. Anyone who calls for the lengthening of their lives rejoices in Allah being disobeyed in His land.

The fourth thing to avoid is accepting any gifts or presents from princes, even if you know it is permissible. Coveting things from them corrupts religion, as it gives rise to flattery, subservience, and approval of their oppression—all of which corrupt religion. The least of its harms is that if you accept their gifts and benefit from their worldly possessions, you will grow fond of them. And whoever loves someone naturally desires the prolonging of their life and presence. To love the continuance of an oppressor's life implies a willingness for the oppression of Allah's worshippers and the ruin of the learned. What could be more damaging to religion and the afterlife than this? Be very careful lest the allure of Satan deceive you. Someone might argue that it is better to take their gold and silver and distribute it among the poor and needy, reasoning that since they spend it in profligacy and apostasy, your spending it on the weak is preferable. But indeed, the Cursed One has led many astray with this evil whispering, and its harm is immense. We have discussed this in *The Revival of the Religious Sciences*, so refer to it there.
