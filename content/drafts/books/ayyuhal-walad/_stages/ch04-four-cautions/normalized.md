# Normalized — ch04-four-cautions (house voice via Gemini)

Young man, I advise you on eight matters. Heed them, lest your knowledge become a witness against you on the Day of Judgment. Practice four, and avoid four.

The four you are to avoid are:

First, avoid arguing with anyone on any matter, as much as you can. This is because argumentation leads to great harm, its drawbacks far outweighing any benefit. It is the root of every blameworthy trait: hypocrisy, envy, pride, malice, enmity, boasting, and similar vices. However, if a question arises between you and another person or group, and your sole aim is for the truth to emerge and not be obscured, then discussion is permissible. Such a desire is marked by two signs: first, it makes no difference whether the truth is revealed through your words or someone else's; and second, you prefer to discuss the matter in private rather than in public.

Listen, for here I draw your attention to a crucial point: asking about obscure matters is like presenting a heart disease to a physician, and the answer is an attempt to cure that disease. Understand that the ignorant have diseased hearts, and the learned are their doctors. The partially learned cannot perfect the treatment. Moreover, the truly learned do not treat every sick person, but only those they hope will accept the treatment and find a cure. If the ailment is chronic, fatal, or incurable, a physician will not exert effort to administer medicine, as it would be a waste of time.

Know then that the sickness of ignorance comes in four forms: one is curable, and the others are not.

Among the incurable is the one whose questions or objections stem from envy and hatred. No matter how excellent, clear, or plain your answer, it only intensifies their rage and envy. The proper course is not to attempt an answer.

One hopes for the removal of every enmity
Except enmity arising from envy.

Therefore, you must distance yourself from such a person and leave them with their ailment. Allah the Exalted said, "Withdraw from whoever turns away from our warning and desires nothing except the present life." The envious person, in all they say and do, fuels the fire of their own actions. As the Prophet, peace be upon him, said, "Envy consumes good deeds as fire consumes wood."

The second type is one whose weakness stems from stupidity, and they too are incurable. As Isa, peace be upon him, said, "Indeed I did not fail in bringing the dead to life, but I failed in curing the stupid." This is the person who has spent only a short time seeking knowledge, acquiring a superficial understanding of intellectual and sacred law sciences. In their ignorance, they pose questions and objections to a truly learned person who has dedicated their life to these very fields. This foolish individual fails to grasp that what is obscure to them is not necessarily obscure to the highly learned. Since their questions arise from such stupidity, you must not attempt to answer them.

The third type is someone seeking guidance who attributes their lack of understanding of the words of great scholars to their own intellectual shortcomings. Their questions are genuinely aimed at seeking benefit, but they are dull-witted and cannot grasp the truth of matters. You must not attempt to answer them either, as the Prophet, peace be upon him, said, "We, the company of the prophets, have been commanded to speak to people according to their understanding."

The curable sickness, however, belongs to the intelligent and perceptive seeker of guidance. This person is not overcome by envy, anger, or the love of worldly vanities, wealth, and honor; instead, they genuinely seek the straight path. Their questions and objections do not stem from envy, a desire to cause trouble, or to test others. Such a person is curable, and it is not only permissible but necessary to attempt to answer them.

Second, avoid becoming a preacher and admonisher, as this role carries great harm unless you first practice what you preach before addressing others. Consider what was said to Isa, peace be upon him: "O Son of Miriam, preach to yourself, and when you have preached to yourself, then preach to others; and otherwise, be ashamed before your Lord." If you are compelled to undertake this work, beware of two conditions. The first is affectation in speech—in explanations, allusions, vehement cries, versification, and poetry. Allah the Exalted detests pretension, and excessive pretentiousness reveals inner confusion and a heedless heart.

The significance of admonition is to remind the worshipper of the fire of the afterlife, prompting them to dedicate themselves to the service of the Creator. It is to make them reflect on their past life, which they squandered on matters that did not concern them, and to consider the obstacles to the security of faith at life's end. They should ponder their state in the grasp of the angel of death, whether they will be able to answer Munkar and Nakîr, and their condition during the resurrection and its stages—will they cross the bridge safely or fall into the pit? These thoughts should remain in their heart and disturb their tranquility. Stirring up these fears and lamenting these afflictions is called "warning." Notifying people, drawing their attention to these matters, warning them about shortcomings and omissions, and making them see the defects of their souls—so that the intensity of these fears touches the assembly, and these afflictions make them eager to rectify past years according to their ability, and they feel regret over days spent in disobedience to Allah the Exalted—all this, carried out in this manner, is called "preaching."

Imagine seeing a sudden torrent rushing towards someone's house, with them and their family inside. You would cry out, "Danger! Danger! Flee from the flood!" In such circumstances, would your heart yearn to deliver your message to the homeowner with pretentious explanations, witticisms, and allusions? Certainly not. Such is the situation of the preacher, and they must avoid such affectations.

The second condition to avoid is letting your concern in preaching be for people to become agitated in your assembly, showing excitement and tearing their clothes, so that it might be said, "What a powerful gathering that was!" All this indicates an inclination towards worldly acclaim. Instead, the true purpose of preaching is to guide people from heedlessness to the afterlife, from apostasy to obedience, from greed to abstinence, from stinginess to generosity, and from vanities to the fear of God. It is to make them love the afterlife and detest this world, and to teach them the knowledge of worship and asceticism. This is because the predominant tendency in human nature is to deviate from the clear path of the law, to exert effort in what displeases Allah the Exalted, and to occupy themselves with their evil characteristics. Instill fear in their hearts, frighten them, and warn them about the terrifying things they will encounter. Perhaps then the qualities of their inner lives will change, their outer deeds will transform, and a craving and desire for obedience, and a return from apostasy, will emerge. This is the true method of preaching and admonition. Any preaching that deviates from this is a blight upon both speaker and listener. Indeed, it is even likened to a *ghul* or a devil that leads people astray and destroys them. People must flee from it, for the corruption this speaker inflicts upon their religion is greater than what Satan himself can achieve. Whoever holds power and authority must remove such individuals from the pulpits of the Muslims and forbid them from their proclamations, in accordance with the principle of "enjoining good and forbidding evil."

Third, avoid associating with princes and sultans, and do not seek their company. Seeing them, sitting with them, and mixing with them leads to great harm. If you are compelled to do so, refrain from praising or commending them. Allah the Exalted is angered when an oppressor and impious person is praised. Anyone who prays for the lengthening of their lives effectively rejoices in Allah being disobeyed in His land.

Fourth, do not accept any gifts or presents from princes, even if you believe it to be permissible. Coveting things from them corrupts religion, as it leads to flattery, subservience, and approval of their oppression—all of which are detrimental to faith. The least of its harms is that by accepting their gifts and benefiting from their worldly possessions, you grow fond of them. And whoever loves someone naturally desires the prolongation of their life and presence. To desire the continued life of an oppressor is to implicitly consent to the oppression of Allah the Exalted's worshippers and the ruin of the learned. What could be more damaging than this to religion and the afterlife? Be extremely careful—beware lest the allure of Satan deceive you. Someone might tell you that it is better to take their gold and silver and distribute it among the poor and needy, arguing that they are squandering it in profligacy and apostasy, and your spending it on the weak is better than theirs. Indeed, the Cursed One has led many people astray with this evil whispering, and its harm is immense. We have discussed this in the *Ihya ʿUlum al-Din* (The Revival of the Religious Sciences), so refer to it there.
