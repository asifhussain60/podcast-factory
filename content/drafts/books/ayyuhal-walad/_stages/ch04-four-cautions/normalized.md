# Normalized — ch04-four-cautions (house voice via Gemini)

O youth, I offer you eight pieces of advice. Heed them, lest your knowledge become an adversary against you on the Day of Judgment. You are to perform four of them and avoid four.

The first thing to avoid is arguing with anyone on any matter, as far as you are able. This practice leads to great harm; its evils outweigh its benefits, and it is the source of every blameworthy quality: hypocrisy, envy, pride, malice, enmity, boasting, and similar traits. Of course, if a question arises between you and an individual or group, and your sole purpose is to uncover the truth, then discussion is permissible. Such a desire has two signs: first, it makes no difference whether the truth is revealed by your tongue or another's; and second, you prefer private discussion over public.

Here is a helpful point: asking about obscure matters is like presenting a heart disease to a physician, and the answer is an attempt to cure that disease. Understand that the ignorant suffer from diseased hearts, and the learned are their doctors. The partially learned cannot perfect the treatment, and the perfectly learned do not treat every sick person, but only those they hope will accept the treatment and cure. If the weakness is chronic, fatal, or incurable, a wise physician will not labor to give medicine, as this would be a waste of time.

The sickness of ignorance comes in four forms: one is curable, the others are not. Among the incurable is the person whose questions or objections stem from envy and hatred. Every time you offer the best, clearest, or plainest answer, it only increases their rage and envy. The proper approach is not to attempt an answer.

One hopes for the removal of every enmity
Except enmity arising from envy.

Therefore, you must depart from such a person and leave them with their disease. Allah the Exalted said, "Withdraw from whoever turns away from Our warning and desires nothing except the worldly life." The envious person, in all they say and do, fuels the seeds of their own actions. As the Prophet, peace be upon him, said, "Envy eats up good deeds as fire eats up wood."

The second incurable type is one whose weakness stems from stupidity. As 'Isa (Jesus), peace be upon him, said, "Indeed, I did not fail in bringing the dead to life, but I failed in curing the stupid." This is the person who has briefly sought knowledge, learned a little about intellectual and sacred law sciences, and then, in their foolishness, questions and objects to a highly learned person who has dedicated their life to these very sciences. This ignorant individual does not realize that what is obscure to them is not necessarily obscure to the highly learned. Since their questions arise from such stupidity, you must not attempt to answer them.

The third type is someone seeking guidance. Whatever they do not understand from the words of great scholars, they attribute to their own deficient understanding, and their questions aim to seek benefit. However, they are dull and cannot grasp the truth of matters. You must also not attempt to answer them, as the Prophet, peace be upon him, said, "We, the company of the prophets, have been commanded to speak to people according to their understanding."

The curable sickness, however, belongs to the intelligent and perceptive seeker of guidance. This person is not overcome by envy, anger, or the love of worldly vanities, wealth, or honor. Instead, they seek the straight path, and their questions and objections do not arise from envy, a desire to cause trouble, or to test others. Such a person is curable, and it is not only permitted but necessary to attempt to answer them.

The second thing to avoid is becoming a preacher and a warner, as this role carries much harm unless you first practice what you preach before addressing others. Consider what was said to 'Isa (Jesus), peace be upon him: "O Son of Miriam (Mary), preach to yourself, and when you have preached to yourself, then preach to others; otherwise, be ashamed before your Lord." If you feel compelled to undertake this work, guard against two conditions. The first is affectation in speech—in explanations, allusions, vehement cries, versification, and poetry—because Allah the Exalted dislikes pretension. A person who is excessively pretentious reveals inner confusion and a heedless heart.

The significance of warning is to remind the worshipper of the fire of the Afterlife, so they confine themselves to the service of the Creator. It is to make them reflect on their past life, which they squandered on matters that did not concern them, and to consider the obstacles to the security of faith at life's end. They should ponder their state when grasped by the angel of death, whether they will be able to reply to Munkar and Nakir, and their condition during the Resurrection and its stages—will they cross the bridge safely or fall into the pit? These thoughts should remain in their heart and disturb their tranquility. Stirring up these fears and lamenting these afflictions is called "warning." Notifying humanity, drawing their attention to these matters, warning them about shortcomings and omissions, and making them see the defects of their souls—so that the intensity of these fears touches the people in the assembly, and these afflictions make them eager to rectify past years according to their ability, feeling regret over days spent in disobedience to Allah the Exalted—all of this, carried out in this manner, is called "preaching."

Imagine seeing a torrent suddenly rushing towards someone's house, with them and their family inside, and you cry out, "Danger, danger, flee from the flood!" Would your heart, in such circumstances, crave to deliver your message to the master of the house with pretentious explanations, witticisms, and allusions? Certainly not. Such is the condition of the preacher, and they must avoid such things.

The second condition to avoid is letting your concern in preaching be that people become agitated in your assembly, showing excitement and tearing their clothes, so that it is said, "What an assembly this was!" All this is an inclination towards worldly matters. True preaching aims to turn people from this world towards the Afterlife, from disobedience to obedience, from greed to abstinence, from stinginess to generosity, and from vanities to the fear of God. It aims to make them love the Afterlife and hate this world, and to teach them knowledge of worship and asceticism. This is because the predominant tendency in human nature is deviation from the clear path of the law and engagement in what Allah the Exalted does not approve, busying themselves with their evil characteristics. Cast fear into their hearts, frighten them, and warn them about the terrifying things they will encounter, hoping that the qualities of their inner lives will change, their outer deeds will transform, and a craving and desire for obedience will appear, leading them back from disobedience. This is the true method of preaching and admonition. Any preaching not of this sort is a pest for both speaker and listener; indeed, it is even called a *ghul* and a satan, which leads humanity astray and destroys them. People must flee from it, because the corruption this speaker inflicts upon their religion is greater than what Satan himself can achieve. Whoever holds power and authority must remove such a person from the pulpits of the Muslims and forbid them from what they proclaim, in accordance with the principle of "enjoining good and forbidding evil."

The third thing to avoid is mixing with princes and sultans, and even seeing them. For seeing them, sitting with them, and mixing with them leads to great harm. If you are compelled to do so, avoid praising and commending them, for Allah the Exalted is angered when an oppressor and an impious man is praised. Anyone who calls for the lengthening of their lives has delighted in Allah being disobeyed in His land.

The fourth thing to avoid is accepting any gifts or presents from princes, even if you believe it to be permissible. Coveting things from them corrupts religion, as it gives rise to flattery, subservience, and approval of their oppression—all of which corrupt religion. The least of its evils is that if you accept their gifts and benefit from their worldly possessions, you will grow fond of them. Whoever loves someone necessarily desires the prolongation of their life and presence. To love the continuance of an oppressor's life is to be willing for the oppression of the worshippers of Allah the Exalted and for the ruination of the learned. What could be more injurious than this to religion and the Afterlife? Be careful—be very careful—lest the fascination of Satan prove deceptive. Someone might tell you that it is better and preferable to take gold and silver from them and distribute it among the poor and needy, arguing that since they disburse it in profligacy and disobedience, your spending it on the weak is better than theirs. Indeed, the Cursed One has led many astray with this evil whispering, and its harm is excessive. We have discussed this in *Ihya ʿUlum al-Din* (The Revival of the Religious Sciences), so refer to it there.
