Imam Al-Ghazali has been emphasizing the idea that knowledge for its own sake holds no merit, and that knowledge without action serves as a proof against the one who learns or studies. The context for this advice is crucial: Al-Ghazali is addressing a senior scholar, as the scholarly class in his era had become a recognized profession, bringing with it respect, position, accolades, proximity to power, wealth, and recognition. Consequently, a segment of this class may have experienced compromised views and intentions.

Al-Ghazali, having reached the pinnacle of his career as one of the foremost scholars of the Muslim Ummah in his age, had witnessed and experienced various types of people and situations. Thus, when he speaks about action and the idea that knowledge should not be studied merely for its own sake, he is addressing individuals who have already dedicated decades to acquiring knowledge. This is distinct from the contemporary context where one might hesitate to attend a class or study due to perceived shortcomings in practice. His counsel is not intended to discourage seeking knowledge at foundational levels. He is speaking to someone at the culmination of their scholarly journey, reflecting on the path. Therefore, his message should not be interpreted as anti-intellectual or anti-clerical.

Tahajjud is among the most beloved acts of worship. The Shaykh recalls his teachers stating that it is a hallmark of the *Salihin* (righteous). One of his Shaykhs once asked him if he knew any righteous person who did not pray Tahajjud, prompting the Shaykh to reflect on his own spiritual journey. He also recounted asking another scholar for advice on waking up for Tahajjud, given the challenges of late nights and early mornings. In response, his Shaykh casually asked the other scholar, "When was the last time you missed Tahajjud?" The scholar, initially caught off guard, replied, "Never." This anecdote illustrates that Tahajjud is a profound mark of the righteous in the Muslim world.

Imam Al-Ghazali emphasizes Tahajjud not as a singular act that guarantees spiritual status, but as a means for intimate communion with Allah. Just as individuals seek privacy for important conversations with their loved ones, those who desire intimate colloquy with Allah do so in the dark of the night, undisturbed. When the people of the world turn to their spouses or their hobbies, the lover of Allah turns to their Beloved. This is a time when such intimate discourse takes place.

It is acknowledged that this practice is not easy for everyone and requires effort. Modern lifestyles, with late nights and early starts, can make it challenging, unlike traditional societies where sleep patterns might allow for daytime naps to facilitate night worship. It is important to remember that Tahajjud does not necessarily mean reciting a large portion of the Quran every night. It begins with intention. Simply setting an alarm to wake up is an intention. Even if one finds it difficult to rise from a warm bed on a cold night, the intention is paramount, for Allah sees intentions and attempts. Some *Awliya* (saints) prayed for extended periods, others recited Quran, and some simply engaged in *dhikr* (remembrance of Allah). The primary step is simply to be awake during that time. If one wakes up, even to use the bathroom, and cannot perform ablution or prayer, one can still turn to Allah in supplication before returning to bed. A Hadith, mentioned by Imam al-Nawawi, speaks of a person who wakes in the night, makes ablution, and quickly prays or seeks forgiveness, and their prayer is accepted, and their sins are forgiven.

Imam Al-Ghazali quotes three Quranic verses related to night vigil.
1.  "And a part of the night stand in vigil." (Surah Al-Isra, 17:79) – This is presented as a divine command, not only to the Prophet but to all righteous servants of Allah, to dedicate some time to night prayer, at least with intention and effort.
2.  "And by the early light, they ask forgiveness." (Surah Adh-Dhariyat, 51:18) – This is described as an act of gratitude. When servants wake to pray, they feel gratitude for Allah's blessings and for being enabled to remember Him. The commentator notes that this gratitude is also from Allah to His servants, as He appreciates their devotion by mentioning their act.
3.  "Those seeking forgiveness in the early dawn." (Surah Ali 'Imran, 3:17) – This is described as a remembrance of Allah. Seeking forgiveness (*Istighfar*) is a form of *dhikr*. Furthermore, when a servant remembers Allah, Allah also remembers them, signifying Allah's own *dhikr* in the world through His creation. This highlights the elevated status of this time for worship. All deeds should be performed with knowledge, but the most private acts of worship hold a special secret within them.

The Messenger of Allah said, "There are three sounds which Allah, most exalted, loves: the sound of the rooster when it crows, the sound of a person reciting the Quran, and the sound of those making *Istighfar* to Allah in the wee hours of the morning, just before dawn."

The rooster's crow is beloved because it makes *dhikr* of Allah and helps people wake up to remember Him. It is *Mustahab* (recommended) to say, "Allahumma inni as'aluka min fadlik" (O Allah, I ask You for Your virtues) upon hearing a rooster crow. The Shaykh shared his experience of living in the West Indies, where roosters would crow around 1:00 AM, which for early Muslims who slept at the beginning of the night, would be the later part of their night, indicating an early start to Tahajjud. Aisha (may Allah be pleased with her) narrated that the Prophet would wake up for Tahajjud when the rooster crowed.

The sound of a person reciting the Quran is beloved because it is like keeping company with Allah. The commentator notes that the Arabic word *mukalama*, used to describe this, in modern usage refers to a phone call, emphasizing the intimate connection one has with Allah when reciting His words.

The sound of those asking for forgiveness in the night is special because it represents a private, intimate conversation with Allah. Unlike loud public speech, this is a time for whispers, honest emotions, and raw pleas for forgiveness, often accompanied by tears, for shortcomings and inability to fulfill Allah's rights. This time is unique because the busyness of the day is absent, allowing for mental clarity and a heart full of devotion, free from worldly distractions and negative thoughts. It is when the channel between the servant and Allah is clearest, without the *wasawis* (whispers) of Shaytan.

Sufyan al-Thawri (may Allah have mercy on him), a great early scholar, is quoted as saying, "Verily Allah, most exalted, created a gentle breeze which blows in the wee hours of the morning, carrying the *adhkar* and *Istighfar* and entreaties of forgiveness to the majestic and all-powerful King, Allah." This illustrates that it is a time when "the court of the King is open," a moment to present one's requests to Allah when His attention and *Rahma* (mercy) are particularly close.

A caller from under Allah's Divine Throne announces the times for night worship:
1.  At the first part of the night, a caller cries out for the *Abidoon* (worshipers) to rise. They stand and pray as much as Allah allows. These worshipers find more comfort and relief in standing before Allah than in their beds, experiencing ecstasy and joy greater than worldly pleasures.
2.  At half the night, a caller cries out for the true devotees of Allah to rise. They stand and pray until morning.
3.  At the pre-dawn time, a caller cries out for those who seek forgiveness to rise. They get up and ask Allah for forgiveness.

The spiritual masters explain that the ecstasy and joy experienced by those who worship Allah in the night are so profound that worldly desires cannot compare. Some have said that if any pleasure of Paradise could be felt in this world, it would be in the sweetness of intimate discourse with Allah in the dead of the night. To experience this, one must first aspire and intend to be among those who perform night vigil.

The Shaykh mentions Ibn Bakr, a great saint of the past, who said that for 40 years, nothing saddened him except the coming of dawn, for it marked the end of his intimate time with his Beloved.

The commentator also outlines seven levels of Tahajjud, drawing from Imam Al-Ghazali's other works:
1.  **Staying up the entire night:** This is for those who find such sweetness and joy in their intimate discourse with Allah that it becomes a form of nourishment, preferring to sleep during the day when others are busy with worldly affairs. Some early Muslims would pray Fajr with the *wudu* of Isha.
2.  **Standing for half the night:** This involves sleeping for the first third of the night, waking for the middle third, and then sleeping for the final sixth. For example, in a nine-hour night (8 PM to 5 AM), one might sleep from 8 PM to 11 PM, wake from 11 PM to 3:30 AM for prayer, and then sleep again until Fajr. This post-Tahajjud sleep helps prevent drowsiness during the day.
3.  **Standing for a third of the night:** The Prophet (peace be upon him) would pray his Witr in the last part of the night, then lie down in his prayer spot until Bilal would call him for Fajr, ensuring he did not sleep too deeply. This sleep after worship is said to be a cause of spiritual unveilings and good dreams. This was also the practice of Prophet Dawud (peace be upon him).
4.  **Standing for a sixth or a fifth of the night.**
5.  **Praying as much as one desires:** This involves praying until tired, sleeping, and then waking to pray again, without a fixed measure. The Prophet also practiced this. Such a practice requires a "living heart," attuned to Allah, much like how one's internal clock wakes them for an important flight or exam.
6.  **Waking for two to four *rak'ahs*:** This involves making *wudu*, praying a short amount, and then sitting facing the *qibla* for a moment, engaging in *dhikr* or *dua*, simply to be counted among those who perform Tahajjud. This is suitable for those with busy schedules.
7.  The seventh type is not mentioned, as it refers to the person who does not wake up at all.

When Fajr rises, a caller announces, "Lo, let the heedless people rise." These individuals rise to pray Fajr "like the dead rise from their graves," performing what is obligatory but without the deeper devotion of night vigil. It is akin to expecting a reward for simply going to work or dressing one's child for school—it is what one *has* to do.

The scholars also explain that laziness in waking for Tahajjud is often due to sins. One must reflect on what one has seen, allowed into the heart, said, or done that might have caused this deprivation. Even missing Tahajjud once, after consistent practice, is seen by the pious as a sign of a sin committed. Sufyan al-Thawri once stated he was deprived of seven months of a particular act of worship due to a single sin: he saw a man crying in *dua* and thought to himself, "He is showing off." This illustrates the profound impact of even subtle internal faults on one's spiritual state. The Shaykh encourages comparing oneself to these lofty figures—the Prophets, saints, and scholars—not to feel depressed, but to set high aspirations and avoid complacency. A person on a spiritual path constantly strives to become more beloved to Allah.

Imam Al-Ghazali continues by quoting Luqman the Wise, whose wisdom is immortalized in the Quran. Al-Ghazali uses Luqman's example to define true wisdom, especially in an era where philosophical traditions sometimes diverged from orthodox Islam. A *Hakim* (wise person) is not one engrossed in worldly philosophies but one who has perfected their ego by drawing light from the knowledge of the *Deen* and achieved mastery in performing virtuous deeds to the best of their ability. Wisdom, in essence, is living a good life in all its meanings.

Luqman advised his son, "O my son, the rooster is more intelligent than you are; it calls out while you are sleeping." This highlights that all of Allah's creation makes *tasbih* (glorification) to Him, whether verbally or intrinsically by their very existence. The animals, with their own tongues, glorify Allah.

Al-Ghazali then quotes a poem:
"In the dead of the night, a dove was weeping atop a branch while I was sleeping.
I've lied by God's house, for if I was in love, I would not be outraced to weep by a dove.
I claim longing and love for my Lord, yet I lie while I do not weep, even animals cry."
The poet, upon hearing a dove cry in the night while he slept, felt shame, realizing that if his love for Allah were true, he would not be outdone in devotion by an animal. This serves as a reminder to awaken and cry out to Allah.

Imam Al-Ghazali then states the summary of all knowledge: "that you know what obedience and worship really are." Everything learned and done should actualize this understanding. It is not merely about knowing the definition, but grasping its reality. He implies that acquiring enough knowledge to understand these two things is sufficient, and further study beyond this point is not necessarily expected. True knowledge lies in understanding the secrets and subtleties of worship and obedience to Allah. This perspective emphasizes implementation over mere accumulation of facts. While acknowledging the need for scholars who dedicate themselves to compiling knowledge, Al-Ghazali's advice is directed at those who have already achieved significant learning, urging them to prioritize action and understanding the essence of their studies.

He clarifies that obedience and worship entail following Allah's commands and prohibitions in speech and deed, in accordance with the Sacred Law (*Sharia*). If an act is not in line with the *Sharia*, it is a sin, even if one perceives it as good. For example, fasting on Eid or the days of Tashriq is forbidden, and praying in a stolen garment is sinful, despite the outward appearance of worship. This warning addresses those who might be lax in their approach to *Sharia*, dressing up sins as worship; extremists who commit violence or misapply *fiqh* while believing they uphold the law; and innovators who introduce practices not sanctioned by *Sharia*.

Al-Ghazali advises against being deceived by the ecstatic statements and abstruse utterances of some Sufis. He stresses that true spiritual progress on this path occurs only through *mujahada* (struggle) against one's *nafs* (ego or lower self), by cutting off its desires and slaying its follies with spiritual rigors and practices. He warns that a loose tongue with a closed heart, filled with distraction and desire, is a sign of eternal damnation. The *nafs* cannot be purified, nor can one be brought to life by the lights of experiential closeness with Allah, except through sincere struggle against the ego.

The commentator notes that the best way to embark on this path is to limit food and drink to what is necessary, as the stomach is the source of other desires. From the stomach, desires extend to private parts, then to wealth, and then to glory (*Jah*). Wealth and glory, in excess, lead to calamities like pride, showing off, envy, and enmity. The Prophet (peace be upon him) emphasized hunger and fasting, not necessarily constant fasting, but avoiding constant satiation and always getting what one desires. It is about holding back, even leaving a small portion of a delicious meal. Aisha (may Allah be pleased with her) is attributed with saying, "Keep knocking on the door of Jannah and it will open to you," and when asked how, she replied, "By hunger and thirst," meaning not always satisfying oneself. This is particularly challenging in modern times with abundant food and conveniences. The commentator also clarifies that Al-Ghazali's caution against ecstatic Sufi utterances refers to those expressions lacking a basis in *Sharia*.

Finally, Al-Ghazali addresses his student, stating that some questions in the letter cannot be answered through writing or speech. These are matters of experiential knowledge (*dhawq*), which must be tasted directly. Just as the sweetness of honey or the bitterness of vinegar cannot be described in words but only known through tasting, so too are certain spiritual realities. He recounts an anecdote of an impotent man who asked a friend to describe the pleasure of sexual intercourse. The friend replied that the man was not only impotent but foolish, as such pleasures are experiential and cannot be conveyed through words. Al-Ghazali uses this to convey that the student must apply himself to the path of Allah to understand the answers to his deeper questions. Not all knowledge comes from books; some is experiential. The sweetness and pleasure of reaching Allah surpass any worldly pleasure, but one must attain that state to comprehend it.
