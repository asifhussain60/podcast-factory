The sixth lesson of Imam al-Ghazali's 'Ayyuhal Walad,' or 'O Beloved Son,' began. Recalling the previous week, Imam al-Ghazali had introduced the concept of inner knowledge, distinguishing it from outward knowledge. Outward knowledge is that which can be read, reasoned with, and expressed verbally. However, he explained to his student, and by extension to all seekers, that certain inquiries pertained to *dhawqi* knowledge – experiential knowledge that cannot be conveyed through words. He likened describing experiential knowledge to explaining carnal desire and its fulfillment to someone who has never experienced it; words are insufficient, one must feel it to understand. Imam al-Ghazali also hinted that all sensory experiences in this world serve as indications towards what is felt in the higher, spiritual realm. He conveyed that some truths cannot simply be told; the seeker must reach a certain level in their spiritual practice and pursuit of Allah for these answers to unfold and make sense. He reiterated that some of the student's questions were experiential, pertaining to a spiritual reality that is not tangible, and thus could not be answered in writing.

Regarding other questions in the student's letter that *could* be answered, Imam al-Ghazali referred to his previous magisterial work, 'Ihya Ulum al-Din,' and other writings where these topics were already addressed. However, recognizing the extensive nature of 'Ihya Ulum al-Din' (often spanning four to eight volumes), he offered a few concise points for immediate benefit, rather than simply directing the student to search through his larger works.

The first question posed by the student concerned what is incumbent upon a spiritual traveler. Imam al-Ghazali responded by outlining four essentials:
1.  Correct beliefs, free from innovation.
2.  Sincere repentance, without returning to old sins.
3.  Satisfying all opponents, ensuring no one has a claim against oneself.
4.  Attaining knowledge of the sacred law (Sharia) to the extent necessary to carry out Allah's commands.

The Shaykh noted that while Imam al-Ghazali presented these points concisely, assuming his senior student's familiarity, they warrant elaboration for contemporary seekers. Many aspire to spiritual experiences, inner peace, and lofty character traits, but Imam al-Ghazali emphasizes these four foundational elements as prerequisites.

The first essential is **correct beliefs without any innovation**. This refers to the *Aqidah* (creed) of Ahlus Sunnah wal Jama'ah, the prophetic Sunnah, and the majority of Muslims. It means possessing sound beliefs, free from theological intricacies, foreign ideas, or *bid'ah* (innovation). *Bid'ah* is defined as anything introduced into the religion that contradicts the Quran and Sunnah, lacking any basis within the tradition. This definition is crucial because the concept of innovation is often misunderstood today, with some mistakenly believing that any novelty within the Islamic community is inherently wrong. This is not the case. For instance, broadcasting lessons via video, speaking into a microphone, or reading commentary on an iPad with a stylus are technological innovations that aid the spread of knowledge without conflicting with Islamic values. Similarly, spiritual litanies and formulas composed after the Prophet's era, consisting of Quranic verses, *dhikr*, and prayers to Allah, are permissible because they align with Islamic teachings and do not contradict the Quran or Sunnah. Such innovations can even be *wajib* (obligatory) or *sunnah* (recommended).

However, Imam al-Ghazali refers to *bid'ah sayyi'ah* (bad innovation), which involves innovations in belief. Examples include believing in prophethood after the Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him), attributing created qualities to Allah, or declaring something antithetical to Islam as permissible due to changing times (e.g., claiming pork is halal). The danger of such innovations in belief is evident in previous divine traditions, which gradually went astray by allowing changes to their practices and beliefs. This opened the floodgates to widespread alterations, eventually transforming their scriptures and religions into something fundamentally different from their original divine revelation, and from Islam. Imam al-Ghazali, living in an era of numerous splintered Muslim sects and philosophical movements, emphasized the importance of correct beliefs, urging seekers to relinquish any attachments to various innovations. To ensure sound beliefs, one should adhere to the majority of Muslims, whose creed is generally sound. The Shaykh cautioned against individuals who claim the vast majority of Muslims are misguided, asserting that only they and their followers are on the path of truth (*haq*). He recommended taking foundational courses in creed, such as 'Aqidat Tahawiyyah' or 'Kharidah,' to establish a firm understanding.

The second essential is **sincere repentance (*Tawbatan Nasuh*)**. The term *naseeha* implies sincere advice and well-wishing. *Tawbatan Nasuh* is not merely verbal repentance followed by a return to sin. *Tawba* signifies turning back from a path. Allah uses this term for both our turning back to Him from sin and His turning back to us with mercy. Sincere repentance means genuinely abandoning past sins, particularly those committed out of ignorance or desire. If one slips, they repent again. It implies a firm intention, not a superficial acknowledgment, especially for those struggling with addictions or ingrained habits that require deeper self-examination to overcome. Spiritual masters emphasize the importance of this 'digging in' as *takhliya* (emptying) before *tahliya* (adorning). One must first cleanse the soul of negative traits before adorning it with virtues. This is akin to cleaning an old, decrepit house before furnishing and decorating it; otherwise, any adornment is superficial.

Imam al-Ghazali implies that any spiritual endeavor without first removing the negative character traits that hinder one's connection to Allah is like building on water – it lacks foundation and will inevitably collapse.
