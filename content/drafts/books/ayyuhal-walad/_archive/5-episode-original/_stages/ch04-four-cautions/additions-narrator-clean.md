Imam al-Ghazali, in the concluding section of his letter 'Ayyuhal Walad' (O Beloved Son), offers eight pieces of advice to his senior student, emphasizing that this counsel is crucial lest knowledge become a proof against him on the Day of Judgment. These eight pieces of advice comprise four actions to undertake and four to desist from. The Shaykh begins with the four things to desist from. The first of these, as previously discussed, is to avoid debating anyone on any matter as much as possible. Debate often brings forth blameworthy character traits such as showing off, envy, pride, and enmity. While there are rare circumstances where debate for the sake of truth (haq) is permissible, the sign of such a sincere debate is indifference as to whether the truth is revealed through one's own tongue or that of an opponent. Furthermore, a sincere seeker of truth prefers private inquiry over public confrontation, as private discussions are more conducive to sincerity. Imam al-Ghazali now elaborates further on this first point, offering a beneficial insight.

Imam al-Ghazali states that asking a question about problematic issues is akin to revealing a sickness of the heart to a doctor. When one poses a question to a scholar, it signifies a discrepancy in understanding or a lack of knowledge that one seeks to resolve. The scholar's answer, in turn, is like a doctor's treatment working towards curing that sickness. Ignorant people, therefore, possess a sickness of the heart, and scholars are the doctors tasked with treating this condition. Ignorance is likened to a sickness because a lack of knowledge in matters of faith (deen) can lead to deficiencies in one's practice, understanding, or fulfillment of rights, thereby creating a problematic situation. This refers specifically to ignorance of matters pertinent to one's spiritual well-being and the Hereafter, not general worldly knowledge.

Continuing this analogy, Imam al-Ghazali explains that a deficient scholar is not adept at treating hearts. This implies that not every individual who claims knowledge or has studied superficially is equipped to answer all types of questions. Just as one would not trust an untrained or unspecialized doctor to perform complex surgery, a deficient scholar is likely to err in treating spiritual ailments. A common saying reflects this: "A half-baked doctor is a threat to your life, but a half-baked scholar is a threat to your Iman (faith)." The danger of consulting an unqualified scholar is thus even greater than that of an unqualified doctor. Unfortunately, while people would not self-diagnose serious medical issues via online searches, they often seek religious answers from unreliable online sources rather than qualified scholars. An answer from a deficient scholar can be detrimental: it might be incorrect, leading to incorrect religious practice; it might lack wisdom (hikmah), causing doubt or confusion; or it might even induce spiritual misgivings (waswasa). A sign of an unqualified teacher, as some scholars have noted, is that their students develop waswasa.

In contrast, a complete scholar, though capable, does not attempt to treat every patient. Such a scholar only engages with those from whom they anticipate acceptance of the treatment and genuine improvement. If an illness is terminal, incurable, or incapacitating, an intelligent doctor, based on experience, will not expend effort on medication, as it would be a waste of their valuable time. Similarly, a scholar who discerns that answering a particular person's questions will not alleviate their ignorance, for various reasons, will refrain from doing so. A complete scholar values their time and does not wish to waste it on endeavors unlikely to yield a positive outcome for the sake of Allah. While intention is rewarded, the scholar also considers the effective result. Imam al-Ghazali explains that the disease of ignorance manifests in four types. One type of person will respond positively to treatment, meaning they will understand and benefit from the knowledge. However, three other types of people will not benefit from an answer, even if provided.

The first of the three types of people who will not respond to treatment are those whose questions or objections stem from envy or hatred. This is a grave issue, often seen when individuals harbor jealousy or dislike for a scholar from a different group, or one who is popular, pious, or knowledgeable. Such feelings can lead to disparaging remarks, online attacks, or the deliberate posing of controversial questions to trap or discredit the scholar. Envy (hasad) is defined as seeing a blessing in another and wishing for its removal, or desiring it for oneself. This often arises from pride, a feeling of undeservedness, or rivalry. Envy is a primal sin, linked to Iblis's jealousy of Adam and Cain's murder of Abel. Shaitan continues to envy humanity due to Allah's love and mercy towards us. This destructive emotion is a serious problem even within religious circles. Unlike permissible emulation (ghibta), where one desires a good quality without wishing its loss for another, envy seeks to diminish others. When questions arise from envy or hatred, they are not sincere inquiries but attempts to undermine. Imam al-Ghazali states that responding to such individuals, even with the most brilliant, eloquent, and clear Islamic answers, only intensifies their anger and jealousy. The more one engages, the more aggressive they become, seeking to nitpick, invent falsehoods, or attack unrelated aspects like language skills. Therefore, the best course of action is not to engage in a response, but to turn away from such a person and leave them with their illness. As Allah states in the Quran, "So turn away from those who turn away from Our remembrance and desire nothing but the worldly life." This implies that such individuals are not seeking proximity to Allah and His Messenger, but merely engaging in debate.

Imam al-Ghazali advises the scholar not to waste time responding to haters and enviers. The best approach is silence, turning away, and leaving them with their illness, which is not merely ignorance but a disease of the heart that a scholar cannot extract. He quotes Abdullah ibn Mubarak: "For every type of enmity there is a remedy, except when they hate due to envy and jealousy."
