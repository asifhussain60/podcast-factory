# Narrator additions — ch05-method-and-closing-prayer (Shaykh Abdullah Misra, attributed commentary)

_Cleaned from lecture transcript(s): lec12-four-things-to-do. This is the explainer's commentary (an attributed ADDITION), distinct from the treatise core._

Imam al-Ghazali concludes his epistle, *Ayyuhal Walad*, with eight pieces of advice to ensure that one's knowledge does not become a burden against them on the Day of Judgment. These advices are divided into four things to refrain from and four things to do. The focus here is on the four things one ought to do.

The first thing to do, as Imam al-Ghazali states, is to "make your dealings with Allah Most High such that if you had a servant and your servant were to deal with you in the same way, you would be pleased with it and pleased with them, and that you would not be annoyed by them or get angry." This means reflecting on one's inner and outer conduct with Allah. One should consider how an ideal servant would behave: obediently, respectfully, punctually, diligently, and sincerely. If one would not tolerate disobedience, rudeness, tardiness, slacking off, taking advantage, or secret dislike from a metaphorical worldly servant, then one should not offer such conduct to Allah, the true Master. The term "servant" is used metaphorically in a worldly context, as only Allah is the true Master, and all humans are His servants. This high standard of behavior expected from a worldly servant should be applied to one's relationship with Allah. This prompts a profound self-evaluation of one's servanthood, extending beyond mere adherence to halal and haram, to encompass how much of one's daily actions are truly tied to service, love, and worship of Allah.

The second thing to do is that "every time you behave with people or interact with people, make your interaction the way that you would want them to interact with you, because the faith of a servant is not complete until he loves for all of people what he loves for themselves." This is often referred to as the "golden rule": treat others as one wishes to be treated. This principle applies to all interactions, from driving in traffic to personal relationships. One should consider if they would want others to be rude, unhelpful, or to backbite them, and then refrain from such actions. Conversely, one should extend courtesies they would appreciate, such as cleanliness in shared spaces. The standard for treating others should be what one expects for oneself, not a minimal effort deemed "good enough" for them. This advice is rooted in the Hadith of the Prophet Muhammad, narrated by Sayyidina Anas ibn Malik, which states, "None of you truly believes until you love for your brother or your sister what you love for your own self." This encompasses treating others with gentleness, mercy, respect, overlooking their faults, humility, covering their sins, refraining from complaint, and avoiding harm in speech or action. It encourages breaking cycles of retaliation, instead choosing to act based on how one desires to be treated.

The third matter to act upon is that "whenever one pursues knowledge or reviews knowledge, it is befitting that it should always be knowledge that repairs one's heart and purifies one's soul." Imam al-Ghazali, a scholar of immense stature, emphasizes that after acquiring foundational knowledge, one's continued pursuit should focus on spiritual benefit. This includes the sciences of *akhlaq* (character) and spirituality, but also any knowledge that fosters greater obedience to Allah. A common challenge for students of knowledge is to study extensively without personal transformation, treating spirituality as merely another academic subject rather than an integrated way of life. Traditionally, great scholars were also profound spiritual masters. Al-Ghazali illustrates this point with a powerful analogy: if one knew they had only a week left to live, they would not busy themselves with intricate details of *fiqh*, polemics, *usul al-fiqh*, or theology (*kalam*). Instead, they would focus on "watching over the heart" (*murakabat al-qalb*), understanding the qualities of the self (*ma'rifat sifat al-nafs*), turning away from worldly concerns, purifying the self from lower character traits, seeking forgiveness, settling debts, and dedicating oneself to the love and worship of Allah, adorning oneself with good qualities. Since death is ever-present, one should not delay this internal rectification.

It is crucial to understand that Imam al-Ghazali is not discouraging the study of essential Islamic sciences for everyone. He is addressing a senior scholar who has already mastered these fields, advising against the endless accumulation of knowledge for its own sake or as a mere hobby. Foundational knowledge necessary to practice one's religion remains obligatory. Knowledge, in this context, is a *means* to action and to cultivate a proper state of the heart, not an end in itself. Historically, Imam al-Ghazali himself continued his scholarly work, fulfilling the collective obligation (*fard kifaya*) of teaching and writing. His advice is directed at those who might study extensively without internalizing or applying the knowledge to their inner state.

Regarding "busying oneself with the love of Allah," the Shaykh explains that while divine love is a gift from Allah, one must strive to acquire it. This involves emptying the heart of all desires except for Allah, allowing His name to become firmly rooted within. When this occurs, one experiences the "savor of being cut off from everything except Allah," finding sweetness in His remembrance and becoming disinclined to attach the heart to anything else. This, the Shaykh notes, is the entire point of the spiritual path.

Al-Ghazali further illustrates his point with another parable: if a king were to visit one's home in a week, one would meticulously prepare every detail that the king might observe—one's clothes, body, and house. This serves as an analogy for preparing one's heart for Allah. The Prophet Muhammad is reported to have said, "Verily Allah does not look at your outward forms nor at your wealth, but rather He looks at your acts and your hearts." This emphasizes that Allah looks beyond physical appearance, material possessions, or even the outward form of deeds, focusing instead on the inward aspect of actions and intentions found in the heart. For deeper knowledge of the states of the heart, al-Ghazali refers his student to his magnum opus, *Ihya Ulum al-Din*. He asserts that knowledge of the heart's sins and virtues is *fard ayn* (individually obligatory) on every Muslim, just like learning the basics of prayer or fasting. Other sciences, while important, are often *fard kifaya* (communal obligations), meaning if enough people fulfill them, the obligation is lifted from the rest of the community. However, the knowledge required to fulfill one's personal religious duties is *fard ayn*.

The fourth and final thing to do is "not to stock up more than a single year's worth of worldly needs," such as food and provisions, just as the Messenger of Allah would do for some of his wives. The Prophet Muhammad would save a year's worth of grain for certain wives who needed that sense of security. However, for wives with profound *yaqeen* (certainty and reliance on Allah), such as Aisha, he would save only enough for a day or even half a day. This demonstrates the Prophet's sensitivity to individual needs and mentalities; some individuals require a sense of material security to prevent worry, while others possess a deep reliance on Allah that allows them to live day-to-day. The Prophet himself preferred to live simply, making the supplication, "O Allah, make the sustenance of the family of Muhammad just enough." This advice implies a balance: one can secure necessities for a year, but should avoid excessive accumulation. It also cautions against imposing extreme austerity on others, especially family members, without considering their individual spiritual journey and level of reliance on Allah. One should provide for one's family's needs within reasonable means.

In summary, the eight advices from Imam al-Ghazali are:
The four things to refrain from:
1.  Do not debate, avoiding needless arguments and confrontation.
2.  Do not be a preacher in a way that leads to ostentation and hypocrisy.
3.  Do not mingle with rulers, to avoid compromising one's religion for status.
4.  Do not accept gifts from rulers, to guard against greed and dependency.

The four things to do:
1.  Reflect on one's dealings with Allah, cultivating true servanthood.
2.  Treat others as one wishes to be treated, with equality and respect.
3.  Prioritize spirituality and character development in the pursuit of knowledge.
4.  Maintain a balance in worldly possessions, stocking up only for necessary needs, not excessively.

The entire *Ayyuhal Walad* letter can be summarized in several key points: Prophetic Hadiths offer sufficient guidance; knowledge alone is insufficient for salvation, actions are paramount; spiritual realization, not mere information, is the essence of knowledge and action; the ultimate goal of knowledge is to understand true obedience and worship; some knowledge is experiential and requires practice; there are four essentials for the seeker; eight wisdoms are presented; the importance of a true spiritual guide and proper etiquette (*adab*) with them; and the core characteristics of spirituality (*tasawwuf*)—slavehood, reliance on Allah (*tawakkul*), and sincerity (*ikhlas*). The letter culminates with these eight advices, designed to ensure that one's knowledge serves as a benefit, not a detriment, on the Day of Judgment.

Imam al-Ghazali concludes his letter by addressing his student's requests, urging him to act upon the advice given and to remember him in his pious prayers, as a form of gratitude. Regarding the student's request for a specific supplication (*dua*), al-Ghazali first directs him to the authentic Prophetic duas found in Hadith literature, then provides a comprehensive prayer. This concluding dua seeks Allah's mercy, ease in attaining experiential knowledge of Him, protection from sins, comprehensive well-being, the best of this life and the hereafter, excellence, and abundant generosity. It asks Allah to pour mercy upon sins, rectify deficiencies, make consciousness of Him one's provision, direct striving towards His path, make reliance solely upon Him, and lead to His supreme pleasure. It also prays for steadfastness, entry into paradise, refuge from worldly regrets, ease from burdens, a life of good deeds, protection from evil, and freedom from Hellfire for oneself and one's family. The essence of the entire work, as summarized by a question from the audience, is indeed the truly prophetic way of living—spiritually, physically, and mentally.
