# Do Not Say — Guardrails for *The Master and the Disciple*

**[NotebookLM Instruction]** These are anti-patterns specific to this book. They are NOT general podcast advice — they exist because this work has been misrepresented in predictable ways. If you find yourself about to say one of these, stop and re-read the corresponding `[Editorial Clarification]` paragraph in the relevant chapter, then rephrase.

This file is read alongside `02-glossary.md` (meaning), `01-pronunciation-guide.md` (sound), and `03-source-integrity-notes.md` (citation handling).

---

## 1. Theological reductions

| Do not say | Why it fails | Say instead |
|---|---|---|
| "This is basically a mentorship book." | Reduces a treatise on covenant, interpretation, hierarchy, and living guidance to a self-help framing. | "This is an early Ismaili dialogue about how a seeker becomes capable of receiving knowledge through covenant, interpretation, and rightful authority." |
| "The book rejects outward practice." | False. Ch 3 and Ch 6 explicitly hold the outward and inner together. | "The book argues that outward practice (Sharee-ah) and inner meaning (baatin) belong together — the inner does not abolish the outward." |
| "Inner meaning makes Sharee-ah unnecessary." | A serious theological misrepresentation. The book teaches the opposite. | "Inner meaning is reached *through* Sharee-ah, not around it. Ch 6 names this as the body-soul relationship." |
| "Just find a wise mentor." | Empties the word *Imaam* of its theological content. The book is making a claim about a specific structure of rightful guidance, not a generic mentor-hunt. | "The book argues guidance has a rightful structure — Naa-tiq, Wasi, Imaam, Hujjah, Daa-ee — not that any wise figure will do." |
| "Submit to a charismatic teacher." | Conflates *Wilaayah* with personality worship. Ch 4's listener objection block addresses this directly. | "The book presents allegiance as discipline under verified, rightful guidance — not personality worship. The jewel-and-glass metaphor in Ch 6 is precisely about how to verify." |
| "All Muslims believe X." (for any X drawn from this book) | This is an early **Ismaili** text. Its claims about the seven Imaams, the Naa-tiq cycle, and living guidance after prophecy are tradition-specific. | "The Ismaili tradition teaches… Other Muslim traditions differ on this." |
| "Knowledge equals information." | The book's whole argument is that knowledge is a *trust* that changes the carrier. | "The book treats knowledge as a trust — something the seeker becomes worthy to bear, not something to collect." |

## 2. Format reductions

| Do not say | Why it fails | Say instead |
|---|---|---|
| "It's like *Plato's dialogues*." | Tempting, lazy, and misleading. Plato is dialectic toward propositional truth; this work is dialogue toward initiatic formation. | "It's a dialogue — but unlike Plato's, the goal is not the proof of a thesis. The goal is the formation of the seeker." |
| "It's basically Sufi." | The Ismaili Daa-ee tradition has overlaps with Sufism but is distinct in its claims about living guidance and the structure of authority. | "Ismaili and Sufi traditions share concerns with inner meaning and disciplined interpretation, but the book's claims about living guidance through the Imaam are specifically Ismaili." |
| "It's a wisdom book." | Generic. Strips the theological content. | "It's an early Ismaili dialogue, in the tradition associated with Ja'far ibn Mansoor al-Yaman, about seekership and rightful guidance." |
| "Old book, ancient wisdom, deep stuff." | Vague reverence. Fails the *podcast-best-practices.md* "name why" test. | Name a specific stake: covenant, interpretation, authority, or daily action. |

## 3. Quotation handling

| Do not say | Why it fails | Say instead |
|---|---|---|
| "Hadith says…" (followed by a paraphrase) | Treats traditional attribution as fully-footnoted academic source. See `03-source-integrity-notes.md`. | "In the tradition, the Prophet, peace be upon him, is remembered as having taught…" |
| "The Quran says…" (followed by a transliteration spoken aloud) | The transliteration is editorial; spoken delivery should use the **translation** for the listener. | Quote the translation. The transliteration is a reading aid in the text, not a spoken-delivery target. |
| "Ali said exactly…" (followed by a quotation with no edition citation) | Wording in Nahj al-Balagha varies across translations; the book's wording is itself a translation choice. | "Ali, peace be upon him, is remembered in the tradition collected as *Nahj al-Balagha* as having taught…" |
| Crediting any attributed teaching without honorific | Disrespectful by the standards of the source community. | First mention: full name + "peace be upon him." See `01-pronunciation-guide.md` §"Honorific protocol." |

## 4. Pronunciation discipline

| Do not say | Why it fails | Say instead |
|---|---|---|
| "Saa-LEE" / "Sah-LEE" / "SAY-lih" | Mangles the seeker's name. | "**SAA-lih**" — long-A first syllable, light second syllable. |
| "AL-luh" / "Al-LAH" | Compresses to one syllable; loses the long-A reverence cadence. | "**Al-LAAH**" — two clear syllables, stress on the second. |
| "Sha-REE-uh" with a schwa ending | Common error. The "-ah" is open. | "**Sha-REE-ah**" with an open final syllable. |
| Skipping the Quranic transliteration in delivery | Acceptable — the translation is the spoken target. | The English translation, cleanly. The transliteration is a reading aid in the text. |
| Guessing at a name not in the pronunciation guide | Risks doublings and mangling. | Stop. Spell it slowly letter-by-letter, or skip it and surface the name in the human-review queue. |

## 5. Modern-analogy discipline

The chapters use specific modern analogies (software, GPS, family business, jeweler verification). Each analogy has a defined job. **Do not invent new modern analogies on the fly**, and do not stack analogies inside a single beat.

| Do not say | Why it fails | Say instead |
|---|---|---|
| "It's like an algorithm." | Vague tech-flavored mush. The chapter uses *specific* tech analogies (interface/code/database for the three levels; authorized access for the oath). Use those. | Use the chapter's own analogy if one is offered. Otherwise stay in the chapter's register. |
| "It's basically deep learning." / "neural network" / "blockchain" / "AGI" | Anachronistic and reductive. | None. Don't reach for these. |
| Three analogies in one sentence | Buries the original idea under metaphor stacking. | One analogy per beat. Return to the chapter's language between beats. |

See `03-source-integrity-notes.md` §"Editorial-clarification paragraphs" for the canonical analogy → chapter map.

## 6. Tone discipline

| Do not say | Why it fails | Say instead |
|---|---|---|
| Casual jokes about sacred concepts, names of Imaams, or Quranic verses | Disrespectful by the source community's standards. | Keep humor for *modern listener confusion*, not for sacred names or claims. |
| "Spoiler alert: he becomes wise." | Trivializes the chapter arc. | Use the spoiler-policy line in `00-NotebookLM-Source-Index.md`. |
| "I don't really get it either, but it sounds deep." | Fails the listener. The book has structure; if a host is lost, the customize prompt is wrong. | Either explain the difficulty directly (using the chapter's Listener Difficulty block) or surface the confusion as a host question. Never wave it away. |
| Reverent flattening — turning every passage into "and the lesson is, be humble" | Replaces analysis with admiration. | Name the specific tension the chapter holds: covenant vs. understanding, outward vs. inner, hierarchy vs. ego, etc. |

## 7. NotebookLM-specific failure modes (from prior transcript audits)

| Do not | Why | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|
| Repeat the same Arabic term in three consecutive sentences | NotebookLM tends to do this when it has a "favorite" term from the source. Listeners hear it as mechanical. | After defining a term once, refer to it by its English meaning in subsequent sentences for the next minute of audio. |
| Read inline parentheticals aloud | NotebookLM will literally pronounce `(SAA-lih)` if it appears in the source text. | The chapter prose files for this book do NOT have inline phonetics. They live in `01-pronunciation-guide.md` only. Per R-PHONETICS-OUT in the podcast skill. |
| Stack honorifics on every mention of every name | Listener fatigue, and the book itself models a lighter cadence. | First mention: full honorific. Subsequent mentions: name alone. See pronunciation guide §"Honorific protocol." |
| Add irrelevant biographical background ("born in the year…") | The book is not a biography. Tradition-attributed authorship and dating are uncertain (see `03-source-integrity-notes.md`). | Cite tradition association, not biography. |

---

**[NotebookLM Instruction]** When in doubt between a faithful framing and a punchier framing, pick the faithful framing. The book is dense; the audience is rewarded by clarity, not by cleverness. The host's job is to make a hard book feel *navigable*, not to make it feel *casual*.
