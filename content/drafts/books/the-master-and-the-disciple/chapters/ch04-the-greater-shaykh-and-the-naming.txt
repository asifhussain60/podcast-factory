# The Greater Shaykh and the Seventh-Day Naming

*This chapter covers the fourth long teaching of* The Book of the Master and the Boy *(*Kitāb al-ʿĀlim wa-l-Ghulām*), attributed to Sayyidina Ja'far ibn Mansur al-Yaman. Chapter three closed on a deferral: the boy had enacted the doctrine in five shares of his wealth, had asked to be taken by the hand to the door of the* generous one *— the Imam named only by his attributes — and the Master (the* scholar*) had answered* this is your duty — but only with his permission and his decision. *This chapter is the* answer *to that deferral. The Master goes to the boy's father with the report that the boy is now of the company of the people of goodness; the father marvels and tells the Master to* make haste*. The Master returns and finds the boy* in a different state of body *— weakened, yellowed, made ill by the longing of the absence. He delivers the announcement —* I cast mercy upon you into the heart of the one who has charge of you. So take up your traveling provisions, for I have high hopes for you, and I will accompany you to the door of your goal*. The boy and his father set out with the Master and reach the* greater Shaykh *— the elder of the chain, the master of the house in whose council the boy is so overcome with awe that he cannot speak. The Shaykh receives them, delivers an opening discourse on God's* justice *as the* middle between two extremes*, and a second discourse on the* dignity of minds *— the chain of rights running through* inquiry → good manners → knowledge → action → obedience*. He then turns his face to the boy and conducts the* seventh-day naming *— the central ritual of the chapter and one of the great initiation-figures of the* da'wa *(the call, the missionary apparatus of the Ismaili tradition). The boy gives his birth-name —* Ubayd Allah, son of Abd Allah*; the Shaykh receives it; the boy* withdraws *it —* I am free, son of Abd Allah*; the Shaykh presses the implication and, when the boy admits he is unable to claim a name for himself, defers the naming for* the completion of seven days*. The boy, in his childlike piercing way, asks the questions that turn the deferral into doctrine —* why seven? * /* for the dignity of the newborn*. /* and if the newborn dies before the seventh day? * /* nothing harms him. He is named after that*. /* is this the name by which you have named me? * /* would you be like an idol, even though you have been born? * /* how can such a thing be said of it? * /* the name belongs to you, and you belong to the name. So it does not appear except within your limit, and it travels with your duration*. On the seventh day the boy washes, puts on his purest garments, relieves himself ("as a sign of the good that was to come after it"), goes out repeating* the day of Sa'd, and the guardian of Sa'd*, and is received by the Shaykh* with the implements of his work. *The Shaykh then brings the boy near and delivers — in a veil the source itself never lifts — the* highest transmission*:* matters upon which no fancy had ever descended, which no pen had ever traced, and which had never occurred to the heart of any human being — matters that are not to be mentioned in the assemblies of preachers, and that are not permitted in mosques. *The transmission complete, the boy* removed his ihram *(the consecrated state of the Hajj pilgrim)* by glorifying the sanctities of Allah, took hold of the rope, circumambulated the ancient House, spent his rites in remembrance, and performed Hajj by the great sign. *Then attendance, the watering of the root, the raising of the branch. Then the* farewell*: the boy's praise (*you are the gate of the people of heaven and the ascent of the people of the earth*); the Shaykh's blessing (*may He ennoble your station with the method of the call*); the Shaykh's three reasons for parting (the next journey, the family awaiting the boy's return, the duty to* worship by it and call others to it*); and the Shaykh's enjoinder —* fear of Allah, preservation of the trust, safeguarding of the father. *Then the scholar walks the boy back toward his city, and, on the edge of the city, gives him the* six qualities *(*trust in Allah when you are resolved; speak with a ruling when you are called upon; do not grow angry when you are wronged; do not weary if you are slandered; whoever responds to you with good, be patient with him; and devote yourself to the best of those upon whom you call*) and the* interpretation key *for everything they have walked through together: every praiseworthy parable is* the truth and its people*; every reprehensible parable is* the enemies of the truth and its people. *The final parting — the boy* kissing the scholar's feet and wiping their dust upon his face *— closes the chapter. He returns to his house with his heart absent, and his family becomes afraid for him. The father, angry, comes to him. The chapter ends on that anger — the next chapter will pick up the confrontation, the revelation of the boy's name (*Salih*,), and the further* faces of seeking.*

## Where this chapter picks up

Chapter three ended on a deferral. The boy had laid all his wealth in the Master's hands in five shares — *zakat*, expiation, thanksgiving, the brethren's share, and a share for the Master himself — and the Master, in tears, had refused his own share, reapportioning the rest as *trusts held for their rightful owners*. The boy had asked who the *generous one* was; the Master had named him only by his attributes — *the one whose right Allah has made binding upon every believer, in whose hand are the keys of the heavens, whose palms are opened with the light of Sinai* — and had placed himself in the chain: *I am his son and one of his servants in good works*. The boy had then asked to be taken *by the hand to the door of his chamber*. The Master had answered: *this is your duty — but only with his permission and his decision*.

Chapter four is the *bringing*. The Master does not go directly to the *generous one* with the boy — he goes first to the *elder* of the chain, the *greater Shaykh*, in whose council the *naming* will happen. The bringing to the Imam's *door* is mediated: the boy must first *be named*, and the naming happens at the hand of the *Shaykh* who is the *cause* between the Master and the *one whose right is binding*. The chain works one way: the Master does not step over the Shaykh; the Shaykh does not step over the Imam; the Imam does not step over Allah. The boy who in chapter three learned the *form* of the chain (the *hawl* who *turns* the Creator's words; the *quwwa* who carries them) is now *moved through* the chain — and the first step is the seventh-day re-birth at the Shaykh's hand.

## The boy's weakness, the Master's announcement

The narrator opens with the report. The scholar took leave of the boy and went to his father, and *described the boy to him* — described him as *now of the company of the people of goodness and of the lineage of truthfulness*. The father, who had once been so anxious for the boy that the Master had to instruct the boy in chapter one to *stand with him with outward agreement and keep this your secret*, now *marvels*. The marvel is not yet repentance — that comes much later — but it is the first opening. The father gives the urgent instruction: *make haste with this boy, for I hope that he will be a door of mercy that Allah opens for the people of his age*. The father, who does not yet know the *door* the boy himself has asked to be brought to, voices the very figure the chapter will spend its strength on.

The scholar then returns to the boy — and finds him *in a different state of body than the one he had left him in*. The exchange that follows is the chapter's first interior moment. *What is this I see in you, different from the state in which I left you?* asks the Master. *And what is it that you have noticed in me — may my father and mother be your ransom?* asks the boy. *Weakness of body and yellowness of color*, says the Master. The boy explains: *that is from a trial that befell me after your departure*. The Master asks for the cause; the boy gives it. *Your absence grieved me, and fear of two events before meeting you again weakened me*. The two events are not named — but the listener, who has just walked chapter three's settlement on the *Imam's permission* and chapter three's close on the *generous one's door*, can hear them. The boy's body is yellowed by the longing of the absence: yellowed in the same way the brethren of chapter three were yellowed by *vigil* and *fasting* — *colors yellowed without cause*, the same word in the same key. The boy is being *folded into* the form of life he described to the Master in chapter three. The Master's brethren have become his brethren by the *yellowing*.

The Master's reply is the chapter's first doctrine. *Allah has known what is in your heart, and so has made the affliction easy. I cast mercy upon you into the heart of the one who has charge of you. So take up your traveling provisions, for I have high hopes for you, and I will accompany you to the door of your goal — and there is no power except by Allah*. Three things at once: the *affliction has been made easy* by Allah's knowledge of the heart; the *one who has charge of you* — the father — has had *mercy cast into his heart*, the very permission the boy in chapter one had been forbidden to ask for directly; the Master will *accompany you to the door of your goal*, the answer to chapter three's request. And the closing formula — *there is no power except by Allah* (*la hawla wa la quwwata illa billah*) — is the formula whose dropped *ta'wīl* the Master gave in chapter three: the *hawl* as the Imam who *turns* the Creator's words; the *quwwa* as his *hujja* and *bāb*. The Master invokes it as a doctrinal seal: *the journey is being carried by the Imam and his hujja; there is no other power*.

The boy is so overcome by the announcement that *his soul almost left his body* — and *until Allah took hold of him, knowing his condition; then He returned him*. The figure is striking: Allah *takes hold* of the boy's near-leaving soul and *returns* it. The boy revives. He says: *Praise be to Allah, who has not yet withdrawn from believers the mercy of compassion*. Then he turns to his father, *kisses his palms in service*, takes the traveling kit, and they set out.

The yellowing-by-longing the chapter places in the boy's body is, in the school's wider literature, the bodily signature of the seeker who has begun to live the doctrine in his own person. The Imam to whom the school attributes its great cycle of supplications returns to the figure repeatedly: he pleads to be granted *a body wearied in His service, a face yellowed in the night of His remembrance, an eye watered with the fear of Him* — the inward labor inscribed on the apparent body until the body becomes its own evidence of the work that has been done in it (paraphrased from *The Psalms of Islam*, supplication 20 on noble traits of character, trans. Chittick, London: Muhammadi Trust of Great Britain and Northern Ireland, 1988, pp. 84–89). The Master's reading of the boy's yellowing as the same yellowing the brethren of chapter three wore is not metaphor; it is the chapter's claim that the boy is now of the brethren in body, not merely in name.

## The journey to the greater Shaykh

The boy and the Master and the boy's father travel until they reach the *greater Shaykh*. Three figures travel; a fourth waits. The *greater Shaykh* — the elder of the *da'wa* chain — is the one whose council holds the *bāb* the chapter will lead to. They seek permission and are admitted. They greet, sit at the Shaykh's permission, and — when settled — the boy turns to his teacher (the Master who has brought him) and asks the natural question of one bewildered: *teach me how to speak and how to ask for what I need*.

The Master's reply is itself a teaching. *It is not for me to instruct you in this place. Out of respect for the master of this house, you are in the hands of one who knows your need and knows what you will*. In the Shaykh's house, the Master defers. He is not the *teacher in this place*; the teacher in this place is the Shaykh, who *knows your need and knows what you will* — knows it without being told. The chain works one way and one way only: the *cause* speaks; the *causes-of-the-cause* listen. The Master, who in chapters one through three has been the *Master*, becomes, in this council, the *brother of the Shaykh*. The chain has its own discipline of speech.

The boy sits with his teacher in the Shaykh's house *for some time* — *until the boy had completed his guidance and reached the end of his hope*. The phrasing is striking: *completed his guidance*, *reached the end of his hope*, *came to know what he had and what was required of him*. The Shaykh, the source says, *prayed and went out*; the Master and the boy *withdrew*. *They had spoken no words in the council save those of peace and well-being; the boy had turned yellow from the intensity of his awe and could not speak*. The boy who in chapter three was the active interlocutor, who pressed the Master through the great *ta'wīls* and enacted the wealth-doctrine in five shares, is now *silent from awe* — *yellowed* a second time, this time by the weight of the Shaykh's presence, *could not speak*. The hierarchy is visible in the body: the boy who could speak before the Master cannot speak before the Shaykh, because the Shaykh stands closer to the *generous one*.

The Shaykh — *the master of the house* — orders them *honored*, that *whatever they needed be done*. He brings them into his own house, greets them, and shows his *pleasure in them*. Then the Shaykh turns to the Master — *and between them was brotherhood and an old companionship in learning* — and asks: *Brother, is this the boy we used to hear about?* The Master replies: *Yes — the one whose remembrance is sweet in the hearts of the believers*. The boy is staggered. *Has the report of my story really reached the saints of Allah, until they study it in their gatherings of remembrance?* *Yes, my brother — that is how it is*. The boy who in chapter three described his *brethren* as the scholars yellow with vigil whom the public scorned, learns now that *he himself* is the subject of *their* gatherings of remembrance. The chain has been knowing him longer than he has been knowing the chain. He answers: *You have surrounded me from every side and overtaken me from every direction. I do not know whom to thank, nor whom to repay*.

Then *they all sat down and conversed through the night*. The chapter does not record the conversation: the night of the brethren is not for the record. The chapter's record begins again at morning, when they seek permission to enter upon the Shaykh.

## The Shaykh's opening discourse: justice between two extremes

When the three have been admitted to the Shaykh's chamber and have completed their greetings, the Shaykh gives them permission to sit, and they sit. Then the Shaykh begins to speak. The discourse is the first of two sustained openings the Shaykh gives. The first is on *God's justice*; the second is on the *dignity of minds*.

The Shaykh opens with the doxology. *Praise be to Allah, who has opened their hearts with His light, and who is pleased by His justice when He is remembered as He requires, and is pleased that remembrance becomes a kindness to His servants and a mercy from Him to whoever He wills*. The opening is the *light* the Shaykh has presided over their being opened to — the *light* the boy in chapter three was told would be *completed through him*. The Shaykh proceeds to the doctrine of the *middle*. *He has not taken creation as a duty owed to Him, nor has He charged them with what they cannot bear in disbelief of His blessings. Rather, the affair is set in the middle between two extremes — and this is what is called justice*. The doctrine of *capacity* the Master delivered in chapter three (*God charged the whole of His creation according to its capacity and its work*) is here given as a doctrine of *the middle*: not the extreme of *duty owed* (which would burden the creature beyond what he can bear), nor the extreme of *no obligation at all* (which would leave the creature without a path), but *the middle* — and that *middle is what is called justice*.

The Shaykh then completes the figure with the doctrine of *unrestricted reward* and *unrestricted reproach*. *He has spread out His hands with goodness; He has not disappointed one who calls upon Him, nor turned away one who is silent. Indeed, the bounty of the heavens and the gratitude due to the Lord are not reserved to those who have given thanks for them, on account of the multitude of His reward — and there is no withholding from the one who is ungrateful for them, on account of the greatness of His punishment. So He has shielded His blessings from any sting of denial, and made His proofs against denial fully documented; and He has expanded over what exists, so that the evidence against ingratitude is itself unlimited, known yet not fully describable*. The figure of *spread-out hands*: bounty is given to grateful and ungrateful alike (because the *measure* is the *multitude of the reward*, not the *deserts of the receiver*); punishment is not *withheld* on grounds of ingratitude (because its *measure* is its own *greatness*). And the chapter's contribution: the *evidence against ingratitude is itself unlimited* — the bounty, *spread out*, *documents* the *denial* of those who deny it. The denier denies in a world saturated with the very thing he denies. *Denial* is not *neutral*; it is *an act against documentation*.

The Shaykh closes the first discourse with the chain of attributes. *He is the most merciful of His creation and the mightiest; the most merciful of His servants and the most all-powerful; the most just in His ruling; the highest and yet the closest to His people; and the nearest one whom I keep close, banishing illusions*. The pairing of *most merciful* and *mightiest* — *most merciful* and *most all-powerful* — *most just* and *highest yet closest* — is the chapter's structural rule: the same God who is *most* in mercy is *most* in power; the same who is *highest* in transcendence is *nearest* in immanence. The two *extremes* are joined in *Him*, not divided between *two gods*. The Shaykh closes: *So worship Him and give thanks to Him — to Him you will return*. The opening discourse has placed creation in the *middle* and named *return* as its end.

The doctrine of *justice as the middle between two extremes* the Shaykh delivers is the school's reading of a teaching the Quran itself anchors and that the Commander of the Faithful gave its sharpest theological formulation. The Quranic anchor: *Allah does not burden a soul beyond its capacity* (verse 286 of the chapter on the cow, trans. Yusuf Ali, *The Holy Quran: Text, Translation and Commentary*, Beirut: Dar al-Arabia, 1968, p. 116). The theological formulation, in the canonical compilation: *Justice places things in their proper places, while generosity takes them out of their direction; justice is the policy of the masses, while generosity is the policy of the few* (*The Path of Eloquence*, Wisdom 437, trans. Reza, Tehran: World Organization for Islamic Services, 1980, p. 691). The Shaykh's *middle* is the *placement-in-proper-place* of that aphorism: not the extreme of *duty owed* (which would push obligation past *capacity*), nor the extreme of *no obligation* (which would leave the creature undirected), but the *middle* on which capacity and command meet.

## The dignity of minds: the chain of right

The Shaykh's second discourse opens with a transitional phrase the source uses to mark the move to a new register. *But, beyond that: it belongs to the dignity of minds that they be put to use through inquiry*. The doctrine of *inquiry* — that the mind is *dignified* by being *put to use*, not by being *spared the labor* — turns the *minds* of the boy and the Master toward the chain that follows.

The Shaykh names what *inquiry* yields. *One of the fruits of inquiry is what genuinely exists, and one of the marks of existence is its sweetness*. *Inquiry* yields *existence*, and *existence* has the *mark of sweetness*. The figure is the figure of the *freshwater spring*. *Every freshwater spring is a drink whose surface is plain but whose depths are veiled; it never tires of the thought that pursues it, and creation never reaches the end of its purpose*. The *zahir* is *plain*; the *batin* is *veiled*. But the *spring* — the *spring* of *existence* — *never tires* of being pursued, and *creation never reaches the end of its purpose*. The pursuit is *inexhaustible*; the *quenching* is *real*; the *depth* is *always still there*. This is the doctrine of *zahir-batin* applied to *being itself*: the *zahir* of the world is *plain*, and one can drink at it; the *batin* of the world *never empties*, and one can never finish drinking. The boy who in chapter two heard the *batin*-doctrine applied to the *Qur'an*, and in chapter three heard it applied to *parables* and to *dreams*, hears it now applied to *the freshwater spring* — that is, to *every drop of being one ever encounters*.

The Shaykh then walks the chain of *rights*. *Its proper features are its rights, and a right is a duty — and from that it is truly called to fulfill its obligation. Whoever withholds from a right what is due to it has acted unjustly*. The structure is *every right is a duty*; *every duty is owed*; *withholding the due is injustice*. Then the chain itself, posed as questions whose answers are the chain's links. *Is it not the right of thought to pay zakat to the hearts through good manners?* *Thought* is owed *zakat* — *purification* — and that purification is *paid to the heart through good manners*. *And the right of good manners is to seek knowledge*. *Good manners* are owed *knowledge*. *And the right of knowledge is to be acted upon*. *Knowledge* is owed *action*. *And the right of action is that it be performed in purity, in obedience to the First*. *Action* is owed *purity* and *obedience to the First*. *And the right of obedience is that it be perfected in both hardship and ease, in goodness and in trial — and only the patient receive it, and only the truly fortunate receive it*.

The chain links *thought → good manners → knowledge → action → obedience*, and the *terminal* of the chain is *perfected obedience in hardship and ease*, which *only the patient* and *only the truly fortunate* can *receive*. The Shaykh has put the boy on a *map* and shown him where he stands. The boy has come through *inquiry* — has paid *zakat* to his heart through *good manners* — has *sought knowledge* (the long catechism of chapters one through three) — has *acted upon it* (the five-share enactment of chapter three) — is now standing at the threshold of *obedience perfected in hardship and ease*. The Shaykh has not yet named the obedience. The naming will be the *seventh-day name*.

The chain of rights the Shaykh delivers in compressed form has its longer cousin in one of the most distinctive ethical documents of the school's inheritance: the *Treatise on Rights* attributed to the same Imam to whom *The Psalms of Islam* is ascribed — an enumeration of more than fifty graded rights, in which every faculty, every act, every relationship is treated as bearing both a *duty to* and a *due from* (*The Treatise on Rights*, appended to *The Psalms of Islam*, trans. Chittick, pp. 278–296). The longer catalogue opens with the *right of the self* and the *right of the tongue* and the *right of the eye* — the inward faculties — and ascends through the *right of one's teacher*, the *right of one's neighbor*, the *right of one's ruler*, to the *right of one's Imam*. The structure is the same as the Shaykh's: *every right is a duty*; *every duty is owed*; *withholding the due is injustice*. The Shaykh's five-link chain — *thought → good manners → knowledge → action → obedience* — is the longer doctrine in its compressed initiatic form, the form sufficient for the boy who is about to be named.

## What is your name? — the seven-day deferral

The Shaykh turns his face toward the boy. The framing of the ritual that follows is the framing of *guest-honor*. *O boy, I have honored you as an estranged friend, and I greet you as a visiting guest. So what is your name?* The Shaykh has *honored* the boy as a *stranger* and *greeted* him as a *guest*; the natural ritual move is the *naming-of-the-guest*. The boy gives his birth-name. *Ubayd Allah, son of Abd Allah*. The name carries the *Abd Allah* construction — *servant of Allah*, *Ubayd* a diminutive of *Abd*: *little servant of Allah, son of the servant of Allah*. The name announces the boy's standing as the inheritor of a line of *servanthood*.

The Shaykh receives the name and immediately frames its inadequacy. *That is your description, and your news has already been brought to us*. The Shaykh has heard the *report* of the boy in *gatherings of remembrance* — *your news has already been brought to us*; the *name* the boy has just given is, then, only *the description* by which the report has carried him. The Shaykh has heard *who the boy is* through the brethren; he is asking for *the boy's name in his own mouth*, not the *description* the chain has been using.

The boy reads the framing rightly and *withdraws* the birth-name. *I am free, son of Abd Allah*. He drops the *Ubayd* — the *servanthood* — and substitutes *free* (*hurr*). The substitution is doctrinal: the *servant of Allah* has, in the *batin*, become *free* — *freed* from the servitudes that *Abd* names, not from the *servanthood of Allah* (the patrilineal *son of Abd Allah* is kept), but from the *idol-servitudes* of the world. The Shaykh tests the substitution. *And who freed you from a master until you became free?* The Shaykh is not granting the substitution at the boy's word; he is asking for *the warrant*. The boy gives the doctrinal account. *This world possessed me, and I have handed it back to the One who called it*. *The world* was the *master*; the *handing-back of the world* — the *five shares of wealth* of chapter three — is the *manumission*. The *freedom* is real because the *previous master* has been *handed back*.

The Shaykh accepts the warrant but tightens the implication. *I have understood. If this one owns and that one is owned, is it permissible for you to claim otherwise?* The *owner* is *Allah*; the *owned* is *the world*. If the *world* is the *owned* and *Allah* the *owner*, is it *permissible* for the boy to claim *anything otherwise* — to claim *self-ownership*, to claim a *name of his own*? The boy admits the impossibility. *It is not permissible*. The Shaykh then asks again: *What, then, is your name?*

And — the chapter's first silence of the *naming*-arc — *the boy fell silent, puzzled by the answer*. He cannot name himself. *Ubayd Allah* is the *description* the report has been carrying; *free* is the doctrinally-accurate but *not-fully-warranted* withdrawal of the description; he has *no third name* to give. The Shaykh names the impasse with the sharpest line of the ritual. *O boy, how can a thing know that which has no name?* The line is doctrinal: *knowing* (a state of the *known*) requires *a name* (a *condition* of being *known*). If the boy has no *name*, then nothing can *know him* — neither he himself, nor the Shaykh, nor anyone who would carry him *forward* in the chain. *Nameless* means *unknown to the chain*.

The boy concedes. *I am newborn to you, and you nurture me*. The figure is decisive: the boy is *newborn* — born into the chain *now*, at this council, at this Shaykh's hand — and the Shaykh is the *nurturer*. The Shaykh accepts the figure and uses it to set the ritual term. *Then wait until the completion of seven days*. The boy asks the natural question. *Why has that been deferred until the completion of seven days?* The Shaykh: *For the dignity of the newborn*. The figure is now a *legal* figure. In the *zahir* of *fiqh* (the schools of jurisprudence), the *naming* of a newborn child is, by long tradition, performed on the *seventh day* (the *ʿaqīqa*, the *naming-day-with-sacrifice*); the Shaykh is applying that *zahir* figure to the *batin* *re-birth* of the boy into the chain. *The dignity of the newborn* requires *seven days* between *birth* and *name*.

The boy presses with the rigor of a child. *And if the newborn dies before the completion of the seven days?* The Shaykh: *Nothing harms him. He is named after that*. Even *post-mortem*, the *newborn* is *named* in *the chain* — the *naming* is not contingent on the *living-out* of the seven days. The *name* belongs to the *born*, not to the *lived*. The boy, having understood, asks: *Is this the name by which you have named me?* — that is, *is "newborn" itself the name*? The Shaykh replies with the chapter's most piercing line. *Would you be like an idol, even though you have been born?* The boy who has just *manumitted himself from the world* cannot be *named with a noun* (*newborn*, *free*) as if he were a *thing* — an *idol* — that could be *labelled* and *placed*. *Being born* requires *receiving a name from the chain*, not *self-asserting a category*. The boy receives the rebuke. *How can such a thing be said of it?* The Shaykh gives the chapter's doctrine of the *name*.

*The name belongs to you, and you belong to the name. So it does not appear except within your limit, and it travels with your duration*. Two propositions, knit. *The name belongs to you, and you belong to the name* — name and named are *mutually-binding*. *It does not appear except within your limit, and it travels with your duration* — the name has the *limit* (*ḥadd*) of the named and the *duration* (*mudda*) of the named. The *name* is *coextensive* with the *named*. This is the *da'wa* doctrine of *coextensivity of name and named* and the warrant for the *seven-day defer*: the name cannot be *given* before the *named* has been *fully born*; the *seven days* are the time the *born-one* takes to *establish his limit*. The Shaykh is not naming the boy now because the boy has *not yet finished being born*.

The doctrine the Shaykh delivers in his closing line — *the name belongs to you, and you belong to the name; it does not appear except within your limit, and it travels with your duration* — was given its philosophical elaboration two generations later by one of the school's great Persian systematizers. In the doctrine of the name and the named, the *name* is not an arbitrary label affixed to a thing; it is *coextensive* with the *limit* (the *boundary of what the thing is*) and the *duration* (the *temporal extent of what the thing is*) of the named. To name something *before* its limit is established is to mis-name; the name will not *hold* what has not yet been *bounded*. The seventh-day deferral the Shaykh imposes is therefore not a ritual interval but the *time the named takes to establish his limit* — the time the *inward birth* requires before its *inward name* can be given (paraphrased and summarized in Paul E. Walker, *Early Philosophical Shia Thought: The Neoplatonism of a Tenth-Century Persian Philosopher*, Cambridge: Cambridge University Press, 1993, pp. 105–112).

## The seventh day: bathing, the day of Sa'd, the implements of work

The boy rises and hurries to obey *without looking back, out of good manners*. He returns with his father to their lodging. The Master greets them. *What is the state of this brother concerning his need?* The Master answers in the chain-register: *It has been promised, the one awaited is thirsty, and the term is near*. *Promised*, *thirsty*, *near* — the *promise* is the seventh-day naming; the *awaited* is the boy; the *thirst* is the *yellowing* of body and the *silence* of awe; the *term* is the *seventh day*.

They remain until the seventh day. Then they seek the Shaykh's permission, and he grants it. The Shaykh orders the boy to *wash* and *put on his purest garments*. The ritual is paired: *physical-zahir* purification before the *naming*. Then the chapter records a detail unusual in its candor. *When the boy attended to that, his heart felt the need to relieve himself on that day — and this is what I commanded him to do, as a sign of the good that was to come after it*. The narrator (the elder Master in a rare first-person line) names the *embodied detail* as a *sign of the good*. The *zahir* of *bodily relief* is, in the *batin*, the *seal* that *the time has come*.

When the boy has *completed what was commanded*, the three go out. The boy goes out *saying, "The day of Sa'd, and the guardian of Sa'd," repeating it again and again*. *Sa'd* is the Arabic for *good fortune*, *felicity* — the *day of Sa'd* is the *day of felicity*, and the *guardian of Sa'd* is the *one who keeps that day*. *Nothing drove him to this saying except the abundance of his joy and the intensity of his longing to know what would bring him closer to the pleasure of Allah*. The boy's spontaneous *liturgy* — a private formula, not from the Qur'an or from any teacher — is the boy's *naming-of-the-day* before he himself has been *named*.

When they come to the Shaykh, they find that he *had taken up the implements of his work*. The *implements* are the *tools* of the *naming-and-transmission* — and the Shaykh has *taken them up* before the boy arrives. The *work* is *under way* before the *workmen* enter. The Shaykh greets them with peace, and they return the greeting.

## The highest transmission, veiled

The Shaykh then orders the boy *to come down to him*, and *brings him near*. What follows is the chapter's central moment and the one moment the source deliberately *veils*. The narrator's wording is one of the most extraordinary disclosure-and-non-disclosure passages in the *da'wa* literature. *He began to speak of matters upon which no fancy had ever descended, which no pen had ever traced, and which had never occurred to the heart of any human being — matters that are not to be mentioned in the assemblies of preachers, and that are not permitted in mosques. These books are among the greatest of His bounties, and are unveiled only to His own people*.

Five propositions are packed in. *No fancy had ever descended*; *no pen had ever traced*; *never occurred to the heart of any human being*; *not to be mentioned in the assemblies of preachers*; *not permitted in mosques*. The matters are, in short, the *innermost batin* — the *yolk* of the egg-parable of chapter two; the *rope of the rope of God* of chapter three; the *batin-of-batin* the Master halted at. The Shaykh, having brought the boy through the *outer rope* (chapter one), the *cosmology of will-command-and-seven* (chapter two), and the *ethics of body-and-wealth and the first direct speaking of the Imam* (chapter three), is now *delivering* the *inner-most matters* themselves.

The chapter does not record them. The narrator names them as *the greatest of God's bounties, unveiled only to His own people*, and *moves on*. The veil is the veil of the *taqiyya* (pious dissimulation) — but it is also the veil of *necessity*: what is unveiled to *His own people* cannot be recorded in a book that may fall into *other hands*. The boy receives the transmission; the listener does not. The source's discipline is to *honor the veil* — to name the veiling rather than violate it.

The discipline by which the chapter records the *fact* of the highest transmission and refuses to record its *content* is the discipline of the school in its most distinctive form. The British historian of the school's institutional life describes the gradient: the apparent doctrine is taught openly to the public; the next layer is opened to the initiated; the innermost layer is communicated only by the highest figures of the chain, and only to those whose preparation is complete (Farhad Daftary, *The Isma'ilis: Their History and Doctrines*, 2nd ed., Cambridge: Cambridge University Press, 2007, pp. 90–94). The American scholar of the school's exegetical literature has pressed the point further: the *boundary* between what may be uttered and what may not is itself the *pedagogy* by which the seeker learns where he stands in the chain. The veil is not a barrier appended to the teaching; it is *part of the teaching* (David Hollenberg, *Beyond the Quran: Early Isma'ili Exegesis and the Secrets of the Prophets*, Columbia: University of South Carolina Press, 2016, pp. 116–125).

When the Shaykh *had discharged his duty and brought him out of his bewilderments — the bewilderments that had led him astray — to the very end of guidance, he explained to him his doctrine and gave him his draught*. The boy is *out of his bewilderments*, *at the end of guidance*. The *doctrine has been explained*; the *draught has been given*. Then the chapter delivers a single sentence that — in a single string of Hajj-figures — names what has happened. *Then he and his father continued to attend the Shaykh in a particular school. He gave to him while he patiently kept on; he did not tire of seeking his honor, and did not turn away from the one whose answer carried such sweetness*. But just before that — between the *draught* and the *attendance* — the chapter delivers what reads as a *sealing*: *of his Lord, so that he removed his ihram by glorifying the sanctities of Allah, took hold of the rope, circumambulated the ancient House, spent his rites in remembrance, and performed Hajj by the great sign*.

The figure is total. The boy has *performed the Hajj* — not a *zahir* Hajj at Mecca (he is at the *greater Shaykh's* house, not in the Hijaz), but the *batin* Hajj that the *zahir* Hajj is *a sign of*. He has *removed his ihram* (the consecration that *enters* the rite) by *glorifying the sanctities of Allah*; he has *taken hold of the rope* (the rope of chapter three: the *hujja* and the *bāb* of the Imam); he has *circumambulated the ancient House* (the *batin* of the Kaʿba); he has *spent his rites in remembrance*; and he has *performed Hajj by the great sign*. The *great sign* (*al-āya al-kubrā*) is, in the *da'wa* reading, the *Imam* himself — the *batin* of every Hajj-figure is the *visitation* of the *house* by the *one* who *receives* the *one whose right is binding*. The chapter is saying, in the only register strong enough to say it: the boy has now *performed the inward Hajj* that the *outward Hajj* points to.

The reading of the apparent pilgrimage as an inward journey is one of the school's signature exegetical claims, and the French historian of the school's imagination devoted an entire study to its transposition: every stage of the apparent rite — the consecration, the circumambulation, the standing, the casting of the pebbles — is, in the inward, a stage of the soul's *visitation* of the *one whose right is binding*, with the *great sign* at the journey's end being the encounter with the Imam in his inward presence (Henry Corbin, *Cyclical Time and Isma'ili Gnosis*, London: Kegan Paul International, 1983, pp. 95–105). The chapter's *removed his consecrated state by glorifying the sanctities* and *performed the pilgrimage by the great sign* is therefore not metaphor decorating an account of inward instruction; it is the chapter saying, in the only liturgical idiom strong enough to carry the claim, that the pilgrimage has *actually been performed* — in the only register the school regards as fully *real*.

## Attendance: the root watered, the branch raised

After the transmission and its Hajj-sealing, the boy and his father *continue to attend the Shaykh in a particular school* — *a school* the source does not specify because the *school* is the *batin school*, not a *school in a city*. The Shaykh *gave to him while he patiently kept on*; *he did not tire of seeking his honor*; *he did not turn away from the one whose answer carried such sweetness*. The figure echoes the *spring*-figure of the Shaykh's opening discourse: the *spring* that *never tires of the thought that pursues it*. The boy is the *thought* that *pursues*; the Shaykh is the *spring*. The pursuit and the giving are *symmetrical* and *inexhaustible*.

*So they stayed in that state for a time, until he knew that the root had been watered and the branch raised*. The figure of the *root and the branch* echoes — in deliberate reversal — the chapter-three figure of the *sower* who *prepares the land, sows, waters*, but *cannot bring out the plant and the leaves*. The Master in chapter three had said: *the sower cannot bring out the plant*. The Shaykh in chapter four — by the *batin* transmission and the *attendance* — has *raised the branch*. What the Master *could not do alone* the *chain* has *done together*. The boy's *root has been watered* (by the *transmission*); his *branch has been raised* (by the *attendance*). He is now *grown* in the *batin*.

The figure of the *root watered* and the *branch raised* is, in the Quran, the parable of the good word: *Have you not considered how Allah presents a parable, a good word like a good tree — its root firmly fixed and its branches in the sky, yielding its fruit at all times by the permission of its Lord* (verses 24–25 of the chapter on Abraham, trans. Yusuf Ali, *The Holy Quran*, p. 627). The chapter is reading the apparent parable as the inward arc of *initiation*: the *root* is the *transmission*; the *branch* is the *attendance*; the *fruit at all times* is what the boy is being sent home to *yield* — the *worship by it and call others to it* that the Shaykh's third reason for parting will name in a moment. The Quranic *yielding at all times* is the *permanence-of-fruit* the chain commits its initiate to: not a single moment of *raising* but a *yielding* that does not stop.

## The farewell: the boy's praise, the Shaykh's blessing, the three reasons

The boy now speaks. He gives the Shaykh a praise that walks the cosmology-figures of chapters two and three back into the Shaykh's own person. *Praise be to Allah, who has made you welcome to this station, so that through you He revives religion and brings death to ignorance, and has made you a stronghold for the people and a refuge for us*. The Shaykh is the *one through whom Allah revives religion* — the *living* the chapter three brethren's *yellowed bodies* had been *waiting for*. He is *a stronghold for the people and a refuge for us*. Then the figures. *You are the gate of the people of heaven and the ascent of the people of the earth*. *Gate* downward (*bāb* — the *gate* of *the people of heaven* to the *people of the earth*); *ascent* upward (*the people of the earth*'s *way up*). The Shaykh is *both directions of the chain*. *From the light of your majesty the lights appeared, and from the cup of your palm the rivers flowed; may you remain so until you are pleased with the One who is pleased with you*. The *lights* — the *plural lights* of the *chain* — come from the *light* of the Shaykh; the *rivers* (an echo of chapter three's *freshwater spring* now multiplied into *rivers*) flow from the *cup of his palm*. The wish that closes: *may you remain so until you are pleased with the One who is pleased with you* — the *Shaykh's pleasure* is bound to the *Imam's pleasure*, which is bound to *Allah's pleasure*; the chain is *closed* on its own pleasure.

The Shaykh replies with the blessing the chapter has been preparing the boy for. *As for you, may Allah bless you with what He has given you, help you to preserve what has come to you, employ you in fulfilling His will, light your path with the light of His guidance, expand your breast with the keys of wisdom, ennoble your station with the method of the call (daʿwa), complete His favor upon you with the modes of response, and grant you such instruction in obedience to Him that you may have the best hope of His good pleasure*. Eight clauses in a single arc — *blessing*, *preservation*, *employment*, *illumination*, *expansion*, *ennoblement*, *completion*, *instruction*. The *method of the call* (*da'wa*) is named directly: the boy's *station* is to be *ennobled* with *the method of the call*. The boy who has been *received* is now to be *sent*. The *eighth clause* — *grant you such instruction in obedience to Him that you may have the best hope of His good pleasure* — closes the arc on the *obedience* of the chain-of-rights from the opening discourse.

The Shaykh then names the *three considerations* for *parting* now. *I bid you farewell, my son, and I give you permission to depart. As for the weight of our parting — we have resorted to three considerations*. The three: *the first is that we are traveling to another country*; *the second is your separation from your family for all this time, with the fear you had of going out without your father's permission, for there is great grief and great anger in him that will be lifted only by your return — even though I am vexed with your father, if you are kind to him*; *and the third is the duty of fulfilling the right of this grace that has reached you, for it is incumbent upon you to worship by it and to call others to it*.

The three are doctrinally arranged. (1) *We are traveling to another country* — the Shaykh and the *generous one* are *moving*; the chain *moves*; the boy who has been *received here* cannot *stay here* because the *here* is *not staying here*. (2) *Your family* — and *the father* in particular, who *will* (*if the boy is kind*) be *closer to what you want than anyone else* even though the Shaykh is *vexed* with the father for *what came before*. The father-doctrine of chapter one (*stand with him with outward agreement, keep this your secret*) is now extended: *be kind to him*; the father will be *moved closer*. (3) *The duty of fulfilling the right of this grace* — *to worship by it and to call others to it*. The boy who has *received the call* (*daʿwa*) is now to *call*. The third reason is the *theological* reason; it is the *real* reason; the other two are the *practical* warrants for the *third*.

The third reason — *to worship by it and to call others to it* — has, in the Quran, the formula the missionary apparatus of the school took as its founding directive: *Invite to the way of your Lord with wisdom and goodly exhortation, and argue with them in the way that is best* (verse 125 of the chapter on the bee, trans. Yusuf Ali, *The Holy Quran*, p. 678). The directive carries three structural commitments the school has read into it ever since: *wisdom* (the *call* is not coercion); *goodly exhortation* (the *call* moves by *gentleness*, not by force); *in the way that is best* (the *call* takes the *highest* of the available approaches, not the most expedient). The Shaykh has put the boy under that very rule, and the boy who in chapter three was the *receiver* of the call is, by the close of this chapter, the *caller* under instruction.

The boy answers in the *grief-register* that has run through the chapter from the moment of the first announcement. *Your parting is sorrow for me, and your loss is a deprivation for me — but your counsel is more rightly guided, your obedience more binding, your judgment more sound. So recommend to me what you see fit, and dismiss me when you will, and be kind to me by sending my father with me to my country. I will lean upon him, act through him, and entrust to him all my affairs*. He has not asked to *stay*; he has asked for *recommendations* and for *his father to be sent with him*. The boy who could not speak from awe in the council can now *ask for what he needs* — the Shaykh's earlier word (*you are in the hands of one who knows your need*) has come *full circle*: the boy has *learned what he needs*.

The Shaykh accepts the request. *I shall do that, and I enjoin upon you the fear of Allah who created you, the preservation of the trust for which you have struggled, and the safeguarding of your father, who raised you young and toiled long in your affairs — with his own person and with his wealth. Do not clothe him in the garment of contempt, do not board him on the ships of harm, do not omit his name, do not become distracted from his remembrance, and do not deceive him in your city — for there is a sting at the very outset*. Five negatives in a single chain: *do not clothe in contempt*; *do not board on harm*; *do not omit his name*; *do not become distracted from his remembrance*; *do not deceive him in your city*. The father is *not to be reduced* in any of the registers in which he can be *reduced* — clothing, vehicle, mention, remembrance, dealings. *There is a sting at the very outset* — the warning of *what will happen if these rules are broken at the start*.

The boy gives the closing prayer. *May Allah reward you, on behalf of the Master of grace, with every grateful one's thanks; and may He reward you for the length of your shepherding, when no hand can repay you. I will keep your commandment — do not cut off my share from the blessing of your supplication*. The phrase *do not cut off my share from the blessing of your supplication* asks the *Shaykh* to *keep praying for the boy* — to keep the *connection of the chain* open across the *physical separation*. The Shaykh has *named the boy*; the boy has been *born* into the chain; the chain holds across the parting.

*Then they stood up, shook hands, and embraced, and each said his farewell to his friend — unable to contain himself from the weight of the parting, able to speak only by gesture. Then they took their separate ways*. The chapter does not describe what the *gestures* said. They are *gestures of the chain*. They are not for the page.

## On the edge of the city: the six qualities, the interpretation key

The Master (the elder scholar who has brought the boy from chapter one through chapter three to chapter four) walks the boy back toward the boy's city. As they *draw near*, on the *edge of the city* where the boy's father lives, the scholar gives the boy the *parting instruction*. *O my son, I have understood the will of the Shaykh, and you have not. Guidance lies only in his words. This is your city, and we have come to the edge of it. So sit with us off the road, for I wish to remind you of some of my concerns and to recommend to you what you should do*.

When they sit, the scholar opens with the father-doctrine. *O my son, I am aware of your father and his enmity toward the people of this matter; and this, together with your going out with me and your absence from him without his permission or his trust. I wish to honor your father's view by concealing some of these affairs, so that you may return to him and he may be at ease again and remain among us*. The *concealment* — the *taqiyya* doctrine introduced in chapter one — is now extended to the *re-entry*. The boy will *return* to his father; *some affairs* will be *concealed*; the father will *be at ease* and *remain among us*. *Remain among us* is the chapter's quiet revelation: the father, who has been *opposed*, will (if the boy is *kind*) be *kept in the circle of the brethren*. The *enmity* will be *worked on* by the *concealment* and the *kindness*; the *outcome* the scholar hopes for is *the father within the circle*.

The scholar walks the boy through the *standing rule*. *He has been worn down by your absence, and his solitude has become frightening to him*. The father has *suffered* in the *absence*; the *solitude* has *become frightening*. *Know, my son, that Allah has opened to you the least of your aspirations even before you suppose yourself to be a full-grown man, until a stretch of time has passed. And Allah has been good to you, so be good to yourself, and honor the one Allah has honored you through*. The boy has been *given more than he expected at his age*; the *Allah's goodness* requires *the boy's goodness to himself* and *honor of the one Allah honored him through* — both the *Shaykh* and the *father*.

Then the *six qualities*. *I commend to you six qualities: trust in Allah when you are resolved; speak with a ruling when you are called upon; do not grow angry when you are wronged; do not weary if you are slandered; whoever responds to you with good, be patient with him; and devote yourself to the best of those upon whom you call — for Allah will help you and will open the way to you*. Six rules in a single string: *(1) trust in Allah when resolved*; *(2) speak with a ruling when called*; *(3) do not anger when wronged*; *(4) do not weary when slandered*; *(5) be patient with the one who responds with good*; *(6) devote yourself to the best of those upon whom you call*. The first four are *postures against opposition*; the fifth is a *posture toward the receptive*; the sixth is the *posture toward the chain* — *the best of those upon whom you call* are the *brethren*, and the *devotion* to them is the *condition of the way being opened*.

The boy answers in the *triple-thanks* register the chapter has been teaching him. *May Allah reward you with three goods: an imam whose argument you have fulfilled and whose trust you have advised; and a dead man whose life I treated with kindness — my heart is full and my wings are light with joy at the great things you have set forth. I do not know which of your words is more excellent: the outward, in which you arranged it in its sequence, or the inward, in which you expounded its meanings*. The boy reads the scholar's *six qualities* as a *zahir-batin* speech — the *outward* is the *arrangement-in-sequence*; the *inward* is the *expounded-meanings*. The boy has *internalized the rule*; he reads everything now in the *batin*-register.

The scholar gives the *closing teaching* — the *interpretation key* for *everything they have walked through together*. *Praise be to Allah, who has made His way easy for you and brought you near to it. As I bid you farewell and am drawn away from you, is there anything in which someone can still help you carry a portion of his burden?* The boy gives the *three needs*. *There are three needs: a need I have already fulfilled, a need I beg for, and a need I ask you to kindly fulfill*. The scholar answers each. The *fulfilled* need: *your life is in knowledge after death — may Allah bless you with a life in it, and grant you the best of what you intended*. The *prayed-for* need: *act upon what you know, and accept what He brings, and Allah will bring it about for you by His mercy*. The *asked-for* need: *it was as if you wished for an increase upon what you have heard*.

The boy: *there is nothing greater than your knowing what is within me — so do as you see fit*. The scholar gives the *interpretation key*. *As for my knowing what is within yourself: only Allah knows the unseen, but the believer sees by the light of Allah*. The doctrine of *the believer sees by the light of Allah* is the *da'wa*'s answer to the *unseen-knowledge* question: the *believer* does not *know the unseen*, but *sees by the light* — and what he *sees* is *enough*. *As for the increase, I will make it more luminous for you, and stop you upon the foundations on which you are working — so that you may look upon all those matters whose outward I have mentioned to you, and which you have treated as parable. Whatever in it is praiseworthy is like the truth and its people — the virtues of their virtuous, and its witnesses pointing to them. And whatever was a reprehensible parable, I spent it upon the enemies of the truth and its people — their elders and their young, and their children — and its evidence points to them. These are the foundations of the science that works by them: if she preserves them, she has no need of any others*.

The *interpretation key* is the *binary rule* of the *zahir-batin science*: *every praiseworthy parable* is *the truth and its people* (the *brethren*, the *chain*, the *generous one*); *every reprehensible parable* is *the enemies of the truth and its people* (in *all three generations* — *elders*, *young*, *children*). The rule is *symmetrical* and *exhaustive*: *every parable* falls *into one of the two categories*; the *evidence within the parable* points *to one or the other*. *These are the foundations of the science that works by them* — the *foundations*; *if she preserves them, she has no need of any others*. The *science* the scholar has been teaching the boy is, in its *foundations*, *two rules*: *praiseworthy = truth-and-its-people*, *reprehensible = enemies-of-the-truth-and-its-people*. *Everything else* is a *consequence* of these *two*. The scholar has handed the boy *the master key*.

The *binary rule* the scholar names is the school's most compressed hermeneutic. The German historian of the wider Shia world finds this binary at the operational core of how the early missionary apparatus read every text it encountered: the apparent narrative is held in place, its inward referent is sorted into one of two columns, and the chain of bearers is read as the *truth-and-its-people* against whose *enemies* the apparent parable can always be measured (Heinz Halm, *The Shia Tradition*, 2nd ed., trans. Watson and Hill, Edinburgh: Edinburgh University Press, 2004, pp. 169–175). The scholar is teaching the boy a *closed grammar*: every parable has two possible referents, and only two; the *evidence within the parable* determines which. The *master key* is, in this reading, the school's claim that *every text in creation is a sortable text* — and the *sorter* is the inward science the chain has just handed down.

The scholar closes: *I commend to you the fear of Allah, from whom you come, with whom you have done nothing, and the keeping of His covenant, and the fulfillment of the right He has imposed upon you and upon all His creation*. *Fear of Allah* — *from whom you come*; *with whom you have done nothing* (acknowledging *grace*, not *merit*). *Keeping of His covenant* — the *covenant* of chapter one, the *rope of God upon His earth*. *Fulfillment of the right* — the *chain of rights* of the Shaykh's second discourse, now *internalized* by the boy.

## The final parting; the return to the city; the father's anger

The scholar finishes his discourse, *informs the boy of his safety*, and *completes his nurture*. *O my son, take care of yourself. I have others besides you, and this is the parting between you and me*. The chapter does not allow the boy to *hold* the scholar with him; the scholar *has others*; the *parting* is *between you and me*. The chapter is *exact* about the *plural ministry* of the scholar — the boy is *one of many*, and the *parting* is *now*.

The boy's response is the chapter's most physical line. *The boy's gasp at the mention of parting was such that he almost killed himself — his spirit nearly took flight and his soul nearly left his body*. The figure echoes the *announcement-scene* at the chapter's opening (when the Master had announced the journey and the boy's *soul almost left his body* until *Allah took hold of him*). The pattern returns at the parting: *the soul almost leaves the body*. *Then he kissed the scholar's feet and wiped their dust upon his face; then he bade him farewell and left*. The *kiss of the feet* and the *dust upon the face* are *the bodily completion* of the *gestures-of-the-chain* the Shaykh and the Master had exchanged at the council. The boy is *now of the chain* in *body* as much as in *naming*.

The boy returns to his house — *his heart absent from him in fear, and unable to return any utterance of his grief over the parting from his elder father and the father who had been the cause of his grace*. The phrase the chapter quietly places: *his elder father*. The *elder father* is the *Shaykh*; the *father who had been the cause of his grace* is the *Master*. The boy returns *with two fathers absent* — neither of whom is the *biological* father who is about to walk into the scene. *Until his family and his neighbors became afraid for him. He spent his day and night agitated with sorrow and alarm*. The boy who has been *received* and *named* and *transmitted-to* and *blessed* and *taught the interpretation key* is now *afraid in the family house* — *agitated with sorrow and alarm* — because *he has been re-born into a chain whose other end is not in this house*.

*Then his father came to him, angry*. The chapter ends on the father's anger. The *biological father*, who *welcomed* the Master's report at the chapter's opening (and *marvelled* and said *make haste*), has — by the end of the chapter — become *angry*. The reasons are not given; the chapter does not name them. The *re-named son* has come home *yellowed*, *silent*, *frightened*, *spending his day and night agitated*; the father, who sent the boy off, *welcomes* him with *anger*. The chapter ends here. The next chapter — the *Salih chapter* — will pick up the confrontation, the *revelation of the boy's name*, and the *further faces of seeking*.

The chapter has done its work. The boy has been *brought to the door* he asked to be brought to at the close of chapter three. He has been *named* on the *seventh day* by the *Shaykh*. He has *received the highest transmission*. He has performed the *batin* Hajj. He has been *enjoined* with the *eight blessings*, the *six qualities*, and the *interpretation key*. He has been *parted from the chain* and *returned to the city*. And the *father's anger* at the chapter's close is the *seam* into the chapter that will *reveal* the *boy's name in the world* — the name *Salih* — and the *father's identity* as *al-Bakhtari*. *The naming has happened*; *the name in the world* will *now happen*. The chapter is the *re-birth*; the next chapter is the *re-entry*.
