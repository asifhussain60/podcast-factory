## Background

This is Chapter 6 of a standard graduate textbook on statistical learning. It is the chapter that names the methods modern regression actually uses when the predictor list is long — when ordinary least squares is too eager and the analyst needs a principled way to constrain flexibility. Section 6.1 walks **subset selection**: best subset (consider all two-to-the-p models; conceptually clean, computationally infeasible past around p equals thirty), forward stepwise (greedily add one predictor at a time, p-squared model fits), backward stepwise (greedily drop one predictor at a time, requires n larger than p). Then the chapter's catalog of selection criteria for choosing among the candidate models: Mallows's Cp (training error plus a complexity penalty), AIC (essentially the same for normal-error linear models), BIC (same shape, harder penalty — grows with the log of n), adjusted R-squared (the friendly heuristic), and cross-validation (the assumption-free direct estimate). Section 6.2 walks the **shrinkage** methods: ridge regression (adds lambda times sum of squared coefficients; shrinks every coefficient toward zero but never to zero), and the lasso (adds lambda times sum of absolute values; shrinks AND snaps coefficients to exactly zero, producing automatic variable selection). Lambda is chosen by cross-validation. Section 6.3 walks **dimension reduction**: principal components regression (build leading principal components of X, regress on those — unsupervised) and partial least squares (build components that aim at Y from the start — supervised, but the supervised advantage usually washes out against added variance). Section 6.4 names the high-dimensional setting (p ≥ n) where ordinary least squares fails outright and these constrained methods become essential. Section 6.5 is the R lab. Section 6.6 is exercises.

The full chapter is in scope for MAS-I; there are no exclusions and no prose seams are required. The R lab in §6.5 is referenced but not narrated — no code recitation.

For the MAS-I-prep listener, this is the chapter most directly aligned with the syllabus's "model selection" block. Ridge and the lasso appear by name on the MAS-I outline. The Lasso paper — Tibshirani 1996 — is worth flagging as the historical anchor, because Tibshirani is one of the four authors of the textbook the listener is reading.

## Central tensions to reach

There are three named tensions in this chapter, and the conversation must reach each one.

First. Too many predictors over-fit; too few under-fit. Model selection is the principled middle path. The choice of selection criterion — Cp, AIC, BIC, adjusted R-squared, cross-validation — is itself a choice, and BIC will steer you toward parsimony while Cp and AIC will steer you toward retention. The student should leave understanding that there is no single right answer, only a defensible posture toward simplicity.

Second. Ridge shrinks every coefficient toward zero but never to zero. The lasso produces exact zeros. Automatic variable selection is the difference between the L2 penalty (squared coefficient values) and the L1 penalty (absolute coefficient values), and it's why both methods still ship side by side. Ridge wins when many predictors contribute modestly; the lasso wins when a few predictors carry most of the signal. Cross-validation chooses lambda for both. The student should leave with the "shrink all the dials a little vs turn most of the dials all the way off" image firmly in mind.

Third. PCR and PLS are dimension-reduction routes — the same regression problem, attacked by first summarizing the predictors. The order matters: PCR summarizes X first, then regresses; PLS builds components that already aim at Y. The supervised advantage of PLS sounds compelling and usually washes out in practice. The student should leave understanding that all four methods — ridge, lasso, PCR, PLS — are doing the same job (controlling variance by constraining flexibility) through different mechanisms, and that on a typical dataset their test errors land in the same neighborhood (the lab's Hitters numbers all cluster near each other, with PLS landing at 151,995, comparable to the others).

## Permission to disagree

Yes, lightly. The textbook is candid that none of these methods universally dominates. Ridge vs lasso is genuinely an empirical question — the lasso wins on sparse problems, ridge wins on dense ones, and you don't know which you have until you check. PLS sounds like it should beat PCR (it's supervised; it uses the response) and in practice usually doesn't (the supervised reduction adds variance roughly equal to the bias it saves). A host may surface that BIC's preference for parsimony can over-shrink if your real problem has lots of small but real effects; a host may note that cross-validation isn't free (it costs k-times the compute and is noisy on small datasets). The textbook's candor is the model — name the trade-offs honestly, don't pick fights with the methods.

## Three-part focus

Focus 1. Why model selection matters; subset selection as the combinatorial baseline; the menu of selection criteria. Land the "too many predictors over-fit, too few under-fit, and we need a principled middle path" framing. Walk best subset selection as the conceptually clean two-to-the-p baseline that is computationally infeasible past around p equals thirty. Then forward stepwise (start empty, greedily add) and backward stepwise (start full, greedily drop) as the p-squared practical approximations. Then the criteria for choosing among candidate models: Mallows's Cp adds a complexity penalty proportional to the number of predictors, scaled by the noise variance — adjusts the training error upward by just enough to account for the optimism of having fit those predictors. AIC lands at essentially the same place for normal-error linear models. BIC has the same shape but the penalty grows with the log of n, so BIC consistently prefers smaller models than Cp or AIC. Adjusted R-squared is the friendly heuristic — ordinary R-squared with a correction that keeps it from rising automatically every time you add a predictor. And cross-validation is the assumption-free direct estimate the resampling chapter built — the preferred criterion when it's computationally feasible. Land the point that on the Hitters dataset, different criteria pick different but reasonable model sizes; cross-validation lands on a ten-variable model.

Focus 2. Shrinkage — ridge and the lasso. The chapter's central image: a tuning dial called lambda. Ridge regression adds lambda times the sum of squared coefficients to the residual sum of squares, and as lambda grows every coefficient shrinks continuously toward zero but none of them ever reach exactly zero. Ridge keeps all predictors in the model, just turning down their volume. The standard practice is to standardize the predictors first, because the squared penalty treats large-scale predictors more harshly than small-scale ones. Then the lasso — same setup, but the penalty is lambda times the sum of *absolute values* of the coefficients (the L1 penalty instead of L2). The absolute-value shape has sharp corners at zero, and the optimization tends to land on those corners — so as lambda grows, the lasso shrinks coefficients AND snaps them to exactly zero, one by one. The lasso does shrinkage and variable selection in a single step. The diamond-vs-sphere geometric argument may be named in one sentence — the corners do the work — but no visual description beyond that. The intuitive contrast is the whole chapter: ridge turns all the dials down a little; the lasso turns most of the dials all the way off. Ridge wins when many predictors contribute modestly; the lasso wins when a few predictors carry most of the signal. Cross-validation chooses lambda for both. The historical anchor: the lasso came from Tibshirani's 1996 paper — Tibshirani is one of the four authors of the textbook the listener is reading — and that paper is among the most-cited in statistics. Lineage matters; land it in one sentence.

Focus 3. Dimension reduction (PCR, PLS), the high-dimensional setting, and the closing seam. Pivot cleanly from "fit everything, shrink some" to "summarize the predictors first, then regress on the summaries." Principal components regression — build the leading principal components of the predictor matrix (the directions of greatest variance, found by an eigendecomposition; the mathematical machinery named, not derived) and regress on the first M of those. The hope: the directions in X with the most variance also carry the most information about Y. Sometimes that hope holds; sometimes not, because PCR builds the components without consulting Y. PLS is the supervised alternative — build components that aim at Y from the start, weighting each predictor by its correlation with the response. PLS sounds like it should beat PCR; in practice the textbook is candid that the supervised reduction adds variance roughly equal to the bias it saves, so the overall benefit is a wash. PLS is the chemometrics workhorse; for general regression, PCR and PLS perform comparably. The order matters — PCR summarizes X then regresses; PLS builds components that already aim at Y — but the practical winner is rarely clean. Then high dimensions: when p approaches or exceeds n, ordinary least squares fits the training data perfectly and predicts terribly. The methods of this chapter — forward stepwise, ridge, the lasso, PCR — work in high dimensions precisely because they constrain flexibility. On the Hitters lab, all four methods land in the same neighborhood; the PLS test MSE is 151,995, comparable to the others — name the concrete number once, then move on.

Landing. Close on the seam to the next walk. Name that the textbook now turns to methods that drop the linear assumption itself — polynomial regression, splines, local regression, generalized additive models. Do not recap what was discussed. Do not say "so today we covered". The strongest closing is a single line about what the next walk will be about.
