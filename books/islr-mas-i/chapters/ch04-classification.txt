This is Chapter 4, the chapter where the response variable becomes a category rather than a number, and the same picture of bias against variance extends into a new family of methods. The MAS-I scope is §4.1 through §4.4, excluding the LDA portion of §4.4, plus §4.6 on generalized linear models as a coda; §4.5 (the method-comparison section) and §4.7 (the R lab) sit outside that scope and are not narrated here.*

## The opening problem — Default, and what makes classification different

Ten thousand individuals. Two predictors — annual income and monthly credit-card balance. One outcome — whether the individual defaulted on the card. The textbook opens with the simulated Default data set and a single observation that organizes everything that follows: the people who defaulted tended to carry higher balances, and the overall default rate sits at about three percent.

The job of classification is to take new inputs and predict a category, not a number — the kind of medical condition behind a set of symptoms, whether an online transaction is fraudulent, which DNA mutations are disease-causing. The framework Chapter 2 set up — a true function the inputs carry about the response, plus noise — survives, but the response is now qualitative and the apparatus has to change to match.

## Why not linear regression — the failure mode that motivates everything

Linear regression breaks on classification, and the textbook is patient about saying why.

Suppose the response has three categories — stroke, drug overdose, epileptic seizure. To use linear regression, you would have to encode them as numbers, say one, two, three. That encoding silently insists on an ordering, and that the distance from stroke to overdose is the same as the distance from overdose to seizure. Neither is true. Any other arbitrary numbering would give a fundamentally different model. With more than two unordered categories there is no honest way to use linear regression at all.

For a two-category response, the situation is a little better. You can code default as zero and one, fit a line, predict "default" when the fitted value is above one-half. But the line will produce probability estimates outside the zero-to-one interval — negative probabilities at very low balances, probabilities greater than one at very high balances. Those numbers are not probabilities. The line is the wrong tool for a quantity that must live between zero and one.

That single failure — predicted probabilities escaping the unit interval — is the entire motivation for logistic regression. We need a model that builds the bounded-probability shape into itself.

## Logistic regression — the S-shaped curve that fixes everything

Logistic regression models the probability of the response category directly. For the Default data, it models the probability that an individual defaults given their balance. The model uses the **logistic function** — the S-shaped curve that squashes any real number into the open interval between zero and one. Feed in any linear combination of predictors, however negative or positive, and the output is a probability — bounded below by zero, bounded above by one, smoothly varying in between. The shape of the curve is the entire intuition. The formula is named; it is not derived on a walk.

Once the model is fit, for low balances the predicted default probability is close to zero, for very high balances it climbs close to one, and in between the probability rises slowly, then steeply through the middle, then levels off. The curve does what probabilities should do.

The textbook then names the **odds** — the ratio of the probability of default to the probability of no-default. The **log of the odds**, called the **logit**, is what the logistic regression model assumes is linear in the predictors. That is the structural assumption: the logit is linear; the probability is the S-shaped image of that linear quantity. The formula is mentioned by name and never recited symbol by symbol; the picture of the S-curve riding on a straight-line backbone is what carries.

Coefficients are estimated by **maximum likelihood** — pick the parameter values that make the observed pattern of defaults and non-defaults the most probable thing the model could have produced. Least squares from Chapter 3 is in fact a special case of maximum likelihood; here the more general version is required because the response is binary. The mathematical details sit outside the walking-listen frame.

The Default regression of default on balance returns a positive coefficient, a tiny p-value, and an enormous z-statistic. The z-statistic plays the same role here that the t-statistic played in Chapter 3 — how many standard errors the estimate sits from zero. Same hypothesis-test logic, same interpretation. Balance is unmistakably associated with the probability of default.

Logistic regression generalizes to multiple predictors without surprise — one intercept, one coefficient per predictor, each interpreted holding the others fixed. And exactly as in Chapter 3, coefficients can shift in size and sign when other predictors enter. The Default data: student status alone shows students more likely to default; student status with balance and income in the model shows students less likely to default at the same balance. Both true. Students carry higher balances on average, and balance is the real driver — the simple regression reads the balance signal through student status. The textbook calls this **confounding**. At any given balance the student is the safer bet; on average across the population the student is the riskier bet, because students sit at higher balances.

For three or more unordered categories, the textbook names **multinomial** logistic regression — the natural generalization that picks one baseline category and models the log-odds of every other category against it as linear in the predictors. Baseline choice does not change predictions; it changes which coefficient gets which interpretation. The variant called **softmax** coding treats every category symmetrically and yields identical fitted values.

## Reading the output — thresholds, confusion matrices, and what error rate hides

Once a logistic model is fit, the natural rule is to predict the positive class when the predicted probability is above one-half. That rule minimizes the total error rate. But total error rate hides what kind of errors you are making. The Default data is the illustration: only three-and-a-third percent of individuals actually default, so a classifier that predicts "no default" for every single person hits a three-and-a-third percent error rate. That trivial classifier is useless to a credit-card company. The error rate did not detect the uselessness.

The **confusion matrix** lays bare what the error rate hides. It crosses predicted class against true class — true positives, false positives, true negatives, false negatives. The diagonal cells are correct predictions; the off-diagonal cells are the two kinds of mistakes. **Sensitivity** — the fraction of true defaulters the classifier catches — and **specificity** — the fraction of true non-defaulters it correctly clears — are the two gauges that matter when classes are imbalanced. With the half-threshold on the Default data, sensitivity is low and specificity is near a hundred percent. Lower the threshold to one-fifth and the trade-off flips: more defaulters caught, more non-defaulters wrongly flagged, total error rate slightly higher, but the kind of error has shifted. Which threshold is right depends on what each error costs the business.

The **ROC curve** plots the two kinds of error against each other as the threshold sweeps from zero to one. The area under it, the **AUC**, summarizes classifier performance across all thresholds. AUC of one is perfect; AUC of one-half is no better than chance. The Default data hits an AUC around 0.95 with logistic regression — very good. ROC curves and AUC are the standard tools for comparing classifiers when the operating threshold has not been chosen.

Logistic regression is the algorithm whose spirit sits under the hood of most credit-scoring at industrial scale. FICO's modern engine is more elaborate, but logistic regression is the parent shape: a linear combination of inputs squashed through an S-curve into a probability that drives a decision.

## The generative route — Bayes' rule, and the seam at LDA

Logistic regression models the probability of the class given the predictors directly. The textbook now pivots to a different route: model how the predictors are distributed *within* each class, then use **Bayes' rule** to flip those class-conditional distributions into the probability of class given predictors. Stated in words — posterior is prior times likelihood, normalized. The prior is how common each class is in the population. The likelihood is how typical the observed predictor values are within each class. Multiply, divide by the sum across all classes, and you have the posterior — the probability the observation belongs to each class. The Bayes classifier picks the class with the largest posterior.

That single rule is the engine of every generative classifier in the chapter. The differences between methods are differences in how the within-class distribution of the predictors is assumed to look.

ISL builds up to QDA by first introducing LDA — linear discriminant analysis — but MAS-I tests QDA and Naive Bayes rather than LDA, so we'll skip the LDA derivation and arrive at QDA directly with the per-class covariance idea already on the table. What you would have heard in the LDA derivation is the assumption that the predictors are normally distributed inside each class and that every class shares the same spread structure. We skip past it and pick up where the assumption loosens.

## QDA — letting each class have its own shape

Quadratic discriminant analysis, **QDA**, assumes the predictors are normally distributed inside each class — but it allows each class its own spread structure rather than forcing all classes to share one. The picture: if you imagine the data for each class as a cloud, QDA lets the rich and the broke clouds tilt and stretch differently when predicting default. Linear discriminant analysis would have forced both clouds to share the same tilt and stretch.

The cost of that flexibility is parameters. With many predictors, allowing each class its own spread structure requires estimating a great many additional quantities — one set per class instead of one set total. With a small training set, the variance from estimating all those extra parameters can swamp the bias QDA was supposed to fix. With a large training set, the variance is cheap and the flexibility is free; QDA tends to dominate. The trade-off is, once again, bias against variance — the same trade-off Chapter 2 named. QDA is the right choice when the training set is very large or when the shared-spread assumption is clearly wrong; the simpler linear alternative is the right choice when the training set is small or the shared-spread assumption is roughly right.

The mathematical detail sits outside the walking-listen frame. What carries is the picture and the trade-off: each class gets its own cloud shape, and with enough data, that flexibility pays.

## Naive Bayes — the assumption that makes high dimensions tractable

The **Naive Bayes** classifier is named for its assumption, which is the only thing it really has to remember. Within each class, the predictors are treated as conditionally independent of each other. Stated in plain language: pretend the predictors do not interact inside any given class. That assumption is almost never true. It is made because it works.

Why does it work? Because the joint distribution of many predictors inside a class is genuinely hard to estimate from any realistic dataset. Assuming independence reduces it to a stack of one-dimensional problems, one per predictor. Each one-dimensional density inside each class can be estimated cheaply — a normal fit, a histogram, a smoothed-histogram kernel for quantitative predictors, simple proportions for qualitative ones. The high-dimensional problem dissolves.

The bias-variance argument is back in its purest form. Naive Bayes introduces bias — the independence assumption is wrong. But it reduces variance enormously — there is so much less to estimate. In settings where the number of predictors is large and the number of observations is modest, the variance reduction wins by a wide margin.

Naive Bayes is the original mass-deployment classifier of the internet era — the engine behind email spam filters from the late nineteen-nineties onward. Each word in an incoming message is a predictor; spam-versus-not-spam is the class. The independence assumption is obviously false. Spam filters trained on Naive Bayes worked anyway, and worked very well, because the variance reduction was decisive in that high-dimensional setting.

## The seam to §4.6 — generalized linear models, where logistic regression has siblings

The textbook follows the classification material with §4.5, a comparison of all the classification methods, and §4.7, an R lab — both outside the MAS-I scope and not narrated here. Section 4.6, on generalized linear models, is in scope as a coda because GLMs ARE exam-relevant and because logistic regression turns out to be one member of a family that includes the actuarial profession's most-used regression tool.

## GLMs — and Poisson regression as the actuary's home

A **generalized linear model**, or **GLM**, is the unifying family that contains linear regression, logistic regression, and Poisson regression as siblings. The pattern is simple to name: the response is assumed to come from some distribution in the exponential family — normal for linear regression, Bernoulli for logistic regression, Poisson for count data; a transformation called the **link function** is applied to the mean of that distribution; and the transformed mean is modeled as a linear combination of the predictors. Choose a different distribution and a different link, get a different GLM.

The **Poisson** distribution models counts — non-negative integers, zero included. The textbook's example is the Bikeshare data: the response is the number of bikers in a given hour, predicted from month, hour of day, weather, temperature, working-day indicator. Linear regression on this response produces nonsense — about ten percent of its fitted values are negative numbers, and a negative count of bikers is impossible. Worse, the variance of the count rises with the mean — between two AM and four AM during a December snowstorm there are very few bikers and very little variability; between seven AM and ten AM on a clear April morning there are many bikers and much more variability. Linear regression's constant-variance assumption is straightforwardly violated.

Poisson regression fixes both problems by construction. The Poisson distribution puts probability only on non-negative integers, so fitted means are non-negative. And it has the signature property: its variance equals its mean. That single equality — variance equals mean — is the Poisson signature, worth filing away. When you see count data whose variance scales with its mean, Poisson regression is the natural first reach.

For the actuarial student, this is the chapter's hook. **Claim-frequency modeling** — how many claims a policyholder files in a year — is count data: non-negative integers, zero common, variance scaling with mean. Poisson regression is the textbook approach. The coefficients are estimated by maximum likelihood, just as in logistic regression — same general approach, different likelihood matched to the different distributional assumption. A coefficient is interpreted as a multiplicative effect on the expected count: increasing the predictor by one unit multiplies the expected count by a fixed factor. That multiplicative interpretation is the practitioner's tool.

The textbook closes the section by naming a few other GLMs: Gamma regression for positive continuous data, negative-binomial regression for over-dispersed counts where variance exceeds mean (a common refinement of Poisson regression for real insurance data). All follow the same recipe — pick a distribution from the exponential family, pick a link function, fit by maximum likelihood, read the coefficients with the link in mind.

## What to leave with

Three pictures.

The logistic function is the S-shaped curve that squashes any real number into the unit interval and turns a linear combination of predictors into a probability. That picture is logistic regression in one sentence.

Bayes' rule — posterior is prior times likelihood, normalized — is the engine of every generative classifier. QDA lets each class have its own cloud shape and pays in variance for that flexibility; Naive Bayes pretends the predictors are independent within each class and wins enormous variance reduction in high dimensions for the bias it accepts.

GLMs are the family that contains linear regression, logistic regression, and Poisson regression as siblings — pick a distribution from the exponential family, pick a link function, fit by maximum likelihood. Poisson regression is the actuarial home: claim frequency is count data, count data's variance equals its mean under the Poisson assumption, and Poisson regression is the textbook tool. The chapter ends here for the MAS-I scope; the textbook turns next to resampling — cross-validation and the bootstrap.
