# Linear Regression, Honestly

*From* An Introduction to Statistical Learning *(2nd edition, Springer 2021) by Gareth James, Daniela Witten, Trevor Hastie, and Robert Tibshirani. This is Chapter 3 — the Advertising data set, the apparatus an analyst uses to fit and judge a linear regression, and the practitioner's diagnostic checklist. The MAS-I scope is §3.1 through §3.3 plus the in-scope §3.6 labs; §3.4, §3.5, and §3.7 sit outside that scope and are not narrated here.*

## The opening problem — Advertising, again

Two hundred markets. Three advertising channels — TV, radio, newspaper. One outcome — sales. The client does not control sales; the client controls budgets. The textbook opens with seven questions a working analyst would want answered: is there any relationship between spend and sales, how strong, which media matter and how much each, how accurate the predictions will be, whether the relationship is linear at all, and whether splitting budgets across media produces synergy.

The chapter's quiet claim is that linear regression — the oldest tool on the menu — answers each of those questions if you read its output carefully. The job of the chapter is to teach the careful reading.

## Simple linear regression — the line, named

The simplest case has one predictor and one response. The textbook writes the model as a response approximated by an intercept plus a slope times the predictor. Read plainly: there is some line, with some height where the predictor is zero and some tilt as the predictor moves, that captures the systematic part of how sales tracks TV budget. Every observation sits some vertical distance off that line; that distance is the residual.

The line is chosen by the **least squares criterion**. The intuition is one picture: for any candidate intercept and slope, every observation is some vertical distance above or below the candidate line. Square each distance — so positive and negative misses both count and big misses count disproportionately — and add them up. That total is the **residual sum of squares**. The least squares fit is the one pair of intercept and slope that minimizes that total. The formulas are named; they are never derived on a walk. The picture — the line that minimizes total squared vertical distance to the points — is the entire intuition for what regression does.

On the Advertising data, the simple regression of sales on TV gives an intercept of about seven and a slope of about 0.0475. With no TV advertising, sales sit around seven thousand units, and every additional thousand dollars on TV is associated with roughly forty-eight additional units sold. That is what one coefficient pair, on one predictor, tells you.

## Assessing the coefficients — the instrument panel begins

Once the line is fit, the next question is how much to trust the coefficient numbers. The textbook lays out a small instrument panel, and the analyst learns to read each gauge.

The **standard error** comes first. If you collected a new sample of two hundred markets, the slope you estimate would not be exactly the same. The standard error of the slope is the typical amount by which a single estimate misses the slope you would get if you could average over many samples. It shrinks when the predictor values are more spread out — you have more leverage to pin down a slope when the inputs cover a wider range — and it shrinks as your sample grows.

The **confidence interval** turns the standard error into a range. A ninety-five percent interval for the slope is the range, computed from your sample, with the property that if you could repeat the whole exercise many times, ninety-five percent of those intervals would contain the true slope. For TV on sales, that interval runs roughly forty-two to fifty-three additional units per additional thousand dollars of TV spend. The interval, not just the point estimate, is the honest summary.

The **t-statistic** and the **p-value** are tied together. The t-statistic asks how many standard errors the estimated slope is away from zero; if that distance is large, explaining the estimate as random sampling noise becomes implausible. The p-value turns the distance into a probability — assuming the slope is truly zero, how likely is it to see a t-statistic at least this big by chance. Small p-values mean unlikely under the no-relationship story, so the story gets rejected. Customary cutoffs are five percent and one percent. For the TV slope, the t-statistic is around 17.7 and the p-value is essentially zero — the evidence that TV spend is associated with sales is overwhelming.

The hypothesis phrased this way — the **null hypothesis** that the slope is zero versus the **alternative** that it is not — is the workhorse for every coefficient on every predictor. Does this predictor actually do anything? The t-statistic and the p-value answer.

## Assessing the model — RSE and R-squared

Two gauges judge the model as a whole. The **residual standard error**, or RSE, is in the units of the response. Even if you knew the true coefficients, the response carries randomness the predictor cannot explain — the irreducible noise Chapter 2 named. The RSE estimates its typical size; for TV on sales, RSE is about 3.26 thousand units. Sales average fourteen thousand, so a typical miss of 3.26 is about twenty-three percent.

**R-squared** is the same quality on a unitless scale: the fraction of variance in the response explained by the predictors, between zero and one. For TV on sales, R-squared is about 0.61. In simple regression, R-squared equals the squared sample correlation — book-keeping that does not carry over once we add more predictors. What counts as a good R-squared depends on the field; in physics anything below 0.99 might mean a broken experiment, while in marketing or biology R-squared under 0.1 can be the realistic ceiling.

## Multiple linear regression — and why the F-statistic matters

The Advertising data has three predictors. Fitting three separate simple regressions is unsatisfactory: each fit ignores the others, and if the budgets are correlated across markets, the separate fits mislead.

The better approach is **multiple linear regression** — one model, one intercept, a separate slope for each predictor. The interpretation of each slope shifts meaningfully. The slope on radio is no longer "how does sales move as radio moves"; it is "how does sales move as radio moves, holding TV and newspaper fixed." That phrase — holding the others fixed — is the whole interpretive shift. The chapter does not write the multiple-regression formulas because their cleanest form is matrix algebra, outside the walking-listen frame. Software computes the estimates; what we learn is how to read them.

The Advertising regression on all three media gives the surprise. In the simple regressions, all three media looked associated with sales. In the multiple regression, TV and radio hold up, but the newspaper coefficient is essentially zero, p-value around 0.86. Newspaper was a *surrogate* for radio: markets that spent on newspaper also tended to spend on radio, and the simple regression on newspaper alone was reading the radio signal through it. The textbook's absurd anchor is shark attacks and ice cream sales: both rise with summer temperature, so they correlate strongly, but neither causes the other. Coefficients shifting in size, sign, and significance as other predictors enter the model is the first practitioner skill the chapter builds.

With more than one predictor, the first question is no longer "is this one slope zero" — it is "is any slope nonzero". The **F-statistic** answers. It compares the variance explained by all predictors together against the variance still unexplained; if no predictor matters, F sits near one, and if at least one is doing useful work, F climbs above one. For the full Advertising regression, F is around five hundred and seventy and its p-value essentially zero. F is what you read first; individual t-statistics tell you which predictors are responsible.

Why not skip F and read the t-statistics? Because of multiple testing. With a hundred predictors that truly do not matter, about five will clear a five-percent p-value by chance alone. The F-statistic adjusts for the number of predictors and does not have that problem. When predictors are many, F is the gate.

## Variable selection, model fit, prediction — three practitioner notes

**Variable selection.** Which subset of predictors do you want? Trying every possible subset is infeasible — with thirty predictors, more than a billion models. Three classical approximations: *forward selection* starts empty and adds the predictor that most lowers the residual sum of squares, then the next; *backward selection* starts full and drops the predictor with the largest p-value; *mixed selection* combines them. Backward only works when there are more observations than predictors; forward always works. Chapter 6 takes this topic seriously with subset selection, ridge, and the Lasso.

**Model fit with more predictors.** R-squared always rises a little when a predictor is added, even a useless one. So a small increase in R-squared — like the tiny bump from adding newspaper to a TV-and-radio model — tells you the predictor is not really helping. The RSE, which divides by degrees of freedom, can actually rise when a useless predictor is added.

**Prediction uncertainty has three sources.** The coefficients are estimates, the linear shape is an approximation, and the response itself is noisy. A **confidence interval** covers uncertainty around the average response at a given input. A **prediction interval** covers the average plus the noise — uncertainty around a single observation. Prediction intervals are always wider; both are reported by any standard package.

## Section §3.3 — extensions and the eight failure modes

Section 3.3 turns to the adjustments analysts need, then to the diagnostic checklist.

**Qualitative predictors.** Some predictors are categories, not numbers. To put a category into a regression, create **dummy variables** — indicators, or in the machine-learning community, *one-hot encoding*. For a two-level category, one dummy is enough. For a three-level category like region, two dummies, leaving one level as the **baseline** that gets no dummy. The choice of baseline is arbitrary and does not affect predictions, but it does change which coefficient gets which interpretation. To test whether the category as a whole matters, use an F-test on the dummies jointly.

**Interaction terms.** Plain multiple regression assumes the effects of predictors add. That is convenient and often wrong. In the Advertising data, a residual plot of the TV-plus-radio model shows it overpredicts when one medium dominates and underpredicts when budgets are split — splitting multiplies effectiveness in a way neither medium does alone. In marketing this is a synergy effect; in statistics it is an **interaction effect**. You capture it by adding the product of TV and radio as a third predictor. Doing so raises R-squared from about 0.897 to about 0.968. The **hierarchical principle**: if you include an interaction, include the main effects too, even if their individual p-values look unimportant.

**Non-linear transformations.** If a scatterplot shows curvature, the simplest fix is to add a squared version of the predictor. The model is still linear in the coefficients, so standard software fits it; the squared term lets the relationship curve. The Auto data set's mpg-against-horsepower regression lifts R-squared from about 0.61 to about 0.69 when the squared term is added. Going further — cubed, fourth, fifth — produces fits that wiggle without reflecting anything real. Chapter 7's splines and GAMs are the cleaner answer.

### The eight failure modes — every regression practitioner's checklist

The chapter closes with six numbered failure modes plus two qualitative-predictor pitfalls — eight things to watch for, each with a diagnostic plot or statistic.

**One. Non-linearity.** If the truth bends and your model is straight, every conclusion is suspect. The diagnostic is the **residual plot** — residuals against fitted values. Clean random scatter means the linear assumption is holding; a U-shape, arch, or any obvious curve means it is not. Try a log or square-root transformation on the predictors, or move to a non-linear method.

**Two. Correlated errors.** Linear regression assumes the error terms are independent. If they are not — time-series data, observations from the same family — the standard errors are too small, the confidence intervals too narrow, the p-values too generous. For time series, plot residuals against time; positive correlation shows up as *tracking* — consecutive residuals drifting together. Good experimental design is the cheapest cure.

**Three. Heteroscedasticity** — non-constant variance of the errors. Regression assumes the noise spread is the same everywhere; often it is not. A household earning thirty thousand and one earning three hundred thousand do not have the same spread in their spending. On a residual plot, heteroscedasticity is a *funnel shape* — residuals fanning out as the fitted values grow. The common fix is to transform the response with a log or square root.

**Four. Outliers** — observations whose response value is far from the model's prediction. Do not delete reflexively; the outlier may signal a data error or a missing predictor. The diagnostic is the **studentized residual** — the raw residual divided by its estimated standard error. Values above three in absolute value are flagged. A single outlier can move RSE and R-squared meaningfully even when it barely moves the line.

**Five. High-leverage points** — observations whose *predictor* values are unusual. A high-leverage point has unusual pull on the fitted line. In simple regression you spot them by eye; in multiple regression they hide, because an observation can sit inside the normal range of every individual predictor and still be unusual jointly. The diagnostic is the **leverage statistic**. The 2008 financial crisis is the anchor outside the textbook: default correlations across mortgage portfolios that sat near zero through normal years rose toward one in the crisis, and models calibrated on the pre-crisis regime saw those new observations as enormous-leverage outliers that redrew the lines retroactively.

**Six. Collinearity** — two predictors strongly related to each other. The regression cannot tell which is doing the work. Coefficient standard errors balloon, t-statistics shrink, p-values rise, and you may fail to detect a real effect because two predictors are sharing credit. The Credit data set's *limit* and *rating* are highly collinear; in a model with both, the standard error on *limit* increases twelvefold and its p-value rises to 0.70. Pairwise correlations catch simple cases. **Multicollinearity** — collinearity among three or more predictors that need not show up as any single high pairwise correlation — is harder to spot. The diagnostic is the **variance inflation factor**, or VIF; values above five or ten flag worrying multicollinearity. The practical fixes: drop one of the offending predictors, or combine them into a single composite.

**Seven and eight: the qualitative-predictor pitfalls.** Two traps recur. The *baseline-coding pitfall*: the choice of baseline changes coefficient interpretations even though predictions are identical, and reading coefficients without knowing which level is baseline produces nonsense. Always check the baseline before interpreting. The *no-interaction-by-default pitfall*: a categorical and a quantitative predictor in the same regression, without an interaction, force the *same slope* on the quantitative predictor across every category. If you expect the slope might differ by category, you must add the interaction explicitly. Forgetting the interaction is silent — the model fits, the output looks normal, the conclusion is wrong.

## What to leave with

The single picture: the least squares line is the line that minimizes the total squared vertical distance from the points to the line. That picture is the entire intuition.

The instrument panel: every coefficient comes with a standard error, a confidence interval, a t-statistic, and a p-value. RSE and R-squared judge the model as a whole; the F-statistic asks whether any predictor is useful and is the right first question with many predictors.

The practitioner's checklist: eight ways regression fails — non-linearity, correlated errors, heteroscedasticity, outliers, high-leverage points, multicollinearity, and the two qualitative-predictor pitfalls. The residual plot is the first thing to look at; the studentized residual catches outliers; the leverage statistic catches high-leverage observations; the VIF catches multicollinearity. The chapter ends here for the MAS-I scope; the textbook turns next to classification.
