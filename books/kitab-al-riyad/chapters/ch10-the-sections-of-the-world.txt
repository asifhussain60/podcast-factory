# The Sections of the World

*This chapter covers all seven sub-chapters of* Chapter Seven *of* this book: *the author's adjudication of the architectural question that follows directly from* Chapter Six*. If* prime matter *(prime matter) and form together constitute the body, if motion and stillness are the modes of the moved thing with respect to its mover, then what are* the sections of the world *itself? The author of* the book *The Correction* *had offered a clean architectural picture: the whole world is composed of four jewels, four structures, and four classes of beings, three of them without humankind, the fourth being humankind. Within this picture, the horse pairs with humankind in essence, the way gold pairs with silver; humankind itself has no peer among the world's parts, since humankind is the* counterpart of the whole world, *the small world, the microcosm, that gathers all the world's parts into one. The author of* the book *The Defense* *had refused the four-jewels-four-classes division and offered in its place a pairing-by-acceptance scheme: the dense lower world as pair of the subtle upper world; the horse as the human's pair because the horse accepts teaching from humankind more than other animals do.* Hamid al-Din Ahmad the author, *the author of* this book *and the great mediator between the two earlier Ismaili schools, works the seven sub-chapters as the same triangulating adjudication he gave* Chapters Four*,* Five*, and* Six. *The settlement: the world has many more sections than four; the generated beings divide cleanly into three genera (metal, plant, animal) with the human as a fourth-by-distinction within the animal genus, distinguished by the speaking soul; humankind has not* no peer *and not* one peer; *humankind has* many pairs *by occasion (angels for the virtuous, lions for the brave, wolves for the cruel, crows for the treacherous, tigers for the deceitful, monkeys for the adulterous); the strict pair of humankind is the angels, and what we mean by* humankind *here are the prophets, guardians, imams, and those who follow in their footsteps, not those merely in the* form *of humankind whose souls are the souls of beasts; the* bestial soul *and* speaking soul *that Bandhaqlis (Empedocles) named are best read not as horse-and-human but as the receivers and the teachers, namely the prophets, guardians, and imams kindling judgment in those in the form of humankind who accept education; the architectural map of the microcosm is closer to* the book *The Correction**'s likening (body to bodies, growth to plants, sense to animals, speaking soul as the unique fourth) than to* the book *The Defense**'s; but the human is the counterpart not only of* this *world, since the human is the counterpart of* all *the worlds, with parallels at every joint of the higher world too; the human is preferred over the world by a noble essence that is not of this world, yet stands in a* between*-state like the state of the boy between his two parents, matter from below, image from above, the* limits *of the* call *the midwife by whose hand the gift of the higher world is conveyed.*

## Where this chapter picks up

*Chapter Six* of this book settled the architecture of the natural-world end of the chain: *prime matter* and form as *inseparable correlates, actor and acted-upon, the first emanation*; motion and stillness as *opposites that do not meet in the same place at the same time*; the soul as one non-bodily reality called the soul and the spirit by aspect; above the First Effect, no motion or rest of any kind; the Creative IS the creativity, in the name of monotheism.

*Chapter Seven* turns to the *world that the architecture makes*. If the body divides into nine sections by the nine accidents, what are *the sections of the world*, the world being the totality of the natural composite, from the encompassing ocean down through the throne, the planets, the four elements, and the born? And: where does the human being sit among those sections? Is the human one part among others, paired with the horse (as the book *The Correction* claims)? Is the human the *counterpart* of the whole world, with no peer (as the book *The Correction* also claims)? Is the human the *fruit* of the higher world only? Or is the human a microcosm that is also a *between*-state, a part of all the worlds at once?

The question is not idle. Where the human is placed among the sections of the world determines whether the doctrine of *walaya*, the doctrine of the limits, the doctrine of the inner interpretation, and the doctrine of the *Ma'ad* can stand the way the call tradition needs them to stand. Seven sub-chapters. The first three settle the vocabulary of pairing and place the human among the generated beings. Sub-chapter four turns to Bandhaqlis's distinction of *bestial soul* and *speaking soul* (Empedocles by name, the Greek source the *falsafa* tradition reached for on the souls), and gives the call reinterpretation. Sub-chapter five settles the architectural map of the microcosm in its disputed form. Sub-chapter six fills in the map at full breadth, with the creative-emanative world impressed upon the human at every joint, pointing the reader to *the author's other treatise* (*The Repose of the Intellect*, called in this chapter by its sub-title *the Peace of Mind*). Sub-chapter seven seals the doctrine: the human is preferred over the world by an essence that is not of this world, yet stands in a *between*-state, matter from below and image from above, the *the limits of the call* the midwife of the conveyance.

## Sub-chapter one: the four jewels, the four classes, and what the world actually is

The author of the book *The Correction* opens *Chapter Seven*. *The whole world is composed of four jewels, four structures, and four classes of beings — three of them without humankind, the fourth being humankind.* Four jewels. Four structures. Four classes: three of generated beings, plus humankind as the fourth.

*The book *The Correction** explains the doctrine of *pairing* the scheme requires. *The knotted and the dissolved are all one essence.* (Solids and liquids share one essential category: they are both bodies.) *The pair of the horse and its form is what belongs to its essence — just as the pair of gold and silver, the pair of ruby and emerald.* The horse pairs with its *form* (the equine type) because pairings are of essences. *And its shape is that of the animals: the elephant, the falcon, the lion, and so on. Because they are all animals, it is not permissible to say that the horse is the pair and form of humankind, since humankind is alone in possessing the rational soul and has no peer among the parts of the world and its generated beings.* The horse's pair is *within* the animal class. Humankind has *no peer* because humankind alone possesses the *rational soul*. *Rather, humankind is the counterpart of the world, because it gathers in itself all the jewels of the world. For this reason it is called the small world — the microcosm.*

*The book *The Defense** enters with a refinement. *The state of pairing is not only the state of sexual union; the state of pairing is also the state of acceptance.* Pairing is also *acceptance*: receivership. By this broader category, *this dense lower world is the pair of the subtle upper world, and if you look between them you will find more between them than between the horse and the human.* And the book *The Defense* defends the book *The Correction*'s horse-and-human pairing. *He made the horse the human's pair because it accepts teaching from him more than the other animals accept it.*

The author enters with the verdict the chapter has earned. *The investigators of these matters did not rely on sound research. The author of* the book *The Correction* *divided the world into four jewels, four structures, and four classes including humankind — and that is impossible, because the divisions of the world are more numerous than what we have mentioned. "World" is a name that refers to many things, from the center to the encompassing ocean — the throne, the planets, fire, air, water, earth, and the born.*

The world is not a four-fold thing. It runs from the *center* (the inert mineral core) to the *encompassing ocean* (the outermost limit, in the *falsafa* image of the cosmos). Between the two: *the throne* (al-'Arsh, also a name for the First Intellect in *Chapter Two*); *the planets* (the seven heavens); *fire, air, water, earth* (the four elements); *and the born* (the generated beings).

*It is clear from his words that he means the generated beings of the world, not the world and its births themselves, which are three genera with no fourth, each of them having many species under it.*

*The first of these is metal — the nearest of the four arches: everything that exists in a specific place in the earth without growing in its parts.* The mineral genus (gold, silver, copper, gems, salts): *what exists in the earth without growing*. *The second is plant — all that grows from the earth, gathering together the bush and the tree.* The vegetative genus — what *grows*. *The third is animal — all that moves of those that have species, gathering together what flies and what does not.* The sensitive genus — what *moves and senses*. *The generated beings of the world do not exceed this; and the human, mentioned as the fourth, is of the genus of animal yet distinguished by a kind of consciousness.* The three genera are exhaustive. The human is *of the animal genus*, distinguished by *the speaking soul that comes to rest in the human*. A fourth-by-distinction, not a fourth-by-genus.

> *And from the natural world comes the first perfection of the human being, and from the higher world comes the second; the natural world prepares the body for the descent of the three souls, the higher world consummates the spiritual image by the conveyance of the gift through the limits.* — the author, *the author's other treatise* (the chain of receivership at the natural-world end)

The picture *the author's other treatise* gives, and *Chapter Seven* settles in its first sub-chapter, is the picture the rest of the chapter will work in: three genera at the natural-world end (mineral, vegetable, animal); the human as a fourth-by-distinction within the animal genus; the throne, planets, and elements as the architecture above the generated beings; the encompassing ocean as the outermost limit.

## Sub-chapter two: humankind has many pairs

Sub-chapter two presses the consequence of the book *The Correction*'s no-peer claim. *That humankind has no peer and that the horse has no pair — this is impossible from two angles.*

*The first: humankind is a type within the animal genus, and as a type it is a pair to the other types within its genus, since all types are forms of one another in the chapter on quality.* Sub-chapter one settled that the human is *of* the animal genus. If the human is a *type* within a genus, then by the book *The Correction*'s own classification the human pairs with the other types in the genus.

*The second: humankind, since it is similar in its structure, morals, actions, conditions, motions, and natures to all that the universe of existence presents — as he himself mentioned, and as is the very reason it is called the small world — must be a pair to all things, in accordance with the occasion and the case at hand.* The microcosm doctrine itself generates the refutation. To be the *small world* is to be *like* every part of the world; and to be like something is to *pair* with it. The microcosm is *not* what has no peer; the microcosm is what has *all peers*, by occasion.

And the human being's pair shifts *by what the human being is like*. *If it is good, virtuous, wise, religious, knowing, and pure, it is married to the angels by the occasion between them. If it is courageous, brave, strong, and a helper, its pair is the lions. If it is unjust, cruel, and murderous, then its pair is the wolves and the cunning foxes. If it is treacherous and thievish, then its pair is the crows and the hooks. If it is deceitful, cunning, and overpowering, then its pair is the tiger. If he is adulterous, immoral, given to amusement and play, then his pair is the monkeys.*

*And on this pattern, whatever a person is content to resemble — whether by his actions, his morals, his customs, or in any part of them — that becomes his pair.* The principle settled. *Pairing tracks resemblance*. The human writes his own pairing by content of choice. *If all this is established, then what he said is not borne out. Humankind has many forms and many pairs from many sides.*

The doctrinal payload. The human is not the *unmatched part*; the human is the *plastic part*, the part whose resemblance to other parts is *chosen* rather than given. That is what the speaking soul does: it gives the human the capacity to resemble, and therefore to *pair*, at will. The pair is what the part has decided to become.

> *Your remedy is within you, but you do not perceive it. Your illness is from yourself, but you do not see it. You suppose yourself a small body, yet in you is folded the greater world. You are the manifest book, by whose letters what is hidden becomes apparent.* — traditionally attributed to Imam the Commander of the Faithful (peace be upon him), collected in *Diwan al-the Commander of the Faithful*

The Commander of the Faithful names the same structural fact. *You suppose yourself a small body, yet in you is folded the greater world.* The microcosm doctrine. *You are the manifest book, by whose letters what is hidden becomes apparent.* The human is the part of the world by whose *content*, the morals and actions written into the self, the hidden architecture of the world becomes legible. The pairing-by-resemblance is what *writes the letters of the book*. And *the remedy is within you*: the choice of which pair to write into the self is the human's own.

The same principle has its Prophetic anchor at the level of the most concrete choice the human makes: the company he keeps. Imam the canonical hadith compiler preserves the hadith on the authority of Abu Musa al-Ash'ari:

> *The example of a good companion and a bad one is like that of a perfume-seller and a blacksmith. As for the perfume-seller, either he will give you some perfume, or you will buy from him, or you will perceive a pleasant smell from him. As for the blacksmith, either he will burn your clothes or you will perceive a foul smell from him.* — (Sahih the canonical hadith compiler, Book 73, Hadith 5534; narrated by Abu Musa al-Ash'ari)

The Prophet's image runs the same architecture the author has just settled. The perfume-seller and the blacksmith are the two pair-types; *sitting with* one or the other writes the resemblance into the self. The hadith does not say *learn from* the perfume-seller; it says the pleasant smell reaches you whether you buy or not. The pairing happens *by occasion*: by the company one is content to stand near. Sub-chapter two's doctrine is the architectural reading of what the Prophet states at the level of the everyday choice of companion: the human becomes what he keeps near.

## Sub-chapter three: the angels as the pair of those who follow the prophets and imams

Sub-chapter three turns to the book *The Defense*'s gloss that the horse-and-human pairing rests on *acceptance*. The author: *if it is on the basis of acceptance and education that the horse becomes its pair, then the horse has no priority over the other animals, for among the animals there are those that accept teaching and discipline other than the mare.* The dog accepts teaching; the falcon accepts teaching; the elephant accepts teaching. The horse has no monopoly on acceptance. On the book *The Defense*'s footing, the human's pair *multiplies again*: many pairs, not one.

And the author lands the positive doctrine. *In truth, the pair of humankind can only be the angels — and what we mean by "humankind" here are those of all virtues: the prophets, the guardians, and the imams (prayers of God be upon them) and those who follow in their footsteps, uphold their obedience, and worship Allah with both worships.*

The strict-sense *pair of humankind* is the *angels*. But the strict sense requires a strict definition of *humankind*: the prophets, the guardians (*awsiya'*, *awliya*), the imams of the Ahl al-Bayt (peace be upon them all), *and those who follow in their footsteps, uphold their obedience, and worship Allah with both worships*: those who keep the *the apparent* (the outer *sacred law*) and the *the hidden* (the inner the inner interpretation).

*Not those people who are merely in the form of humankind whose souls are the souls of beasts and predators — as we have said and shown — like monkeys, donkeys, foxes, lions, and wolves.* Those who look human but have taken on the moral state of beasts are *not* the *humankind* whose pair is the angels.

*As for those who walk in the traces of the Imams (may Allah's prayers be upon them) — their diligence in obedience through the two acts of worship, and their adherence to the command and prohibition, distinguishes them from the beasts even as they resemble them in form. So they will be saved in their entirety, and none will be lost from them, except as the verse says, "and he who takes them as guardian from among you is one of them."* The verse is *al-Ma'ida* on *walaya*: *he who takes Allah and His Messenger and the believers as guardians, indeed the party of Allah, they are the victorious* (Q 5:55–56 in spirit). The followers of the prophets-and-imams are those one takes as guardians; he who takes them as guardians is counted *one of them*. The chain of *walaya* runs from the Prophet (peace and blessings of Allah be upon him) through the imams of his pure family to those who follow them. All are counted *humankind* in the strict sense. All have the angels as their pair.

The doctrinal payload. The pair of humankind in the strict sense is the angels *because* the strict-sense *humankind* is *the rank that resembles the angels by occasion*: pure obedience, pure worship, pure adherence to command-and-prohibition. The pair-by-occasion principle of sub-chapter two gives the architecture. This completes *Chapter Five*'s formula of *two sides — one part natural, one part divine*. The divine side, written into the soul by virtue, is what pairs with the angels.

## Sub-chapter four: Bandhaqlis on bestial soul and speaking soul, read by the call

Sub-chapter four turns to the book *The Defense*'s appeal to *Bandhaqlis*, Empedocles, the pre-Socratic Greek philosopher (c. 490–430 BC) of Acragas in Sicily, whose doctrine of the four elements and the two cosmic forces (love and strife) was a major resource for the *falsafa* tradition and for the call through the *falsafa*'s mediation. *The book *The Defense** quotes Bandhaqlis on the souls.

*The bestial soul refers to the soul that speaks of love.* The desire-soul, the affection-soul. (In the original Empedoclean doctrine, *love*, *philotes*, is one of the two cosmic forces.) *And as for the speaking soul — if what returns from her is good, she will be kind to her with benefit and victory.* The rational soul, *the soul al-the speaker-propheta*, *returns good for what is offered*: educates, lifts, benefits the bestial soul that has offered its love. *Her image is desirable, and there is no better image among the bestial souls than the image of the horse, and none more eager to receive the effects of the speaking soul — so he made the horse the pair for which this is manifest.* The horse, in the book *The Defense*'s reading of Bandhaqlis, is the most beautiful and most receptive of bestial-soul images; therefore the horse pairs with the speaking soul (taken as the human).

The author enters with the methodological correction. *If the author of* the book *The Defense* *had drawn his evidence from the Book of Allah, Lord of the Worlds, and from the sayings of our followers, the pure Imams (may Allah's prayers be upon them all), to support what he wants concerning the matter of the soul from the angle of interpretation, that would be richer than the sayings of the Prophet.*

the call tradition does not draw its doctrine of the souls from Bandhaqlis-quotations carried over through Greek and Syriac transmissions. the call draws from *the Book of Allah* and *the sayings of the pure Imams*. To go to Bandhaqlis when the Book and the Imams stand at hand is to draw water from a remote well when a fresh stream runs at the door.

*If he and his peers had lived in our time, they would have had to draw from the seas of the knowledge of the House of Revelation — which overflows from the reservoir of God in His land — and from our pure loyalist Imams. They would have spoken differently about much of what they held; being absent from the senses, speaking from the minds and their ranks, the soul and its principles, they would have returned and believed otherwise.* The reformers wrote at the high-water mark of the *falsafa*'s incorporation into the call. Had they lived in the author's own time, under al-Hakim bi-Amr Allah in Cairo, they would have drawn from the comprehensive the inner interpretation the Cairo court produced (the *the Fatimid caliph's interpretive treatise* of Imam the Fatimid caliph, the *majalis* of the later Fatimi teacher al-Shirazi), and would have *spoken differently*.

*They would have grasped that Murad (Banadqlis) said what he said in a sense other than what the author of* the book *The Defense* *took it for — namely, that the bestial soul is the horse and the speaking soul is humankind.* *The book *The Defense** has *taken Bandhaqlis literally*. The literal reading misses the inner interpretation.

The architectural principle the author is about to articulate, that the sensual soul *would accept the ruling of the speaking soul if nothing impedes it*, and the speaking soul *would kindle its judgment* in the sensual soul fit to receive, is anchored at the Prophetic root. The Prophet's hadith on the affinity of souls names the structural condition that the inner interpretation of Bandhaqlis rests on:

> *Souls are conscripted soldiers; those of them that recognize one another come together in harmony, and those that do not recognize one another fall into discord.* — (Sahih Muslim, Book 45, Hadith 6376; narrated by Aisha)

Souls find their pair by *recognition*: the inner affinity that runs prior to any external pairing. The sensual soul that accepts the ruling of the speaking soul *recognizes* it; the speaking soul that kindles its judgment in the sensual soul *recognizes* the soul fit to receive. Where the soul is *not* fit, recognition fails and discord follows. Bandhaqlis's bestial-soul-and-speaking-soul, read on the inner interpretation, is the architectural reading of the Prophetic doctrine of *recognition-as-pairing*. the call tradition reads the principle of soul-affinity as the structural condition for the work the limits do: the raising-by-degrees that the rest of sub-chapter four is settling.

*If returned to the balance of consideration and taken on the side that is correct: since the sensual soul, which is bestiality, would accept the ruling of the speaking soul if nothing impedes it, and the speaking soul, when it sees itself as the midwife of that ruling, would kindle its judgment in accordance with what it considers, we say: by "the sensual soul" he means those in the form of humankind who would accept education, discipline, and reason — not the horse — and by "the speaking soul" he means the teachers, who are the prophets, the guardians, the imams (peace be upon them), and their followers, who would be kind to those in the form of humankind, teach them their interests in religion and worldly affairs, benefit them in ways they do not perceive with their senses, and raise them in degrees according to their acceptance — being in truth the mothers of humankind.*

The full the inner interpretation. The *sensual soul*, Bandhaqlis's bestial soul, is *those in the form of humankind who would accept education*. The receivers-in-human-form, the people of the world's bulk. The *speaking soul*, Bandhaqlis's rational soul, is *the teachers*: the prophets, guardians, imams, *and their followers*. The relation between them is *kindness, benefit, raising in degrees according to acceptance*: the work of the call. And the speaking-soul side is *the mothers of humankind*: just as a mother gives birth, nurses, raises through dependency to maturity, so the *the limits of the call* give birth to those-in-the-form-of-humankind by the inner interpretation, nurse them with the milk of inner knowledge, raise them through degrees toward the rank of *humankind in the strict sense*.

The Qur'anic anchor lands precisely here:

> *O reassured soul, return to your Lord, pleased and pleasing; enter among My servants; enter My Paradise.* (Quran 89, the chapter of the dawn, verses 27 through 30; translation rendered after Sahih International)

*The reassured soul*, *the soul al-mutma'inna*, is the soul *raised through the degrees of acceptance* until it reaches *reassurance*, the rank that pairs with the angels. *Return to your Lord*: the *Ma'ad* of that soul to its rank-of-origin. *Pleased and pleasing*: pleased by what it has received, pleasing to what receives it. *Enter My servants*: the rank of *those who serve Allah by both worships*. The verse names exactly what Bandhaqlis's bestial-soul-and-speaking-soul doctrine indicates when read *on the correct face*.

*To take his statement upon the correct face is preferable to taking it upon a face that is not correct. The horse is not more accepting than those in the form of humans, nor is its image better than the human image; nor are the guardians, imams, and their followers less in sympathy with such people than some humans are in the sport and gentling of the horse — they are far more so.* The human-formed receivers are *more accepting* than any horse, by the speaking soul planted in them. The kindness, patience, care the guardians-and-imams pour into the raising of the *mu'minun* is *far more* than any horse-trainer's patience. *If this is the case, and no plain statement shows the contrary, there is no evidence that the image of the horse is the best among the bestial soul-images, nor that the horse must be the pair of humankind on the side of education and good image. To invoke this is impossible.*

## Sub-chapter five: the architectural map of the microcosm

Sub-chapter five turns to the strict architectural mapping of the microcosm.

*The author of* the book *The Correction* *said: Humankind is the counterpart of the whole world and is preferred to it by a noble essence that is not of this world.* The speaking soul, which does not belong to the natural-world end of the chain but is conveyed from the higher world through the limits.

*Its body stands in contrast to all bodies through their perceiving genera and solidity, and the dissolved, because in his body there is something corresponding to each of these — such as bone, flesh, nerves, veins, and cartilage — and all the liquid substances that flow from his body, such as blood, semen, sweat, and what flows from the eye, ear, mouth, and other parts. All of these belong to the essence of bodies and to their kinds, which correspond to the genera of the essence that is one of the three essences of the world: the essence of bodies, which is not joined to any of the three breaths.*

The first layer of the map. The human body parallels the world's bodies: knotted (bone, flesh, nerves, cartilage) and dissolved (blood, semen, sweat). This is the *first essence* — bodies as such, *prime matter*-informed, before the soul-ranks descend.

*His growth, set parallel to the growing species — the various kinds of trees — corresponds to the kinds of the second essence, which is joined to the developing soul.* The second layer. The human *grows* — as hair grows, as the body increases. Growth parallels the *plant genus*. The second essence is *body-plus-vegetative-soul*.

*His animal sense, in its different kinds, corresponds to the kinds of the third essence, which is joined to the sensitive soul, namely the beasts.* The third layer. The human *senses* — sees, hears, moves, imagines, desires. Sense parallels the *animals*. The third essence is *body-plus-vegetative-and-animal-souls*.

*This is his meeting-point with the three jewels of the world, and his uniqueness lies in the fourth rank: he became the fourth essence, which is the essence of the speaking soul, and he stands alone in this station.* The fourth layer. The speaking soul (*the soul al-the speaker-propheta*) is the rank no other generated being shares. The human is the fourth-essence-by-distinction *within* the animal genus.

*The book *The Defense** refuses the map at one of its joints. *As for his saying that the dissolved and the contracted are one essence, this is not warranted. Rather, the dissolved, the knotted, the growing, the sensitive, and the speaker are all one essence by way of genus; as for chapters and properties, the concluded essence is not identical with the dissolved.* And he re-maps. *He set the dissolved bodies opposite his growth, because of the dissolution within them. And dissolution occurs only by the motion of the jewels that dissolve. In like manner, growth occurs only by motion; so when they resemble each other in the chapter of motion, each of them is almost identical with its companion. He set the body opposite his sense, and opposite this he set the animal sperm.* *The book *The Defense**'s map in brief: knotted-bodies / dissolved-bodies / body / animal-sperm — running parallel to body / growth / sense / fourth-rank.

The author enters with the verdict. *The dispute between the two contenders in this chapter is that the author of* the book *The Correction* *set, in the place of human growth, the plant of the great world that grows and develops; whereas the companion (the author of* the book *The Defense**), in contrast, set the dissolved bodies there. And the author of* the book *The Correction* *set, opposite human animal-sense, the great animal world; the author of* the book *The Defense* *set opposite his sense the developing bodies. And the author of* the book *The Correction* *made of man's speaking soul the very thing that singled him out, preferred him over the world, and made him its master. The author of* the book *The Defense* *made the speaker stand against the animal. The saying of the author of* the book *The Correction* *is closer, in respect of likening, to the kind of thing he is comparing, and has a conformity that is not present in the opinion of the author of* the book *The Defense*.

*The book *The Correction** wins this round. Growth-to-plants is natural; sense-to-animals is natural; speaking-soul-as-unique-fourth is natural. *The book *The Defense**'s re-mapping crosses the natural joints.

*In general, the human being is not only a counterpart of this world; he is a counterpart for all the worlds, since he is the collector of all the things that preceded him in existence, similar to what is in him for both of them. The matter rests on this, not on what he said.*

The chapter's deepest move on the microcosm doctrine. The human is the counterpart of *all the worlds* — both the natural and the creative-emanative, both the lower and the higher, both the world-of-bodies and the world-of-the-Intellect. *He is the collector of all the things that preceded him in existence.* The human gathers in himself everything the chain of creation produced *before* him — from the First Intellect down through the universal Soul, the limits, the elements, the genera. *The matter rests on this.* The true map is *both-worlds-microcosm*.

## Sub-chapter six: the parallels at full breadth, and the creative-emanative world

Sub-chapter six (labeled in the manuscript *Chapter Six of Section Seven*) fills in the both-worlds-microcosm at full architectural breadth.

*Man, who is a human being, is the counterpart of the worlds because he is the sum of many things that exist between the creation and the second revival. He then became one, and became parallel in himself to the creative and emanative world, and the collector embodies his bodies parallel to the physical world. The two worlds are thus alike.*

The human is the sum of *all that exists between the creation* (*al-origination*, the origination of the First Intellect from no thing at all) *and the second revival* (*al-Ma'ad*, the soul's return to its rank-of-origin). He gathers, in himself, every rank of the chain.

The parallels at the natural-world end, joint by joint.

*The world is composed entirely of body: of it the gentle, which is the high, and of it the dense, which is the low. The human body is likewise gentle and dense; the gentle is the high.* Celestial bodies are *gentle*; elemental bodies are *dense*. The human's higher organs are *the gentle*; the lower organs are *the dense*.

*In the world there are planets and stars revolving, and in the human body too there is skin surrounding the whole body and so on, which covers the bones. What runs in the course of the planets, such as the ears, eyes, nose, and mouth, finds its parallel in man as well.* The senses parallel the courses of the planets. (The Ismaili tradition routinely names the five senses as the *five spheres of the body*, eye-as-sun, ear-as-moon, and so on.)

*In him there is also a moving engine, which is the self; and in the world the four elements; and in the human being also the four natures.* The *self* (the *nafs*) is the moving engine; the four elements outside parallel the four natures (hot/cold/wet/dry) inside.

*And in the world rivers, valleys, and waters of different tastes, mountains, and minerals — and in the human being likewise, as the author of* the book *The Correction* *notes: bones, cartilage, flesh, blood, and what flows from him and what comes out of him.* Bones-as-mountains, cartilage-as-stones, flesh-as-earth, blood-as-rivers — the canonical microcosm-macrocosm parallels in their the call-architectural setting.

*In the world there is what grows and develops without sensing, and in the human being there is also what grows and develops without sensing, such as hair and nails; and as the world grows and increases from its youth to its greatness, so too in the world there are sensitive animals, and in the human being likewise the strong sensitive faculties.* Inert growth in the world (the plant genus); inert growth in the human (hair and nails). Sensitive animals in the world; sensitive faculties in the human.

*The whole world is composed and complete, and has been supplemented by the existing — and this is an analogy to the world, although all that we have mentioned here is a summary that is detailed in our book known as the Peace of Mind.*

The natural-world end of the parallels lands here. The reference: *the Peace of Mind* — *the author's other treatise* — *The Repose of the Intellect*, the author's magnum opus, the architectural treatise on the whole chain from the First Creator down through the universal Intellect, the universal Soul, the seven seas, the limits, the celestial spheres, the elements, the genera, and the human being. *Al-Riyad*'s sub-chapter six is a *summary* of an architecture *detailed* there.

> *Man is composed in his being of the body that is informed by* prime matter *and form, the vegetative soul that draws nourishment from the elements, the animal soul that perceives the sensible by the five senses, and the speaking soul that ascends by the gift conveyed through the* limits *of the* call. *The whole human being is the chain of receivership made one.* — the author, *the author's other treatise* (the composite architecture of the human being)

But the natural-world parallel is only the lower half. The chapter turns to the upper half — the creative-emanative world.

*As for what is similar to it in the world of creativity and emanation: first, that world is the world of intellect, the world of praise and praising and purification, the world of monotheism and divinity, the world of virtues, the world of goodness, and the world of excellences. The human being, who is also a human being, is the collector of these virtues — of intellect, praise, monotheism, divinity, virtue, existence over others and in oneself.*

The upper world named at its joints: *intellect* (the rank of the First Intellect and the universal Soul); *praise and purification* (the constitutive activities of the angelic ranks); *monotheism and divinity* (monotheism and *uluhiyya*); *virtues, goodness, excellences*. The human is *the collector* — the *jami'* — of all these. Intellect-in-the-human parallels the universal Intellect. Praise-by-the-human parallels the praising of the angels. The monotheism affirmed by the human parallels the monotheism the higher ranks affirm at every joint. *Existence over others and in oneself* — the existence-by-receivership from above and existence-by-being-itself below, the *two sides* *Chapter Five* settled.

*That world has impressed him, and his body has been marked by this world; and given that this is so, there is no distinction between him and what has gone before except by rank alone.* The closing formula. *That world* has *impressed* its image upon his soul; *this world* has *marked* his body. The human bears two impressions. And *there is no distinction between him and what has gone before except by rank alone*. The distinction is *only* of rank — the human and the chain are *one architecture, ranked*.

> *Man is the purpose of the second world and the end of the action of nature; the world of creation produces him as its fruit, and the world of emanation perfects him as its receiver; he is one being whose body is the chain made dense and whose soul is the chain made gentle.* — Imam the Fatimid caliph Maad ibn Ismail, *the Fatimid caliph's interpretive treatise*

Imam the Fatimid caliph — the Fatimid caliph and *imam* of the Cairo court that defined the high the call synthesis — names in *the Fatimid caliph's interpretive treatise* the very doctrine sub-chapter six is settling. The lower world *produces* the human by nature; the higher world *perfects* him by the gift conveyed through the limits. *He is one being whose body is the chain made dense and whose soul is the chain made gentle.* The natural composite is the chain *dense*; the speaking soul informed by the universal Intellect is the chain *gentle*. The human is the single being where the dense-chain and the gentle-chain meet.

## Sub-chapter seven: humankind between the two worlds

Sub-chapter seven (labeled *Chapter Seven of Section Seven*) closes on the deepest architectural question. If the human is the counterpart of *both* worlds, where does the human *belong*?

*The author of* the book *The Correction* *said: The human being is preferred over the world by the noble essence that is not of this world — not in the sense that the essence which was forbidden by saying it to be of this world is from that world; I mean the upper world, according to what he has set forth. Yet if he were only of that world and not of this world at all, we would not say that he is of all the worlds. He is in a state of existence between the two worlds, like the state of the boy in his existence between his two parents: it is not permissible to attribute him to one of them alone, because he is from both of them, and from them he came to be.*

The doctrinal landing. *He is in a state of existence between the two worlds.* Not *of* this world alone. Not *of* that world alone. *Between* — *bayna*, *barzakh*. And the image the book *The Correction* offers — and the author lets stand — is *the boy between his two parents*. A child is *not* purely the father; *not* purely the mother. The child is *from both, and from them he came to be*. So too the human between the two worlds.

*So it is permissible to attribute to this world what runs in him from the course of matter, which is the acceptable, and to attribute to that world what runs in him from the course of the image, which is the acceptable, just as in the case of the child who has, on the one hand, his parents in the highest and most honorable picture, and, on the other hand, the one who delivered that piece — namely the midwife.*

From *this world* runs the *course of matter* (the *prime matter*, the *acted-upon* of *Chapter Six*) — *the acceptable* (what receives the form). From *that world* runs the *course of the image* (the *sura*, the *form*, the *actor*) — *the acceptable* (what comes from above to inform). The human is *matter-and-image* jointly. Matter from below; image from above.

The Qur'anic anchor for the *between*-state is the *barzakh* the sub-chapter has just named. *Surat al-Furqan* gives the figure at full architectural breadth:

> *And it is He who has released [simultaneously] the two seas, one fresh and sweet and one salty and bitter, and He placed between them a barrier and prohibiting partition.* — (Quran 25, the chapter of the criterion, verse 53he criterion; translation rendered after Sahih International)

The two seas — *the gentle and the dense*, *the fresh and the salty* — meet without mingling. *A barrier and prohibiting partition* keeps each in its own kind even as they touch. The verse names what sub-chapter seven names: the human stands at the *barzakh* of the two worlds. Matter from below, image from above; the two run in him without confusion of kinds, *the acceptable* from each meeting at the rank that *is* the meeting. The *prohibiting partition* is what holds the architectural distinction even as the gift is conveyed across it — and that partition, in the architecture sub-chapter seven is settling, is what the *the limits of the call* are. The two seas meet at the human; the partition that lets them meet without mingling is the chain of receivership at which the gift descends.

The image now has a third figure. *The parents in the highest and most honorable picture* — the higher world, the universal Intellect and the universal Soul. *The midwife who delivered* — the *the limits of the call*: the prophets, the imams, the *hujjas*, the *du'at* who *receive the gift from the higher world and convey it into the receiver-in-human-form who has reached the rank that can receive it*. The midwife is what sub-chapter four named — *the mothers of humankind* — taken now in its architectural sense. *There are many proofs of this in our book known as the Peace of Mind. May God Almighty guide us to the home of His religion, and grant us the intercession of Muhammad and the Imams of his pure family — peace be upon them all.*

> *the limits are the limits set by Allah for the conveyance of the gift; the speaker-prophet is the first hadd; the foundation is the second; the imam is the third; the hujja is the fourth; the da'i is the fifth; the ma'dhun is the sixth; the mukasir is the seventh; through this descending chain the speaking soul reaches the receiver in the form of humankind, and through the same chain ascending the reassured soul returns to its Lord.* — the author, *the author's other treatise* (the architecture of the limits)

The architecture *the author's other treatise* gives — and sub-chapter seven settles in its image of *the midwife who delivered* — is the architecture of *the limits*: the descending chain by which the gift of the higher world is conveyed into the natural-world receiver, and the ascending chain by which the receiver, having become the *reassured soul*, returns to its Lord.

## The chapter's settled formula

The seven sub-chapters land on one settled architecture. The world divides into many more sections than four jewels and four classes — from the encompassing ocean down through the throne, the planets, the four elements, and the born; the *born* divide cleanly into three genera (mineral, vegetable, animal), with the human as a fourth-by-distinction within the animal genus, distinguished by the speaking soul. Humankind has *many pairs by occasion* — angels for the virtuous, lions for the brave, wolves and foxes for the cruel and cunning, crows for the treacherous, tigers for the deceitful, monkeys for the licentious — and the *strict* pair of humankind is the angels, because the *strict-sense humankind* is the rank of the prophets-and-guardians-and-imams and their followers, those who write into themselves the angelic resemblance by virtue and worship-of-both-kinds. The Bandhaqlis-doctrine of bestial-soul-and-speaking-soul is best read on the inner interpretation as those-who-receive-and-those-who-teach — the *the limits of the call* as the teachers (the *mothers of humankind*), the receivers-in-the-form-of-humankind as the bestial-souled who accept the teaching and are raised by degrees toward the rank of the *reassured soul* that *returns to its Lord, pleased and pleasing*. The architectural map of the microcosm runs joint-by-joint: human body parallels world's bodies, human growth parallels the plants, human sense parallels the beasts, human speaking soul stands alone as the fourth essence — and the book *The Correction*'s scheme is preferred to the book *The Defense*'s because it preserves the natural joints. But the human is the counterpart not only of *this* world; the human is the counterpart of *all the worlds*, with parallels at every joint of the higher world too (intellect, praise, monotheism, divinity, virtue). The human's rank is a *between*-state — like the boy between his two parents — matter from below, image from above; the parents *in the highest and most honorable picture* are the universal Intellect and the universal Soul; the midwife who delivers is the *the limits of the call* by whose hand the gift is conveyed.

The political stake of *Chapter Seven* sits one layer below the metaphysics. If the book *The Correction*'s four-classes scheme held, the chain of receivership at the natural-world end would lose its rank-by-rank discipline. If the no-peer claim held, the moral architecture (pairing-by-occasion as the *choosing* of one's resemblance) would lose its hold. If the book *The Defense*'s horse-by-acceptance gloss held, the doctrine of the limits would slip into a beast-trainer image and the *walaya* of the imams over the believers would lose its rank. If Bandhaqlis's bestial-soul-and-speaking-soul were taken literally, the architecture of raising-by-degrees would have no purchase. If the book *The Defense*'s re-mapping of the microcosm held, the natural joints of the chain would cross and the architectural conformity would lose its fit. The author's refusals at each joint are the protection of the chain — whose head is monotheism and whose foot is the *Ma'ad* and whose midstream is the limits by whose hand the gift is conveyed.

## What comes next

*Chapter Eight* of this book will turn to *qada* (judgment) and *qadar* (destiny) — *how the chain runs in time*, how what is decreed at the head of the chain becomes what is measured at the rank of the receiver, how the *reassured soul* receives both judgment-from-above and destiny-as-the-measure-of-its-acceptance.

And before that chapter opens, *Chapter Seven* leaves the reader with the image it has earned. *The book *The Correction** and the book *The Defense* arguing about whether the horse is paired with humankind, whether the world's classes are four or three-plus-distinction, whether the speaking soul singles him out or stands against the animal. The author, generations later, arriving at the doctrine that the human being is the counterpart of *all the worlds* — that he stands in a *between*-state, matter from below and image from above; that his strict pair is the angels because his strict-self is the rank that has chosen virtue; that his teachers are the *the limits of the call* — the midwives by whose hand the gift of the higher world is delivered into the receiver-in-the-form-of-humankind; that the chain runs from the encompassing ocean to the human-as-microcosm-of-all-the-worlds; that the receiver, having been raised by degrees, becomes the *reassured soul* that *returns to its Lord, pleased and pleasing*. *You suppose yourself a small body, yet in you is folded the greater world* — Imam the Commander of the Faithful, settling at the rank of the *manifest book* the very doctrine *Chapter Seven* settles all the way down. The architecture holds. The chain runs. The monotheism the chapter is built to protect stands at every rank.
