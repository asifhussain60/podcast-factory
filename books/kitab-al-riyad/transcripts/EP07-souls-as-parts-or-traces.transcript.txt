Defining the precise nature of the human soul once threatened to collapse an entire theological architecture. We are looking at chapter 4 of our source text today, where two early scholars debated A deceptively simple question. Are the speaking souls within us parts of the first truths, the universal soul, or are they merely traces of them? And the combatants in this dispute were the author of the book, The Correction, and the author of the book, The Defense. They represented opposing fronts in a struggle to articulate the exact relationship between humanity and the divine. The stakes were absolute. Choosing the wrong answer would violate their core tenets, unraveling the structural integrity of prophethood and Sharia. Yes. Our goal for this discussion is to track how a later mediating author meticulously threads the needle between these two thinkers. He refuses both the parts answer and the traces answer. He arrives at a highly specific doctrine that protects both the immaterial nature of the soul and the rigid hierarchy of spiritual authority. We should begin with the clash of terms itself. Well, the author of the correction insisted that human souls are merely traces. His deep motivation behind his insistence on the word traces was a profound fear of body talk. Body talk. So in this philosophical framework, if you call a soul apart, the immediate implication is that it functions as a physical object. Physical objects can be divided, broken, separated into pieces. To use the language of parts is to imply that the universal soul can be carved up. The correction wanted to maintain a strict barrier to protect the absolute immateriality of the upper ranks. He feared that language of parts reduced the divine to the realm of physical bodies. Bodies subject to fixed physical accidents, yes. We need to define accidents before we move forward. When medieval scholars use the word accident, they do not mean a collision. They mean a temporary physical trait. A property that is not essential to the object's nature. An accident is something that happens to an object. It becomes hot or turns red or gets wet. So the correction argued that if the universal soul has parts, it is a body. And if it is a body, it is subject to these changing physical accidents. That is the specific trap he wanted to avoid. But then we have the sharp rebuttal from the defense. This second author argued human souls absolutely must be parts. Why the insistence on parts? The defense looked at the word trace and thaw a void. If the soul is a mere impression, an ephemeral mark that does not share the substance of what created it, then how can it receive divine truth? If the soul is just a shadow cast on the wall, it has no real capacity to understand the light making the shadow. Precisely. The prophets claim to have seen the upper world. To the defense, this required the soul to be of the same substance as the universal soul. Without that shared substance, the capacity to apprehend the divine is lost. So if human souls are nothing more than ephemeral traces thrown off by the universal soul, the prophets would have no real substantial connection to the upper worlds. The mechanism of revelation would fail. The prophet must be able to reach back up through the chain to receive the message. I have to challenge the logic of the defense here. If we're discussing an immaterial, universal entity, an entity defined by its existence entirely outside of spatial dimensions, how can anyone argue it has parts? It presents a severe contradiction. The moment you apply the logic of divisible pieces, You are treating the divine as a physical body. You cannot carve up something that lacks physical dimension. It seems the defense falls into the very trap the correction was trying to avoid. The mediating author recognizes this logical contradiction. He steps in to dismantle the parts argument, and he does this using a precise logical dilemma regarding potentiality and actuality. We should define those terms for the listener. Think of actuality as a state of complete realization. A thing in actuality possesses its full perfection. It is fully real in its existence. While potentiality is a state of unrealized capacity, it is what a thing might become, but is not yet. You and I exist in a state of potentiality. We are constantly learning, changing, moving towards something else. We are not fully realized. That brings us to the dilemma. The text strictly establishes that the universal soul, which it calls the second, exists in complete actuality. It's fully realized. Partial human souls in this world, however, exist only in a state of potentiality. That disparity is the crux of the mediating author's argument. If the universal soul is simply a whole composed of partial human souls, and those partial souls exist only in potentiality, then the universal soul itself would be reduced to potentiality. Because a whole can only possess the actuality of its parts. If the pieces making up the whole are incomplete and unrealized, the whole itself must be incomplete and unrealized. The math of parts fails the test of actuality. The universal soul cannot be a sum of partial souls. To drive this point home, the mediating author introduces the analogy of iron held in fire alongside the concept of a black body. Let us examine the iron in the fire to see how physical bodies differ from the soul. When you hold a piece of iron in a flame, it receives the accident of heat. It turns red. But eventually it reaches a terminus. Hits a hard limit. The iron can only get so hot. A black physical object can only get so black. Physical bodies are strictly bounded. We have a fixed capacity past which they receive nothing further. This bounded capacity is the defining characteristic of physical objects. Now, the defense had attempted to use these exact examples, the iron and the black body, to argue that traces are merely ephemeral accidents on a receiver. Yes, the defense argued that therefore the soul must be a part to have any real permanence. But the mediating author turns this observation completely around. He argues that the iron and the fire proves that physical bodies are inherently limited receptors. The human soul, by contrast, keeps gaining from the light of the upper world without itself being altered in substance and without ever reaching a fixed terminus. A person can continue to learn, continue to purify their character, and continue to receive spiritual truth indefinitely. There is no wall where the soul simply cannot hold any more capacity for truth. Because the soul has no strict terminus of reception, it cannot be a physical body. And because it is not a physical body, it cannot be divided into spatial parts. The argument from the defense collapses under this scrutiny. So the soul is not a piece broken off, but the author has also established it cannot be a fleeting ephemeral mark or else revelation fails. What is the solution? The author reveals his definitive answer. The soul is the second emanation. The second emanation. The universal soul itself is the first emanation, brought forth in the first intellect. The partial speaking souls, the human souls within us, are the second emanation. They are brought forth from the universal soul by the same originating act. We need to distinguish between emanation and composition. Emanation is a continuous overflowing of existence. The source gives rise to the second entity without losing any of its own substance and without being divided. It is not like breaking a stone into smaller stones. They are not pieces of the universal soul by composition. They are derived from it by emanation. They possess their own distinct essence and their own survival. The text grounds this theological architecture directly in scripture. The author references Quran, the chapter of the night journey. The verse stating, the spirit is by command of my Lord. This affirms that the soul belongs to the order of the divine command. It exists entirely outside the order of physical bodies. It is not subject to the rules of spatial division. The text also points to Quran, the chapter of the sun, which describes the soul as a natural force that is fashioned and inspired. The verse says, he has succeeded who purifies it, and he has failed who corrupts it. This names the structure of the soul perfectly. The soul is a real essence fashioned in its place, and it emits an image corresponding to its purification or corruption. It perfects itself through the work of obedience and worship. It is not an arbitrary piece of the divine substance trapped in a human body. It is a distinct emanation shaped by its own actions in the lower world. This introduces the crucial mechanism of rank. The author states that the speaking soul finds in itself what the universal soul has, but it possesses this by rank, not by composition. We must clarify the distinction between sharing a substance and sharing a rank. To possess something by rank means that what is present in the whole is found in the part according to the part's capacity to receive. There's no division of substance taking place. The soul is a distinct receiver positioned perfectly beneath the divine in the chain of emanation. The text turns to a Hadith Quidsi from Al-Bukhari to demonstrate this mechanism of rank. The report outlines how a servant draws near to God. It states that the servant continues to draw near with voluntary works until God loved him. The crucial line from the text says, I am his hearing with which he hears, his sight with which he sees. This illustrates the soul's unbounded capacity for reception through obedience. When the text says I am his hearing, it does not mean the human ear physically becomes a piece of the divine substance. The servant's hearing becomes the rank at which the divine hearing is received. The human faculty is elevated. The human being remains human, and the divine remains divine. But the servant has reached a rank where the divine power flows through them without obstruction. The capacity increases through those voluntary works entirely without limit. The Sufi tradition offers an aphorism from a later Alexandrian teacher that confirms this same metaphysical architecture, though using a different vocabulary. What is the aphorism? It states, lights are the riding mounts of hearts and innermost beings. The light acts as the carrier. It brings the heart to the exact place of reception. The heart is not the light and the light is not the heart. They remain distinct. The heart is the rank brought by the light to the place where the gift arrives. It is a framework of perfect positioning, rather than a mixing of substances. This brings us to the most intense, consequential portion of the text. The mediating author must directly address the fears of the defense. Recall that the defense claimed that denying parts nullifies prophethood, the emit, walaya, and revelation. We should define these terms for the listener. Prophethood is the delivery of the divine message. The emit refers to the ongoing spiritual leadership and guidance after the prophet, and walaya is the spiritual authority and guardianship vested in those leaders. The defense believed that without substance composition, without the soul being a literal piece of the universal soul, Prophets and imams could not perceive the upper world. This entire structure of authority would collapse. The mediating author delivers a load-bearing rebuttal to this fear. The integrity of the prophets does not rely on their souls being literal pieces of the universal soul. He refutes the necessity of shared substance by introducing the technical concept of the 60s. What exactly are the 60s in this cosmological framework? The 60s is the boundary station. It stands at the extreme edge of the upper world, marking the limit between the immaterial realm and the physical realm. The prophet's soul does not need to be a piece of God to receive revelation. Instead, the prophet's soul traverses the ranks of obedience, ascends through the spiritual limits, and arrives at this specific boundary station. We see the specific boundary cited in Quran, the chapter of the star. The verses detail how the prophet ascended, stating he was at a distance of two bow lengths or nearer. The vision was real. The prophet reached the boundary station through the chain of obedience, not because his soul was a physical fragment of the divine substance. The revelation happens at that boundary. The servants stand at the station and the revelation comes down through the chain. The prophet sees from the station that the chain has brought him to. The ultimate theological grounding for this mechanism of reception comes from Ma'ad ibn Ismail, the Fatimid Khalif, in his interpretive treatise. He teaches that the intellect is the word and the word is the intellect. They are a single self under two names. The power runs through the chain by proportions of receivership, not by physical division. The higher you rise in rank through obedience, the greater your proportion of receivership. Let us look at the immense political consequence hidden in this metaphysics. This debate was not just an academic exercise in logic. It was a battle over spiritual authority. The political implications are vast. If you define souls as literal parts of the universal soul, believers could claim direct compositional access to the divine. A person could assert that since their own soul is a physical piece of the divine substance, they have no need for intermediaries. They could bypass the prophets. They could bypass the imams. They could ignore the rigorous limits and laws of the spiritual hierarchy claiming they already carried the divine within them as a physical component. It would dissolve the entire social and spiritual structure of the community. By establishing the second emanation architecture, the author proves that the only path upward is through the convoy of the limits and strict obedience. Because the soul is a distinct emanation receiving by rank rather than a piece of divine substance, It is entirely dependent on the established chain. The soul must ascend through voluntary works. It must be guided by the ranks above it to reach the boundary station. The political stability of the community, the authority of the Ums, and the legitimacy of the Sharia rely entirely on this metaphysical distinction. If you are a trace, the prophet cannot hear God. If you are a part, you do not need the prophet. The second emanation solves both. The architecture of the faith holds firm. because the single originating power runs through the proportions of receivership at each rank. The dispute over vocabulary, whether to use the word parts or traces, opened into the discovery of a vast, rigorously protected framework of bounded versus unbounded reception. We have tracked a profound philosophical journey. We saw the transition from the limitations of physical bodies, which can only receive heat or color up to a strict limit, to the unbounded reception of the speaking soul. If the exact rank of the soul has now been settled as the second emanation within this grand chain, what then is the whole human being? Consider the soul combined with the natural composite of the human body. Think about what this means for your own place in the order of the worlds. The human being acts as the ultimate fruit or harvest of this entire cosmological chain, the specific creature in which the gift of the upper world finally finds its perfect bearer in the lower.
