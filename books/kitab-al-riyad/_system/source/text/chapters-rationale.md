Sub-chapter eight refuses *nothing after the thing* (an empty negation that posits a *something with no name*) anchored to Q 42:11 (*Nothing is like Him — and He is the Hearing, the Seeing*). Sub-chapter nine works the first parallel correction — *the Intellect created with all the images of the worlds* belongs to the *acquired intellect* (the *second emanation from the world of nature*, the chain of the speaker-Prophet, foundation, imam, hujjah, da'i — the *hudud of the daʿwa*) — not the First Intellect (which is *self-knowing alone*, *does not need the perception of what is within it*, knows only *itself and the One above it*). Sub-chapter ten lands the chapter's central doctrinal identity — *loneliness is not a cause of reason... rather it is the intellect itself, and the intellect is the unity, which is the One. It is the cause; it is the effect; it is the creativity; it is the Creator; and it is the Perfect* — and refuses al-Mahsul's *unity is the cause of the Intellect* (which posits *unity* as a rank between Allah and the First Intellect — and there is no such rank). Sub-chapter eleven presses four impossibilities against *unity as other than the Intellect* (if unity is *other than Allah*, then either God is *originated-after-being-creative*, or *unity itself is originated*, or *unity behind the source* — positing *something behind the source* where *behind the source there is only Allah*, or *unity is the creator-for-the-sake-of-which* — which means it has created a multitude, *so there is no unity if it exists*). Sub-chapter twelve works the second parallel correction — *the forms became eternal* applies only to *acquired intellects* (the *paradoxical intellect called the proximate sovereign, nicknamed the Pen* — *al-Qalam* (al-qa-lam) — is the rank to which acquired intellects attain by *uniting* with it). Sub-chapter thirteen works the third parallel correction — *the Intellect became like the Word* applies only to acquired intellects (the First Intellect *is identical with* the Word, *not like* the Word — *likeness* requires two things alike, *identity* is one thing under two names). Sub-chapter fourteen refuses calling the *intellect* the *Creator* — *this is shirk, and it is not permissible to say, because God is far above being identical in His essence to that which exists by His creative act*. Sub-chapter fifteen works the fourth parallel correction — the *Intellect-as-intermediary* doctrine (with the sun-and-plates image) applies only to *the intellects of the prophets and their followers, derived from their cause which are others they quote — not the First Intellect, which is the First Creator*. Sub-chapter sixteen lands the chapter's centerpiece — *creativity IS the Creator*; the *intermediary* doctrine collapses because *creativity-as-something-other-than-the-Creator* has only two options (exists for itself — invalidated by self-fragmentation; exists with God — invalidated by the dual-likeness reductio) — anchored to Q 6:103 (*Vision perceives Him not, but He perceives all vision*), Q 57:3 (*He is the First and the Last, and the Outward and the Inward*), and the closing Imam Ali aphorism from *Nahj al-Balagha* (Tier 4) — *whoever describes Him has limited Him; whoever limits Him has counted Him; whoever counts Him has denied His pre-eternity* — from the sermon *al-Khutba al-Ashbah*. The closing section works the soteriological settlement: tawhid is *difficult*; Allah has *from time to time singled out one perfected man — the Imam, peace be upon him*; the Imam is *complete in accepting the overflow taking place in the natural world from the side of the angels who are near to Him*; *there is no way to this except by adopting the two acts of worship* (the *zahir* and the *batin* — the *body and spirit of the religion* settled in Imam al-Mu'izz's *Ta'wil al-Shara'i'*); the chapter closes with the prayer for *Dar al-Quds* (daar al-quds — Jerusalem, the Abode of Holiness), *a noble station*, and the book closes with *Praise be to God, until He is praised as is due; and prayers upon Muhammad and his good family, all of them, the pure*. The chapter is enriched with placed inline material from al-Kirmani's own *Rahat al-'Aql* (raa-hat al-aql — *The Repose of the Intellect*, Tier 1 — surfaced in sub-chapter one as the canonical formulation of the *limbs-of-the-body-of-the-daʿwa* doctrine of cooperation, and through the chapter as the reference for the architecture of the First Intellect as *pure unity self-knowing alone* and the *acquired intellect* as *the assembly of the images of the worlds*), Imam al-Mu'izz li-Din Allah Ma'add ibn Isma'il (peace be upon him)'s *Ta'wil al-Shara'i'* (Tier 5 Ismaili — surfaced in sub-chapter five as the highest *da'wa* authority on the Cairo-court doctrine of *huwa*), four Quranic anchors (Q 3:18 for the verse of self-witness in sub-chapter five; Q 42:11 for the verse of incomparability in sub-chapter eight; Q 6:103 for the exhaustion of language at the threshold in sub-chapter sixteen; Q 57:3 for the doctrine that there is no *intermediary* between Allah and what issues from Him in sub-chapter sixteen), and an aphorism attributed to Imam Ali ibn Abi Talib (peace be upon him) in *Nahj al-Balagha* (Tier 4 — surfaced at the chapter's close from the sermon *al-Khutba al-Ashbah* on *whoever describes Him has limited Him*). The doctrinal arc is the *closing humility* of the book: the speaker who has worked the chain joint-by-joint through *Chapters One through Nine* stops at the last joint — *the rank above which there is no rank, the rank of al-mubdi al-awwal Himself* — and *will not speak past it*; he only says what may *not* be said. The chapter file lands at 9,142 words — upper Extended band (5,500–9,500), with the source-spine ratio earning its place because *Chapter Ten* is the book's *closing critique* — *the highest doctrinal stake of the entire treatise* — and the cumulative weight of sixteen patient critiques is the chapter's argumentative force. The book closes with the chapter, and the chapter closes with the prayer.
