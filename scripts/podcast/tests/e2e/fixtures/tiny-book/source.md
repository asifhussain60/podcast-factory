## Chapter 1 — The Encounter at the Mosque

The young seeker first met the old scholar on a Friday afternoon, in the small mosque at the edge of the bazaar. He had heard of the scholar from his uncle, who had said only that the man was *muhaddith* (mu-HAD-dith) — one who carried the chain of teachings forward — and that he did not give his time easily. The seeker had walked for almost two hours through the heat to reach the mosque, his sandals worn thin against the stones of the road, and when he arrived he found the old scholar sitting in the shaded corner, leaning against a pillar, reading aloud from a small book bound in dark leather.

The seeker stood by the door for a long time, unsure whether to interrupt. The scholar did not look up. He continued to read, and his voice was low and steady, like a person rehearsing a familiar prayer. At last the seeker found his courage and stepped forward and said, very quietly, *as-salaamu alaykum* (as-sa-LAA-mu a-LAY-kum) — the customary greeting, peace be upon you — and the scholar lifted his eyes and answered with the same words, more slowly, returning the greeting in full.

"Sit," the old man said. "What do you carry?"

The seeker did not understand the question and said so. The scholar smiled and put the book aside. "Every person who walks into a learning carries something already. Their training. Their language. Their question. The question is what brought you here. I am asking what your question is."

The seeker was quiet for a moment. He had not expected to be examined so quickly. He had imagined a kind of long beginning, in which the scholar would speak about the importance of *ilm* (ILM) — knowledge — and the discipline of *sabr* (SAH-br) — patience — and the seeker would listen attentively and ask permission to study, and there would be no question put back to him in the first minute. He understood now that this was not how it would go.

"My question," he said carefully, "is about knowledge. About what knowledge is for. I have read many books, but I do not yet know what to do with them."

The scholar nodded once, as if he had heard this exact answer many times before. "Then your question is a good one. Most questions about knowledge fall into one of three shapes. The first shape asks: what is true? The second shape asks: how is the true to be known? The third shape asks: now that I know what is true, what am I to do? You are at the threshold of the third shape, which is the shape that troubles the rest of the life."

He paused, and the seeker felt the weight of the pause. Outside the mosque, a vendor was calling out the price of pomegranates. A child laughed. The scholar continued.

"The most common error is to think that knowledge ends at the second shape. To think: once I know the way of arriving at the true, my work is done. This is the error of a scholar who has read all the books but has not understood that the books were given for the sake of action. The books are *miftah* (mif-TAAH) — the key — but they are not the room. To stop at the key and admire its making is to refuse the room itself. The room is *amal* (a-MAL), the doing, and the room is what was always being asked of you."

The seeker said, "I do not know what doing is asked of me. That is my difficulty."

"Then we begin there," said the scholar.

What followed in that first sitting was less a lesson than an exchange. The scholar asked questions, and the seeker answered as best he could, and the answers were corrected, gently, but not always with words — sometimes the scholar would simply pause, or look away, or shake his head once, and the seeker would understand from the silence that his answer had not been right. The seeker learned in that first hour that the relationship he had entered into was not a teacher giving a student facts. It was something stranger, and older, and harder.

When the call to the afternoon prayer came, the scholar stood up — slowly, with effort — and said, "Come back tomorrow. We will begin with the seven stations."

The seeker did not know what the seven stations were, and the scholar did not say. The seeker walked home in the long shadows, repeating the phrase to himself: the seven stations. Whatever they were, they were waiting for him.

That night the seeker wrote in his small notebook the four words the scholar had used and explained: *muhaddith*, *ilm*, *sabr*, *amal*. He underlined the word *amal* twice. He had the strong impression that the scholar had said it carefully, as if it carried weight. He resolved that whatever the seven stations turned out to be, he would not treat any of them only as a key to be admired.

He did not sleep well. He could not stop turning the conversation over. He had asked one question and been given, in return, not an answer, but a different question — the third shape — and a single word, *amal*, that pointed forward without saying where. He thought of his uncle, who had told him to find the scholar. His uncle had not warned him that the scholar would put the seeker's own question back at him. His uncle had only said: he will not give his time easily. Now the seeker understood what that meant. The scholar gave time slowly, and to use it well was the first piece of the discipline.

He rose before dawn and dressed and walked to the mosque a second time, the path already familiar.

## Chapter 2 — The Seven Stations of the Soul

When the seeker arrived at the mosque the next morning, the old scholar was already seated, with two cups of tea, one of them placed near the empty cushion where the seeker would sit. The seeker took this as a sign that he was expected, and he settled himself carefully, and he drank from the cup, and he said nothing until the scholar spoke.

"The tradition," the scholar began, "teaches that the soul, the *nafs* (NAFS), is not a single thing but a thing in motion. It moves through stations. Each station is a kind of life, with its own temptations and its own discipline. The tradition names seven stations, and the work of the seeker is to know which station the soul is currently in, and to know the work proper to that station, and to do that work without trying to skip ahead to a station that has not yet been earned."

The seeker waited, expecting the scholar to enumerate the stations. The scholar did not enumerate them. He continued.

"It is enough, today, to know that there are seven, and that they ascend, and that to confuse the work of one station with the work of another is the most common cause of spiritual collapse. A seeker who imagines that he has reached the third station while still doing the work proper to the first will, when difficulty comes, lose both. He will lose what he has already earned, and he will lose the trust of his guide. So the discipline is to remain at the station that is yours, and to do its work fully, and not to ask too quickly what comes next."

The seeker, who had hoped to write down the names of the seven stations in his notebook, kept his pen still. He was learning, already, that not every named thing in the tradition is named for the seeker's own reach. Some things are named, and the naming is itself a kind of test: can the seeker accept that there are seven, without needing immediately to know all seven?

The scholar continued. "There is another teaching that follows from this. Each station has its own *hikmah* (HIK-mah) — its own wisdom, its own characteristic insight. The wisdom of one station is not the wisdom of another. A teaching that is useful at the first station may be a distraction at the third. A teaching that is necessary at the fifth would not even be intelligible to one who is still at the second. So the seeker does not seek wisdom in general. He seeks the wisdom proper to where he is. This is why the same book can be read by two people and one will be helped and the other will be unmoved — they are at different stations, and the book's *hikmah* is meeting them at different points."

The seeker asked, "How does one know which station one is at?"

"By the difficulty," said the scholar. "The station you are at is the one whose difficulty is currently consuming you. If your present struggle is with the *nafs* — with the appetites, with the lower self that pulls you toward what is easy and away from what is true — then you are at the first station, or you are still doing its work. If your struggle has moved past the appetites and is now with the question of which good thing to choose between two good things, you are at a later station. The difficulty is the marker. When the difficulty changes shape, the station has changed."

The scholar paused and looked at the seeker carefully. "You may suspect that you are at a later station than you are. That is normal. Almost every seeker suspects this. The discipline is to be honest about the difficulty that is actually consuming you, and not the difficulty you would prefer to be consumed by."

The seeker thought about this. He had imagined, before coming to the mosque, that his struggle was about the choice between several good paths in life — a struggle of the higher stations. Listening to the scholar, he began to wonder whether his struggle was really still about the lower appetites. About impatience. About wanting to be admired. About reaching for what felt easy rather than what was true. He was honest enough, in that moment, to suspect that his real station was lower than he had hoped.

The scholar saw this, somehow, and nodded once, and said, "Good. The first true work has begun."

They sat for some time without speaking. The mosque was nearly empty. A bird called from outside. The scholar drank his tea. At last he said, "There are also seven principles of the path, parallel to the seven stations, and they are not the same as the stations. The principles describe the discipline; the stations describe where the discipline is being applied. *Tawhid* (taw-HEED) — the recognition of unity — is the first principle, and it is the principle to which all the others return. We will speak of *tawhid* tomorrow."

The seeker wrote in his notebook: *nafs*, *hikmah*, *tawhid*. He underlined *tawhid* once, and then again. He noticed that the scholar had not enumerated the seven stations, and had not enumerated the seven principles, and yet had used both numbers as if they were known and settled. He understood that the numbers were not the lesson. The lesson was elsewhere.

He walked home that afternoon along a different street and stopped at a bookshop and bought a small bound notebook with a green cover, because the one he had been using was running out of pages. He thought he would need more pages than he had imagined.

The scholar's words about the difficulty being the marker stayed with him for a long time. That night, when he was alone, he tried to be honest about which difficulty was currently consuming him. He found that the honest answer was not what he had wished. He wrote it down, in the new notebook, on the first page, and underneath he wrote: *this is the station I am at, until it changes shape*.

## Chapter 3 — Adab and the Return

On the third morning, the scholar was different. He had a small package wrapped in cloth beside him, and he did not begin by speaking. He waited until the seeker had settled and drunk his tea and looked up, and then he said, "Yesterday we spoke of the stations and the principles. Today I will speak of *adab* (AH-dab), which is the discipline of conduct, and which is the soil in which both the stations and the principles grow. Without *adab*, none of the rest takes root. With *adab*, even a small beginning can grow large."

The seeker said, "I have heard the word but I am not sure of its meaning."

"It is sometimes translated as etiquette, but that is too small a word. *Adab* is the proper conduct that befits each relationship and each occasion. *Adab* with one's teacher is not the same as *adab* with one's parents, which is not the same as *adab* with a stranger one meets in the road, which is not the same as *adab* in prayer. Each form of *adab* has its own shape. Together they make up the character of the person who has them. A person without *adab* may have learning, may even have piety, but the learning and the piety will not be received well by others, and over time they will not be received well by the person himself. *Adab* is what carries the rest. The Arabs say: *adab* is the salt of the food. Without it, the food has no taste; with it, every other quality is brought forward."

He paused, and then unwrapped the small package beside him. Inside was a worn copy of a book the seeker did not recognize. The scholar offered it to him with both hands. "This is yours now. I have written in the margins where I thought it would help you. Read it slowly, one or two pages at a time, and return to me when you have a question. Do not read it the way one reads a newspaper. Read it the way one reads a letter from a parent who has been far away. With attention. Without rushing."

The seeker took the book with both hands, as he had been given it. He felt that *adab* was already being demonstrated to him in the very giving of the gift — the slow handing-over, the both-handed reception, the absence of any flourish or speech.

"There is something else I will say to you today, and then we will sit in silence for a while, because today is not a day for many words. The path has twelve marks of the true seeker. I will not tell you the twelve, because the knowing of them is not what makes a seeker true. The seeking of them is. Some of the twelve you will find in this book, in the margins where I have written. Some you will find in your own struggle. Some you will find years from now, when you do not remember that you were looking, and you will discover that one of them has already become part of you. The marks are not a checklist. They are a description, after the fact, of what a true seeker looks like to others. You do not work toward becoming the marks. You work toward the *amal* — the doing — and the marks appear as a consequence."

The seeker thought about this. He noticed that the scholar had now done the same thing twice in three days: he had used a number — seven stations, seven principles, twelve marks — without enumerating. The seeker understood this was not an oversight. It was a teaching. The teacher was modeling the discipline by which the tradition itself had handed down its content: name what is settled, but do not pre-empt the seeker's own arrival at it.

The scholar continued. "The principle for today is *qalb* (QALB) — the heart. Not the muscle. The center where intention lives. The tradition teaches that knowledge that does not reach the *qalb* is knowledge that has not yet completed its journey. The *qalb* is the place where *amal* is decided. If your knowledge has not yet changed your *qalb*, your knowledge has not yet finished its work in you."

He looked at the seeker for a long time, and then he said, "I do not say this to discourage you. I say this so that you do not mistake the early stages for the final ones. The journey of knowledge from the mind to the *qalb* is long. There is no shortcut. There are only practices that, over time, soften the *qalb* enough that knowledge can sink into it. *Adab* is one of those practices. *Sabr* is another. The companionship of someone who has already walked further than you is a third."

They sat for a long while in silence after that. The seeker did not feel that the silence was empty. He felt that the scholar was teaching him still, by sitting, by not filling the space with more words. He understood that he was being taught the *adab* of presence: that not every minute with a teacher must be filled with speech; that the silence is also being given, and to receive it is also a discipline.

When at last the scholar spoke again, it was only to say, "Go home now. Read what I have given you. Come back when you have a question that the book has not answered, but not before. Do not rush back. Take days, or weeks, if needed. The book will not leave you. I will not leave you. The path is patient. *As-salaamu alaykum*."

The seeker said, "*Wa alaykum as-salaam*," and gathered the book to his chest with both hands and stood up, slowly, in the way he had now understood was the right way to leave the presence of a teacher. He walked out of the mosque into the late afternoon and along the same road home, and the air was cooler now, and the bazaar was quieter, and he felt different than he had felt three days before. He could not say in what way exactly. He only knew that some small thing in him had moved.

That evening he opened the book and read the first page slowly, and then he closed it. He had been told to read one or two pages at a time. He would not read more, even though he wanted to. The wanting itself, he understood, was something to be watched. It was a small motion of the *nafs*. He noted it in his green notebook, and then he sat for a while, and then he slept.

In the morning he opened the book again, and he was ready.
