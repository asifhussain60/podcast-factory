# The Author's Mukaddima — Sayyidina Ali bin Hibatullah Opens *Kunooz al-Hikmah*

*From the second lecture in a recorded series unpacking* Kunooz al-Hikmah al-Mazboora li-Arbaab al-Ismah — *"Treasures of Wisdom Stored for the Lords of Infallibility" — by Sayyidina Ali bin Hibatullah al-Makrami. After a brief return to the question of recitation order that closed the prior session, the speaker enters the author's mukaddima itself: the moment Sayyidina Ali bin Hibatullah names his book, declares for whom it is written, confesses his own weakness, makes a claim no earlier Dai has made about the first and last intellect, and seals the framing with a long dedicatory prayer.*

## Opening — the question of recitation order

The lesson opens by returning to a thread left hanging from the previous session: when, in the sequence of a Quranic recitation, should each opening formula be said?

The speaker reminds the listeners of Allah's instruction in His Holy Quran — that the believer who has gone astray should turn to Allah in repentance, and that even the believer who has acted rightly should still seek refuge with Allah. So before the Book is opened, the Ta'wuz is recited: *A'udhu billahi min ash-shaytan ir-rajim* — "I seek refuge with Allah from the accursed Satan." But this raises a question. The Book itself is the speech of Allah, the very source of guidance. Why, then, must one seek refuge before reading it?

The answer, the speaker explains, lies in what was done with the Book by those who came before. A scholar once raised this Book of Allah, placed it upon his head, and said: *this is the Book of Allah* — and then misled the community through wrong interpretation. The misguidance came not from the Book but from the meanings he extracted from it. So we who follow the instruction of Allah recite the Ta'wuz before the Quran, the duas, the verses of recitation, and the formulae of the Dawah.

But the Adhan, the call to prayer, is not preceded by Bismillah. There is no hard and fast rule that one must begin the Adhan only after the Waqf-e-Alaihim and the Takbeera; in fact, one may recite the Salawat first. The speaker recalls his uncle's commentary on this point: because the Salawat ought to come first, the Qul opens the recitation, and the Salawat is recited later in the sequence rather than at the very start.

A particular verse receives careful attention — the last verse of the Quran, called *Ayat-ul-Izzat*, the Verse of Might. The way it ends carries a name; the way one recites it differs from the way one recites the verse before it. Where the earlier line was read at full pitch, this final line should be read in a lighter register, with a clear difference between them. The reason is the dignity of what the verse points to — the last of the long line of those who finish the work of guidance, the figure for whom one should pray with restraint and not with strain.

## The Dai's harvest: asraar and anwaar

Having closed the recitation question, the speaker turns to a specific prayer the author of *Kunooz al-Hikmah* has placed inside his text, and reads it slowly:

> *Bismillahir Rahmanir Rahim. Allahumma waffiq da'iyal-jami'in asrarihim al-mustamayin hadihi anwarihim* —
> May Allah grant success to the Dai, the gatherer of their secrets, the assembler of these their lights.

The prayer is for the Dai of the gathering. Two things are gathered, the speaker says: the *asraar*, the secrets, and the *anwaar*, the lights. These are not two names for the same thing. In the life of a Dai, the *asraar* are the wisdoms — the meanings carried inside the structures of the *hudood-e-Dawah*, the ranked offices of the call, and the limits within the *mahmood-e-divi*, the praised station of the believer. These wisdoms do not yet shine as light in the world. The *anwaar*, the lights, come out only after death. After burial — within three days, the speaker says — these emanations begin to issue from the body. That is later. But the gathering, the *jamma'*, comes to the Dai now. The secrets gather in him while he lives. The lights belong to a different timetable. *Wa hauzatahum* — and his enclosure, the wall thrown around the central Dawah, the *markaz* of the call.

## Every Dai has his pharaoh

The speaker reads on: *liqool-e-da'ee pharaon* — "for every Dai of the call, there is a pharaoh." Every authentic Dai has an opponent. The pharaoh is not always a tyrant of his own age. Sometimes the era is peaceful, and the opposition is latent. He cites the time of Sayyidina Hussain bin Hussain — a brilliant time, a quiet time. The upheavals came later. Even then, the speaker says, there was a pharaoh in a softer form: a man who did not in fact oppose the Dawah, only because he lacked the opportunity. Had he been given the moment, he too would have claimed the call and made himself the rival. So the pharaoh-figure is structural, not merely historical. The Dai who completes his work in a peaceful era still walks beside one.

The author then turns his prayer outward to his Imam and to the line of Du'at: that their desires be fulfilled, that they be granted what they seek, that this gathering of secrets and lights move forward to its proper place.

## Rahman, then Rahim — the order beneath the rhyme

The speaker pauses to notice something about Quranic Arabic. Wherever the pair *Rahman-Rahim* appears, *Rahim* is the second word; the order is not free. This is not a stylistic choice but a structural feature. There is a *Qafia*, a rhyming convention, that holds across the Quran: Rahman first, Rahim second. The pattern is found again in Surah al-Hajj, where one of the recurring pairs is *al-Rahim al-Ghafur*. There are eight verses in that Surah of which it is said that they are the chambers of Paradise — that as Paradise has eight gates, these verses are their keys. To recite them is itself a virtue of high station.

## The naming of the book

Now the author reaches the moment that gives the lesson its weight — he names his book. The speaker reads the author's own words and then explains them.

> *I have named this risalah Bikunul-Hikmah, the Treasures of Wisdom — al-Mas'ura al-Arbaab al-Ismah, those that have been stored for the Lords of Infallibility.*

The phrase requires unfolding. "*Al-Mas'ura*" — stored, preserved, set aside. "*Arbaab al-Ismah*" — the Lords of Infallibility. The speaker takes care here. The Arbaab al-Ismah are not, in the author's primary sense, only the Imams. The Imams of the Ahl al-Bayt — those who cannot be misled, who are free from sin — they are the holders of total infallibility. But the *Du'at*, in the rank below them, share in this infallibility in their partial way. They are infallible in their station, within the limit Allah has set for them. So the book has been written for them — for the Du'at, the people of Ismah in its participatory sense.

And the author tells us how the book came to be written. Questions had been put to him. They came through Sayyidina Hatim Muhyiddin, the elder, the senior of the Du'at — questions sent to him by Sayyidina Ahmad, asking for clarification. The Imam was present, the speaker says, in the manner that the Imam is present at all times. The Imam answers his questions; the Dai is the instrument. But the author confesses something the speaker draws out as the key to the whole mukaddima.

## A weakness and a tongue

The author writes that he cannot answer with his pen. He cannot answer with his speech. *I am very weak in knowledge. I am very weak in my understanding. There is a great weakness in my pen.* This is not a literary humility-formula. The author is taking inventory. He has been asked by serious men, in serious matters, and he is reporting his condition honestly. He says that he placed before his Master a kind of helplessness: *here are the questions of the people from various provinces; here are the things they want answered. I do not see how I can answer them.*

And then, the author says, his Master helped him. Allah opened his tongue. Whatever has been put into the book has come not from his own knowledge but from the Master's grace working through him. The trust the author offers back is a trust on the Master's grace — *I have trusted your trusted material.*

The speaker pauses here to mark the distinction the author is making. There is a difference between the author's personal form, which has not yet been gathered into its final shape — that gathering happens only after death — and the author's personality, which is already so brilliant that the listener must imagine its dimensions. The form of the Sayyid is formed only after the death of the Sayyid. But the person, alive, is already such that those who sit with him have to reach toward an imagination of what he is. This is why the speaker says the book is named *Bikunul-Hikmah, the Treasures of Wisdom*: it is the deposit left for those who must read the Sayyid through what he has written, while waiting for the final form to appear.

## First intellect, last intellect — a claim no Dai has made before

What follows is the most striking sentence the author writes in this opening, and the speaker reads it carefully twice.

The first existent and the last existent, the author says, are equal — *sawa-sawa* — in their station before the Imam. The first existent is the First Intellect, *al-Aql al-Awwal*, who exists before all things. The last existent is the believer who, having entered the sphere of Iman through the oath of allegiance, has taken his place at the end of the line. Their relation to the knowledge of the Creator is equivalent. The First Intellect, in his nearness, has no further increase of knowledge to receive. The last existent, the perfected believer, has reached the same boundary by the opposite road. They are equal in not-increasing. They are equal in their hold on what they have.

The speaker stops on this and says: before this, no earlier Dai of the call had spoken this sentence aloud. This is the author's own contribution. It is not borrowed from an earlier text. Allah has put it on his tongue.

## The taste of the reward

The reward of this work, the author writes, comes in the form of taste. The speaker uses the word *lazzat* — sweetness, savor. He recalls something from his own life in Hyderabad: there used to be a way of speaking among certain elders, who would say of a young person who had eaten well at a host's table, *you ate of his portion* — meaning you took something of his reward. *Jazakallah bil khairi wa sawab* — may Allah reward him with good and goodness. The taste of the work is the reward. The tongue becomes the place where the reward is delivered, because it was the tongue that did the work.

## The edge of the book — who is permitted to hold it

The author places a warning at this point in the mukaddima, and the speaker reads it without softening it.

The verse the author cites is of the most powerful kind. *Al-Mushajjab*, the speaker says — its testimony is severe, its covenant strict. *Mishaqun shadid* — a binding covenant of great severity. The one who comes to hold this book in his hand, the one to whom its meanings are made known, must take care. He should not simply read what is written. He should not extract meanings from what is written. He may do so only on one condition: that he has been permitted.

To be permitted, in this context, is to have a certain capacity. The author defines it the way the speaker explains it. The permitted reader is the one capable of distinguishing the good from the bad in what he reads. He is the one who can adjudicate, who can decide what is right and what is wrong, who can move forward and back as the matter requires — the mind that has within it both the deliberation and the authority to place what is read in its proper rank. Anything less than this — a reader who carries the book without the equipment to judge it — risks misreading and, by misreading, misleading.

The unpermitted reader is the one who has not been freed of his attachment to *Ali al-Ma'bud*, the Most High who is worshipped. He has not yet been freed of his attachment to the worship-self, the *kifayat-e-ma'budi* — the sufficiency of the worshipper-state, the *ta'at-e-ma'budi*, the obedience-state. Such a reader is still inside the formal religion in a way that has not yet been refined into discernment.

## The shirk of inclination

Here the speaker draws out a thread that the author has placed in the mukaddima as a quiet warning. He tells a story from the line of Du'at to illustrate what is at stake.

There was a brother of Sayyidina Ali bin Hussain — a man who was not himself a great scholar, but who was constantly in the presence of the Dai. He sat in the lessons. He carried out the work alongside. He was a brother in proximity. But there came a moment in his orientation when he inclined — *mahal* — toward Abu Bakr. The inclination itself was sufficient to become a *hijab*, a veil. He was lifted in proximity to the Dawah, and then, by his own internal motion, he placed a veil between himself and what he had been near to. He could not bear the weight of *Ismah* — the carrying of infallibility-by-participation that the line of the Du'at receives. Later, when *Ismah* itself moved toward him, he was not equipped to receive it.

The speaker draws the verse of the Quran the author quotes here: *lain ashrakta layuhbatanna 'amaluk* — "if you commit shirk, your deeds will be made to fall." The verse is addressed, in form, to the Prophet himself. From whom did the Prophet learn the lesson of Iman? The speaker leaves the inference open. What the verse establishes is that *shirk* is not only outward — not only the worship of an idol or the naming of another god alongside Allah. There is an inward shirk, a *shirk-e-bandagi*: an inclination of the inner orientation toward one who is not the proper object of the Dawah's wilayat. This too falls under the warning. *Even a little*, even a sideways inclination of the heart, is enough for the deeds to be made to fall.

So when the author says his earlier sins are forgiven, the speaker explains that this is not a casual amnesty. It is a clearing of the previous burden so that what comes next — the work of writing this book, the gathering of secrets, the eventual emergence of lights — can proceed without obstruction.

## The dedicatory prayer

The mukaddima closes with the author placing himself before his Imam in the formal language of the prayer of dedication. The speaker reads it in Arabic, then renders it.

> The favors you wish to bestow upon me, *wa tafazil*, and the kindnesses, and the rewards — *qadamu bayna yegai ma asalu anu rizaika* — I place them before you in my asking, and I ask of your pleasure. *Wa prahu mimma anta la dhiyana* — and I disavow what is not of your way. *Wa asrahu rizaika beupul alamina na'amidha alakiram* — and I express my dependence on you, taking my hold on the world-renowned Imam who is greatly respected, by his right, the right of every vastness that is great, by the blessings of the Zubr-e-Sharifa, the blessings of Allah great and exalted.

The author then makes the request that gives the prayer its narrative shape. He addresses the Imam directly: *do not disappoint me by leaving the request unfulfilled. I have taken your goodness as my pledge. Your virtues, your generosity, your help, your hospitality — I rely on them in this world and in the next.* And he marks his own condition with honesty: *what I will say is filled with nerves and emotions. It is the speech of a man without a clean excuse. If I were asking from someone other than you, the asking itself would be undone by the nerves in it.* Put a veil across my chest and my forehead, he says. Let the asking be received under your continual kindness.

The closing line of the dedicatory prayer asks the Imam not to count him as a claimant of fluency. *I do not claim to be eloquent. I do not claim to be learned.* I have been pressed into this work. Whatever has come, has come by your charity. The book is the reward returned in the form of taste, and the taste belongs to you.

## What the mukaddima has done

By the end of this opening, the author has accomplished four things and the speaker has surfaced each of them.

He has named the book. He has named for whom it is written — the Arbaab al-Ismah in the participatory sense, the line of Du'at who carry infallibility within their station.

He has placed his own condition on the record. He is weak in knowledge, weak in understanding, weak in his pen. Whatever follows is not the work of his own equipment.

He has made a claim about the first intellect and the last existent that, the speaker says, no earlier Dai of the call has made. The two ends of the line, *al-Aql al-Awwal* and the perfected believer, are equivalent in their station before the Imam. This is the theological key the rest of the book will turn.

And he has placed a warning at the door. The book is not to be picked up casually. The one who holds it without the capacity to distinguish the good from the bad in it is in danger of becoming the next misreader — the one who, in handling the Book of Allah without permission, became a hijab to those who read after him.

The lesson ends here. The next session, the speaker says, will move into the body of the text itself.
