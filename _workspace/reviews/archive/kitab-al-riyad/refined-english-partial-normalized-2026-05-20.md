Introduction

The Islamic world at the end of the third century of the Muhammadan Hijra was distinguished by the character of its sciences, philosophy, and literature, which shaped its cultural sphere and society. New international currents, complex doctrinal disagreements, and prolonged debate did not come to rest with any single party nor settle in any single place. How useful it is, then, for the researcher who wishes to study the origins of these differences in depth — to weigh the opinions of the writers and scholars who lived in that distant era, who left scientific traces and novel modes of expression characteristic of their time and of their understanding of philosophical issues, and the extent of their effort in interpreting texts and explaining verses — so that he may then arrive at a reasonable view that brings him closer to reality and truth.

The disputes mentioned were often not confined to the oral discussions formerly raised in salons and scientific centers; they grew into long treatises that appeared on the pages of books and publications. Among the sects that contributed a large share to this field were the *Ismailis*. Yet it is strange that we do not see this group concluding its verbal argument with other groups; instead, another and altogether new kind of debate arose — a debate among

<!-- page 8 -->
those who themselves called for it. In this is clear proof of the intellectual freedom that prevailed in their lives — freedom from the constraints that dominate conservative societies and that impose upon them a balance which obliges them not to be anomalous and to stay away from any departure from inherited tradition.

The first debate, or in more precise terms the first scientific discussion, opened new horizons for the Ismailis and moved them into an atmosphere of broad scientific liberation. It made them a subject of conjecture and talk throughout the Islamic world. This began with the emergence of the book *al-Mahsul* (The Harvest).

This book entered circulation at the beginning of the fourth century AH, or the tenth century AD. It is attributed to the great preacher of Khurasan, *Muhammad ibn Ahmad al-Nasafi*, who was credited with converting the Samanid state in Transoxiana to Ismailism, and who was executed in the year 331 AH (942 AD), as stated in the book *al-Farq bayn al-Firaq* by its author Abd al-Qahir al-Baghdadi. It is very strange that *Hujjat al-Iraqayn*, its greatest exponent and its foremost philosopher *Hamid al-Din Ahmad al-Kirmani*, who discussed the subject and went deeply into its examination, did not mention the author of *al-Mahsul*; rather his discussion focused on *Abu Hatim al-Razi and Abu Ya'qub al-Sijistani*. There must then be fundamental reasons for this omission that justify it, and perhaps the book *al-Mahsul* is wrongly attributed to al-Nasafi.

Whatever the case, the acclaim that the book *al-Mahsul* received among Ismaili intellectuals and scholars of that time prompted Abu Hatim al-Razi, the chief preacher in northwestern Persia, to compose a book in which he discussed what was said in *al-Mahsul*. His book was called *al-Islah*. It is clear that the opinions which al-Razi advanced provoked a reaction in another great preacher, named Abu Ya'qub al-Sijistani, who regarded them as contrary to the truth and to first principles. This is what led him to write a book in which he challenged the views of al-Razi, called *al-Nusra*, in which the author defended

<!-- page 9 -->
*al-Mahsul* and attacked Abu Hatim al-Razi relentlessly. It seems that *al-Nusra* is among al-Sijistani's earliest works, as al-Kirmani himself alludes in his book. Not long afterwards, al-Kirmani reviewed the entire subject in a comprehensive review and composed his precious treatise *al-Riyad* — a judgment between the *two reformers*, as he called them (al-Islah and al-Nusra). With great skill he summarized the opinions that appeared in the three books and subjected them to scholarly criticism. He compared them with the *Law of the Guiding Mission* — or, in other words, with the philosophical and doctrinal theories officially adopted by the Fatimid court in his day. In general, we find that al-Kirmani always prefers al-Razi's theories against al-Sijistani's, and objects to the latter because they do not conform to truth and tend toward deviation. Moreover, he notes that al-Razi overlooked some of the statements that appeared in the book *al-Mahsul*, as we note in the tenth chapter of the book *al-Riyad*.

It is also worth mentioning the scientific ideas these works contain. A philosophical group such as this deserves to be studied, examined, and scrutinized deeply, but unfortunately the books *al-Mahsul* and *al-Nusra* have been lost. As for the book *al-Islah* by al-Razi and *al-Riyad* by al-Kirmani, they are still preserved.

*Kitab al-Riyad*

Al-Kirmani's book *al-Riyad* is considered one of the important works that express the philosophical theories of Ismailism in the fourth century AH. I have obtained two copies of it: the first from India, and the second from Tehran, sent to me by the Orientalist W. Ivanow, on behalf of the Ismaili Society in India (The Ismaili Society), which wished me to investigate and explain the book and to write its footnotes and commentary as one of the working members of this association.

Before entering into details, I must research and review what is contained in the book in terms of opinions, sayings, arguments, and statements, by way of a brief overview of

Al-Razi:

He is Abu Hatim Ahmad ibn Hamdan al-Laythi al-Warsanani al-Razi, and his name in Ismaili circles is "our master (Abu Hatim) the Exalted." He was a preacher of Ismailism in the land of Rayy, then moved to Baghdad and made it his residence. The date of his birth is not known exactly. His activity in the Ismaili da'wa fell during the imamate of Muhammad al-Mahdi Billah (1) (Ubayd Allah), the founder of the Fatimid state in the Maghrib. He was distinguished by the sweetness of his speech and the eloquence for which he is famous. He attracted (al-Marwazi), the prince of Rayy, who was a strict Sunni, and persuaded the princes of those regions to enter into Ismailism, until he became one of the standards of politics and religion, and one of the guides of the Islamic scientific renaissance in the late third and early fourth centuries AH. He took refuge in the theological and philosophical sciences. The great Orientalist Paul Kraus (2) said of him: "He was a leading preacher of the Ismailis and played an important role in political affairs in Tabaristan and Daylam, especially in Rayy and Isfahan, until the chief statesmen responded to him, such as Asfar ibn Shirawayh, Mardawij the commander, and others." In truth, Abu Hatim al-Razi was one of the standards of the scientific renaissance not only within Ismailism, but in the Islamic world more broadly. He contributed to the dissemination of philosophical teachings in all the eastern lands, and especially in the periphery of the general Islamic culture. Despite all this, he

He was a contemporary of Abu Bakr Muhammad ibn Zakariya al-Razi, the famous physician and author of well-known philosophical views in which he departed from many of Aristotle's theories — natural and metaphysical — denying the reconciliation of philosophy and religion, and holding that philosophy is the only way to reform the individual and society. Between the two Razis there were violent and repeated discussions, attended by some scholars and political leaders. Abu Hatim recorded these discussions in his book *A'lam al-Nubuwwa* (Signs of Prophethood). Although he does not name the person he is criticizing, calling him only "the atheist," there is conclusive evidence that this atheist was none other than al-Razi, Abu Bakr.

The great philosopher al-Kirmani (Hujjat al-Iraqayn) has noted in his book *al-Aqwal al-Dhahabiyya* (The Golden Sayings) that discussions about prophethood and legal rituals took place between Abu Bakr al-Razi and Abu Hatim on the island of Rayy in the days of Mardawij, and in his presence. Abu Hatim was able to humble Abu Bakr al-Razi and to refute his views in his book *A'lam al-Nubuwwa* — pages overflowing with charm and marvels — in which he closed off, for the arrogant and the stubborn, every means of evasion and flight.

His most important works are the book *al-Zina* (1) — on the Arabic letters, their derivatives, the reasons for their forms, and the meanings of words. It is a great book on authentic Arabic vocabulary and on the framework within which the Qur'an was articulated and given legal force by the Muslims, and from which Islamic terminology took shape. This book runs to 1200 pages. He dedicated it to the Fatimid imam who succeeded Muhammad al-Mahdi Billah (Ubayd Allah), and to him

Al-Sijistani:

Abu Ya'qub Ishaq al-Sijistani — or (al-Sijzi) — is considered to be at the forefront of those scholars who struggled, labored, and dedicated themselves to establishing cosmic philosophical principles upon doctrinal intellectual foundations, solid scientific bases, and fixed pillars that do not tremble whatever crises may befall them. Indeed, he is among those who sacrificed all they had in order to spread it and to circulate it in other lands. Late in his life he was accused of blasphemy and atheism by the conservative public, and was finally killed for the sake of his faith. Al-Sijistani played an important role in the field of philosophy and rendered significant intellectual services in the third century of the Hijra. His intellectual impact appeared in his disciple Hamid al-Din al-Kirmani (Hujjat al-Iraqayn), who followed his method, called to his teachings, and continued the flow of his springs. If we recognize that al-Kirmani studied philosophy in al-Sijistani's school of thought, we may place al-Sijistani in the first rank among the famous Muslim scholars, thinkers, and philosophers of the world.

He was a contemporary of the esoteric Ismaili da'wa in the Age of Manifestation — that is, the period of the flourishing of the Fatimid state and its emergence as an Islamic state of civilizational, scientific, social, and political stature — although he lived in a country whose people held to a doctrine that differed from his madhhab. He was forced to adopt (taqiyya) as a cover, and to be very cautious in his movements and his preaching. For this reason his life has come down to us with a certain mysterious ambiguity, and one may not be blamed if we cannot come to know his biography fully or relate everything about him. On the other hand, we have received only a little about the preacher al-Khayr al-Nasafi *al-Nakhshabi* the author of *al-Mahsul* — beyond what authors have mentioned of his efforts and of his contact with Nasr ibn Ahmad

the Samanid in Transoxiana, until that Samanid embraced the Ismaili da'wa. We also know little about Abu Hatim al-Razi, the linguist and author of the oldest work in the science of the Arabic language — the book *al-Zina* — and about *Hujjat al-Iraqayn* Hamid al-Din al-Kirmani, despite the survival of most of his writings and works down to us — even though al-Mu'ayyad fi'l-Din Hibatallah al-Shirazi, the friend and companion of Abu al-Ala al-Ma'arri, wrote his biography in his own hand. We cannot really know much about him, and the same may be said of the obscurity surrounding the lives of the senior Ismaili da'wa men — the hujjas and preachers of Algeria, Syria, Iran, Yemen, and elsewhere. Their lives are even more shrouded in mystery, since the books in which they recorded their biographies have been lost, and only a few of them remain.

He is attributed to (Sijistan), a province in the south of Khurasan, of a Persian family. It is said that he was of the family of the Persian hero *Rustam*, and there are those who say that he was of Iraqi origin, and that his grandfather came from Kufa and settled in Sijistan. Some researchers who studied his philosophy claim that he died in 331 AH. But this view does not accord with historical reality, for it is known of al-Sijistani that he was the teacher of al-Kirmani, and al-Kirmani remained alive until the year 411 AH. So at what time, then, did al-Kirmani study the philosophical sciences of the da'wa under him? There is an explicit text in the book *al-Iftikhar* by al-Sijistani himself in which he mentions that he composed it in 360 AH. The book *al-Iftikhar* is in turn mentioned in the book *al-Riyad* by al-Kirmani — that is, he was a preacher in the province of Bukhara during the caliphate of the imam *al-Mu'izz li-Din Allah al-Fatimi*. The meaning of this is that he was a contemporary of the great preacher *Ja'far ibn Mansur al-Yamani*, and of the scholar and jurist the qadi *al-Nu'man ibn Muhammad ibn Hayyun al-Maghribi al-Tamimi*, the chief judge of the Fatimid state, and of other great authors and scholars of the da'wa in that golden age of manifest science. There is no shortage of evidence for al-Sijistani's scholarly value from his books and writings, which

He left behind these works in Arabic, with some of them composed in Persian. Isma'il ibn Abd al-Rasul ibn Mata Khan al-Abini, who died in 1183 AH, recorded them in his collection and catalogue. Al-Biruni also referred to them in his books, and al-Baghdadi mentioned them in his *al-Farq bayn al-Firaq* (The Difference Between the Sects).

As we said, al-Sijistani left behind a prolific body of scholarly writing that ranks among the deepest works in philosophy — more than thirty titles in number. Among the most famous are *Ithbat al-Nubuwwat* (Proofs of Prophecy), *Kashf al-Mahjub* (Unveiling the Veiled) (1), *Tuhfat al-Mustajibin* (Masterpiece of the Respondents) (2), and a third book (3) — which he divided into forty springs (*yanabi'*), and which has come to be regarded as a work whose depth is rarely surpassed. He appears to have composed it for a particular audience: preachers and those occupying the highest ranks of the da'wa, men whose philosophical studies had reached their climax. His reason for dividing it into forty springs goes back to his wish that each spring should interpret one of the forty limits that compose the council of the highest da'wa.

Among the other books of his mentioned by the great traveler and Persian poet Nasir-i Khusraw in his *Zad al-Musafirin* (Provisions for Travelers) are: *The Foundations of the Da'wa*; *The Interpretation of the Laws*; *The Iris of Blessings*, or *The Iris of Survival*; *The Brilliant Message*; the *Kitab al-Iftikhar*; *The Scales*, which is divided into nineteen scales; *The Ladder of Salvation*; *The Victory*; *The Hooks*; *Amusements of Sorrows*; *Secrets of the Return*; *Sermons on Ethics*; *The Stranger on the Meaning of the Elixir*; *Sociable of Hearts*; *Composition of Spirits*; *Security from Confusion*; *Closets of Evidence*; *The Proof*; and others.

In what follows we summarize al-Sijistani's views on theology as expressed across most of his writings.

He holds that the Creator of all creators is an Ancient, Exalted, and Primordial Creator, anterior in the order of being; and that the world of beings and creatures is contingent and originated in time, since otherwise something would necessarily have preceded it — even on the supposition that the world is itself old. The Creator is prior to the world, for it is impossible to attribute might to what is co-eternal with Him, or to ascribe His existence to nothingness or to necessity. He transcends every attribute one might affix to Him: He is not attained by sense, does not fall under the eyes, is not perceived by sight, is not called naturalized, is not subject to conjecture, and is not seen. He is not described by the senses, nor grasped by analogy, nor likened to creatures; He is exalted above any rival, equal, or anything of that kind. Exalted is He above the bewildered conjectures of the imagination concerning His might, and above the understanding that falls short of the character of His kingdom — a kingdom whose greatness is seen without being grasped. He has no likeness and no counterpart; He has no equal and no opposite. The very notion of opposition is against Him, for an opposite would be a rival, one that would point to His identity through what He has created and through His names, in the way His prophets indicate. The intellect has no foothold and no analogy by which to attain His heavens, for any analogy that places the creature alongside its Creator is impossible. He is the cause of every existent, since He is the Creator of creators and the Inventor of inventors, the cause of all beings, the Lord of all things, the Originator, the Perfecter, and the One who brings them to the best of their states. He is not bounded by any thought, nor encompassed by any estimation. He has no names — for names are coined for what exists in His creation and not for attributes, and attributes belong to essences — and the letters of language cannot reach the pronunciation of His name, nor name some of Him by them, since the letters themselves are among His inventions. All the names He has created make Him to be that Ancient, Pre-eternal Source, the Owner of the source of primacy in the order of being.

For from Him the first limit emerged and the first One overflowed, while He remains the Creator of creators, the Originator who reveals, the One who brings forth from nothing, and the Perpetual whose being subsists in His own individuality and steadfastness. The first act of creation was accompanied by the first number, which He made the origin of numbers — just as He made the Intellect the origin of created things and the first articulator of the world of religion. Added to all of this, He acquires none of the attributes; He is not a body, nor in
<!-- page 16 -->
a body; He is not rational of Himself; He is not perceived by the senses; He is not in any image, nor in any form, nor in any substance; and there is nothing in any language by which He may be expressed. He exists — but it is not correct to say that He exists in the manner of those existents that have come into being, nor is His existence inferred from the existence of other existents in the manner that an effect is inferred from its cause. He does not come to be without a cause, and exists only as He must exist; while contingent beings depend upon one another in their existence, some resting upon others, and likewise some of them are not fixed in being and do not endure thereafter.

Al-Sijistani then moves on to the existents in their order and sequence, saying:

The Creator brought nothing into being at the beginning of creation except the Intellect, in whose essence are bounded the images of all created things. The name "Pen" is given to the Intellect, because the pen marks the inscriptions of creation from its beginning. It is also called "the Throne" — that is, the seat of the One who sits upon it, whose Majesty knows He is exalted above being settled upon it. The Intellect is also called (1) the primordial from which creatures emerged — meaning all that exists; and what is imprinted upon it precedes the acceptance of the effects of wisdom in advance of the remaining limits, because of its closeness to that source and its union with it, which is knowledge and command taken in one sense. It is therefore permissible to say that the act of the Intellect precedes its power; and this virtue is found in nothing other than it, for all the limits below it have their powers preceding their acts. This virtue belongs to the Intellect in particular, so that perfection may reside in it. The Intellect is also called "the Pronouncement," because by the Intellect the soul resolves to perceive what it learns and to obtain what is required of it; and it is permissible to say that the Intellect is the decree of God Almighty among His creation. The Intellect is likewise called *al-hayuli* (prime matter), meaning that the Intellect has the strength to receive what is reflected upon it of the images; and it is called "the Sun," meaning that the Intellect perceives the realities.

Then the Soul, which is the second creation, is the one eternalized by the first creation; it is called "Soul" (*nafs*) because it always breathes (*yatanaffas*) in order to draw benefit from it,
<!-- page 17 -->
and the frequency of that breathing is the texture of creation. The Soul is also called "the Tablet," meaning what was separated off from the Intellect; from it the dominion of wisdom passes down upon the Soul, and from the Soul flows what it gives forth, in measure of its purity and refinement. The Soul is called "the King," meaning that the Soul is the property of the Intellect and its content, for in the Soul a virtue has appeared that is revealed by the Intellect and is likewise revealed by the King. The Soul is called "the Follower," meaning the one who recites after the Intellect at the threshold of accepting the effects of the Word; and it is permissible to say that the Soul, by its power, recites the Intellect through its own act. The Soul is also called "Destiny," meaning that what unites with the Soul is among the benefits of the Intellect, since estimation and determination encompass it. The Soul is called "the Image," and the meaning of this is that the Soul is conceived from the very essence of the Intellect; and when one tries to follow it down to its station, its light is true to the Intellect's light — just as the moon draws its light from the sun, and when they meet at one place, the moon's light becomes its own. Of the Intellect and the Soul together it is said in one word: "the Two Principles."

These are some of al-Sijistani's philosophical views on theology, presented in summary and joined to whatever we have been able to recover of his biography. The guide that drove him to undertake these sciences, and his habit of laying out their texts in attractive order and exquisite arrangement, is perhaps what carried him to this depth.

**Hamid al-Din Ahmad al-Kirmani**

A scholarly master whose life history is shrouded in mystery, and a great philosopher who lived in a flourishing scientific age — leaving behind a glorious farewell written across the pages of thought. He undertook deep researches and the most penetrating lines of inquiry, and left to the following generations a body of writings that, at the very least, may be called a precious treasure and an immortal heritage.

The philosophers of the Islamic world consider him the greatest mind produced by the Ismaili school
<!-- page 18 -->
of thought during the era of the Fatimid state. His book *Rahat al-Aql* (Rest of the Mind) (a) is one of those precious works that scarcely finds an equal among the books of the philosophers — or among what remains of their legacy — in strength, durability, and depth. Demand for it was small, its popularity slow, and its readership limited to a special class of brilliant scholars and philosophers capable of mastering it.

The great preacher and Yemeni historian Idris Imad al-Din mentioned him in his book, saying: "He is the foundation of the da'wa upon which it rests and by which it is raised up; through him its beacon was set straight, and by him problems were identified and dilemmas resolved." The Syrian Ismaili preacher Nur al-Din Ahmad described him in his book *Fusul wa Akhbar* (Chapters and Reports), saying: "If the Ismaili da'wa had produced nothing but al-Kirmani, that alone would have been ample pride and glory, and that alone would have been enough."

His impact and stature appeared during the reign of the Fatimid Caliph al-Hakim bi-Amr Allah. His famous title was *Hujjat al-Iraqayn* (the Hujjah of the Two Iraqs), meaning that he had charge of the affairs of the da'wa's cultural centers throughout Persia and Iraq. In Cairo, his standing was that of a Hujjah — one of the twelve hujjahs in charge of managing the affairs of the da'wa, the intellectual vanguard of the world. He was later appointed head of *Dar al-Hikma* (the House of Wisdom) in Cairo, the cultural institution that we may rightly call the first university established in the world.

He travelled in a delegation to Cairo in 408 AH at the request of al-Sadiq al-Ma'mun, then the foremost of the Fatimid state's preachers in the era of al-Hakim bi-Amr Allah — at a moment when religious battles were heating up and new movements were flooding the market of innovation with calls aimed at exaggeration and at deviation from the realities and the foundations of the faith. He gave talks and lectures in *Dar al-Hikma*, and produced a great many

<!-- page 19 -->
researches and books, the most famous of which is *Risalat al-Wa'iza* (the Preacher's Message), composed in reply to al-Hasan al-Farghani, who held that the Ruler al-Hakim was divine by the command of God. Among his other works are the epistles known as *al-Mabasim* (the Good Tidings) and *al-Masabih* (the Lamps), in which he was able to prove the Imamate as a necessary cosmic reality by means of reasoned, compelling arguments — arguments richly furnished with Hebrew, Syriac, and Persian expressions drawn from the books of the prophets of the heavenly utterance. He then took up the Platonic opinions as the basis for his researches and deployed them with a skill no one before him had brought to bear. All of this served as a general call to consolidate the philosophical-intellectual system, to raise its level, and to erase every trace of doubt, controversy, suspicion, and dispute.

In any case, in these lines I will not attempt to introduce the philosopher al-Kirmani merely as one of the Ismaili preachers who played an important role in the field of thought during the reign of the Fatimid Caliph al-Hakim bi-Amr Allah, or merely as one of the philosophers who served the Fatimid da'wa with clear intellectual services. I present him, rather, as a philosopher among the philosophers of the world — one who walked upon the stage of universal philosophy and exerted everything in him to found a philosophical school upon a new intellectual foundation, drawn from the modern, innovative theories of his time and from the mental systems then current. This applies in particular to the theory of the unity of being, which the Second Teacher [al-Farabi] and the Chief Sheikh [Ibn Sina] hold, here expanded by him in exposition and somewhat departing from the general curriculum laid down for the Ismaili preachers — whether those who came before him or those of his own age — who were obliged to follow a well-defined general framework whose limits they could not transgress or cross.

Returning to al-Kirmani's writings, and reading them with careful analysis of what we see in them, he has resolved that there is contradiction, disharmony, and an attempt on the part of some [of the existents] to erase others, yet that these existents persist in spite of this dissonance and this contradiction; for none of them is lost by the presence of an opposite — rather they are all preserved under existence. All of this is identical to the theory of the Second Teacher

<!-- page 20 -->
on creation, in which he says: "The preservation of the continuance of the existence of a thing that does not possess, in itself, a permanence unrelated to any of the contingents — other than the Creator." (1) And Ibn Sina, in the course of speaking of the possible and the necessary among the other existents, gives an account that clarifies the meaning of the continuity of existence among existents, saying: "As for the fact that the contingents can exist in themselves, while not being obligated by the existence of others, this does not contradict their being always present in others." (2) What these three philosophers — the Second Teacher, the Chief Sheikh, and the Hujjah of the Two Iraqs — go to in this regard nearly approaches what the philosopher Descartes said in the modern era: "There is, between the act by which God preserves the world and the act by which He created it, no [real difference]." (3) This is what is known in his philosophy as the theory of *continuous creation*.

In fact, al-Kirmani shared something with the Muslim philosophers, and was influenced by some of them — especially those who took up Greek philosophy. He is distinguished from them, however, by the fact that, when he presented these philosophies, he stamped them with the marks of novelty, wit, and innovation, and was the precursor of the modern European philosophers and the ancient Oriental renewers. Accordingly, one may say that al-Kirmani left behind books and produced ideas that should be looked upon not only as Ismaili intellectual views, but as Islamic philosophical views of a high, public-facing order — looking into the essence of things and into the deep intellectual aspects in a steady, elaborated manner in which genius and brilliance are manifest.

Yes, al-Kirmani spoke within the Neoplatonic systems, and exerted all his efforts to apply some of their materials in his attractive philosophical and rhetorical style; and he worked to prove the matter of Prophethood and the Imamate from both the philosophical and the religious sides.

On this point we can say that he upheld the theory that adherence to the outward Shari'ah leads to scriptural worship, while the further reach of philosophical interpretation and discussion of the boundaries (ḥudūd) is reserved for those permitted ones who have attained a high level in philosophy and science. We find all these features when we look closely at *The Illuminating Message*, *The Lamps in Proving the Imamate*, *Alerting the Guide and the Guided*, and *Rest of the Mind* (*Rāḥat al-ʿAql*). Beyond this, al-Kirmani is a major asset in developing the intellectual system and expanding theoretical discourse — adding new elements, broadening certain topics, laying down the basic rules of interpretation, and treating contradiction and similarity, especially on the subject of the Imamate, which had been the focus of unstable and shifting opinions and theories. For this reason his book *Peace of Mind* defined the rules, origins, ranks, and status of the Imamate, and set new foundations, systems, and order for it.

Al-Kirmani was prominent in the doctrine of the call to existence, in the negation of essence (*aysiyya*) and substance (*laysiyya*), in the doctrine that the divine attributes are subject to absolute negation, in the doctrine of the call to Tawhid, in the doctrine of the call concerning the two principles of origination, in the elaboration of emanation, and in the question of priority between the Imamate and the Prophethood — all in a purely scientific, logical manner. He did not stop there: at the outset he gathered the two first principles — the Active Intellect and the Universal Soul — together with the system of the ten Platonic intellects upheld by the Second Teacher (al-Farabi). He set the world of origination (the world of intellects, the spiritual world) and the corporeal celestial world (the world of stars and planets) alongside the physical world (below the lunar orbit) and the world of religion (the world of the boundaries of religion); he then drew geographical and astronomical diagrams for it, together with terrestrial and corporeal plans that came out highly artistic and creative, showing his ingenuity of expression and his eminence in philosophy.

On returning to *Peace of Mind* and reviewing what is stated in it, you will see that it is full of formulations and arguments concerning the apostolic statements about God and the negation of the divine attributes. Among the positions he stated and supported: that no attribute attaches to Allah; that He is not a body nor in a body; that He neither rationalizes Himself nor senses Himself; that He is not in a form or in a substance; that He has no opposite and no likeness; that no language can express Him; and that He has no rank among existents. This indicates the breadth of his knowledge across the various sciences, especially the rational ones. On this the Mu'tazila and the theologians (*mutakallimūn*) agree, and he agrees with Ibn Rushd. On the *aʿtīq* (ancient) argument for the existence of God we see him agreeing with Descartes, even if they differ in the details and the method of expression. If we add a little further research, we see that he folded into his solutions and ideas some of the views of philosophers who preceded him and of his contemporaries — Plato, Aristotle, al-Kindi, al-Farabi, and Ibn Sina. We note from al-Baqillani, al-Baghdadi, and al-Ghazali that they ruled the Ismailis, by their denial of the attributes, to have fallen into invalidity (*taʿṭīl*). But al-Kirmani responded to this charge, saying: "Outright denial is to direct the particle of negation (*lā*, 'no') against the identity itself — as in saying 'He is not' or 'there is no god.' This is not what the Ismailis say. Their negation is the negation of the attributes alone; the direction of the negative particle *lā* for them is toward the attributes, leaving the identity untouched." However the matter stands, al-Kirmani gave special attention to this subject in his book *Peace of Mind*. And in this book we see him departing from all philosophers, writers, historians, and scientists: instead of dividing his book into the usual books, chapters, and sections that other men of thought and authors employ, he likens his book to a city encircled by seven walls. Whoever enters it must pass through the seven walls, and to pass through them he must face seven gates branching off from each wall — except the seventh wall.
