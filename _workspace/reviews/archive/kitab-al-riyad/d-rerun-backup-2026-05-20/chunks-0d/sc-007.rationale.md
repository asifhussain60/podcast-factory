`ch09-divisions-of-the-world.txt` covers Bāb 7 (On the Judgment Between the Two Disputants in Their Discourse on the Divisions of the World) in its entirety — all seven fusūl as a single coherent episode that closes the anthropological arc Bāb 5 opened. The source slice is 3,823 words, below the extended-tier band but well-suited to single-episode treatment with Phase 0e enrichment. The bāb carries three load-bearing claims for the rest of the treatise: the *threefold-not-fourfold* correction of the world's actual genera (mineral, plant, animal — with the human *within* the animal genus, distinguished by *a kind of conscious awareness*); the *plural-pairing* anthropology that grounds the bāb's moral cosmology (the human, being a species within the animal genus, has *many pairs in many respects* — paired by *what he is satisfied with* with angels, lions, wolves, foxes, crows, hawks, tigers, or monkeys, on the universal principle that *alliance constitutes belonging*, anchored in Quran 5:51); and the closing image of *the child between his parents and the midwife* that articulates the matter-form distinction Bāb 6 secured as the anthropological architecture of the human at the seam of the worlds — *this world* gives the matter, *that world* gives the form. Fasl 1 opens with al-Islah's fourfold cosmology (*four jewels, four structures, four divisions of human beings*) and his microcosm-claim (the human as *the counterpart of the world as a whole, because he gathers all the jewels of the world*), al-Nusra's reframing of pairing as *the state of acceptance* (the dense lower world as pair of the subtle upper, the horse as pair of the human because the horse accepts instruction), and al-Kirmani's structural correction: *world* in al-Islah's mouth refers to the *offspring* of nature, which are three genera, not four; the human shares the animal genus *with a kind of conscious awareness*. Fasl 2 takes apart al-Islah's denial of any intra-cosmic pair on two grounds (logical: species within a genus form each other; metaphysical: the microcosm-claim itself entails resemblance to all cosmic things) and gives the catalogue of bestial pairings — *according to whatever a person is satisfied with, he will have a pair of like kind*. Fasl 3 dismantles al-Nusra's horse-pairing on the structural ground that *acceptance of instruction* picks out too many candidates and confers no priority on the horse, then delivers the bāb's positive teaching: *in truth, the pair of human beings can only be the angels — the prophets, the awliya, and the imams, and those who follow in their footsteps*, with Quran 5:51 (*whoever takes them as allies, he is one of them*) read positively of the prophetic-and-Imamic line. Fasl 4 rereads al-Nusra's Empedoclean invocation (the bestial soul as the horse, the rational soul as the human, the horse's image as the most refined among bestial souls) under the lens of the teaching prophets and Imams as *the mothers of human beings* who give birth to the rational form by their teaching; the verse of *the reassured soul* (Quran 89:27–30) names from the side of revelation what Empedocles named from the side of philosophy. Fasl 5 carries the disputants' competing maps of human-as-microcosm (al-Islah: body ↔ minerals, growth ↔ plants, sense ↔ animals; al-Nusra: body ↔ contracted bodies, growth ↔ dissolved bodies, sense ↔ animal sperm) and al-Kirmani's verdict: al-Islah is locally closer but both are globally incomplete because *the human is not only a counterpart to this world; he is a counterpart to all the worlds*. Fasl 6 (labeled *Chapter Six of Book Seven* in the manuscript) walks through the detailed microcosm-and-macrocosm correspondences — skin around the body as planets around the cosmos; ears, eyes, nose, mouth as openings that run the course of the planets; the four natures as the four cardinal directions; rivers, valleys, waters, mountains, minerals as bones, cartilage, flesh, blood; hair and nails as what grows without feeling — paralleled at the upper register by the human's *intellect, praise, monotheism, divinization, virtue* mirroring *the world of intellect, praise, monotheism, divinization, virtues*; *there is no differentiation between what has been brought into being before him except by rank only*. Fasl 7 (labeled *Chapter Seven of Book Seven*) closes on the tender image of the human as *the child in his coming-to-be between his parents* — *this world* giving the matter, *that world* giving the form, *the midwife* carrying the matter — followed by the closing prayer for guidance through the intercession of Muhammad and the Imams of the pure house. The episode is enriched (per Phase 0e) with three Qur'anic anchors at the head (95:4 *aḥsani taqwīm* for the *best of forms*, 17:70 for the human's *preferred rank*, 5:51 for the *whoever takes them as allies, he is one of them* principle al-Kirmani himself quotes at Fasl 3), a paraphrase in the manner of Imam Ali ibn Abi Talib's sayings on self-knowledge (*in you the great world is folded* / *you think yourself a small body, but in you the great world unfolds*), two hadith of the Prophet ﷺ (Sahih Muslim 2586 on *the believer is the brother of the believer, like one body*, and Bukhari 1358 / Muslim 2658 on *every child born upon the fiṭra, his parents make him*), the Qur'anic *al-nafs al-muṭmaʾinna* verse (89:27–30) anchored to al-Kirmani's own quotation at Fasl 4, and a Du'a-side closing paraphrase in the manner of *al-Sahifa al-Sajjadiyya* (the standing-at-the-seam supplication), preserved alongside al-Kirmani's own corpus pointer to *Rahat al-'Aql* (referenced at Fasl 6 for the full demonstration of the correspondences). There is no thematic seam inside the seven fusūl that would justify a split — Fasl 1 sets the dispute and gives al-Kirmani's threefold correction; Fasl 2 develops plural pairing; Fasl 3 lands the bāb's positive teaching on the angelic pair and the principle of pairing-as-alliance; Fasl 4 rereads Empedocles under the teaching-line lens; Fasl 5–6 carry the microcosm-and-macrocosm correspondence; Fasl 7 closes on the matter-form anthropological image.
