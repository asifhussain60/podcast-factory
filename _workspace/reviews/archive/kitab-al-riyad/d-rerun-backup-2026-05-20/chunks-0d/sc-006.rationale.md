`ch08-motion-stillness-form-and-image.txt` covers Bāb 6 (On Motion, Stillness, and Form) in its entirety — all nine fusūl as a single coherent episode. The source slice is the second-slimmest of any bāb in the treatise (2,340 source words) and is sharper in its dialectical polemic than Bāb 5 — al-Kirmani repeatedly names the disputants' analogies as *built upon a corrupt root* and as *irregular disorder* — but it carries three load-bearing claims for the rest of the dispute: the *contraries-vs-correlatives* distinction that breaks both al-Islah's graded-ladder analogy and al-Nusra's dimensional-resemblance analogy at their shared metaphysical foundation; the principled definition of motion (*motion is that by which a thing's selfsame state changes — by place, by quality, by corruption, by increase, by decrease, or by impossibility — and its occurrence is from the mover which is the principle in the thing in which it inheres, naturally and not by width*); and the apophatic correction at the originative apex (*above the First Effect, there is no kaf, no nun, no motion, and no stillness; what is there is pure ibda and complete causation*) that rereads al-Islah's *kun*-as-cosmological-diagram under *analogical understanding* and refuses al-Islah's *grave error* — the suggestion that *Allah became Creative through ibda*, which is corrected by the structural teaching that *ibda is nothing other than the Creator Himself*. Fasl 1 opens with al-Islah's proportional analogy (motion-stillness gentler than hayula-form, hayula-form gentler than body) and al-Nusra's structural counter (motion divides in three as body divides in length-width-depth, while hayula is undivided like the soul's part-homogeneous unity), then carries al-Kirmani's refusal of both on the foundational ground that motion and stillness are *contraries that cannot coexist in the same place at the same time* while hayula and form are *correlatives whose existence together cannot dispense with the other* — form as agent, al-hayula as the object in which the action occurs. Fasl 2 destroys al-Islah's analogy at its first joint: the soul is not embodied, so density and gentleness (predicates of bodies) do not apply to it, and the graded ladder loses its destination. Fasl 3 destroys al-Nusra's analogy at its first joint: defining the soul's limit by *part-homogeneity* (illustrated with water, fire, air) imports an embodied homogeneity into the soul, which has the nine symptoms that bodies have *not existing in it*. Fasl 4 takes up al-Nusra's three-fold division of motion: body is in fact divisible into nine (al-Kirmani points to his *Ma'alim al-Din* for the demonstration), not three; dimensions are not bodies; and the principled definition of motion is laid down — broad enough to subsume the six modes of natural change, with the mover named as *the principle in the thing in which it inheres, naturally and not by width* (immanent, not external). Fasl 5 carries al-Islah's reading of the First Cause as the divine word *kun* — the two letters (moving *kaf*, stationary *nun*) standing in for body, motion-and-stillness for soul — and al-Kirmani's rereading under *analogical understanding*: the two states of the word *point* (by inference) to *two honorable relations* of the originative apex — its being a *cause* (motion-terminating-in-stillness) and its being *effected* (stillness-whose-end-is-motion). There is no motion or stillness here except by way of analogical understanding; otherwise the First would be a cause only incidentally and would be the agent of His own Essence. Fasl 6 carries al-Islah's expansion (the First Effect is imaginal, the image emanates downward, motion-and-stillness support the body's image in the natural order) and al-Nusra's apophatic correction (which al-Kirmani endorses): at the level of the Self itself, *above the First Effect there is no kaf, no nun, no motion, and no stillness*; what is there is *pure ibda and complete causation*. Fasl 7 names the grave error: al-Islah had implied that *Allah became Creative through ibda* — that Creativity was a property acquired when ibda appeared — which would imply that *something other than Allah* came into being prior to His Creativity (shirk at the level of structure). Al-Kirmani's structural correction: *what is other than Allah Almighty had no existence at all; and even if it had, ibda is nothing other than the Creator Himself*. Fasl 8 records al-Nusra's general approval of al-Islah's argument, with the single correction that no motion or stillness can be affirmed of the First Effect. Fasl 9 closes on the compressed teaching: *contradictions occur in bodies, not in spirits* — and even in bodies, the contradictions are *not in their being-bodies* (where bodies are all the same as receptacles) but *in the images by which what animates them is animated*. The episode is enriched (per Phase 0e) with two Qur'anic anchors at the head — Surat Ya-Sin 36:82 (*kun fa-yakun*, the Qur'anic name of the creative command, which authorizes al-Islah's reading) and Surat al-Shura 42:11 (*laysa ka-mithlihi shay'*, the Qur'anic apophatic limit, which al-Kirmani's *above the First Effect there is no kaf, no nun, no motion, and no stillness* renders in technical vocabulary) — a paraphrase in the manner of Imam Ali ibn Abi Talib's opening sermon in *Nahj al-Balagha* (*He created creation without imagined thought, without need experienced, without motion brought into being, without restlessness of soul*) that names the apophatic teaching from the side of the khutbah, three internal pointers to al-Kirmani's own corpus (*Rahat al-'Aql* for the hayula-and-form architecture, *Ma'alim al-Din* for the body's division into nine sections, *al-Masabih* for the demonstration of the soul's not-embodiment) preserved from al-Kirmani's own references in the source, and a closing prayer-figure in the manner of *al-Sahifa al-Sajjadiyya* (*To You belongs the kun, and to creation belongs the fa-yakun*) that names the apophatic structure as a vocation. There is no thematic seam inside the nine fusūl that would justify a split — fusūl 1–4 dismantle the two analogies on shared metaphysical ground, fusūl 5–7 carry the *kun* analogy through al-Kirmani's rereading under *analogical understanding* and the grave-error correction, fusūl 8–9 close the bāb on the apophatic verdict and the compressed location-of-contrariety teaching. The bāb ends abruptly in the manuscript (al-Nusra's closing line trails off as imperfectly transmitted); the episode does not paper over the abruptness with synthetic closure but lets the architecture stand on its own. Landed at 9,486 words — upper-band for the extended tier, consistent with the seven sibling episodes (6,595 / 6,695 / 6,113 / 6,825 / 7,349 / 8,476 / 8,932), slightly heavier than Bāb 5 to carry the bāb's denser dialectical content.
