`ch07-human-as-fruit-of-the-world.txt` covers Bāb 5 (On the judgment between the two disputants concerning whether the human being is the fruit of the natural world) in its entirety — all seven fusūl as a single coherent episode. The source slice is among the slimmest of any bāb in the treatise (2,655 source words) but holds three of the most weight-bearing claims in the *Kitab al-Riyad*: the doubled anthropology (the human as the fruit of *both* worlds — first perfection from nature, second perfection from the hudud), the cumulative anthropology (the human as the fruit of *all* the worlds, the last in the universes), and the architectural axiom *the last of them was already in the first of them* — the structural pivot on which the recursive emanation-chain of the previous bābs is reread anthropologically. Fasl 1 opens with al-Islah's claim that the human is the fruit of *this whole world* — produced by the long arc of motion-and-stillness, the heads of corporeal bodies, the composites, and the natures from which the form ready to receive the three souls (vegetative, sensitive, rational) is constituted; *for his sake this whole world has been created*. Fasl 2 carries al-Nusra's polemical objection — *to call the human fruit of this world is the doctrine of the Dahriyya* (eternalists) — and al-Kirmani's joint synthesis: both speak truly in one aspect, both speak falsely in another; the human receives his *first perfection* (the physical form) from the natural order and his *second perfection* (the perfected spiritual form abiding through knowledge of tawhid) from the higher world via the hudud, with the citation of Maulana Imam al-Mu'izz li Din Allah in *Ta'wil al-Shari'a* as the authority for *the human is the purpose of the Second Emanation and the end of the action of nature*. Fasl 3 turns al-Nusra's *reductio* against his own answer (if the human were fruit of the higher world, that world would hold him too) and states the doubled claim in its strongest form: *the human is constituted by the meeting of the overflow of the worlds — bodily existence from nature, form from the world of the intellect — through intermediaries*. Fasl 4 takes the middle metaphysical detour: al-Islah's chain of emanations (Soul from Intellect, Nature from Soul, form from hayula) is corrected at its second joint (hayula does not emanate from the Soul — Soul and hayula are the dyad of the first emanation, neither prior to the other), and al-Nusra's heaven-and-earth marriage analogy is shown to refute itself by his own admission that the products existed in potency in *both* terms equally, so the proposed asymmetry of complete-male-heaven and deficient-female-earth does no metaphysical work; the deeper correction lays down the principle that *deficiency is not in essences but in ranks compared and in actions performed on something else* — each rank perfect in the existence proper to it. Fasl 5 corrects al-Nusra's polemical charge that al-Islah failed to address *al-Tamamiyyah* (perfection) being applied to the Creator: the wise teaching that *what is perfect is perfect by virtue of nothing other than itself* entails that *al-Tamamiyyah belongs first to the Creator*, not derivatively to creativity. Fasl 6 corrects the Creator-and-creativity relation: He is the cause and the effect, the perfect and the complete, the names synonymous with respect to additions — one thing under aspects, structurally parallel to Bāb 4's *Intellect is the Word*. Fasl 7 gathers the two final movements — first, the Soul's act is not a *seeking* of a rank she had not attained but the *pouring forth* of a perfection already given (*God enriches her by what is with Him*), and the bāb's culminating anthropological claim emerges: *the human is the fruit of all the worlds, the last in the universes, united in eternity with what has been acquired by time — the last of them was already in the first of them*; second, al-Nusra's claim that order in the cosmos comes from the Intellect as a vehicle of arrangement is corrected to *the Intellect perceives only the truth* — order benefits from the Intellect's truth-seeing without the Intellect being itself ordered, preserving the apophatic structure of the originative apex. The episode is enriched (per Phase 0e) with three Qur'anic anchors (33:72 on the Trust offered to heavens-earth-mountains and undertaken by man, 95:4 on the human as the best of forms, 17:70 on the human's preferred rank), a paraphrase in the manner of Imam Ali's sayings on self-knowledge (*Do you reckon yourself a small thing, when in you the great world is folded?* — the folded-microcosm reading), the verbatim citation of Imam al-Mu'izz li Din Allah in *Ta'wil al-Shari'a* preserved from the source itself (the only place in the bāb where al-Kirmani names a textual authority), and a closing prayer-figure in the manner of *al-Sahifa al-Sajjadiyya* that names the bāb's whole architecture as the believer's vocation. There is no thematic seam inside the seven fusūl that would justify a split; fusūl 1–2 establish the question, fusūl 3–7 dispatch the surrounding metaphysical errors that prevent the doubled-and-cumulative answer from being received, and Fasl 7 returns the bāb to its anthropological climax. Landed at 8,932 words — upper-mid-band for the extended tier, consistent with the six sibling episodes (6,595 / 6,695 / 6,113 / 6,825 / 7,349 / 8,476).
