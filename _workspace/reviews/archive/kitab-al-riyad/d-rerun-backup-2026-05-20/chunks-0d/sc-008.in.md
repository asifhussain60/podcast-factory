Chapter Eight of the Book of al-Riyad

On the Adjudication Between the Two Disputants

(He spoke about this in the chapter on Decree and Destiny.)

Chapter One of Chapter Eight

The author of al-Islah said:

As for saying that judgment (qada) is the prior and destiny (qadar) is the subsequent — this is incorrect, because destiny is before judgment, and the prior is before the subsequent; it is not permissible that the judgment, which is what comes after destiny, be the prior to that which is the subsequent. "Judgment" and "destiny" are two words whose meanings are known from common speech: destiny is estimation, and judgment is detail. There is no detail except after estimation, and the first is before the second by the farthest distance. The proof of this is from the Book of God (peace and blessings be upon him) where He says, "The matter concerning which you consult has been decreed" — meaning, has been separated and finished; and his saying, "When you have decreed the prayer," meaning, when you have finished it. This is like a garment that the tailor estimates: he estimates it before he separates and cuts it. It may be increased or decreased, widened or narrowed; but once it is dismissed — finished — it is over, and no increase

So long as the thing in estimation can be increased, decreased, widened, or narrowed, after that it is not possible to increase it or decrease it.

The author of al-Nusra said:

Judgment is not detail, because the standing of the eye of detail in judgment is not less than the standing of the eye of the separated. As for destiny, it is estimation by what he said — but there is no estimation except by a judge who obliges that estimation. As for the evidence he cited from the Book of God, that judgment is emptying-out, this is not warranted; for the wise have said: when God created the intellect, He emptied of it the [forms of the] worlds, because it is the gathering-place of the forms of the two worlds, and the forms of the worlds had not departed from the intellect — they are one thing, and that is pure emptying-out. But the author of al-Islah has erred in likening destiny and judgment to the garment that the tailor estimates: he made his estimation like destiny and his elaboration like judgment, and this analogy is not straight. He ought to have made the form of the sewing — which is in the very soul of the tailor — like judgment, because it is spiritual; and the estimation by which the tailor estimates the sewing is like destiny in respect of what it represents, and it too is a spiritual matter that has not stepped beyond the boundary of reason — this is the meaning of the form of the originals. Then the elaboration that he separates after estimation is the physical boundary that emerges from the first and the second. If the matter is as we have described, then judgment precedes destiny, just as sewing precedes estimation, and by that sewing the tailor is prepared for the estimation as he estimates. It is true, then, that judgment is the prior and destiny is the subsequent. This is the substance of his saying:

We say:

The interpretation of the two disputants concerning judgment and destiny, in the way each of them has invoked it — even though the meaning of the words "destiny" and "judgment" is, on the showing we have given, an impossible interpretation — in any case yields that judgment and destiny are the prior and the subsequent. This is so because the author of al-Islah committed himself to saying that destiny is estimation, and judgment is detail, and to the analogy he mentioned about the garment and the form of the sewing. By this account, destiny is what was established by potency and could be otherwise, and judgment is what was established in actuality, having gone out from the door of possibility — which is potency — into actuality. If the meaning of destiny and judgment is this, it is impossible to say that the prior and the subsequent are reversed; for neither in actuality, in some respect, nor in any other respect, is anything of the kind made to come out into act except according to what we have established from the evidence. So it proceeds.

Chapter Two of Chapter Eight

**Chapter Twenty-One of Title Eight**

The author of al-Nusrah said:

Section Twenty-Three of Chapter Eight

The author of al-Nusra said:

Allah has permitted the prophets, in the matter of the laws, to abandon their use, in His saying:

"Those who believe and do good deeds will not be blamed for what they have eaten, so long as they fear Allah and believe and do good deeds — then fear and believe — then fear and do good. And Allah loves those who do good." (the verse)

We say:

The verse contains no mention of permission for the prophets to abandon the use of the laws. I do not know how this point escaped him until he released this statement without investigating it. The interpretation of this verse is other than what he has said.

Allah Almighty knew that souls do not attain happiness, nor reach the degree of perfection, nor cross from the limit of potentiality to the limit of actuality, except through work and knowledge — those two acts of worship which cost every human being whatever is required so that he may attain happiness in the two abodes. If souls obtain their perfection and happiness only through these two acts of worship, then any soul that abandons both of them, or even one of them, is far from attaining perfection. This obligation remains binding upon them so long as the person is alive; and so long as he is alive he stands before it, until the time of his transfer — either while it is intact and he passes into Paradise, or, by leaving both or one of them, he is in the Fire, until Allah forgives him, as we have explained in our books.

What has been laid out above is that the only work of souls is the reformation of morals — which is the greatest jihad, as the Messenger of Allah ﷺ said — together with the acquisition of virtues by way of spiritual exercise and knowledge, in proportion to the soul's existence above the body, as the call requires. If the condition of souls in worship is on this analogy, then how can it be permissible to license oneself, or to license the Speaker, or anyone below them in rank, to abandon the work and the goodness of soul that are completed by them and lacking without them? No — the verse has an interpretation other than the first, and it has a specific application to those who summon the preachers to the Oneness of Allah Almighty and to the knowledge of His limits from the side of the Imams (peace be upon them).

The Speaker (peace be upon him), when he knew that his community would differ after him, and that each group would isolate itself in a doctrine framed by their own opinions — by their imperfect minds and their preference of themselves over the community whose guidance lies in the prayers of Allah upon those for whom Allah has not made a [special] light — and when he knew that the preachers would suffer at the hands of the various communities all the severity of their opposition in harming them and in challenging their doctrine: in response to this, our loyal Imams (peace and blessings of Allah be upon them) have licensed the preachers of the call to consider the sciences that are not of their own belief, on conditions. He said:

"Those who believe and do good deeds will not be blamed for what they study, so long as they fear Allah and believe and do good deeds."

That is, those who call to the Oneness of Allah and who know His limits from the side of the trusted Imams (peace be upon them) — those who believe in the respondents when they hold to the rope of Allah and grasp the wilayah of the Imams away from doubts and uncertainties about the religion of Allah, and so reach a degree of perfection and connection — will not be blamed in what they study, meaning in what they read of the sciences that are not their belief, so that those sciences become like a weapon by which they fight opposing positions in establishing the truth, provided they fear Allah and believe and do good deeds. That is one condition: that their path remains straight in obeying Allah Almighty by following His Messenger and believing in his limits, and in establishing both acts of worship in their entirety as he commanded.

"And fear and believe" — this is a second condition, meaning: obey the Messenger in the matter of his commandment and in the one whom he places in his stead, and call to him outwardly and inwardly.

"Then fear and do good" — this is a third condition, meaning: obey the Imam in the matter of the one whom he establishes and assigns, and do good in calling to the Oneness of Allah through his leadership.

When they have done this and observed these conditions, no blame falls upon them in turning to the doctrines and sciences belonging to other peoples; there is no constraint upon them in those religions, [for these] aid them in what they need to resist the enemies, and they aid whoever wishes to be reassured concerning the limits. This is the interpretation of the verse, and it contains no permission to abandon the use of the Shari'ah. Rather, it is a license to consider the [outside] sciences on the aforementioned conditions.

The summary of the matter: every interpretation that leads to declaring permissible what Allah has forbidden, or forbidding what He has permitted, or altering the ranks of the limits of Allah Almighty, and to disregarding the Shari'ah and the rites of the Hanifi religion — calling to the abandonment of work in it and to the acquisition of happiness through refusing to do so — is a corrupt interpretation. Our loyal Imams (peace of Allah Almighty be upon them) are pure of what he has said, and of every interpretation contradicting what the Prophet ﷺ and his companions said.
