## The Third Chapter of the Book of *al-Riyad*

On the judgment among the disputants concerning what is spoken of in regard to soul and nature: whether they are like the First or not.

### Chapter One

The author of *al-Islah* said:

Concerning what the author of *al-Nusra* has established: we do not say that nature and darkness are born of the Second in the way that the Second is born of the First, because that world is the world of nobility and virtue, while this world is the world of darkness (1) and (?) the world, and the meaning of the Second's being born from the First is that the Second resembles the First. Nature does not resemble the condition of *al-Hayuli* (prime matter / hyle).

The author of *al-Nusra* said:

This statement is wrong. If that world were light with no darkness in it, and this world were dark with no light in it, then there would be no link between this world and that. Rather we say that the soul has two extremes: one of them toward the intellect, and it is in the intellect's horizon —

There is some darkness in this side, after the manner of a circle, because nature is in the soul's horizon. We likewise say that nature has two ends: one toward the partial soul, in which there is some light because it lies in the soul's horizon; and the other side facing the elements, which are the darkness in which there is no light. By making these distinctions we speak of the soul, of nature, and of their darkness and their light; and we do not say that this whole world is the world of light with no darkness in it, nor that this whole world is a world of darkness with no light in it — for if it were as he described, there would be no conjunction between the two worlds, nor any mating.

He said:

It is true that the natural beings are born of the soul, even though they do not resemble it, just as the soul is born of the First even though it does not resemble it. It has only fallen into a state of weakness and darkness because of its distance from the First Cause, in order that it may be born from the soul; just as the falling-off in the soul itself occurred because it is beyond the First Cause — I mean beyond what came before it. This is what he said.

We say:

The wording of what the author of *al-Islah* said in his book differs from what the author of *al-Nusra* said. The kernel of his saying, in answer to the statement of the author of *al-Mahsul*, is this: that the Second is born of the First yet is not like the First. He said: I do not say so. Rather we say that the Second is born of the First, and is not like the First, and is similar to it — even though the First is of the highest rank and most honorable station; as with male and female in every species and substance: the male, though higher and nobler than the female, is nevertheless the counterpart of his companion and her like.

Then we say:

The Second is more honorable and lofty than that its relation to its like should be like its relation to the First. The First and the Second are two counterparts, the very source of honor, perfection (1), and elevation. There is no honor above them; God has made them the source of perfect honor, of perfect light,

Yet the Second has manifested *al-Hayuli* with the strength learned from the First, and then it has brought forth from it the image which is nature itself.

To press the speech further with what we might think on our own behalf is not our intention here; the matter rests in the foregoing summary (1).

## Chapter Two of Part Three

The author of *al-Nusra* said:

This saying is wrong, because if that world were light and there were no darkness in it, and this world were dark and there were no light in it, then there would be no link between this world and that world.

We say:

This statement, as it stands, is established against the author of *al-Islah* unless he can be cleared of error. In terms of what he himself has said, it is in fact correct: he is from the world of origination and re-emergence in such a way that what is shared in him befits what is above him by way of being emanated, and what is below him befits him by way of being a body. He has two aspects, neither of them like the First, from which he derives his existence. The Second resembles the First on the one side and does not resemble it on the other: the side from which he resembles it is that he is emanated; the side from which he does not resemble it is that he is a natural body — and that nature, when it was added to the world of origination and resurgence, was a life that proceeds valid from that world without it, and gave it from itself what it has to receive: backwardness, and perception. And when it was added to this world, it was the principle of movement, and it became the very thing in which it exists by way of self-attribution, not by way of accident; and so it became nature — even though, in its existence, it is emanated, or a medium between the two emanations of the First and the Second.

which is the noble luminous limit; and the other part is toward nature, in nature's horizon, in which there is some darkness.

We say:

This is not correct. The proof is that what has sides is what has diameters measured by area. As for the Second — which we have named "soul" by what we explained above — if it is non-corporeal, then it is no body; and in that case one is obliged to say that it has a luminous side that lies in the horizon of the intellect, and a side in which there is some darkness that lies in what borders on the nature apparent to the intellect. The first of these accords with the description of nature already mentioned, and it is impossible for the second [side] to have what the first denies. For its existence originates from the noble proportion within the First — namely, that the First is cause; and we have already said that the First is, in that it exists, and is not in itself a cause. The effect [*ma'lul*] is a cause in addition to what is below it — which is the lower proportion within the First; and the effect, by addition to itself (which is the lower proportion within the First), is from its own intellect: it is a cause that emanates something like itself, since the cause has its like as its effect. And from its own intellect — in that it is *malaisa* (non-being) — it emits what is not like itself, by being an emission from the lower proportion within the First, namely the proportion of being effect; because the proportion of effect is more honorable than the proportion of cause. So the cause obtains the higher [aspect] from what arises about it, except that what lies below the effect (that exists about the cause) testifies to what we have mentioned: that [the foundation] is, in this respect, the parable of the Second in the science of the body. The body, being invisible to the speaker [*natiq*], is all that was originally there; it is present in the speaker, from whom the foundation took its existence. And likewise, not everything that the speaker has is there for the foundation, because the speaker has what the foundation does not share — namely the visible, in which the foundation does not participate. So [we have established] that the foundation is one in which the speaker participates, and that there is nothing in the speaker that the [foundation] does not indicate; and if it does not indicate it,

## Chapter Four of Part Three

The author of *al-Nusra* said:

*al-Hayuli* (prime matter) fell into darkness because of its distance from the First Cause, so that nature might be born from the soul.

We say:

The fact that *al-Hayuli* is not like the First, and the Second is not [made *al-Hayuli*] because of distance from the First in proportion: this is the case of the First's effect, which combines two proportions relative to its being cause — the proportion of being effect, and the existence of something that issues from the proportion that is effect, which is unlike the existence of something that issues from the proportion that is cause; for the cause (1) is more honorable than the effect in every respect, and prior to it in rank and nature. Because that of which it is said to be a cause, and that of which it is said to be an effect, are one and the same self: it acquires these proportions only by way of addition.

## Chapter Six of Part Three

The author of *al-Nusra* said:

The proportion of the birth of nature from the soul is no greater than the generation of the soul from the intellect. The soul is less than nature; so that the soul, although it is permissible for it to be generated from the intellect with [the intellect's] perfection and honor and its closeness to the First Cause of the soul, is itself permissible to give birth to (1) nature — for nature more closely resembles the soul in all its states, in its actions and its powers, than the soul resembles the intellect in its actions and powers. For nature, among the ancients, is labeled as the principle of movement; and some of them said that it is the active force. If you look at nature from these two limits, it is the best fortune and the best installment, because the soul among the early thinkers is the surviving moving essence, and is nevertheless labeled with mind — not in the sense that "mind" is one of the characteristics that delimit the soul. The soul also seriously challenges the intellect: if the limit of the intellect is that it is the first of things, that it is the Creator, that it is the first effect, and that it is the first mediator — then it is not permissible to say that the soul is the first thing, nor that it is creative, nor that it is the first effect. So it is permissible for [the soul] to be born from the intellect with [the intellect's] perfection and honor and closeness to the Word — like the soul that does not bear the limitation of the intellect — and it is not denied that it be permissible for [nature] to be born from the soul, since nature is similar in its limits to the limits of the soul.
