Chapter Nine of the Book of al-Riyad

On Ruling Between the Two
(He spoke about this in the chapter on the Shari'ah of Adam and the guardianship of Nuh, peace be upon him.)

Section One

The author of al-Islah said:

Whoever claims that the first of the Speakers (peace be upon them) had no Shari'ah is in error. He has also erred in counting him among the determined ones (al-muqarrarun), as we have explained, because determination is only by abrogating a Shari'ah or by renewing it; and the first Speaker had no Shari'ah before him to abrogate, so the name of "determined one" does not apply to him by what we have presented. He was the first to draw out the laws of the religion — of command and prohibition, and of the obedience by which one benefits — in the manner that the rest of the Speakers drew out their apparent laws, which are inferred from the subtleties deposited within them in the meanings of the parables. That [process of inference] is what is called interpretation: the manifestation of the words of the wise. Every drawing-out of law pronounced by them (peace be upon them) — every command, prohibition, and obligation of servitude — is a Shari'ah, whether small or great, whether of few rulings or many. Each Speaker has a body of laws in proportion to what he was commanded with, and he does not exceed it. Some of them brought many rulings; some were limited to a few, restricted to the laws of their wording — as we see fasting in the Shari'ah of Musa (peace be upon him) being less than what is in the Shari'ah of 'Isa (peace be upon him); or as in the Shari'ah of 'Isa (peace be upon him), where there is more than what is in the Shari'ah of Muhammad ﷺ. So too the other rulings in the religion decrease according to the difference of their cycles and the measure of what they were commanded with. (1)

No one should think that this great cycle, which was the longest of these cycles, passed without work, and that its people went without benefit — that there were no apparent laws by which they were drawn to its rulings. For if there had been no work, no benefit, no laws, no interpretation, no inner meaning, and no genuine call, then nothing would stand — because the call is built upon the Shari'ah, the interpretation depends on revelation, the inner depends on the outer, and the meaning depends on the parable. If there is no outer, then there is no inner, no call, and no Shari'ah; creation is neglected, and people are left ignorant — because knowledge is paired with work, and that is the root of wisdom among the wise, as they said: "Wisdom is action with knowledge." It is impossible to say that the people of that cycle were in such a state.

Rather, we say:

The first pronouncement requires the name of "Speaker," meaning the one who composes the Shari'ah; otherwise he would not have been counted among the six pronouncements, even granting that it is necessary for him to establish a foundation that calls to the unification of Allah Almighty. If he had no Shari'ah and no call, there would be no laws for those belonging to his cycle to live by, so that those after him might frame their additions upon the framework of the rest of the pronouncements. That is a sick belief. It is not permissible to say that there was no revelation in that cycle and no Shari'ah, because the command and prohibition are inspired by Allah, and the inspiration of Allah's command is itself revelation; and once command, prohibition, and revelation are proven, then the Shari'ah is proven by the manifestations of the laws — which itself indicates that the first Speaker had a Shari'ah, in addition to what indicates the necessary proof, as the wise men have said.

Allah said:

"And recite to them the news of the two sons of Adam in truth, when they offered a sacrifice, and it was accepted from one of them and not from the other." (the verse)

The offering (al-qurban) is one of the laws, and it is the most firmly fixed of the foundations of the Shari'ah. Allah has commanded it in every Shari'ah, and He has made it the root of asceticism and purity. This is why the sacrifice is called a "rite" (mansak), as Allah says:

"And for every nation We have appointed a rite, that they may mention the name of Allah over what He has provided them of the beasts of grazing livestock." (the verse)

"We have appointed a sanctuary, and they are its sanctuary-keepers, so let them not dispute with you" (the verse) — meaning the place to which the sacrifice is brought. In the hadith, "the lame woman drags her leg to the sanctuary" — and the sanctuary itself is the offering. A man was called a "monk" (rahib) when he was known by religion, piety, and purity; and the offering is called "asceticism" because it is the purpose of the duties of religion, for the purpose of closeness to Allah is in every Shari'ah and in all the nations, and among the foundations of the Shari'ah. It is called an "offering" (qurban) because by it they draw close (yataqarrabun) to Allah. By being close to Allah Almighty, it is the first cause of obedience and disobedience, of disbelief and faith, of command and prohibition; and it is the first of the laws of the Shari'ah.

The author of al-Nusra said:

The author of al-Islah argued for the Shari'ah of Adam (peace be upon him) by Allah Almighty's saying: "And recite to them the news of the two sons of Adam in truth, when they offered a sacrifice." But the sacrifice is only one of the laws after the distinction is drawn between determination and Shari'ah, and the most this evidences is no greater than the poetry of the Arabs — a saying with no benefit lying beneath it, and no argument here to confirm what he wished to prove. The interpretation of this verse is not as he supposed. For if the sacrifice in this place referred to nothing other than what appears in the apparent laws — the slaughtering of sheep and camels — then it would have been as he mentioned. But the command of sacrifice in this subject refers to something not found in the apparent laws: the establishing of arguments through those beneath their hands, and the establishing of who will take their place if they depart from the world. This is a story whose apparent meaning is to be interpreted by the wise, even if he reads the story to its end. For Adam (peace be upon him) had no Shari'ah, because none of his sons acted well [in this matter], until Allah sent a raven to scratch the earth, to show him how to bury his brother's corpse. If hiding the dead and burying them had been among the laws, and Adam had a Shari'ah, he would not have hidden the corpse — he would have been obliged to place it in its [prescribed] place, or to act upon it [according to the law]. This is what he said.

We say:

The Shari'ah of Adam (peace be upon him) is proven by what the author of al-Islah argued for, and what the author of al-Nusra mentioned does not undermine it. For the author of al-Islah argued for proving the Shari'ah — which is one of the duties of "speaking" (al-nutq) in the worship of Allah Almighty — by way of knowing the foundations from the apparent meaning of the Book in the verse in which the sacrifice is mentioned, together with the verse that is universally affirmative in the Almighty's saying: "And for every nation We have appointed a rite, that they may mention the name of Allah," and the other verse: "And for every nation We have appointed a sanctuary; they are its sanctuary-keepers." So it is established that the matter is not as the author of al-Nusra opined, and the interpretation of this verse is not as he supposed; nor does it contradict (2) the statement of the author of al-Islah — for the author of al-Islah did not argue for proving the Shari'ah only by the interpretation of this verse, but also by its apparent meaning. So if the statement of the author of al-Islah is confirmed regarding the Shari'ah of Adam (peace be upon him), then the saying of the author of al-Nusra in response does not contradict it: the Shari'ah is fixed as he has established it.

Chapter Three of Title IX

The author of al-Islah then said: The purpose of the sacrifice is the establishing of arguments by those whose hands rule, and by those who stand in their place. Adam must have had something to summon them to, if arguments were to be established and someone were to take his place. The boundaries are set up in order to call to what the Speaker proclaims and to mark out what lies within them — namely, the welfare of those who are summoned to Him in this world and the next, in religion and in worldly affairs. Adam, peace be upon him, must therefore have prescribed a Sharia and set down the ordinances that act as judge. He did not prescribe nothing, nor did he leave unmanifest any of the laws of worship, whether small or large. He needed to establish arguments and to dispatch preachers from among those who take his place, lest something be lost that needs preserving and that the community is supported by. With the affirmation of the author of al-Nusra, by way of interpretation, that Adam established arguments, the Sharia and its ordinances become obligatory — for the Sharia belongs to Adam, peace be upon him.

Chapter Four of Title IX

Then we say: the need for the prophets, peace be upon them, was for the education of the community, so that it might attain happiness in the Hereafter and in this world, and be guided to its interests in both. Adam, peace be upon him, was a prophet; he must therefore have been a teacher and a guide. Chapter Six of Title IX

The interpretation of the verse and its content likewise does not indicate anything of what he claimed. Rather, this is the situation: both the adversary and the wali believed that in the worship of Allah Almighty there is something one offers as sacrifice, through which one draws near to Allah Almighty. But the adversary held that worship is nothing other than what is apparent — only outward works — and that salvation lies in that alone, and is attained by such belief and by sincerity in it toward the one who stands in the place of the Prophet (peace). The wali, however, believed that the worship of Allah Almighty consists in both: in the outward, which is works, and in the inward, which is knowledge — and that salvation is in both together. The one who stands in the place of the Prophet (peace), from the position of the Messenger (peace and blessings of Allah be upon him and his family), is established on that, and they disputed about it.

Chapter Seven of Title IX

It has been mentioned that laws, dispensations, ordinances, and customary practices may increase or diminish according to the era in which a Speaker arises, and according to what the welfare of the community at that time requires. Reports have reached us that washing the dead, embalming, shrouding, and burial are among the things that Adam, peace be upon him, prescribed; and although the reports of this have been repeated as stated, this verse is not among what they have cited about the mention of the raven and the scratching of the earth when the son of Adam was killed. The verse does not run along the line of parables that call for interpretation; its apparent sense is not what is taken according to what we have done. By what we have shown, the Sharia of Adam (peace be upon him) is established beyond dispute.

Chapter Nine of Title IX

For Allah's command to the angels to prostrate — even if it appeared to come not from the side of Adam, peace be upon him — was only effected through him. This is shown by the fact that the command of prostration was either impossible for him, in which case he is not the Chosen One, or it was not impossible for him to be the Chosen One. It has been proven that he is the Chosen One, as Allah says (interpretation of the meaning): "Allah chose Adam and Noah" (verse). If he is the Chosen One, then it is impossible that Allah command those who are below the Chosen One except through the Chosen One. So Allah's command to the angels to prostrate to Adam was effected only through him; and if it was through him, then the Sharia is established for him. So we say:

Chapter XIV of Title IX

The author of *al-Nusra* said:

"We must also know the reason for the existence of sharia, so that it may be sound for us to say that Adam (peace be upon him) had no sharia."

The reason for the obligatoriness of sharia is the neglect of the *hudud* (legal limits), their disruption, and the pursuit of contraries by creation—and their claim that it is what God has not made it to be. This meaning did not appear in the time of Adam, for the world that existed before him and his people were neighbors at the border, and none of them violated a single limit, as they themselves declared when they said, "Will You place in it one who corrupts it and sheds blood, while we glorify You with Your praise and sanctify You?" The reason for the necessity of sharia's existence differs from what the author of *al-Nusra* claimed in saying that the limits are neglected, that they are disrupted, and that creation pursues contraries. Its existence—that is, the prescriptions of the laws—has become a necessary existence, because the souls in human beings each fail to restrain themselves from what is peculiar to them. From these actions, the prescriptions are indispensable that the community uses to silence the sit-down strike of their own claims and by which they secure one another. If it were the case that the separateness of the letters of Adam's name necessitated that Adam have no Sharia, then the fact that these letters are not separate in themselves would oblige him to have a Sharia — for the alif of the name is composed of alif, lam and fa; the dal is composed of dal, alif and lam; the mim is composed of mim, ya, and mim — and likewise for what is signified. If separateness did not entail that he have a Sharia, separateness would have obliged a Sharia; provided that inference of this kind is sound. But it is not, because if it were sound, it would entail that the one charged with the authorship of the Qur'an — namely the Prophet (peace be upon him), Muhammad, whose name is composed of four letters — would be impossible to be the owner of a Sharia, since his name is a compound. And it cannot be that the separateness of the letters of Adam's name proves that he is not the author of the Sharia while the meeting of the letters of the name of the Qa'im (peace be upon him), who is Muhammad,

Chapter Thirty-One of Title IX

The author of al-Islah said:

As for the saying that Noah (peace be upon him) was the first to establish its foundation and was struck like the ship — this has been treated in the chapter on the basics, where there is abundance enough.

We say:

Rather, Noah was singled out by the Ark because he was the first to have intentions, and the first to abrogate laws and to compose a new law. His law was as a ship that he likened to the Flood: by it he destroyed the people of the earth, except those who responded to his law and boarded his ship — that is, established obedience to its foundation — meaning that this law of his held during his lifetime; and when he completed it he entrusted the matter to its foundation and commanded it to establish the call upon his law, and ordered the attendants to obey it. They did not receive the obedience of the servant except through it. Whoever obeyed its foundation was in the ship; and whoever disobeyed it and clung to the first law, do you not see that he was described as having drowned by the Flood — those who worshipped Wadd, Suwa', Yaghuth, Ya'uq, and Nasr? Allah Almighty said: "Because of their sins they were drowned, and were made to enter the Fire."

The author of al-Nusrah said:

If the status of the ship were the status of the apparent law, then those who held fast to the apparent would have been saved — and the inward, true call would have been annulled — provided that what appears from the book of Allah Almighty differs from what the author of al-Islah went to. Likewise, Allah Almighty described the beginning of the command of Noah (peace be upon him): He sent him to his people and called them to his law, and then ordered him to build the Ark. But these

The description applies in Surat Hud as well: even if the Ark stands for the apparent shariʿah, God would have ordered Noah first to construct the Ark. But the actual sequence of the story shows otherwise. Noah composed the apparent law and called the creation to live by it, and only then was he commanded to make the Ark — which signifies the establishment of his guardian (waliyy). As for our position: regarding the statement of the author of al-Islah, who interprets the Ark as a figure of the new shariʿah — because whoever enters under its rulings becomes like one who is aboard a ship, safe from indignity and every calamity, just as the one inside an actual ship is delivered from drowning — this interpretation is possible. As for the author of al-Nusrah, who interprets the Ark as a figure of the daʿwah and the guardian — because whoever enters the serene daʿwah and binds himself to obedience to the foundation has been delivered from drowning in error, has been brought to the unity of God Almighty and to the knowledge of His limits, with the guardian serving as his teacher and holding for him the rank of a refuge — this too has merit. The two interpretations are correct in their own respects: the first concerns the order of this world, and the second concerns the order of the brethren, and neither dispenses with the other, since each of them raises its own head.

## Chapter Thirty-Two of Title IX

As for the claim of the author of al-Nusrah that Noah was the first to establish a guardian by way of interpretation (taʾwil), this is not correct. The prophethood of Adam (peace be upon him) and his shariʿah are established, and it is firmly established that Seth (peace be upon him) was his guardian. The precedent of shariʿah, of the guardian, and of the guardian's appointment did not begin with Noah. It is also a matter of reason that each

speaker (natiq) requires a guardian who benefits from him, just as every moved thing necessarily has a mover — the mover being one, even if many derive their motion from it. And since the speaker in his own age is the source from which all souls draw inspiration through his summons to remain steadfast by performing the two acts of worship, he must necessarily have a one-of-a-kind successor through whom the course of the learner is traced, even though many learners take from him. ## Chapter Thirty-Three of Title IX

The author of al-Nusrah said:

After relating what God Almighty said in the story of the son of Noah — "I will take refuge in a mountain that will protect me from the water," and Noah's reply, "There is no protector today from the command of God" — he claimed that the matter of the guardian was unknown before Noah's time. He argued that the son of Noah did not know the rank of the guardian, and that if Adam had had a guardian, his son would have known of it.

So he said.

We say:

This statement of his contradicts what he himself said in the chapter on the cycles, namely that the cycle of Adam (peace be upon him) was followed by the Imams who succeeded him. I do not know how this slipped past him, and I suspect that the manuscript of his book has been altered and added to — for it is the failing of those who concern themselves with religious matters that they neglect to correct what they write by review and recitation to the people of knowledge and religion. And God is the helper.

The proper interpretation of the prayer of the son of Noah is that this son, though attributed to

He had drawn benefit from Noah, had learned what he knew, and so became, by the strength he derived, his rival; people too imagined that he was the guardian himself. When the command of God Almighty came to make the Ark — that is, the mother of Noah's daʿwah — the office of guardianship was assigned by prophetic warrant to another beside him, in accordance with what God had commanded. Noah called the people who followed him to enter under his command and to obey him. That is when he said, "Do not be among the disbelievers" — meaning, do not stand outside of what others have entered, namely obedience to his foundation (asas). His refusal to enter the Ark, then, was Noah's son's refusal to obey, when Noah (peace be upon him) said, "O my son, ride with us and be not with the disbelievers."

He held back from accepting the commitment of the order, hoping in particular to return to the position he had originally occupied. From what Noah had observed of him by way of fine obedience to his commands when he prayed to him, he came to despair, taken in total, of the rank he had been hoping for — and so he became hypocritical, arrogant, and refused obedience on the basis of what had happened. He said in effect, "The command of God is in this — if you do not restore my presidency to me, it will be voided," and the opposite happened with our guardian, peace be upon him. Noah accordingly counseled him at times and frightened him at others. When the son heard this from him, he openly defied him and said, in effect: "It is not for you to gather the whole presidency into your house, even if it appears as good intention; I have been backed by chieftains who have tightened my hand and helped me to ward off humiliation from me." This is the meaning of his words, "I will take refuge in a mountain that will protect me from the water." The water here is what divides Noah's people against the force that destroys them, and the mountain is what is sustained by men and saints.
