Section V of *Kitab al-Riyad*

On the judgment between the two disputants in the discourse concerning whether the human being is the fruit of the natural world.

Section V, Chapter One

The author of *al-Islah* said:

The human being is the fruit of this entire world, and it is for his sake that this whole world has been created — from its principles and foundations: first, the principles of the simple individuals brought into being from them by motion and stillness; then the individuals that are the heads of corporeal bodies; then the composites that are the generated things of this whole world; and the natures from which is constituted the human form, ready to receive the three souls: the vegetative, the sensitive, and the rational.

The author of al-Nusrah said:

To say that human beings are the fruit of this world is also the doctrine of the Dahriyya (the eternalists), who do not affirm the soul's survival after its separation from bodies, because (1) if the human being were the fruit of this world, this world would have held him and would not let him move on to another world. Both speak truly in one aspect of what each has said, and both speak falsely in another. The aspect in which both speak truly is that each has said of the human being what is rightly his. The aspect in which both speak falsely is in denying what is true of him — that he is the fruit of all the worlds, because the human being is brought forth from between the two worlds. From this world he receives his first perfection, and from that world the second perfection: for the end of the natural world is to give to his existence its first perfection, which is the production of the physical form by the circular bodies, the luminous planets, and the causes that bring the form to the state of accepting what is proper to it — so that the human being has the capacity to receive, and is the fruit beyond which there is nearly nothing further. And the end of that [higher] world is to give what comes before the existence of the natural world: the second perfection, which is the perfecting of the spiritual form (2) — the natural precursor — intrinsically, and abiding [through] the knowledge of the oneness of Allah Almighty (3) by means of all the hudud (peace be upon them), and obedience to them in both acts of worship — in knowledge and in deed. This is its fruit, and it corroborates what we have said in that statement of Maulana Amir al-Mu'izz li Din Allah Ma'add ibn Isma'il (may Allah's prayers be upon him) in the book *Ta'wil al-Shari'a*, where he says that the human being is the purpose of the Second [Emanation] and the end of the action of nature.

Section V, Chapter Three

The author of *al-Islah* said:

The Soul emanates from the First Intellect in completeness; Nature emanates from the Soul in completeness; and form emanates from prime matter (hayula) in completeness — and all of these are complete.

We say:

As for the statement of the author of *al-Islah* that the Soul is the second [emanation] emanating from the First Intellect in completeness, it is correct, in accord with what has been said above. But as for his statement that prime matter (hayula) emanates from the Soul, we have already shown that its existence is not from the Soul — for the Soul is the Second [Emanation] from the First Intellect, and neither of the two is presented to the other as a basis. Neither of them exists abstracted from the other except in the mind as a supposition; outside the mind, they exist only together.

As for the statement of the author of *al-Nusrah* that one cannot suppose the products come only from the pairing of two — one rich in perfection, the other deficient in need — so that there may be a coupling between them, and his likening of this to the heaven and the earth: he himself refuted it by what followed in his own words, namely that there appeared from them the various kinds of metals, forms, plants, and animals, none of which were actually visible in the heaven, in the earth, or in the elemental mothers, because they existed in potency. And this means: if some of these things — I mean the generated things — are not actually visible in the heaven and the earth because they exist in potency, then the heavens and the earth are equal in respect of their being in potency. Neither of them shows any preference over the other in this regard, contrary to [his claim] that between two existing things one must necessarily be complete and the other incomplete. We have shown that deficiency is found in things not in their essences, but in their ranks when they are compared with one another, and in their actions, which do not occur except in something other than the thing that performs them. Each thing's existence is perfect in itself; for if its essence were not perfect in the existence proper to it, no act could proceed from it, either by itself or by another. **Chapter Four of Section Five**

The author of *al-Nusrah* said:

He did not answer what was said at the end of this chapter concerning the name *al-Tamamiyyah* (perfection) being applied to the Creator Almighty, so as to apply it instead to complete creativity.

We say:

The author of *al-Islah* did not mention any of that. The statement he quoted from the wise men in the *Book of al-Islah* — that the perfect creative is a complete creativity, and that what is perfect is perfect by virtue of nothing other than itself — is a saying than which none is more correct. It follows that what is most perfect and complete is the Creator, the Originator Almighty, and that the name *al-Tamamiyyah* belongs first to the Creator (swt). His statement does not contain anything otherwise, and the assertion of the author of *al-Nusrah* on this point is a prejudice against the author of *al-Islah*.

**Chapter Five of Section Five**

The author of *al-Nusrah* said:

We all say that creativity is the effect of the Creator, and that He and the creativity are, from the side of the Creator, one thing — namely the perfect; and the creative through complete creativity is nothing other than the perfect. Nor do we need to say that the Creator is perfect, or that He is glorified and exalted; the Creator and the creative are one thing.

We say:

That is a correct statement, but we are not saying that the Creator is complete creativity and nothing but perfect, because creativity is perfection. What we are saying is that the creative who is the perfection is nothing but perfect, and that the Creator is the creativity, He is the cause and the effect, the perfect and the complete. The names are synonymous with respect to additions, because of the plurality of essences, so the meanings of these names apply to all of them. It is one thing only. We have already mentioned this in this book and in others, so let this suffice for length.

**Chapter Six of Section Five**

The author of *al-Nusrah* said:

If it is established that this world is the act of the Soul, and is nevertheless highly ordered and arranged, with perpetual circular motions, then we know with certainty that by acting she sought to reach the noblest rank before she actually began. For if this were not the case, there would not have been in the world any noble being capable of receiving the effects of the Soul from her. We also know with certainty that human beings are the fruit of the Soul's reaching her end; this is estimated by way of her cause — so understand this.

We say:

We have already said earlier what implies that the universal Soul, in her action, did not seek to reach a rank she had not yet attained. The universal Soul — which is the second — rather pours forth upon this world, not because she is drawn toward it for something not yet hers (for God enriches her by what is with Him), nor by seeking something she has not been given. Rather, her action is to establish wisdom by bringing what is in potency into act, and to make that

**Chapter Seven of Section Five**

As for the saying of the author of *al-Nusrah* — that every agent who performs an act does so in seeking something he wishes to attain, especially if the act is from the agent in motion and in order, with affirmation — this is not a necessary condition for the First Creator, the Originator. For action there is in the way of metaphor, not in motion, nor in order. The First is the last; the beginning of Him is the middle of Him is the end of Him; there is no order in Him and no sequence. Rather, the systems and the orderings in what follows benefit from the lights of what is not in order or system — namely the First Intellect. The Soul too may resemble the Intellect closely: if you look at her, you perceive the powers of things that are not in order or sequence, but you perceive things in a single grasp. This belongs to her if she is united with the Intellect and pushes back the monstrous things of corporeal(2) dark nature.
