## The Second Treatise of the Book of al-Riyad

On the adjudication between the two disputants concerning what they discussed about the intellect, which is the first originated being.

### Chapter One of Part Two

The author of al-Islah said:

We say: Motion and rest are two effects that the first and the second share in common. For when the first was originated, it was united with origination, so that origination produced in it two effects necessarily. We say "two effects" because origination and the originated are two — one of them is origination itself, and the other is the originator itself. The originator and the originated, even if they are two, may be understood as one essence, which is the whole. These two effects exist in the first by potentiality; then the second is emanated from the first, and so the emanation gives the intellect two effects: one of them is the emanation itself, and the other is that in which the two origins are united in the act. One of the two effects is motion, which is higher than the other, which is rest; and the one from which it is, which is the first, is higher than the other, which is the second. The motion of the first is what benefits the second of all the essences in general, and the rest of the second is what receives the benefit of the first of all the essences in general and what is contained within it.

I do not say that motion and rest are produced from the soul by potentiality which it has learned from the intellect; rather I say that they are two effects of the soul by means of the power learned from the intellect in matter and form, so that matter and form became the foundation of this world.

The conclusion of his statement in this chapter is: that motion and rest are two effects shared by the first and the second; that they exist in the first by potentiality; that one of them is higher than the other, namely motion; that motion benefits the first, and rest is the acceptance by the second of what the first conveys; that the soul receives these from the power learned from the intellect in matter and form; and that when the first was originated, it was united with origination, and that origination and the originator are two, even though they are one essence.

The author of al-Nusra said:

This statement contains some confusion, because motion is only on account of seeking. As for the motion of bodies, it is like the motion of fire upward to seek its place, and the motion of earth downward to seek its place, and the motion of souls to seek benefit. When seeking (1) is established (2), then need is established, because seeking arises only from need. It is impossible to say that the intellect needs anything for which it would move with a motion. Even if it were permissible to ascribe motion to the two origins, ascribing it to the soul is more fitting than to the intellect, because the soul needs to benefit from the intellect, moving with the motion of longing and the motion of effect toward cause. If it is established that motion belongs to the soul, then rest belongs to the intellect — for the intellect rests in what it receives of the virtues and lights bestowed upon it by its cause, and it is not a seeker of increase, because its cause has overflowed upon it from the lights and illumination that the intellect cannot know in any further way through motion in seeking it. The intellect has not discharged all that overflowed upon it onto the soul, but only some of what overflowed onto it, so that the soul might dwell upon it and grasp it when it is set forth.

For some seek to move in their seeking. The conclusion of the statement is: that motion arises only from need, that the intellect is not in need of motion, that ascribing motion to the soul is more fitting than to the intellect, that motion belongs to the soul, and rest to the intellect; and that the intellect has not discharged everything it has upon the soul except in part, so that this becomes the cause of the soul's motion in seeking the remainder of what it has.

We say: As for the statement of the author of al-Islah, that motion and rest are effects shared by the first and the second, and that in the first they are by potentiality, and in the second they are by actuality — this is to affirm that the first is at rest by actuality, and moving by potentiality, since it is moving and resting in itself by potentiality, and that the second is moving by actuality, and that the second is moving and resting in it by potentiality, and that the second is at rest by actuality, since it is moving and resting in it, and that the second is moving. This is impossible in several respects.

As for the statement of the author of al-Nusra — that the intellect is not in need, and therefore does not move — it is correct, because motion in all its varieties is only found in natural bodies. Yet the first and second intellects, being non-corporeal beings, cannot be either moving or stationary, on the proof that the body must (1) be a substance, and the need of either of them in existence for the other prevents its existence from being from God Almighty as the first existent. For what exists from God is the first existent in itself, and is nothing other than the very act of which it is the first existent. The Almighty and Most Great — His same act has one subject, not two. If the existence of one of them were not the first existence, it would have to be preceded in existence by another, in which case it would be a thing preceded in existence by another. Nothing precedes the first intellect in its existence, since it is the very act in which origination resides, behind which there is only God, the Creator, the Almighty, whose existence is indicated. Since it is excluded that anything precedes it in its existence, it is invalidated that it should be a body. If

### Chapter Two on Chapter Two

The author of al-Islah said:

We said "two effects" because origination and the originator are two: one of them is origination itself, and the other is the originator itself. Origination and originator, even though they are two, are not one whole; this is an image in which it should not be believed, so it follows that origination and originator are contrary to what he mentioned — namely, that they are two, that origination is the originator, and the originator is origination. They are one only when added to that whose existence began through being his act, and through being the same act. If added to itself, however, it is the principle, namely that it is an object of another whose existence is from it.

### Chapter Three of Part Two

On what the author of al-Islah said, that motion and rest in the first are by potentiality, and in the second by actuality:

We say: What is first in existence is only mere act and the agent of the act. Even if it were permissible for motion and rest to be in the first by potentiality, then the ruling of the second would have to be more honorable than that of the first — because the ruling of what is in actuality is, in honor, not like the ruling of what is by potentiality. And it is impossible for the second to surpass the first in honor. What he mentioned is among the qualities of that which comes later in existence, which requires action for its outward manifestation, and that which already exists. The first intellect is purely intellectual, and so too is the second, and what follows is paradoxical for the substances of the intellects. It is not permissible to believe that what necessarily exists from the House of Origination is specialized in matters that lie outside the higher limits.

### Chapter Four of Chapter Two

The author of al-Islah said:

Motion and rest are effects of the soul by the power learned from the intellect, in matter and form.

What is meant is that the bodies which exist in matter and form are moved by the natural power present within them, which derives from what is outside, namely from the first and second intellects — the motion of longing (1). He made the two effects come about in matter and form. The form is its mover, and that it should itself be movable is impossible. This is what is "learned from the intellect," in the sense that its existence is from it, and it is correct in this manner, and it is the better of the two ascriptions. What does the meaning of what he said amount to?

### Chapter Five of Part Two

The author of al-Nusra said:

Motion belongs to the soul and rest to the intellect, so it is not permissible to believe that these exist within those transcendent limits.

We say in summary:

The world of origination is the totality of before and after, and the pure intellects, which are the highest paradoxical substances of all things, are above any object of supposition that could be ascribed to them — whether motion or rest. For motion in what moves is that in which the thing in which it resides is something other than it,

and possesses an existence of its own. If motion is in what moves, the thing in which it resides is other than it, and it possesses an existence of its own and what it is. Where existence of self exists in the presence of different things, the self is deficient and in need. The second, which he called "soul," from the world of origination, is specialized in perfection and completeness; and that which is in no way deficient and in no way in need cannot be different in itself. If it is excluded that there be in itself something other than itself, and if it is excluded that what is not itself be in it, then it cannot be moved. And if it is excluded that motion be in it — for motion is that in which the thing in which it is located moves away from its first state to a subsequent state, whether in the self or in its states — and that which follows, which he called "soul," whose existence is eternal because of its arising by way of origination from the second, is intellectual; and if it is eternal, then it is impossible for it to be transferred from the state in which it is found first — then it is invalidated that motion should be in it. To speak of rest is like speaking of motion. The first intellect has no rest, nor does the next, which he called "soul," have motion. Rather, what the author of al-Nusra wanted to say is what he said concerning those souls and intellects which are within the House of Nature, where a secondary emanation is emitted, and the being is moved according to what we have explained in our well-known book *Rahat al-'Aql*: that motion and rest are specialized in the world of body and its contents, because they are among the causes of its perfection, not in what is specific to the House of Origination. But he transferred the meaning to confusion in what he said by way of supporting his sheikh, through misconceptions. May God ennoble his standing, and may He gather them and us together with our pure followers (God's prayers be upon them all) (2).

It may be said that movement and stillness are properly attributed only to what is a body or what resides in a body. The First and the Second, of whom we are speaking, are not embodied, so any talk of their movement or stillness is to be set aside, in the sense of movement and stillness that we have already explained in our well-known book *Rahat al-Aql* (Repose of the Intellect).

As for the claim that this is true of what was originated by way of re-emergence: the Second was not creative, and its existence consists in the first emanation, not in any movement.

In every instance where need is invoked for what he mentioned, the movement in question is in a natural thing — as one of the causes of its perfection, like the motion of the heavens, which does not move because of need in itself for something it lacked at the moment of its origination, but rather because that motion *is* its perfection. So too with the movement of the speaker-prophet, and his striving to gather people to worship, and his exertion in matters of governance: this is among the perfections that necessarily belong to him, because by it something proper to him is brought about after he has attained perfection — namely, his rising to the rank of prophetic utterance — even though he already exists.

So the author of *al-Nusra*'s statement that movement for the sake of need is not established is, in this absolute form, not verified. Rather, his ruling about *some* natural things is correct, on the basis that need is inseparable from them, as we have mentioned.

## Chapter Seven of Part Two

The author of *al-Nusra* said:

What does he hold concerning the soul, other than that some… What he meant by this saying is that the First is distinguished by the rank of priority and the highest station in what is not proper to anything other than itself — what he called "soul" — which is the noblest thing that may be attributed to it by way of interpretation.

Otherwise, if the matter were as he described, and each of the *hudud* received its existence from what is above it by way of overflow, then the Prophet (peace and blessings be upon him) would have been wronged: the blessings that reached him from the world of *al-Quds* (Holiness) would have flowed onward, leaving no trace of them upon the *wali* (guardian) and his followers, and he would not have communicated anything of them to anyone — which is plainly invalid. The consequence would be that the *wali* (peace be upon him) would be made doubtful in his perfection and merit, and his standing in the place of the Prophet (peace and blessings be upon him)

## Chapter Eight of Part Two

The author of *al-Nusra* said:

Furthermore, action is the essence of encompassing — it surrounds things — and this is not the condition of what is moveable, for the moveable does not surround things; rather it stands in need of something (1) to abide in a state of stillness, so that by its stillness it may encompass. Even when the intellect moves, it surrounds something; but the soul does not surround itself with what it would benefit from. When we found that an ocean gathers things while remaining itself unmoved and surrounds them — whoever encompasses what he obtains, gaining from its light and virtues — we know that the intellect is utterly still and that no notion of movement attaches to it; whereas the soul only moves in order to reach the threshold of the intellect, and then to abide there, and it does not move in order to surround. By drawing benefit from what it stands in need of encompassing — and so long as the soul is in motion — it does not surround, and it does not attain the virtues and the lights, until it abides by drawing benefit from it, and then it surrounds things. The soul thus moves a movement of subsisting that comes to rest by drawing benefit from the one that is settled. This is what he said.

We say:

It might be said that the intellect is not moving with respect to what does not endure, and not that the intellect, if it is not moving, must therefore be at rest in the way of bodies (which are not free (2) from being either at rest or in motion), because its existence is not like the existence of bodies that are never free from these. The arguments already given are sufficient, and they indicate that movement and stillness do not properly apply to it. Therefore it is required to say that, so long as the soul is in motion, it

## Chapter Nine of Part Two

Then he said:

The soul is moving in a movement of subsisting which comes to rest by drawing benefit from the one that is at rest. He spoke truthfully when he gave a meaning to this; he spoke falsely on the face of what he says. To speak truthfully is to take "soul" here as the natural souls, in which case it is said that the soul is moving, because the natural souls, which exist in potency, move toward acquisition; and his calling it "a movement of subsisting" is correct because such acquisition leads them into subsistence. His saying that "it comes to rest by drawing benefit" is correct, because by acquiring and drawing benefit and hunting down knowledge — virtues both in knowledge and in action — the soul goes out of potency into actuality. As for the one with whom it comes to rest, he is the one from whom the soul already takes its being. The reason he is in error if his statement is carried at face value is that the Second is not a moving thing, and the intellect is not a resting thing in the sense in which we apply rest to bodies; they have no need of movement, stillness, and the rest. One ought, then, to occupy oneself with knowing the levels of the *hudud*, so that the matter of perception is not confused — for things grasped only through their accidents lead to corruption of the essences of souls. God is the light of our intellects by means of these *hudud*, and we are made happy by following the pure stages of the divine sciences and the treasuries of the lights of *al-Quds* (Holiness) — may God's prayers be upon them.
