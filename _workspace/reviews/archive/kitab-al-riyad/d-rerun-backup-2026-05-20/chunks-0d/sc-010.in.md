## Chapter Ten of the Book of Riyad

On what is more befitting in his treatment from the book of al-Mahsul, in the chapter on monotheism and innovation, which is the first of what comes first — to set right what was discussed, and to set right …

### Chapter One of Chapter Ten

We say:

The author of al-Mahsul, may God have mercy on him, exerted himself, exaggerated, and made up his mind concerning the right of the daʿwah and its sons, and concerning the opening of the gates of its landmarks, in what he composed from among the books of those charged with the work whose appearance he deferred. Sheikh Abu Hatim, may God have mercy on him, did not set this right, even though he was capable. Most people supposed that this proceeded from him: he reproached the author of al-Mahsul, made apology for the author of al-Mahsul's emendation of the matter in his book, and so let the matter slip; in turn others overturned the case — and that is corrupt estimation. They acted at a remove from the truth, while the actual situation is otherwise than what they imagined.

The speaker (peace be upon him), when he came to lay things out, knew that the sciences of religion are too vast to be encompassed in the single breath of an imam, or to be memorized in a single utterance, or to be arranged by any number of persons short of the imamate. The imams therefore cooperate with one another on all of the sciences — on awareness of them, on memorization of them — like the twelve sources (ḥujjas), so that each of them takes benefit from those whom God has appointed

They are, in this respect, like one human being whose parts come together, and like the senses, which perceive things in such a way that if one sense misses something, another perceives it without that second perception being a defect for the one that missed it or for the others.

If that is so, then the emendation that Abu Hatim, by God's elevation, did not lift up — what he repaired in the book of al-Mahsul — is no fault on his part, nor is it a deficiency in the author of al-Mahsul. There is no objection against either of them, except what we have already said: namely, that the people of the rank charged with setting right the sciences of religion are like the senses — if one sense misses the perception of something in the world of nature, the other does not miss it, but rather cooperates with it.

The judgment of the truth in religion, and the confirmation of the obligation owed to the Lord of the Worlds, is what it is. Since that is so, when I saw in the book of al-Mahsul something that Abu Hatim was not obligated to address — and that he was not bound to expound and set right of what he had set right and not spoken of, but had passed by — it was incumbent on me to cooperate in discharging the right of religion by stating what is at stake in what I have mentioned. It is not permissible to believe in the chapter on tawḥīd, and on the First Creator, without addressing the other branches — which God help us through by deferring the term — that we have treated in other than this book. So it is not permissible for me to make apology for that, because the souls of religion are all on the same footing. If I make apology for myself, may God accept from us what we draw near to Him with from the guardianship that leads, by the satisfaction of God Almighty, the Lord of the worlds, and gather us together with him — the foremost, the alternates, and the followers, and the latecomer — together with our pure loyalists in the Abode of Holiness, by a noble degree.

If its existence with the intellect would require all this, then its existence is impossible. God is exalted above all multiplicity, additions, and othernesses, and above whatever could become a cause for Him.

Chapter Eleven of Section Ten

The author of *al-Mahsul* said:

Since the image was made manifest in the intellect from the One, the image too is eternal in the intellect, because the intellect has become eternal in its entirety, not in part only.

This is a description of something whose power gives rise to the image projected within it, and it cannot rightly be said that this comes about in accordance with the great limit which already exists — rather, only by the power within it. Since it is difficult, and since Allah Almighty knows that all His servants do not attain the degree by which they reach perfection in knowledge of the limits of Allah and in His tawhid from Allah — among them is in every age one man, perfect and chosen, like the Imam, may Allah's prayers be upon him — by "perfection" I mean to be complete in accepting the overflow taking place in the natural world from the side of the angels who are close to him. Thus he is sovereign over them, raised to His wholeness, and Allah makes obligatory upon them obedience to him, and His love so that they may become part of him, and His compassion for them — and there is no way except by adopting the two acts of worship and fulfilling the truth from them in knowledge and action, both outward and inward, and being keen to preserve them. This is only by remembering death and what follows from it — the paradox of parents, children, relatives, and homeland — and the certainty that no one reaches the promised time except by surplus, and that there is no surplus to be stored except by obedience. Its opportunities are an obligation: fulfilling its right, doing good deeds, and knowledge of the realities of things.
