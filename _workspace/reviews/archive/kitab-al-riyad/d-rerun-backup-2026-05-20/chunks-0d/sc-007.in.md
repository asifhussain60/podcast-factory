Chapter Seven of the Book of al-Riyad.

On the judgment between the two.

So speak of it in the sections on the world.

Section One of Chapter Seven.

The author of al-Islah said:

He says: the entire world is composed of four jewels, four structures, and four divisions of human beings. It stands upon three jewels apart from human beings, and the fourth is human beings. The knotted and the dissolved are all one essence. We do not say that the pair of the human in form is the horse; rather, the horse's pair and its form belong to its own essence — just as gold and silver are a pair, and as ruby and emerald are a pair, and as the horse and its form make pairs among animals: horses, elephants, falcons, lions, and so on. Because they are all animals, it is not permissible to say that the horse is the pair and form of the human being — for the human is alone in possessing the rational soul, and he has no counterpart in any of the parts of the world or their offspring. Rather, he is the counterpart of the world as a whole, because he gathers all the jewels of the world. For this reason he is called the small world (microcosm), because he counterparts the whole that gathers its parts. — 143

We say:

The "whole of human beings" is the result of his saying that the world is divided into four parts. The horse is not in the form of humans, and humans have no counterpart among the parts of the world; rather, the human is the equivalent of the whole world.

The author of al-Nusrah said:

The state of pairing is not merely the state of [biological] sex; the state of pairing is the state of acceptance.

We say:

This dense lower world is the pair of the subtle upper world. If you look between them, you will find a distance beyond the distance between the horse and the human, for the horse is not of the human's species — they are joined only in being of the genus of life, and the horse shares in that. He made this dense world the pair of the spiritual world because of the connection between them in the chapter of bestowal and reception of benefit: the forces of the higher world are connected to this world, and this world receives those forces from the upper world; so he made this world the pair of that world. Likewise the human and the horse — he made the horse the human's pair because the horse accepts instruction from him more than the other animals accept it. The result of his saying that the horse was made a pair for the human is that he accepts instruction from him, and no animal accepts it like that acceptance.

We say:

The investigators in what they discuss have not relied upon sound research — for instance the author of al-Islah, who divided the world into four jewels, four structures, and four sections together with human beings. That is impossible, because the divisions of the world are more than what we have mentioned, as we have said. The word "world" refers to many things, from the center to the ocean: the sphere — 144

and the planets, fire, air, water, earth, and the offspring [of nature], according to what he estimated and inferred. It is clear from his words that he intends the offspring of the world, not the world together with the offspring; for they are three genera, with no fourth, and each of them has many species under it. The first of these is the nearest of the four arcs, the foremost of which is the metals — all that comes to be in a fixed place of the earth without growing in its parts, and which combines what dissolves and what does not, and what breaks and what does not break. The second is the plant — all that grows from the earth, and this combines low growth and trees. The third is the animal — all that moves and has species, combining what flies and what does not fly. The offspring of the world do not exceed that. The human being who is named as the fourth shares in the genus of the animal, with a kind of conscious awareness.

Section Two of Chapter Seven.

He said:

The human has no counterpart and no pair, and the horse has no pair. That is impossible, on two grounds. The first is that the human is a species of the animal genus, and inasmuch as he is a species, he is the pair of the other species of his genus — since all species form each other within the chapter on quality. If human beings are a species, then they are a pair to others among the species. The second is that human beings would not have been likened in their structure, morals, actions, and all their conditions, motions, and natures to all that they encounter in the universe of existence — according to what he mentioned, and on account of which it is said that he is the small world — unless he were a pair to all cosmic things according to occasion and circumstance. If this is the case, then human beings, if they are good, virtuous, wise, religious, knowledgeable, and pure, are paired with the angels on account of the occasion between them; if he is courageous, brave, strong, and helpful (1), his pair is the lions; if he is unjust, cruel, and murderous, then he is paired with the wolves and the deception of foxes; if he is a treacherous thief, he is paired with the crows and the hawks; if he is deceitful, cunning, and overpowering, the tiger is paired with him.

Section Three of Chapter Seven.

As for the author of al-Nusrah, he said that what was correct concerning the meaning of common genus is what he referred back to. He said that the horse was made a pair for human beings because the horse accepts the teaching of human beings. But if it were on the basis of acceptance and instruction that it became his pair, then the horse has no priority over others — for in the animal kingdom there is one who accepts education and refinement and is not the mare; and if the criterion is qualitative, then the mare is not such either, to be a pair. He has a priority over all other animal species, since he has many pairs rather than one pair.

In sum, we say:

In truth, the pair of human beings can only be the angels. What we mean by that is human beings of all virtues — the prophets, the guardians (awliya), and the imams (may the prayers of God be upon them) — and those who follow in their footsteps, establish their obedience, and worship Allah with both modes of worship. Not the others, from among the people who are in the form of human beings but whose souls are themselves the souls of beasts and predators — as we have said and shown, like monkeys, donkeys, foxes, the wild beasts, and the wolves. And those who follow the traces of the Imams (may Allah's prayers be upon them) and above them, from among the prophets and guardians (may Allah's prayers be upon them) — their diligence in obedience, in both modes of worship, and their adherence to command and prohibition distinguishes them from the beasts, so they resemble them. Peace be upon them; they will be saved in their entirety, and none among them except: ("and who will take care of them from among you — 146

Section Four of Chapter Seven.

The author of al-Nusrah said:

He mentioned Bandhaqlis [Empedocles], who said:

The bestial soul looks toward the rational soul out of love; and the rational soul, if what comes back from her is good, will be kind to her with benefit and aid. Her image is desirable, and there is no better image among the bestial souls than the image of the horse, and none more eager to accept the influences of the rational soul than the horse. So he made the horse her pair, and this is clear.

We say:

If the author of al-Nusrah were drawing inference from the Book of Allah, the Lord of the Worlds, and from the words of our followed pure Imams (may Allah's prayers be upon them all) concerning what he wants regarding the command of the soul from the standpoint of interpretation, that would be richer than the words of the Prophet [taken alone in this regard]. If he and his like had lived in our time, they would not have failed to draw from the seas of knowledge of the People of Revelation — knowledge which overflows from the side of God's reservoir on His earth, namely our pure clients (may Allah's prayers be upon them all) — and they would then have spoken on much that they took to be hidden from the senses. They would have spoken from the intellects and their ranks, and from the soul and its principles, and would have come back and believed otherwise: namely, that what Bandhaqlis intended is what he said, not what the author of al-Nusrah went toward in interpreting him — that the bestial soul is the horse and that the rational soul is the human being. If he were to return to the balance of consideration and take the face that is true, then, since the sensual soul, which is bestiality, would accept the ruling of the rational soul if it is not hindered, and the rational soul, if it sees the sensual soul as midwife to — 147

its ruling, would have its judgment kindled toward her by virtue of its own discernment — we say: what he means by "the sensual soul" is the one who is in the form of human beings, the one who would accept education, discipline, and reason — not the horse. And what he means by "the rational soul" is the teachers, who are the prophets, the guardians, and the imams (peace be upon them) and their followers — those who would be kind to those who are in the image of human beings, and teach them their interests in their religion and worldly affairs, and benefit them according to their senses, and raise their grades according to their acceptance — they being in truth the mothers of human beings. Indeed, everyone agrees with what is indicated by the interpretation of the words of Allah Almighty: "the reassured soul — return to your Lord, well-pleased and well-pleasing" (the verse), and we carry his saying on the face that is correct. It is, in the first place, a statement on the face that is not correct: the horse is not more receptive than those who are in the form of humans, nor is its image better than their image; nor are the guardians, imams, and their followers — with the least of their sympathy for those whom they take up — less in worth than some people on account of the training of the horse and its rectification. On the contrary, they are greater and greater and greater still. If this is the case, and there is no clear statement that constitutes any evidence that there is no better image among the bestial souls than the image of the horse — nor was the matter, regarding the existing animal image, more than the goodness of the horse's image — nor is it the case, as he said, that the horse is to be the pair of human beings on the side of education, good image, and invocation — that is impossible.

Chapter Six of Book Seven

We have said that the human being — meaning man — is the counterpart of the worlds because he is the collector of much of what exists between the first creation and the second resurrection. He then became one, parallel in himself to the creative and emancipative world, and the collector of bodies parallel to the physical world. The two worlds, then, are like this, and the world is wholly contained in him.

A body may be of two kinds: gentle — that is, high — and dense — that is, what was. The human body, likewise, is gentle and dense; the gentle is not the highest. In the world there are planets and stars around it; and in the body of the human being, likewise, there is the skin that surrounds the whole body, and so on, covering the bones. There are openings that run the course of the planets, such as the ears, eyes, nose, and mouth. In the human being there is also a moving driver, which is the self; and in the world the four cardinal directions; and in the human being likewise the four natures. In the world there are rivers, valleys, and waters of different tastes, mountains and minerals; and in the human being likewise — as the author of al-Islah has it — there are bones and cartilage, flesh and blood, what flows from him and what comes out of him. In the world there is what grows and develops without feeling; and in the human being likewise there is what grows and develops without feeling, like hair and nails — and just as he grows and increases from his youth to his full stature. In the world there are sensitive animals; and in the human being likewise there are strong sensitive powers. The whole world is composed and integrated, and is supplemented by what exists in him — this is the analogy to the world. All of what we have mentioned is a summary detailed in our book known as "The Repose of the Intellect."

What corresponds to this in the world of creation and emancipation: that world is the world of the intellect, the world of praise and laudation and sanctification, the world of monotheism and divinization, the world of virtues, the world of goodness and merits. The human being — likewise meaning man — is the collector of these virtues: intellect, praise, monotheism, divinization, virtue, existence over others and within oneself.

Chapter Seven of Book Seven

The author of al-Islah said:

The human being is preferred over the world by the noble essence that is not of this world — not in the sense that the essence has been forbidden by his saying that it is "of this world." Rather, he is from that world — I mean the upper world — according to what has been revealed; and even when he is only from that world and not from this world, we say that he is from all the worlds, since he exists in the state of existing between the two worlds — the state of the child in his coming-to-be between his parents. It is not permissible to attribute (1) him to one of them to the exclusion of the other, because he is from both of them, and from both of them he came to be. From this it is permissible to attribute to this world that which has, from it, the course of matter — which is the receptive — and to attribute to that world that which has, from it, the course of form — which is what is received — as in the case of the child, who on one side has his parents (the highest, most honored form) and on the other side the one who carried that matter, the midwife. There are many proofs of this in our book known as "The Repose of the Intellect."

May God Almighty guide us to the abode of His religion, and grant us blessing through the intercession of Muhammad and the Imams of the pure house. May God's blessings be upon them.
