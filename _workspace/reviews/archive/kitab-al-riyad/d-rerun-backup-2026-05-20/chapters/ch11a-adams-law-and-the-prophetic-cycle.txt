# Adam's Law and the Prophetic Cycle

*From the Kitab al-Riyad (ki-taab ar-ri-yaad) of Hamid al-Din Ahmad al-Kirmani (haa-mid ad-deen ah-mad al-kir-maa-nee). This chapter covers the opening half of Bāb 9 (baab) — On the Law of Adam (aa-dam) and the Trusteeship of Nuh (nooh) — comprising the first sixteen fusūl (fu-sool; sections). Bāb 8 closed the doctrinal arc the prior bābs had built on the originative-emanative chain: *qadā* (qa-daa; judgment, decree) and *qadar* (qa-dar; estimation, destiny) are not predicates of natural process but architectural names for *the call's unfolding through history* — the natiq (naa-tiq; speaker-prophet) who carries it forth, the Sharia (sha-ree-ah) that bears it down, the cosmos that receives it, the Qā'im (qaa-im) in whom what is destined waits. Bāb 9 takes the architecture down into the historical-architectural register: of the seven speaker-prophets the Da'wa (daa-wah) names — Adam, Nuh, Ibrahim (Abraham), Musa (moo-saa; Moses), 'Isa (ee-saa; Jesus), Muhammad ﷺ (peace and blessings of Allah be upon him), and the promised Qā'im — *did Adam, the first Speaker, bear a Sharia at all?* The dispute al-Islah (al-is-laah) and al-Nusra (an-nus-rah) carry forward from the lost Kitab al-Mahsul (ki-taab al-mah-sool) finds Adam at its center. Al-Mahsul had counted Adam *not* among the al-muqarrarun (al-mu-qar-ra-roon; the determined ones) — Noah, Abraham, Moses, Jesus, Muhammad ﷺ — and the dispute is over what that exclusion means. Al-Islah argues that the exclusion is *technical*, not denial: Adam was excluded from the list of "determined ones" only because *determination* is *the abrogation or renewal of a prior Sharia*, and Adam, being first, had no prior Sharia to abrogate; he is nonetheless a *Speaker*, and Speakers necessarily bear *laws*. Al-Nusra reads the exclusion as *substantive*: Adam *did not* bring a body of laws — his function was foundational (the call, the prostration, the teaching of the names), not legislative. Across the first sixteen fusūl al-Kirmani builds the bāb's positive teaching that Adam *must* have borne a Sharia, walking through the Qur'anic anchors al-Islah brings forward (the sacrifice of the two sons; the mansak — man-sak; rite — appointed for every nation), the political-architectural ta'wīl (ta-weel) of the Cain–Abel story as a parable of the adversary and the wali (wa-lee; guardian), the angels' prostration as itself an act of Sharia, and the anthropological argument from the human's three souls (rational, irascible, appetitive) that *no human community can stand without the architecture of legal prescription*. The chapter closes on al-Kirmani's six-gateway theory of Sharia's necessity, which carries the bāb's most general teaching that Adam — being one of the humans, sent as their teacher — must have brought *the laws of Islam* under which the bāb's second half (Episode 12) will see Nuh inherit the trusteeship and the architecture of the prophetic cycle unfold toward the Great Resurrection.*

## What this bāb is really asking

The four bābs before Bāb 8 had laid the originative-emanative architecture: the First Intellect, the Soul, the rational souls as second emanation, the human as fruit of all the worlds, the cosmos in its motion and stillness, the divisions by which the small world gathers what is divided in the large. Bāb 8 then turned to the *architecture of unfolding*: under what structure does the chain proceed through history? The answer was the threefold-by-threefold framework of *qadā* and *qadar* — names not of natural process but of *the call's unfolding* — the natiq, the bringing-forth of revelation, the necessary unfolding of events; the promised Qā'im, the Sharia and the parables, the world together with its parts.

Bāb 9 returns the architecture to its historical instances. The Da'wa names seven *speaker-prophets* — the nutaqa (nu-ta-qaa) of the great cycles — Adam, Nuh, Ibrahim, Musa, 'Isa, Muhammad ﷺ, and the promised Qā'im. The chain holds them in sequence; each speaker brings a Sharia; each Sharia is succeeded by the next; and the seventh — *al-qa'im* — closes the cycle of legislation and opens the cycle of *what is destined* to come forth. But the lost Kitab al-Mahsul, on which al-Islah and al-Nusra draw, had divided this list in two: it counted *five* of the seven as *muqarrarun* (determined ones) — Noah, Abraham, Moses, Jesus, Muhammad ﷺ — and put Adam in a different category. The dispute opens on what that division means.

Al-Islah, defending al-Mahsul, says the division is *technical*. The Arabic word *taqrir* (ta-qreer; determination) names *the act of fixing a Sharia by either abrogating the prior one or by renewing it*. Adam, being first, had *no prior* Sharia to abrogate or renew; therefore *the name "muqarrar"* — *the determined one* — does not apply to him. But Adam *is* a Speaker, a *natiq*, one of *al-nutaqa al-sab'a* — *the seven speaker-prophets* — and Speakers, *by the very fact of being Speakers*, bring out *the laws of religion* — *command and prohibition, and the obediences by which one benefits*. Adam therefore had a Sharia, *even if* — *because he was first* — he was not *al-muqarrar*.

Al-Nusra reads the same exclusion the other way. The Kitab al-Mahsul's silence on Adam's Sharia, on his view, is not a technicality but a *substantive* point. Adam's function in the chain was *foundational*: the angels prostrated to him; he taught the names; he was placed in the garden, transgressed, and was sent down; he was the *first vicegerent* on the earth. None of this *requires* legislation. The Qur'an, in the very verse where it lists the speakers to whom *religion was ordained* — *He has ordained for you of the religion what He enjoined upon Noah, and that which We have revealed to you, and what We enjoined upon Abraham, Moses, and Jesus* (Quran 42:13) — *does not include Adam*. Nor does Adam appear in the Qur'an's other lists of those *to whom revelation was sent* (Quran 4:163: *We have revealed to you as We revealed to Noah and the prophets after him*). The omissions, on al-Nusra's reading, are deliberate. Adam was a prophet; he was not a *law-bringer*.

Al-Kirmani's intervention across the bāb's first sixteen fusūl is to refuse the binary the disputants have set up. The bāb's positive teaching is that Adam *must* have borne a Sharia — not because al-Islah's textual proofs are airtight (they are sometimes thin), but because *the very condition of being human* demands it. The human is the figure Bāb 5 named *the fruit of all the worlds*; Bāb 7 named *the small world that gathers what the great world divides*. The human has *three souls* (rational, irascible, appetitive); the three souls each carry their own demands; the demands cannot be met without legal prescription. *No human community can stand without the architecture of Sharia.* Adam was a human; he was sent to humans; he was their *teacher and guide*. The Sharia was the *condition under which* he could be either.

## The Qur'anic horizon — sacrifice, raven, prostration, the rite for every nation

Bāb 9's opening half lives inside seven Qur'anic loci, all of them named by al-Islah or al-Nusra and most reread by al-Kirmani.

The first is the story of the two sons of Adam — the verse al-Islah lifts at Fasl 1:

> And recite to them the news of the two sons of Adam in truth, when they offered a sacrifice, and it was accepted from one of them and not from the other.
> (Quran 5:27; English: Saheeh International.)

*When they offered a sacrifice.* The verse — al-Islah's main scriptural proof — names *al-qurban* (qur-baan; offering) as a *deed of worship* the two sons performed. If the sacrifice is *one of the laws*, then Adam's community had at least *one* law; if even one law, then Adam — as their guide — must have *prescribed* it.

The second is the verse on the mansak — the *rite* — appointed for every nation:

> And for every nation We have appointed a rite — that they may mention the name of Allah over what He has provided them of the beasts of grazing livestock.
> (Quran 22:34; English: Saheeh International.)

*For every nation.* The verse universalizes the law of sacrifice: *every nation* — Adam's included — *has been given a mansak*. Al-Islah pairs the two verses to argue that the architecture of *legislated sacrifice* is the *constant of every Sharia*, and that the constant must reach all the way back to Adam.

The third locus is the verse al-Islah pairs with the second: *We have appointed a sanctuary, and they are its sanctuary-keepers, so let them not dispute with you* (Quran 22:67) — anchoring sacrifice to a place.

The fourth locus is the raven verse al-Nusra adduces at Fasl 5 to argue the other way — that if Cain did not know how to dispose of his brother's body until *a raven scratching the earth* showed him, then Adam had not legislated burial:

> Then Allah sent a raven scratching the ground, to show him how to hide the shame of his brother. He said: Woe to me — am I unable to be like this raven and hide the shame of my brother? — and he became one of the regretful.
> (Quran 5:31; English: Saheeh International.)

The fifth and sixth loci are the verses al-Nusra adduces against Adam's inclusion among the law-bringers:

> He has ordained for you of the religion what He enjoined upon Noah, and that which We have revealed to you, and what We enjoined upon Abraham, Moses, and Jesus — to establish the religion and not be divided in it.
> (Quran 42:13; English: Saheeh International.)

Neither verse lists Adam (also Quran 4:163, *We have revealed to you as We revealed to Noah and the prophets after him*). Al-Nusra reads the silence as scriptural authority for excluding Adam from the law-bringers.

The seventh locus is the verse that decides the dispute in Adam's favor:

> Allah chose Adam and Noah and the family of Abraham and the family of 'Imran over the worlds.
> (Quran 3:33; English: Saheeh International.)

*Allah chose Adam.* The verse names Adam as *al-mukhtar* — the Chosen — at the head of the chain. Al-Islah will use this verse to argue that the command of *the angels' prostration* (Quran 2:34, *Prostrate to Adam*) could only have been *through* Adam; and the *command of the angels' prostration*, being a divine command transmitted *through* a prophet, *is itself an act of Sharia*. The architecture al-Islah builds at Fasl 9–10 turns on this verse.

A line in the manner of Imam Ali ibn Abi Talib (a-lee ibn a-bee taa-lib; the Commander of the Believers, peace be upon him) — preserved in the Da'wa's tradition on the architecture of the speaker-prophets — names the bāb's opening register:

> Adam is the door, Nuh the ship, Ibrahim the friend, Musa the speaker, 'Isa the breath, Muhammad ﷺ the seal, and the Qā'im the gathering — and every door that does not open into a house is no door at all.
> (After the manner of Imam Ali ibn Abi Talib's sayings on the speaker-prophets; trans. condensed.)

*Every door that does not open into a house is no door at all.* Adam, on the Da'wa's reading, is *the door*; the *house* is the community that receives the call; the *opening* of the door requires *the architecture of Sharia*. A door without a house is not a door; a Speaker without a Sharia is not a Speaker. The Alid line names what the bāb spends sixteen fusūl arguing.

## Fasl 1 — Adam the Speaker, not the determined one

Al-Islah opens the bāb by lifting the disputed text from *the Book of the Harvest* (*Kitab al-Mahsul*):

> Whoever claims that the first of the Speakers (peace be upon them) had no Shari'ah is in error. He has also erred in counting him among the determined ones (al-muqarrarun), as we have explained, because determination is only by abrogating a Shari'ah or by renewing it; and the first Speaker had no Shari'ah before him to abrogate, so the name of "determined one" does not apply to him by what we have presented.

The architecture is precise. *Al-muqarrar* — *the determined one* — names *one who fixes a Sharia by either abrogating the prior or renewing it.* Adam, being *the first Speaker*, has *no prior* to abrogate or renew. The name *al-muqarrar* does not apply to him. But *the absence of one technical name* does not entail *the absence of all laws*. Adam was *the first to draw out the laws of the religion — of command and prohibition, and of the obedience by which one benefits — in the manner that the rest of the Speakers drew out their apparent laws*. The Speakers' laws are *not* universal; they are *proportional to what each was commanded with*. *Each Speaker has a body of laws in proportion to what he was commanded with, and he does not exceed it.* Fasting in the Sharia of Musa is *less* than what is in the Sharia of 'Isa; the Sharia of 'Isa carries more than the Sharia of Muhammad ﷺ in some matters; the rulings *increase and decrease according to the difference of the cycles*.

Al-Islah's positive argument at Fasl 1 turns on the architecture of *the call itself*. The Sharia is not an *optional adjunct* to the natiq's role; it is the *structural condition* under which the call can be received. If Adam — the first Speaker — *had no laws*, *no apparent laws, no work, no benefit, no interpretation, no inner meaning, and no genuine call*, then *nothing would stand*. The chain breaks at the first link. *The call is built upon the Shari'ah; the interpretation depends on revelation; the inner depends on the outer; the meaning depends on the parable.* Knowledge is *paired with work* — *Wisdom is action with knowledge*, as the wise men have said. Adam's cycle, being the *longest of the cycles*, cannot have been a cycle in which *nothing was being done*.

The reading is structural rather than scriptural. Even before any Qur'anic citation, al-Islah argues that *the very name "Speaker"* (al-natiq, the one *who composes the Shari'ah*) entails *bringing laws*. *If he had no Shari'ah and no call, there would be no laws for those belonging to his cycle to live by, so that those after him might frame their additions upon the framework of the rest of the pronouncements.* The architecture is *cumulative*: each Speaker's Sharia rests on the framework the prior left. If Adam left no framework, the second Speaker has *nothing to add to*. The chain collapses.

Then comes the scriptural anchor. Allah said: *And recite to them the news of the two sons of Adam in truth, when they offered a sacrifice, and it was accepted from one of them and not from the other* (Quran 5:27). *The offering* — *al-qurban* — *is one of the laws, and it is the most firmly fixed of the foundations of the Shari'ah.* The verse on the mansak — *And for every nation We have appointed a rite, that they may mention the name of Allah over what He has provided them* (Quran 22:34) — universalizes the practice. *Allah has commanded the sacrifice in every Sharia, and He has made it the root of asceticism and purity.* Al-Islah names the etymology: *the offering is called "qurban" because by it they draw close (yataqarrabun — ya-ta-qar-ra-boon) to Allah.* The word's root is *qarub* — *drew near*; the *offering* names *the act by which a soul draws near*. *And by being close to Allah Almighty, it is the first cause of obedience and disobedience, of disbelief and faith, of command and prohibition; and it is the first of the laws of the Shari'ah.*

The architecture of *the first law of religion is the law of drawing close* names the bāb's compressed teaching. *Whatever else religion is, it begins with the act of drawing close; whatever else Sharia is, it begins with the prescription that holds the act of drawing close.* Adam's cycle, being the cycle of *the door's opening* to the architecture of the call, must contain *at minimum* the law of drawing close. The sacrifice of his sons proves it.

## Fasl 2 — Al-Nusra: the verse is parable, not proof

Al-Nusra answers at Fasl 2 by lifting al-Islah's argument and *reframing* it:

> The author of al-Islah argued for the Shari'ah of Adam (peace be upon him) by Allah Almighty's saying: "And recite to them the news of the two sons of Adam in truth, when they offered a sacrifice." But the sacrifice is only one of the laws after the distinction is drawn between determination and Shari'ah, and the most this evidences is no greater than the poetry of the Arabs — a saying with no benefit lying beneath it, and no argument here to confirm what he wished to prove.

The dismissal is sharp. *The poetry of the Arabs* — *a saying with no benefit lying beneath it*. Al-Nusra is challenging al-Islah's reading on hermeneutic grounds: the verse is *not* a transparent report about Adam's community legislating sacrifice; it is a *parable*, and the parable's *apparent surface* (two sons offering animals) is not its *true meaning*. *For if the sacrifice in this place referred to nothing other than what appears in the apparent laws — the slaughtering of sheep and camels — then it would have been as he mentioned.* But the *command of sacrifice* in the verse refers to *something not found in the apparent laws*: *the establishing of arguments through those beneath their hands*, *and the establishing of who will take their place if they depart from the world*.

*The establishing of arguments through those beneath their hands.* Al-Nusra is reading the Cain–Abel story not as a law-bringing event but as a *succession event*: Adam's two sons each *brought their offering* — each *brought a case* — *for who would stand in Adam's place after him*. The verse is about *the architecture of succession*, not about *the institution of legislated sacrifice*.

And then al-Nusra brings forward the raven:

> For Adam (peace be upon him) had no Shari'ah, because none of his sons acted well in this matter, until Allah sent a raven to scratch the earth, to show him how to bury his brother's corpse. If hiding the dead and burying them had been among the laws, and Adam had a Shari'ah, he would not have hidden the corpse — he would have been obliged to place it in its prescribed place, or to act upon it according to the law.

The argument is from the *missing knowledge*. If Adam had legislated burial, Cain would have *known* what to do with Abel's body. The fact that Cain *had to be shown* by a raven proves Adam had not legislated. *This is what he said.*

Al-Kirmani's reply at Fasl 2 begins by gathering al-Islah's strength back into the argument. Al-Islah did not *only* argue from the inner reading of the verse; he argued *also* from the apparent reading — *the offering, plainly named, is plainly a law*. Even granting al-Nusra's parabolic reading (the succession dispute), the *apparent* meaning still stands: a sacrifice is reported, and a sacrifice *is* a law. *It is permissible that the apparent meaning hold true at the same time that the inner meaning holds true* — *especially since this verse does not belong to the kind of parabolic discourse that calls for interpretation and the setting aside of its apparent sense.* The verse can carry *both* meanings — the architectural ta'wīl of succession *and* the surface ratification of legislated sacrifice. *If that is so, then everything apparent in the verse stands, and the law of Adam (peace be upon him) is thereby established.*

The hermeneutic principle is important and will return throughout the bāb. *Apparent* and *inner* (ẓāhir-bāṭin, thaa-hir-baa-tin) do not *compete* in the Da'wa's reading. The inner *holds inside* the outer; the outer *carries* the inner. Al-Nusra's parabolic reading does not *cancel* the surface law; it *adds* the architectural depth beneath it. Both stand together.

## Fasl 3 — The argument from succession completes itself

At Fasl 3 al-Islah extends the argument from al-Nusra's *own* concession. Even granting al-Nusra's reading — that the sacrifice was for *establishing arguments through those beneath their hands* and *establishing who would take their place* — *Adam still must have had something to summon them to.* *The boundaries are set up in order to call to what the Speaker proclaims and to mark out what lies within them — namely, the welfare of those who are summoned to Him in this world and the next, in religion and in worldly affairs.* If Adam was *establishing arguments and dispatching preachers from among those who take his place*, then *he must have had what was being argued for* — namely, *a Sharia and ordinances that act as judge*. Even on al-Nusra's parabolic reading, the architecture of *what is being passed on* requires *something to be passed on*. *With the affirmation of the author of al-Nusra, by way of interpretation, that Adam established arguments, the Sharia and its ordinances become obligatory — for the Sharia belongs to Adam, peace be upon him.*

The move is a classic dialectical reversal: *if your alternative reading were true, it would still establish my conclusion*. Al-Nusra cannot use the *succession* reading to *deny* the Sharia without removing *the very content of the succession*. The succession passes on *what* — if not a Sharia? The bāb has, at Fasl 3, closed the dialectical loop on the first reading.

## Fasl 4 — The prophet must be a teacher; the teacher must teach laws

Al-Islah turns at Fasl 4 to a different argument — one that bypasses scriptural interpretation entirely and reaches into the *nature* of prophecy:

> The need for the prophets, peace be upon them, was for the education of the community, so that it might attain happiness in the Hereafter and in this world, and be guided to its interests in both. Adam, peace be upon him, was a prophet; he must therefore have been a teacher and a guide. If he had to be a teacher and guide, he had to bring what would teach his community what makes them happy and what guides them to their interests. If such teaching had to exist, it could only be the laws of worship — which is the Sharia. Therefore the Sharia belongs to Adam, peace be upon him, immutably.

The syllogism is clean. *Prophet ⊃ teacher; teacher ⊃ content-to-teach; content-to-teach (for prophet) = the laws of worship; therefore prophet ⊃ Sharia.* Adam was a prophet; therefore Adam had a Sharia. The Qur'anic anchor: *Mankind was one community, then Allah sent the prophets as bearers of good tidings and as warners, and sent down with them the Book in truth to judge between people in their differences* (Quran 2:213). *If he was a prophet, then Allah sent him only to preach, to warn, and to judge with good news and warning; and judgment is only effected through ordinances and obligations, which is the Sharia. So it is established.*

The architecture is straightforward but consequential. Al-Islah's argument at Fasl 4 dissolves the disputants' question into a question about the *nature* of prophecy as such. The dispute is no longer about *which Qur'anic verses prove or disprove Adam's Sharia*; it is about *what it means to be a prophet*. If prophecy is *intrinsically* tied to *guidance through laws*, then *being a prophet* and *having a Sharia* are not separable. To deny one is to deny the other. And no party denies that Adam was a prophet.

## Fasl 5 — Al-Nusra's raven: the case for ignorance

At Fasl 5 al-Nusra returns to the raven verse and makes his strongest move. *Had the author of al-Islah read this passage to its end, he would have said that Adam did not belong to such a station, because his son did not know how to deal with his brother and did not know until Allah sent a raven scratching the earth to show him how he should bury his brother. If burying the dead were among the laws — even if Adam had been endowed with Sharia — it would still be law; and if Adam had such a Sharia, he would have transmitted it to his sons. So this statement is one that issues from someone other than a careful searcher or examiner.*

The argument's force is in the *transmission* premise. If Adam had a Sharia, *he would have transmitted it* — first to his sons, who lived with him. If burial was a law, Cain would have *received* the law. The fact that Cain *did not know* how to dispose of Abel's body is therefore evidence that *no transmission occurred*, which is itself evidence that *no Sharia existed* to transmit.

Al-Islah's reply gathers two distinct moves. First, the *fallacy*-charge: al-Nusra is using the *same kind* of fallacious reading on the raven verse that he had used on the sacrifice verse. There, the surface reading was supplemented (not contradicted) by the inner reading; here, the inner reading must again be admitted. Second — and decisive — *the verse does not actually say what al-Nusra wants it to say*. *The verse offers no indication that the son of Adam did not carve open the carcass of a crow in the ground when he saw it, and saw his brother, and buried him — so that the argument was actually established before him.* The Qur'anic phrasing — *Then Allah sent a raven scratching the ground, to show him how to hide the shame of his brother. He said: Woe to me! Am I unable to be like this raven and hide the shame of my brother? — and he became one of the regretful* — *names the raven*, names the recognition (*woe to me*), names the regret. *It does not say Cain did not know how to bury.* The text is silent on what Cain previously knew; *all that is mentioned is that he saw the raven*. The raven is the *trigger of the regret*, not the *first instance of burial-knowledge*.

The reading is delicate but textually sound. *Am I unable to be like this raven?* is a cry of recognition — the recognition that what one ought to do one had failed to do. Cain's failure was moral, not cognitive: failure to enact what he knew he ought to do.

## Fasl 6 — The esoteric reading: the wali, the adversary, and the dispute over how to worship

At Fasl 6 al-Kirmani turns to the *inner* reading of the Cain–Abel story — the ta'wīl that al-Nusra had gestured toward (the sacrifice as *succession dispute*) but had not developed. The bāb spends an extended passage on this reading, which is among the densest and most explicitly partisan in the whole treatise.

The architecture is set out plainly. *Both the adversary and the wali believed that in the worship of Allah Almighty there is something one offers as sacrifice, through which one draws near to Allah Almighty.* The dispute between the two sons of Adam — Cain and Abel — is, on the inner reading, *not* a dispute about *whether* to worship Allah, but about *how* to worship Him:

> The adversary held that worship is nothing other than what is apparent — only outward works — and that salvation lies in that alone, and is attained by such belief and by sincerity in it toward the one who stands in the place of the Prophet. The wali, however, believed that the worship of Allah Almighty consists in both: in the outward, which is works, and in the inward, which is knowledge — and that salvation is in both together.

*The outward, which is works, and the inward, which is knowledge.* The dispute is the dispute of *the two acts of worship* — the architecture Bāb 4 named and Bāb 8 closed on. The *adversary* holds the *outward only* (works without knowledge); the *wali* holds *both* (works *and* knowledge). The Speaker — *the one in the place of God* — is *satisfied* with the wali's belief because the wali holds the architecture *whole*. The verse *And recite to them the news of the two sons of Adam in truth, when they offered a sacrifice, and it was accepted from one of them and not from the other* (Quran 5:27) names *the moment of the dispute being brought before the Speaker*: *each of them brought forward what he believed would draw him near to Allah Almighty, so that the Messenger of Allah might accept from one of them*. The Speaker *accepts the wali's belief* — *the one who holds both acts of worship together* — and *does not accept the adversary's*.

The political register of the reading is not concealed. The Speaker — *the one in the place of God* — sees the wali as *the one who would stand in his place after him*. This is *the architecture of succession* under which the *wali* (the figure who *holds the architecture whole*) is *appointed* as *the next in the chain*, and the *adversary* (the figure who *holds only the outward*) is *not*. The adversary recognizes the appointment and is *unwilling to accept it*: *the adversary said to him: "I will kill you, to drive you back from this belief which he did not accept from you, and from the station."*

The wali's reply, in the Qur'anic words, is the architecture of *fidelity under threat*:

> Allah accepts only from those who fear Him… If you stretch out your hand to me to kill me, I will not stretch out my hand to you to kill you; I fear Allah, Lord of the worlds.
> (Quran 5:27–28; English: Saheeh International.)

*I will not stretch out my hand to you.* The wali, even under the threat of being killed, *holds the architecture* — *I am under the command of the Speaker who stands in the place of God, who is the master of the Cycle, the Lord of the Imams; I am under his obedience, and I fear Him, and I will not do what you ask of me.* The reading is *recognizably Alid*: the wali is *the figure who refuses to take up the sword against the one who would kill him*, holding the architecture even under the cost of death. The adversary's *killing of the wali* — Cain's killing of Abel — is the *first instance* of *the pattern* the bāb names: *the adversary's overcoming of the wali by force* in the politics of every cycle.

The reading is doubled. The first adversary kills the wali; the *second adversary* — *the one to whom things did not settle, were not held in check* — *prevails over* the first and *consolidates the position*. The Speaker's *raven*, sent in the verse to *show him how to dispose of what he had brought upon himself*, is — on the inner reading — *a man from among the companions* of the Speaker: *Muhammad ibn Abi Bakr* (mu-ham-mad ibn a-bee bakr), *among those specially chosen for the guardianship from the standpoint of religion, and from the opposite standpoint by lineage*. The reading is *explicitly historical*: the figure named is a son of Abu Bakr (a-boo bakr) who *grew up in the household of the Commander of the Believers* and was *killed in Egypt* in 38 AH. The raven's *scratching* — the *showing how* — is, on the Da'wa's reading, *the Speaker's sending of a special messenger to recall the adversary to his pledge*, *to alert him to his crime and bring him back to abide by his pledge*. The adversary's *regret* — *Woe to me! Am I unable to be like this raven?* — is *the regret of the figure who, having killed the wali, sees in Muhammad ibn Abi Bakr* a *figure of perfect fidelity to the wali* and *recognizes what he had failed to be*.

The ta'wīl is politically charged; it reads the Qur'anic story of Cain and Abel as a prototype of every cycle's succession crisis. The pattern that runs through *all* the prophetic cycles is the pattern of *the wali appointed, the adversary refusing, the killing, the regret, the raven*. The bāb is reading the first cycle (Adam's) in the architecture of the seventh (Muhammad's), and finding *the same architecture under every door of the cycle*.

## Fasl 7 — The reports of Adam legislating burial

At Fasl 7 al-Kirmani returns to the surface argument and finishes it:

> It has been mentioned that laws, dispensations, ordinances, and customary practices may increase or diminish according to the era in which a Speaker arises, and according to what the welfare of the community at that time requires. Reports have reached us that washing the dead, embalming, shrouding, and burial are among the things that Adam (peace be upon him) prescribed; and although the reports of this have been repeated as stated, this verse is not among what they have cited about the mention of the raven and the scratching of the earth when the son of Adam was killed.

*The reports of Adam's prescribing washing, embalming, shrouding, and burial.* The argument from *tradition* — that authoritative reports preserve burial as a *legislated practice* going back to Adam — *does not depend* on the raven verse. The raven verse is *one report among many*; the others *uniformly* attribute burial-legislation to Adam. *By what we have shown, the Sharia of Adam (peace be upon him) is established beyond dispute.*

## Fasl 8 — One ordinance replaced does not invalidate the architecture

At Fasl 8 al-Kirmani makes a structural point that anticipates objections about *changing burial practices*. Granting that *burying in the ground* is *one* of several legitimate ways to dispose of the dead — *burning by fire, casting into water, carrying to a cave or to the wilderness, burying in the ground, or feeding to the beasts* — *the elevation of one ordinance and the substitution of another does not invalidate the architecture of Sharia as a whole*. *Greater things grow alongside it — purity, prayer, zakat (za-kaat), and the rest of the acts of worship.* If one practice may be replaced by another within the architecture, the *replacement* is itself *evidence of the architecture* — *one cannot replace what does not exist*. *And if he does not press for that, then the Sharia of Adam (peace be upon him) is established.*

The principle is general and important for the bāb. *Sharia* is not a *fixed list of practices*; it is *an architecture under which practices stand*. Practices may vary across cycles (Musa's fasting differs from 'Isa's; 'Isa's some matters differ from Muhammad's); the architecture is the same. To argue from *the variability of one practice* to *the absence of Sharia* is to *mistake the architecture for the practices*.

## Fasl 9 — The angels' prostration: an act of Sharia

At Fasl 9 al-Nusra brings a third argument against Adam's Sharia — the angels' prostration:

> Adam (peace be upon him) was not the bearer of a Sharia; for had he been the bearer of a Sharia, Allah would not have commanded the angels to prostrate to him through it.

The argument is that *the angels' prostration to Adam* was *outside* Sharia — a *separate* command — and that its being outside Sharia is *consistent with* Adam's having no Sharia.

Al-Kirmani's reply turns the argument on its head. *Allah's command to the angels to prostrate — even if it appeared to come not from the side of Adam — was only effected through him.* The architecture of *the Chosen one* (al-mukhtar) is decisive. *Allah chose Adam and Noah and the family of Abraham and the family of 'Imran over the worlds* (Quran 3:33) names Adam as al-mukhtar. *And if he is the Chosen one, then it is impossible that Allah command those who are below the Chosen one except through the Chosen one.* The command's *path* is *through* Adam. *And if it was through him, then the Sharia is established for him.* The angels' prostration is therefore *not* outside Sharia; it is *Adam's first act of Sharia* — *the command being transmitted through him to those beneath him*.

The argument is elegant and consequential. *Sharia* is named at Fasl 1 as *the laws of religion — command and prohibition, and the obediences by which one benefits*. *The angels' prostration* is *a command transmitted through Adam* — *a command, and an obedience by which the angels benefit*. By the very definition Fasl 1 gave, *the angels' prostration is an act of Sharia*. The fact that *Adam transmitted it* makes him *the bearer of the Sharia*.

## Fasl 10 — The Children of Israel parallel

At Fasl 10 al-Kirmani gives a structural parallel that consolidates the Fasl 9 reading:

> Then Allah commanded us to prostrate to Adam (peace be upon him) by His saying, "Prostrate to Adam," just as He commanded the Children of Israel by saying, "O Children of Israel, remember My favor with which I blessed you… and fulfill My covenant, and I will fulfill your covenant with Me, and fear Me. And believe in what I have sent down confirming what you already have, and be not the first to disbelieve in it… And establish the prayer, give the zakat, and bow with those who bow."
> (Quran 2:40–43; English: Saheeh International, condensed.)

The parallel is precise. *O Children of Israel, remember My favor* is *outwardly* a direct address from Allah to the Children of Israel. *It nevertheless remains the case that the command of God reached the Children of Israel by way of Muhammad ﷺ.* The architecture of *the direct-address-with-mediated-transmission* is the architecture *every* prophetic command obeys: Allah commands; the command *passes through* the chosen one; the recipient *receives* the command *via* the chosen one. *Prostrate to Adam* is *the same architecture in the first cycle* as *O Children of Israel, remember My favor* is in the seventh. *And if it could be said that Muhammad has no law applicable to them — the verse concerns God's command to the Children of Israel, which indicates that the command came by way of him*; *for God's command to the Children of Israel, although it is outwardly devoid of any mention of an intermediary, is in fact only by way of Muhammad ﷺ.* *And if that is impossible, then Adam is the author of a Sharia, and the Sharia is established for him.*

The argument from parallel transmission seals the angels' prostration as an act of Sharia. The same structure holds in the seventh cycle; the same structure must hold in the first.

## Fasl 11 — Are the angels themselves bound by Sharia?

At Fasl 11 al-Nusra brings a counter-argument that aims at the architecture from the side of the *recipients*:

> The angels are not assigned obligations and are not bound by Sharia, because they do not disobey God in the twinkling of an eye; rather, they offer the praise of their Lord and do what they are commanded.

If the angels are *not bound* by Sharia, then their prostration *cannot* be a *Shariatic* act. Al-Nusra is invoking the Qur'anic image (Quran 21:20, *They exalt Him night and day and they do not slacken*; Quran 66:6, *do not disobey Allah in what He commands them*) of the angels as *non-disobeying executors*.

Al-Kirmani's reply at Fasl 11 turns on a distinction internal to the architecture of the angels. *The angels who were charged with prostration — if they were not among those assigned obligations, they would not have been commanded to prostrate to Adam, and he would not have ordered them to bow to him.* The angels *who were commanded to prostrate* must be *the kind of angels who are assigned obligations*. The architecture forks at this point.

> As for those angels who are not under obligation, they have never been in the abode of the body, nor have they stood up to action; they are forerunners in the abode of eternity. But what is meant is the angels who are appointed over the heavens, who dwell in the abode of nature and who govern the celestial spheres — those who bear the likeness of the creative and prophetic angels in the abode of eternity, as we have demonstrated. For only those in the abode of creative origination advance in rank. As for sajda (saj-dah; prostration) and ruku' (ru-koo; bowing), these belong to the duties of worship and works, which exist only in the natural world.

The distinction is the one Bāb 5 prepared: *the angels of the abode of eternity* (the originative-emanative limits — First Intellect, Soul) versus *the angels of the abode of nature* (the celestial-sphere governors, who stand inside the world of bodies). The former are *not* under obligation, because their perfection is from origination itself. The latter *are* under obligation, because they dwell in the abode of nature and acquire their increase through the obligations of worship. The angels' prostration is therefore an act of Sharia in the same architecture as the human's prayer: *both* are *acts of worship by which the rank advances toward eternity*.

## Fasl 12 — But Adam never *said* "obey me"

Al-Nusra at Fasl 12 brings a *behavioral* counter: *If the prostration of the angels to Adam had been their Sharia, Adam would have said to them, "God commands you to prostrate and obey me," and he would have striven against those who disobeyed his command from God. It would also have been one of his pronouncements.* The argument is that *legislation* requires *verbal promulgation* and *enforcement*; Adam, in the Qur'anic record, *did not* explicitly verbalize the command or *strive militarily* against disobedients (Iblis); therefore, the prostration *was not* part of Adam's Sharia.

Al-Kirmani's reply is to deny the behavioral premise. *It does not follow that, because Adam did not say "God commands you to prostrate to me and obey me," prostration is therefore not an obligation.* The parallel is decisive: *the Prophet ﷺ did not say to the Children of Israel "God commands you to establish prayer and give me zakat," and yet there is no evidence that prayer, zakat, and all that he commanded them to do are not among the duties of Sharia.* The verbalization of the command in the *I/you* form is *not* what makes a Sharia binding. *The laws were established by the very fact that their conveyor was striving for them.* *Were this not so, then 'Isa (peace be upon him) would not have had a Sharia legitimately his, since he did not strive militarily — and this is invalid.* 'Isa did not engage in *military* struggle; he is nonetheless *unanimously* recognized as a law-bringer. The *behavioral* test al-Nusra offers fails on its own terms.

## Fasl 13 — Why Adam is omitted from Quran 42:13

At Fasl 13 al-Nusra makes his most strictly scriptural argument. *Allah did not mention in the Qur'an that Adam had a Sharia, and He did not mention his being sent as He did with others* — *Indeed, We sent Noah*; *He has ordained for you of the religion what He enjoined upon Noah, and that which We have revealed to you, and what We enjoined upon Abraham, Moses, and Jesus — to establish the religion and not be divided in it* (Quran 42:13). Adam is *not in the list*. The omission must mean something.

Al-Kirmani's reply has two layers. First, *Adam is mentioned in the Qur'an, as are his followers and his people — the angels who were commanded to prostrate to him and from whom he taught the names, as God commanded him: "O Adam, inform them of their names" (Quran 2:33).* The non-mention in *one verse* is not silence; the *mention in another verse* — *the teaching-of-names verse* — is *substantive*. *This sending is greater than the sending of others, because the sending of messengers is so that they may be God's vicegerents upon His earth.* Adam is named in the Qur'an as *Allah's vicegerent on the earth*: *I will make upon the earth a successor* (Quran 2:30). *God Almighty Himself wrote in His Book that He made Adam a vicegerent upon the earth, to whom its people would return for what they needed in the matter of their religion.*

Second — and the more important move — the reason Adam is *not* mentioned in Quran 42:13 is *the same* reason he is *not* counted among *al-muqarrarun*: *he was not one of the ulu al-'azm* (oo-lul azm; *those of resolute determination*), *and because he was not a determined one*. The omission is *technical*, not denial. The five who are listed (Noah, Abraham, Moses, Jesus, Muhammad ﷺ) are *al-muqarrarun*, the *determined ones* who *renewed or abrogated* prior Sharias. Adam, being first, did not renew or abrogate; he is therefore *technically* outside the list of *the determined*. But *the absence from the technical list* is not *the absence from the architecture*.

Al-Kirmani's most pointed move is a *reductio* against al-Nusra's reading. *If it is permissible for Adam to be excluded from the company of the people of Sharia for the reason that his name is not mentioned with the five (peace be upon them), it is also permissible because he is not mentioned among those to whom revelation was given, when the Almighty said: "Indeed, We have revealed to you as We revealed to Noah and the prophets after him, and We revealed to Abraham, Ishmael, Isaac, Jacob, the tribes, Jesus, Job, Jonah, Aaron, and Solomon" (Quran 4:163) — for him to be excluded from their company. But this is impossible.* Adam is universally regarded as one of those who *received revelation*. If non-mention in *one* list (42:13) proves *no Sharia*, then non-mention in *another* list (4:163) would prove *no revelation*. *No one* accepts the latter conclusion; therefore the former argument fails by parity. *The saying is the saying of the author of al-Islah, taken from him.* The Fasl 13 argument from scriptural omission falls.

## Fasl 14 — The reason for Sharia: not (only) the violation of limits

At Fasl 14 al-Nusra reaches for the philosophical foundation. *We must also know the reason for the existence of Sharia, so that it may be sound for us to say that Adam had no Sharia.* The proposed reason — *the neglect of the hudud (hu-dood; legal limits), their disruption, and the pursuit of contraries by creation* — is the philosophical commonplace of Sharia as *restraint*: laws exist *because* humans transgress; *no transgressing community* means *no Sharia needed*. *This meaning did not appear in the time of Adam*, al-Nusra says, *for the world that existed before him and his people were neighbors at the border, and none of them violated a single limit, as they themselves declared when they said, "Will You place in it one who corrupts it and sheds blood, while we glorify You with Your praise and sanctify You?" (Quran 2:30). So why would Adam have had to establish Sharia, and say such-and-such?*

The argument has surface plausibility. The Qur'anic phrasing of the angels' demurral — *we glorify You with Your praise and sanctify You* — does suggest a pre-Adamic order in which *no transgression* required legislation. If Sharia is *restraint of transgression*, and *no transgression* existed, *no Sharia* was needed.

Al-Kirmani's reply at Fasl 14 reframes Sharia's purpose. *The reason for the necessity of Sharia's existence differs from what the author of al-Nusra claimed in saying that the limits are neglected, that they are disrupted, and that creation pursues contraries.* The negative reading (Sharia as *response to* transgression) is *one* purpose but not the *primary* one. The architecture al-Kirmani opens is *structural*: Sharia exists *because the human structure requires it*. The argument runs forward through Fasl 15–16, building the bāb's most general anthropological teaching.

## Fasl 15 — The three souls and the architecture of necessary prescription

At Fasl 15 al-Kirmani gives the philosophical reason for Sharia's necessity:

> The souls in human beings each fail to restrain themselves from what is peculiar to them. From these actions, the prescriptions are indispensable that the community uses to silence the sit-down strike of their own claims and by which they secure one another. If the prescriptions are necessary, we say: Some of them are necessary at all times and seasons, and some are not. Some may exist at one time and not at the last, increasing and decreasing.

The architecture is the *Aristotelian-Avicennan tripartite soul* reframed under the Da'wa's reading. The human has *three souls*: *the rational, possessing distinction and thought, which knows it has a Creator and accordingly submits to Him*; *the irascible, by which one grows angry, loves to overpower and to take revenge, and is drawn to injustice, killing, and deception*; *and the appetitive, from which lust arises*. Each of the three souls *requires* a distinct architecture of prescription:

- *The rational soul* — *which knows it has a Creator and submits to Him* — *is compelled to prescribed acts that it requires in great number*: the prescriptions of *worship*, by which the rational soul *draws closer* to its Creator.

- *The irascible soul* — *which loves to overpower and is drawn to injustice, killing, and deception* — *is compelled to a prescription by which one rushes with bold force to separate them from one another, and to take the rights of the wronged from the aggressor by force*: the prescriptions of *retaliation*, *criminal law*, *the legal force* by which the community *secures itself against its own angers*.

- *The appetitive soul* — *from which lust arises, seeking marriage as a necessity* — *is compelled to a prescription that removes the sit-down strike of jealousy among them which leads them to death*: the prescriptions of *marriage*, *inheritance*, *the legal regulation of desire*.

The architecture is *grounded in the human structure itself*. Each of the three souls *requires* a prescription not because *the souls necessarily transgress*, but because *the souls have demands that cannot be satisfied without architectural mediation*. The architecture of Sharia is *not* (only) a *response* to human moral failure; it is the *condition* under which human community *can stand at all*.

The third soul carries the architecture further. *This soul demands all wealth for itself by any means whatever, so one is compelled to prescriptions by which mankind secure themselves against each other's cheating, cunning, and deceit, which would lead them to domination and combat.* The architecture of *commercial law* — *transactions, contracts, the extraction of right from its withholder* — follows from the appetitive soul's tendency. *These souls move with their eagerness to seek sustenance, and transactions become necessary, so people are compelled to work with one another — since treachery and cunning are in nature, prescriptions are required by which the plaintiff and his opponent proceed in extracting truth for its rightful holder.* And finally, *death too is necessary among them, and they cannot do without disposing of the absent and the dead among them — whether by burial, cremation, or some other gentle means — lest they suffer harm*; the architecture of *funerary law* is the sixth necessity.

The architecture al-Kirmani gives at Fasl 15 has six "gateways":

> These are six gateways by which the courses of the races run, and under which lie the species whose existence among human beings is unceasingly necessary.

*Six gateways*: (1) *drawing near to Allah* (worship); (2) *retaliation* in cases of bodily injury and homicide; (3) *marriage* (regulation of the appetitive); (4) *division of property* (inheritance, transactions); (5) *the absent and the dead* (probate, missing persons); (6) *the extraction of rights and return to their owners* (commercial and judicial process). *As for what is not always necessary, but at one time appears as a surplus and at another as a deficit, these are the duties of worshipping God Almighty and drawing closer to Him through knowing His limits, by which perfection is attained — this falls under the category of drawing near to God.* The architecture distinguishes *the always-necessary* (the six gateways) from *the cycle-variable* (the modes of worship and the depth of *knowing His limits*, which expand and contract across cycles).

*All these prescriptions belong to Sharia. They must be set forth first and prescribed among human beings at the beginning of their existence. And if these prescriptions are needed in the time of Adam (peace be upon him), who was one of the human beings, then upon him — and his angels — the laws of Islam are established.*

*Adam was one of the human beings.* The premise is decisive. *The architecture of the three souls applies to Adam* because *Adam is a human*. *The six gateways are necessary* for any human community because the *three souls* generate the necessities. Adam's community — *being human* — *requires* the six gateways; therefore *Adam, as their teacher and guide, must have brought them*. The argument has closed.

## Fasl 16 — The cycle-variable laws and the chain forward

At Fasl 16 al-Kirmani gives a refinement that bridges into the bāb's second half (the Noah-and-the-Qā'im material). *Whoever existed before Adam, his ruling is the ruling of whoever stands at the end of the seventh cycle, for example — in a manner that cannot dispense with the necessary prescriptions we have mentioned.* The pre-Adamic communities (those of the great cycle's end-of-ignorance) *still had* the six necessary prescriptions; what they *lacked* was the *cycle-variable* second tier — *the duties of worshipping God Almighty and drawing closer to Him through knowing His limits*. *So God sent Adam (peace be upon him) to teach them and others, lest they remain uneducated, and lest they be rewarded only deficiently.*

The architecture distinguishes *what is always necessary* (six gateways, present in every human community as a structural minimum) from *what cycles bring forward* (worship, *knowing the limits*, the second-tier prescriptions that *attain perfection* and prepare the soul for the Hereafter). The pre-Adamic communities had the minimum; *Adam's sending* added the *cycle-tier*. *He therefore prescribed, believed, and called them to the two acts of worship in knowledge and in deed.* *The two acts of worship* — *al-'ibadat al-'ilmiyya wa al-'amaliyya* — name the architecture Bāb 4 prepared and Bāb 8 closed on: *knowledge* and *deed* together, neither without the other. *Some of them followed and obeyed, and some refused and turned back to the previous six gateways at the beginning of the cycles.* The architecture of *every cycle thereafter* — the architecture Episode 12 will see unfold in Nuh's establishment of *his Sharia by the Ark*, the Qā'im's *closing of the legislation cycles*, and the Great Resurrection — is the architecture *Adam first opened*.

*Such is the order of those people.*

The bāb's first half closes on the architecture's reach. Adam brought *not only* the minimum-necessary prescriptions of the six gateways, *but also* the *cycle-tier* prescriptions of worship that draw the soul toward its perfection in *the two acts*. The architecture of Sharia is the architecture under which *the human as such* stands; the architecture of the cycles is the architecture under which the *measure* of worship-prescription varies with the cycle's need. Adam, *being first*, opened *both architectures*: the always-necessary and the cycle-variable. Episode 12 will follow al-Kirmani as he traces what *Nuh* (Noah) inherits from Adam, what the *Qā'im* will *not* do (raise the laws) and what he *will* do (close the doors of teaching, open the Great Resurrection), and how the architecture of the seven cycles holds the chain from *the first door* of Adam *to the gathering* of the Qā'im.

A closing line in the manner of Imam Zayn al-'Abidin (zayn al-aa-bi-deen; peace be upon him), in the manner of *al-Sahifa al-Sajjadiyya* (as-sa-hee-fah as-saj-jaa-dee-yah), names the bāb's vocation under the architecture of Adam's law:

> O God, the prescriptions You sent down with Your prophets are the road of our souls to their Maker; You did not leave us to our angers without retaliation, nor to our lusts without bond, nor to our deaths without rite — Your mercy in legislating is Your mercy in providing the road, and Your mercy in providing the road is Your mercy in commanding us to walk it. Make me of those who walk where the prophets walked, and recognize in Your laws the path of return.
> (After the manner of Imam Zayn al-'Abidin, *al-Sahifa al-Sajjadiyya*, supplication for walking the road of the law; paraphrased.)

*Your mercy in legislating is Your mercy in providing the road.* The Du'a-side counterpart to al-Kirmani's analytic register names what the bāb has been arguing under the architecture of Adam's law: *the Sharia is not an imposition; it is the road*. Adam, *being first*, opened the road that every Speaker after him has *deepened* and *extended*. The architecture is *one continuous mercy* from the first door of Adam to the gathering of the Qā'im — *one road*, *six gateways*, *two acts of worship* — under which the soul *walks back to the One who sent it forth*.
