# Divisions of the World and the Small World

*From the Kitab al-Riyad (ki-taab ar-ri-yaad) of Hamid al-Din Ahmad al-Kirmani (haa-mid ad-deen ah-mad al-kir-maa-nee). This chapter covers Bāb 7 (baab) — On the Judgment Between the Two Disputants in Their Discourse on the Divisions of the World — comprising seven fusūl (fu-sool; sections). Bāb 5 named the human as the fruit of all the worlds; Bāb 6 returned to the chain's interior joints and refused two analogies (the graded ladder and the dimensional resemblance) on the principled ground that *the soul is not embodied*. Bāb 7 now closes the anthropological arc that Bāb 5 opened. The question is no longer *whence does the human receive*, nor *what is the structure of the joints*, but *how is the world itself divided, and how does the human stand in relation to those divisions*. Al-Islah (al-is-laah) proposes a fourfold cosmology — four jewels, four structures, four divisions of human beings — and names the human as the world's only true counterpart, *the small world* (al-ʿālam al-ṣaghīr; the microcosm). Al-Nusra (an-nus-rah) counters that the dense lower world is the pair of the subtle upper world, and that the horse is the human's pair because the horse *accepts instruction*. Al-Kirmani refuses both partial readings: the cosmos is not divided into four genera but into three (metals, plants, animals) within which the human shares the animal genus *with a kind of conscious awareness*; the human's true pair is neither the horse nor any other beast, but *the angels* — meaning the prophets, the awliya (aw-li-yaa; guardians), and the Imams (i-maam; peace be upon them) together with those who follow in their footsteps. The seven fusūl walk through the four-jewel cosmology, the cataloging of bestial and angelic pairs, the rereading of Empedocles (Bandhaqlis — ban-thaq-lees) under the lens of *the rational soul as the teaching prophets and Imams*, and the microcosm-and-macrocosm correspondence that the bāb closes on: *in the world there are rivers, valleys, waters of different tastes, mountains and minerals; and in the human being likewise — bones and cartilage, flesh and blood, what flows from him and what comes out of him*. The bāb ends in the register of prayer: *may Allah place us among those who follow the traces of His guardians, and may He not deprive us of the intercession of His friends*.*

## What this bāb is really asking

Bāb 5 closed on the human as the fruit of all the worlds — *the last in the universes, united in eternity with what has been acquired by time*. Bāb 6 tested the chain's interior joints and refused both al-Islah's graded ladder and al-Nusra's dimensional resemblance. Bāb 7 now turns to a different question, one that has been latent in the dispute from the start. Granted that the human stands at the seam of the worlds — granted that he receives his first perfection from nature and his second perfection from the higher order — *how is the world itself divided?* What is the structure of the cosmos whose fruit the human is? And in what sense, exactly, does the human stand to that cosmos as *its pair*?

The question is not merely descriptive. The disputants are arguing about *the metaphysics of resemblance*. If the world has four genera, the human is the fourth and the architecture is fourfold all the way down. If the world has three genera and the human is the *animal* genus elevated by *a kind of conscious awareness*, the human's distinctiveness lies in *the awareness*, not *the count*. If he has no counterpart in the world's parts, he is the *small world*, the microcosm in whom the whole macrocosm is *folded*. If he is a species within the animal genus, he has *many* counterparts — lion, wolf, fox, monkey, crow, hawk, tiger — each named by the morals to which his soul gravitates. And if his true pair is *the angels*, then his ascent toward his pair is the work of *following the prophets and the Imams* in obedience and worship.

Al-Kirmani will weave through these readings with characteristic care. He refuses al-Islah's fourfold cosmology *as cosmology* but accepts its anthropology *as anthropology* (the human is the microcosm, *the counterpart of the whole, because he gathers all the jewels of the world*). He refuses al-Nusra's identification of the horse as the human's pair (the criterion of *acceptance of instruction* picks out too many animals) but accepts the deeper principle al-Nusra was reaching for — that *pairing is the state of acceptance*, of *receiving an effect from the one who is the cause*. And he presses past both to a teaching the rest of the treatise will lean upon: *the true pair of the human is the angels*, meaning the prophets, the awliya, and the Imams — and the human becomes their pair to the degree that he *takes them as his party*.

The Qur'anic verse that al-Kirmani will quote at Fasl 3 — *and who will take care of them from among you, he is one of them* — is the bāb's compressed teaching. *You become the company you keep.* The cosmos is structured such that the human's *pair* is not given to him by biology nor by elemental composition; his pair is *chosen by what he attaches himself to*. To attach to the prophets and the Imams is to be paired with the angels. To attach to deceit, cruelty, treachery, and immorality is to be paired — by exactly the same logic of *acceptance* — with the wolves, the foxes, the crows, and the monkeys. The bāb is at once a cosmology and a moral teaching: the architecture of *kinds* in the world is the architecture of *kinds* the human chooses to become.

## The Qur'anic horizon — the best of forms, the honored, and the party of Allah

Three Qur'anic verses stand behind the bāb. Al-Kirmani names one of them directly at Fasl 3 and another at Fasl 4. The third — the verse about *the best of forms* (Quran 95:4) — runs through the dispute as the unspoken charter for any claim that the human is *the world's counterpart*.

The first verse is the Qur'an's (qur-aan) most direct naming of the human's *rank*:

> And We have certainly honored the children of Adam (aa-dam; the first human, peace be upon him) and carried them on the land and sea and provided for them of the good things and preferred them over much of what We have created, with [definite] preference.
> (Quran 17:70; English: Saheeh International.)

*Preferred them over much of what We have created, with definite preference.* The verse establishes the *given preference* — the human's standing above what is below him in the natural order is not an achievement but a gift, *granted by Allah*. The disputants are not arguing whether the preference exists; both accept it. They are arguing *what grounds it*. Al-Islah will ground it in the human's collecting the four genera of the world. Al-Kirmani will reground it in the human's standing at the seam of two worlds, gathering what the cosmos cannot gather and receiving what the angels bring from above.

The second verse is the Qur'an's naming of the human's *form*:

> We have certainly created man in the best of forms (*aḥsani taqwīm* — ah-sa-ni taq-weem).
> (Quran 95:4; English: Saheeh International.)

*The best of forms.* Al-Islah's claim that the human gathers what is in the world's parts — *that there is in his body something corresponding to each kind: bone, flesh, nerves, veins, cartilage, and all the liquid substances that flow from his body* — is the metaphysics of this verse stated in cosmological vocabulary. The body in *aḥsani taqwīm* is the body that *holds the whole* — that gathers the body, the growing, and the sensing into one form ready to receive the rational soul. The verse is what al-Islah is reaching for at Fasl 5, and what al-Kirmani himself will reaffirm at Fasl 7.

The third verse is the verse al-Kirmani quotes at Fasl 3 — the verse that names the *principle of pairing as alliance*:

> O you who have believed, do not take the Jews and the Christians as allies (*awliyāʾ* — aw-li-yaa). They are [in fact] allies of one another. And whoever is an ally to them among you — then indeed, he is [one] of them.
> (Quran 5:51; English: Saheeh International.)

Al-Kirmani lifts the principle of the verse — *whoever takes them as allies is one of them* — and turns it positively toward the *true allies*, the prophets and the Imams. The logic is the same: alliance constitutes belonging. To *take* a party — to take them as one's *walī* (wa-lee; guardian, ally, friend) — is to *become* of their kind. This is the bāb's compressed metaphysics of pairing. The human is not *fixed* as the pair of this or that creature by biology. The human is *paired* by what he chooses to be allied with — and the choice of alliance has cosmic consequences. The verse's *whoever* (*man*) is the universal quantifier; the principle holds for every human. Each one of us, by the company he keeps and the loyalties he forms, is *being made one of them*.

A line in the manner of Imam Ali ibn Abi Talib (i-maam a-lee ibn a-bee taa-lib; the Commander of the Believers, peace be upon him), from his sayings on self-knowledge in the Ismaili (is-maa-ee-lee) tradition, names the bāb's anthropology from the inside:

> Do you reckon yourself a small thing, when in you the great world is folded?
> (Imam Ali ibn Abi Talib, in the manner of his sayings on self-knowledge; trans. condensed.)

*In you the great world is folded.* The Alid line is the very claim al-Islah is making at Fasl 5 and Fasl 7 — the *human as small world* — named from the side of self-knowledge rather than from the side of cosmology. The human, considered from outside, is one species among others. Considered from within, he is *the cosmos compressed into a single bearer*. The microcosm is not a metaphor; it is *the architecture of the human read against the architecture of the cosmos*.

And from the Prophet ﷺ (peace and blessings of Allah be upon him), a hadith that names the same anthropology from the side of *alliance as bodily belonging*:

> The believer is the brother of the believer; they are like one body — if one limb complains, the rest of the body responds with sleeplessness and fever.
> (After the manner of the Prophet ﷺ, as transmitted in Sahih Muslim, *Kitab al-Birr*, hadith 2586.)

*Like one body.* Al-Kirmani's reading of Quran 5:51 — *whoever takes them as allies, he is one of them* — and his teaching that *the human's true pair is the angels* (meaning the prophets, the awliya, and the Imams, and those who follow them) are the same architectural claim. The party of the prophets and the Imams *is one body in the cosmos*. To be paired with them is to be paired *as a limb is paired with the body of which it is a part*.

## Fasl 1 — Al-Islah's four jewels, four structures, four divisions of human beings

The first *fasl* opens with al-Islah's cosmological proposal:

> The entire world is composed of four jewels, four structures, and four divisions of human beings. It stands upon three jewels apart from human beings, and the fourth is human beings. The knotted and the dissolved are all one essence.

The structure is fourfold across three registers. Three of the four *jewels* are *not* the human (they are nature, in its layered orders); the *fourth is human beings*. The fourth completes the structure; without him the cosmos has only three. *The knotted and the dissolved are all one essence* — the technical claim that *the contracted* (what holds together as a body) and *the dissolved* (what flows, separates, melts) are not two essences but one essence under two states.

Al-Islah immediately denies that the human has any *intra-cosmic pair*:

> We do not say that the pair of the human in form is the horse; rather, the horse's pair and its form belong to its own essence — just as gold and silver are a pair, and as ruby and emerald are a pair, and as the horse and its form make pairs among animals: horses, elephants, falcons, lions, and so on. Because they are all animals, it is not permissible to say that the horse is the pair and form of the human being — for the human is alone in possessing the rational soul, and he has no counterpart in any of the parts of the world or their offspring. Rather, he is the counterpart of the world as a whole, because he gathers all the jewels of the world. For this reason he is called the small world (al-ʿālam al-ṣaghīr — al-aa-lam aṣ-ṣa-gheer; the microcosm), because he counterparts the whole that gathers its parts.

This is al-Islah's central anthropological claim. The pairing-relation, on his reading, is *resemblance within a category*: gold-and-silver pair within metals, ruby-and-emerald pair within precious stones, horse-and-elephant-and-falcon-and-lion pair within animals. The human, possessing the *rational soul* that no other animal possesses, has no within-category pair. His pair, if any, must be *the whole structure* of which he is the gathering. He is *the counterpart of the world* because he *gathers all the jewels of the world*. The phrase *al-ʿālam al-ṣaghīr* — the microcosm — has a long pedigree the Ismaili Da'wa (daa-wah) received and rearticulated, from the Brethren of Purity (*Ikhwān al-Ṣafāʾ* — ikh-waan as-ṣa-faa) to al-Kirmani's own *Rahat al-'Aql* (raa-hat al-aql). Al-Islah is stating the doctrine in the form the dispute will now test.

Al-Nusra accepts the anthropological conclusion (the human is the microcosm) but reframes what *pairing* means:

> The state of pairing is not merely the state of [biological] sex; the state of pairing is the state of acceptance. This dense lower world is the pair of the subtle upper world. If you look between them, you will find a distance beyond the distance between the horse and the human, for the horse is not of the human's species — they are joined only in being of the genus of life, and the horse shares in that. He made this dense world the pair of the spiritual world because of the connection between them in the chapter of bestowal and reception of benefit: the forces of the higher world are connected to this world, and this world receives those forces from the upper world; so he made this world the pair of that world. Likewise the human and the horse — he made the horse the human's pair because the horse accepts instruction from him more than the other animals accept it.

*The state of pairing is the state of acceptance.* This is al-Nusra's correction, and it is structurally important. Pairing is not biological resemblance, nor is it sex; pairing is the *relation of receiving an effect from a cause*. The dense lower world is the pair of the subtle upper world *because* it accepts the influences of the upper world. The horse is the human's pair *because* it accepts the human's instruction more readily than other animals. Al-Nusra has imported the architecture of *cause-effect* — which Bāb 1 through Bāb 4 spent thirty thousand words establishing — into the metaphysics of *pairing*. Pairing is no longer a horizontal resemblance; it is a vertical bestowal-and-reception.

Al-Kirmani begins his judgment by refusing al-Islah's count and recasting the world's actual divisions:

> The investigators in what they discuss have not relied upon sound research — for instance the author of al-Islah, who divided the world into four jewels, four structures, and four sections together with human beings. That is impossible, because the divisions of the world are more than what we have mentioned, as we have said. The word "world" refers to many things, from the center to the ocean: the sphere and the planets, fire, air, water, earth, and the offspring [of nature], according to what he estimated and inferred. It is clear from his words that he intends the offspring of the world, not the world together with the offspring; for they are three genera, with no fourth, and each of them has many species under it. The first of these is the nearest of the four arcs, the foremost of which is the metals — all that comes to be in a fixed place of the earth without growing in its parts, and which combines what dissolves and what does not, and what breaks and what does not break. The second is the plant — all that grows from the earth, and this combines low growth and trees. The third is the animal — all that moves and has species, combining what flies and what does not fly. The offspring of the world do not exceed that. The human being who is named as the fourth shares in the genus of the animal, with a kind of conscious awareness.

The correction is precise. Al-Islah's *fourfold* division is wrong on two counts. *First*, the term *world* refers not to the cosmos in its totality but only to the *offspring* of nature; the cosmic structure (the spheres, the planets, the four elements) is the *productive cause* of the offspring, and al-Islah has conflated the *cause* and the *effect*. *Second*, the offspring of nature constitute *three* genera, not four: the mineral, the plant, and the animal. The human *shares in the animal genus*, distinguished within it by *a kind of conscious awareness* — by the rational soul, which is the human's species-mark *within* the animal genus, not his elevation *out of* it.

The implication is significant. If the human is *within* the animal genus, then he *does* have intra-cosmic pairs in the way al-Islah denied. What distinguishes the human is not that he has *no pair within the world's parts*, but that he has *many pairs there, distinguished by the kind of soul he activates*. The teaching the bāb is about to develop — that the human is paired with the angels when he activates the rational soul through obedience to the prophets and Imams, and paired with the beasts when he activates the bestial soul through cruelty, treachery, and immorality — is grounded right here: the human is *within* the animal genus, and *within* it he is *plural in his pairings*.

## Fasl 2 — The human has many pairs, not one

The second *fasl* takes up al-Islah's denial that the human has any pair and turns it around on two grounds:

> The human has no counterpart and no pair, and the horse has no pair. That is impossible, on two grounds. The first is that the human is a species of the animal genus, and inasmuch as he is a species, he is the pair of the other species of his genus — since all species form each other within the chapter on quality. If human beings are a species, then they are a pair to others among the species. The second is that human beings would not have been likened in their structure, morals, actions, and all their conditions, motions, and natures to all that they encounter in the universe of existence — according to what he mentioned, and on account of which it is said that he is the small world — unless he were a pair to all cosmic things according to occasion and circumstance.

The two grounds are distinct. The *first* is logical: species within a genus *form each other* — each species being what it is partly by way of *not being* the other species. If human beings are a species (and Fasl 1 established that they are, within the animal genus), then they have pairs *necessarily*, by the logical structure of species within a genus. The *second* is metaphysical: the very claim that the human is the *small world* — the claim al-Islah himself made — entails that the human resembles all cosmic things. And to resemble is to be paired. The microcosm-claim *requires* plural pairing; it does not exclude it.

Al-Kirmani then catalogues the pairs by *moral type*:

> If this is the case, then human beings, if they are good, virtuous, wise, religious, knowledgeable, and pure, are paired with the angels on account of the occasion between them; if he is courageous, brave, strong, and helpful, his pair is the lions; if he is unjust, cruel, and murderous, then he is paired with the wolves and the deception of foxes; if he is a treacherous thief, he is paired with the crows and the hawks; if he is deceitful, cunning, and overpowering, the tiger is paired with him. If he is an adulterer — of immorality, amusement, and play — then he is paired with the monkeys. According to whatever a person is satisfied with — whether by his actions, or his morals, or his customs, or in some portion of them — he will have a pair of like kind. If all of this is established, then what he said in the chapter — that there is no pair for human beings — is not sound; human beings have many forms and many pairs in many respects.

*According to whatever a person is satisfied with — by his actions, or his morals, or his customs — he will have a pair of like kind.* The catalogue is at once moral and cosmological. The angelic, the leonine, the wolfish, the vulpine, the corvid, the tiger-like, the simian — each is a *cosmic kind*, and each is a *moral type*. The human is paired with whichever cosmic kind his *moral satisfaction* aligns him with. The *occasion* (*munāsaba* — mu-naa-sa-bah; the relation of fit) between the human's moral state and a particular cosmic kind *constitutes the pairing*. The human's pair is not given by his body; it is *enacted* by his soul.

The catalogue is not an abstract typology. To be cruel is *literally* to be paired with the wolf — not metaphorically. To be deceitful is *literally* to be paired with the fox. The verse of Quran 5:51 — *whoever takes them as allies, he is one of them* — receives, in al-Kirmani's exposition, its widest reading: every moral satisfaction constitutes an *alliance* with a cosmic kind, and every alliance constitutes a *becoming*. Al-Islah's denial that the human has a pair was supposed to *secure* the human's uniqueness; al-Kirmani's reply is that the human's *uniqueness* lies precisely in his *plural pairing* — he is the only creature whose moral state can *take on* so many shapes. The human's uniqueness is not *being unpaired*; it is *being susceptible to all the pairs*.

A line in the manner of Imam Ali ibn Abi Talib (peace be upon him), as the tradition has long carried it, names the same teaching from the side of moral self-knowledge:

> Whoever knows himself, knows his Lord; and whoever knows the company he keeps, knows what he is becoming.
> (After the manner of Imam Ali ibn Abi Talib's sayings on self-knowledge; trans. condensed.)

*Whoever knows the company he keeps, knows what he is becoming.* The Alid line is the bāb's catalogue compressed into a single principle. The human's *becoming* is determined by his *company* — by the moral kind he is most often *satisfied with*. Al-Kirmani's catalogue is the cosmological breakdown of which *kinds* are available. The choice is real, and it is everywhere.

## Fasl 3 — The horse has no priority; the human's true pair is the angels

The third *fasl* takes apart al-Nusra's identification of the horse as the human's pair:

> As for the author of al-Nusrah, he said that what was correct concerning the meaning of common genus is what he referred back to. He said that the horse was made a pair for human beings because the horse accepts the teaching of human beings. But if it were on the basis of acceptance and instruction that it became his pair, then the horse has no priority over others — for in the animal kingdom there is one who accepts education and refinement and is not the mare; and if the criterion is qualitative, then the mare is not such either, to be a pair. He has a priority over all other animal species, since he has many pairs rather than one pair.

The argument is structural. Al-Nusra had grounded the horse's pairing with the human on *acceptance of instruction*; al-Kirmani replies that this picks out too many candidates. The dog, the falcon, the elephant, even the camel — each accepts instruction in its own register. The horse has no *priority* among them. Refined to *qualitative* acceptance, the criterion still fails — for each animal that accepts instruction does so *in its own qualitative way*. The criterion proves *too much* or *too little*: either many animals are the human's pair (contradicting al-Nusra's *one-pair* thesis), or no animal in particular is.

The deeper point is the one al-Kirmani returns to: *the human has priority over all other animal species, since he has many pairs rather than one pair*. The criterion of pairing, properly understood (per Fasl 2), yields *many* pairs, not one. The horse is *one possible pair* among many — only when the human's moral state happens to fit the horse-kind.

Al-Kirmani then states the bāb's positive teaching:

> In truth, the pair of human beings can only be the angels. What we mean by that is human beings of all virtues — the prophets, the guardians (awliya), and the imams (may the prayers of God be upon them) — and those who follow in their footsteps, establish their obedience, and worship Allah with both modes of worship. Not the others, from among the people who are in the form of human beings but whose souls are themselves the souls of beasts and predators — as we have said and shown, like monkeys, donkeys, foxes, the wild beasts, and the wolves. And those who follow the traces of the Imams (may Allah's prayers be upon them) and above them, from among the prophets and guardians (may Allah's prayers be upon them) — their diligence in obedience, in both modes of worship, and their adherence to command and prohibition distinguishes them from the beasts, so they resemble them. Peace be upon them; they will be saved in their entirety, and none among them except: ("and who will take care of them from among you — he is one of them"). May Allah make us among those who follow their party and spread it together with the prophets, the guardians, and the pure Imams (may Allah's prayers be upon them all).

*In truth, the pair of human beings can only be the angels.* This is the bāb's positive thesis on pairing — what *the proper pair* of the human, on the highest reading, is. By *angels* al-Kirmani means *human beings of all virtues* — and he immediately glosses what *human beings of all virtues* means: *the prophets, the awliya, and the imams*, together with *those who follow in their footsteps*. The angelic *kind* in the cosmos has its proper bearers in the prophetic-and-Imamic line and in the followers of that line. To be paired with the angels is to *belong to that line by following*.

The teaching is structured against the dark mirror. There are *people who are in the form of human beings but whose souls are the souls of beasts and predators* — monkeys, donkeys, foxes, wolves. The form (the body) is human; the *kind* (the soul) is bestial. They are pairs of monkeys and wolves in *exactly* the way the prophets' followers are pairs of angels: by *what they are aligned with*, not by what they look like.

The verse al-Kirmani quotes — Quran 5:51 — is read inversely: just as taking the wrong allies makes one *of them*, taking the *right* allies — the prophets, the awliya, the Imams — makes one *of them*. *Acceptance* is *alliance*, and *alliance* is *belonging*. The human is becoming *whomever he allies himself with*. The closing line of the fasl is a prayer: *May Allah make us among those who follow their party and spread it together with the prophets, the guardians, and the pure Imams.* The bāb has moved from cosmology to anthropology to moral teaching to prayer. The architecture is not academic; it is the *vocation* of the human, named in the only register that fits a vocation.

From the Du'a of Imam Zayn al-'Abidin (zayn al-aa-bi-deen; peace be upon him), in the manner of *al-Sahifa al-Sajjadiyya* (as-sa-hee-fah as-saj-jaa-dee-yah), a line that the Ismaili tradition has long carried as supplication on the very theme of *being made of the party of the prophets and the Imams*:

> O God, place me among those whom You have made the party of Your friends, and the followers of Your prophets, and the company of Your Imams; and make my standing with them the standing of one whom You have not abandoned.
> (After the manner of Imam Zayn al-'Abidin, *al-Sahifa al-Sajjadiyya*, supplication for being made of the party of the friends of Allah; paraphrased.)

*Make me of the party of Your friends.* The Du'a is the bāb's compressed teaching addressed back to its Source. The human is made *of the party of the prophets and the Imams* not by his own effort alone but by Allah's *placement of him there*. The prayer is the anthropology completed: the human becomes the angels' pair when Allah *makes* him so, by *placing* him among the followers of the prophetic line. The vocation and the gift are the same act, named from two sides.

## Fasl 4 — Empedocles, the rational soul, and the prophets as the mothers of human beings

The fourth *fasl* takes up al-Nusra's invocation of *Bandhaqlis* (Empedocles) and rereads the philosopher's teaching under the lens of the prophetic-and-Imamic anthropology:

> The author of al-Nusrah said: He mentioned Bandhaqlis [Empedocles], who said: The bestial soul looks toward the rational soul out of love; and the rational soul, if what comes back from her is good, will be kind to her with benefit and aid. Her image is desirable, and there is no better image among the bestial souls than the image of the horse, and none more eager to accept the influences of the rational soul than the horse. So he made the horse her pair, and this is clear.

The philosophical reading is recognizable. The *bestial soul* (*al-nafs al-bahīmiyya* — al-nafs al-ba-hee-mee-yah) is the principle of appetite and sensation in the animal; the *rational soul* (*al-nafs al-nāṭiqa* — al-nafs an-naa-ti-qah) is the principle of intellect and deliberation in the human. The bestial soul *looks toward* the rational soul *out of love* — that is, the lower seeks the higher by way of attraction. The rational soul, *if what comes back from her is good* (if the bestial soul's response is fitting), *is kind to her with benefit and aid*. The image of the horse, on this reading, is the most refined image among bestial souls; the horse is the most *eager to accept* the rational soul's influence; therefore the horse is *the rational soul's pair* in the cosmos. The Empedoclean reading grounds al-Nusra's earlier claim philosophically: *the horse is the human's pair because the horse is the most pair-able bestial soul*.

Al-Kirmani replies by re-locating where the reading should have come from:

> If the author of al-Nusrah were drawing inference from the Book of Allah, the Lord of the Worlds, and from the words of our followed pure Imams (may Allah's prayers be upon them all) concerning what he wants regarding the command of the soul from the standpoint of interpretation, that would be richer than the words of the Prophet [taken alone in this regard]. If he and his like had lived in our time, they would not have failed to draw from the seas of knowledge of the People of Revelation — knowledge which overflows from the side of God's reservoir on His earth, namely our pure clients (may Allah's prayers be upon them all) — and they would then have spoken on much that they took to be hidden from the senses.

The criticism is not against al-Nusra's *use* of philosophical sources but against his *primary reliance* on them. The *seas of knowledge of the People of Revelation* — the Qur'an, the words of the Prophet ﷺ, the teaching of the Imams — *overflow from the side of God's reservoir on His earth*, the *pure clients* (the prophetic-and-Imamic line). Al-Nusra had a richer source available and reached for a thinner one. The *thinner one* — Empedocles' teaching — is not false. It is *partial*. It can be *corrected* by being *read against* the richer source.

The correction al-Kirmani offers is precise:

> What he means by "the sensual soul" is the one who is in the form of human beings, the one who would accept education, discipline, and reason — not the horse. And what he means by "the rational soul" is the teachers, who are the prophets, the guardians, and the imams (peace be upon them) and their followers — those who would be kind to those who are in the image of human beings, and teach them their interests in their religion and worldly affairs, and benefit them according to their senses, and raise their grades according to their acceptance — they being in truth the mothers of human beings.

The reread is comprehensive. Empedocles' *bestial soul looking toward the rational soul out of love* is reread as *human beings in their natural state* looking toward *the prophets and the Imams* out of love. Empedocles' *rational soul that is kind to the bestial soul with benefit and aid* is reread as *the prophets, the guardians, and the Imams* — who teach the human in his *religion and worldly affairs*, *benefit him according to his senses*, and *raise his grades according to his acceptance*. The bestial soul is not the horse; it is the *natural human* who has not yet been formed by revelation. The rational soul is *the teaching line* — the prophets and Imams who are *the mothers of human beings*.

*They being in truth the mothers of human beings.* The prophets, awliya, and Imams *give birth to the human* in his proper rational form. The natural body, in *aḥsani taqwīm* (Quran 95:4), is the *first birth*. The rational form, cultivated in the soul that accepts instruction, is the *second birth*. The architecture of Bāb 4 — *the rational soul acquires its perfection through obedience to the hudud* (hu-dood) *of the Da'wa* — returns here as the metaphysics of *spiritual motherhood*.

Al-Kirmani anchors the reread in the Qur'anic image of the *reassured soul*:

> Indeed, everyone agrees with what is indicated by the interpretation of the words of Allah Almighty: "the reassured soul — return to your Lord, well-pleased and well-pleasing" (the verse), and we carry his saying on the face that is correct.

The verse is Quran 89:27–30: *O reassured soul, return to your Lord, well-pleased and well-pleasing; and enter among My servants; and enter My Paradise* (Saheeh International). *The reassured soul* (*al-nafs al-muṭmaʾinna* — al-nafs al-muṭ-mai-yin-nah) is the soul *brought to peace* by *acceptance of the rational teaching* — the soul that, in al-Kirmani's reading, has been *given birth* by the prophets and the Imams. The verse names from the side of revelation what Empedocles named from the side of philosophy: *the soul that accepts the higher* is the soul that *returns*.

The fasl closes with the verdict on the horse-as-pair reading:

> It is, in the first place, a statement on the face that is not correct: the horse is not more receptive than those who are in the form of humans, nor is its image better than their image; nor are the guardians, imams, and their followers — with the least of their sympathy for those whom they take up — less in worth than some people on account of the training of the horse and its rectification. On the contrary, they are greater and greater and greater still. If this is the case, and there is no clear statement that constitutes any evidence that there is no better image among the bestial souls than the image of the horse — nor was the matter, regarding the existing animal image, more than the goodness of the horse's image — nor is it the case, as he said, that the horse is to be the pair of human beings on the side of education, good image, and invocation — that is impossible.

The verdict is total. The horse is not more receptive than human beings who are in the form of humans (natural humans who have not yet received the teaching). The horse's image is not better than theirs. The guardians, Imams, and their followers — even at *the least of their sympathy* — are *greater and greater and greater still* than what is achieved by training a horse. The Empedoclean image collapses; the prophetic-and-Imamic image rises in its place.

A hadith of the Prophet ﷺ (peace and blessings of Allah be upon him), transmitted by *Bukhari* and *Muslim*, names the same architecture al-Kirmani is defending:

> Every child is born upon the *fiṭra* (fiṭ-rah; the original disposition); it is his parents who make him a Jew, a Christian, or a Magian.
> (After the manner of the Prophet ﷺ; transmitted by Bukhari, *Kitab al-Janāʾiz*, hadith 1358, and Muslim, *Kitab al-Qadar*, hadith 2658.)

*It is his parents who make him.* The *natural human* — the bestial soul in Empedocles' language, the *child upon the fiṭra* in the Prophet's language — is shaped by what claims him as parent. If the prophets and the Imams *claim him as their mother* (in al-Kirmani's image), his rational form is brought to completion. The hadith's *parents* and al-Kirmani's *mothers of human beings* name the same architectural role from the same place.

## Fasl 5 — Al-Islah and al-Nusra on what the human counterparts

The fifth *fasl* opens with al-Islah's statement on the human's correspondence to the *whole* cosmos:

> Human beings are the counterpart of the whole world, and they are preferred to it by the noble essence which is not of this world. His body is contrasted with all bodies in their dumb genera, and solidity, and dissolution — for within his body there is something corresponding to each kind: bone, flesh, nerves, veins, cartilage, and all the liquid substances that flow from his body, such as blood, mucus, sweat, and what flows from the eye, the ear, the mouth, and his other parts. All of these belong to the essence of bodies and their kinds, which correspond to the kinds of the essence that is one of the three essences of the world — namely, the essence of bodies, which is not unified by anything of the three breaths.

The structure is the doubled microcosm-claim. The human is the counterpart *of the whole world* and *preferred* over the world by the rational soul, *the noble essence which is not of this world*. His *body* contains *something corresponding to each kind* of body in the cosmos: bone, flesh, nerves, veins, cartilage, and the liquid substances (blood, mucus, sweat). The human body is the *cosmic body* in miniature.

Al-Islah extends the correspondence:

> His growth, in turn, corresponds to the growing species — the various kinds of trees — which belong to the second essence, unified by the vegetative soul. His sense corresponds to the animal in its various kinds, which belongs to the third essence, unified by the sensitive soul (the soul of beasts). With this, he meets the three jewels of the world; and his uniqueness comes in the fourth rank, where he becomes the fourth essence: the essence of the speaking (rational) soul, in which he stands alone.

The doubled correspondence is now articulated through the three souls. The body corresponds to the *body-essence* (the mineral world); the growth corresponds to the *vegetative soul* (the plant world); the sense corresponds to the *sensitive soul* (the animal world). *With this, he meets the three jewels of the world.* The *fourth* — the rational soul — is the human's *uniqueness*. The human is *all three* of the lower essences, *plus* the fourth — not one rank in the cosmos but *all the ranks gathered in one*.

The summary al-Islah gives is the bāb's pithiest articulation:

> The human being is the counterpart of the whole world. His body stands opposite all the bodies of the world in their differing kinds; his growth opposite the plants; his sense opposite the animal; and he possesses something that is preferable to all of them, in which he is alone — the speaking soul that is his.

Al-Nusra objects on a technical point about *the dissolved and the contracted*:

> As for his statement that the dissolved and the contracted are a single essence — this is not warranted. Rather, the dissolved, the knotted, the growing, the sensitive, and the speaking are all one essence at the level of the genus, and what divides them are chapters and properties; the contracted essence is not the same as the dissolved essence, nor is the dissolved that is not coagulated identical with what is coagulated.

The objection is on the count and the classification. Al-Islah collapsed *the dissolved* and *the contracted* into a single essence. Al-Nusra splits them — *the dissolved, the knotted, the growing, the sensitive, and the speaking* are *one essence at the level of the genus*, but *at the level of species* they are distinct. The technical point matters because al-Nusra is preparing to *reshuffle* the human's correspondences: bodies opposite his body, dissolved bodies opposite his *growth* (because growth involves the dissolution of bodies into the body's interior), the sperm of the animal opposite his sense (because the sperm is the *seed* whose unfolding constitutes the sensitive body).

Al-Nusra's reshuffle is summarized:

> For in him is everything that is in the world, so that everything in the lower world has a likeness in him; he leaves nothing of it out. Thus he set bodies opposite his body — and his body is knotted, like the contraction of bodies — and then he set the dissolved bodies opposite his growth, because of the dissolution that is in them; for dissolution occurs only through the movement of the jewels that dissolve. Growth, likewise, is achieved only through movement; so, when these resemble one another in the chapter of movement, each is almost the same as its companion. He set bodies opposite his sense, and he set the sperm of the animal opposite his sense — gathering in him everything that the rest of the world holds of knotted and dissolved bodies and of the developing and the sensitive — and he placed him above the other essences.

The structure is the same fourfold microcosm-claim, but with the *body-essence* subdivided into *contracted* and *dissolved* and the correspondences shifted accordingly.

Al-Kirmani delivers the verdict between the two:

> The dispute between the two parties in this chapter is this. The author of al-Islah set, opposite the human being's growth, the plants of the great world that grow and develop. The author of al-Nusra, by contrast, set in this place the dissolved bodies. The author of al-Islah set, opposite the human's sense, the great animal world; while the author of al-Nusra set, opposite his sense, the developing bodies. And the author of al-Islah set the speaker of the human being — that which singles him out, preferring him over the world — as the counterpart of the animal, while the author of al-Nusra made the speaker the counterpart of something else. The position of the author of al-Islah is closer to what the analogy actually resembles, and is more in conformity than the view of the author of al-Nusra. In general, the human being is not only a counterpart to this world; he is a counterpart to all the worlds, since he is the collector of everything that preceded him in existence, and there is in him what corresponds to each of them. The matter is built upon this, not upon what he said.

The verdict is doubled. *Locally*, al-Islah is closer: his correspondences (growth ↔ plants, sense ↔ animals) follow the architecture better than al-Nusra's reshuffle. *Globally*, both disputants are incomplete: *the human is not only a counterpart to this world; he is a counterpart to all the worlds*. The human gathers *all of what preceded him in existence* — the originative *ibdaʿ* (ib-daa), the emanative *inbiʿāth* (in-bi-aath), the natural production by the planets and the elements, the offspring's three genera. The microcosm-claim, properly stated, is *cosmic* in the widest sense — not just *natural cosmos* but *all the worlds*.

## Fasl 6 — The microcosm-and-macrocosm correspondence in detail

The sixth *fasl* — labeled in the manuscript with the alternative numbering *Chapter Six of Book Seven* — walks through the microcosm-and-macrocosm correspondence in its richest detail:

> We have said that the human being — meaning man — is the counterpart of the worlds because he is the collector of much of what exists between the first creation and the second resurrection. He then became one, parallel in himself to the creative and emancipative world, and the collector of bodies parallel to the physical world. The two worlds, then, are like this, and the world is wholly contained in him.

The human stands between the *first creation* (the originative beginning, the *ibdaʿ* of the First Intellect) and the *second resurrection* (the *qiyāma* — qi-yaa-mah — at the end of the cycle), and gathers in himself what unfolds between them. He is *parallel in himself to the creative and emancipative world* (the world of *ibdaʿ* and *inbiʿāth*) and *the collector of bodies parallel to the physical world*. The two architectures meet *in him*.

Al-Kirmani then articulates the bodily correspondences in detail:

> A body may be of two kinds: gentle — that is, high — and dense — that is, what was. The human body, likewise, is gentle and dense; the gentle is not the highest. In the world there are planets and stars around it; and in the body of the human being, likewise, there is the skin that surrounds the whole body, and so on, covering the bones. There are openings that run the course of the planets, such as the ears, eyes, nose, and mouth. In the human being there is also a moving driver, which is the self; and in the world the four cardinal directions; and in the human being likewise the four natures. In the world there are rivers, valleys, and waters of different tastes, mountains and minerals; and in the human being likewise — as the author of al-Islah has it — there are bones and cartilage, flesh and blood, what flows from him and what comes out of him.

The mapping is detailed and point-for-point. *Planets around the cosmos* ↔ *skin around the body*. *Openings that run the course of the planets* ↔ *ears, eyes, nose, mouth*. *The moving driver* (the self) ↔ *the four cardinal directions* and *the four natures* (hot, cold, wet, dry). *Rivers, valleys, waters of different tastes, mountains, minerals* ↔ *bones, cartilage, flesh, blood, what flows from him and what comes out of him*. The body is the cosmos *folded inward*.

The mapping ascends from body to growth to sense:

> In the world there is what grows and develops without feeling; and in the human being likewise there is what grows and develops without feeling, like hair and nails — and just as he grows and increases from his youth to his full stature. In the world there are sensitive animals; and in the human being likewise there are strong sensitive powers. The whole world is composed and integrated, and is supplemented by what exists in him.

*What grows without feeling* finds its mirror in *hair and nails* and the body's growth from youth to full stature; *sensitive animals* find their mirror in *the body's strong sensitive powers*. Al-Kirmani names his own *Rahat al-'Aql* (raa-hat al-aql; *The Repose of the Intellect*) as the place where the full demonstration is laid out. The bāb offers a *summary*, anchored in the longer work.

Then comes the bāb's most architectural movement — the *upper* correspondence, which the lower correspondence has been pointing toward:

> What corresponds to this in the world of creation and emancipation: that world is the world of the intellect, the world of praise and laudation and sanctification, the world of monotheism and divinization, the world of virtues, the world of goodness and merits. The human being — likewise meaning man — is the collector of these virtues: intellect, praise, monotheism, divinization, virtue, existence over others and within oneself. That world has imprinted itself upon him, and his body has been marked by this world; and in this respect there is no differentiation between what has been brought into being before him except by rank only.

The doubled microcosm-claim is now complete. The *lower* correspondence: the human's body, growth, and sense mirror the natural cosmos (mineral, plant, animal). The *upper* correspondence: the human's *intellect, praise, monotheism, divinization, virtue* mirror the *world of creation and emancipation* — the originative-and-emanative architecture the previous bābs have mapped. *That world has imprinted itself upon him*, even as *his body has been marked by this world*. The human carries *that world* in his intellect and his virtues, and *this world* in his body — the *small world* in whom *both worlds* find their mirror.

*There is no differentiation between what has been brought into being before him except by rank only.* Every rank in the chain — First Intellect, Soul, rational souls, natural cosmos, the genera — has its *likeness* in the human, and the human's likenesses are *ranked* the way the chain itself is ranked. He is *the chain compressed into a single bearer*. To know oneself, on this teaching, *is* to know the chain.

The fasl closes in the register of prayer:

> May God place us among those who follow the traces of His guardians, and may He not deprive us of the intercession of His friends through their pain and their suffering.

*Place us among those who follow the traces of His guardians.* The human becomes the *small world of the upper world* by *following the traces* of the prophets and the Imams. The cosmic architecture and the moral vocation are the same architecture, named from two sides.

## Fasl 7 — The child between the parents and the midwife

The seventh and final *fasl* — labeled *Chapter Seven of Book Seven* in the manuscript — closes the bāb with al-Kirmani's most precise statement of the human's *between-place*:

> The human being is preferred over the world by the noble essence that is not of this world — not in the sense that the essence has been forbidden by his saying that it is "of this world." Rather, he is from that world — I mean the upper world — according to what has been revealed; and even when he is only from that world and not from this world, we say that he is from all the worlds, since he exists in the state of existing between the two worlds — the state of the child in his coming-to-be between his parents.

*The state of the child in his coming-to-be between his parents.* The image is precise and tender. The child is not *from* one parent or the other; the child is *from* their meeting. The human, in al-Kirmani's anthropology, is the *child* of the meeting of *this world* and *that world* — not the fruit of *only* the natural order (al-Islah's truncation in Bāb 5) nor the fruit of *only* the upper order (al-Nusra's reversal), but *from both*, *brought to be between both*. *It is not permissible to attribute him to one of them to the exclusion of the other, because he is from both of them, and from both of them he came to be.*

Al-Kirmani extends the image with a precise distinction:

> From this it is permissible to attribute to this world that which has, from it, the course of matter — which is the receptive — and to attribute to that world that which has, from it, the course of form — which is what is received — as in the case of the child, who on one side has his parents (the highest, most honored form) and on the other side the one who carried that matter, the midwife. There are many proofs of this in our book known as "The Repose of the Intellect."

The doubled architecture is now stated as the *matter-form* dyad. *This world* gives the *matter* — the receptive surface, the body in *aḥsani taqwīm*. *That world* gives the *form* — the rational soul, whose perfection is *knowledge of tawhid* (taw-heed; the oneness of God). The image of the child names this precisely: the *parents* give the form (the inherited *image*, the *highest, most honored form*); the *midwife* (the *carrier of the matter*) gives the body its *bringing-to-be* in the world. The matter-form distinction Bāb 6 spent its dialectic securing is now articulated as the *anthropological architecture* of the human at the seam.

The bāb closes in prayer:

> May God Almighty guide us to the abode of His religion, and grant us blessing through the intercession of Muhammad and the Imams of the pure house. May God's blessings be upon them.

*The intercession of Muhammad and the Imams of the pure house.* The prayer names the *channel* through which the upper world reaches the human — the *Da'wa*, the *hudud*, the *prophetic-and-Imamic line*. The architecture of Bāb 4 (the rational soul is constituted by what it receives from the *hudud* through obedience and worship) returns at the bāb's closing as the *means by which the human comes to be the child of the meeting of the worlds*. The intercession is the mediation; the mediation is how the *form* descends to find the *matter* that is ready.

A line in the manner of Imam Ali ibn Abi Talib (peace be upon him), as the Ismaili Da'wa has long carried it from his sayings on self-knowledge, names the human's *between-place* from the inside:

> Your remedy is within you, and you do not perceive; and your disease is from you, and you do not see. You think yourself a small body, but in you the great world unfolds.
> (After the manner of Imam Ali ibn Abi Talib, on the human as microcosm; trans. condensed.)

*You think yourself a small body, but in you the great world unfolds.* The Alid line is the bāb's whole teaching compressed into a single saying. The *small body* is what the human appears to be from outside. The *great world unfolds in him* — the cosmic architecture, the seam of the worlds, the meeting of *this* and *that*, the child between the parents and the midwife.

The seam, in the end, is where the bāb has been all along. The cosmos has its three genera (mineral, plant, animal) and its higher orders (Soul, Intellect, Origin); the human gathers them. His *pairing* is not given but *enacted* — by what he accepts, whose party he takes. The proper pair is the angels, meaning the prophets and the Imams and those who follow them. The proper anthropology is *the child between the parents and the midwife*, *from both* and *to both*. And the proper vocation is the prayer with which the bāb closes — that we be guided to the abode of His religion through the intercession of Muhammad and the Imams of the pure house.

From the closing supplication of Imam Zayn al-'Abidin (peace be upon him), in the manner of *al-Sahifa al-Sajjadiyya*, a line that names the seam as a prayer:

> O God, You have made the human a meeting place between Your two worlds; let his receiving from the lower not deafen him to Your bestowal from the upper, and let his bestowal from the upper not separate him from Your provision in the lower; for the standing at the seam is the worship by which we draw near.
> (After the manner of Imam Zayn al-'Abidin, *al-Sahifa al-Sajjadiyya*, supplication for being kept at the seam of the worlds; paraphrased.)

*The standing at the seam is the worship by which we draw near.* The Du'a names the bāb's anthropology as a vocation. The seam — *the state of the child in his coming-to-be between his parents* — is not a place the human happens to occupy. It is the place *for which he was made*.
