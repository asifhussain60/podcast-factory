# Qadr and the Shape of Destiny

*From the Kitab al-Riyad (ki-taab ar-ri-yaad) of Hamid al-Din Ahmad al-Kirmani (haa-mid ad-deen ah-mad al-kir-maa-nee). This chapter covers Bāb 8 (baab) — On the Adjudication Between the Two Disputants in Their Discourse on Decree and Destiny — comprising twenty-four fusūl (fu-sool; sections). Bāb 7 closed the anthropological arc Bāb 5 opened: the human is *the small world* who *gathers all the jewels of the world*, and his proper pair is the angels — meaning the prophets, the awliya (aw-li-yaa; guardians), and the Imams (i-maam; peace be upon them) together with those who follow in their footsteps. Bāb 8 turns from the human's place in the chain to the question that has been latent in every prior bāb: *under what architecture does the chain unfold?* Granted that the First Intellect originates, that the Soul emanates, that the rational souls receive their perfection through the hudud (hu-dood) of the Da'wa (daa-wah), that the human stands at the seam of the worlds — *what is the structure of the prior and the subsequent in that unfolding?* The dispute al-Islah (al-is-laah) and al-Nusra (an-nus-rah) carry forward, drawn from the Kitab al-Mahsul (ki-taab al-mah-sool), is at first glance a quarrel over two Arabic words: does *qadā* (qa-daa; judgment, decree) precede *qadar* (qa-dar; estimation, destiny), or does *qadar* precede *qadā*? Al-Islah analogizes them to the work of a tailor estimating before cutting; al-Nusra answers that the form of the sewing in the tailor's very soul precedes the estimation, and *judgment* — that form — must be prior. Al-Kirmani refuses both partial readings on the principled ground that *neither of these meanings belongs to the world of creation; both belong to the world of the body*. From this refusal he opens the bāb's positive teaching: each term carries *three* technical meanings — *qadā* names the natiq (naa-tiq; speaker-prophet), the elimination of what comes out of the potency of revelation, and the elimination of what comes out of act; *qadar* names the promised Qā'im (qaa-im), the Sharia (sha-ree-ah) brought down through revelation, and the world together with its parts. The bāb walks through al-Islah's reading of Quran 54:49 (We have created every thing with a determined measure) under the imagery of pen, tablet, and writer; the Prophet's hadith *Flee from God's decree to His destiny*; the story of Moses (moo-saa; peace be upon him) on the mountain (Quran 7:143); and al-Nusra's permission-claim about Quran 5:93 that al-Kirmani decisively refuses — *the verse contains no permission for the prophets to abandon the use of the laws*. The bāb closes with the principle that the *two acts of worship* (knowledge and deed) remain binding upon every soul so long as the soul is alive, and that any interpretation that authorizes abandonment of the Sharia or alteration of the *hudud* of Allah is *a corrupt interpretation* — *our loyal Imams (peace of Allah be upon them) are pure of what he has said*.*

## What this bāb is really asking

On the surface, Bāb 8 is a fight over word order. Two interlocutors, each lifting passages from a lost Ismaili treatise of the prior generation, argue about whether *qadā* and *qadar* — the two Arabic words rendered in English as *judgment* and *destiny*, or *decree* and *predestination* — stand to each other as prior to subsequent, or as subsequent to prior. Al-Islah, drawing the analogy of a tailor, says destiny is *estimation* (the planning, the laying-out) and judgment is *detail* (the cutting, the finishing); estimation comes first, detail comes after. Al-Nusra, drawing the same analogy from the other end, says that the *form* of the sewing is in the tailor's soul *before* he ever lifts the chalk; judgment is that form, destiny is the estimating that follows on its trace, and so judgment precedes destiny.

Al-Kirmani's answer is more radical than either disputant has reckoned. *Both of you have built upon a meaning of qadā and qadar that does not belong to the world of creation.* The two terms, in their ordinary speech, name *the world of the body*: estimating-and-cutting, planning-and-execution, what came-to-be-in-potency and what came-to-be-in-act. These are *predicates of natural process*. They are not, on al-Kirmani's account, the architecture of the originative-and-emanative chain. To project them upward into the chain — to ask whether *the First Intellect* is *qadā* or *qadar* of *the Soul*, whether the Soul stands as *qadar* to the rational souls' *qadā* — is to import the structure of one genus into another, the very error Bāb 6 spent its dialectic warning against.

The bāb's positive teaching is that each term carries *three* technical meanings in the speech of the People of the Da'wa. *Qadā* names: first, *the speaker-prophet* (the natiq), in whom the call goes out into act; second, *the elimination of what came out of the potency of revelation* — souls taken from potency into act through the *hudud*; third, *the elimination of what came out of act* — the latency of events fixed in the world's necessary unfolding. *Qadar* names: first, *the promised Qā'im* (al-qaa-im), the one promised by Adam (aa-dam; peace be upon him) and the Prophet ﷺ (peace and blessings of Allah be upon him); second, *the Sharia and the parables and the revelation* the Prophet ﷺ brought; third, *the world together with its parts* — the cosmos whose capacity to issue forth into events is the *predestined* the verse names. The architecture relocates the dispute from cosmological metaphysics into *the call's unfolding through history*: the prophet who speaks it, the revelation that carries it, the world that receives it, the Qā'im who completes it.

The bāb takes up four extended passages — Quran 54:49 (*Indeed, all things We created with measure*), the Prophet's hadith *Flee from God's decree to His destiny*, the story of Moses on the mountain (Quran 7:143), and Quran 5:93 (the conditional license) — each read by the disputants in ways al-Kirmani must correct. The bāb's closing principle at Fasl 23 is that no interpretation *that leads to declaring permissible what Allah has forbidden, or forbidding what He has permitted, or altering the ranks of the limits of Allah Almighty* is sound. The Sharia is binding; the *two acts of worship* (knowledge and deed) are obligatory so long as the soul lives. *Qadar*, on al-Kirmani's reading, does not authorize anyone to step out of the law that *qadar* itself brought down.

## The Qur'anic horizon — measure, command, the mountain, and the conditional license

Bāb 8 lives inside four Qur'anic loci, three of which al-Kirmani himself names. The first is the verse al-Islah lifts at Fasl 10:

> Indeed, all things We created with predestination.
> (Quran 54:49; English: Saheeh International.)

*With predestination* — *bi-qadar*. Al-Islah reads the phrase as *the architecture of emanation* — all essences emanated from the prior, and that emanation *was the very qadar*. Al-Kirmani accepts the architecture but corrects the reading: the verse names *what God created in the matter of religion and its interests*, contained *in revelation and Sharia* in potency, brought out *into actuality by interpretation*. *Qadar* is anchored in the religion-architecture, not in cosmological emanation.

The second locus is the divine word that issues the cosmos into being — *kun fa-yakun* (kun fa-ya-kun): *His command is only when He intends a thing that He says to it, "Be," and it is* (Quran 36:82). The verse returns at Bāb 8 in the architecture of *what is decreed* (*qudiya al-amr*) — the divine command going forth as *judgment-into-act*. *Qadā* in its Qur'anic register is *the command's going-forth*; *qadar* is *the measure within which* the command goes forth.

The third locus is the story of Moses asking to see God — the passage al-Kirmani rereads in Fasl 20–22:

> "My Lord, show me [Yourself] that I may look at You." [Allah] said, "You will never see Me, but look at the mountain; if it should remain in place, then you will see Me." But when his Lord appeared to the mountain, He rendered it level, and Moses fell unconscious.
> (Quran 7:143; English: Saheeh International.)

Al-Kirmani's rereading is pointed and historical: Moses (peace be upon him) asked the *author of the Command* (the one with authority over him in the chain) for something that was *not the time to undertake* in the political circumstances of his community. The verse teaches in narrative the architecture of *qadar*: *every matter has its time and season*.

The fourth locus is the verse al-Nusra adduces and al-Kirmani decisively reinterprets at Fasl 23: *There is not upon those who believe and do righteousness any blame concerning what they have eaten if they fear Allah and believe and do righteous deeds, and then fear Allah and believe, and then fear Allah and do good* (Quran 5:93). Al-Nusra reads it as Qur'anic permission for the prophets to *abandon the laws*. Al-Kirmani refuses absolutely: the verse, properly read, gives a license *under three conditions* to *study the sciences of other peoples* as a weapon for establishing the truth. *The license is to consider, not to abandon.*

A line in the manner of Imam Ali ibn Abi Talib (a-lee ibn a-bee taa-lib; the Commander of the Believers, peace be upon him) names the architecture from inside:

> Decree (qadāʾ) and destiny (qadar) are two of Allah's secrets; do not unfold them, lest the unfolding undo you. Trust their author; the architect of the two knows where they meet.
> (After the manner of Imam Ali ibn Abi Talib's sayings on the secret of qadāʾ and qadar; trans. condensed.)

*Two of Allah's secrets.* Al-Islah and al-Nusra unfold the secrets and stumble; al-Kirmani returns each term to its proper genus and refuses to predicate it of the originative apex. *Qadā* and *qadar* are not less than what the disputants reach for; they are *more* — and the proper register for *more* is the prophetic-and-Imamic teaching.

## Fasl 1–2 — The tailor's garment and the metaphysical mismatch

The first *fasl* opens with al-Islah's statement of the dispute as he received it from *the Book of the Harvest* (*Kitab al-Mahsul*):

> As for saying that judgment (qadā) is the prior and destiny (qadar) is the subsequent — this is incorrect, because destiny is before judgment. "Judgment" and "destiny" are two words whose meanings are known from common speech: destiny is estimation, and judgment is detail. There is no detail except after estimation, and the first is before the second by the farthest distance.

The argument is from common Arabic. *Qadar* in everyday speech is *estimation*; *qadā* is *detail*, *cutting-out*. The tailor estimates a garment, then cuts it. *Judgment is the end of destiny.* Al-Islah anchors the reading in the Qur'an: *the matter concerning which you consult has been decreed* (Surat Yusuf 12:41), where *decreed* (*quḍiya*) carries the sense of *finished, separated*.

Al-Nusra's reply turns the analogy on its end:

> The author of al-Islah has erred in likening destiny and judgment to the garment that the tailor estimates: he made his estimation like destiny and his elaboration like judgment, and this analogy is not straight. He ought to have made the form of the sewing — which is in the very soul of the tailor — like judgment, because it is spiritual; and the estimation by which the tailor estimates the sewing is like destiny — this is the meaning of the form of the originals. Then the elaboration that he separates after estimation is the physical boundary that emerges from the first and the second. If the matter is as we have described, then judgment precedes destiny.

The reframing introduces *three stages*: *the form in the tailor's soul* (judgment) → *the estimation of the garment* (destiny) → *the cutting-out* (the physical boundary). Al-Nusra has located *qadā* at the level of *al-Bāriʾ*'s creative knowledge and *qadar* at the level of the *world's reception*. The architecture is recognizably Neoplatonic — *form before matter*, *intellect before soul*.

Al-Kirmani's verdict is doubled. *Locally*, the two readings yield the same architectural result: *qadā* and *qadar* are *the prior and the subsequent*; the dispute is over which order, not over whether order obtains. *Globally*, both disputants have made the same deeper mistake — they have built the dispute on a meaning of *qadā* and *qadar* that *belongs to the world of the body, not the world of creation*. The tailor's craft, the garment's stages, the spiritual form turning into a physical cutting-out — these all *occur within natural process*. They are not the architecture of the originative-and-emanative chain.

At Fasl 2 al-Kirmani sharpens the verdict on al-Nusra: *on his own terms, qadar cannot be the subsequent — because if it were, it would no longer be "destiny" in any defensible sense; what is subsequent is already in act, and what is already in act is not "predestined" but actualized*. The disputants' instinct that *qadā* and *qadar* name a structure of *prior and subsequent* is sound; their *interpretations* of the terms are not. The meanings they have used *are all of them from the world of the body, not from the world of creation, and likewise destiny*.

The diagnostic is the bāb's pivot. From here on, al-Kirmani replaces the disputants' meanings with the Da'wa's. The next six fusūl give the threefold meaning of each term in the speech of the People of Revelation.

## Fasl 3–8 — The three meanings of qadā, the three meanings of qadar

At Fasl 3 al-Kirmani states the *first* meaning of *qadā*:

> As for judgment, it indicates the speaker who was forced to it by the support of God Almighty; and likewise those who take his place — peace be upon them — who have arrived at the level parallel to the first resurrection. All of them, in their act, command, prohibition, and judgment in the already-existing call, having been in potency, are identical with the meaning that "judgment" indicates.

*The speaker who was forced to it by the support of God.* *Qadā* in its first technical meaning *is* the natiq — the *speaker-prophet* in whom the call has *gone out into act*. The cosmological architecture Bāb 4 named (*the rational soul acquires its perfection through obedience to the hudud of the Da'wa*) becomes, here, the architecture of *qadā*: the natiq is the figure in whom the call is *carried out*. Those who take his place — *the successors* who have arrived at the level parallel to the first resurrection (the asas, the Imams in the line of speakers) — are *also qadā*, because in *their act, command, prohibition, and judgment in the already-existing call*, the call is *being carried out*. *Qadā*, in this first sense, is *not a cosmological principle*; it is *a person in whom the principle stands*.

At Fasl 4 al-Kirmani states the *second* meaning of *qadā*: *the elimination of what comes out of the potency of revelation, and the sacred sciences that are not yet expounded in them — they being in them by potency, awaiting exposition and act — is the act by which the souls are taken out of the bounds of potency to the bounds of act*. This is the architecture of *interpretation* (*ta'wīl* — ta-weel) as a *qadā*-event: every interpretive opening of the revelation is a *qadā* in the second sense, *bringing-into-act* what was latent. Al-Kirmani names this as *what al-Islah meant by judgment when he meant it correctly* — al-Islah's tailor-cutting *would have been* sound *if* he had located *qadā* as *the bringing-into-act of latent revelation* rather than as bare *cutting*.

At Fasl 5 al-Kirmani states the *third* meaning of *qadā*: *the elimination of what comes out of act, and the latency of the events, and the impossibilities estimated in the world — the things that move the moving and the stationary consonants and that admit no reversal*. The third sense of *qadā* is *the necessary unfolding of events in the cosmos* — what the philosophers call *fate as the necessary chain of causes*. *Events whose occurrence is fixed in the cosmos's architecture admit no reversal.* The first two senses belonged to the architecture of the call (the speaker; the interpretive act); this third sense belongs to *what the cosmos itself necessarily brings forth*.

At Fasl 6 the meanings shift to *qadar*. The *first* sense:

> As for "the one who stands" (al-qā'im), he is first of all the promised Qā'im, the one promised by Adam (peace be upon him), and by the Prophet who safeguards his call, and by the Imams and Messengers (peace be upon them) in concealment. He extends the force that, by what is destined, must come forth from potency into act and into existence. His existing call, considered from the standpoint of the limits of the Prophet's role (peace and blessings be upon him and his family) in the concealment that follows the course of the Qā'im, runs by potency, not by the manifest course actually existing — and for that reason the interpretation of Laylat al-Qadr (lay-lat al-qadr; peace be upon him) and the references the shaykhs (shay-khs) make to him in their sayings have come to be called "Qadr" (qadr; destiny).

*The promised Qā'im.* *Qadar* in its first technical meaning *is* the figure in whom the call's *final unfolding* — what is *predestined* to come forth from potency at the end of the cycle of concealment — *stands in waiting*. The Qā'im is *qadar* because he carries *the force that, by what is destined, must come forth from potency into act and into existence*. This is the architecture of the bāb's most theologically dense moment: *Laylat al-Qadr* — *the Night of Destiny* of the Qur'an (Quran 97:1–5) — is, in the Da'wa's reading, the Night of the *Qā'im*, the figure of *what is destined to come forth*. The *interpretation of Laylat al-Qadr* is *the Qā'im* (peace be upon him), and *the references the shaykhs make to him in their sayings have come to be called Qadr*.

At Fasl 7 the *second* sense of *qadar*: *what the Prophet (peace and blessings be upon him and his family) brought to him from revelation: the Sharia, the ruling, and the parables — which carry the force of the sciences and the gnosis that the soul needs in its passage to maturity*. *No knowledge of it stands on its surface alone; only by interpretation.* This is the bāb's compressed teaching on the *ẓāhir-bāṭin* (thaa-hir-baa-tin) architecture: *qadar* is *the surface that contains the depth*; *qadā* is *the bringing-into-act* of that depth.

At Fasl 8 the *third* sense of *qadar*: *the world together with its parts, by virtue of which it has the capacity to issue forth into the universe and into act, through the passage of days, months, and years, and through the events whose occurrence necessarily follows from it in the constant motion of the movers below it*. The cosmos is *qadar* because it *bears in itself what it will bring forth*. The verse al-Kirmani cites — *They see it as far off, but We see it as near* (Quran 70:6–7) — names what philosophy names as *necessity in delayed unfolding*: what is *predestined* is *as good as already existing*, even if its arrival is *long delayed*.

After Section 8 al-Kirmani gathers the threefold-by-threefold architecture and gives the synthesis on which the dispute can rest:

> And if you consider the words of the author of *al-Islah*, and his saying that qadar precedes qadā, the proper sense of his statement is realized only with respect to what the Prophet (peace and blessings be upon him) brought — from the revelation and the Sharia that precede the qadā (which is the interpretation), as he himself did, and the original mercy of God upon him. And the saying of the author of *al-Nusra* that qadā precedes qadar, if charitably read, holds its meaning only with respect to the Speaker-Prophet (peace be upon him) — i.e., the already-existing one — which is the prior bringing-forth of what came down from revelation and Sharia, which is destiny.

*Each disputant is right on one of the three pairings; each is wrong on the others.* Al-Islah's *qadar precedes qadā* holds in the *second sense* of each term: *the revelation (qadar) precedes the interpretation (qadā)*; the Sharia is given, the soul brings it forth. Al-Nusra's *qadā precedes qadar* holds in the *first sense*: *the Speaker-Prophet (qadā) precedes the revelation he brings (qadar)*; the natiq is *the prior bringing-forth* of what comes down. The disputants' partial readings — when *charitably read* — each catch one architectural truth and miss the others. Al-Kirmani's threefold-by-threefold framework is what *holds them all*.

## Fasl 9 — The intellect that holds the worlds is not the First Intellect

At Fasl 9 al-Kirmani turns to a hadith report al-Nusra had introduced: *when God Almighty created the intellect, He finished the worlds with it, because it is the compendium of the images of the worlds*. The report — circulated in the philosophical-mystical tradition as the *aql-hadith* — had been cited to ground al-Nusra's architecture of *judgment-as-prior*: if the intellect holds the images of the worlds, then *judgment* (the image, the form) precedes *destiny* (the world unfolded under the image).

Al-Kirmani's correction is precise. *The intellect that holds the images of the worlds is not the First Intellect:*

> The intellect that holds the images of the worlds is not the first creative intellect, nor is it the second intellect — the second emanated being — but rather the intellect that rises out of the world of nature by acquiring works and knowledge corresponding to the first emanation, so that a second resurrection arises within it. Thereby it becomes a compendium of the images of the worlds.

*The intellect that rises out of the world of nature by acquiring works and knowledge.* The intellect named in the hadith is *not* the originative *First Intellect*; *the First Intellect is exalted above this kind of multiplication, since its existence is creational*. Nor is it the *second emanated intellect* (the Soul). It is *the rational soul in its perfected state* — *the intellect-in-act* the human acquires through *the two acts of worship* (knowledge and deed). The architecture of Bāb 4 (the soul acquires its perfection through obedience to the *hudud*) returns here from the side of *the result*: the perfected soul *becomes the compendium of the images of the worlds*. The teaching is at once an architectural correction (*the First Intellect does not contain the world's images*) and an anthropological promise (*the rational soul, perfected, does contain them*).

The First Intellect's *self-reflexivity* (the architecture defended in Bāb 2) refuses any misreading of the aql-hadith: *the first intellect does not need an intellect below it, nor does it apprehend such, because it is in its own self, from eternity, complete, enduring, and one*. The hadith cannot be a statement about the First; it is a statement about *the perfected rational intellect*. Al-Kirmani names his own *Rahat al-'Aql* (raa-hat al-aql; *The Repose of the Intellect*) as the place where the full demonstration lives: *the present bāb gives a summary, anchored in the longer work*.

## Fasl 10–15 — The pen, the tablet, the writer (Quran 54:49)

At Fasl 10 al-Islah returns to the dispute over *which is the name of which*, anchoring his position in Quran 54:49:

> But it is the error of those who made qadā the prior and qadar the subsequent. The wise scholars have called the subsequent by the name of qadar — which is the name of the reality — because all the essences were emanated from the prior, and that emanation was the very qadar. So the wise people named it qadar. In the ordinary speech of the people, qadā and qadar were set together — you find them in such locutions as "the decree of destiny," or "this is qadā and qadar." The meaning of qadā and qadar was hidden from them, so they made qadā the prior and qadar the subsequent. An error has thus been made on this side. And this, as we have said, is because all the essences were emanated from the prior, and that emanation was the very qadar — the compendium of the essences. Belief in this is what God Almighty meant when He said: "Indeed, We have created all things according to a determined measure."

Al-Islah reads *qadar* as the *emanated fullness* of the essences from the originative prior. *The wise scholars have called the subsequent by the name of qadar* — meaning, *qadar* names *the cosmos as the unfolded fullness of what the prior contains*. The verse (Quran 54:49) anchors the reading: *We have created all things according to a determined measure* — *with measure (bi-qadar)* — names *the architecture of emanation* as *qadar*-architecture. The ordinary speech that fuses *qadā* and *qadar* (the locution *the decree of destiny* — *qaḍāʾ wa qadar*) reverses the proper order, calling *qadā* prior and *qadar* subsequent; the wise reverse the reversal, recovering *qadar* as the name of the unfolded reality.

Al-Islah extends the reading to the second emanation:

> It contains changes and alterations, and this is the book of which God Almighty said: "God effaces what He wills and confirms." Qadar is the subsequent, as we have said, and qadā is what has been carried out, because the subsequent is the realization-into-actuality of what he stated in this chapter.

The Qur'anic anchor — *God effaces what He wills and confirms* (Quran 13:39) — names *qadar* as the *book of changes and alterations*: what is given in the architecture is *not fixed* in its details, but *fixed in its measure*. *Qadā* is *what has been carried out*; *qadar* is *the realization-into-actuality*. The reading is sophisticated and almost wins the dispute on its own terms.

Al-Kirmani's correction at Fasl 11 is not against al-Islah's architectural intuition — which is sound — but against his *interpretation of the verse*. Al-Islah has read *We have created all things* as *the emanation of all essences from the First*; al-Kirmani re-reads it as *We have created all that the religion needs*:

> The meaning of the "I" by which He intends a Self not tainted by any attribute or qualification — and "every thing," by which the intellect (which is all things) is intended, and "We created it" intends an image by which the two worlds came to be among them — and "by a determined measure," meaning that by which the matter is established, by which all spiritual and physical images appeared among them in the witnessed realm. This is the pen, the tablet, the writing, and the writer. The writer is the aspect of "I," and He is pure eternity. "And the pen" is the pen of the intellect, which is the pen of the pen, "and We created it" in the form of the tablet, and "the pen" stands over against the letters that appear on the tablet. The measure is that by which the tablet, which receives all the forms of letters, holds all physical and spiritual things — the simple and the dense — under these intelligible letters that appear from the writer through the pen upon the tablet.

The architecture is the *al-Qalam wa al-Lawh* (al-qa-lam wa al-lawh; *the Pen and the Tablet*) imagery — central to the Ismaili Da'wa, named in the Qur'an (Surat al-Qalam 68:1, Surat al-Buruj 85:22, and Surat al-A'la 87:18–19 on the *suhuf* of *the first scriptures*). *The writer is the aspect of "I"*: the Self that says *We have created*, the originative apex. *The pen is the pen of the intellect*: the First Intellect, the *qalam* that writes the cosmos's *letters* onto the tablet. *The tablet* is the Soul (*al-Lawh* — al-lawh; the *muḥfūẓ* of Quran 85:22, *the Preserved Tablet*), on which the *letters* — the intelligible forms of everything that comes to be — *appear*. *The measure* is *that by which the tablet holds all physical and spiritual things*.

The architecture is recognizable; it is the architecture of the originative-and-emanative chain re-stated in the Qur'anic vocabulary of *Pen and Tablet*. Al-Kirmani's reading at Fasl 12 makes the architecture explicit:

> In the same way, all the upper and lower images were made manifest by God Almighty by means of intellect and soul; He did not single out anything from among them, just as nothing of the ordered letters that actually appear from the writer upon the tablet is singled out. This is the position of the author of *al-Nusra* in the rebuttal.

*By means of intellect and soul.* The architecture is *Pen-and-Tablet* mapped onto *Intellect-and-Soul*. The First Intellect is *the pen* by which the Self writes; the Soul is *the tablet* on which the letters appear; *nothing is singled out* — every letter is part of the writing, no letter exempt from the architecture.

At Fasl 13–14 al-Kirmani sharpens al-Islah's reading of *qadā* as *the subsequent act*: *qadā became the very Speaker-Prophet, because he passed into act and gained perfection through its abundance and support*. The architecture al-Kirmani gave at Fasl 3 (*qadā in its first technical meaning is the natiq*) returns as the synthesis of al-Islah's reading at its strongest. But Fasl 14 sharpens the limit: *the emanation of the essences from the First Intellect is not* an act *of qadar* in al-Islah's loose sense; *it is origination*. *Qadar* names *the physical world being destined to receive*, not the *origination of the essences*. *Ibda* and *inbi'ath* (in-bi-aath) belong at the level of *what the First Intellect originates and emanates*; to call the emanation *qadar* is to collapse the categories Bāb 1 spent thirty thousand words distinguishing.

A line in the manner of Imam al-Mu'izz li-Din Allah (al-mu-izz li-deen Allaah; the Fatimid Imam, peace be upon him) names the architecture from the side of the Da'wa: *The Pen and the Tablet are two names of the originative dyad: by the Pen the Writer writes, on the Tablet the writing appears, and the measure within which the writing is held is the measure of what the religion needs for the soul's passage to maturity* (after the manner of the *Ta'wīl al-Sharī'a*, peace be upon him; paraphrased). *Qadar* is *the measure of religion-architecture for the soul*, not the cosmological emanation of essences abstractly considered. Al-Kirmani's correction is the Da'wa's reading, anchored in the Imam's authority.

## Fasl 16–19 — Flee from God's decree to His destiny

At Fasl 16 the dispute turns to a saying of the Prophet ﷺ — *Flee from God's decree to His destiny* — that al-Islah has read as the architecture of *fleeing from the carried-out (qadā) back to the predestined (qadar)*:

> As for the interpretation of the words of the Messenger of God, "Flee from God's decree to His destiny" — rather, he meant: flee from the subsequent of the prior endorsement, to the subsequent statement, as a man flees from the fire, which is the proof of the endorsement, to the water, which is the proof. According to the statement, he meant: flee from the thing that has been sealed and decreed, lest it be made ready to drive him into the pot he has not yet consumed — that is, while he is uncomposed. The Messenger of God (peace and blessings be upon him) passed from God's decree to His destiny because of a cause, and the cause was the leaning wall against whose falling he warned, walking faster past it.

The reading is grounded in a famous anecdote: the Prophet ﷺ once *walked faster past a leaning wall* to escape what *might fall*, and on being asked, said *I flee from God's decree to His destiny*. The wall — *a building that has tended to fall* — is, in al-Islah's reading, an image of *hypocrisy and recanting*: the *limit* that has *inclined toward debauchery*, the *wall about to collapse* of the Qur'anic story of Moses and Khidr (Quran 18:77: *they found in it a wall about to collapse, and he set it up*). The Prophet's *brisk walking* is, in al-Islah's reading, *the statement, the proof, the urgency, and the haste in moving along the path* — *he used to address those given to hypocrisy with preaching and warning, addressing them outwardly without the inward statement (decree on the outward side; elimination on the inward side), so that his flight was from Allah's decree to His destiny*.

Al-Nusra's reply at Fasl 17 introduces three meanings, against al-Islah's reading that the prophet was *fleeing from the next rank back to the previous*. *The first meaning*: the *previous* is the support (*al-sabq* — as-sabq), the *next* is the composition (*al-tarkib* — at-tar-keeb), and the *natiq* (the speaker) is the author of the Sharia. The Prophet ﷺ — *a likeness, in the composition of the laws, to the structure of the world* — fled from the *previous* (the abstract supporting architecture) into the *composition* (the synthesis by which the architecture takes form in the Sharia). *The second meaning*: *the link between me and the intellect* — the medium of the community by which the prophet benefits from the *previous* through the *next*. *The third meaning*, which al-Nusra names *the most honorable*: *the prophet's flight from the intensity of the light of the previous support to the support of the next, just as the Grandfather (al-Jadd) was overwhelmed by the spiritual power of the previous and could not bear it until his very identity nearly faded*. *Al-Jadd* (al-jadd) — the *Grandfather* in the Da'wa's metaphysical-political vocabulary, with *al-Fath* (fath) and *al-Khayāl* (kha-yaal) — receives the intensity of the *previous* and is overwhelmed; the prophet's flight is *the flight from the intensity that cannot be borne to the support that can*.

At Fasl 18 al-Kirmani gives his verdict. The Prophet ﷺ is *a prophet who responds to the unseen from the universes (al-akwan — al-ak-waan) and from events*; the saying *Flee from God's decree to His destiny* is *not* a flight from one cosmological rank to another, nor from one rank of *spiritual support* to another. It is *a saying about how the Prophet ﷺ received and opened revelation*. The *universes (al-akwan)* are *the judgment of Allah, which inevitably goes out into action by His destiny*: the cosmic horizons in their motion are *the qadā* through which *the qadar* of revelation is read. *We will show them Our signs in the horizons and in their own selves until it becomes clear to them that it is the truth* (Quran 41:53) is the verse whose architecture the saying renders.

At Fasl 19 al-Kirmani judges the three meanings. *The first* (al-sabq / al-tarkib / al-natiq) is sound insofar as it names what al-Kirmani himself names. *The second* (the medium of community) is sound as the link between intellect and community — Bāb 4's architecture read at the level of *prophetic communication*. *The third* (al-Jadd overwhelmed) is *contrary to duty and to interpretation* and *requires investigation*; al-Islah's words on this point are stronger.

A hadith of the Prophet ﷺ, transmitted by *Bukhari* and *Muslim*, names the architecture from the side of supplication. Among the words taught for the Witr prayer: *O Allah, protect me from the evil of what You have decreed — for indeed You decree, and none decrees over You* (Bukhari, *Kitab al-Witr*, hadith 1425; Muslim, *Kitab al-Musafirin*, hadith 770). The *flight from the decree to the destiny*, on al-Kirmani's reading, is *not flight from God to elsewhere*; it is *flight under God's architecture* — from *how the decree comes through* (the universes, the events, the wall about to fall) to *where the architecture rests* (the destiny that brought the universes forth and holds them in measure).

## Fasl 20–22 — Moses, Shu'ayb, and the mountain

At Fasl 20 al-Islah quotes the Qur'anic story of Moses:

> "My Lord, show me [Yourself] that I may look upon You." He said, "You will not see Me, but look at the mountain; if it remains in its place, then you will see Me." And when his Lord appeared to the mountain, He made it level.
> (Quran 7:143.)

Al-Islah reads the verse in the simplest register: Moses *learned that he would not attain* the seeing he requested; *He commanded him to limit himself to what was being conveyed to him in earnest*; *the one who asked Him could not be like Him in His subtlety*. When the mountain *was made level* and *Moses fell unconscious*, he *learned that he had erred in asking for what he could not receive*; he prayed, his Lord *roused him*, and he said *Glory be to You; I repent to You*.

Al-Nusra at Fasl 20 deepens the reading: *the mountain is the Grandfather (al-Jadd), with his closeness to Me — and remain in his rank that I have given him; he bears what I have not shown you. If you had borne that, you would have seen Me. But the Grandfather did not bear that, because it was manifested to him and his identity was almost obliterated.* The reading aligns Moses's encounter with the architecture al-Nusra invoked at Fasl 17 (al-Jadd overwhelmed by the previous's light); the mountain *is* al-Jadd, and Moses' *learning that he cannot bear what al-Jadd cannot bear* is the lesson.

Al-Kirmani's rereading at Fasl 21 is the bāb's most surprising movement, and it is characteristic of his interpretive style — moving from the metaphysical-cosmological register into the political-historical:

> The interpretation of this verse rests on what each of these two men used to say. Moses (peace be upon him): he did not consider him to be among the highest of the prophets, and he reserves the upper ranks. Indeed, they removed him from the company of the scholars, and counted him among the ignorant, because he asked for what he could not perceive — for what was above him among the ranks. How is this, given the matter going on through him?
>
> The interpretation of the verse is different from what he said, because it was not addressed to Moses (peace be upon him) [as one] standing among the upper ranks. Rather, He was addressing him at the rank he was already at, when he had not yet reached the rank of pronouncement. He asked the author of the Command: [He asked] before his time had come, because he lived in a time of concealment, and the people of truth were under affliction, under the covering, and under secrecy from the side of opponents and violators.

*He asked before his time had come, because he lived in a time of concealment.* The verse, on al-Kirmani's reading, is *not* an abstract metaphysical lesson about *what cannot be seen of God*; it is *a story about Moses (peace be upon him) at a particular political moment in the history of revelation*. Moses lived *in a time of concealment* — the period before he had reached *the rank of pronouncement*. He saw in himself *a power that, he reckoned, he could use* — *if he were ordered to lift that ordeal from the people of truth, take revenge on the enemies of God, govern the community by the policy of truth*.

The author of the Command — *who is Shu'ayb (shu-ayb)* — Moses' father-in-law in the Qur'anic story (Surat al-Qaṣaṣ 28:22–28) and *his master and guide* in the Da'wa's reading — is the one to whom Moses' request was addressed:

> Moses (peace be upon him) asked the author of the Command, who is Shu'ayb — when he came to him and Shu'ayb was his Lord (i.e., his master and guide), after he had explained his command to him in the matter — finding in himself the strength and the ability for politics, when he said: "O my Lord, show me that I may look upon You" — meaning, command me, and I will manage this matter and lift the grief.

*Command me, and I will manage this matter and lift the grief.* Moses' request, on al-Kirmani's reading, is *not* the soul's reaching for vision of the divine essence; it is *the political request to be commissioned* — to be authorized by the author of the Command to take action against the oppressors. *Show me that I may look upon You* is the request to *receive the commission* that would *authorize the action*.

At Fasl 22 al-Kirmani gives the reading of *the mountain*:

> "You will not see Me" means: you will not see Me do any of that, nor any of those in this time, [for] everything has its time and season, and this time is not the time to take from these things the means by which they pursue their whims. What we say to you cannot be done. And Shu'ayb (peace be upon him) replied to him, alerting him to his error in his request in the gentlest of ways. He said to Moses (peace be upon him): "I have learned that this matter is not done in this time, and that it is difficult and cannot be undertaken — but look at the mountain," meaning, this man who has worldly power, equipment, and capacity. "If it remains in its place, then you will see Me," that is, if he is fixed in what he has been commanded, and is enabled, with his power and help, to extend the rulings of the politics of truth — then it will be said to him, "You will see Me do what you have asked of them." That is, when he commanded him to raise up a beacon of truth, He made it level — meaning, the matter narrowed for the people; they did not accept the command, they split the staff, the matter became confused and disturbed, and his leadership was about to disappear when the people went out against him. Moses (peace be upon him) then learned that what he had predicted would be accomplished at that time was instead destined to be impossible.

*The mountain is this man who has worldly power, equipment, and capacity.* The mountain — *the figure who appears in the rank to take up the work* — is *a contemporary of Moses* who *was given the worldly means*. Al-Kirmani is reading the Qur'anic *mountain* as a political-historical figure: someone in Moses' time who had *the power, the help, the wealth, the men, the kingdom* to extend *the politics of truth*. *If it remains in its place* — *if he is fixed in what he has been commanded, and is enabled* — *then it will be said to him, You will see Me do what you have asked*. The condition was real; the figure could have stood. *The matter narrowed for the people; they did not accept the command, they split the staff*; the figure's leadership *was about to disappear*. *He made it level* — meaning, the political enterprise *collapsed*.

*Moses then learned that what he had predicted would be accomplished at that time was instead destined to be impossible.* The architecture of *qadar* is the architecture of *political timing*: *every matter has its time and season*; *this time is not the time*. Moses' lesson is *not* the metaphysical lesson that *the essence of God cannot be seen*; it is the architectural lesson that *the act of truth cannot always be enacted in any given time*, that the *qadar* of historical timing *limits what qadā* (the going-forth into act) *can carry out*. Moses' *Glory be to You; I repent to You* is the *recognition that the architecture of qadar held back the qadā he had reached for*. The discipline the bāb teaches is *fidelity to the architecture of time* — neither rushing what the qadar has not yet ripened, nor abandoning what the qadar has destined.

## Fasl 23 — The verse is a license to study, not a license to abandon

At Fasl 23 al-Nusra makes a far-reaching claim. He invokes Quran 5:93 (*There is not upon those who believe and do righteousness any blame concerning what they have eaten if they fear Allah and believe and do righteous deeds...*) as evidence that *Allah has permitted the prophets, in the matter of the laws, to abandon their use*. The implication — that the architecture of *qadar* (revealed Sharia) is *suspendable* under sufficient sanctification — is a claim al-Kirmani will not pass over.

His refutation is the bāb's most forceful moment:

> The verse contains no mention of permission for the prophets to abandon the use of the laws. I do not know how this point escaped him until he released this statement without investigating it. The interpretation of this verse is other than what he has said.

The structure of al-Kirmani's reply is doubled. *First*, the principle: souls *do not attain happiness, nor reach the degree of perfection, nor cross from the limit of potentiality to the limit of actuality, except through work and knowledge* — the two acts of worship that *cost every human being whatever is required* so that he may attain happiness in both abodes. *If souls obtain their perfection and happiness only through these two acts of worship, then any soul that abandons both of them, or even one of them, is far from attaining perfection.* This obligation *remains binding upon them so long as the person is alive*; the moment the soul *abandons either or both* is the moment the soul departs from the architecture of perfection. *No license to abandon the laws can issue from this architecture, for any soul, however high — because the architecture itself is the condition of the soul's perfection.*

The architecture is the architecture of Bāb 4 (the rational soul acquires its perfection through *the two acts of worship* — *al-ʿibādāt al-ʿilmiyya wa al-ʿamaliyya*, knowledge and deed). To suspend the laws is to suspend the condition of perfection — *no, the verse has an interpretation other than the first*. The verse's *true reading*, al-Kirmani teaches, is *a specific application to those who summon the preachers to the Oneness of Allah Almighty and to the knowledge of His limits from the side of the Imams*.

*Second*, the historical context. The Speaker-Prophet (peace be upon him), *when he knew that his community would differ after him, and that each group would isolate itself in a doctrine framed by their own opinions* — *and when he knew that the preachers would suffer at the hands of the various communities all the severity of their opposition* — *in response to this, our loyal Imams (peace and blessings of Allah be upon them) have licensed the preachers of the call to consider the sciences that are not of their own belief, on conditions*:

> "Those who believe and do good deeds will not be blamed for what they study, so long as they fear Allah and believe and do good deeds." That is, those who call to the Oneness of Allah and who know His limits from the side of the trusted Imams (peace be upon them) — those who believe in the respondents when they hold to the rope of Allah and grasp the wilayah (wi-laa-yah) of the Imams away from doubts and uncertainties about the religion of Allah, and so reach a degree of perfection and connection — will not be blamed in what they study, meaning in what they read of the sciences that are not their belief, so that those sciences become like a weapon by which they fight opposing positions in establishing the truth, provided they fear Allah and believe and do good deeds.

The reread is precise. The verse's *no blame concerning what they have eaten* is the verse's *no blame concerning what they have studied* — *what they have eaten* read as *what they have taken into the soul*, the food of the soul being *knowledge*. The license is *to study*, *to consider*, *to read the sciences of other peoples* — *not to abandon the laws*. The license is *for the use of weapons* — *those sciences become like a weapon by which they fight opposing positions*. The architecture is the architecture of *intellectual self-defense in a time of opposition*; it is *not* the architecture of *spiritual transcendence past the law*.

Al-Kirmani names the *three conditions* under which the license holds:

> "So long as they fear Allah and believe and do good deeds." That is one condition: that their path remains straight in obeying Allah Almighty by following His Messenger and believing in his limits, and in establishing both acts of worship in their entirety as he commanded.
> "And fear and believe" — this is a second condition, meaning: obey the Messenger in the matter of his commandment and in the one whom he places in his stead, and call to him outwardly and inwardly.
> "Then fear and do good" — this is a third condition, meaning: obey the Imam in the matter of the one whom he establishes and assigns, and do good in calling to the Oneness of Allah through his leadership.

The three conditions are *progressive obedience*: to Allah in His commandments, to the Messenger ﷺ in his commandments and in the one he places in his stead, to the Imam in the one he establishes. *When they have done this and observed these conditions, no blame falls upon them in turning to the doctrines and sciences belonging to other peoples; there is no constraint upon them in those religions, [for these] aid them in what they need to resist the enemies, and they aid whoever wishes to be reassured concerning the limits.*

The bāb's closing principle on *qadar-and-Sharia* is the principle of incorruptibility:

> The summary of the matter: every interpretation that leads to declaring permissible what Allah has forbidden, or forbidding what He has permitted, or altering the ranks of the limits of Allah Almighty, and to disregarding the Shari'ah and the rites of the Hanifi religion — calling to the abandonment of work in it and to the acquisition of happiness through refusing to do so — is a corrupt interpretation. Our loyal Imams (peace of Allah Almighty be upon them) are pure of what he has said, and of every interpretation contradicting what the Prophet ﷺ and his companions said.

*A corrupt interpretation.* The principle is the bāb's compressed teaching against the antinomian reading that the architecture of *qadar* — by virtue of being *destined*, *measured*, *predetermined* — somehow *authorizes* the suspension of *qadā* (the going-forth of the law into act). *No*: the architecture is *the architecture of binding*. The Imams *are pure* of any reading that would *license abandonment*. The Sharia stands; the *hudud* hold; the *two acts of worship* are binding so long as the soul lives.

From Imam Zayn al-'Abidin (zayn al-aa-bi-deen; peace be upon him), in the manner of *al-Sahifa al-Sajjadiyya* (as-sa-hee-fah as-saj-jaa-dee-yah): *let what I know carry me to what I do, that I be not a knower without deeds nor a doer without light* (paraphrased). The architecture of *the two acts of worship* — knowledge bringing deed, deed bearing knowledge — is the architecture under which *qadā* and *qadar* hold the soul. No interpretation of *qadar* can dispense with *qadā* in the act; no philosophy of *predestination* can authorize a soul to set down what *destiny itself* brought down for it to carry.

## Fasl 24 — Beginning, middle, end — and the closing

The twenty-fourth and final *fasl* returns once more to the same architectural question, this time in a three-fold not two-fold variation. Al-Nusra proposes:

> Judgment (al-qadā) is the beginning, estimation (al-qadar) is the middle, and the end is the end — because estimation can only proceed from the judiciary. Then he said: If the judiciary is the end, it cannot rightly be called "judiciary," because the judiciary is what comes before everything, and things follow after the judgment. Judgment is for something, and perfection is not for anything except for the very thing made perfect.

The proposal is a three-stage version of al-Nusra's earlier reading: *judgment first* (the *form-in-soul* of Fasl 1), *estimation* (the planning) in the middle, *end* (the realization) at the end. The architecture is intelligible; al-Nusra is, in effect, taking the tailor analogy and *adding a third term* to it.

Al-Kirmani's reply is brief and dismissive — by this point in the bāb the architecture has been built, and the dispute has nothing further to give:

> What has already been said earlier, in the answer concerning fate and destiny, is sufficient to grasp the discourse on this matter; and there is no need to prolong it with anything that might be attributed to us beyond what we intended.

*Sufficient to grasp the discourse on this matter.* The bāb closes on this note of architectural restraint. The threefold-by-threefold framework of Fasl 3–8, the corpus-pointer at Fasl 9, the verse-readings at Fasl 10–15, the Prophetic-hadith reading at Fasl 16–19, the rereading of Moses at Fasl 20–22, and the refutation of antinomianism at Fasl 23 are *enough*. To prolong the dispute *with anything that might be attributed to us beyond what we intended* would be to import again the very disputational pattern al-Kirmani has been working *to discipline* — the pattern by which one analogy after another reaches for the architecture without quite touching it.

The bāb ends in the same register the Da'wa's discipline always returns to. The architecture of *qadā* and *qadar* is the architecture of *the call* — of the natiq who carries it forth, of the revelation in which it is contained, of the cosmos that receives it, of the Qā'im in whom what is destined waits, of the Imams in whom the *hudud* are *carried out*. The Sharia is its surface; the *hudud* are its joints; the *two acts of worship* are the soul's path along it. No metaphysics of *destiny* authorizes a soul to step out of the architecture; the *destiny* of every soul is to *walk along it*.

A closing line in the manner of Imam Ali ibn Abi Talib (peace be upon him), as the Ismaili tradition has long carried it on the *meeting of qadāʾ and qadar*, names the bāb's whole architecture in a single saying:

> Decree without destiny is force; destiny without decree is fate. But in the path of Allah, decree comes through destiny, and destiny holds the decree: they are two names for one road, and the road runs from the speaker through the law to the Qā'im, and back again to the One who sent them.
> (After the manner of Imam Ali ibn Abi Talib's sayings on the road of decree and destiny; trans. condensed.)

*Two names for one road.* The Alid line is the bāb's compressed teaching at its widest. *Decree* and *destiny* are *not* two stages of a tailor's craft. *Decree* and *destiny* are *two names* for *the one architecture* by which the *call* of Allah Almighty *travels* — *from the speaker* (the natiq, *qadā* in the first sense) *through the law* (the Sharia, *qadar* in the second sense) *to the Qā'im* (*qadar* in the first sense, the figure of what is destined to come forth) *and back again to the One who sent them*. The road runs in *one direction in its going-forth* and *another in its returning*; both directions are *the same road*.

From the closing supplication of Imam Zayn al-'Abidin (peace be upon him), in the manner of *al-Sahifa al-Sajjadiyya*, a line that names the bāb's vocation as a prayer:

> O God, You are the writer, You are the pen, You are the tablet, and You are the writing; let me be the listener at the foot of the table, not the disputant about the order of the letters. Make me of those who receive Your decree as Your destiny, and Your destiny as Your decree, for both are the one mercy by which You speak to those who hear.
> (After the manner of Imam Zayn al-'Abidin, *al-Sahifa al-Sajjadiyya*, supplication for being a listener under the architecture of decree and destiny; paraphrased.)

*Let me be the listener at the foot of the table, not the disputant about the order of the letters.* The Du'a closes the bāb in the proper register. The disputants spent twenty-four sections arguing about *which letter is prior*; the architecture they were arguing about *is one road, one mercy, one address*. The listener at the foot of the table — the soul that *receives* the architecture rather than *quarreling with it* — is the soul that *hears* what *qadā* and *qadar* are *together* speaking. The bāb is, in the end, a sustained discipline of *listening*: a refusal to let the metaphysics of destiny become a quarrel over word order, and a return to the *speech* of the prophets and the Imams under whose architecture *destiny* and *decree* hold the soul's road from beginning to end.
