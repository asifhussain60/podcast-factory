## The Esoteric Meanings of the Pillars of Islam

Imam Jaʿfar al-Ṣādiq (a.s.) declared: "The pillars of Islam rest upon seven invocations." In the manifest world, all things display a sevenfold structure; absent such division, they remain imperceptible. The human face contains seven apertures; the letters of Islamic practice number seven; the week comprises seven days. All creation manifests this sacred number.

Yet in the spiritual world, souls once turned their hearing toward the summons of the spiritual adversary, an inclination that engendered seventy spiritual maladies. To remedy these afflictions, the Originator appointed seventy medicines, each concealed within the seven pillars of Islam (duʿāʾim al-islām). Each pillar encompasses ten healing remedies that restore both body and soul, purging the contaminations of Iblīs and Shaytān so that the soul returns to its primordial state.[^cite-1] The outward observances of Islam refer to inner realities.

### Wilāyat (Guardianship)

**Exoteric Dimension**

Wilāyat (guardianship) denotes the love of Allāh, love of His Messenger, and obedience to His ordinances. In the interpretation of this obligation, the *ḥikmah* (wisdom teaching) affirms that no human being would have possessed wilāyat in their heart had they not loved the True Guides in the primordial light-world. Since souls acquired love for these celestial beings in the spiritual realm, wilāyat from them became possible in this manifest age. In the spiritual world itself, the station of remaining steadfast in tawḥīd (divine unity) is itself called wilāyat. The inner wilāyat is the turning of one's repentance toward the Originator.[^cite-2]

### Ṭahārah (Purity)

**Exoteric Dimension**

With purity comes the concept of impurity, for without impurity there would be no need for purity. Ritual purity encompasses four domains:

**Esoteric Dimension**

In the primordial world, we heard the summons of the Originator and simultaneously heard the word of the adversary. Not only did we obey his commands, but we invited others to follow him. From these three actions—listening, obeying, and summoning others—impurity arose in this world. The esoteric meaning of purity is to distance oneself from ignorance of the Originator and to cleanse oneself from the filth of the adversary and his corrupted concepts. In the spiritual world, all our faculties were complete and pure; yet through hearkening to the devil's word, acting upon it, and calling others to it, these faculties became marred and now appear in this world as spiritual defilement and external impurity. Through the practice of the pillars, we must restore these faculties to their original sanctity.[^cite-3]

### Ṣalāt (Prayer)

**Exoteric Dimension**

In contrast to the apparent five daily prayers, there are five persons who are the Five Purified Ones: Muḥammad, ʿAlī, Fāṭimah, al-Ḥasan, and al-Ḥusayn (a.s.). These five have appeared in every age and era, and obedience to them has been obligatory upon the people of each time. Similarly, in the spiritual realm, the standing-firm in the daʿwa (the summons) is called ṣalāt. The Originator issued five invitations to His followers, and various commands were incumbent upon those who accepted them—the fulfillment of these commands constitutes the true obligatory prayer.

**Esoteric Dimension**

The inner ṣalāt is the establishment of the daʿwa and obedience to its representatives.

### Zakāt (Obligatory Alms)

**Exoteric Dimension**

In the inner spiritual world, when forty portions of the knowledge of the Originator (a.s.) accumulate in a soul, one portion of this knowledge is termed zakāt. Zakāt takes several forms:

**Zakāt al-Māl (Alms on Wealth)**

Zakāt becomes obligatory only upon one whose wealth has accumulated forty units for a complete year; he is then commanded to give one part as zakāt for every forty. Esoterically, zakāt is owed to the Ḥujjat al-Zamān (the Proof of the Age), who is the son of the Imam destined to become Imam. Zakāt is obligatory on forty dīnārs after one year. The interpretation is this: when the children of the Imam (a.s.) gather in one of the ordained stations—whether as a believer (muʾmin), an assistant (makāsir), a dignitary (maʿdūn), or a summoner (dāʿī)—and after one year one of them is elevated to the next rank, this elevation itself is the zakāt. Thus, when forty believers assemble, one is elevated to the rank of assistant.[^cite-4]

**Zakāt al-Khums (The One-Fifth Share)**

Should knowledge arrive unexpectedly as a gift, one-fifth is distributed—this is called *khums*. When Sayyidinā Ismāʿīl ibn Hibatallāh was unexpectedly blessed by his Imam with knowledge, he revealed the *khums* of these sciences in his treatise *al-Zaytunat al-Thāniyya*. The esoteric principle is this: Zakāt belongs to the Proof, while the *khums* belongs to the Imam.

**Zakāt al-Fiṭr (Alms of Breaking the Fast)**

These are sciences that a Muslim may teach to any other Muslim, yet it is obligatory that he impart them. For this reason, *fiṭrah* is given for each adult and child in the household. When we extend our hands to acquire the necessities of life—food, garments, and the like—we obtain what is ours through the blessing of Zakāt al-Fiṭr.

**Zakāt al-Zirāʿah (Alms on Agricultural Produce)**

Land is an emblem of the noble daʿwa (summons), and cultivation is the cultivation of faith. The believers are the fruits and plants. If the field is watered from heaven and cultivated thereby, then ten percent of the produce is obligatory as zakāt. If water is obtained through labor from a stream or well, then five percent is due. Esoterically: if the knowledge and wisdom of the Imam is directly channeled by the Imam himself, one must pay ten percent of that knowledge. If a summoner gathers wisdom from diverse books and sources and prepares discourses through his own intellectual labor, then five percent of that knowledge is obligatory.[^cite-5]

**Zakāt al-Anʿām (Alms on Livestock)**

For every forty animals, one is given as zakāt. That is, when forty followers gather under a Ḥujjat, zakāt is paid by elevating one's station in the way of Allāh—the Imam (a.s.) elevates one of those forty to a higher rank. Similarly, when forty proofs accumulate, the Imam raises one to the rank of Imam and brings him into the covenant.

### Ṣiyām (Fasting)

**Exoteric Dimension**

Allāh has ordained fasting, both outwardly and inwardly, to instill *taqwā* (God-consciousness) and reverence. The Merciful did not intend to leave us hungry and thirsty, but rather, in a state of fasting, we abstain from eating, drinking, and marital relations for a time, even when we possess the means to indulge.

**Esoteric Dimension**

In the spiritual world, when the Originator imparted knowledge to all His followers, He made it obligatory upon them not to disclose this knowledge to the undeserving, despite their capacity to do so—they were commanded to come in a state of spiritual fasting. Similarly, in this present age of occultation, we observe a spiritual fasting, refraining from teaching the knowledge of our daʿwa to unbelievers. When the time of *ifṭār* arrives—when the Mahdī (a.s.) appears—all these truths will be proclaimed from the pulpits.[^cite-6]

**Statement**

Human health is maintained through eating and drinking; it is beneficial to do so in the eleven other months. Yet in the way of Allāh, the human soul is nourished through the hunger and thirst of the fast day.

### Ḥajj (Pilgrimage)

**Exoteric Dimension**

The meaning of ḥajj is to journey toward the House of Allāh, an obligation imposed once upon every Muslim and believer in their lifetime. By "Allāh," the Imams refer to the Imam of the Age (Imām-uz-Zamān), and by "House" (*Bayt*), His inner gate (*Bāb al-Bātin*), which gathers all spiritual forms. The intention to perform ḥajj is the intention to unite oneself with the inner sanctum of the Imam. Since this fusion of our form within the Imam's sanctuary occurs but once in a lifetime, ḥajj is obligatory only once.

**Esoteric Dimension**

The journey to the House of Allāh once in a lifetime has esoteric significance: by Allāh is meant the Imam of the Time, and his house is the collection of spiritual forms he possesses. Ḥajj occurs once in a lifetime because only one part of a person's being undergoes this transformation. Five stations of ḥajj exist through which pilgrims approach the Kaʿbah; correspondingly, five spiritual channels of the Imam exist through which the spiritual forms reach the Imam. These five consist of the Imam's four inner gates (*Abwāb al-Bātin*) and one outer gate (*Bāb al-Ẓāhir*), represented by the Summoner.

### Jihād (Striving)

**Exoteric Dimension**

Jihād literally means striving and exertion. Jihād with the sword is the lesser jihād, while jihād against one's self is the greater jihād. The true meaning of jihād is to restrain oneself from transgression and to imprison the base self within the bounds of Islam and truth. For this reason, when the Prophet (s) returned from battle, he said, "We return from the lesser jihād to the greater jihād." The Companions inquired, "O Messenger of Allāh! What is the greater jihād?" He replied, "Struggle against your self!" To attain a place in the sanctuary of the Imam of the Age, one wages jihād by resisting the invitations of Iblīs and Shaytān in this world and traversing the stages of patience and tribulation.

**Esoteric Dimension**

Jihād means to strive. The fulfillment of the Imam's commands and the confrontation with the inner Iblīs—the commanding self (*al-nafs al-ammārah*)—constitutes the inner jihād.[^cite-7]

## The Slaughter of the Kid and the Sacred Covenant

One of the conditions of ḥajj is the sacrifice. Sacrifice is obligatory upon every pilgrim performing ḥajj. The ritual animal offering is not obligatory upon one who merely performs ḥajj, though it is deemed desirable. Similarly, the Prophet (s.a.w.s.) encouraged all Muslims in the sacred sanctuary to sacrifice on the day of ʿĪd al-Nahr and expounded its benefits through his prophetic traditions. Apparently, there is no inherent merit or benefit in this ordinance save that the wealthy provide meat to the poor, who rarely obtain it, permitting them to enjoy good food and satisfaction for several days afterward.

### The Outer Sacrifice

⟪ar-quote:ظاهر⟫

When the Infallible Imams (a) were questioned concerning its wisdom and inner secrets, they replied: "Apparently, the purpose of slaughtering an animal is to execute the animal in Allāh's name and by His command, such that man takes it as sustenance and incorporates it into his body. The animal should be elevated from the baseness of brute nature to the highest level of human spirituality, so that it dissolves into human blood and becomes a force of humanity. This goal cannot be achieved until the animal's life is extinguished and its autonomous movements cease, becoming subordinate to man."

### The Inner Sacrifice

⟪ar-quote:باطن⟫

In contrast to this apparent sacrifice, the Imams—who are the people of remembrance—possess another sacrifice, called the covenant or sacred pledge (ʿahd sharīf). This pledge obliges every human being to restrain himself from lustful desires, to abandon base human habits, and to cultivate noble moral character. Through this, one attains the attributes of angels and spirituality—the first degree in the Hereafter. When a believer takes the pledge, every action of that believer proceeds not from his own will but from obedience to the command of the Imam (a.s.), just as an animal ceases its movement after being slaughtered and becomes wholly subservient to man.

### The Specific Animal for Sacrifice

⟪ar-quote:مخصوص جانور کی ذبیحت⟫

Not every animal is suitable for sacrifice. Only goats, cattle, and camels may be slaughtered. Esoterically, not every human being is brought into the covenant of the Imam of the Age, but only the *Mustajīb* (the Respondent—one who accepts the summons) is presented with the covenant.[^cite-8]

### The Distribution of Parts in Sacrifice

⟪ar-quote:ذبیحت میں حصے⟫

The scholars have stated that evil forces ride upon animals, and their number is fixed. One form dwells in a goat, seven in a cow, and ten in a camel. When these animals are slaughtered, they are separated from those who are truly rewarded. Similarly, the development of humanity is possible only through this separation. The yearning that occurs at the time of sacrificing an animal is the yearning for the separation of the wicked from the righteous; otherwise, there is no suffering for the animal slaughtered according to the Sharīʿah.

### The Soundness and Wholeness of the Sacrificial Animal

⟪ar-quote:جانور کا صحیح الاعضا اور صحت مند ہونا⟫

Defective organs are not acceptable for sacrifice. Apparently, it makes sense that a sick animal should not be offered, lest it transmit disease to the consumer. But what is the inner reason for requiring complete organs? This signifies that only one who is pious and correct in religious belief may enter into the sacred pledge with the Imam (a.s.). He must be free from hypocrisy and *shirk* (associating partners with Allāh), and must not deviate from obedience to the Imam (a.s.).

Defective limbs mean a deficiency or excess in the organs of an animal. Just as an apparently defective animal is unsuitable for sacrifice, so too is one suffering from the disease of doubt unfit for the covenant. He must be free from the harmful qualities of the Guru and firm in his faith—sound and whole. The hypocrite who decreases from or increases beyond the proper station shall not be included in the daʿwa and the covenant of the Imam of the Age (a.s.).[^cite-9]

### The Animal Facing the Qiblah

⟪ar-quote:جانور کو قبلہ رخ لٹانا⟫

The inner *qiblah* (direction of prayer) is the essence of the Imam (a.s.). This means that one who takes the pledge must direct one's entire being toward the Imam of the Age.

### Watering the Sacrificial Animal

⟪ar-quote:جانور کو پانی پلانا⟫

Before taking the covenant, the significance and importance of the pledge must be proven and clarified through both the outward rational proofs and the inward rational proofs of the Imam of the Age, and through the wilāyat (guardianship) of the Imam of the Age. Water is a metaphor for knowledge. That is, one should not enter anyone into the pledge while they remain in ignorance; rather, one must distinguish between true and correct belief and false summons, separating the two clearly.[^cite-10]
