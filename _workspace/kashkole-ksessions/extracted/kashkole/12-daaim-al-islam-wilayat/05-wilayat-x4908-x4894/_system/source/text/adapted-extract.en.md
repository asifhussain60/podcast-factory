## The Designation (Naṣṣ) of ʿAlī by the Messenger of Allāh

One day ⟪ar:رسول اللہ (ﷺ)⟫ was standing in the miḥrāb of the Mosque of Qubāʾ when ⟪ar:سورۃ النصر⟫ was revealed to him, bearing the intimation that his appointed term was drawing near. He was given the sign that he would soon meet his Lord, and that his Lord longed for his meeting; he was likewise given the glad tidings that the conquest of ⟪ar:مکہ معظمہ⟫ would be granted to him. When the Messenger of Allāh disclosed to the community that his departure was imminent, the people were stricken with grief and said: "O Messenger of Allāh! Whatever pertains to the sharīʿa we settle by coming to you, asking you, and obeying your command. What shall become of your community once you are gone?" He replied: "Whoever among you has followed my command, let him follow the command of ʿAlī after me."[^cite-1]

Then came the conquest of Makka. Through ʿAlī he shattered every idol set within the Kaʿba. And since the season of pilgrimage had at last come within his reach, he resolved to perform his first and final Ḥajj in that year, in the tenth year of the Hijra — the pilgrimage that would come to be called ⟪ar:’حجۃ الوداع‘⟫ (the Farewell Pilgrimage). Because the Prophet himself was undertaking the rites, the people gathered in vast multitudes that year. When the Ḥajj was complete he said: "I am setting out from ⟪ar:’جحفا‘⟫ for Madīna." The people supposed that, as a worshipper enters the mosque for the Friday prayer by one path and returns home by another, so the Prophet had come to the pilgrimage by one road and would return by another. Yet in this the Messenger of Allāh bore a singular wisdom.

When he reached ⟪ar:جحفا⟫, a revelation descended commanding him to announce the wilāya (guardianship) of ʿAlī. He paused, and then moved on. Jibrīl came once more with the same revelation, and again he paused and continued. When he reached ⟪ar:’غدیر خم‘⟫, he feared that the throng would disperse and the pilgrims would scatter to their homes. At that very moment the verse descended upon him a third time, bearing the sternest rebuke from Allāh:

By this verse Allāh warned His Prophet that, were he to withhold the command entrusted to him, the entire labour of his prophethood would be undone, as though no message had ever been delivered. In the same breath he received the assurance that Allāh would protect him from the malice of the people.[^cite-2]

A note on the terms: in Arabic, *ghadīr* means "pond," and *Khumm* is the name of the pond where this halt was made.

### Did the Messenger of Allāh Disobey His Lord?

Some have held that the Messenger of Allāh ought to have obeyed the command at its first descent, and that by pausing he disobeyed his Lord. The reply is plain: did the Messenger of Allāh not know better than the community what is obedience and what is disobedience to Allāh? Could he who taught the umma the very meaning of submission himself be guilty of its breach? God forbid.

The truth is that the Messenger of Allāh wished this severest of verses to descend from Allāh, so that the gravity of ⟪ar:أمر ولایت⟫ (the matter of the wilāya) of ʿAlī might be proved by the very weight of the warning. He sought a guarantee of safety; and when the glad tidings came in the phrase ⟪ar:وَٱللَّهُ يَعْصِمُكَ مِنَ ٱلنَّاسِ⟫, he did not pause for a moment longer, but at once proclaimed the wilāya of ʿAlī.

When the Messenger of Allāh had received from his Lord the promise of ⟪ar:عصمت⟫ (protection), he halted at that very spot at Ghadīr Khumm. He said: "Stop those who are with me, turn back those who have gone ahead, and wait for those who lag behind, until the entire company is gathered." Then he ordered that the thorny shrubs and brambles at that place be cleared away — a sign, the dāʿīs have read, of the clearing away of every rancorous heart that would later despise ʿAlī. It was a gathering for which the heavens themselves had longed since their creation. A pulpit was raised from the saddles of camels, and he ascended it and delivered a sermon. He then put to the people this question: "Have I not conveyed to you every command of Allāh? Did I not teach you purification, the prayer, the zakāt, the fast, the Ḥajj, and the jihād? Have I withheld any command of Allāh from you? Did I not bring you forth from the darkness of ignorance?" They replied with one voice: "O Messenger of Allāh, may our fathers and mothers be ransomed for you! We were as beasts, and you have made us men." Then he asked: ⟪ar-quote:الست اولا بکم من انفسکم؟⟫ "Have I not more right over you than your own souls?" The people answered: ⟪ar-quote:بلا یا رسول اللہ!⟫ "Indeed, O Messenger of Allāh, you are dearer to us than our own souls." He put the question to them three times, and three times they returned the same answer. Then he raised Mawlānā ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib so high that the whiteness of his underarms could be seen, and said: "If I have more right over you than your own souls, then I am your master; and as your master I declare to you—"

After this the Holy Prophet and Mawlānā ʿAlī prayed two rakʿas and withdrew to a tent. Thereupon came ⟪ar:ابو بکر⟫, ⟪ar:عمر⟫, ⟪ar:عثمان⟫, ⟪ar:طلحا⟫ and ⟪ar:زبیر⟫ — the foremost of them — saying: ⟪ar-quote:بق بق یا ابن ابی طالب کنت مولائی و مولایا لکل المؤمنین و المؤمنات⟫ "Bravo, O son of Abī Ṭālib! From this day you are our master, and the master of every believing man and woman." They each took the hand of the Messenger of Allāh and of Mawlānā ʿAlī, and kissed their feet. The Messenger of Allāh, ⟪ar:رحمت للعالمین⟫ (the Mercy to the Worlds), watched the bearing of these hypocrites, for he knew well what they would later do to ʿAlī. Then he said: "O people, those among you who are hypocrites bear two hearts; if I wished, I could point them out. But I do not wish to do so. I say only this: if you desire Paradise, drive out the rancour for ʿAlī from your hearts and love him — otherwise the Fire awaits you."[^cite-3]

Then the Prophet fell ill, and he wished to set down in writing what he had pronounced concerning ʿAlī. He called for a pen and parchment by which to record ʿAlī's testament, but ʿUmar prevented him, saying that the Messenger of Allāh was speaking deliriously from his illness. So the ⟪ar:رحمت للعالمین⟫ departed from this world. ʿAlī performed every rite of his washing and burial and laid him in the sacred grave, while the rest of the community busied themselves over the choosing of a new caliph.

## The Outward Caliphate of ʿAlī

No sooner had the Prophet closed his eyes than the Muhājirūn and Anṣār gathered at the accursed ⟪ar:بنو سقیفہ⟫ — the Saqīfa of the Banū Sāʿida — to choose the next caliph. There the strongest among them, ʿUmar, declared: "We choose Abū Bakr as our caliph." None dared contradict him, and so Abū Bakr became the first caliph. ʿUmar had nominated Abū Bakr on the understanding that Abū Bakr in turn would appoint ʿUmar after him. Thus after Abū Bakr came ʿUmar, and after ʿUmar came ʿUthmān. Over those thirty years, the caliphs visited many cruelties upon ʿAlī and the Ahl al-Bayt. The land of Mawlātnā Fāṭima was seized. ʿUmar struck her with the lash and threw down the door of her house, an outrage that brought about the miscarriage of the infant Muḥsin. Her house was set ablaze. A horseman such as ʿAlī was tethered with a rope and dragged through the marketplace. ʿAlī and his companions bore these outrages in silence, for Allāh had commanded patience.

When ʿUthmān had been killed and none was willing to assume the caliphate, the people were driven at last to the door of ʿAlī. They found him seated and stitching his torn sandal — and from this is taken his epithet ⟪ar:’قاصف النعل‘⟫ (the Sandal-Stitcher). He asked them: "O people, what worth do you reckon this sandal of mine?" They answered: "O ʿAlī, what could this tattered sandal be worth?" He said: "By Allāh, in my eyes the worth of this torn sandal is greater than your caliphate. Yet because the people have pressed me so much, I accept the outward caliphate for the sake of the survival of Islam." He charged them openly to call him ⟪ar:’خلیفۃ اللہ‘⟫ (the Caliph of Allāh), and to acknowledge that it was not they who were appointing him.

By that time the people had grown so unacquainted with him that, when his caliphate was announced in the mosque and he rose to make his way forward, those near him took hold of him and made him sit down, saying: "O shaykh, where are you rising to? It is ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib who is called for! We have heard great tales of his exploits! He is ⟪ar:حیدر کرار غیر فرار⟫ — the lion who charges and never flees." He answered: "O brothers, let me go; I am ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib." With that he rose and went straight to the pulpit of the Prophet. There he delivered a sermon whose like has no rival in any chronicle. Its opening was as follows:

### Why Did ʿAlī Remain Silent for Thirty Years?

The answer is contained in a judgement once delivered by ʿAlī himself. Two women came to him with a single child, each claiming the child for her own. He commanded his servant Qanbar: "O Qanbar, take the sword and cut the child in two, and give each woman a portion." Hearing this, the true mother cried out: "What are you doing, O ʿAlī? Give the child to this other woman — even though it is mine. At the least let it live." Since Islam was the child of ʿAlī, and he was a mother grieved for her child and anxious for its survival, ʿAlī bore the silence of thirty years. Had he not done so, Islam would have been torn apart and divided into many sects. Were he to have demanded his right, neither did Dhū al-Faqār lie idle in its scabbard, nor had the strength of the arm that uprooted the gate of Khaybar departed from him.[^cite-5]

## The Wilāya and Maʿrifa of the Five Pure Ones

One day Mufaḍḍal came to Imām Jaʿfar al-Ṣādiq. The Imām asked: "O Mufaḍḍal, do you recognise Muḥammad, ʿAlī, Fāṭima, Ḥasan and Ḥusayn with the recognition that is their due? Have you arrived at the truth of their maʿrifa (gnosis)?" Mufaḍḍal said: "O my master, what is the truth of their maʿrifa?" He replied:

"O Mufaḍḍal, know that whoever attains the true maʿrifa of these is a believer of the highest rank of faith."

Mufaḍḍal said: "O my master, then make them known to me." The Imām said:

"O Mufaḍḍal, know that these noble ones know all that Allāh has created and brought forth from non-being. They are the manifestations of the piety (taqwā) of Allāh. They are the treasurers of the heavens, the earths, the mountains, the deserts and the rivers. They know the number of the stars in the sky and the angels of the kingdom. They know the weight of the mountains, the measure of the waters of the seas, and the count of the streams and springs. No leaf falls from any tree without their knowing it. They know every grain in the darkness of the earth, and there is nothing wet nor dry but it is contained in the clear Book — for it is they who know it and they who are it. They are versed in the science of what was, what is, and what shall be."

Mufaḍḍal said: "O my master, I understand it, I confess it, and I believe it." The Imām replied: "Yes, O Mufaḍḍal, you have spoken aright. O noble one upon whom blessing has descended, O pure one whose purity is established — happiness be upon you, and upon every one who holds such faith." [^cite-6]

(Manāqib Ahl al-Bayt, peace be upon them.)

## The Luminous Essences of Allāh

Allāh, Most High, created His beloved Muḥammad Muṣṭafā from a light that issued forth by His own grandeur and majesty. This is the very divine light that shone for Mūsā ibn ʿImrān upon Mount Sinai, the light he had not the strength to behold: at its sight he cried out, fell to the ground, and was overcome.

When Allāh willed to create His beloved, He divided this light into two portions. From the first He created Muḥammad, and from the second ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib. Apart from these two essences, no other being was fashioned from this light. Allāh created them by His power and breathed into them of His Spirit. He cast upon them an image of Himself and took them for His own. They are His witnesses over His creation, His vicegerents among His servants, His seeing eye and His speaking tongue among them. He entrusted to them His knowledge, granted them the gift of unveiling it, and disclosed to them His hidden secrets. The one He called His soul, and the other His self. Each of them is the support and confirmation of the other; in this manner neither can subsist without his companion.

Outwardly they appear in the human form (al-haykal al-nāsūtī), but inwardly their being is joined to the breath of divine Spirit (al-rūḥ al-lāhūtī). Their outward is anatomical and creaturely; their inward is attributed and bound to Allāh. They have appeared among the people in the form of people, that the people might see them and bear their company.[^cite-7]

## The Accounting of the Soul (Muḥāsabat al-Nafs)

Amīr al-Muʾminīn (Commander of the Faithful) ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib once turned to those gathered about him and said, "Shall I tell you of the wisest and the most foolish among the people?" They answered, "Yes, O Messenger of Allah." He then drew the contrast in plain terms: the wisest is the one who calls himself to account and who labours, while still in the world, for what awaits him in the realm of the Barzakh after death; the most foolish is the one who lets his nafs (the lower self) run after its appetites and who clings to false hopes, extending his desires while his allotted span quietly shortens. To bind one's heart to those vain hopes — and then to ask God to ratify them — is, on this teaching, the very signature of folly.

A man asked the Imam how a person might undertake this muḥāsabat al-nafs (the accounting of the soul). He instructed him to make the close of every day a tribunal in miniature. When night falls after the day's exertions, he taught, one should withdraw, fix one's attention upon oneself, and speak as one's own examiner: "O soul! The day that has passed will never return. God will ask you, 'What did you do in it?' Did you remember God and engage in His praise? Did you fulfil the rights of your brother in faith, or ease his hardship? Did you guard the honour of his household in his absence and the sanctity of his heirs after his death? Did you restrain yourself from the backbiting of a believing brother? Did you behave towards every Muslim as a Muslim ought? Remember all your works of this day."

The believer is then to weigh his hours item against item — recitation of the Qurʾān against forgetfulness, the praise of God against negligence, the remembrance of sins against the recollection of His forgiveness, blessings upon Muḥammad and his pure family and prayers upon ʿAlī and the good (al-Ṭayyibīn) against the curse of Iblīs and his party — and so to renew his allegiance to Amīr al-Muʾminīn, accepting his command and disavowing his enemies who usurped the rights of the Ahl al-Bayt.[^cite-9] The exercise is thus not a private examination of conscience alone but a daily ratification of one's walāya (allegiance and spiritual authority of the Imam) to the rightful guides — a discipline by which the believer keeps his covenant fresh.

Should the believer, on such a night, find his book of deeds sound, he is to praise the One who enabled him. Should he find error, he is to seek forgiveness, resolve not to return to it, and invoke blessings upon the Prophet and his Family in compensation. To this, in the report transmitted from the Imams, God responds: "Because of your walāya towards My saints and your disavowal of My enemies, I will not be severe with you over your sins; I do not press the charge."

(*Manāqib Ahl al-Bayt*)

## The Walāya of Zulaykha

Imam Jaʿfar al-Ṣādiq narrates that Zulaykha sought the king's permission to go to Yūsuf. "Are you not afraid," the king asked her, "to come before him after what you once did?" She answered, "I do not fear from the one who fears God."

When she arrived, Yūsuf saw at once that the colour of her face had altered, and he asked what had befallen her. "Praise belongs to God," she said, "who has made kings into slaves through disobedience, and slaves into kings through obedience." Yūsuf asked her what had compelled her to act as she once had towards him; she replied, "Your beauty, and the radiance of your countenance."

Yūsuf then said: "What would you do were you to behold the Messenger who is to come in the last days, whose name is Muḥammad? His beauty, his exalted character, and his generosity surpass mine by every measure." Zulaykha replied, "You have spoken the truth." Yūsuf asked her how she could know this. "From the moment you uttered his blessed name and described his qualities," she said, "his love entered my heart."[^cite-10]

At that, the Lord addressed Yūsuf: "What Zulaykha has said is true. I love her on account of the love of My Beloved that has taken root in her heart." And in that hour He commanded the prophet Yūsuf to take her in marriage. Her recognition of the Prophet — and through him, of the cycle of friends of God — is preserved here as the first opening of her walāya: a love that was the kindling of every later love.

(*Manāqib Ahl al-Bayt*)

## The Conversation between the Messenger of Allah and Abū Dharr

Abū Dharr al-Ghifārī relates that he was sitting one day in the house of Umm Salama, attentive to the Messenger of God, when ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib entered. The Prophet's face brightened at the sight of him; he kissed ʿAlī upon the forehead, then turned to Abū Dharr and said, "O Abū Dharr! Do you know who has just come to us?" Abū Dharr answered, "O Messenger of God, he is your brother and your cousin; he is the husband of Fāṭima, the lord of the youth of Paradise; he is the father of al-Ḥasan and al-Ḥusayn."

The Messenger of God said: "O Abū Dharr! ʿAlī is the radiant and luminous Imam. He is the lofty banner of the Lord of the Worlds and the great gate of God's mercy; whoever desires to reach God must enter through this gate. O Abū Dharr! He is the establisher of justice, the guardian of God's sacred boundaries, the helper of His religion, and His decisive argument against the creation. Through him and through us, God will continue to set up a proof among the nations — for I have sent a Messenger into every nation."[^cite-11]

"O Abū Dharr! God has appointed seventy thousand angels at each pillar of His Throne, whose praise and whose glorification is to pray for ʿAlī and for the Shīʿa (followers) of ʿAlī, and to curse his enemies. O Abū Dharr! Were it not for ʿAlī, truth would not have been distinguished from falsehood, nor the believer from the unbeliever, nor would God have been worshipped — for it was ʿAlī who subdued the polytheists until they entered Islam and worshipped God. Were it not for them, there would be no reckoning of reward or punishment. There is no veil between God and them; they themselves are the veil and the curtain."

Then the Messenger of God expounded the verse:

That to which you invite the idolaters is heavy for them; and Allah draws to Himself whomever He wills." He guides him who turns to Him.

"Whoever desires to be guided," the Prophet continued, "must come to know the walāya of ʿAlī. Whomever God wills to seal the heart of, He keeps far from the knowledge of ʿAlī. O Abū Dharr! ʿAlī is the standard of guidance, the proof of piety, the firm rope of God, the guide of My friends, and a beacon of light for those who obey Me. His friends are the believers and his enemies the unbelievers. Whoever severs his bond with him goes astray himself and leads others astray; whoever denies his walāya is a hypocrite."

## The Walāya of the Black Slave-Girl

ʿAmash relates: I once saw a slave-girl in Medina whose face was dark and whose eyes were sightless. She was distributing water to the people and saying, "Drink, in the friendship and love of ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib." Some time later, I saw her again in Mecca, and her eyes were now sound. She was still drawing water for the people, but now she said, "Drink, for the love of him by whom God has given me sight."

I went up to her and said: "I saw you in Medina, blind, calling people to drink in the friendship and love of ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib; and today I see you in Mecca with your sight restored. Tell me what happened."

She said: "One day a man came to me and said, 'O servant-girl, do you confess the walāya and the love of ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib?' I said, 'Of course.' Then he prayed: 'O God, if this servant-girl is truthful, restore her sight to her.' By God, through the blessing of his prayer my eyes were opened and I could see. I asked him, 'Who are you?' He answered, 'My name is Khamdār, and I am one of the Shīʿa of ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib.'"

(*Manāqib Ahl al-Bayt*)

## Recognition of ʿAlī through His Luminous Station

Salmān al-Fārisī and Abū Dharr once came before the Commander of the Faithful and asked, "O Amīr al-Muʾminīn, what is the light by which you are known?" He replied that the Messenger of God (peace and blessings be upon him and his family) had said, "Knowledge of ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib through the light is knowledge of God Most High, and knowledge of God through the light is the pure religion (al-dīn al-khāliṣ)."

The Commander of the Faithful continued: "The weight of a person's deeds is lighter in my walāya (guardianship) than even his innermost being. O Salmān, no believer's faith is complete until he recognises me as light. When he has come to know me as light, he is a believer whose heart God has tried with faith and whose breast has been opened to Islam; he alone is a believer and a keeper of his religion. Whoever falls short of this is a doubter and a whisperer of Satan. O Salmān and Jundub, knowledge of me is indeed knowledge of God, and knowledge of God is knowledge of me — and this is the pure religion." God Most High has said:

> ⟪quran 98:5⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَمَآ أُمِرُوٓا۟ إِلَّا لِيَعْبُدُوا۟ ٱللَّهَ مُخْلِصِينَ لَهُ ٱلدِّينَ حُنَفَآءَ وَيُقِيمُوا۟ ٱلصَّلَوٰةَ وَيُؤْتُوا۟ ٱلزَّكَوٰةَ ۚ وَذَلِكَ دِينُ ٱلْقَيِّمَةِ ‎⟫
> *And they were commanded not, but that they should worship Allah, and worship none but Him Alone (abstaining from ascribing partners to Him), and perform As-Salat (Iqamat-as-Salat) and give Zakat: and that is the right religion.*
> And they were commanded only that they should worship Allah, devoting themselves to Him, devoting their faith to Him, and that they should perform As-Salat (Iqamat-as-Salat) and pay the poor-rate. That is the right religion.
> — **Quran 98:5 (inline widget)**

It is through my walāya that sincerity in the worship of God comes to maturity. The religion of the ḥanīf is the affirmation of the prophethood of Muḥammad al-Muṣṭafā (peace and blessings be upon him and his family), for the religion of Muḥammad is the simplest and easiest of paths. The very purpose of prayer is the acknowledgement of my walāya: whoever has taken us as his shelter has truly established the prayer — and the establishment of prayer is the heaviest and most arduous of stations. The giving of zakāt signifies the recognition of the rank of the Imāms, and this is the upright religion of God. The Qurʾān bears witness that ⟪ar:’دین قائم‘⟫ requires sincere tawḥīd, together with the confession of prophethood and walāya. Whoever follows this has indeed attained the religion.[^cite-13]

O Salmān and Jundub! A tried believer is one for whom, when something comes to him by our command, God opens his breast to its acceptance, and he entertains no doubt regarding it. Whoever asks 'how?' or 'why?' has slipped into denial. So obey the command of God, for we are the very ⟪ar:أمر اللہ⟫ itself. God has made me trustee over His creatures and has appointed me His vicegerent over His lands, His cities, and His servants. He has bestowed upon me what neither describers can describe nor mystics can comprehend. If you know me in this manner, you are true believers.

O Salmān and Jundub! God has said:

> ⟪quran 2:45⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَٱسْتَعِينُوا۟ بِٱلصَّبْرِ وَٱلصَّلَوٰةِ ۚ وَإِنَّهَا لَكَبِيرَةٌ إِلَّا عَلَى ٱلْخَشِعِينَ ‎⟫
> *And seek help in patience and As-Salat (the prayer) and truly it is extremely heavy and hard except for Al-Khashi'un [i.e. the true believers in Allah - those who obey Allah with full submission, fear much from His Punishment, and believe in His Promise (Paradise, etc.) and in His Warnings (Hell, etc.)]. *
> and seek help through patience and prayer, and indeed the prayer is difficult except for those who humble themselves.
> — **Quran 2:45 (inline widget)**

Here patience signifies ⟪ar:محمد مصطفی صلی اللہ علیہ وآلہ وسلم⟫, and prayer signifies my walāya — which is why He at once added, ⟪ar-quote:وَإِنَّہا لَكَبِيرَة⟫, "Indeed, it is exceedingly heavy." But He also made plain that only the people of khushūʿ (humble submission) can bear it: these are the people of humility who have received insight from our light, and whom God has therefore set apart. Most people acknowledge the prophethood of the Messenger and entertain no doubt or dispute concerning him; it is my walāya that the majority deny. In another place He says:

> ⟪quran 22:45⟫
> ⟪ar:‏فَكَأَيِّن مِّن قَرْيَةٍ أَهْلَكْنَهَا وَهِىَ ظَالِمَةٌۭ فَهِىَ خَاوِيَةٌ عَلَىٰ عُرُوشِهَا وَبِئْرٍۢ مُّعَطَّلَةٍۢ وَقَصْرٍۢ مَّشِيدٍ ‎⟫
> *And many a township have We destroyed while it was given to wrong-doing, so that it lies in ruins (up to this day), and (many) a deserted well and lofty castles!*
> how many a town have We destroyed while it was a wrong-doer, so that it lies on its roofs, and how many a well have fallen in ruins, and how many a palace in ruins!
> — **Quran 22:45 (inline widget)**

The towering palace here is ⟪ar:محمد (ﷺ)⟫, and the abandoned well is my walāya — denied by the people, and from which therefore no one has drawn water. Whoever denies my walāya gains nothing by his confession of the prophethood of ⟪ar:حضرت محمد (ﷺ)⟫.

O Salmān and Jundub! We are the secrets of God that are never laid bare, and the light of God that cannot be seen. We are His grace for which there is no recompense. I cause death and I give life by God's leave. I know what you eat and what you store in your houses, and I know what is in your hearts. All of the Imāms from among my descendants share in this knowledge; they may act upon it whenever they will, for we are in truth one. Our first is Muḥammad, our middle is Muḥammad, our last is Muḥammad — so all of us are Muḥammad. Whoever recognises us in this way has fulfilled his religion. Therefore make no distinction between us. What we will is what God wills, and what God wills is what we will. Every misfortune falls upon the one who denies our excellence. God has bestowed upon us the Greatest Name by which we have access to the heavens, the earth, the celestial spheres, and even the fire. Everything obeys us: the sky, the earth, the sun, the moon, the stars, the mountains, the trees, the animals, the rivers, the heavens, and the fire. Yet despite all these qualities, we eat and drink and walk in the marketplaces. We are God's servants who do not outstrip Him in speech and who obey His commands and prohibitions. He has made us immaculate and pure. Whoever loves us, God will not punish him; whoever bears us enmity, God will not show him mercy.

O Salmān and Jundub! Muḥammad and I were one light, glorifying God before all who glorify Him, and before any creature had been brought into being. Then God divided this light between the Prophet and his successor. To one half He said, "Be Muḥammad!" and to the other half, "Be ʿAlī!" For this reason the Prophet (peace and blessings be upon him and his family) said, ⟪ar-quote:أنا و علی من نور واحد⟫, "I and ʿAlī are of one light," and he said, ⟪ar-quote:أنا من علی و علی منی ولا یودی عنی الا أنا و علی⟫, "I am from ʿAlī and ʿAlī is from me; none fulfils the trust on my behalf save myself and ʿAlī."[^cite-14] Pointing to this, God used the words ⟪ar-quote:أنفسنا و أنفسکم⟫ in the verse of Mubāhala — a sign of the unity of the two of us in the world of lights (ʿālam al-anwār). And God's saying, "If the Prophet dies or is killed," means: whether the Prophet should die or the successor be slain — for the two of them are one ⟪ar:’شئ واحد‘⟫, one ⟪ar:’معنی واحد‘⟫, and one ⟪ar:’نور واحد‘⟫. We are united in meaning and attribute, though distinct in body and name. We are therefore one and the same in the world of souls, just as the Messenger of God (peace and blessings be upon him and his family) said: ⟪ar-quote:أنت الروح التی بین جنبی و کذا فی عالم الاجساد انت منی و انا منک ترشنی وارثک انت منی بمنزلہ الروح من الجسد⟫ — "You are the soul that lies between my sides; so too in the world of bodies you are of me and I of you, you are my heir and I am yours; your standing with me is that of the soul to the body." In another verse God has said:

> ⟪quran 33:56⟫
> ⟪ar:‏إِنَّ ٱللَّهَ وَمَلَٓئِكَتَهُۥ يُصَلُّونَ عَلَى ٱلنَّبِىِّۚ يَٓأَيُّهَا ٱلَّذِينَ آمَنُوا۟ صَلُّوا۟ عَلَيْهِ وَسَلِّمُوا۟ تَسْلِيمًا ‎⟫
> *Allah sends His Salat (Graces, Honours, Blessings, Mercy, etc.) on the Prophet (Muhammad PBUH) and also His angels too (ask Allah to bless and forgive him). O you who believe! Send your Salat on (ask Allah to bless) him (Muhammad PBUH), and (you should) greet (salute) him with the Islamic way of greeting (salutation i.e. As&shy;Salamu 'Alaikum).*
> Allah and His angels send blessings upon the Prophet. O you who believe, send blessings and peace.
> — **Quran 33:56 (inline widget)**

This means: send blessings upon Muḥammad and submit (sallimū) to ʿAlī. Thus God has joined us in one body and distinguished us by attributes and qualities. He has said, "Send blessings upon the Prophet, and accept ʿAlī." Sending your blessings upon the Prophet alone profits you nothing unless you accept the walāya of ʿAlī.

When Abū Bakr was sent to Mecca to deliver the renunciation of the polytheists, Jibrīl descended and said, "O Messenger of God, your Lord commands you either to do this yourself or to send someone of your own house." So the Messenger of God (peace and blessings be upon him and his family) dispatched me after Abū Bakr to recall him. O Salmān and Jundub, how then can a man who lacks the standing even to convey a single message of the Messenger of God to the people become the Imām and leader of the community?

O Salmān and Jundub! In his time, Muḥammad (peace and blessings be upon him and his family) was the Speaker (nāṭiq) and I was the Silent One (ṣāmit); in every age, the nāṭiq and the ṣāmit are one. So Muḥammad is the Lord of the Gathering and I am the Lord of the Resurrection; Muḥammad is the warner and I am the guide; Muḥammad is the Master of Paradise and I am the Master of the Rejection; Muḥammad is the Lord of the Cistern and I am the Lord of the Banner; Muḥammad is the bearer of Revelation and I am the bearer of Inspiration; Muḥammad is the worker of miracles and I am the wielder of marvels. Muḥammad is the ⟪ar:خاتم النبین⟫ and I am the ⟪ar:خاتم الوصین⟫; Muḥammad is the Straight Path and I am the One Who Treads It; Muḥammad is the All-Merciful and I am the Prophet — I am the Great Tiding!

> ⟪quran 78:1-3⟫
> ⟪ar:‏عَم يَتَسَآءَلُونَ ‎ ‏عَنِ ٱلنَّبَإِ ٱلْعَظِيمِ ‎ ‏ٱلَّذِى هُمْ فِيهِ مُخْتَلِفُونَ ‎⟫
> *What are they asking (one another)? About the great news, (i.e. Islamic Monotheism, the Quran, which Prophet Muhammad (Peace be upon him)brought and the Day of Resurrection, etc.), About which they are in disagreement.*
> What is it that they ask one another about? About the great news about which they are at variance.
> — **Quran 78:1-3 (inline widget)**

I am the seeing eye of God, the speaking Book among His creatures. I am the light of God that is never extinguished. I am the gate of God through which one enters, and I am His proof over His servants.

O Salmān! God has said, "He casts the Spirit (al-rūḥ) upon whomever He wills of His servants," and this Spirit is given to none save him to whom command and authority have been entrusted. So I revive the dead, and I know all that is in the heavens and on the earth. I am the keeper of the Book. Muḥammad is the one who upholds the proof, and I am the proof of God over creation. The Spirit ascended with him to heaven. I am the one who set Noah in the ark; I was the companion of Jonah in the belly of the fish; I am the one who carried Moses across the sea and destroyed Pharaoh and his hosts; I am the one to whom alone the title "Heir of the Prophets and the Apostles" has been given; through me was the prophethood of Muḥammad (peace and blessings be upon him and his family) brought to completion. I am the one who set the rivers and the seas in motion; I am the one who released the springs into the earth. I am as the Father of the world. I shall send down the chastisement of the Day of Judgement. I am the teacher of Moses' teacher, Khiḍr; I am the teacher of David and Solomon. I am Dhū'l-Qarnayn. I am the one who, by God's command, levels the heights and depths and spreads out the earth. I am the one who will send down the chastisement of the Day of Darkness. I am the herald who shall call from a distant place. I am the Beast of the Earth. I am the one to whom the Messenger of God said, "You are both sides of Dhū'l-Qarnayn." The first is yours, and the last is yours.

O Salmān! If one of us dies, he has not died; if one of us is slain, he has not been slain. What is missing in us is not truly missing. We are not bound by birth-lineage, and we cannot be judged after the manner of common people. I spoke from the cradle in the tongue of Jesus; I am the helper of Noah and the helper of Abraham; I am the dispatcher of chastisement, the Lord of the quaking and the Lord of the earthquake. I am the Tablet, and what is inscribed upon it is entrusted to me. I can take whatever form I will: whoever has seen these forms has seen me, and whoever has seen me has seen them. We are, in truth, the light of God which never fades and never changes.

O Salmān! Every prophet was honoured because of us. Do not call us God — and then say of us what you will, and all you say will be true. Whoever perishes, perishes because of us; whoever is saved, is saved because of us. Whoever believes in what I have said and made plain is a believer whose heart God has tried with faith, and he is content. Whoever doubts is a hypocrite (nāṣib), and even should he claim our walāya, he is a liar. I, and the guides who are of my Ahl al-Bayt, are the secrets of God and the saints near to Him. We are all one: our immortality is one, and our secret is one. So do not divide us, lest you perish. We appear in every age according to the will of the All-Merciful. Woe to the one who denies what I say: such a one is foolish and dull, his heart and ears are sealed, and his eyes are veiled.

O Salmān! I am the father of every believer. I am the one who shall level the mountains, the one who shall come swiftly and encompass all. I am the trial that shall be unveiled. We are the signs of God and His proof, the veil and the face of God. I am the one whose name was inscribed upon the Throne — for the sake of which it was made an object of prostration; whose name was written upon the heavens — by which they were made firm; whose name was inscribed upon the earth — and it was established; upon the mountains — and they were raised up; upon the air — and it began to flow; upon the lightning — and it shone; upon the raindrops — and they were released; upon light — and it kindled; upon the clouds — and they began to rain; upon thunder — and it raised the voice of awe; upon night — and it darkened; upon day — and it grew bright and seemed to smile.

Salmān said: "⟪ar:یا امیر المومنین علیہ السلام⟫, I have found your attributes thus in the Torah and the Gospel alike. May my parents be ransomed for you, O you who will be slain in the mosque of Kūfa. By God, were I not afraid that people would say, 'May God curse Salmān's killer' — that is, that they should imagine I had taken ʿAlī, God forbid, for God — I would speak openly of you. I would utter such things of you that the hearts of the people could not bear them. For you are the proof of God by which Adam's repentance was accepted, by which Joseph was rescued from the well, and by which Job was granted relief."

## Our Lady Fāṭima at the Resurrection

⟪ar:سیدنا قاضی نعمان⟫ narrates, in his ⟪ar:شرع الأخبار⟫, from ⟪ar:امام جعفر الصادق علیہ السلام⟫, that ⟪ar:رسول اﷲ صلی اﷲ علیہ و آلہ سلم⟫ said: "On the Day of Resurrection, pulpits of light will be raised for all the prophets. ## The Mutual Union of Muḥammad and ʿAlī

A close reading of the history of Islam discloses a singular bond between Muḥammad (peace be upon him and his family) and ʿAlī (peace be upon him) — a union so complete that the actions of one anticipated the trust of the other. The clearest illustration is the night of the Hijra. Pressed by the persecutions of the Quraysh and commanded by God to depart for Madīna, the Prophet entrusted ʿAlī with all the deposits that the disbelievers and polytheists had left in his keeping, charging him to restore each to its owner. He did not, however, enumerate them or specify their contents. And yet, in all the chronicles of that period, no record survives of any claimant returning afterwards to say that ʿAlī had withheld what was his. This silence is itself an argument: between Prophet and waṣī (legatee) there subsisted a knowledge of one another's affairs so intimate that explicit instruction had become superfluous.[^cite-16]

The principle behind this correspondence is one of summons answering summons. The phrase *lā ilāha illā Allāh* calls forth the name of Muḥammad as God's Messenger, and the name of Muḥammad in its turn calls forth ʿAlī as Walī Allāh (the Friend of God). The "patron" (rabb) of the Messenger of God, in the order of human nurture, was Mawlānā Abū Ṭālib (peace be upon him); and his "praise" (ḥamd) — that is, the living embodiment of the praiseworthy qualities he was sent to perfect — was Mawlānā ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib. So when the verse came down,

> *"As for the favour of your Lord — proclaim it."*

— the Messenger of God turned at once to extol ʿAlī, transmitting in his honour thousands of traditions concerning his virtues, ranks, and merits: that ʿAlī is the one who discharges his debts, that ʿAlī is the apportioner of Paradise and Hell, and so forth.

It is worth pausing on what such pronouncements achieved. The Prophet bestowed these honours upon ʿAlī with utter composure — but upon whom did the weight of them descend? It was ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib who was bound, over the thirty-two years that followed the announcement of the Prophet's mission, to bear that weight without a single failure. He was charged to allow no word of those traditions to come to nothing. This is the love that is also a discipline; it is a vast field for contemplation.

History bears witness that the Prophet (peace be upon him and his family) never had cause to feel that he had over-praised ʿAlī, and never had cause to retract a single saying because of any conduct of ʿAlī's. ʿAlī, for his part, allowed none of those thousands of traditions to be falsified. This is the proof of their mutual trust: that the Prophet would never utter what ʿAlī could not fulfil, and ʿAlī would lay down his very life to make good what the Prophet had said. Such a covenant of mutual fidelity is found nowhere else in the historical record.[^cite-17]

## Imam al-Bāqir and the Old Man

One day Imam Muḥammad al-Bāqir (peace be upon him) was sitting in a gathering when an aged man came in, leaning upon his staff. He approached the Imam, stood before him, and said, "Peace be upon you, O son of the Messenger of God, and the mercy of God and His blessings." Then he fell silent and waited for an answer. The Imam returned his greeting in kind. The old man then turned his face to the assembly, greeted them in the same manner, and again waited until each had answered.

Then he turned back to Imam al-Bāqir and said, "O son of the Messenger of God, grant me a place beside you. May God draw me near to you through your nearness. By God, I love you and I love all who love you. O God, this love of mine is not for the greed and gain of this world. I hate your enemies and proclaim openly my disavowal of them. O God, this hatred of mine is not for any rancour or grudge between me and them; it is solely because of their enmity towards you. Have I any hope from you, and do you have hope for me? May God grant me your nearness."

The Imam said, "O Shaykh, come close to me." And he seated him at his side. Then he said: "O Shaykh, a man came to my father, ʿAlī ibn al-Ḥusayn, and asked him this very question. My father replied:

> In matta ʿalā hādhihi al-ḥālati laqīta rasūla-llāhi wa ʿAliyyan wa al-Ḥasana wa al-Ḥusayna wa ʿAliyyan,
> wa tatalaqqā bi-l-rawḥi wa-l-rayḥāni wa-l-kuttābi al-kirām.

If you die in this state, you will be received by the Messenger of God (peace be upon him and his family), the Commander of the Faithful ʿAlī, Imam al-Ḥasan, Imam al-Ḥusayn, and Imam ʿAlī ibn al-Ḥusayn, in a state in which your heart shall be at ease, your breast made glad, your eye refreshed; and when your soul reaches your throat, the angels will open their arms to welcome you with the spirit (rūḥ), the sweet basil (rayḥān), and the noble scribes."

Hearing this, the old man began to weep; his breath caught upon his sobs, and he sank to the ground. The whole assembly, seeing him in this state, wept with him. Imam al-Bāqir raised him up and wiped his tears with his blessed hands. The old man then took the Imam's hand, kissed it, and pressed it to his eyes and his face before laying it upon his breast. He rose, took his leave with greetings, and departed.

When he had gone, the Imam (peace be upon him) said to the gathering:

> Man aḥabba an yanẓura ilā rajulin min ahl al-jannati fal-yanẓur ilā hādhā al-shaykh.

*Whoever among you desires to behold one of the people of Paradise, let him look upon this Shaykh.*[^cite-18]

(*Manāqib Ahl al-Bayt* [peace be upon them])

[^cite-18]: The same teaching is given pointedly by ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib: "A man is with whom he loves" — that the bond of walāya draws the lover into the company of the beloved on the Day of Reckoning, whatever the lover's own deeds may amount to. See *Nahj al-Balāgha*, in the short sayings.

## God, the Most High, is the Forgiving, the Merciful

He looked again and saw another man and woman in the same sin; he cursed them, and they too perished. A third time he beheld a man and a woman committing the like, and as he began to curse them, God revealed to him:

Therefore curse not My handmaidens and My servants. You are indeed My servant; I have charged you with warning them, but I have not made you a partner in My dominion, nor permitted you to take precedence over Me in the disposal of My slaves.

There are three orders of My servants in their relation to Me. First, there are those who repent of their sins, and I accept their repentance, forgive their offences, and conceal their faults. Second, there are those from whose punishment I withhold My hand, for I know that from their loins shall come forth believing servants; for the sake of these unborn believers I am gentle with their unbelieving fathers and turn aside My chastisement from their unbelieving mothers, until those believing descendants have been born. Only when this is accomplished, and the unrepentant remain unrepentant, does My punishment descend upon them and My calamity overtake them. Third, there are those who belong to neither of these classes; and for them I have prepared a chastisement more severe than any you could have intended, for I shall requite them in a measure befitting My majesty, My greatness, and My pride.[^cite-19]

## God's Firm Rope

Imam al-Bāqir (peace be upon him) said:

> Naḥnu janb Allāhi, wa naḥnu ṣafwat Allāhi, wa naḥnu khiyaratu-llāhi, wa naḥnu mustawdaʿu mawārīthi al-anbiyāʾ,
> wa naḥnu umanāʾu-llāhi taʿālā, wa naḥnu ḥujaju-llāhi, wa naḥnu ḥablu-llāhi al-matīn, wa naḥnu raḥmatu-llāhi ʿalā khalqihi.

We are the side of God; we are the elect of God; we are the chosen of God; we are the repository to whom the inheritance of the Prophets has been entrusted; we are God's trustees; we are His proofs; we are His firm rope (ḥabl Allāh al-matīn); and we are God's mercy upon His creatures.[^cite-21]

We are those with whom God began His creation and with whom He shall end it. We are the guides of right guidance. We are the standards and the signs set up for the peoples of the world. We are "the foremost" mentioned in the Qurʾān; and we are also "the last," for we come after the ranks of others in the order of time. We are those to whom precedence has been granted over all the creatures of the universe.

Whoever has grasped our handhold has joined us and been saved; and whoever has lagged behind and refused to walk with us has drowned in the sea of his own arrogance and ignorance. We are the leaders of those whose faces shall be made radiant. We stand within God's sovereignty and under His support. We are the straight, illumined path to God. We are God's mercy towards His servants. We are the lamp of the sharīʿa of the Prophet, and we are the realities and secrets of the Messenger of God.

We are the root and foundation of religion. The angels come and go between us. We diffuse the light for those who seek it. We are the way of salvation for those who follow us. We bring the people to Paradise. We are the standard-bearers of Islam, its firmest evidence, and the cause of its honour. We are like the bridge that whoever traverses arrives at his goal, and whoever lags behind or turns aside perishes. We are the summit of elevation and grandeur. It is through us that God sends down His mercy upon His servants and causes the rain to descend, and it is through us that He lifts His chastisement away from you.

> Man abṣaranā wa ʿarafanā wa ʿarafa ḥaqqanā wa akhadha bi-amrinā fa-huwa minnā wa ilaynā.

*Whoever has insight into us, knows us and our right, and takes hold of our command — he is from us, and to us is his returning.*[^cite-22]

(*Manāqib Ahl al-Bayt* [peace be upon them])

[^cite-21]: The figure of the Imāms as the "firm rope of God" (ḥabl Allāh al-matīn) — drawing on Q. 3:103, *wa-iʿtaṣimū bi-ḥabli-llāhi jamīʿan* — is a central trope of Fatimid daʿwa exegesis. Jaʿfar ibn Manṣūr al-Yaman reads the rope as the continuous cord of walāya stretching from the Prophet through the line of the Imāms, by which the believer ascends from the world of generation to the world of light. See *Kitāb al-Kashf*, on the taʾwīl of "the firm handhold."

[^cite-22]: Qadi al-Nuʿmān gathers traditions from the Imāms of the household to the effect that maʿrifa (recognition) and ṭāʿa (obedience) are inseparable: to know the Imām is to be bound to act upon his command, and this twin act of cognition and submission is the meaning of being "from them and to them." See *Daʿāʾim al-Islam*, Kitāb al-Walāya, on the duty of recognition.

## The Six Forms in the Grave

Imam al-Bāqir, or Imam Jaʿfar al-Ṣādiq (peace be upon them both), said: The Messenger of God (peace be upon him and his family) said:

> Idhā khaja al-muʾminu min al-dunyā dakhalat maʿahu sittu ṣuwarin fī qabrihi, fīhinna ṣūratun aḥsanu wajhan wa abhā hayʾatan wa aṭyabu rīḥan wa anqā ṣūratan.

When the believer departs from this world, six forms enter the grave with him. Among them is one whose face is the most beautiful, whose figure is the most resplendent, whose fragrance is the sweetest, and whose purity is the most complete.

Then the Imam said: One of these six shall stand upon his right, another upon his left, another before him, another behind him, another at his feet, and the most beautiful of them shall stand above his head. If a torment approaches from any side, the form on that side turns it aside; and so each of these forms guards him from the side at which it has been stationed.

Then the most beautiful of them shall say to the others: "Each of you, introduce yourself." The one on the right says, "I am the ritual prayer (ṣalāt)." The one on the left says, "I am the alms-purification (zakāt)." The one in front says, "I am the fast (ṣawm)." The one behind says, "I am the ḥajj and the ʿumra." The one at the feet says, "I am the good you used to do to your brothers." Then they all say to the most beautiful one: "And now you — introduce yourself to us, for you are the fairest of us." She replies:

> Anā al-walāyatu li-Āli Muḥammadin ʿalayhim al-salām.

*I am the walāya — the loving allegiance — to the Family of Muḥammad (peace be upon them), which you held fast throughout all your life.*[^cite-23]

(*Manāqib Ahl al-Bayt* [peace be upon them])

[^cite-23]: This is the same teaching that Qadi al-Nuʿmān places at the head of the *Daʿāʾim al-Islam*: that walāya for the Imāms is the first of the seven pillars and the one without which the other four — prayer, alms, fast, and pilgrimage — cannot ascend, for it is walāya that opens the door to their acceptance. The image of walāya as the "fairest form" that guards the believer above his head is a homiletic enactment of this same hierarchy: the other pillars stand at the sides, but walāya is over them all. See *Daʿāʾim al-Islam*, Kitāb al-Walāya, on the precedence of walāya over the remaining pillars.

## Nearness Is Impossible Without Obedience

⟪ar:حضرت جابر الانصاری⟫ served ⟪ar:امام باقر علیہ السلام⟫ for seventeen years. When at last he was about to depart at the Imam's command, he said: "O Imam, grant me a saying I may carry with me and live by." The Imam replied, "O Jābir, do you still ask for counsel after seventeen years in my service?" Jābir answered, "Yes, my lord. You are an ocean from which, however much one draws, nothing is diminished, and however hard one strives, the depths of your knowledge cannot be sounded." The Imam then spoke:

⟪ar-quote:یا جابر! بلغ شیعتی عنی السلام و أعلمھم أنہ لا قرابۃ بیننا و بین اﷲ عزوجل

ولا یتقرب الہ الا بالطاعۃ لہ یا جابر من اطاع اﷲ وأحبنا فھو ولینا ومن عصی اﷲ لم ینفعہ حبنا⟫

"O Jābir, convey my greetings to my Shīʿa and tell them that there is no kinship between us and God, Mighty and Exalted. None may draw near to Him save through obedience to Him. O Jābir, whoever obeys God and loves us, he is our friend; and whoever disobeys God, our love shall not avail him."

O servant of God, who has ever asked of God and not been shown mercy? Who has placed his trust in Him and not been delivered? The Imam's teaching here lays bare the architecture of the daʿwa (the summons): love of the Ahl al-Bayt is no substitute for the discipline of obedience, but its necessary completion. Affection without compliance is sentiment; compliance without affection is servitude without light. Only their union opens the way to nearness.[^cite-24]

Consider this world, the Imam went on, as a wayside inn in which the traveller halts briefly before resuming his journey. Is it not like a mount ridden in a dream — when the sleeper wakes, he holds no memory of it, neither of mounting nor of the reins in his hand, for he was reclining upon his bed all the while? Or like a garment worn for an hour, or a companion of a single night?

The world, said the wise, is a shadow. The ⟪ar:کلمہ لا الہ الا اللہ⟫ is the cornerstone of faith; prayer is the lamp of sincerity and the antidote to arrogance and self-regard; almsgiving is the means by which sustenance is increased; fasting and pilgrimage settle the heart in tranquillity; the institution of retribution and legal limits forestalls the shedding of blood; and our love and walāya (guardianship) of the Ahl al-Bayt orders the affairs of religion. May God number us and you among those who fear Him in private and dread the Day of Reckoning.

To love ʿAlī is to obey ʿAlī's word and to enact ʿAlī's deeds. Otherwise the claim of love is hollow.

God alone is sovereign in His kingdom. He made His servants know Him, then entrusted His command to those whom He chose, and made Paradise their inheritance. Whichever heart among jinn and mankind God wishes to purify, He acquaints with our walāya; whichever He wishes to leave corrupted, He keeps distant from our knowledge. Then the Imam said:[^cite-25]

⟪ar-quote:وﷲ ما ستوجب آدم ان یخلقہ اﷲ بیدہ وینفخ فیہ من روحہ الا بولایۃ علی، وما کلم اﷲ موسیٰ تکلیماً الا بولایۃ علی

ولا اقام اﷲ عیسیٰ بن مریم آیۃ للعالمین ال بالخضوع لعلی ! اجمل الا ما استاھل خلق من اﷲ النظر الیہ الا بالعبودیۃ لنا⟫

"Ādam did not merit that God should fashion him with His own hand and breathe into him of His spirit save through the walāya of ʿAlī. God did not address Mūsā in direct speech save through the walāya of ʿAlī. Nor did God establish ʿĪsā son of Maryam as a sign to the worlds save through submission to ʿAlī. No creature has been worthy of God's regard except through servitude to us."

Then he said:

⟪ar-quote:نحن الصلوۃ فی کتاب اﷲعزوجل و نحن الزکاۃ، ونحن الصیام، و نحن الحج نحن البلد الحرام، و نحن کعبۃ اﷲ،

ونحن قبلۃ اﷲ، و نحن وجہ اﷲ، قال اﷲ تعالی فأینما تولو فثم وجہ اﷲ، ونحن الأیات ونحن البینات⟫

"In the Book of God, we are the prayer; we are the alms; we are the fast; we are the pilgrimage. We are the inviolable city; we are the Kaʿba of God; we are the qibla of God; we are the face of God, of whom the Exalted said, 'Wheresoever you turn, there is the face of God.' We are the signs and we are the manifest proofs."

In the same Book, the Imam continued, evil, transgression, intoxicants, gambling, idolatry, blood unjustly shed, and the flesh of swine signify our enemies. God created us and made our creation beautiful; He raised us in dignity and gave us precedence over the rest. He made us His treasurers over the heavens and the earth and what lies between. In His Book He named us by the most beautiful names; and by the very names that the righteous shun, He named our adversaries — a taʾwīl (esoteric exegesis) that reads the Book as a single, coherent disclosure of friend and foe.[^cite-26]

Nearness to God lies only in obedience to God. Whoever obeys God and loves us — to him belongs our walāya. And whoever does not rise for the tahajjud prayer is not of our Shīʿa: God's enmity is grave against a servant who passes the night as though dead and squanders the day in vanities.

## The Majesty of the Prophet (peace be upon him)

The forty years that the Prophet — peace and blessings be upon him — lived before his prophetic mission were of such magnitude that they outweighed the lifetimes of all the one hundred and twenty-four thousand prophets who preceded him. It is for this reason that, when he was raised to messengership, God declared:

  (image preserved at .. /images/008.png)

This verse was revealed when he was forty-one. Though his prophethood had only just begun and twenty-three years still lay before him, he was crowned with the supremacy of moral excellence from the outset. The testimony, therefore, does not refer to the virtues he would display across the twenty-three years to come, but rather to the forty that had already passed.[^cite-27]

The Messenger's progress was swift because his age was a grievous one. His companions were tortured for uttering the kalima of "Lā ilāha illā Allāh," and open preaching in Makka was impossible. Even within such constraint he raised the standard of conduct so high that the lives of all the prophets paled before it. He summoned, in silence, by his character; the disbelievers were brought to Islam by what they saw in him. This is why, in the supplication of Ḥayāt al-Qāf, where the miracles of the prophets are enumerated, the miracle ascribed to the Prophet himself is his moral nature.

Among the clauses of the Treaty of Ḥudaybiyya was the stipulation that any Madinan who came over to the Quraysh would be sheltered, while any Makkan who fled to Madina must be returned. He accepted these unjust conditions. Yet the outcome was unexpected: every disbeliever who came to Madina was so disarmed by what he beheld that he embraced Islam, and on returning to Makka became himself a preacher. Every Madinan who reverted to Makka had already imbibed something of the new faith. By this paradox his daʿwa was doubled.

God says in Sūrat al-Balad:

"Indeed I swear by this city in which you dwell." Here God swears by Makka, in which His own House stands. Yet He does not say, "by the city in which My House is set"; rather, He honours the Prophet's life by binding the oath to his presence. The dignity of the city is borrowed from the dignity of its dweller.

It is for this reason that when the Qurʾān recalls the earlier prophets, it does so by their names and at times alongside their imperfections. To Ādam was said, "We did not find in him firm resolve." To Mūsā was said, "Remove thy sandals, for thou art in the holy valley." When Maryam's hour drew near she was bidden to withdraw from Jerusalem, since it was a house of worship and not a house of birth. But when the Messenger is invoked, it is by his honorifics of endearment — Ṭāhā (the Pure), Yāsīn, al-Muzzammil, al-Muddaththir — and God Himself pronounces eulogies upon him. To the other prophets the address comes by name: "O Mūsā," "O ʿĪsā," "O Ibrāhīm." To him alone it is never "O Muḥammad," but always a title of intimacy.

### The Prophet's Silence

People put many questions to the Messenger, but he refrained from answering by his own judgement lest he err; he waited rather for revelation to descend. His silence was so habitual that the desert Arabs who came to meet him sometimes supposed the one claiming prophethood to be mute. In a ḥadīth he said, "If serenity were divided into ten parts, nine would be silence and one would be speech."[^cite-28]

(Lessons of *Tuḥfat al-Ṣayrafī*, that is, the letters of *al-Muʿjam al-Nawādir* of Sayyidnā Ḥusayn ibn ʿAbd al-Malik.)

### A Rank Higher than the Angels

The Prophet, peace be upon him, said: "O ʿAlī, were it not for us, God would not have created Ādam or Ḥawwāʾ, nor Paradise, nor the heavens and the earth."

How then could we not be higher than the angels? It was we who glorified, magnified, and sanctified God before the angels did so, for God created our souls first and placed upon our tongues the speech of His unity and His praise. Only then did He create the angels. When the angels beheld our unrivalled light, they reckoned us great. We glorified the Exalted that the angels might know we are His creatures, and that He is far above the attributes we declare of Him; seeing our glorification, the angels glorified Him likewise, taking their cue from us.

When the angels saw our majesty, we proclaimed our dissolution before the One who is unique, and that we are His servants, and that none is worthy of worship with Him or after Him — and the angels then recited the kalima of "Lā ilāha illā Allāh." When the angels saw the loftiness of our station, we pronounced the takbīr, that they might know God is greater than any who would attain a high station without His causation. When they saw our grandeur, we said, "Lā ḥawla wa-lā quwwata illā billāh al-ʿalī al-ʿaẓīm," that they might know no power is exercised apart from His cause. When the angels witnessed the bounty God had bestowed upon us and the obedience He had made due to us from creation, we said, "Praise be to God," that the angels might learn that praise belongs to Him alone for His favours — and they too then said, "Praise be to God." Through us the angels were taught tawḥīd, tasbīḥ, tahlīl, and taḥmīd, and were guided to the knowledge of God.[^cite-29]

(*Manāqib Ahl al-Bayt*, peace be upon them.)

### Why Did the Angels Prostrate to Ādam?

The Messenger of God, peace be upon him, said: "Then God created Ādam and placed us as a trust within his loins; thereafter He commanded the angels to prostrate before Ādam, in reverence of us."

The angels' prostration was thus an act of worship toward God; Ādam's reception of it was an act of obedience and homage to God, for we were within his loins. How then should we not be higher than the angels, when all of them bowed down before Ādam on our account?[^cite-30]

عليٌّ  ما كذب ، لقد أفتاكم بما أنزل اللہ فيّ ، وأفتيت أھل القرآن بقرآنھم حتی ينطق القرآن فيقول صدق علي ّ ما كذب، لقد أفتاكم بما أنزل اللہ فيّ  ثم قال سلوني قبل أن تفقدوني ، فو الذي فلق الحبۃ وبرأ النسمۃ لو سألتموني عن أيۃ آيۃ في ليل أُنزلت أوفي نھار أُنزلت ، مكيّھا ومدنيھا ، سفريھا وحضريھا ، ناسخھا ومنسوخھا ، ومحكمھا ومتشابھا ، وتأويلھا وتنزيلھا لأخبرتكم⟫ ⟪ar:وَٱللَّهُ يَعْصِمُكَ مِنَ ٱلنَّاسِ⟫ ⟪ar:‏ شَرَعَ لَكُم مِّنَ ٱلدِّينِ مَا وَصَّىٰ بِهِۦ نُوحًۭا وَٱلَّذِىٓ أَوْحَيْنَآ إِلَيْكَ وَمَا وَصَّيْنَا بِهِۦٓ إِبْرَهِيمَ وَمُوسَىٰ وَعِيسَىٰٓ ۖ أَنْ أَقِيمُوا۟ ٱلدِّينَ وَلَا تَتَفَرَّقُوا۟ فِيهِ ۚ كَبُرَ عَلَى ٱلْمُشْرِكِينَ مَا تَدْعُوهُمْ إِلَيْهِ ۚ ٱللَّهُ يَجْتَبِىٓ إِلَيْهِ مَن يَشَآءُ وَيَهْدِىٓ إِلَيْهِ مَن يُنِيبُ ‎⟫ ⟪ar:‏ يَٓأَيُّهَا ٱلرَّسُولُ بَلِّغْ مَآ أُنزِلَ إِلَيْكَ مِن رَّبِّكَ ۖ وَإِن لَّمْ تَفْعَلْ فَمَا بَلَّغْتَ رِسَالَتَهُۥ ۚ وَٱللَّهُ يَعْصِمُكَ مِنَ ٱلنَّاسِ ۗ إِنَّ ٱللَّهَ لَا يَهْدِى ٱلْقَوْمَ ٱلْكَفِرِينَ ‎⟫ ⟪ar:‏إِنَّ ٱللَّهَ وَمَلَٓئِكَتَهُۥ يُصَلُّونَ عَلَى ٱلنَّبِىِّۚ يَٓأَيُّهَا ٱلَّذِينَ آمَنُوا۟ صَلُّوا۟ عَلَيْهِ وَسَلِّمُوا۟ تَسْلِيمًا ‎⟫ ⟪ar:‏بَلْ بَدَا لَهُم مَّا كَانُوا۟ يُخْفُونَ مِن قَبْلُ ۖ وَلَوْ رُدُّوا۟ لَعَادُوا۟ لِمَا نُهُوا۟ عَنْهُ وَإِنَّهُمْ لَكَذِبُونَ ‎⟫ ⟪ar:‏عَم يَتَسَآءَلُونَ ‎ ‏عَنِ ٱلنَّبَإِ ٱلْعَظِيمِ ‎ ‏ٱلَّذِى هُمْ فِيهِ مُخْتَلِفُونَ ‎⟫ ⟪ar:‏فَكَأَيِّن مِّن قَرْيَةٍ أَهْلَكْنَهَا وَهِىَ ظَالِمَةٌۭ فَهِىَ خَاوِيَةٌ عَلَىٰ عُرُوشِهَا وَبِئْرٍۢ مُّعَطَّلَةٍۢ وَقَصْرٍۢ مَّشِيدٍ ‎⟫ ⟪ar:‏فَمَا لَنَا مِن شَفِعِينَ ‎ ‏وَلَا صَدِيقٍ حَمِيمٍۢ ‎ ‏فَلَوْ أَنَّ لَنَا كَرَّةًۭ فَنَكُونَ مِنَ ٱلْمُؤْمِنِينَ ‎⟫ ⟪ar:‏قُلْ إِن كُنتُمْ تُحِبُّونَ ٱللَّهَ فَٱتَّبِعُونِى يُحْبِبْكُمُ ٱللَّهُ وَيَغْفِرْ لَكُمْ ذُنُوبَكُمْ ۗ وَٱللَّهُ غَفُورٌۭ رَّحِيمٌۭ ‎⟫ ⟪ar:‏لَا يُسْـَٔلُ عَمَّا يَفْعَلُ وَهُمْ يُسْـَٔلُونَ ‎⟫ ⟪ar:‏وَأَمَّا بِنِعْمَةِ رَبِّكَ فَحَدِّثْ ‎⟫ ⟪ar:‏وَأَنتَ حِلٌّۢ بِهَذَا ٱلْبَلَدِ ‎⟫ ⟪ar:‏وَإِنَّكَ لَعَلَىٰ خُلُقٍ عَظِيمٍۢ ‎⟫ ⟪ar:‏وَلَوْ جَعَلْنَهُ مَلَكًۭا لَّجَعَلْنَهُ رَجُلًۭا وَلَلَبَسْنَا عَلَيْهِم مَّا يَلْبِسُونَ ‎⟫ ⟪ar:‏وَمَآ أُمِرُوٓا۟ إِلَّا لِيَعْبُدُوا۟ ٱللَّهَ مُخْلِصِينَ لَهُ ٱلدِّينَ حُنَفَآءَ وَيُقِيمُوا۟ ٱلصَّلَوٰةَ وَيُؤْتُوا۟ ٱلزَّكَوٰةَ ۚ وَذَلِكَ دِينُ ٱلْقَيِّمَةِ ‎⟫ ⟪ar:‏وَٱسْتَعِينُوا۟ بِٱلصَّبْرِ وَٱلصَّلَوٰةِ ۚ وَإِنَّهَا لَكَبِيرَةٌ إِلَّا عَلَى ٱلْخَشِعِينَ ‎⟫ ⟪ar:‏وَٱعْلَمُوٓا۟ أَنَّ فِيكُمْ رَسُولَ ٱللَّهِ ۚ لَوْ يُطِيعُكُمْ فِى كَثِيرٍۢ مِّنَ ٱلْأَمْرِ لَعَنِتُّمْ وَلَكِنَّ ٱللَّهَ حَبَّبَ إِلَيْكُمُ ٱلْإِيمَنَ وَزَيَّنَهُۥ فِى قُلُوبِكُمْ وَكَرَّهَ إِلَيْكُمُ ٱلْكُفْرَ وَٱلْفُسُوقَ وَٱلْعِصْيَانَ ۚ أُو۟لَٓئِكَ هُمُ ٱلرَّشِدُونَ ‎⟫ ⟪ar:‏وَٱلَّذِينَ تَبَوَّءُو ٱلدَّارَ وَٱلْإِيمَنَ مِن قَبْلِهِمْ يُحِبُّونَ مَنْ هَاجَرَ إِلَيْهِمْ وَلَا يَجِدُونَ فِى صُدُورِهِمْ حَاجَةًۭ مِّمَّآ أُوتُوا۟ وَيُؤْثِرُونَ عَلَىٰٓ أَنفُسِهِمْ وَلَوْ كَانَ بِهِمْ خَصَاصَةٌۭ ۚ وَمَن يُوقَ شُحَّ نَفْسِهِۦ فَأُو۟لَٓئِكَ هُمُ ٱلْمُفْلِحُونَ ‎⟫
