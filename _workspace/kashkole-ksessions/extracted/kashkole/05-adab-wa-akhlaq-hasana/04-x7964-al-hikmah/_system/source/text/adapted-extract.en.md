## Aqwāl Ahl al-Bayt — Sayings of the Prophet's Family

Imam al-Ḥusayn (peace be upon him) spoke thus to his companions at Karbalā: ⟪ar-quote:أنی لا أرا اصحاب أوفا من اصحابی⟫ "I do not see companions more faithful than mine."[^cite-1] The companions of the Prophet (may God's prayers and peace be upon him) and Maulana ʿAlī were indeed mingled with both believers and hypocrites; yet those who gathered with Imam al-Ḥusayn upon the plain of Karbalā represented the purest and most sincere of hearts.

A man grows frustrated and distressed when, rather than striving to please his Lord, he becomes preoccupied with earning the approval and affection of others. Your prosperity reveals to the world who you truly are; but your trials disclose to you what the world itself is. When you observe a man's virtue, proclaim it! Yet should you discover a flaw in him, therein lies a test of your own character.

Silence is worship without exertion; silence is reverence without dominion; silence is a fortress without walls; silence is victory without weapons.[^cite-2] It is the stronghold of the faithful, the strength of the humble, the sovereignty of the wise, the repository of wisdom, and the refuge of the ignorant.

People profess belief in four matters from Allah, yet act contrary to them. They declare themselves servants of Allah, yet conduct themselves with liberty. They affirm that Allah is the guardian of sustenance, yet their hearts remain unsettled by this truth. They proclaim that the Hereafter surpasses this world, yet they hasten after worldly wealth and turn away from the Hereafter as though it were transgression. And they acknowledge that death shall come to all, yet they conduct themselves as though they shall endure forever.

Justice is the foundation upon which rests a structure never abandoned. The bitterness that concludes in sweetness is patience (*ṣabr*); the sweetness that terminates in bitterness is desire. The malady for which no remedy exists is ignorance; the calamity from which one must flee is indulgence.

Seek not three things, for you shall never find them: a scholar whose knowledge is complete in deed; a worker whose sincerity matches his knowledge; a brother without blemish or defect.[^cite-3] For man receives nothing in this world save that his portion in the Hereafter is thereby diminished.

Religion is true intellect; intellect is true knowledge; and true knowledge is patience. The sage attains eminence by humbling himself, while the ignorant exalts himself through ostentation. The test of the brave appears upon the battlefield; the test of the friend emerges in times of hardship; the test of the wise manifests in moments of anger and wrath.

Four deeds rank among the most difficult: to forgive when one possesses the power to avenge; to remain humble even in the state of humiliation; to preserve chastity even in solitude; and to speak truth without hope or fear of anyone.

Three intoxicants rise swiftly: the intoxication of wealth, the intoxication of beauty, and the intoxication of knowledge. The first two diminish and draw toward their end; yet the intoxication of knowledge grows ever more potent and advancing.

Nobility of character dwells in three qualities: to forgive even when you possess the strength to retaliate; to remain humble even in disgrace; and to give without seeking thanks or expecting return. Ten things consume ten things: goodness consumes evil; knowledge consumes ignorance; repentance consumes sin; justice consumes oppression; charity consumes anger; intellect consumes sorrow; shame consumes backbiting; and generosity consumes meanness.

The wealthy endure six grave disadvantages. Their hearts are forever anxious and restless. Their worship is constantly marred by negligence. Disobedience toward Allah increases in their hearts. They shall bear a heavier reckoning. They are reduced in fulfilling the rights owed to others. And their spiritual reward remains diminished.

Conversely, the poor enjoy six signal benefits. Their sorrows are fewer and their contentment greater. They remember Allah more frequently. They are protected from enmity and rancor. Their reckoning is lightened. Their punishment is reduced. And their reward is exalted.

## Aqwāl ʿAlī, Peace Be Upon Him

People would approach ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib (peace be upon him) with diverse questions, and he would answer them upon the pulpit of the Prophet (may God's prayers and peace be upon him) through his invitation: ⟪ar-quote:سلونی قبل ان تفقدونی⟫ "Ask me before you are deprived of me."[^cite-4] A questioner posed thus:

| Question | Amīr al-Muʾminīn's Reply |
| --- | --- |
| What is hardest and what is harder still? | Sin is hard; but harder still is sin that renders one's reward void. |
| What approaches and what approaches yet nearer? | Tomorrow draws near, and death approaches nearer still—it may arrive at any moment. |
| What is strange and what is strangest of all? | This world is strange, for men love it despite its deceptions; but strangest of all is heedlessness of the Hereafter, whose reward is certain. |
| What is obligatory and what more obligatory still? | Repentance is obligatory, and yet more obligatory still is abandoning sin. |

## Spending on the Undeserving (cautionary maxim)

Beware! To bestow wealth upon those unworthy of it is mere squandering. Though such a person may attain exaltation in this present world, upon the Day of Resurrection he shall be humiliated. He may command respect among men, yet in the sight of Allah he shall be abased. When one gives his wealth to the undeserving, Allah ensures that they do not even offer gratitude to the giver, and instead their affection is directed toward others entirely. Should the giver then slip into misfortune or find himself in need, seeking the support of those whom he had enriched, he shall discover them to be the most wretched of companions and the basest of friends.

## Ṣabr — Patience (a saying)

⟪ar:قال رسول اللہ (ﷺ) الصبر صبران! صبر الفریضہ و صبر النافلۃ۔ فصبر الفریضۃ ترک الحرام و صبر النافلۃ الزھد فی الحلال⟫ The Messenger of Allah (may God's prayers and peace be upon him) proclaimed: "Patience (*ṣabr*) is of two kinds. One is obligatory patience, and the other is supererogatory patience. Obligatory patience is the abandonment of what is forbidden; supererogatory patience is abstinence even from what is permissible."[^cite-5] Through this distinction, the Prophet illuminated the graduated stations of those who seek proximity to the Divine through self-restraint: the first constitutes the minimum required of all believers, while the second represents the aspiration of those whose hearts incline toward the remembrance of Allah and whose ambition reaches toward higher stations of the soul.
