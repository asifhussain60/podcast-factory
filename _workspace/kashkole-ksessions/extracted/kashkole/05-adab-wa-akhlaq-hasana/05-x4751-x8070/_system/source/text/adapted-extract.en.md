## Further Sayings of Wisdom

⟪ar:ان الحکیم قال⟫:

| {{IMG_URL:.. /images/quote_open.gif}} Al-Bahour Arba'a! The world is the sea of sins, the sea of desires, the sea of death, and the grave is the sea of IMG_URL regret. /images/quote_closed.gif}} |
| --- |
| There are four types of oceans. The world is an ocean of sins, and the soul is a sea of lusts, and death is an ocean of deeds, and the grave is an ocean of regret. |

The sea, here, denotes both abundance and the distinctive character that pervades all things. The world is a dwelling wherein none can escape the commission of sin; alternatively, the world—which is the house of God's wrath (dār al-siqaṭ)—presents countless occasions for transgression. Similarly, the soul generates desires in waves, and should a person neglect the exercise of intellect, the nafs (the lower self) will lead him toward perdition. After death, nothing remains of a human being save his deeds, whether righteous or corrupt; thus must one strive ceaselessly for virtue. When a person lies buried in the grave and experiences its chastisement, he is seized by profound regret, wishing he had refrained from wickedness or accomplished yet greater good in the world.[^cite-1]

---

| {{IMG_URL:.. /images/quote_open.gif}} Al-Masjid Parts and Zikr Allah Parts and Qur'an Parts {{IMG_URL:.. /images/quote_closed.gif}} |
| --- |
| There are three castles! A mosque is a fortress, the remembrance of Allah is a fortress and the recitation of the Qur'an is a fortress. |

A fortress is a place wherein one shields himself from the enemy. The speaker explains that three means exist by which a person may guard himself against Satan. First, he should engage himself in worship within the mosque. Second, he should maintain constant remembrance of Allah (dhikr Allāh), for the invocation of the Divine strengthens the powers of the human soul whilst enfeebling the forces of Satan. Third, one must recite the Qur'ān with understanding and contemplation. As ⟪ar:مولانا علی زین العابدین علیہ السلام⟫ it is said: "When you recite verses of mercy, your heart yearns for the blessings of Allah; when you recite verses of punishment, your body trembles in awe of Him."[^cite-2] A person should thus occupy himself perpetually with these three pursuits—whether through formal worship, constant invocation, or the recitation and meditation upon the Qur'ānic verses and their divine ordinances.

---

| {{IMG_URL:.. /images/quote_open.gif}} Arba'a Tammaha Barba'a. All the prayers in prostration, and the fasting of the righteous in the form of al-Fitr, and the pilgrimage of the fidyyah, and the faith of jihad {{IMG_URL:.. /images/quote_closed.gif}} |
| --- |
| There are four things that accomplish four things. The completion of prayer is through prostration, the completion of fasting is through the observance of the fitrah, the completion of Hajj is from the animal of sacrifice, and the completion of faith is through jihad. |

This saying reveals that rewards remain suspended until the final action completes the work begun. Should one expend all labor in prayer yet omit the prostration, his prayer amounts to nothing. If one fasts yet neglects the obligatory fitrah alms, thirty days of exertion dissolve into futility. If one undertakes all the rigors of pilgrimage yet fails to sacrifice the animal, the entire Hajj becomes void. In the same manner, the fulfillment of faith (īmān) is achieved only through continuous struggle and perseverance. For this reason the conclusion is paramount: if a person labors throughout his life but does not end in the wilāya (devotional allegiance) of ʿAlī and his descendants, his life's endeavor is wasted.[^cite-3]

| Four Things |

There are four things that increase the light of the heart (nūr al-qalb). The first is to engage in worship whilst in a state of hunger, being careful not to interrupt devotion through the gratification of appetite.

## Arbaʿa Jawāhir fī Jism Banī Ādam — The Four Jewels in the Body of the Children of Ādam

| {{IMG_URL:.. /images/quote_open.gif}} Arba'a Jawahir fi Body's Bani Adam Yazilah Barba'a Things. Ima al-Jawahir al-Aql al-Aql wa al-Hayya wa al-Amal al-Salih. Al-Ghazrab Yazil al-'Aql, al-Envy Yazil al-Din, Wa'l-Ta'a'a Yazil al-Hayya, and al-Ghaybah Yazil al-Salih {{IMG_URL:.. /images/quote_closed.gif}} |
| --- |
| There are four essences (real forces) in the human body which are destroyed by four things. The Four Jewels |

The human body contains four jewels—the intellect, religion, modesty, and righteous deed—each of which is destroyed by a corresponding vice: anger destroys intellect; envy destroys religion; greed destroys modesty; and backbiting destroys good works.

### Anger

The intellect is the first jewel, for it is through intellect that humankind has been granted the rank of being ⟪ar:اشرف المخلوقات⟫ the noblest of all creatures. God is the Creator of all things and the Sustainer of all His creation. Though many noble attributes exist among the created beings, it is to humankind alone that the Divine has bestowed the designation of noblest of the noble. This nobility derives from the intellect (al-ʿaql) through which one discerns and chooses. For this reason, humanity alone has been promised Paradise or Hell, for we alone possess the capacity to embrace the good and reject the evil, or to abandon virtue and embrace wickedness. Among all humankind, those alone are truly noble whose deeds—wrought through the exercise of intellect—have been refined and elevated into forms of surpassing worth. It is not mere possession of wisdom that constitutes nobility, but its rightful use. Similarly, it is not the taking of an oath that is truly noble, but rather its faithful application in one's beliefs, intentions, pursuit of knowledge, and actions.[^cite-4]

Anger and wrath destroy the intellect. It is God alone Who has the right to be wrathful with His servants for their misdeeds. Anger impairs memory and sound judgment. The Commander of the Faithful (Amīr al-Muʾminīn) has said: ⟪ar-quote:الغضب، أولہ الجنون و آخرہ الندم۔ ولم یندم فھو مجنون⟫ "The beginning of wrath is derangement, and its end is regret; he who does not regret has remained in madness."[^cite-5] The Prophet—peace and blessings upon him—declared: "The best among you is he who grows angry yet quickly becomes appeased; the worst is he who becomes wrathful and slow to forgive." When Mūsā (Moses) grew angry, he struck down an adversary in haste; yet immediately he recognized his transgression and said:

> ⟪quran 28:15⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَدَخَلَ ٱلْمَدِينَةَ عَلَىٰ حِينِ غَفْلَةٍۢ مِّنْ أَهْلِهَا فَوَجَدَ فِيهَا رَجُلَيْنِ يَقْتَتِلَانِ هَذَا مِن شِيعَتِهِۦ وَهَذَا مِنْ عَدُوِّهِۦ ۖ فَٱسْتَغَثَهُ ٱلَّذِى مِن شِيعَتِهِۦ عَلَى ٱلَّذِى مِنْ عَدُوِّهِۦ فَوَكَزَهُۥ مُوسَىٰ فَقَضَىٰ عَلَيْهِ ۖ قَالَ هَذَا مِنْ عَمَلِ ٱلشَّيْطَنِ ۖ إِنَّهُۥ عَدُوٌّۭ مُّضِلٌّۭ مُّبِينٌۭ ‎⟫
> *And he entered the city at a time of unawareness of its people, and he found there two men fighting, - one of his party (his religion - from the Children of Israel), and the other of his foes. The man of his (own) party asked him for help against his foe, so Musa (Moses) struck him with his fist and killed him. He said: "This is of Shaitan's (Satan) doing, verily, he is a plain misleading enemy."*

Thus does even the prophet acknowledge his transgression, for anger clouds the intellect. The Commander of the Faithful has also said: ⟪ar-quote:العفو عند الغضب⟫ "The most difficult act is forgiveness in the midst of wrath," and elsewhere: ⟪ar-quote:العفو عند المقدرۃ⟫ "To forgive whilst possessing the power of retribution."[^cite-6] He further declared: ⟪ar-quote:لکل شئی زکواۃ۔ العفو زکواۃ الظفر⟫ "Everything has its purification; forgiveness is the purification of victory."

### Envy

Envy and jealousy corrode religion itself. The purpose of religion (dīn) is the perfection and purification of the soul—it is the instrument by which one constructs one's world and prepares for the Hereafter. Yet when a person succumbs to envy, he ceases to pursue his own advancement and instead turns his energy toward the destruction of others, heedless of his own ruin. Thus did Qābīl become seized with envy of his brother Hābīl and murdered him; yet this killing brought Qābīl no benefit—only great sin and the corruption of his Hereafter.

### Greed

Greed and avarice strip away modesty and shame, leaving a person shameless and debased. It is the mark of shame and honor that one should not extend his hand in supplication save to God alone. Yet when greed overwhelms him, he casts off all restraint and stretches his hand before people, caring nothing that his dignity is diminished in their eyes. His sole concern is the satisfaction of his appetite, a condition most dire.

### Backbiting

Backbiting (al-ghaybah) consumes righteous deeds and devours one's virtues. Backbiting always involves two parties, for the tongue of the slanderer requires a listening ear. Therefore, the hearer of backbiting becomes an equal partner in the sin and shares responsibility for the destruction of good deeds. Once, when the Prophet—peace be upon him—was in the chamber of ʿĀʾisha, a woman came from afar seeking knowledge and jurisprudential guidance. Upon her departure, ʿĀʾisha remarked: "O Messenger of Allah, how dark and corpulent was that woman, how harsh her voice, how strange her gait!" The Messenger of Allah responded: "ʿĀʾisha, you have engaged in backbiting!" ʿĀʾisha protested: "O Messenger of Allah, you yourself know that I have spoken only truth; all that I said was factually accurate." He replied: "This is precisely the nature of backbiting—it is always true! But your truth is such that had the woman heard your words without the barrier between you, she would have been wounded. Backbiting is a truth that ought never to be uttered."

Moreover, God has compared backbiting to the consumption of one's dead brother's flesh. The flesh of the deceased is already forbidden and repugnant to human nature; yet God calls backbiting the meat of one's dead brother precisely to emphasize how repugnant and vile it is.[^cite-7]

## Al-Qalb Thalātha — The Heart Is of Three Kinds

⟪ar:و قال علی علیہ السلام:⟫

| {{IMG_URL:.. /images/quote_open.gif}} Al-Qalb Triple! The heart is busy with the world, the heart is busy with the eagle and the heart is busy with the master. The heart is busy in the world. Wama al-Qalb is busy with the Aqaba, Filah al-Jannah. Wama al-qalb al-mula engaged Fula al-Din {{IMG_URL:.. /images/quote_closed.gif}} |
| --- |
| There are three types of hearts. One heart is engaged in the world, the other is engaged in the Hereafter and the third heart is engaged in its Lord. For the heart that engages in the world, there is misery, torment and curse. Paradise is for the heart that is engaged in the Hereafter. And the heart that is preoccupied with its Lord attains religion. |

The heart (al-qalb) is the chief of the body; every human action flows from the heart's inclination. In this pronouncement, the Commander of the Faithful describes three objects of human striving: the world, the Hereafter, and one's Lord. A person who pursues this world and makes it his final destination will receive three things: a heart consumed by anger, humiliation in this life, and entanglement in calamities. Such are the attributes of worldly preoccupation. Conversely, the heart that ever contemplates the Hereafter and prepares its deeds for that realm shall receive Paradise. This saying indicates that if Paradise is the reward of those who turn toward the Hereafter, then surely Hell awaits those who chase after the world.[^cite-8] 

The heart devoted to the knowledge of its Lord—to His sublime limits, His invocations, and His great Imāms—attains religion (dīn), which is the positive counterpart to worldly existence. If pursuit of the world brings misery, humiliation, and calamity, then the attainment of religion brings mercy, honor, and blessing.

A second matter worthy of reflection is this: engagement with the world and the Hereafter concerns one's own benefit. He who pursues the world does so for his own advantage; he who seeks Paradise is likewise concerned with his own reward. But the person who strives toward knowledge of his Lord directs his effort not toward his own perfection, but toward the pleasure and glorification of his Superior. For this reason, such a person's nobility increases greatly in the sight of God.

## ʿĀrif Billāh Labīb — The God-Conscious Sage of Discernment

It has been narrated that:

| {{IMG_URL:.. /images/quote_open.gif}} Arif Billah Labib wal Qalb Basir wal Amal Li Allah Kathir wa Arif Billah Wafi wa Qalb Zaki wa Amal Safi {{IMG_URL:.. /images/quote_closed.gif}} |
| --- |
| He who has the knowledge of Allah is wise. And his heart is all-seeing, and he is much more proactive in the cause of Allah. And Arif is loyal to Allah, his heart is pure and his deeds are pure. |

He who possesses the knowledge of God (maʿrifat Allāh) is possessed of discernment and wisdom. His heart is all-seeing (baṣīr), capable of perceiving both the manifest forms and penetrating to the inner realities. His sign is that he is engaged abundantly in striving for the sake of God. The Qur'ān affirms:

> ⟪quran 8:74⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَٱلَّذِينَ آمَنُوا۟ وَهَاجَرُوا۟ وَجَهَدُوا۟ فِى سَبِيلِ ٱللَّهِ وَٱلَّذِينَ آوَوا۟ وَّنَصَرُوٓا۟ أُو۟لَٓئِكَ هُمُ ٱلْمُؤْمِنُونَ حَقًّۭا ۚ لَّهُم مَّغْفِرَةٌۭ وَرِزْقٌۭ كَرِيمٌۭ ‎⟫
> *And those who believed, and emigrated and strove hard in the Cause of Allah (Al-Jihad), as well as those who gave (them) asylum and aid; - these are the believers in truth, for them is forgiveness and Rizqun Karîm (a generous provision i.e. Paradise).*

And in another place:

> ⟪quran 22:78⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَجَهِدُوا۟ فِى ٱللَّهِ حَقَّ جِهَادِهِۦ ۚ هُوَ ٱجْتَبَىٰكُمْ وَمَا جَعَلَ عَلَيْكُمْ فِى ٱلدِّينِ مِنْ حَرَجٍۢ ۚ مِّلَّةَ أَبِيكُمْ إِبْرَهِيمَ ۚ هُوَ سَمَّىٰكُمُ ٱلْمُسْلِمِينَ مِن قَبْلُ وَفِى هَذَا لِيَكُونَ ٱلرَّسُولُ شَهِيدًا عَلَيْكُمْ وَتَكُونُوا۟ شُهَدَآءَ عَلَى ٱلنَّاسِ ۚ فَأَقِيمُوا۟ ٱلصَّلَوٰةَ وَآتُوا۟ ٱلزَّكَوٰةَ وَٱعْتَصِمُوا۟ بِٱللَّهِ هُوَ مَوْلَىٰكُمْ ۖ فَنِعْمَ ٱلْمَوْلَىٰ وَنِعْمَ ٱلنَّصِيرُ ‎⟫
> *And strive hard in Allah's Cause as you ought to strive (with sincerity and with all your efforts that His Name should be superior). He has chosen you (to convey His Message of Islamic Monotheism to mankind by inviting them to His religion, Islam), and has not laid upon you in religion any hardship, it is the religion of your father Ibrahim (Abraham) (Islamic Monotheism). It is He (Allah) Who has named you Muslims both before and in this (the Quran), that the Messenger (Muhammad PBUH) may be a witness over you and you be witnesses over mankind! So perform As­Salat (Iqamat-as-Salat), give Zakat and hold fast to Allah [i.e. have confidence in Allah, and depend upon Him in all your affairs] He is your Maula (Patron, Lord, etc.), what an Excellent Maula (Patron, Lord, etc.) and what an Excellent Helper!*

Two meanings adhere to the term jihād (striving) in these verses. In the first, one is commanded to wage jihād in God's path—encompassing jihād of the self, jihād through one's wealth, and other forms of sacred struggle. In the second, God declares: "Strive in the cause of God, that is, strive to attain the knowledge of God's Oneness and understanding of His nature; know which being stands in what rank, whose dominion is the heavens and whose is the earth." To know these limits and to exercise one's rights accordingly—this is what ⟪ar:'جاہدو فی اللہ'⟫ truly signifies.[^cite-9]

The person who strives to attain knowledge of his Lord is wise (labīb), and his heart is visionary—able to perceive both the outward form and to reach the inner reality. His mark is that he engages abundantly in deeds for God's sake. He does not confine himself to a bare minimum of religious duties, but rather follows all the additional acts of worship, glorification (tasbīḥ), and praise. He endeavors to please his Lord through every possible means and to gain His good pleasure. Such a one, possessing the knowledge of God, remains faithful to the Imām and devoted to the daʿwa (the summons)—fulfilling both external and inward obligations entrusted to him. It is as the Qur'ān describes:

> ⟪quran 18:18⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَتَحْسَبُهُمْ أَيْقَاظًۭا وَهُمْ رُقُودٌۭ ۚ وَنُقَلِّبُهُمْ ذَاتَ ٱلْيَمِينِ وَذَاتَ ٱلشِّمَالِ ۖ وَكَلْبُهُم بَسِطٌۭ ذِرَاعَيْهِ بِٱلْوَصِيدِ ۚ لَوِ ٱطَّلَعْتَ عَلَيْهِمْ لَوَلَّيْتَ مِنْهُمْ فِرَارًۭا وَلَمُلِئْتَ مِنْهُمْ رُعْبًۭا ‎⟫
> *And you would have thought them awake, while they were asleep. And We turned them on their right and on their left sides, and their dog stretching forth his two forelegs at the entrance [of the Cave or in the space near to the entrance of the Cave (as a guard at the gate)]. Had you looked at them, you would certainly have turned back from them in flight, and would certainly have been filled with awe of them.*

The dog mentioned here exemplifies the one who is utterly devoted and faithful to his master. He obeys every command without hesitation, bearing any difficulty without complaint. His heart is purified of all impurities of doubt, polytheism, and disbelief; his deeds are pure and unmixed, performed solely for God's sake. In this, he neither acts upon his own desires nor seeks to gain praise or material advantage through his actions.

## ʿAlī's Verses at the Burial of Mawlātnā Fāṭima

As Mawlānā ʿAlī placed Mawlānā Fāṭima in the grave and covered her with earth, the people gathered near. He called out: ⟪ar:فاطمۃ! فاطمۃ!⟫ "Fāṭima! Fāṭima! But who will answer?" Then, in verses of profound grief, he said:

⟪ar-quote:مَا لِی وَقَفتُ عَلیَ القُبُورِ مُسَلِّمَۃ         قبرُ الحبیبِ فلا یَرُدُّ جوابِ⟫

What is it that I stand here beside the graves, greeting them in turn? But at the grave of the beloved, there comes no response.

⟪ar-quote:أحبیبُ مَا لَکَ لاَ تَرُدُّ جوابَنا                   أنَسیتَ بَعدِ خُلَّتِ الأحبابِ⟫

O beloved, why do you not answer us? Have you forgotten the companionship of those who loved you?

⟪ar-quote:لکلِّ اجتماع من خَلِیلَینِ فُرقۃٌ           وکُلَّ الذَّی قَبلَ المماتِ قَلیلُ⟫

Every gathering of kindred souls must end in separation; all that exists before death is brief and fleeting.

⟪ar-quote:أِنَّ افتِقَادِی فاطمۃَ بعد أحمدا           دلیلٌ علی ان لَّا یَدُومُ خلیلُ⟫

That I have now lost Fāṭima, after having lost Aḥmad before her, is proof that no friendship endures in this world.

With these verses, the Prince of the Faithful lamented that he had twice committed beloved ones to the grave—first the Prophet Muḥammad, and now his daughter. The unity of their departure became clear to him: no human bond, however strong, can withstand the separation that death brings. All companionship, however long, must dissolve.[^cite-10]
