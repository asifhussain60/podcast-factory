## Further Sayings of Wisdom

⟪ar:ان الحکیم قال⟫:

| {{IMG_URL:.. /images/quote_open.gif}} Al-Bahour Arba'a! The world is the sea of sins, the sea of desires, the sea of death, and the grave is the sea of IMG_URL regret. /images/quote_closed.gif}} |
| --- |
| There are four types of oceans. The world is an ocean of sins, and the soul is a sea of lusts, and death is an ocean of deeds, and the grave is an ocean of regret. |

The sage teaches that the world and the human condition present themselves as four distinct oceans, each characterized by its own peril. The world constitutes an ocean of sins—a vast expanse in which no soul can navigate without encountering opportunities for transgression. This is not metaphorical abundance alone; rather, it reflects the profound reality that the world, which the tradition names *dār al-saqṭ Allāh* (the house of God's wrath), presents manifold occasions for spiritual stumbling. Similarly, the self generates desires in endless waves. When a person fails to exercise intellect—the faculty of discernment and judgment—the soul becomes a tempestuous sea that carries him toward destruction.[^cite-1] 

After death, only the deeds remain of a person: the good and the evil recorded in his account. Thus, one must strive continuously to accumulate righteous acts. When the person is placed in the grave and experiences its punishment, profound regret seizes him—a wish that he had refrained from evil in life, or that he had performed still more good. This regret is itself the ocean of the grave: boundless, inescapable, and irreversible.[^cite-2]

---

| {{IMG_URL:.. /images/quote_open.gif}} Al-Masjid Parts and Zikr Allah Parts and Qur'an Parts {{IMG_URL:.. /images/quote_closed.gif}} |
| --- |
| There are three castles! A mosque is a fortress, the remembrance of Allah is a fortress and the recitation of the Qur'an is a fortress. |

A fortress is a stronghold where one defends oneself against the enemy. The speaker explains that whoever wishes to fortify himself against Satan possesses three means of refuge. The first is to engage oneself in worship within the sacred precinct—the mosque—where the Divine presence is nearest. The second is to maintain constant remembrance of God (*dhikr Allāh*), for such remembrance strengthens the faculties of the human soul and attenuates the forces of Satan's seduction. The third is to recite the Qur'an with understanding and contemplation.

As ⟪ar:مولانا علی زین العابدین علیہ السلام⟫ states: "When you recite the verses of mercy, your heart yearns for the blessings of Allah; when you recite the verses of punishment, your body trembles with fear of Him." Thus, a person must occupy himself perpetually with these three practices—engaging in worship, remembering his Lord, or reciting and meditating upon His revelation. In this threefold discipline lies the defense of the soul.[^cite-3]

---

| {{IMG_URL:.. /images/quote_open.gif}} Arba'a Tammaha Barba'a. All the prayers in prostration, and the fasting of the righteous in the form of al-Fitr, and the pilgrimage of the fidyyah, and the faith of jihad {{IMG_URL:.. /images/quote_closed.gif}} |
| --- |
| There are four things that accomplish four things. The completion of prayer is through prostration, the completion of fasting is through the observance of the fitrah, the completion of Hajj is from the animal of sacrifice, and the completion of faith is through jihad. |

This teaching reveals a profound principle: until the final action is performed, the reward of all preceding labor remains in suspension. A person who exhausts himself in prayer but omits the prostration (*sajda*) has not truly prayed; his effort bears no fruit. One who fasts for thirty days but neglects the *fiṭra*—the obligatory alms at the conclusion of the fast—squanders his month of discipline.[^cite-4] The pilgrim who undertakes all the hardships of *ḥajj* (pilgrimage) yet fails to offer the sacrificial animal renders the entire pilgrimage void.

Likewise does faith achieve completion only through *jihād* (struggle). The end matters as much as the beginning. A person may labor throughout his lifetime, but if that life does not culminate in loyalty to ʿAlī and his descendants—in recognition of their rightful authority (*wilāya*)—then all his striving is rendered hollow. The path must be walked to its destination, or it has not been walked at all.[^cite-5]

There are four things that illuminate the light of the heart. The first is to spend time in worship while hungry—that is, to be vigilant lest the satisfaction of the stomach disturb one's concentration in worship.

## Arbaʿa Jawāhir fī Jism Banī Ādam — The Four Jewels in the Body of the Children of Ādam

| {{IMG_URL:.. /images/quote_open.gif}} Arba'a Jawahir fi Body's Bani Adam Yazilah Barba'a Things. Ima al-Jawahir al-Aql al-Aql wa al-Hayya wa al-Amal al-Salih.  					Al-Ghazrab Yazil al-'Aql, al-Envy Yazil al-Din, Wa'l-Ta'a'a Yazil al-Hayya, and al-Ghaybah Yazil al-Salih {{IMG_URL:.. /images/quote_closed.gif}} |
| --- |
| There are four essences (real forces) in the human body which are destroyed by four things. The Four Jewels |

### The Intellect and Anger

There are four precious faculties (*jawāhir*) within the human person, each of which may be destroyed by a corresponding vice. The first and noblest of these is the intellect (*ʿaql*)—that faculty which confers upon man his distinctive honor. God has created numerous noble beings; among all creation, humanity alone has been granted the title ⟪ar:اشرف المخلوقات⟫ (the noblest of creatures). This pre-eminence derives from the intellect, through which man discerns good from evil and makes his choices.[^cite-6] Man alone among earthly creatures has been granted paradise and hell, for only he possesses the capacity to embrace good and reject evil—or conversely, to abandon good and embrace evil. Among all people, those are truly noble who have been given the wealth of intellectual discernment, those whose righteous deeds are not merely isolated acts but gradually transform into noble character. Yet nobility consists not in the mere possession of wisdom, but in its exercise: in the refinement of one's beliefs, intentions, pursuit of knowledge, and actions.

Anger and wrath (*al-ghāḍab*) destroy this intellect. Only God possesses the right to anger at His servants for their misdeeds. When anger overcomes a person, it clouds memory and judgment. The Commander of the Faithful (*Amīr al-Muʾminīn*) said: ⟪ar-quote:الغضب، أولہ الجنون و آخرہ الندم۔ ولم یندم فھو مجنون⟫ "The beginning of wrath is madness, and its end is regret; whoever does not regret at the end remains mad." The Prophet (may God's blessings be upon him) declared: "The best of you is one who becomes angry yet hastens to composure; the worst is one whose anger lingers." When Moses (*Mūsā*) grew angry and struck a man, killing him, he at once recognized the act as satanic: ⟪ar-quote:هذا من عمل الشيطان⟫ "This is of Satan's doing."

> ⟪quran 28:15⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَدَخَلَ ٱلْمَدِينَةَ عَلَىٰ حِينِ غَفْلَةٍۢ مِّنْ أَهْلِهَا فَوَجَدَ فِيهَا رَجُلَيْنِ يَقْتَتِلَانِ هَذَا مِن شِيعَتِهِۦ وَهَذَا مِنْ عَدُوِّهِۦ ۖ فَٱسْتَغَثَهُ ٱلَّذِى مِن شِيعَتِهِۦ عَلَى ٱلَّذِى مِنْ عَدُوِّهِۦ فَوَكَزَهُۥ مُوسَىٰ فَقَضَىٰ عَلَيْهِ ۖ قَالَ هَذَا مِنْ عَمَلِ ٱلشَّيْطَنِ ۖ إِنَّهُۥ عَدُوٌّۭ مُّضِلٌّۭ مُّبِينٌۭ ‎⟫

The Commander of the Faithful further taught: ⟪ar-quote:العفو عند الغضب⟫ "The noblest act is to forgive whilst in anger," and again: ⟪ar-quote:العفو عند المقدرۃ⟫ "To pardon when one has the power of retribution." He also said: ⟪ar-quote:لکل شئی زکواۃ۔ العفو زکواۃ الظفر⟫ "Everything has its *zakāt* (purifying alms), and forgiveness is the *zakāt* of victory."[^cite-7]

### Religion and Envy

The second jewel is religion (*dīn*)—the sphere through which one distinguishes right from wrong, the instrument by which man builds both his temporal life and his eternal destiny. Yet envy (*al-ḥasad*) destroys religion. The jealous person, instead of pursuing his own perfection, turns his gaze toward the destruction of others, heedless of his own ruin. When Cain (*Qābīl*) became envious of Abel (*Hābīl*), he murdered him—though the killing profited him nothing and cost him damnation in the Hereafter.[^cite-8]

### Modesty and Greed

The third jewel is modesty and shame (*al-ḥayāʾ*)—the restraint that keeps a person from extending his hand to anyone but God, and from degrading himself through base supplication. Yet greed and avarice (*al-ṭamʿ*) destroy this modesty. The greedy person becomes shameless, stretching out his hand before people without regard for his own honor, caring only to satisfy his appetite. This is a profoundly corrupting condition.[^cite-9]

### Good Deeds and Backbiting

The fourth jewel is righteous deeds (*al-ʿamal al-ṣāliḥ*). Yet backbiting (*al-ghaybah*)—speaking ill of one's absent brother—eats away at virtue and nullifies good works. Backbiting involves two people always, for the speaker requires a listener. Thus both the backbiter and the hearer share equally in the sin, and both squander their good deeds.

The Prophet (may God's blessings be upon him) once sat with ʿĀʾisha when a woman came from afar seeking knowledge and jurisprudence. After she departed, ʿĀʾisha said: "O Messenger of Allah, how dark her skin is, how stout her body, how harsh her voice!" The Messenger replied: "ʿĀʾisha, you have backbitten." She protested: "O Messenger of Allah, you know that everything I said is true!" He responded: "That is precisely the evil: backbiting speaks only truth. Yet your truth wounds without cause. Had this woman heard your words, she would have been grieved. Thus, backbiting is a truth that must not be uttered." 

God has said that backbiting is like consuming the flesh of one's dead brother.[^cite-10] The flesh of the dead is already forbidden, and naturally abhorrent to man. Yet God calls backbiting the meat of a dead *brother*—making it still more repugnant. The intimacy of kinship renders the violation all the more grave.

## Al-Qalb Thalātha — The Heart Is of Three Kinds

⟪ar:و قال علی علیہ السلام:⟫

| {{IMG_URL:.. /images/quote_open.gif}} Al-Qalb Triple! The heart is busy with the world, the heart is busy with the eagle and the heart is busy with the master. The heart is busy in the world. Wama al-Qalb is busy with the Aqaba, Filah al-Jannah. Wama al-qalb al-mula engaged Fula al-Din {{IMG_URL:.. /images/quote_closed.gif}} |
| --- |
| There are three types of hearts. One heart is engaged in the world, the other is engaged in the Hereafter and the third heart is engaged in its Lord. For the heart that engages in the world, there is misery, torment and curse. Paradise is for the heart that is engaged in the Hereafter. And the heart that is preoccupied with its Lord attains religion. |

The heart (*qalb*) is the master of the body ⟪ar:رأئس البدن⟫. Every action of a human being flows from the orientation of the heart. In his teaching, the Commander of the Faithful says that man strives toward three objects. The first is the world; the second is the Hereafter; the third is the limit—his Lord.

The person who pursues the world and makes it his end-goal shall receive three things in return: a heart seized by wrath, humiliation among people, and entanglement in calamities. These three—anger, shame, and curse—are the very attributes of the worldly realm.[^cite-11] By contrast, the heart that contemplates the Hereafter and prepares its deeds accordingly receives paradise. Yet there is a yet higher station: the heart engaged in knowledge of its Lord—in the cosmic limits (*al-ḥudūd al-ʿāliyya*, the upper limits), in supplication, in loyalty to the great Imams. Such a heart attains religion (*al-dīn*), which is the positive form of existence itself. If pursuit of the world brings misery, disgrace, and affliction, surely attainment of the divine religion brings mercy, honor, and blessing.

The second consideration is this: engagement with the world and the Hereafter both concern the self. The worldly seeker benefits himself; the seeker of paradise also thinks of himself. But the person whose effort is directed toward knowledge of his Lord strives toward something *beyond* himself—toward the perfection of a being greater than his own. For this reason, his virtue increases immeasurably in the sight of God.[^cite-12]

## ʿĀrif Billāh Labīb — The God-Conscious Sage of Discernment

It has been narrated that:

| {{IMG_URL:.. /images/quote_open.gif}} Arif Billah Labib wal Qalb Basir wal Amal Li Allah Kathir wa Arif Billah Wafi wa Qalb Zaki wa Amal Safi {{IMG_URL:.. /images/quote_closed.gif}} |
| --- |
| He who has the knowledge of Allah is wise. And his heart is all-seeing, and he is much more proactive in the cause of Allah. And Arif is loyal to Allah, his heart is pure and his deeds are pure. |

The one possessing knowledge of God (*ʿārif billāh*) becomes wise (*labīb*) and his heart becomes seeing (*baṣīr*), able to perceive both the outward and to penetrate to the inward truth. His distinguishing mark is abundant striving in the cause of Allah (*fī sabīl Allāh*). He does not confine himself to a bare minimum of obligatory duties; rather, he engages in all forms of worship, in *tasbīḥ* (glorification), in *takbīr* (magnification), seeking always to please his Lord and to gain His satisfaction. God says in the Qur'an:

> ⟪quran 8:74⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَٱلَّذِينَ آمَنُوا۟ وَهَاجَرُوا۟ وَجَهَدُوا۟ فِى سَبِيلِ ٱللَّهِ وَٱلَّذِينَ آوَوا۟ وَّنَصَرُوٓا۟ أُو۟لَٓئِكَ هُمُ ٱلْمُؤْمِنُونَ حَقًّۭا ۚ لَّهُم مَّغْفِرَةٌۭ وَرِزْقٌۭ كَرِيمٌۭ ‎⟫

And in another verse:

> ⟪quran 22:78⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَجَهِدُوا۟ فِى ٱللَّهِ حَقَّ جِهَادِهِۦ ۚ هُوَ ٱجْتَبَىٰكُمْ وَمَا جَعَلَ عَلَيْكُمْ فِى ٱلدِّينِ مِنْ حَرَجٍۢ ۚ مِّلَّةَ أَبِيكُمْ إِبْرَهِيمَ ۚ هُوَ سَمَّىٰكُمُ ٱلْمُسْلِمِينَ مِن قَبْلُ وَفِى هَذَا لِيَكُونَ ٱلرَّسُولُ شَهِيدًا عَلَيْكُمْ وَتَكُونُوا۟ شُهَدَآءَ عَلَى ٱلنَّاسِ ۚ فَأَقِيمُوا۟ ٱلصَّلَوٰةَ وَآتُوا۟ ٱلزَّكَوٰةَ وَٱعْتَصِمُوا۟ بِٱللَّهِ هُوَ مَوْلَىٰكُمْ ۖ فَنِعْمَ ٱلْمَوْلَىٰ وَنِعْمَ ٱلنَّصِيرُ ‎⟫

The first verse commands *jihād* in the way of God—the striving of the self, the struggle of one's wealth and soul. The second verse states: "Strive in the cause of God as it is due to strive," meaning the exertion necessary to attain knowledge of God's Oneness and recognition of Him, to understand the cosmic order, to know who stands in what position, whose heaven and earth are created.[^cite-13] Knowledge of these limits and the exercise of their rights constitutes the true meaning of ⟪ar:'جاہدو فی اللہ'⟫ *jihād fī Allāh* (struggling in the way of God).

The one who strives toward knowledge of his Lord is therefore wise, and his heart becomes visionary—able to discern the inward from the outward. His characteristic is ⟪ar:'جھد'⟫ abundant exertion in God's cause. He is not content with a few duties but follows all forms of worship and remembrance, seeking to please his Lord by maximizing his obedience and gaining His favor. Such a person—the true ʿārif—remains loyal to the Imam and his *daʿwa* (summons, the invitation to faith).[^cite-14] He fulfills both external and inward obligations entrusted to him. The Qur'an illustrates this through the example of the Companions of the Cave:

> ⟪quran 18:18⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَتَحْسَبُهُمْ أَيْقَاظًۭا وَهُمْ رُقُودٌۭ ۚ وَنُقَلِّبُهُمْ ذَاتَ ٱلْيَمِينِ وَذَاتَ ٱلشِّمَالِ ۖ وَكَلْبُهُم بَسِطٌۭ ذِرَاعَيْهِ بِٱلْوَصِيدِ ۚ لَوِ ٱطَّلَعْتَ عَلَيْهِمْ لَوَلَّيْتَ مِنْهُمْ فِرَارًۭا وَلَمُلِئْتَ مِنْهُمْ رُعْبًۭا ‎⟫

The dog guarding the Companions of the Cave exemplifies unwavering devotion to his Imam (*walī*, his master).[^cite-15] This beast follows every command without hesitation, no matter what trials he faces. His heart is purified of all doubt, polytheism, and disbelief; his deeds are pure, performed solely for God. In such service, ⟪ar:'داعی'⟫ the *dāʿī* (the Summoner, the one who calls to God) acts neither from his own desire nor to gain praise from people, nor to derive worldly benefit from his actions. His every work is illuminated by the intention of serving the divine cause alone.

## ʿAlī's Verses at the Burial of Mawlātnā Fāṭima

When Mawlānā ʿAlī laid Mawlātnā Fāṭima in the grave and covered her with earth, he stood before the assembled people and cried out: ⟪ar:فاطمۃ! فاطمۃ!⟫ "Fatima! Fatima! But who shall answer?" Then, with a voice broken by grief, he recited:

⟪ar-quote:مَا لِی وَقَفتُ عَلیَ القُبُورِ مُسَلِّمَۃ         قبرُ الحبیبِ فلا یَرُدُّ جوابِ⟫

This is the same Fatima who would rise at the slightest signal from me. Now my voice calls out over her grave, yet there comes no reply.

⟪ar-quote:أحبیبُ مَا لَکَ لاَ تَرُدُّ جوابَنا                   أنَسیتَ بَعدِ خُلَّتِ الأحبابِ⟫

Beloved, why do you not answer us? Do the dead forget their companions?

⟪ar-quote:لکلِّ اجتماع من خَلِیلَینِ فُرقۃٌ           وکُلَّ الذَّی قَبلَ المماتِ قَلیلُ⟫

Yes, every meeting of friends must end in parting. All that we have before death is fleeting and small.[^cite-16]

⟪ar-quote:أِنَّ افتِقَادِی فاطمۃَ بعد أحمدا           دلیلٌ علی ان لَّا یَدُومُ خلیلُ⟫

In my longing for Fatima after the loss of Ahmad (the Prophet), I have found proof that no companion, no matter how near, can endure forever. Before this grave, with these very hands, I placed Muhammad (may God's blessings be upon him). Today, with these same hands, I say farewell to Fatima. Thus it becomes manifest to me that no one can sustain another in this realm. Every friend, however close, must one day part.[^cite-17]
