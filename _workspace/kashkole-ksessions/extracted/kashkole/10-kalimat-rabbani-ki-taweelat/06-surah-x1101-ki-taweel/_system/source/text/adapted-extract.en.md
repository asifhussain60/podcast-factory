# Interpretation of Surah Al-Ikhlas

<!-- section 1 (id=1189, raw_sort=1): سورۃ الاخلاص کے معنی -->

## The Meanings of Surah Al-Ikhlas

The good word reaches God, and the good deed takes away the word. Just as the formula of sincerity—*kalimah al-ikhlas* (the word of attestation, "There is no god but Allah")—constitutes the beginning of religion, so Surah Al-Ikhlas represents its conclusion. According to the decree of the All-Wise, it is necessary that the beginning and end of religion should correspond and cohere. Surah Al-Ikhlas is the summary and culmination of the entire Qur'an, revealed at its close so that both the opening and closing of the doors of religion remain pure.[^cite-1]

Numerous correspondences exist between the *kalimah al-ikhlas* and Surah Al-Ikhlas. Just as the word of attestation comprises four words, so too does Surah Al-Ikhlas consist of four verses. Yet their internal structures differ in a revealing way: the first two words of the *kalimah* are negations, and the latter two are affirmations; conversely, the first two verses of the Surah are affirmations, and the final two are negations. This inversion reflects a principle inherent in creation itself. Everything in its initial state of existence remains passive and, in that condition, weak. Power then accrues until existence reaches the active form, becoming mighty and perfected. Thus the word of attestation begins in the limit of negation—*shahādah* (witnessing)—and, having traversed the stages of spiritual ascent, emerges as Surah Al-Ikhlas, attaining the perfection of active utterance.

### Qul: "Say: He is Allah, the One"

This verse of great eloquence condenses within each word distinct realities:

| **Say** | God commands the Messenger to convey the Message to humankind |
| --- | --- |
| **He** | It is defined as pure identity and refers to the Creator of the universe or the Unseen |
| **Allah** | The four letters of the word *Allah* refer to the four principles of religion—intellect, spirit, soul, and foundation—chosen as the instruments of the Divine Word. Of these, two are spiritual and two corporeal, each according to its proper rank |
| **The One** | These four principles upheld the Oneness of the Creator and deemed Him pure and transcendent of all spiritual and corporeal attributes they themselves had acquired |

In a higher sense, these three words—*Hu* (He), *Allah*, and *al-Wāḥid* (the One)—denote three distinct beings ordered in hierarchy, with *Hu* standing supreme. After that rank comes *Allah*, and then *al-Wāḥid*:

| **Hu** | **Allah** | **Al-Wāḥid** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| The Creator of Excellence | The First Intellect (*al-ʿAql al-Awwal*) | The First Emanation (*al-Munbaʿith al-Awwal*) |

### Allah Al-Samad: "Allah is the All-Sufficient"

| **Al-Samad** | The one invoked in all matters is called *al-Samad*. Once these four limits—intellect, spirit, soul, and foundation—have attained the station of Unity, they turned toward Him seeking secondary perfection (*kamāl thānī*). |
| --- | --- |
| **Self-Sufficient** | Yet He is needless of them; these limits found no path to reach Him. |
| **Impenetrable** | *Al-Samad* is that which contains no hollowness. When they sought to approach *Hu*, they discovered no opening through which to pierce His essence. Thus no one can comprehend what is concealed within it. |

### Lam Yalid: "He did not give birth"

The interpretation of this verse reveals that God creates from absolute nothingness. He has established the First Intellect as the cause of all subsequent intellects and intelligences, yet remains transcendent above being the cause of anything.[^cite-2] Should one say that all these intellects are born from this process, one would be attributing causation to God. But causation resembles fatherhood, and fatherhood begets progeny, so that one becomes the son of the father. Yet God is not the cause of anyone.

### Wa-lam Yūlad: "And He was not born"

Just as the Sublime Essence is not the cause of anything, so too is He not caused by anything. A son is the child of his father; everything that has a cause must itself have a cause. Heat causes fire; fire causes smoke. Everything that is a cause is, as it were, born of its own cause. He is neither the cause of anything nor caused by anything, transcending all relations of causality.

### In the Name of Allah and His Perfect Attributes

Whoever believes that God possesses knowledge, wisdom, or power as His attributes thereby considers knowledge the cause of the universe, wisdom the cause of the Wise One, and power the cause of the Almighty—as if attributing generation to God Himself. But God is utterly pure and exalted above all such attributes, being the Creator of all beings and their qualities.[^cite-3]

It has been stated that Allah is the Essence, and that He possesses ninety-nine (99) attributes, each described by a Name. An object becomes known through a name. In the same manner, Allah in reality is the Essence of the First Intellect, and the Ninety-Nine Names are, in truth, the beings and limits (*ḥudūd*, sing. *ḥadd*) created by the First Intellect through which we come to know it. They are the All-Knowing, the All-Wise, and the All-Powerful. The existence of attributes is impossible without essence. We reach the destination of essence only by traversing the path of attributes. When you come to know the attributes, the Essence becomes known, and this is *tawhīd* (the affirmation of Unity). We cannot attain the Unity of the Creator directly, for no direct path exists to that station. Thus we come to know the limits that He has created, and through their knowledge we affirm the Oneness of the Creator.

### Wa-lam Yakun lahu Kufuwan Aḥad: "There is none comparable to Him"

The interpretation of this verse is that the power of divinity—creation from non-existence into being—belongs solely to the Creator, the Mighty One. The First Intellect, despite all its subtlety and majesty, remains incomparable to the Creator, the Almighty. The beginning is that which human imagination cannot attain. Therefore the wise called genesis *al-Mubdiʿ Taʿālā* (the Originator, Most High), for He is the First to proceed into existence, from whom all other beings emanate. He is the Essence of the First Intellect; through Him and by means of Him, all beings learned the meaning of Unity. Since the First Intellect has become perfected in its oneness, none can match it, and however much one strives, one cannot reach its station. It is exalted, and all beings are as naught in comparison to it.

## Surah Al-Ikhlas as One-Third of the Qur'an

The Prophet (peace and blessings of Allah be upon him) said, "Reciting Surah Al-Ikhlas three times is equivalent to reciting the entire Qur'an." This utterance alludes to the three complete cycles within the 3,000-year period. The entire period of the Qur'an comprises the Qur'an in its entirety, in which three complete forms are established. Every complete form, spanning one thousand years, is comparable to Surah Al-Ikhlas.[^cite-4]

Moreover, every *nāṭiq* (Prophet-Speaker) possesses three complete epochs—for instance, *al-Munbaʿith al-Awwal* (the First Emanator) and *ʿAyn al-ʿAẓīmah* (the Perfect Manifestation)—to which Surah Al-Ikhlas also points.

## The Companions of the Two Weeks and Surah Al-Ikhlas

The first week among the seven weeks of the Imams is supreme in virtue, constituting the week of *ʿItmāʾ* (Concealment). Furthermore, the seventh personage of each week is the *Ṣāḥib al-Asbuʿ* (Master of the Week), whose station exceeds that of the other Imams of his cycle. Among these seven companions, four possess virtue of even greater magnitude, and their excellence is indicated by the four verses of Surah Al-Ikhlas. Though the Qur'an contains innumerable verses, these four gatherings are ordained and supremely distinguished. Such assemblies have not been created throughout the entire seventy-thousand-year cycle. These four congregations constitute a summary of the 7,000-year period of the *dawr* (cycle), just as Surah Al-Fatiha is a summary of the entire Qur'an. The culmination of Surah Al-Fatiha is Surah Al-Ikhlas—that is, in the sacred epoch of the *Qāʾim* (the Resurrector), there are four congregations of supreme exaltation, designated the Companions of the Two Weeks (*Aṣḥāb al-Asbuʿayn*), because they gathered together the two epochs preceding them.[^cite-5]

| Qul Hu Allahu Uhud | Allah Al-Samad | Lam Yalid wa Lam Yulad | Wa-lam Yakun Lahu Kufuwan Aḥad |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 7 | 21 | 35 | 49 |
| Muhammad ibn Ismail | Maulana Ali al-Tayyib | Maulana Ahmad Bin Muhammad | Abu al-Qaim |
| Master of the Week | Concealment (*ʿItmāʾ*) | Companion of Two Weeks | Companion of Two Weeks |

Because of the excellence of these four companions, it is ordained that if a person begins to recite Surah Al-Ikhlas in prayer, he must not abandon it but complete it—signifying that it is impossible to abandon knowledge of these four Imams and remain ignorant of all other Imams.

### Maulana Muhammad bin Ismail

Maulana Muhammad bin Ismail is the seventh of the first week, and his compilation is unique. Your glory lies in Unity and in the attestation of Oneness. You surpass all other Companions of the Two Weeks—except Abu al-Qaim. Although the remaining Imams added two weeks to their station, you gathered only the six Imams of your own week. Yet each Imam of the first week equals one complete Imam in rank. Thus the magnificence of your gathering is exceedingly high, and no other personage possesses such an assembly. For this reason, your glory is incomparable.

### Maulana Ali al-Tayyib: Allah Al-Samad

*Al-Samad* (the All-Sufficient) signifies that which cannot be seen or approached. Maulana Ali al-Tayyib initiated the seventy cycles after which all the Imams entered complete concealment (*dawr al-satr*, the Epoch of Occultation). From the time of Imam Ali al-Tayyib onward, believers possess no direct means of reaching the Imams. All supplications, invocations, and proofs became dependent upon him.[^cite-6]

### Maulana Ahmad Bin Muhammad

Maulana Ahmad Bin Muhammad gathered a multitude of great Imams, including the author of the epoch (*ṣāḥib al-asbuʿ*)—the 14th Imam, Maʿad ibn al-Husayn—and the likes of the Night of Power (*laylat al-qadr*)—the 23rd Imam, Muhammad ibn Ahmad. That is, Maulana Ahmad collected diverse forms and structures within himself.

The meaning of "Lam yalid wa-lam yulad" (He did not give birth and was not born) is that God was not created by anyone and no one was born from Him. Maulana Ahmad received from his father Muhammad the first temple—that is, the *daʿwa* (the summons) of Imamate that every father transmits to his son. Yet as the Master of the Epoch, the *Qāʾim* of the Age, and Companion of Two Weeks, he possessed many other stations which he did not receive from his father. These structures allude to the first meaning of "Lam yalid."

Similarly, he did not grant to his son Ali ibn Ahmad—the fourteenth Imam—the standing structure of the epoch, but rather conferred upon him the *daʿwa* of Imamate. For this reason, he too exemplifies the verse "wa-lam yulad" (and was not born of another).

### Abu al-Qaim

Maulana Abu al-Qaim's elevation and exaltation are established in manifold ways. First, you are the Seventh of the Seventh. Just as in the first week every Imam of the first cycle equals one Imam, so in the last week every Imam equals the one in the corresponding position. You gathered not only your own week but also the previous week—the sixth and seventh. Furthermore, you possess a unique station as the father of the *Qāʾim*—an established station whose like does not exist in the entire era. For this reason, he is even greater than Maulana Muhammad ibn Ismail. That is, there is no other Imam like him throughout the entire seventy-thousand-year cycle.

## The Letters and Diacritical Points of Surah Al-Ikhlas

Surah Al-Ikhlas contains ten diacritical points and forty-seven letters, yielding a total of fifty-seven. This points to the existence of a person in the *daʿwa* (the summons) who embodies a comprehensiveness spanning 57,000 years. The *Qāʾim* of the Epoch of Unveiling (*kashf*) gathers the forms of 50,000 years, and the *Qāʾim* of the Seventy encompasses 7,000 years. The final 3,000 years of the Kashf epoch constitute the Period of Separation (*fitr*), in which both unveiling and veiling appear. This period of separation is reckoned within the seventy-thousand-year cycle, so that the period of veiling becomes equivalent to the seventy cycle: 3,000 + 50,000 + 7,000 = 57,000 years.[^cite-7]

Every 57,000 years, there emerges a complete form that gathers the entire mass of that 57,000-year period, and this form is one of the seven complete forms of creation.

| **Letters** | **Points** | **Total** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 47 | 10 | 57 |
| Net Unveiling | Separation + Seventy (3,000 + 50,000) | Unveiling + Separation |

The author of Unveiling and Veiling—who constitutes a gathering of the entire 57,000-year-old form—has been designated in the Qur'an as *al-Rahmān* (the All-Merciful) in one sense. The word *al-Rahmān* appears fifty-seven times in the Qur'an.[^cite-8] In another sense, this personage is also known as the Master of Sincerity (*ṣāḥib al-ikhlas*).

For the 50,000 years of unveiling, there exists a standing form that gathers the 50,000-year-old structures. After that, even after the 7,000 years, there is a standing form that gathers the forms of these 7,000 years. But then there becomes a plateau and gathers the form of the Unveiling that preceded it, which had a collection of 50,000-year-old forms. It is said in the terminology of *daʿwa*: the small fish swallows the great fish. The gathering form of this entire 57,000-year assembly is called *al-Ṣūr* (the Trumpet). Seven such forms arise in one era. During this period, six forms have been established, which are called *al-Satt al-Sūr*.

## Surah Al-Ikhlas and Ali ibn Abi Talib

In a profound sense, *Amīr al-Muʾminīn* (the Commander of the Faithful), Ali ibn Abi Talib, is also an exemplar of this Surah. Ali said: "My relation to the ten commandments is established through the Surah of Oneness, and I am the embodiment of this Surah; every 10,000 years a form appears, and I am within it."[^cite-9]

### Qul Hu Allahu al-Wāḥid: "Say: He is Allah, the One"

That is, Ali was elevated from the station of Imamate and attained the degree of *wasīya* (executorship or will). For this reason, you are not numbered among the Imams. In this sense, you belong to the category of Unity and Oneness.

### Allāhu al-Samad: "Allah is the All-Sufficient"

All the Imams and believers are dependent upon Ali for their spiritual development, while Ali is independent of them for his own perfection.

### Lam Yalid wa-lam Yulad: "He did not give birth, and was not born"

You did not confer upon Imam al-Hasan or Imam al-Husayn the gathered structures of the complete forms (the 10,000-year-old assemblies). You granted to them only the inheritance of the circles of Imamate. You will bestow such forms only upon the one who is the Awaited One. Therefore it is said in the realities (*ḥaqāʾiq*) that the true son of Ali is neither Hasan nor Husayn, but rather the *Qāʾim al-Qiyāmah* (the Resurrector of the Resurrection), Ali in remembrance and peace upon him. That is, spiritually, he is neither the son of Maulana Abu Talib nor the father of Hasan and Husayn.

### Wa-lam Yakun Lahu Kufuwan Aḥad: "And there is none comparable to Him"

That is, no Imam in the previous cycles has been equal to you, nor will any Imam in the cycles to come be equal to you. Your peer and equal is only the *Qāʾim* who is better than you!
