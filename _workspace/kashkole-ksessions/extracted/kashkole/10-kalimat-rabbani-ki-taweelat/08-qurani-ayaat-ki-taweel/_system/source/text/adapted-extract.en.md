# Interpretation of Quranic verses

## That Book Wherein is No Doubt

Allah Most High declares in the Qur'an:

> ⟪quran 2:1-2⟫
> ⟪ar:‏الٓمٓ ‎ ‏ذَلِكَ ٱلْكِتَبُ لَا رَيْبَ ۛ فِيهِ ۛ هُدًۭى لِّلْمُتَّقِينَ ‎⟫
> *Alif-Lam-Mîm. [These letters are one of the miracles of the Quran and none but Allah (Alone) knows their meanings]. That book, whereof there is no doubt, a guidance to those who are Al-Muttaqun [the pious and righteous persons who fear Allah much (abstain from all kinds of sins and evil deeds which He has forbidden) and love Allah much (perform all kinds of good deeds which He has ordained)].*
> This is the Book, in which there is no doubt, a guidance for the God-fearing.
> — **Quran 2:1-2 (inline widget)**

The Qur'anic opening letters ⟪ar:'الٓمٓ'⟫, far from being mere phonetic markers, constitute a profound category of divine signs. ⟪ar:'الف'⟫ signifies Allah Himself, the One and Only, transcendent above all creation. ⟪ar:'لام'⟫ is understood to represent ⟪ar:جبرئیل امین⟫, Gabriel the Trustworthy, the celestial messenger through whom revelation descends. And ⟪ar:'میم'⟫ denotes ⟪ar:محمد علیہ السلام⟫, Muhammad (peace be upon him), the Seal of Prophets. By swearing through these three exalted realities—the Creator, the bearer of revelation, and the final messenger—the Qur'an declares: there subsists no doubt whatsoever regarding the truth and magnificence of this Scripture, nor concerning its function as a guide for those who truly fear God.[^cite-1]

| Alif | Lam | Mim |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Allah | Gabriel the Trustworthy | Muhammad (peace be upon him) |

Yet the verse employs a subtle Arabic construction of profound significance. The demonstrative ⟪ar-quote:ذلک الکتاب لا ریب فیہ⟫ (that Book wherein is no doubt) uses the distant pronoun ⟪ar:'ذلک'⟫ rather than the proximate ⟪ar:'ھذہ'⟫. If the reference were merely to the text in the listener's hand, the nearer demonstrative would be more apt. This grammatical choice points the discerning heart toward a deeper meaning: the Book to which Allah gestures is not confined to the manuscript held before us, but extends to another, concealed reality.

Consider the rhetorical purpose: if Allah addresses the disbelievers with this affirmation, they heed it not; their hearts remain unmoved, their unbelief unshaken. If He addresses the believers already convinced of the Qur'an's truth, what purpose serves an oath to assure them of what they already accept? Such reasoning would render the verse empty of meaning. The Ahl al-Bayt (peace be upon them) have illuminated this obscurity through the science of esoteric exegesis (taʾwīl—the hermeneutical unveiling of hidden meanings). They teach that the 'Book' comprises not merely the written letters and words, but rather the total comprehension of all significations—the meanings inscribed in the heart of the Author, expressed through language, encompassing the divine purposes hidden within creation and the governance of human worship. It contains the reward prepared for the righteous and the chastisement reserved for the wicked; it records all events that have occurred during the age of the Prophets, and it foretells what shall transpire until the Day of Judgment.[^cite-2]

This divine Book exists in two forms. The first is the *silent book* (⟪ar:'صامت'⟫)—the written Qur'an upon parchment, composed of inert letters arranged in sequence. The second is the *speaking book* (⟪ar:'ناطق'⟫)—the living embodiment of the Prophet Muhammad (peace and blessings of Allah be upon him), whose person constitutes the vivid interpreter of the words' meanings, and whose Successors (the Imams) expound both the clear and the ambiguous passages. These are they who arise to expound the *literal descent* (⟪ar:تنزیل⟫) and the *esoteric interpretation* (⟪ar:'تاویل'⟫)—that hidden knowledge which Allah has reserved for Himself and for those firmly grounded in knowledge (⟪ar:'راسخون فی العلم'⟫):

> ⟪quran 3:7⟫
> ⟪ar:‏هُوَ ٱلَّذِىٓ أَنزَلَ عَلَيْكَ ٱلْكِتَبَ مِنْهُ آيَتٌۭ مُّحْكَمَتٌ هُنَّ أُمُّ ٱلْكِتَبِ وَأُخَرُ مُتَشَبِهَتٌۭ ۖ فَأَمَّا ٱلَّذِينَ فِى قُلُوبِهِمْ زَيْغٌۭ فَيَتَّبِعُونَ مَا تَشَبَهَ مِنْهُ ٱبْتِغَآءَ ٱلْفِتْنَةِ وَٱبْتِغَآءَ تَأْوِيلِهِۦ ۗ وَمَا يَعْلَمُ تَأْوِيلَهُۥٓ إِلَّا ٱللَّهُ وَٱلرَّسِخُونَ فِى ٱلْعِلْمِ ۗ يَقُولُونَ آمَنَّا بِهِۦ كُلٌّۭ مِّنْ عِندِ رَبِّنَا ۗ وَمَا يَذَّكَّرُ إِلَّآ أُو۟لُوا۟ ٱلْأَلْبَبِ ‎⟫
> *It is He Who has sent down to you (Muhammad PBUH) the Book (this Quran). In it are Verses that are entirely clear, they are the foundations of the Book [and those are the Verses of Al-Ahkam (commandments, etc.), Al-Fara'id (obligatory duties) and Al-Hudud (legal laws for the punishment of thieves, adulterers, etc.)]; and others not entirely clear. So as for those in whose hearts there is a deviation (from the truth) they follow that which is not entirely clear thereof, seeking Al-Fitnah (polytheism and trials, etc.), and seeking for its hidden meanings, but none knows its hidden meanings save Allah. And those who are firmly grounded in knowledge say: "We believe in it; the whole of it (clear and unclear Verses) are from our Lord." And none receive admonition except men of understanding. (Tafsir At-Tabarî).*
> It is He who has sent down to you the Book, in which some of its verses are definitive, they are the origin of the Book, and others are allegorical. I don't know. They say, We believe in these things; they are only from our Lord. Only those who possess understanding believe the admonition.
> — **Quran 3:7 (inline widget)**

The *silent book* (⟪ar:صامت⟫) encompasses the conditions of all three temporal dimensions—past, present, and future—and discloses every issue that shall unfold until the Final Hour. The same comprehensiveness characterizes the Qur'an itself. The Commander of the Faithful, ⟪ar:امیر المومنین علی علیہ السلام⟫, proclaimed from the pulpit during his caliphate: ⟪ar-quote:سلونی قبل ان تفقدونی! سلونی عما کان وعما یکون الی یوم القیامۃ⟫, "Ask me before you lose me! Question me concerning all that has come to pass and what shall occur until the Day of Resurrection."[^cite-3] The Qur'an itself affirms this universality:

> ⟪quran 6:38⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَمَا مِن دَآبَّةٍۢ فِى ٱلْأَرْضِ وَلَا طَٓئِرٍۢ يَطِيرُ بِجَنَاحَيْهِ إِلَّآ أُمَمٌ أَمْثَالُكُم ۚ مَّا فَرَّطْنَا فِى ٱلْكِتَبِ مِن شَىْءٍۢ ۚ ثُمَّ إِلَىٰ رَبِّهِمْ يُحْشَرُونَ ‎⟫
> *There is not a moving (living) creature on earth, nor a bird that flies with its two wings, but are communities like you. We have neglected nothing in the Book, then unto their Lord they (all) shall be gathered.*
> There is not a moving creature on the earth, nor a bird that flies with its two wings, but they are communities like yourselves. We have left no lag in their destiny. Then to their Lord they are gathered.
> — **Quran 6:38 (inline widget)**

And the Qur'an attests: ⟪ar-quote:أنا بکل شئی علیم⟫, "I am the Knower of all things."

The Righteous Imams (peace be upon them) bear witness: the knowledge revealed to Adam (peace be upon him)—knowledge through which the Prophets were sent to all creation—continues in his noble descendants, the pure progeny of ⟪ar:رسول اللہ خاتم النبین⟫, the Messenger of Allah and Seal of Prophets. Yet now the question emerges with clarity: why does this verse employ ⟪ar:'ذالک'⟫, the distant demonstrative? The answer lies plain: this Book is not a simple reference to the written text, but a gesture toward the Book of Revelation—toward the manifest reality of the Prophet himself. The Book whose truth admits no doubt is the very essence of the Prophet; and the purpose of its declaration is to affirm the succession and guardianship (⟪ar:وصی رسول⟫) of the Guardian, to emphasize that ⟪ar:'امیر المومین علی ابن ابی طالب'⟫, the Commander of the Faithful, ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib (peace be upon him), stands as this guardian, and thereby to dispel from the hearts of the faithful any lingering doubt regarding his authority.

Thus ⟪ar:ھدی للمتقین⟫—this Qur'an, which synthesizes the silent Book and the speaking Book—becomes a guide for the truly pious (⟪ar:'متقین'⟫), those whose hearts harbor no doubt regarding the succession of ʿAlī (peace be upon him), those who permit no suspicion to take root. For the doubters and the hypocrites, this Book offers no guidance whatsoever.

## Those Who Believe in the Unseen and Establish Prayer

Allah Most High proclaims in Surah al-Baqarah:

> ⟪quran 2:3-5⟫
> ⟪ar:‏ٱلَّذِينَ يُؤْمِنُونَ بِٱلْغَيْبِ وَيُقِيمُونَ ٱلصَّلَوٰةَ وَمِمَّا رَزَقْنَهُمْ يُنفِقُونَ ‎ ‏وَٱلَّذِينَ يُؤْمِنُونَ بِمَآ أُنزِلَ إِلَيْكَ وَمَآ أُنزِلَ مِن قَبْلِكَ وَبِٱلْآخِرَةِ هُمْ يُوقِنُونَ ‎ ‏أُو۟لَٓئِكَ عَلَىٰ هُدًۭى مِّن رَّبِّهِمْ ۖ وَأُو۟لَٓئِكَ هُمُ ٱلْمُفْلِحُونَ ‎⟫
> *Who believe in the Ghaib and perform As-Salat (Iqamat-as-Salat), and spend out of what we have provided for them [i.e. give Zakat, spend on themselves, their parents, their children, their wives, etc., and also give charity to the poor and also in Allah's Cause - Jihad, etc.]. And who believe in (the Quran and the Sunnah) which has been sent down (revealed) to you (Muhammad Peace be upon him ) and in [the Taurat (Torah) and the Injeel (Gospel), etc.] which were sent down before you and they believe with certainty in the Hereafter. (Resurrection, recompense of their good and bad deeds, Paradise and Hell, etc.). They are on (true) guidance from their Lord, and they are the successful.*
> Those who believe in the unseen, and perform the prayer, and spend (benevolently) out of what We have provided for them, and those who believe in what has been revealed to you, and what was revealed before you, and who are certain of the Hereafter; it is they who are on the path of their Lord, and it is they who are the felicitous.
> — **Quran 2:3-5 (inline widget)**

### Believers in the Unseen

On the surface of meaning, believers in the *unseen* (⟪ar:'یؤمنون بالغیب'⟫) are those who confirm realities beyond sensory perception: ⟪ar:اللہ تعالی وحدہ لا شریک لہ⟫ the Transcendent Creator, alone in His divinity; the celestial angels; the rewards of Paradise and the torments of Hell; the bridge (Sirāṭ) stretched over the abyss; ⟪ar:بعث من فی القبور⟫ the resurrection of those dwelling in graves; the reckoning and the Hour—all that the Messenger of Allah (peace and blessings of Allah be upon him) has disclosed. Though these realities are indeed veiled from the sensory faculties, they are not absent from intellect, consciousness, and true insight; they dwell present and manifest to the discerning mind. Were the Messenger to invite toward matters hidden alike from sense and intellect, his call would fail in validity, for Allah testifies:

> ⟪quran 43:86⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَلَا يَمْلِكُ ٱلَّذِينَ يَدْعُونَ مِن دُونِهِ ٱلشَّفَعَةَ إِلَّا مَن شَهِدَ بِٱلْحَقِّ وَهُمْ يَعْلَمُونَ ‎⟫
> *And those whom they invoke instead of Him have no power of intercession; except those who bear witness to the truth (i.e. believed in the Oneness of Allah, and obeyed His Orders), and they know (the facts about the Oneness of Allah) .*
> And those whom they invoke besides Him have no power of intercession, except those who testify to the truth and they are confirmed.
> — **Quran 43:86 (inline widget)**

A witness may utter words of truth, yet if his soul remains ignorant and unaware of what he witnesses, his testimony bears no legal validity. Similarly, belief in the unseen—if it consists of mere utterance without intellectual discernment—constitutes a false claim, and the testimony of such an ignorant believer holds no standing.

The unseen resembles the self within the body, or meaning implicit in words. Every rational person knows that the body is fashioned as a vessel for the soul; yet that soul remains invisible to the eye. Without the soul, the body becomes a lifeless husk. In like manner, words are formulated to convey meaning; words devoid of inner significance become mere sound, bereft of purpose. So too in this verse: the unseen refers to the esoteric interpretation (taʾwīl) of the Qur'an, concealed within and indicated through the apparent words themselves. These inward meanings relate to reason and intellect, not to sensation and feeling. For those deficient in intellectual vision, the Qur'an declares:

> ⟪quran 30:7⟫
> ⟪ar:‏يَعْلَمُونَ ظَهَرًۭا مِّنَ ٱلْحَيَوٰةِ ٱلدُّنْيَا وَهُمْ عَنِ ٱلْآخِرَةِ هُمْ غَفِلُونَ ‎⟫
> *They know only the outside appearance of the life of the world (i.e. the matters of their livelihood, like irrigating or sowing or reaping, etc.), and they are heedless of the Hereafter.*
> know the outward appearance of the life of the world, and of the Hereafter they are heedless.
> — **Quran 30:7 (inline widget)**

They perceive only the external dimension of worldly existence—the domain of sensible things—while remaining heedless of the realities of the world to come.

The knowledge encompassed by the Qur'an's unseen reaches across many registers: the knowledge of the Hereafter; the celestial orders; the spiritual and corporeal boundaries of existence; the Throne and the Footstool; Paradise and Gehenna; the resurrection and reunion; the unveiling from the grave—all these, and the sincere monotheism (*tawḥīd*, free from the imperfections of both anthropomorphism and denial), together constitute the unseen. Hidden they remain from sensation, yet they dwell luminous within the intellect. The human mind possesses the capacity to apprehend them, and the testimony of one who confirms such realities merits acceptance.

Yet knowledge itself admits degrees and stations. As the veils of ignorance lift gradually, what appeared unseen to a lower station becomes manifest to a higher. Since knowledge is boundless and ascends without limit, the unseen too is boundless. In other words, when the Messenger of Allah (peace and blessings of Allah be upon him) calls toward understanding the Qur'an's meanings, he functions as a *luminous lamp* (Sirāj Munīr), illuminating perception just as external light grants the eye its vision. A blind man derives no benefit from a lamp; likewise, one blind in spiritual insight cannot profit from prophethood and prophetic guidance. Yet the combination of lamp and sight—of intelligence and prophethood—yields complete benefit. In just this way does the marriage of reason and prophecy render the realities of the hereafter intelligible; severed, they remain obscure and inaccessible.

### And Establish Prayer

The outward meaning concerns the performance of ritual prayer (⟪ar:'الصلاة'⟫). Yet outward prayer resembles the city's walls—they encompass within themselves all the charity, blessing, and benefit distributed to the populace dwelling therein. Thus did the Prophet (peace and blessings of Allah be upon him) call it the sum of all righteousness: prayer comprises the takbīr (opening invocation) that inaugurates the *sacred state* (iḥrām) and concludes with its acceptance; within lie bowing and prostration, recitation and tashhahud, standing and sitting—the totality of these movements constituting prayer. Yet interior to this outward prayer stands an inward prayer: the *summons* (daʿwa—the invitation to truth), and in this dimension too, prayer's literal and metaphorical meanings are *established* (yaqīmūn). This interior summons forms itself a protective boundary; those who enter within it discover the true benefits and objectives of external prayer—realities invisible to the outwardly observant. Likewise, by entering into this summons to truth, one grasps the meaning and purpose underlying alms-giving, fasting, pilgrimage, striving, and all commandments of obligation. This pathway leads to authentic monotheism (*tawḥīd*)—a doctrine free from the defects of simile and denial, transcending all spiritual and corporeal limitation.

Parallel to the *takbīr of the state of sacredness* (Takbīrat al-Iḥrām) stands the covenant and oath incumbent upon every true summoner and upon those called: the obligation to surrender oneself utterly to that summons. The first precept is to accept obedience to the Imam of the Age (⟪ar:امام الزمان⟫, peace be upon him). Whosoever binds himself to this summons to truth shall participate in the congregational prayer of those who establish it and shall reap in the Hereafter the fruits of his outward prayer.

### And Spend from What We Have Bestowed Upon Them

Sustenance divides into two categories. The first is bodily and material sustenance, with which one feeds the hungry and provides for the needy—a noble deed indeed. Yet far greater in station are those who dispense *spiritual sustenance*, nourishing souls with the sciences of faith. This sustenance is granted to those who hold fast to the *cord of the wilayat* (alliance) of ʿAlī (peace be upon him) and have entered the summons through binding covenant. Just as physical food sustains the body's health, so too does the outward Book nourish the masses according to their capacity and discernment, while spiritual wisdom sustains the inward heart. In this harmonious manner, the messengers fulfill both services, dispensing both the Book and Wisdom from Allah's bounties. So too do those who follow their example.

### And Those Who Believe in What Has Been Sent Down to You, and What Was Sent Down Before You, and Who Are Certain of the Hereafter

In this verse, Allah gathers the three epochs—past, present, future—into a unified whole, teaching that felicity emerges from the fulfillment of threefold faith: confession of the prophets who preceded; obedience to the present messenger; and belief in the resurrection yet to come. Whoever renounces any element stands excluded from faith's circle. Thus Allah declares in the verse's conclusion ⟪ar-quote:اولائک ھدی من ربھم و اولائک ھم المفلحون⟫: "Those are they upon whom rests guidance from their Lord, and those are the truly successful."

## Verily, Those Who Disbelieve—Warning Reaches Them Not

Allah Most High proclaims:

> ⟪quran 2:6⟫
> ⟪ar:‏إِنَّ ٱلَّذِينَ كَفَرُوا۟ سَوَآءٌ عَلَيْهِمْ ءَأَنذَرْتَهُمْ أَمْ لَمْ تُنذِرْهُمْ لَا يُؤْمِنُونَ ‎⟫
> *Verily, those who disbelieve, it is the same to them whether you (O Muhammad Peace be upon him ) warn them or do not warn them, they will not believe.*
> as for those who disbelieve, it is all the same whether you warn them or do not warn them, they will not believe.
> — **Quran 2:6 (inline widget)**

In this verse, the term *disbelievers* (al-*kafirun*) does not denote merely the polytheists of Arabia—those who worshipped idols without reflection, who followed uncritically the ways of their ancestors, dwellers in ignorance. Rather, in Arabic lexicography, ⟪ar:'کفر'⟫ (*kufr*, disbelief) literally signifies *concealment* or *hiddenness*; hence the farmer is called ⟪ar:'کافر'⟫ (*kāfir*), he who buries the seed in the earth. In the truest sense, a disbeliever is one who knowingly and deliberately denies something—one who conceals knowledge behind a veil of pretended ignorance, who acts mysteriously and declares, "I know not," though clear knowledge dwells within him. Such a person proves not difficult but *impossible* to turn toward faith.

There are disbelievers among the People of the Book—the Jews and Christians—who possess in their scriptures the prophecies and glad tidings concerning the Final Prophet in explicit and luminous words, replete with descriptions of his excellence. His very name stands recorded in the Torah and Gospel; the nobility of his family, the signs of his emergence, the circumstances of his age—all are documented. Yet they perverted these testimonies out of enmity toward the Prophet (peace and blessings of Allah be upon him).

In the time of the Messenger himself, disbelief took another form: those Muslims who, with full knowledge, willfully rejected the Messenger's explicit designation of his successor. Despite recognizing that ⟪ar:سباقت فی الاسلام⟫ ʿAlī (peace be upon him) held precedence in Islam, and despite witnessing the Prophet's own proclamation before a multitude assembled at Ghadīr Khumm—⟪ar:من کنت مولاہ فھذا علی مولاہ⟫, "Whosoever I am his master, this ʿAlī is his master"—they refused fealty. What constitutes greater disbelief than this willful rejection of manifest truth?

In the view of the outwardly oriented, disbelievers are those outside the faith of Islam, whether idolaters, Jews, or others. But the people of esoteric interpretation (Ahl al-Tawīl) discern in this verse all the sects of Islam who denied and rejected the Prophet's successor (*wasi*—his executor and guardian), just as the Jews and Christians had rejected the prophecy itself. Yet they possessed full knowledge; the Qur'an itself had taught them that among all the prophets before Muhammad, not one had failed to establish his testament, and that prophethood remained incomplete without the *will* (wasiyyah—the designation of succession).

The verb ⟪ar:'أنذر'⟫ (*andhara*, to warn) means to alert and prepare before calamity arrives, as the proverb states: ⟪ar-quote:فقد أعذر من أنذر⟫, "He who warns has discharged his obligation." Thus in Surah al-Fatir, Allah describes *al-shaykh* (the old man) as one who warns:

> ⟪quran 35:37⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَهُمْ يَصْطَرِخُونَ فِيهَا رَبَّنَآ أَخْرِجْنَا نَعْمَلُ صَلِحًا غَيْرَ ٱلَّذِى كُنَّا نَعْمَلُۚ أَوَلَمْ نُعَمِّرْكُم مَّا يَتَذَكَّرُ فِيهِ مَن تَذَكَّرَ وَجَآءَكُمُ ٱلنَّذِيرُۖ فَذُوقُوا۟ فَمَا لِلظَّلِمِينَ مِن نَّصِيرٍ ‎⟫
> *Therein they will cry: "Our Lord! Bring us out, we shall do righteous good deeds, not (the evil deeds) that we used to do." (Allah will reply): "Did We not give you lives long enough, so that whosoever would receive admonition, - could receive it? And the warner came to you. So taste you (the evil of your deeds). For the Zalimun (polytheists and wrong­doers, etc.) there is no helper." *
> And they will cry out therein, Our Lord! Bring us out, and we will do righteousness, the opposite of what we used to do. Did We not give you a life long enough to understand? And there came to you a warner, so taste it, for the wrongdoers will have no helper.
> — **Quran 35:37 (inline widget)**

The warner alerts to the approach of death and the imminence of the world's dissolution. Yet for those intoxicated with worldly pleasures, those who have forgotten the Hereafter, the Messenger's warning avails nothing; the sleeper may be awakened, but the deceiver who feigns sleep shall not stir.

## Allah Has Set a Seal Upon Their Hearts and Their Hearing

Allah Most High declares:

> ⟪quran 2:7⟫
> ⟪ar:‏خَتَمَ ٱللَّهُ عَلَىٰ قُلُوبِهِمْ وَعَلَىٰ سَمْعِهِمْ ۖ وَعَلَىٰٓ أَبْصَرِهِمْ غِشَوَةٌۭ ۖ وَلَهُمْ عَذَابٌ عَظِيمٌۭ ‎⟫
> *Allah has set a seal on their hearts and on their hearings, (i.e. they are closed from accepting Allah's Guidance), and on their eyes there is a covering. Theirs will be a great torment.*
> Allah has set a seal on their hearts and upon their hearing, and on their eyes is a covering, and there is a great punishment for them.
> — **Quran 2:7 (inline widget)**

On the surface, one might conclude: if Allah has sealed the hearts of the disbelievers, barring them from His knowledge, and has shrouded their ears and eyes in veils of ignorance, depriving them of hearing and sight, then they stand as poor and helpless creatures, bearing no responsibility for their refusal. Yet the people of the *summons* (Ahl al-Daʿwa) unveil a deeper significance: the terms 'heart,' 'eye,' and 'ear' here refer not to physical organs, but to those persons and powers who have become the heart, eye, and ear for their people—those who guide and sustain the community.

In the human body, the heart commands all the organs; at its behest, each performs its function, and through it courses the vital force. The human being is a *microcosm* (⟪ar:'عالم صغیر'⟫), a small universe; the heart's station corresponds to that of the sun in the *macrocosm* (⟪ar:عالم کبیر⟫), the greater cosmos. Just so, among religious scholars exists a single chief and leader—the heart and soul of religion itself. In the Prophet's lifetime, he held this station; after his departure, it passed to his successor and the Imams (⟪ar:علیھم السلام⟫, peace be upon them), upon whom the integrity of the Sacred Law depends. These constitute the centers of religious life, from which all the boundaries of the *summons* (daʿwa) and the dimensions of faith derive their eternal sustenance, and through which the system of the Sharīʿah endures.

Similarly, those who summon others away from the truth and usurp the rights of the Imams, seizing their stations, become the false hearts of their peoples.

Their followers regard them as leaders and obey them as the body's organs obey the heart. When Allah says that ⟪ar:ختم اللہ علی قلبھم⟫ He has sealed upon their hearts, this signifies that these misguided leaders themselves are sealed away from divine guidance and light. No more do the *lights of the divine* (⟪ar:'انوار ربانی'⟫) and *divine confirmation* (⟪ar:'تائید یزدانی'⟫) illumine their hearts. They have been deprived of revelation and understanding of truth and reality. Because their hearts have grown dark, their thoughts too remain opaque; they comprehend nothing beyond the narrow confines of their self-fashioned worlds.

The heart's true function is discernment and reflection. The heart that contemplates unveils the knowledge of monotheism, reveals the stations of the Messenger and the Imams—as Allah declares: ⟪ar-quote:ویتفکرون فی خلق السماوات والارض⟫, "They who ponder the creation of the heavens and the earth." As the Messenger of Allah (peace and blessings of Allah be upon him) taught: "An hour of reflection surpasses a year of worship." The heart that fails in such contemplation differs not from the beasts.

### And Upon Their Hearing and Sight a Covering

When the heart's true nature becomes evident—as the source of understanding—we perceive that those who serve it, whether ignorant or the false Imam himself, shall have their ears and eyes sealed. Both ear and eye are receptive organs, windows through which external influences enter. The followers hear only the false claims of their leaders; they see only what their leaders show them to see. They remain obedient to their corrupted hearts, deaf to truth's voice, blind to truth's light. For them in the Hereafter awaits a punishment severe and anguishing.

## The Physically Ill and the Spiritually Afflicted: Their Cure

Allah Most High proclaims in the Qur'an:

> ⟪quran 41:53⟫
> ⟪ar:‏سَنُرِيهِمْ آيَتِنَا فِى ٱلأٓفَاقِ وَفِىٓ أَنفُسِهِمْ حَتَّىٰ يَتَبَيَّنَ لَهُمْ أَنَّهُ ٱلْحَقُّ ۗ أَوَلَمْ يَكْفِ بِرَبِّكَ أَنَّهُۥ عَلَىٰ كُلِّ شَىْءٍۢ شَهِيدٌ ‎⟫
> *&nbsp;We will show them Our Signs in the universe, and in their ownselves, until it becomes manifest to them that this (the Quran) is the truth. Is it not sufficient in regard to your Lord that He is a Witness over all things?*
> We will show them Our signs in the world and in their own souls until it becomes clear to them that it is the truth. Is it not sufficient that their Lord is a witness over all things?
> — **Quran 41:53 (inline widget)**

From this verse emerges a luminous truth: Allah has made manifest His signs in the *horizons* (the external world) and in the *souls* (the internal domain)—in the apparent and the inward alike. He has established His religion upon principles drawn from both registers of reality. Just as the physically ill seeks out a physician and, through proper treatment, achieves recovery, so too does Allah appoint for the spiritually afflicted a physician of the soul—a healer who restores wholeness to the heart.

It becomes obligatory for one who suffers bodily illness to seek a qualified physician for his cure. He must recount his sickness in full, concealing nothing, and implore his physician for remedy. When the doctor prescribes medicine according to the illness's nature, the patient must faithfully consume it in the correct measure and at the proper time. Likewise, whosoever is sick of soul must take counsel from the scholars and elders versed in the inheritance of the Ahl al-Bayt (peace be upon them)—those possessed of power to dispel his doubts through the sciences of faith. He must confess to them the types and varieties of his doubts: whether his doubt concerns the *guardianship* (wilayat—the divinely-appointed authority of the Imam); the ordinances of the Sharīʿah; or belief in the unseen. And just as a physician discerns the patient's ailment through examining the pulse—that gauge hidden within the wrist—so must the religious scholar identify the patient's illness through the patient's own discourse, which flows from the fountain of the pulse, through his manner of speech and self-expression, resembling the examination of the Qur'anic text itself, and then prescribe the cure.

It is essential that a physician unite two qualities: expertise in his craft and unwavering integrity. He must not, for the sake of paltry compensation or worldly praise, counsel the patient incorrectly; nor, when something lies beyond his knowledge, must he compound the patient's sickness by prescribing from ignorance. In the same measure, the religious scholar must not, for the sake of worldly acclaim or brief renown, utterances that would deepen his spiritual patient's doubts. Each scholar should possess mature knowledge—firm, robust, and comprehensive—joined with genuine compassion, gentleness, and tenderness toward the afflicted.

The physician himself must enjoy robust health. He must not suffer from an illness that blinds him to the patient's pulse, nor can he examine the patient's body if his own vision fails. Similarly, the scholar's beliefs must be sound and true; his understanding must be correct; his thoughts must be healthy and clear. He must not himself fall into doubts that would confuse his judgment, but rather his intellect must remain sharp and penetrating—able to discern instantly, upon hearing the patient's words, where the defect in faith resides and by whom it was introduced. A healthy physician possesses a keen pulse; a true scholar is a realist of keen perception.

The patient must consume the medicine prescribed by the physician at proper intervals and in correct doses, with consistency; he must abstain from what the physician forbids him to consume; and he must demonstrate patience until the illness departs and health returns. Similarly, the spiritually afflicted must practice the actions the religious scholar advises—whether rulings of the Sharīʿah or words of wisdom—and must abandon the evils from which he is counseled to refrain, maintaining steadfast patience until the disease dissolves and healing is achieved.

When patient and physician alike embody these qualities, recovery becomes assured. Both the outer remedies (the exoteric teachings) and the inner cures (the esoteric meanings) prove effective and transformative.

In the verse's latter half, Allah proclaims that whoever revives such a soul revives all mankind—meaning: whoever restores the Prophet, the Successor, or the Imam to his rightful station does a favor to all creation, for his return to his position brings salvation to all souls.

## The Seven Repeated Verses and the Mighty Qur'an

Allah Most High says in Surah al-Hijr:

We have indeed given thee the seven *mathānī* (repeated verses) and the Mighty Qur'an.

By the grace of Allah, the scholars recognize seven surahs as constituting the *mathānī*—those beginning with *Surah al-Baqarah* and ending with *Surah al-Anfāl*—though in another recension, the seven verses refer to the *Surah al-Fātiḥah* itself. ## When It Is Said to Them: "Believe as the People Have Believed"

In this verse, Allāh describes the hypocrites whose true nature lies concealed beneath an outward profession of faith. The term "hypocrisy" itself derives from *nafiq al-irbuʿ*, the burrow of a rat or foxhole—a creature that maintains multiple exits to ensure its escape. Just as such an animal possesses many holes through which it may flee when one passage closes, the hypocrites similarly present themselves in multiple guises. Like bats that partake ambiguously of both bird and beast, they navigate between worlds, their gaze fixed solely upon worldly advantage while remaining utterly indifferent to the claims of faith and religion. This group has proven dangerous in every age; even in the time of the Prophet himself, no other faction inflicted comparable harm upon the Muslim community.[^1]

True faith—that is, genuine affirmation (*tasdīq*, the confirmation of truth)—can be valid only when rooted in knowledge. A person who confirms something by mere supposition, assuming the speaker does not lie, offers affirmation of no real value. Allāh does not accept the testimony of a witness who lacks knowledge of what he attests. Therefore, the belief that Allāh commands us to affirm the unseen (*al-ghayb*) while simultaneously depriving us of the means to know it constitutes a false and absurd doctrine. Allāh has ordained for us two pathways of knowledge: the senses, by which we perceive the tangible world and its manifest qualities; and the intellect (*ʿaql*), that faculty also termed insight, by which we comprehend what lies beyond sensory reach.

**Tangible Things:**

Allāh has endowed us with eyes, nose, tongue, and ears to detect and perceive color, smell, taste, and sound—and through our hands and entire bodies, to distinguish delicacy from density in all things we touch.

**Intelligible Things:**

Matters beyond perception yet accessible to reason comprise the second category. These cannot be felt by the senses, yet they may be understood by intellect. All the rulings of religion and religious beliefs fall into this second category of the unseen. Though imperceptible to sensation, they become intelligible to reason when someone possesses both a teacher and the will to understand.[^2]

The beliefs that the Prophet (peace and blessings of Allāh be upon him) proclaimed through the Book and the Sharīʿa—the Throne and the Chair, Paradise and Hell—all belong to this second, intelligible category of the unseen. These hypocrites, having abandoned the way of *taʾwīl* (esoteric exegesis), refuse even to seek the deeper interpretation that clarifies the reality of the unseen. Their affirmation becomes not genuine confirmation but mere mockery. All matters pertaining to the Sharīʿa and the Book lie beyond sensory perception; they are entirely intelligible, yet these hypocrites have taken enmity toward those possessed of intellect. How, then, could their understanding mature? Their faith and affirmation remain ignorant—a mere recitation of assent to the Messenger without comprehension, a praise empty of true knowledge, which must repel every intelligent person and provoke Allāh's wrath. Rejecting such baseless faith, Allāh declares:

> ⟪quran 6:82⟫
> ⟪ar:‏ٱلَّذِينَ آمَنُوا۟ وَلَمْ يَلْبِسُوٓا۟ إِيمَنَهُم بِظُلْمٍ أُو۟لَٓئِكَ لَهُمُ ٱلْأَمْنُ وَهُم مُّهْتَدُونَ ‎⟫
> Those who have believed and have not confused their faith with injustice—theirs is security, and they are upon the right path.[^3]

Among the hypocrites are those possessed of opinions and conjectures alone. They do not truly comprehend the belief that Allāh will raise the dead from their graves on the Day of Resurrection, yet they profess it formally because the Messenger of Allāh has mentioned it in the Book of Allāh. In like manner, they accept all doctrines concerning the Tablet and the Pen, the Throne and the Chair, the Balance and the Straight Path, Paradise and Hell, and the life to come—solely because the Messenger has conveyed them to us. Since the Messenger does not lie, they reason, all these must be true. Yet they refuse to acknowledge that, though unseen, these realities are intelligible to reason, and that the human intellect possesses the capacity to understand them. Therefore, Allāh is not disposed to accept such belief under any circumstance, for it bears no fruit in purifying the soul, cleansing the heart, or elevating the spirit. This faith is rejected in Allāh's sight, for it mingles with *ẓulm* (wrongdoing). Commending the true believers, Allāh says:

> ⟪quran 2:2⟫ *[Image text (from image 005; citation unverified)]*
> *The believers whose faith is untainted by wrongdoing are those upon whom security rests.*

The common Muslims understand *ẓulm* in this verse to mean *shirk* (associating partners with Allāh), and this interpretation is absolutely correct and beyond doubt. Allāh says in His Book:

> ⟪quran 31:13⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَإِذْ قَالَ لُقْمَنُ لِٱبْنِهِۦ وَهُوَ يَعِظُهُۥ يَبُنَىَّ لَا تُشْرِكْ بِٱللَّهِ ۖ إِنَّ ٱلشِّرْكَ لَظُلْمٌ عَظِيمٌۭ ‎⟫
> When Luqmān said to his son in admonishment: "O my son, associate not partners with Allāh; verily association of partners with Allāh is a tremendous wrongdoing."[^4]

Yet *shirk* manifests in two forms: *shirk jālī* (manifest association) and *shirk khafī* (hidden association).

**Manifest Association:**

This is the polytheism in which the Arabs and idolaters of the age of ignorance were embroiled, and in which the worshippers of stone idols and the believers of three gods (Christians) and two gods (Zoroastrians) remain trapped.

**Hidden Association:**

This is the more subtle form into which the Umma of the Messenger of Allāh (peace and blessings of Allāh be upon him) may fall. The Prophet himself declared: "Shirk in my Umma is more hidden than the track of a black ant upon a black stone in the darkness of night." This form of association the average Muslim cannot discern. The ordinary believer supposes that upon reciting the Kalimah Tayyībah, he has thereby achieved true monotheism. He does not grasp that hidden association consists in failing to know the limits (*ḥudūd*) of Allāh properly—both their spiritual and physical dimensions—and in attributing to others the ranks that belong to Allāh alone. Only one who associates others in the ranks of prayer has contaminated his faith with concealed association. He suffers injustice and commits great sin, and thereby deprives himself of peace and guidance.[^5]

> ⟪quran 2:8⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَمِنَ ٱلنَّاسِ مَن يَقُولُ آمَنَّا بِٱللَّهِ وَبِٱلْيَوْمِ ٱلْآخِرِ وَمَا هُم بِمُؤْمِنِينَ ‎⟫
> And of mankind are some who say: "We believe in Allāh and the Last Day," while in truth they believe not.[^6]

Those hypocrites whose faith was tainted with polytheism were therefore addressed: "Believe with sincerity and truth, just as the people—those of genuine faith—believe with sincere intention and affirm with understanding." There exist two categories of people. One group is special to *Ḥizb al-Raḥmān* (the party of the Merciful), while the other constitutes *Ḥizb al-Shaytān* (the party of Satan). In this verse, "the people" refers to those who are *ʿIbād al-Raḥmān* (servants of the Merciful) and who are bound to the congregation of Allāh. Those addressed to believe are not, in Allāh's sight, among "the people," even though they appear human in form and bearing. Yet no correspondence exists between the two groups in spiritual and psychological character. Therefore, this second type was commanded to believe and affirm as does the first type—as the truly human (*al-insān*)—so that spiritual harmony might be established between you, just as physical likeness exists in appearance and bodily form.

It is fitting to draw an analogy to the Messenger and the Prophet, for they are both the spiritual parents of the Umma in their respective times. Likewise, after the Prophet, the Imām and his appointed successors (*ḥujaj*) are the guides to right guidance. Those whom this verse addresses as *ʿIbād al-Raḥmān*—the people of true faith—are the Imāms of the Holy Prophet (peace and blessings of Allāh be upon him). The Qur'ān itself furnishes proof of this claim:

> ⟪quran 4:54⟫
> ⟪ar:‏أَمْ يَحْسُدُونَ ٱلنَّاسَ عَلَىٰ مَآ آتَىٰهُمُ ٱللَّهُ مِن فَضْلِهِۦ ۖ فَقَدْ آتَيْنَآ آلَإِبْرَهِيمَ ٱلْكِتَبَ وَٱلْحِكْمَةَ وَآتَيْنَهُم مُّلْكًا عَظِيمًۭا ‎⟫
> Or do they envy the people for what Allāh has given them of His bounty? We gave the family of Ibrāhīm the Book and wisdom, and We gave them a great kingdom.[^7]

Here *faḍl* (grace) refers to the Khilāfah (Caliphate) and the Imāma (Imamate). Mawlānā Imām Jaʿfar al-Ṣādiq (peace be upon him) explained this verse as follows: "We are the people who have been wronged when people ask: why did Allāh bless us with His grace—the Caliphate and the Imamate—and not bestow it upon them all? In this verse, the great dominion signifies that We have appointed them Imāms whose rule extends over the hearts of the people, binding their obedience and disobedience to our own. The question then arises: why do people acknowledge this supreme station in the family of Ibrāhīm but not in the family of Muḥammad (peace be upon him)?"

**Who Is the Perfect Human?**

The absolute human, or perfect man (*al-insān al-kāmil*), is the same in every age and era. The Prophet in his time, the Successor (*Wāsī*) in his time, and the Imām in his time—each is the perfect human. Whoever draws nearer to the Absolute Human in matters of religion and resembles him more closely in knowledge and belief becomes more deserving of the name humanity and more distant from the nature of beasts and animals. In response to Allāh's call, the hypocrites declared:

> ⟪quran 2:13⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَإِذَا قِيلَ لَهُمْ آمِنُوا۟ كَمَآ آمَنَ ٱلنَّاسُ قَالُوٓا۟ أَنُؤْمِنُ كَمَآ آمَنَ ٱلسُّفَهَآءُ ۗ أَلَآ إِنَّهُمْ هُمُ ٱلسُّفَهَآءُ وَلَكِن لَّا يَعْلَمُونَ ‎⟫
> And when it is said to them: "Believe as the people have believed," they say: "Shall we believe as the fools have believed?" Unquestionably, it is they who are the fools, but they do not know.[^8]

**Who Is Called Foolish?**

Foolishness and stupidity manifest in three domains:

| In Speech | In Opinions and Counsel | In the Spending of Wealth |
|---|---|---|

Allāh says (interpretation of the meaning):

> ⟪quran 4:5⟫ *[Image text (from image 011; citation unverified)]*
> *Do not give your wealth to the foolish, for they will squander it.*

Clearly, the Prophet and his followers suffered from none of these three defects. The Holy Prophet himself was called *al-Amīn* (the Trustworthy) from his childhood—a title his own people bestowed upon him for his integrity, intellect, excellence of character, and virtuous conduct. Even after the advent of Prophethood, when they rejected him as a Prophet, Allāh said:

> ⟪quran 6:33⟫
> ⟪ar:‏قَدْ نَعْلَمُ إِنَّهُۥ لَيَحْزُنُكَ ٱلَّذِى يَقُولُونَ ۖ فَإِنَّهُمْ لَا يُكَذِّبُونَكَ وَلَكِنَّ ٱلظَّلِمِينَ بِـَٔايَتِ ٱللَّهِ يَجْحَدُونَ ‎⟫
> We know that what they say grieves you, for it is not you they deny, but it is the revelations of Allāh that the wrongdoers reject.[^9]

The exegetes have interpreted this verse to mean: they do not deny your trustworthiness and truthfulness, for they know full well that you are trustworthy and could never betray anyone. Their denial is only of Allāh's signs, not of you. Two words merit careful attention in this verse. In the first clause appears a denial, in the second a different form. Stylistically, it would have been more natural to use the same word for denial in both parts. Yet *hajada* (they stubbornly reject) signifies to deliberately avert oneself or deny with arrogance, as Allāh says (interpretation of the meaning):

> ⟪quran 27:14⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَجَحَدُوا۟ بِهَا وَٱسْتَيْقَنَتْهَآ أَنفُسُهُمْ ظُلْمًۭا وَعُلُوًّۭا ۚ فَٱنظُرْ كَيْفَ كَانَ عَقِبَةُ ٱلْمُفْسِدِينَ ‎⟫
> And they denied them, though their hearts were certain of them, out of injustice and arrogance. See, then, how was the end of the mischief-makers.[^10]

This establishes that their refusal springs from cruelty and pride alone. Furthermore, Allāh calls these disbelievers *al-ẓālimūn* (the wrongdoers), not *al-kāfirūn* (the disbelievers). Had they openly denied the apparent Qur'ān and its verses, they would more fittingly be called disbelievers. All of this demonstrates that by *taʾwīl* (inner exegesis), these people are those who have corrupted and polluted their faith with wrongdoing—and the greatest wrongdoing is *shirk*. Therefore, these outwardly believing yet inwardly hypocritical people did not oppose the external commandments of the Messenger of Allāh (peace and blessings of Allāh be upon him). Rather, they opposed his Successor and refused to accept the station of the Imāms who would descend from his progeny until the Day of Judgment—these being the true signs of Allāh. For this reason, they are wrongdoers; yet since outward Islam remains intact among them, such disbelieving and associationist people are not those with whom the Muslim is commanded to sever all ties. Finally, Allāh says:

> ⟪quran 2:256⟫
> ⟪ar:‏لَآ إِكْرَاهَ فِى ٱلدِّينِ ۖ قَد تَّبَيَّنَ ٱلرُّشْدُ مِنَ ٱلْغَىِّ ۚ فَمَن يَكْفُرْ بِٱلطَّغُوتِ وَيُؤْمِنۢ بِٱللَّهِ فَقَدِ ٱسْتَمْسَكَ بِٱلْعُرْوَةِ ٱلْوُثْقَىٰ لَا ٱنفِصَامَ لَهَا ۗ وَٱللَّهُ سَمِيعٌ عَلِيمٌ ‎⟫
> There is no compulsion in religion. Verily, right guidance has become distinct from error. Whoever disbelieves in false deities and believes in Allāh has grasped the most firm handhold that will never break. And Allāh is All-Hearing, All-Knowing.[^11]

Their stupidity lies in their failure to comprehend that *wilāyat* (the guardianship) of the Successor (*Wāsī*) is as obligatory as accepting the Messenger of Allāh himself. This parallels the structure of the Kalimah Tayyībah (*lā ilāha illā-Allāh*), which first negates and then affirms, as Allāh says (interpretation of the meaning):

> ⟪quran 2:256⟫ *[Image text (from image 015; citation unverified)]*
> *Let him disbelieve in false deities and believe in Allāh.*

In this verse, *yakfur bi-l-ṭāghūt* (disbelieving in false deities—the negation) comes first, then *yuʾmin bi-Allāh* (believing in Allāh—the affirmation). And He says:

> ⟪quran 3:7⟫
> ⟪ar:‏هُوَ ٱلَّذِىٓ أَنزَلَ عَلَيْكَ ٱلْكِتَبَ مِنْهُ آيَتٌۭ مُّحْكَمَتٌ هُنَّ أُمُّ ٱلْكِتَبِ وَأُخَرُ مُتَشَبِهَتٌۭ ۖ فَأَمَّا ٱلَّذِينَ فِى قُلُوبِهِمْ زَيْغٌۭ فَيَتَّبِعُونَ مَا تَشَبَهَ مِنْهُ ٱبْتِغَآءَ ٱلْفِتْنَةِ وَٱبْتِغَآءَ تَأْوِيلِهِۦ ۗ وَمَا يَعْلَمُ تَأْوِيلَهُۥٓ إِلَّا ٱللَّهُ وَٱلرَّسِخُونَ فِى ٱلْعِلْمِ ۗ يَقُولُونَ آمَنَّا بِهِۦ كُلٌّۭ مِّنْ عِندِ رَبِّنَا ۗ وَمَا يَذَّكَّرُ إِلَّآ أُو۟لُوا۟ ٱلْأَلْبَبِ ‎⟫
> It is He who has sent down upon you the Book; in it are verses [that are] precise—they are the foundation of the Book—and others [that are] unspecific. As for those in whose hearts is deviation, they follow that of it which is unspecific, seeking discord and seeking an interpretation [suitable to them]. And no one knows its [true] interpretation except Allāh. But those firm in knowledge say, "We have believed in it. All [of it] is from our Lord." And none will be reminded except those of understanding.[^12]

Here too, the recitation of the Qur'ān (affirming it) comes first, and the recitation comes after. Allāh says (interpretation of the meaning):

> ⟪quran 2:285⟫ *[Image text (from image 017; citation unverified)]*
> *The Messenger believes in what has been sent down to him from his Lord, and the believers all believe in Allāh.*

Those who judge others to be fools are themselves the true fools, for through stupidity and ignorance they have squandered and lost a great portion of their religion in the sight of Allāh, and deprived the saints of the knowledge of Allāh—an incomparable treasure for the soul and intellect. They have failed to appreciate the eternal and imperishable blessings of Allāh. They themselves do not know what they have lost or how great is the damage they have suffered.

(From the *Majālis al-Muʾayyidiyya* of Sayyidinā al-Muʾayyad al-Shirāzī)

## Their Likeness Is as the Likeness of One Who Kindled a Fire

*(no content in source)*

**What Is the Meaning of Fire?**

Fire is the noblest of the four elements. Unlike water and soil, it possesses no solid existence. Allāh has created it to perfect and transform all things; its virtue lies in that Allāh has made it subservient to all human beings—but not to other animals. Only humanity has been granted the wisdom to kindle fire from wood, stone, and iron, and to preserve it in substances that will receive and hold it, such as cotton or paper. Sulfur exemplifies such materials: its hidden ingredients possess a latent capacity to receive fire from flint and steel and to store it safely. Were this not so, it would be impossible to kindle even great wooden logs from a spark, for they resist the fire's grasp through their hardness and the moisture they retain.[^13]

In substance, fire first ignites those materials whose composition readily yields their essence. It then gradually consumes harder things, growing progressively in intensity. At times it spreads so greatly that it becomes a conflagration, difficult to control.

In the human being, *humanity* (*al-insāniyya*) lies hidden as fire lies concealed within a striking stone. Of all creatures, only humanity possesses the power to strike fire from stone. In the same way, the person who brings humanity to light is the perfect human—the guide of his age, the Prophet or the Imām. Just as a flint is worthless until it reaches the hand of one who will kindle light from it, so too the intellect of every person remains useless until he draws near to the Messenger and surrenders himself to the Messenger's instruction. The Messenger then rubs the intellect against the commandments of the Sharīʿa, burnishing it until it gleams and glows. He who heeds is preserved; whosoever the Messenger rejects is himself rejected and harmed.

**Spiritual and Sensual Fire**

Fire, hidden in stone and vegetation, can only be kindled by human hands—not by beasts. Similarly, there exists a spiritual fire that Mūsā (Moses) beheld upon the mountain. This is called the Holy Spirit (*al-Rūḥ al-Quds*). This spiritual and divine fire is, in truth, the fire of succession, prophethood, wills, and Imamate—also called the *Sulṭān* (dominion), the authority granted by Allāh to His chosen servants, through which they become exalted and take their place among Allāh's chosen, as Messengers of Allāh.[^14]

This dominion extends over both body and soul of humankind. By the power of this kingdom, the Messenger of Allāh prepares people for the Hereafter. This spiritual fire draws near to all as a divine blessing, yet only those with capacity and ability can benefit from it—and these are solely the saints of Allāh (*awliyāʾ Allāh*). Not every person possesses the capability to receive such benefit.

> ⟪quran 2:8⟫ *[Image text (from image 019; citation unverified)]*
> *And of mankind are some who say: "We believe in Allāh and the Last Day," while in truth they believe not.*

This verse refers to hypocrites who are bound to the Messenger of Allāh (peace and blessings of Allāh be upon him)—who is the *Sulṭān* (possessor of the Fire of Succession) and is attached to the strong rope of Allāh. Yet when

> ⟪quran 2:17⟫ *[Image text (from image 020; citation unverified)]*
> *their likeness is as the likeness of a man who kindled a fire…*

the lights of Prophethood began to spread all around, and Prophethood flourished as a branch through the Will of the Prophet, the Wills of the Prophets, and the Imamate of the Imāms,

> ⟪quran 2:17⟫ *[Image text (from image 021; citation unverified)]*
> *and when it lighted all around him, Allāh took away their light and left them in darkness where they could not see…*

that is, Allāh stripped them of their share of illumination because of their envy, hatred, arrogance, and disdain, and they were plunged back into darkness. The hypocrites were displeased to see the lineage of the Prophet flourish and branch forth, and they attempted to cast shadow upon the light. The light itself could not be extinguished, but the light they had received was withdrawn from them. Allāh deprived them of the light of the Wills and the Imamate, and they reverted to the darkness of ignorance.

**Can Light Mean the Qur'ān?**

In other verses, common exegetes take the word "light" (*nūr*) to mean the "Qur'ān," yet they do not grasp its true significance in this verse. Since the Qur'ān rests in everyone's hands, what meaning could attach to the "snatching away" of light in this context? The Qur'ān in its outward form lies in their hands, their eyes remain intact, and the manifest light of sun and moon shines bright.[^15]

The scholars say that "light" here does not refer to the outward Qur'ān inscribed upon paper; rather, it signifies the Author and Source of the Qur'ān, the *Qur'ān* (in its inner dimension), or the spiritual and inner Qur'ān, which is called the Successor (*Wāsī*) or the Imām of his time. By denying him, people lose the knowledge of *Nūrāniyya* (luminous knowledge).

> ⟪quran 2:17⟫ *[Image text (from image 022; citation unverified)]*
> *their likeness is as the likeness of a man who kindled a fire…*

**What Is Meant by Wrongdoing?**

By denying the Author of the Qur'ān, their vision and heart have been blinded. They have been deprived of the light of spiritual sciences, Qur'anic truths, and gnosis. They stumble at every step, unable to respond to even the smallest scholarly question. Trapped in a labyrinth of words devoid of meaning, they worship without understanding its true purpose. Theirs is an eternal, unrelenting darkness in which they remain ensnared. Though the outward light and darkness shall vanish one day with this world, the darkness that engulfs their souls shall endure forever. Referring to this inner light and darkness, Allāh speaks in the Āyat al-Kursī:

> ⟪quran 2:257⟫
> ⟪ar:‏ٱللَّهُ وَلِىُّ ٱلَّذِينَ آمَنُوا۟ يُخْرِجُهُم مِّنَ ٱلظُّلُمَتِ إِلَى ٱلنُّورِ ۖ وَٱلَّذِينَ كَفَرُوٓا۟ أَوْلِيَآؤُهُمُ ٱلطَّغُوتُ يُخْرِجُونَهُم مِّنَ ٱلنُّورِ إِلَى ٱلظُّلُمَتِ ۗ أُو۟لَٓئِكَ أَصْحَبُ ٱلنَّارِ ۖ هُمْ فِيهَا خَلِدُونَ ‎⟫
> Allāh is the Walī (Guardian) of those who believe; He brings them out from darknesses into the light. As for those who disbelieve, their *awliyāʾ* (guardians) are the *ṭāghūt* (false deities and false leaders); they bring them out of light into darknesses. Those are the inhabitants of the Fire; they shall abide therein eternally.[^16]

That is, Allāh (through the Imām of the time) brings the believers out of the darkness of mere words and the darkness of proverbs and symbols, drawing them toward eternal light and illumination.

Those who disbelieve in the *ḥudūd* (limits, sacred dimensions) of Allāh and associate others with Allāh have as their guardians the *ṭāghūt* (false powers, usurpers of authority). A *ṭāghūt* is any person who opposes the Prophet, the Successor (*Wāsī*), or the Imām—or one who mounts the pulpit in the place of the Messenger of Allāh (peace and blessings of Allāh be upon him) without permission. Their function is to draw people out of the light (inner meaning) into darkness (outward words alone). Lacking knowledge of the Qur'ān's inner meaning, they content themselves with the apparent literal sense of the words and lead all astray into the maze of words. In truth, darkness and obscurity have overwhelmed their souls, for they have turned away from those who would guide them toward the world of the soul and the light.

(From the *Majālis al-Muʾayyidiyya* of Sayyidinā al-Muʾayyad al-Shirāzī)

## He Who Made the Earth a Bed for You and the Heaven a Canopy

Allāh says (interpretation of the meaning):

> ⟪quran 2:22⟫
> ⟪ar:‏ٱلَّذِى جَعَلَ لَكُمُ ٱلْأَرْضَ فِرَشًۭا وَٱلسَّمَآءَ بِنَآءًۭا وَأَنزَلَ مِنَ ٱلسَّمَآءِ مَآءًۭا فَأَخْرَجَ بِهِۦ مِنَ ٱلثَّمَرَتِ رِزْقًۭا لَّكُمْ ۖ فَلَا تَجْعَلُوا۟ لِلَّهِ أَندَادًۭا وَأَنتُمْ تَعْلَمُونَ ‎⟫
> He it is who made the earth a bed for you and the heaven a canopy, and sent down water from the sky, and brought forth therewith fruits as sustenance for you. So do not set up rivals to Allāh while you know.[^17]

Allāh's word extends to every human being—the ignorant and the learned, the elite and the common people alike. Each person grasps its meaning according to his own understanding and knowledge. The ordinary Muslims interpret "heaven and earth" as the literal heavens and earth of this world, wherein both humans and animals dwell and satisfy their physical needs.

Yet the mystics of truth affirm that, since the human being consists of two natures—soul and body—there must necessarily exist, within the spiritual world, a "heaven" and "earth" corresponding to and complementary with the physical heavens and earth. The visible, tangible earth to which our bodily existence is bound is undoubtedly born from this material earth; it serves as our floor, our dwelling place, our soil. At death, we are buried within it and return to dust. But our soul belongs to a different order entirely; it has no kinship with this physical earth.[^18]

The Prophet (peace and blessings of Allāh be upon him) said: "The earth is your mother, and she acts in your interest." If we interpret this statement literally, it becomes neither true nor coherent, for earth—compelled by its own nature, without volition or agency—can neither be a mother nor demonstrate concern. Yet we observe that the earth does nurture vegetation from itself, and through these plants we receive our sustenance.

Therefore, the *ḥadīth* requires us to acknowledge another earth—a spiritual one—to search for its identity. This is not a realm that acts by compulsion, but one possessed of will and agency. From this spiritual earth, every believer draws sustenance and develops spiritually; and upon death, he merges into it and becomes one with it. This is the spiritual and inner earth of the Prophet or the Imām (peace be upon him)—that from which the believer's soul derives its spiritual nourishment. He is born from the divine sciences ('*ilm Allāh*) emanating from this earth, and after his death, he returns to it and becomes united with it. Thus this earth (the Successor or Imām) deserves far more than the literal earth to be called *umm al-barrā* (mother of goodness and virtue).

> ⟪quran 2:22⟫ *[Image text (from image 025; citation unverified)]*
> *He who made the earth a bed and a dwelling…*

Likewise in this verse, the heaven refers to a concealed heaven—the heaven of the religious sciences, which is inward and spiritual. Heaven and earth bear the same relationship to one another as do man and woman. The heaven bestows upon the earth its waters; the earth receives this bounty, absorbs it into itself, and brings forth bodily forms in flowering abundance. Yet this apparent heaven, for all its elevation and majesty, is itself mute, deaf, and blind, for it surrounds only the human body—a dense material form. It exerts no governance over human souls. Conversely, the human soul, despite its confinement within physical form, encompasses this vast universe and possesses complete knowledge of it.

Thus, the soul—or the spiritual heaven of religion—is he who bears the Sharīʿa (divine law), namely the Messenger of Allāh or the Imām of his time. He elevates the soul to its highest station and bestows his blessings upon the religious earth (the Successor or the Proof of the Imām). The earth then develops the soul's form, rendering it worthy of the Hereafter and eternal survival. This spiritual heaven and all the religious sciences connected to it are eternal and perpetual, whereas the outer heaven and its entire system shall pass away and perish.[^19]

| Heaven | Earth |
|---|---|
| Speaker (*mutakallim*) | Guardian (*walī*) |
| Imām | Proof of the Imām (*Ḥujjat al-Imām*) |

> ⟪quran 2:22⟫ *[Image text (from image 026; citation unverified)]*
> *and sent down water from the sky…*

From the structure of the verses, it becomes clear that water and fruits, like heaven and earth, possess hidden meanings in this context. For the common people, water refers to literal water quenching thirst, and fruit means visible fruits from which they derive pleasure and nourishment.

The wise scholars say: The water sent down from heaven refers to the revelation (*waḥy*) that Allāh sent down to the Messenger of Allāh (peace and blessings of Allāh be upon him), which he clothed in Qur'anic words and conveyed to the earth of the Guardian—that is, he transmitted this Qur'anic knowledge to his Successor (ʿAlī). Similarly, water signifies the knowledge received from the Imām in every age through his proof and argument.

**Outer Water and Inner Water**

Allāh says (interpretation of the meaning):

> ⟪quran 8:11⟫
> ⟪ar:‏إِذْ يُغَشِّيكُمُ ٱلنُّعَاسَ أَمَنَةًۭ مِّنْهُ وَيُنَزِّلُ عَلَيْكُم مِّنَ ٱلسَّمَآءِ مَآءًۭا لِّيُطَهِّرَكُم بِهِۦ وَيُذْهِبَ عَنكُمْ رِجْزَ ٱلشَّيْطَنِ وَلِيَرْبِطَ عَلَىٰ قُلُوبِكُمْ وَيُثَبِّتَ بِهِ ٱلْأَقْدَامَ ‎⟫
> When He cast slumber upon you as a repose from Him, and sent down upon you water from the sky, to purify you therewith and to remove from you the uncleanness of Satan, and to strengthen your hearts, and to steady your feet with it.[^20]

If this refers to literal water, then the question arises: are the angels creations of Allāh or not? Certainly they are creations; for should they not exist, they would be non-existent things, and non-existent things are not objects. Every Muslim believes that angels exist and are creations of Allāh just like us. Then do angels require the same water (for physical ablution) for their spiritual sustenance? Allāh then says (interpretation of the meaning):

> ⟪quran 21:30⟫
> ⟪ar:‏أَوَلَمْ يَرَ ٱلَّذِينَ كَفَرُوٓا۟ أَنَّ ٱلسَّمَوَتِ وَٱلْأَرْضَ كَانَتَا رَتْقًۭا فَفَتَقْنَهُمَا ۖ وَجَعَلْنَا مِنَ ٱلْمَآءِ كُلَّ شَىْءٍ حَىٍّ ۖ أَفَلَا يُؤْمِنُونَ ‎⟫
> Have not those who disbelieve seen that the heavens and the earth were joined together as one unit, then We separated them, and We made from water every living thing? Will they not, then, believe?[^21]

Though comprehensible at the literal level—that Allāh sends down apparent water from the sky for our ritual purification—this raises a profound difficulty. How could physical water remove the filth of Satan? Had such power resided in literal water, no disbeliever or associationist would exist in the world today; no one would deny Allāh and His Messenger. Likewise, the revelation of mere physical water does not increase love in hearts, nor does it transform enemies into friends or strengthen wavering steps, nor does it guide the astray.

From these verses, it becomes evident that for the spiritual scholar there is but one water in parallel to the apparent water: the knowledge of the Imām (peace be upon him). This is the knowledge by which the inner life of angels and all souls becomes possible; this is the knowledge by which the filth of Satan—doubt, disbelief, and association—can be removed; this is the knowledge by which scattered hearts are unified and stumbling feet grow firm.[^22]

> ⟪quran 2:22⟫ *[Image text (from image 029; citation unverified)]*
> *and brought forth therewith fruits as sustenance for you…*

The water sent down from heaven divides into two categories: that which benefits directly—such as quenching thirst—and that which, absorbed into the earth, produces fruits and vegetables that become food for humans and animals. In both modes, water is a source of life.

Similarly, the revelation sent down from the spiritual heaven divides into two aspects: the outward commands enjoined for obedience, which instill in the soul the virtues of purity, fear of Allāh, and angelic resemblance—such as prayer, which safeguards against obscenity and wrongdoing, or fasting, which teaches abstinence from eating and drinking as the angels do, thereby protecting oneself from all moral transgression. Wealth is purified through *zakāt* (alms), and one's sins are pardoned through the *Ḥajj* (pilgrimage). The second aspect is the knowledge hidden within the revelation—concealed in the Qur'anic words themselves—which is called *taʾwīl* (esoteric interpretation) and the inward truth (*bāṭin*). Concerning this, Allāh says:

> ⟪quran 3:7⟫
> ⟪ar:‏هُوَ ٱلَّذِىٓ أَنزَلَ عَلَيْكَ ٱلْكِتَبَ مِنْهُ آيَتٌۭ مُّحْكَمَتٌ هُنَّ أُمُّ ٱلْكِتَبِ وَأُخَرُ مُتَشَبِهَتٌۭ ۖ فَأَمَّا ٱلَّذِينَ فِى قُلُوبِهِمْ زَيْغٌۭ فَيَتَّبِعُونَ مَا تَشَبَهَ مِنْهُ ٱبْتِغَآءَ ٱلْفِتْنَةِ وَٱبْتِغَآءَ تَأْوِيلِهِۦ ۗ وَمَا يَعْلَمُ تَأْوِيلَهُۥٓ إِلَّا ٱللَّهُ وَٱلرَّسِخُونَ فِى ٱلْعِلْمِ ۗ يَقُولُونَ آمَنَّا بِهِۦ كُلٌّۭ مِّنْ عِندِ رَبِّنَا ۗ وَمَا يَذَّكَّرُ إِلَّآ أُو۟لُوا۟ ٱلْأَلْبَبِ ‎⟫
> It is He who has sent down the Book to you; in it are verses [which are] firm and clear—they are the foundation of the Book—and others [which are] allegorical. As for those in whose hearts is deviation, they follow that of it which is allegorical, seeking discord and seeking [an interpretation suited to them]. But none knows its [true] interpretation except Allāh and those firm in knowledge say, "We believe in it; all of it is from our Lord." And none takes heed except those possessed of intelligence.[^23]

Just as the human being—the essence of creation and the embodiment of reason—draws forth the fruits and vegetables of the earth through his own effort, so the scholar of *taʾwīl* (inner exegesis) elicits the realities and meanings from the Qur'anic words transmitted through the Successor (*Wāsī*), informing believers of true monotheism (*tawḥīd*) and knowledge of the Qur'ān's source, and bestowing upon them the enjoyment of eternal, perpetual fruits.

One of the supreme virtues of Qur'anic fruits is that, no matter how much one draws from them, they never diminish or wane—unlike worldly fruits, which are consumed and exhausted by eating. The more one contemplates them, the fresher and more profound they become. The more one ponders a verse, the more it unveils a new meaning and new pleasure with each reflection. For this reason, Imām Jaʿfar al-Ṣādiq (peace be upon him) declared that there is no end to the meanings contained in the Qur'anic verses. This is the sea of knowledge (*baḥr al-ʿulūm*), wherein every diver emerges with treasure. The knowledge of the Qur'ān resembles the fruits of Paradise, which do not diminish when eaten but increase instead. Someone once asked Amīr al-Muʾminīn (ʿAlī): "What is the fruit of Paradise in this world?" He replied: "The Qur'ān!" On the other hand, the example of these Qur'anic sciences is that of a lamp that lights countless other lamps while its own light suffers no diminution.

> ⟪quran 2:22⟫ *[Image text (from image 031; citation unverified)]*
> *So do not set up rivals to Allāh while you know.*

That is, do not take anyone other than your Lord as creator and sustainer. The outer sustenance is obtained through the earth; the inner sustenance through the Prophets and Imāms. Do not associate anyone with Him; worship Him alone.

|---|---|---|---|
| Speaker (*mutakallim*) | Apparent Qur'ān (Words) | Guardian (*walī*) | Inner Qur'ān (Interpretation and Realities) |
| Imām | Banner (*rāyat*) | Proof of the Imām (*Ḥujjat al-Imām*) | The Face/Countenance of the Believer |

## "And if you are in doubt about what We have revealed to Our servant, then produce a Surah like it"

God declares in the Qur'an:

> ⟪quran 2:24⟫
> ⟪ar:‏فَإِن لَّمْ تَفْعَلُوا۟ وَلَن تَفْعَلُوا۟ فَٱتَّقُوا۟ ٱلنَّارَ ٱلَّتِى وَقُودُهَا ٱلنَّاسُ وَٱلْحِجَارَةُ ۖ أُعِدَّتْ لِلْكَفِرِينَ ‎⟫

### The Dual Challenge: Outward and Inward

At the manifest level, disbelievers and polytheists questioned the Qur'an's divine origin, claiming it was a human composition. Supported by certain Jews and Christians, they fabricated calumnies against the Messenger. In response, God challenged them directly: if you possess the skill and eloquence you claim, produce a single chapter comparable to those of the Qur'an, and bring your own witnesses to testify to its superiority. Yet no such chapter emerged—nor could any emerge. The challenge remained irrefutable across centuries.[^cite-1]

At the esoteric level (bāṭin), hypocrites harbored a different doubt. They acknowledged Muhammad's prophethood yet denied that God had designated ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib as his successor and legatee (waṣī). Instead, they claimed the succession arose from kinship and worldly affection alone, not from divine command. Thus God extended His challenge to these internal adversaries as well: if you deny that this Messenger's legatee possesses the comprehensive knowledge and spiritual authority befitting God's designated guardian on earth, then present from among your ranks a scholar equal to him—one upon whom the Messenger opened a thousand gates of knowledge, each gate disclosing a thousand more.[^cite-2] Yet show us merely one among your own lesser guides and prove he shares even the ḥadd dānī (lower limit) of the legatee's learning and capacities. This, too, remains impossible.

The Messenger's station in religious law and faith resembles that of a farmer working the land: through his labour and skill, he extracts the means of physical sustenance. Similarly, the Guardian (walī) wields the power of inference (istinbāṭ) upon the land of the Qur'an, drawing forth timeless meanings without compare—meanings that illuminate the eternal life of the human soul, shield it from ruin, and grant it deathless survival.

### Surah: Elevation and Enclosure

The term "surah" derives from two interconnected meanings. First, it signifies exaltation and honor. When a surah was revealed to the Prophet, it elevated his status and magnified his standing before God and creation. Second, surah denotes encompassment, as a city wall encloses and protects all within it. The Qur'anic surah contains remembrance of the Prophet and chronicles of past and future, embracing all matters as a rampart embraces the city and its inhabitants.

In this sense, the Qur'an comprehends true tawhīd (divine unity), distinguishing God from all attributes that would assimilate Him to the physical realm. It encompasses the origins of time itself—all matters and bounds over which the Shariah of Muhammad holds dominion. Each boundary (ḥadd) of the daʿwa (summons/invitation) to faith constitutes a wall of the Gate of Madinat al-ʿIlm (the City of Knowledge), within which resides a civilization of meanings. God addresses those who deny this Gate: if you doubt that it was established by His command and that it is the utterance of His Messenger and His Word, then where are your comparable and comprehensive teachings? Where is your knowledge equal to even one of His invocations? You dare not, therefore, contradict him; instead, point to one of his Companions as witness—Salmān al-Fārisī, Miqdād ibn al-Aswad, Abū Dharr al-Ghiffārī, ʿAmmār Yāsir, or Muḥammad ibn Abī Bakr—and prove that such a one possesses even a fraction of his learning.[^cite-3]

This divine address encompasses both the disbelievers and the internal hypocrites. Both those who deny the apparent Qur'an's divine origin and those who deny the Prophet's designation of his successor contest God's design. The Messenger came as a bearer of glad tidings (bashīr) and a warner (nadhīr) to all humanity, for God says:

> ⟪quran 34:28⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَمَآ أَرْسَلْنَكَ إِلَّا كَآفَّةًۭ لِّلنَّاسِ بَشِيرًۭا وَنَذِيرًۭا وَلَكِنَّ أَكْثَرَ ٱلنَّاسِ لَا يَعْلَمُونَ ‎⟫

Thus the Prophet declared: "I have been sent to all humanity, the white and the red." Consequently, God's challenge applies equally to manifest disbelievers and to hidden hypocrites.

### The Challenge Extended to All Creation

God then expands His declaration:

> ⟪quran 17:88⟫
> ⟪ar:‏قُل لَّىِٕنِ ٱجْتَمَعَتِ ٱلْإِنسُ وَٱلْجِنُّ عَلَىٰٓ أَن يَأْتُوا۟ بِمِثْلِ هَذَا ٱلْقُرْآنِ لَا يَأْتُونَ بِمِثْلِهِۦ وَلَوْ كَانَ بَعْضُهُمْ لِبَعْضٍۢ ظَهِيرًۭا ‎⟫

Were all humankind and all jinn to unite in the effort to produce a Qur'an like this, they would utterly fail—even if they aided one another with their full strength.

Whereas the first verse addressed only disbelievers, here the scope widens to include all human scholars and all jinn. Angels are notably absent from this declaration, for angels obey God's command without wavering; they neither disobey nor rebel. Only jinn and humans, both capable of transgression, are mentioned.[^cite-4]

When God renders the entire world incapable of presenting an equivalent to the outward Qur'an with such force, they are necessarily still more helpless before the esoteric Qur'an (al-Qur'ān al-bāṭin). The outward is form alone; the esoteric is its soul. A sculptor may craft the finest human likeness, yet cannot breathe life into it—that inner essence lies beyond his reach. Therefore, if mortals cannot produce a book matching the apparent Qur'an, they are infinitely more powerless to match the inner Qur'an or the book of the Nāṭiq (the Speaking Prophet). Hence Imām Jaʿfar al-Ṣādiq declared: "Whoever possesses an outward miracle possesses an inward miracle far greater still."

### The Emphasis on Witnesses

God reinforces the weakness and incapacity of the challengers, saying: "Bring your witnesses." The significance of testimony in any dispute lies in its power to adjudicate truth from falsehood between contending parties. When a just witness withstands cross-examination, his testimony is accepted and right is awarded to the rightful claimant. Here, God further challenges: when you present a word against a surah of the Qur'an, summon your own witnesses—not Mine—and let them judge. Let them ask themselves: is your speech superior in Arabic eloquence to God's? Your own witnesses will surely confess that God's Word is exalted beyond all comparison.

The Word of God repeatedly invokes testimony to establish its truth. He addresses the deniers:

> ⟪quran 41:53⟫
> ⟪ar:‏سَنُرِيهِمْ آيَتِنَا فِي ٱلْآفَاقِ وَفِىٓ أَنفُسِهِمْ حَتَّىٰ يَتَبَيَّنَ لَهُمْ أَنَّهُ ٱلْحَقُّ ‎⟫

We shall show them Our signs in the horizons and in their own souls, until it becomes clear to them that this is the Truth. At that point, He declares:

> ⟪quran 25:53⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَهُوَ ٱلَّذِى مَرَجَ ٱلْبَحْرَيْنِ هَذَا عَذْبٌۭ وَهَذَا مِلْحٌۭ ‎⟫

At times, God Himself serves as witness:

> ⟪quran 13:43⟫
> ⟪ar:‏قُلْ كَفَىٰ بِٱللَّهِ شَهِيدًۭا بَيْنِى وَبَيْنَكُمْ وَمَن عِندَهُۥ عِلْمٌۭ مِّنَ ٱلْكِتَبِ ‎⟫

Say: "God suffices as a witness between me and you, as does he who possesses knowledge of the Book." He appoints the angels as witnesses and makes the Messenger and the Community (Ummah) witnesses:

> ⟪quran 2:143⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَكَذَلِكَ جَعَلْنَكُمْ أُمَّةًۭ وَسَطًۭا لِّتَكُونُوا۟ شُهَدَآءَ عَلَى ٱلنَّاسِ وَيَكُونُ ٱلرَّسُولُ عَلَيْكُمْ شَهِيدًۭا ۗ وَمَا جَعَلْنَا ٱلْقِبْلَةَ ٱلَّتِى كُنتَ عَلَيْهَآ إِلَّا لِنَعْلَمَ مَن يَتَّبِعُ ٱلرَّسُولُ مِمَّن يَنقَلِبُ عَلَىٰ عَقِبَيْهِ ۚ وَإِن كَانَتْ لَكَبِيرَةً إِلَّا عَلَى ٱلَّذِينَ هَدَى ٱللَّهُ ۗ وَمَا كَانَ ٱللَّهُ لِيُضِيعَ إِيمَنَكُمْ ۚ إِنَّ ٱللَّهَ بِٱلنَّاسِ لَرَءُوفٌۭ رَّحِيمٌۭ ‎⟫

Thus We have made you a middle nation, so that you may be witnesses over humankind and the Messenger may be a witness over you. Imām Jaʿfar al-Ṣādiq expounded: "We are the middle nation, and we are witnesses to the deeds of all people; the Messenger is our witness as to whether we have conveyed God's commands to His servants." God emphasizes witness and testimony to establish the truth of every matter, great and small. Therefore, addressing those who deny the Prophet's eloquence and the uniqueness of his utterance, He declares: "Bring a word like it and present witnesses that your words surpass the Qur'an—that every utterance the Messenger brought bears witness only to its own deficiency compared to God's Word."

Yet the true witness of the Qur'an is the Qur'an itself, which extracts meaning from the silent words of this Book just as a farmer extracts grain from the earth, or as sustenance is drawn from every fruit and beast. The Qur'an is in everyone's hands, yet they remain unable to fathom it. This virtue is reserved exclusively for the Holy Prophet and his Ahl al-Bayt (Family of the Prophet)—they alone can infer true meaning from its words and expound its genuine purpose. They are the authentic witnesses of the Qur'an. The apparent book is the miracle of the Messenger; its interpretation is the miracle of the true descendants of the Messenger, a privilege none can usurp, no matter how many conspire to do so.

At the verse's conclusion, God says:

> ⟪quran 2:26⟫
> ⟪ar:‏إِنَّ ٱللَّهَ لَا يَسْتَحْىِۦ أَن يَضْرِبَ مَثَلًۭا مَّا بَعُوضَةًۭ فَمَا فَوْقَهَا ۚ فَأَمَّا ٱلَّذِينَ آمَنُوا۟ فَيَعْلَمُونَ أَنَّهُ ٱلْحَقُّ مِن رَّبِّهِمْ ۖ وَأَمَّا ٱلَّذِينَ كَفَرُوا۟ فَيَقُولُونَ مَاذَآ أَرَادَ ٱللَّهُ بِهَذَا مَثَلًۭا ۘ يُضِلُّ بِهِۦ كَثِيرًۭا وَيَهْدِى بِهِۦ كَثِيرًۭا ۚ وَمَا يُضِلُّ بِهِۦٓ إِلَّا ٱلْفَسِقِينَ ‎⟫

Here the terms "truthful" (ṣādiq) and "liar" (kādhib) arise. Ordinarily, "truthful" denotes one who speaks truth, and "liar" denotes one who speaks falsehood. Yet the deeper meaning reveals itself: the Hereafter is truthful and this world is false, for truth is that which endures immutably, never decaying. Falsehood perpetually shifts and dissolves. Observing the world's condition, we see that nothing sustains itself—it rises and falls. Spring gives way to autumn, summer to winter. The three stages of human life—childhood, youth, old age—transform continuously until death. Wealth alternates with poverty, health with disease, life with death, honour with humiliation. No state persists unchanging. The abode of the Hereafter, by contrast, is the house of peace and security, purified from all flux. There dwell health without disease, life without death, honour without shame. All stations remain eternally elevated and stable.[^cite-5]

God has placed proof of the spiritual world's reality in the human heart, for whatever dwells in the heart is truth untainted by falsehood. Yet when that truth emerges upon the tongue—clothed in the density of words, moulded into letters—falsehood appears.[^cite-6] Those who testify to the abode of truth are themselves truthful: the Prophets, saints, Imāms, and their righteous successors. These are the truthful ones God intends when He says:

> ⟪quran 9:119⟫
> ⟪ar:‏يَٓأَيُّهَا ٱلَّذِينَ آمَنُوا۟ ٱتَّقُوا۟ ٱللَّهَ وَكُونُوا۟ مَعَ ٱلصَّدِقِينَ ‎⟫

O you who believe, fear God and be among the truthful. Imām Jaʿfar al-Ṣādiq declared: "We are the truthful, and this verse is intended for us." The realm of the subtle and the spiritual is the abode of the Hereafter, free from all change and flux; the material realm is steeped in falsehood. Therefore, "be with the truthful" means: join those who teach you of the Hereafter and the realm of eternity, who deliver you from worldly fear and material craving, and lead you to nearness with God. Avoid those trapped in the world of falsehood—materialism alone—who imagine it to be everything.

After challenging all disbelievers and polytheists, God announces the consequence:

> ⟪quran 2:24⟫
> ⟪ar:‏فَإِن لَّمْ تَفْعَلُوا۟ وَلَن تَفْعَلُوا۟ فَٱتَّقُوا۟ ٱلنَّارَ ٱلَّتِى وَقُودُهَا ٱلنَّاسُ وَٱلْحِجَارَةُ ۖ أُعِدَّتْ لِلْكَفِرِينَ ‎⟫

Since you cannot produce a surah like the Qur'an, even with false witnesses at your side, then fear the Fire prepared for the disbelievers. God has deliberately set an impossible condition to expose your utter powerlessness and eliminate all future pretence. Thus He confirms His Word: no matter how eloquent creatures may become, their speech cannot compare to the utterance of Aḥsan al-Khāliqu'n (the Best of Creators). Similarly, only God—the Lord of the Worlds—possesses the wisdom to choose the Prophet's successor and legatee. Should servants whose intellect is enslaved by Satan choose, they would select one like themselves: neither pure nor learned. For the Qur'an—the Qur'ān itself—dominates and reigns over all humanity as humanity rules the animal kingdom. This Book's soul is the soul of the human world and the true Qiblah (direction) toward which all souls turn. He is the Refuge (Marja) of the Creator, the Self-Sufficient One (al-Samad), upon Whom the entire universe depends. This is why the Prophet cried from the pulpit: "Ask me before you lose me"—meaning ask ʿAlī, for he is the repository of divine knowledge.

No flaw has been discovered in ʿAlī's speech—called Kalām al-Amīr (the Emir's Discourse)—nor has the entire world, advanced as it has become in jurisprudence, found deficiency in his judgments. The Book of the Universe lay open before him; nothing was hidden. Hence he said: "I have reached such certainty that were all veils lifted from my eyes, my faith would not increase by a hair's breadth. I am aware of every particle of existence through the knowledge and power granted me by the Messenger of God, and I can discern the truth and origin of all things." Who, then, can stand against one of such knowledge and exalted station? You have scaled a height no one has reached nor can reach; yet comparison and contrast with your companions and those who learned from you and received your radiance remain impossible.

### The Inner Fire

Apparent fire possesses two qualities: heat and light, whose union constitutes fire itself. The inner fire—the revelation of prophethood—similarly bears two attributes. For believers, it is light leading them to the realm of illumination, delivering them from darkness. For disbelievers, the deniers, and the hypocrites, it is heat that propels them toward Hell and eternal torment. Prophethood is one, yet its light saves the believer while its heat burns the denier.

| **Prophethood's Fire** |  |
| --- | --- |
| **Light** (Believers) | **Heat** (Disbelievers, Hypocrites) |

### "The fuel thereof is people and stones"

God addresses both disbelievers and hypocrites with warning of this Fire's heat:

> ⟪quran 2:24⟫
> ⟪ar:‏فَٱتَّقُوا۟ ٱلنَّارَ ٱلَّتِى وَقُودُهَا ٱلنَّاسُ وَٱلْحِجَارَةُ ۖ أُعِدَّتْ لِلْكَفِرِينَ ‎⟫

The Almighty's description of this Fire's fuel is striking and troubling. He says its fuel shall be "people and stones," then adds it is "prepared for the disbelievers." A question arises: while stones as fuel is comprehensible—flammable minerals like sulphur ignite swiftly and intensify burning—what of "people" as fuel? And if disbelievers are mentioned separately, are they not themselves people? What, then, is the connection God draws between stones and people with the particle "and" (wa)?[^cite-7]

The particle "wa" (and) joins only things similar and corresponding—goodness and piety, strength and courage, love and affection, Muḥammad and ʿAlī. Yet stone is inanimate, useless because it neither melts nor can be remoulded into productive forms. Secondly, paired terms must be either identical or reciprocal. If "stones" means hard-hearted people, who then are "people"? Both would refer to the same thing. Why mention them twice?

The people of truth (Ahl al-Ḥaqq), in explaining this verse's esoteric meaning, say that those who reject the beneficial influence of the Messenger's teachings are themselves stones. Of these God says:

> ⟪quran 2:74⟫
> ⟪ar:‏ثُمَّ قَسَتْ قُلُوبُكُم مِّن بَعْدِ ذَلِكَ فَهِىَ كَٱلْحِجَارَةِ أَوْ أَشَدُّ قَسْوَةًۭ ۚ وَإِنَّ مِنَ ٱلْحِجَارَةِ لَمَا يَتَفَجَّرُ مِنْهُ ٱلْأَنْهَرُ ۚ وَإِنَّ مِنْهَا لَمَا يَشَقَّقُ فَيَخْرُجُ مِنْهُ ٱلْمَآءُ ۚ وَإِنَّ مِنْهَا لَمَا يَهْبِطُ مِنْ خَشْيَةِ ٱللَّهِ ۚ وَمَا ٱللَّهُ بِغَافِلٍۢ عَمَّا تَعْمَلُونَ ‎⟫

#### The Stones

These are people who recite the Messenger's words yet deny the guidance of the Imāms and guides after him. They clung to the outward Scripture but abandoned the wisdom revealed alongside it. Though human in form, they are worse than stone—their obstinacy and hardness of heart surpass any rock. Their hearts did not break at the martyrdom of the Prophet's saints like Muʿāwiyah or Yazīd.[^cite-8]

#### The People

Al-Nās, part of the aforementioned group, are those who accepted the Imāms after the Messenger and disowned their enemies, yet exceeded the bounds of love for the Ahl al-Bayt. They exaggerated their glory, elevating them above the Messenger himself, calling them "Lord of the Worlds" and "Lord of Lords," and made this their creed. Though they seemed guided, they strayed; they turned from the infallible Imāms' teachings as an arrow flies from the bow.

| **The Stones** (Nāfiṭ) | **The People** (Ghālī) |
| --- | --- |
| Those who believe in the Messenger but reject the Imāms | Those who love the Imāms but elevate them beyond their station |
| The Depreciator | The Exaggerator |

The Messenger said: "O ʿAlī! In your knowledge two parties have perished: one that removes you from your station, and another that raises you above it. The first obeyed only the outward Shariah's rulings and abandoned the Prophet's progeny; the second entered into guardianship (wilāya) and abandoned the Book and its commandments." God likened the first—hard-hearted—group to stones, and the second He called "people."

Then God says:

> ⟪quran 2:24⟫
> ⟪ar:‏فَٱتَّقُوا۟ ٱلنَّارَ ٱلَّتِى وَقُودُهَا ٱلنَّاسُ وَٱلْحِجَارَةُ ۖ أُعِدَّتْ لِلْكَفِرِينَ ‎⟫

#### The Disbelievers (al-Kāfirīn)

Kufr literally means to cover and conceal. A farmer is called kāfir because he presses seed into the earth; a river is called kāfir because all is hidden in its depths. Islam therefore calls those who conceal truth "disbelievers." Two types exist:

First, those who, driven by lust for power and worldly glory and hatred of truth, deliberately veiled it. They denied the Ghadīr Khumm event. After affirming belief, they feigned ignorance—like ʿUmar ibn al-Khaṭṭāb—and turned away. Second, those who blindly followed leaders, dazzled by appearance and deceived by rhetoric, intending no opposition to truth but merely straying from it through imitation of their elders. The first type is the most severe—those who opposed guidance and usurped rights. In this verse, God refers to the first type.

The Fire of prophethood burns these three groups: the disbelievers, the depreciators (those denying the Imāms), and the exaggerators (those elevating them falsely). One group resembles Muʿāwiyah, Yazīd, Pharaoh, and Nimrod—those whom God explicitly named disbelievers. The second comprises those who follow apparent truth, reciting the Qur'an with proper phonetics yet blind to its meaning and reality. The third claims to follow ʿAlī yet believes like the Ṭāghūt (false tyrants). All three shall taste the heat of prophethood's fire and deserve God's punishment.[^cite-9]

## "And give glad tidings to those who believe and do righteous deeds"

God says:

> ⟪quran 2:25⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَبَشِّرِ ٱلَّذِينَ آمَنُوا۟ وَعَمِلُوا۟ ٱلصَّلِحَتِ أَنَّ لَهُمْ جَنَّتٍۢ تَجْرِى مِن تَحْتِهَا ٱلْأَنْهَرُ ۖ كُلَّمَا رُزِقُوا۟ مِنْهَا مِن ثَمَرَةٍۢ رِّزْقًۭا ۙ قَالُوا۟ هَذَا ٱلَّذِى رُزِقْنَا مِن قَبْلُ ۖ وَأُتُوا۟ بِهِۦ مُتَشَبِهًۭا ۖ وَلَهُمْ فِيهَآ أَزْوَجٌۭ مُّطَهَّرَةٌۭ ۖ وَهُمْ فِيهَا خَلِدُونَ ‎⟫

### The Glad Tidings and the Bearer

In Arabic lexicography, a bashīr (bearer of glad tidings) is one who journeys far ahead of his people through forests, seeking water. Upon finding it, he stands in place and waves his sword or cloth, signalling the good news of discovering the life-giving spring.

The Messenger of God similarly ventured far ahead of all humanity in quest of knowledge of the Mother of the Book (Umm al-Kitāb), the Saviour, and eternal life of the human soul. When divine Revelation granted him success, he called all creation to this fountain of life from his exalted station. He summoned with gestures and signs. As a bashīr waves now a sword, now a cloth, so the Messenger employed both sternness and gentleness, warnings of Hell and promises of Paradise, intimidation and understanding—drawing people toward *Lā ilāha illā Allāh* (there is no god save God), the source of eternal life and true divine unity (tawhīd). For this, the Messenger guarantees his followers both Paradise in the Hereafter and security in this world. Since he employed both approaches, he was named not only bashīr but also nadhīr (warner):

> ⟪quran 25:56⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَمَآ أَرْسَلْنَكَ إِلَّا مُبَشِّرًۭا وَنَذِيرًۭا ‎⟫

### The Believers

Īmān (faith) means to affirm or acknowledge, and in another sense, to give security. Faith is a transitive action, not ending in the action itself but requiring both a verb and its object. Two things are essential to faith: the act of affirming and the thing affirmed. If faith means affirmation, one must ask: what is affirmed? The affirmer is the subject; what he affirms is the object. If faith means granting security, there must be an object of that security. The Prophet united both meanings in one ḥadīth: "A believer is one who secures his neighbor from his own harm." Therefore one is called both a believer (muʾmin) and one who gives security (āmin). Among God's most beautiful names is al-Mu'min—the Grantor of Security. A person becomes a believer by affirming God's promises; God is the Grantor of Security because He has protected His creatures from destruction.[^cite-10]

| **Relation** | **Agent** | **Act** | **Object** |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Affirmation | Believer | Verification | God, the Messenger, the Imām |
| Security | Believer | Granting Peace | The Community |
| Security | God | Granting Peace | Doom |

In this verse, believers are those who affirm the Messenger and all his commands and who secure fellow believers and Muslims from the hardships that befall them.

### Doing Righteous Deeds

Generally, righteous deeds mean charity, kindness, honouring parents, compassion toward people, and obedience to Shariah's commandments. These are well-known and commonly understood; even the simplest intellect grasps their virtue. Yet they are not so great and magnificent as to require God's special revelation to explain them to His servants.

Here, therefore, we depart from the conventional meaning. The true purpose of righteous deeds is to follow the ṣāliḥūn al-mu'minīn—the righteous believers who are the Imāms of all times, those profoundly grounded in the Qur'an's commandments and understanding its true meaning. God speaks of their greatness:

> ⟪quran 3:18⟫
> ⟪ar:‏شَهِدَ ٱللَّهُ أَنَّهُ لَا إِلَهَ إِلَّا هُوَ وَٱلْمَلَٓئِكَةُ وَأُوْلُوا۟ ٱلْعِلْمِ قَآئِمًۭا بِٱلْقِسْطِ ۚ لَا إِلَهَ إِلَّا هُوَ ٱلْعَزِيزُ ٱلْحَكِيمُ ‎⟫

God, Gabriel, and the righteous believers bear witness that none deserves worship save Him.[^cite-11] In another sense, the righteous believers refer to ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib, father of all Imāms.

ʿAlī holds among the Imāms the station the Imāms hold among believers. Just as an Imām is the righteous exemplar for believers, ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib is the righteous exemplar for the Imāms. All believers' deeds depend upon him. He is the centre of goodness and the fount of beauty; all righteous deeds flow through him—justice, benevolence, trust, honesty, restoring orphans' and relatives' rights. Only through him may we attain true knowledge of God's unity and the reality of obedience. All moral virtues the Messenger was sent to perfect are embodied in his Ahl al-Bayt (Family). Of them the Messenger said: "Al-Ḥasan and al-Ḥusayn are from me, and I am from them." Of his daughter Fāṭimah, he declared: "Fāṭimah is among the houris of Paradise." Of the father of the Imāms, he said: "O ʿAlī, your flesh is my flesh, your blood my blood; your station with me is as the soul's station in the body."

These pure ones, so close to the Prophet, embody the morality the Qur'an teaches and transmit it. In essence, those who follow the righteous are those who perform righteous deeds. All their actions are righteous. Whoever abandons the righteous believers—what is called *Ḥablullāh al-Matīn* (God's firm rope)—and becomes their enemy will see his apparent good deeds ruined. His charity becomes sin; his Islam becomes pre-Islamic ignorance and polytheism; his soul darkens.

### The Mosque After Ablution

The Commander of the Faithful, ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib, said: "Whoever enters the mosque after performing complete ritual purification (wuḍūʾ) remains in a state of prayer until something nullifies his purity." In Qur'anic interpretation, this means: whoever takes the oath of allegiance to the Imām of the Age and purifies himself from suspicion and polytheism, and enters into the Imām's summons—the mosque, the House of God—shall have all his actions accepted by God, whether worldly or religious, so long as he remains in that summons without breaking his oath through mockery, backbiting, or open opposition. If he nullifies his oath by any such act, he withdraws from the Imām's invitation, and none of his deeds shall be accepted.[^cite-12]

God gives glad tidings of Paradise to those who follow the righteous believers and spend their lives within the circle of their allegiance.

## "And do not say of those slain in God's way that they are dead; rather, they are alive"

God declares:

> ⟪quran 2:154⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَلَا تَقُولُوا۟ لِمَن يُقْتَلُ فِى سَبِيلِ ٱللَّهِ أَمْوَتٌۢ ۚ بَلْ أَحْيَآءٌۭ وَلَكِن لَّا تَشْعُرُونَ ‎⟫

### The Inner Martyrdom

Sayyidunā Ḥusayn ibn Jidayn, in his *Rawḍat al-Mustafīd*, explains that being slain in God's way means the usurpation of the stations of God's saints and the denial of their ranks. Yet such cruel oppression does not sever the saints from their inner service; their ministration continues inwardly. Therefore, God says: "Think not them dead, for they quietly pursue their work in their realm, though you perceive it not."

In another verse, God states that the martyrs are alive, receiving sustenance from their Lord. This signifies that although the usurpers apparently stripped the Imāms and saints of their positions, all continued to receive knowledge and support from the Commander of the Faithful. God's statement—"upon them shall come no fear, nor shall they grieve"—means these saints fear not worldly diminishment nor sorrow over material loss. Though the outer summons was halted, the inner summons continued.[^cite-13]

## "O you who believe, do not nullify your charity by reminders of favour and injury"

God says in *Sūrat al-Baqarah*:

> ⟪quran 2:264⟫
> ⟪ar:‏يَٓأَيُّهَا ٱلَّذِينَ آمَنُوا۟ لَا تُبْطِلُوا۟ صَدَقَتِكُم بِٱلْمَنِّ وَٱلْأَذَىٰ كَٱلَّذِى يُنفِقُ مَالَهُۥ رِئَآءَ ٱلنَّاسِ وَلَا يُؤْمِنُ بِٱللَّهِ وَٱلْيَوْمِ ٱلْآخِرِ ۖ فَمَثَلُهُۥ كَمَثَلِ صَفْوَانٍ عَلَيْهِ تُرَابٌۭ فَأَصَابَهُۥ وَابِلٌۭ فَتَرَكَهُۥ صَلْدًۭا ۖ لَّا يَقْدِرُونَ عَلَىٰ شَىْءٍۢ مِّمَّا كَسَبُوا۟ ۗ وَٱللَّهُ لَا يَهْدِى ٱلْقَوْمَ ٱلْكَفِرِينَ ‎⟫

### The Outward and Inward Charity

This verse has been interpreted variously by every sect and group. We say that God makes a profound point here: in discussing outward charity and giving, He alludes to inward charity. Outward charity is material wealth that the rich bestow upon the poor—a small or large portion of their substance. Two kinds of giving exist: material and physical, which benefit the body and protect from worldly suffering. The second is intellectual and spiritual, which benefits the soul and prepares it for the Hereafter, safeguarding eternal life from all calamity.

The true possessor and bestower of this second wealth is the Messenger of God, who established the Shariah and revealed the path of salvation. The Imāms descended from him are the owners and worthy givers of this treasure. Those rebuked and threatened in this verse are those who seized the Prophet's caliphate without divine authority. The Messenger appointed no successor among them; yet they forcefully usurped the rightful heirs' rights and climbed the Prophet's pulpit, then conquered lands in Islam's name without necessity, earning titles like "the Great Conqueror" (Fatḥ-e-Aʿẓam)—meaning ʿUmar. To maintain their façade, they discussed prayer and fasting—minor portions of Shariah, as charity is a minor portion of wealth. Thus God forbade them to invite others to Muḥammad's Shariah: on one hand, they harmed the Prophet's children, usurping and even murdering them; on the other, they displayed their Islamic service. They were hypocrites seeking fame, disobeying God's command. Their charity is unacceptable to God; since they act without His permission, all their labour is fruitless and sinful, earning them punishment.

### The Stone with Dust

Their likeness is that of a stone with some dust upon it. Because of their hardness of heart and obstinacy, God likened their hearts to stones. Upon stone, wisdom's word casts no light by which wisdom might cultivate and flourish. Just as thousands of buckets or long rains fail to produce growth on bare stone, so too no wisdom takes root in such hearts.

Then He says: "There is dust upon this stone." When soil lies on stone and water falls, vegetation may grow through soil's cooperation with water. But soil on a bare stone is washed away by rain, leaving no cultivation or production. Clay signifies inward faith—the cultivation of the Hereafter—just as apparent soil manifests forms of this world. Thus clay on stone means faith rooted in deceit: they profess belief with the tongue while concealing disbelief in the heart. Inevitably, the waters of the Prophet's knowledge that flow upon such a person are wasted. The dust is carried away; the stone beneath is exposed. False faith vanishes; hidden enmity emerges.

Therefore God says:

> ⟪quran 2:264⟫
> ⟪ar:‏فَأَصَابَهُۥ وَابِلٌۭ فَتَرَكَهُۥ صَلْدًۭا ۖ لَّا يَقْدِرُونَ عَلَىٰ شَىْءٍۢ مِّمَّا كَسَبُوا۟ ۗ وَٱللَّهُ لَا يَهْدِى ٱلْقَوْمَ ٱلْكَفِرِينَ ‎⟫

When heavy rain falls upon this stone, it leaves it barren and hard—these sciences do not touch them. They cannot profit from their own deeds. God does not guide the disbelieving people, for they reject the Prophet's and Imāms' knowledge, which is the source of guidance, and thus perpetually go astray.

In the following verse, God says:

> ⟪quran 2:265⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَمَثَلُ ٱلَّذِينَ يُنفِقُونَ أَمْوَلَهُمُ ٱبْتِغَآءَ مَرْضَاتِ ٱللَّهِ وَتَثْبِيتًۭا مِّنْ أَنفُسِهِمْ كَمَثَلِ جَنَّةٍۭ بِرَبْوَةٍ أَصَابَهَا وَابِلٌۭ فَـَٔاتَتْ أُكُلَهَا ضِعْفَيْنِ فَإِن لَّمْ يُصِبْهَا وَابِلٌۭ فَطَلٌّۭ ۗ وَٱللَّهُ بِمَا تَعْمَلُونَ بَصِيرٌ ‎⟫

### The Garden on a High Ground

The example of those who expend their wealth seeking God's pleasure and strengthening their souls is like a garden on an elevated hill, verdant and flourishing with streams and fruits. Every scholar and expert in religion is a paradise within which dwell the fruits of knowledge from which believers' souls derive comfort and pleasure, and minds find peace and joy—as the human body finds comfort in outward gardens. Therefore, such a scholar of faith occupies a rabwah (high place)—elevated and exalted.[^cite-14]

*Rabwah* means the scholar stands closer to those above him in receiving material and support, and through his elevation, he bestows blessings upon those below him.

When this garden receives heavy rain—when such a scholar receives knowledge from the Alawite (the Imām descended from ʿAlī)—it produces twice its yield. Through such support, he establishes harmony between the external and esoteric, between reason and revelation, making both this world and the Hereafter serve God's purpose.

If this garden receives no heavy rain—if the scholar does not attain the Imām's elevated knowledge—then dew suffices. That is, if such a scholar cannot ascend to the heights to which revelation reaches, then at least his power of thought and imagination provides sufficient substance to cultivate something for the Hereafter's growth and nourish his followers with eternal and timeless life. God is watching your deeds.

This interpretation is found in the *Majālis al-Muʾayyadiyya* (the Sessions of al-Muʾayyad), recorded by Sayyidunā al-Muʾayyad al-Shīrāzī.[^cite-15]

## None Know Its Interpretation Save Allah and Those Firmly Established in Knowledge

When believers are questioned—"Do you claim that the Qur'an possesses meanings beyond its apparent words, meanings grounded in Arabic lexicography yet unknown to ordinary people, knowable only to yourselves?"—the response is clear and instructive. It is not the Ismaili exegetes alone who affirm that the Qur'an (al-Qur'ān) contains layers of taʾwīl (esoteric exegesis), meanings transcending literal translation. Rather, even the Ahl al-Raʾī (the Rationalist theologians) and Ahl al-Ḥadīth (the Traditionalist scholars) find themselves compelled by reason to embrace interpretive methods when the apparent sense of certain verses proves untenable. They deny taʾwīl in principle, yet are forced to practice it when literal meaning undermines core doctrines. Consider three verses whose surface readings contradict the principle of tawḥīd (divine unity):

> — *Image text (from image 061; citation unverified)*
  (image preserved at .. /images/061.png)

| Vijay Rabak | Eli Rabha Nazarah | Zahab al-Rabi |
| --- | --- | --- |
| The Coming of the Lord | Looking to the Lord | Going to the Lord |

The apparent meaning of these three expressions—*coming, looking, going*—implies embodiment and limitation of the Divine, ascribing to Allah attributes proper only to bodies. Yet when confronted with this grave theological flaw (a corruption of tawḥīd itself), even the traditionalists retreat into interpretive readings. Why, then, do they resist taʾwīl when the Qur'ān itself announces:

> ⟪quran 3:7⟫
> ⟪ar:‏هُوَ ٱلَّذِىٓ أَنزَلَ عَلَيْكَ ٱلْكِتَبَ مِنْهُ آيَتٌۭ مُّحْكَمَتٌ هُنَّ أُمُّ ٱلْكِتَبِ وَأُخَرُ مُتَشَبِهَتٌۭ ۖ فَأَمَّا ٱلَّذِينَ فِى قُلُوبِهِمْ زَيْغٌۭ فَيَتَّبِعُونَ مَا تَشَبَهَ مِنْهُ ٱبْتِغَآءَ ٱلْفِتْنَةِ وَٱبْتِغَآءَ تَأْوِيلِهِۦ ۗ وَمَا يَعْلَمُ تَأْوِيلَهُۥٓ إِلَّا ٱللَّهُ وَٱلرَّسِخُونَ فِى ٱلْعِلْمِ ۗ يَقُولُونَ آمَنَّا بِهِۦ كُلٌّۭ مِّنْ عِندِ رَبِّنَا ۗ وَمَا يَذَّكَّرُ إِلَّآ أُو۟لُوا۟ ٱلْأَلْبَبِ ‎⟫

The Qur'ān itself contains the word taʾwīl (ta'wīl). Yet opponents now dispute the verse's punctuation. They claim a mandatory pause (waqf) after "None knows its interpretation save Allah," asserting that the second part—"and those firmly established in knowledge say: We believe in it"—denotes only their faith *without* their comprehension of the deeper meaning. By this reading, the Rasikhūn fī al-ʿIlm (those firmly grounded in knowledge) confess belief in interpretations they themselves do not grasp; only Allah knows the taʾwīl. Yet this reasoning dissolves under scrutiny.

To affirm (āmana) means to confirm, and confirmation requires understanding and truthfulness on the part of the affirmer.[^cite-16] A person cannot genuinely confirm what he does not comprehend; such "confirmation" is merely assent to sound. If the Rasikhūn were to believe in the Divine interpretation while remaining ignorant of it, they would not be confirming truth—merely uttering "yes" to "yes." But Allah Himself declares:

> ⟪quran 3:18⟫
> ⟪ar:‏شَهِدَ ٱللَّهُ أَنَّهُۥ لَآ إِلَهَ إِلَّا هُوَ وَٱلْمَلَٓئِكَةُ وَأُو۟لُوا۟ ٱلْعِلْمِ قَآئِمًۢا بِٱلْقِسْطِ ۚ لَآ إِلَهَ إِلَّا هُوَ ٱلْعَزِيزُ ٱلْحَكِيمُ ‎⟫

Those endowed with knowledge witness to truth; they stand upon justice. Ignorance is no virtue; it is a grave deficiency. To impute such ignorance to the Prophets and Imams would be to ascribe the lowest of conditions to those whom Allah has placed nearest to Himself.

### Did the Messenger Possess Knowledge of the Qur'ān?

Consider the second line of argument. Did the Messenger of Allah know the Qur'ān or not? Should one claim the Prophet remained ignorant of the meaning of revealed verses, this would constitute the gravest reproach—ignorance of his own revelation. When a believer approached the Messenger asking, "O Prophet of Allah, what is the meaning of such a verse? What is the wisdom behind this command? What is the reason for this prohibition?"—what purpose would his reply "I do not know" serve? Would it demonstrate his excellence or his lack? Surely ignorance is no attribute of excellence. 

Conversely, if the Messenger possessed knowledge of the taʾwīl of the Qur'ān, then to forbid others access to this knowledge becomes inexplicable. Those who inherit from the Prophet his knowledge of the Qur'ān participate in the Prophet's own gnosis. And indeed, the Messenger of Allah stands supreme in knowledge among all creatures, exceeding the learned in rank and wisdom. Allah elevates them to a station comparable to the angels:

> ⟪quran 43:86⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَلَا يَمْلِكُ ٱلَّذِينَ يَدْعُونَ مِن دُونِهِ ٱلشَّفَعَةَ إِلَّا مَن شَهِدَ بِٱلْحَقِّ وَهُمْ يَعْلَمُونَ ‎⟫

Not every scholar can be placed among the *ʿulamāʾ ʿalā ḥaqq* (the knowers of truth), yet every such knower is indeed a scholar. Their station is exalted beyond measure.[^cite-17]

We shall show them Our signs in the horizons and within themselves until it becomes clear to them that this is the truth. The Messenger is reported to have said: "Allah has established His religion upon the pattern of His creation, that He might be judged by creation and His religion made one."[^cite-18] The Qur'ān addresses two domains—the universe (al-āfāq) and the soul (al-anfus); the Ḥadīth likewise names creation and religion. Both revelation and prophetic tradition affirm that the principles of religion—the path to eternal felicity—derive from the principles of creation itself.

The things of this world, whether animate or inanimate, possess no motion save through an inner stimulus (rūḥ, spirit). Within all creation lie intrinsic meanings, invisible yet intelligible to the discerning. Creation of the heavens and earth mirrors the establishment of religion; both unfold through internal principle and external manifestation.[^cite-19]

## The First House: Ka'ba and Its Esoteric Significance

Allah declares in the Qur'ān:

> ⟪quran 3:96⟫
> ⟪ar:‏إِنَّ أَوَّلَ بَيْتٍۢ وُضِعَ لِلنَّاسِ لَلَّذِى بِبَكَّةَ مُبَارَكًۭا وَهُدًۭى لِّلْعَلَمِينَ ‎⟫

Verily, the first House appointed for mankind was that at Bakkah (Mecca), blessed and a guidance unto the worlds. The Qur'ān is fundamentally a text of *verses* (āyāt)—some manifest, others concealed in their meanings. The verses beginning with the particle "an" are muḥkamāt (established, clear texts), permitting no ambiguity or deviation. These are what Allah has decreed.

In this verse, Allah affirms: "Blessed is this House for the worlds, and a guidance for them." The plural "worlds" (al-ʿālamīn) indicates not one but many realms of existence. As the Fātiḥa (opening chapter) proclaims: "Praise be to Allah, Lord of the worlds"; and the Prophet is described as "a mercy to the worlds." The Ka'ba is blessed for all these realms, overflowing with guidance.

The Ka'ba stands in Mecca (Makka), yet the verse specifies Bakkah (Bakka). Amir al-Muʾminīn ʿAlī is reported to have said: "I am the dot beneath the letter Bā." Whoever recognizes this dot recognizes its significance; the perspective of one who grasps this point shifts from the outer city to its heart. Mecca denotes the city; Bakkah denotes its essence. In a deeper reading: Mecca is Muhammad; Bakkah is ʿAlī—the very point upon which the House is erected.[^cite-21]

### The Argument of Necessity

For anything to command belief and affirmation, one must first establish the prerequisite condition—namely, *walāya* (sacred guardianship). When a claimant makes a claim, he must present proof. Should Allah be asked, "Why do you call yourself Creator?" the reply would be: "Because I have created all these worlds, and thus I am the Creator." So too, Allah sent one hundred and twenty-four thousand prophets to this world, and all visited this single first House. Had Allah erected multiple sacred houses in different times and places, some prophets would have journeyed to these varied sanctuaries. History testifies, however, that all the prophets made pilgrimage to the same Ka'ba—a sign confirming its primacy.[^cite-22]

### Nobility of Station

This is the place where a single unit of prayer (rakʿa) is rewarded as one hundred thousand units. Prayer not directed toward this qibla (direction of prayer) is not accepted. Seventy thousand angels descend at this place each day and never ascend again.

From the first moment of creation, Allah decreed that the Ka'ba shall not depart from its place; rather, the entire world moves toward it. Though ages pass and the earth becomes water and water becomes earth, the Ka'ba remains unmoved. Similarly, ʿAlī's station is immutable; all believers must turn toward him, advancing in mystical understanding and devotion.[^cite-23]

### Builder of Mosques

All mosques derive from this House, which became the focal point of every place of worship. It is narrated that during Noah's Flood, this House of Allah was raised to the fourth heaven. Subsequently, Ibrāhīm (Abraham) rebuilt it at Allah's command. The lime remaining from its construction was, by divine decree, gathered by Ibrāhīm through Gabriel's guidance, raised to heaven, and scattered across the world. The day of gathering was Friday; the day of scattering, Saturday. Where this blessed soil fell, mosques were built—on earth, stone, and lime.

*Fact:* The Anwār (Lights) are gathered on Friday and dispersed on Saturday. The Prophet gathered all Anwār in ʿAlī; ʿAlī broadcast them to all the Imāms.[^cite-24]

### The Qibla's Transformation

This Ka'ba became the qibla of Ādam (Adam); after him, qibla for one hundred twenty-four thousand prophets. Finally, when the spiritual rank of all saints of this world reached its zenith, the time arrived to declare this House the birthplace of Amīr al-Muʾminīn, Ḥaydar al-Karrār, ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib. Whoever is born of this place bears the name ʿAlī; none else shall be born there save ʿAlī alone. Therefore Allah declares (in the esoteric sense): "Dawwa" in Arabic signifies placing something in its proper position with justice; misplacing it is called "oppression."

### House of Rest

In this verse, *nās* (people) does not denote humanity at large, but those intimate with the daʿwa (the summons to the Imāmate), those who hold fast to the mantle of Mawlānā ʿAlī and truly acknowledge his walāya (guardianship), understanding his reality. Thus the verse declares: ʿAlī was made a house for the adherents of ʿAlī. A house is where one dwells and finds repose. The word *bayt* (house) appears twenty-eight times in the Qur'ān. Bayt signifies the home—essentially, the Imām's very essence—wherein the souls of all believers find eternal rest.[^cite-25]

*Gematria note:* The numerical value of "Bakkah" is twenty-seven (27); the one who possesses it shall be the twenty-eighth.

The word "blessing" in this verse refers to the chain of Imāms descending from ʿAlī and Fāṭima until the Day of Judgment.[^cite-26]

### The Ka'ba and ʿAlī

Every utterance from the Prophet's tongue not constituting revelation is termed *ḥadīth* (tradition). Two types exist: the *hadīth sharīf* (noble tradition)—pleasing to people alone—and the *hadīth qudsī* (sacred tradition)—pleasing to both Allah and humanity.[^cite-27]

*Fact:* May Allah decree that all such traditions be compiled in the Qur'ān with perfect justice, taking their rightful place therein.

There exists a *hadīth qudsī* in which the Prophet declared: "The example of ʿAlī is the example of the Ka'ba." Whatever is thought of the Ka'ba shall be thought of ʿAlī; whatever is thought of ʿAlī shall be thought regarding the Ka'ba.[^cite-28]

If the Ka'ba is preserved in the Book, so too is ʿAlī. If the Ka'ba is appointed for the common believers, so too is ʿAlī for the sincere. If the Ka'ba is blessed and a source of guidance, ʿAlī likewise is blessed and the fountain of guidance. As the Ka'ba possesses the Mustajar and Multazim (corners of suppliant prayer), ʿAlī contains exoteric and esoteric dimensions. As the Mizāb al-Raḥma (Gutter of Mercy) crowns the Ka'ba's height, so too does Mawlātunā Fāṭima serve as his supreme proof. As the Black Stone in the north receives the pledge of all believers, so does ʿAlī possess a succession of Imāms whose covenant suffices for us. The Ka'ba has four sanctified corners—the Syrian, the Iraqi, the Yemeni, and the As'ad; ʿAlī likewise has four sacred precincts.

He whose prayer is directed toward the Ka'ba finds his outward prayer accepted; he who plants ʿAlī in his heart finds his inner prayer accepted.

## Creation of the Heavens and the Earth: Light and Darkness

Allah declares:

> ⟪quran 6:1⟫
> ⟪ar:‏ٱلْحَمْدُ لِلَّهِ ٱلَّذِى خَلَقَ ٱلسَّمَوتِ وَٱلْأَرْضَ وَجَعَلَ ٱلظُّلُمَتِ وَٱلنُّورَ ۖ ثُمَّ ٱلَّذِينَ كَفَرُوا۟ بِرَبِّهِمْ يَعْدِلُونَ ‎⟫

Praise be to Allah, Who created the heavens and the earth and appointed the darkness and the light. Why does the verse use the plural for heavens and darkness, yet employ the singular for light? Mawlānā Ghulām Ḥusayn used to expound that the heavens, earth, darkness, and light do not refer to the merely natural world, but to realities of the inner cosmos—the esoteric knowledge possessed by those of refined inner perception.

These heavens and this earth were created before the Prophet and shall endure until the Last Day. One objection raised by literalist interpreters is troubling: this verse appears to command the creation of darkness, yet reason teaches that darkness is not a substance in itself but merely the absence of light. When the Qur'ān is abandoned, even apparent knowledge slips from the hands of the apparent worshippers, leaving them confused in ignorance, unable to grasp these matters.[^cite-30]

### Inner and Outer Invitation

This verse encompasses both the outer invitation (al-daʿwa al-zāhira) and the inner invitation (al-daʿwa al-bāṭina)—that is, the summons to the exoteric believers (al-mustawa'sīn) and to the esoteric believers (al-mustawdaʿīn, the trustees). The heavens signify the feast of the established believers; the earth, the nourishment of the inner believers. Darkness represents the outer summons; light, the inner summons. The epistemic state of this invitation mirrors light—singular in essence though manifesting infinitely—while outward commandments (numerous though they be: purity, prayer, fasting, and more) yield but one inner meaning. Therefore light appears singular while darkness, representing outward multiplicity and confusion, stands in plural form.

The outward commands derive from Allah's law; hence to preserve the creation of darkness—the outward call—one rightly ascribes it to Allah. Every servant of the summons is called a Lord (Rabb), yet by "Lord" we mean those called to the inner world—the Imāms—sent forth by Allah Himself.[^cite-31]

| Established Invitation | Repository of Invitation | Inner Summons | Outer Summons |

### The Blessing of Intellect

Allah has bestowed upon humanity the blessing of intellect (al-ʿaql al-ʿamm), rendering them worthy to be khalīfas (stewards) of Allah upon the earth. Gratitude for this supreme blessing is scarcely adequate. Yet should one, despite possessing intellect, neglect the training of the soul (tazkiyat al-nafs), he becomes a vessel of disbelief. Should he fail to cultivate these noble virtues, he ruins his own existence. A person's outward conduct may appear virtuous, but without training according to the summons—without the walāya of the Prophet and the love of ʿAlī in the heart—he remains deficient. Even the prayer of a worshipper lacking this inner attachment is rendered useless.

## Those Who Deny and Turn Away in Pride

Allah declares in Sūrat al-Aʿrāf:

> ⟪quran 7:40⟫
> ⟪ar:‏إِنَّ ٱلَّذِينَ كَذَّبُوا۟ بِـَٔايَتِنَا وَٱسْتَكْبَرُوا۟ عَنْهَا لَا تُفَتَّحُ لَهُمْ أَبْوَبُ ٱلسَّمَآءِ وَلَا يَدْخُلُونَ ٱلْجَنَّةَ حَتَّىٰ يَلِجَ ٱلْجَمَلُ فِى سَمِّ ٱلْخِيَاطِ ۚ وَكَذَلِكَ نَجْزِى ٱلْمُجْرِمِينَ ‎⟫

Verily, those who deny Our signs and turn away in arrogance, the gates of heaven shall not be opened to them, nor shall they enter Paradise until the camel passes through the eye of the needle. Thus We recompense the guilty.

### The Verses: Qur'ānic and Imamate

The verses here denote outwardly the verses of the Qur'ān, which the polytheists reject; inwardly, they signify the infallible Imāms (al-aʾimma al-maʿṣūmūn), who expound the verses of the Qur'ān and illuminate the earth with their light, just as fire and radiance emanate from a shining stone. These Imāms of the Qur'ān stand in the position of the soul to a corpse. When they are removed from their station, the path of guidance is destroyed.[^cite-33]

In lexicon, "verse" (āya) signifies knowledge—a flag or sign. The infallible Imāms are signs and proofs (āyāt) of Allah, guides toward eternal survival and immortality. Referring to this inner verse, Allah declares:

> ⟪quran 23:50⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَجَعَلْنَا ٱبْنَ مَرْيَمَ وَأُمَّهُۥٓ آيَةًۭ وَآوَيْنَاهُمَآ إِلَىٰ رَبْوَةٍۢ ذَاتِ قَرَارٍۢ وَمَعِينٍۢ ‎⟫

We made the son of Mary and his mother a sign; and We gave them refuge on a mound where there was rest and flowing water.[^cite-34] ʿAmīr al-Muʾminīn, expressing his excellence, declared: "I am a clear sign!" He employed the plural form because all Imāms who are signs (āyāt) descend from him. "You are their comprehensive beginning and their first."

### Arrogance Against the Signs

The powerful evidence that these verses denote the Imāms rather than the apparent words of the Qur'ān lies in the verse's phrase: they were arrogant *about* these verses. One may deny or refute the apparent words of the Qur'ān, yet words composed of letters possess no consciousness—they know nothing of arrogance. Arrogance is directed toward persons of greatness and power. Words and phrases are lifeless; what sense could arrogance bear against the inanimate? The verses must therefore refer to living, conscious beings—the Imāms.[^cite-35]

### The Gates of Heaven

The verse continues, describing punishment for those who spurned the signs:

The gates of heaven will not be opened to them. The sky is the seat of radiant stars from which divine sustenance descends to creatures in the form of rain and water. Allah elevated the heavens above earthly bodies, casting their shadow upon them. Corporeal bodies cannot reach and touch the heavens; but the human soul, being subtle in nature, easily attains these heavens, gaining knowledge and complete mastery over them. Thus the outer heavens dominate human bodies, while the human soul dominates the outer heavens. Yet Allah created inner heavens that correspond to and transcend the outer ones, dominating the human soul as the outer heavens dominate the body. These inner heavens are the saints of Allah (awliyāʾ Allāh)—majestic, noble, and miraculous beings whose states affect the soul for good or ill. Their gates are opened to believers; veils are lifted; lights are kindled; and rain is poured forth. This clarifies that the warning refers not to the outward heavens but to the inner realities. Therefore, Allah declares:

### Entry into Paradise

Those who turn away in arrogance shall not enter Paradise until the camel passes through the eye of the needle. Consider first the word *Jannah* (Paradise), then the camel, then the needle. *Jannat* derives from *Jinn* (to conceal, hide); all its cognates bear hidden meanings—the fetus (a child hidden in the womb), the shield (concealment behind protection), the jinn (beings invisible to sight). Similarly, paradise denotes a garden whose tall, dense, fruited shrubs block the sun's heat.

Two paradises exist: the potential paradise and the actual paradise. We may illustrate: sperm placed in the womb becomes human; when this sperm develops in the womb and is born by Allah's will through the beneficial effects of gestation—protected from harm—it becomes a human being. Similarly, the invitation of the Family of Muḥammad is a potential paradise leading to the actual paradise. Allah describes Paradise's breadth as equal to the heavens and earth.[^cite-36]

None who have not been established in the invitation of the Imāms possess any reality in Allah's reward-house. Just as existence in this world requires emerging from the father's loins into the mother's womb and developing there, so too: one who believes in the outer invitation of the Prophet must enter the inner invitation of ʿAlī, die while attached to his walāya and the walāya of his descendants, to be reborn in the Hereafter and enter Paradise.[^cite-37]

### The Camel Passing Through the Needle's Eye

The verse concludes:

Until the camel passes through the needle.

The camel is an animal praised in certain contexts and condemned in others. Though the Prophet said: "One should not pray when a camel stands before one, for every camel harbors a devil"—this hadīth, understood through wisdom, reveals a profound truth.[^cite-38]

The camel is a heavy-bodied creature bearing immense burden. In one sense, it represents a person appearing exalted above others through outward observance of the law, display of piety, and hypocrisy—walking with a stiffened neck, bearing marks of prostration upon the forehead, such that people suppose him a great bearer of the Sharīʿa's burden. Just as the camel is elevated among beasts by its height, this person holds prominence among ordinary folk. Yet in truth, he is the enemy and rival of the Prophet, deceiving people into believing him the Prophet's successor. The arrogant is thus likened to the camel. Allah says to him: he shall not enter Paradise until he passes through the needle's eye.

### The Tailor and the Needle

A tailor outwardly is one who tears cloth, cuts it, shapes it into a garment, and adorns the wearer's body. Esoterically, he prepares the garment of true piety—the authentic tawḥīd (divine unity) that is the garment of the self. He clothes the believer in this garment of unity so that he enters Paradise adorned. Allah declares:

> ⟪quran 7:27⟫
> ⟪ar:‏يَبَنِىٓ آدَمَ لَا يَفْتِنَنَّكُمُ ٱلشَّيْطَنُ كَمَآ أَخْرَجَ أَبَوَيْكُم مِّنَ ٱلْجَنَّةِ يَنزِعُ عَنْهُمَا لِبَاسَهُمَا لِيُرِيَهُمَا سَوْءَتِهِمَآ ۗ إِنَّهُۥ يَرَىٰكُمْ هُوَ وَقَبِيلُهُۥ مِنْ حَيْثُ لَا تَرَوْنَهُمْ ۗ إِنَّا جَعَلْنَا ٱلشَّيَطِينَ أَوْلِيَآءَ لِلَّذِينَ لَا يُؤْمِنُونَ ‎⟫

O children of Adam, let not Satan seduce you as he stripped your parents of their garments to show their nakedness.[^cite-39] The walī Allāh (guardian of Allah) is he who prepares the garment of piety, making its wearer a miracle in Allah's sight. Therefore:

> ⟪quran 49:13⟫
> ⟪ar:‏يَٓأَيُّهَا ٱلنَّاسُ إِنَّا خَلَقْنَكُم مِّن ذَكَرٍۢ وَأُنثَىٰ وَجَعَلْنَكُمْ شُعُوبًۭا وَقَبَآئِلَ لِتَعَارَفُوٓا۟ ۚ إِنَّ أَكْرَمَكُمْ عِندَ ٱللَّهِ أَتْقَىٰكُمْ ۚ إِنَّ ٱللَّهَ عَلِيمٌ خَبِيرٌۭ ‎⟫

O mankind, We created you from a male and a female and made you nations and tribes that you might know one another. Surely the most honored of you in Allah's sight is the most godfearing.[^cite-40]

A tailor's needle is the instrument through which he sews—extremely fine, its eye so narrow that only the thinnest thread may pass. Similarly, the subtle sciences of the walī's tawḥīd can enter only the mind of one who has become lean through humility and lowliness, like the camel reduced to nothing before the walī Allāh. The arrogant, with their gross understanding, cannot comprehend these matters. Only when they sit humbly—no matter how despised they deem the walī—can they grasp these sciences and don the garment of piety, the dress of Paradise, and enter therein.[^cite-41]

### The King and the Preacher of Truth

It is narrated that a mighty king in the East asked a preacher for the meaning of this verse. The preacher replied: "I shall explain it, provided you descend from your throne and kingdom, sit in my place, and permit me to sit in yours. Then accept a covenant with me as a slave accepts instruction from a master."

The king acquiesced. He descended and seated himself before the preacher with respect. They exchanged covenant. The preacher said: "Observe! You are the camel by virtue of your power and high station. I am the tailor, and this covenant is the needle's eye through which you have passed by accepting your humiliation. This is the meaning of the verse!"

## They Look Upon You, Yet They Perceive Not

This verse ranks among the most eloquent of the Qur'ān. Allah declares:

> ⟪quran 7:198⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَإِن تَدْعُوهُمْ إِلَى ٱلْهُدَىٰ لَا يَسْمَعُوا۟ ۖ وَتَرَىٰهُمْ يَنظُرُونَ إِلَيْكَ وَهُمْ لَا يُبْصِرُونَ ‎⟫

And if you call them to guidance, they hear not; and you see them looking at you, yet they perceive not.

The distinction lies in three verbs used in this verse—all relating to sight, yet each bearing a distinct esoteric meaning:[^cite-43]

| Perception | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Tara | Seeing with the eye of direct knowledge and divine illumination |
| Yanzur | Looking with the physical eye |
| Basr | Perceiving with the eye of reason and inner understanding |

Allah says to the Messenger: "You see, through your light and support, that all these people look upon you with their outward gaze, yet they lack the insight to comprehend your reality." The apparent and the inner worlds remain for them forever separated.

## The Heavens and the Earth Were One Joined Mass

Allah declares in Sūrat al-Anbiyāʾ:

> ⟪quran 21:30⟫
> ⟪ar:‏أَوَلَمْ يَرَ ٱلَّذِينَ كَفَرُوٓا۟ أَنَّ ٱلسَّمَوتِ وَٱلْأَرْضَ كَانَتَا رَتْقًۭا فَفَتَقْنَهُمَا ۖ وَجَعَلْنَا مِنَ ٱلْمَآءِ كُلَّ شَىْءٍ حَىٍّ ۖ أَفَلَا يُؤْمِنُونَ ‎⟫

Have not the disbelievers seen that the heavens and the earth were one joined mass, then We parted them asunder? And We created every living thing of water. Will they not believe?

### The Apparent Contradiction

A crucial question arises: When did the disbelievers exist at the moment of creation of the heavens and earth to witness their joining and separation? Humans had not yet been created, so how could there be believers or disbelievers to witness this event?

When the common scholars could not furnish a proper interpretation, they resorted to saying: "These are matters of Allah's dominion; we need not employ intellect in them. Accept them without thought. It suffices to argue that the Messenger of Allah came with truth and never spoke falsehood. Thus, if the Messenger said this, it must be true." Yet this response abandons the very faculty Allah granted humanity for understanding His revelation.[^cite-44]

The root of these defects lies in this: after the Messenger, governance of the Sharīʿa fell to those ignorant of the Sharīʿa's reality. They declared reason and religion enemies, forbade the study of rational sciences, and condemned as unbelievers those who questioned the true nature and reality of religion. Had people instead resorted to the heirs of the Prophet's knowledge—those who uphold the Book and the Balance of Justice—Allah would surely have illumined their beliefs, resolved all difficulties, and answered even the most complex questions with ease.[^cite-45]

### The Esoteric Interpretation

> ⟪quran 16:40⟫
> ⟪ar:‏إِنَّمَا قَوْلُنَا لِشَىْءٍ إِذَآ أَرَدْنَهُ أَن نَّقُولَ لَهُۥ كُن فَيَكُونُ ‎⟫

Verily, Our Word unto a thing when We intend it is only that We say unto it: "Be," and it is.[^cite-46]

The true meaning is this: both the heavens and the earth were implicit and hidden in the Word of Allah. Then We manifested them into deed. When Allah wills to bring something into existence or manifestation, it springs forth—from non-existence it becomes actual, from potentiality it becomes real. In its state of potentiality, it is *ratq* (joined, undifferentiated, without form); when made verbal (actual), it is *faqq* (separated, formed, distinct). This separation and forming are inherent in the divine utterance itself. What is the essence by which non-existence becomes existence? What agency transforms from one state to another but the divine Command?[^cite-47]

### Religious Heaven and Earth

There exists also a religious heaven and earth parallel to the natural heavens and earth—themselves unknown to the unaware. The inner heaven is the essence of the Prophet (peace be upon him); the inner earth is his testator (waṣī). The outer heavens send down water; the outer earth absorbs it and brings forth vegetation. In like manner, the Prophet imparts knowledge to his testator, who preserves it in his heart and thereby nourishes the souls of believers. Just as the outer heavens and earth produce only flesh, blood, and bone—all destined for decay—the inner heavens and earth grant eternal life and immortal survival to souls. Their ranks far transcend the natural heavens and earth.[^cite-48]

In another reading: the disbelievers and polytheists witnessed the Prophet standing silent, apparently devoid of divine support, before his prophethood was established. He spoke not, remaining in a state of *ratq* (sealed, without manifestation). But when We bestowed upon him Our revelation and support, he began the call to Allah, ascending to the degree of prophecy. From this came forth the emergence of the successor (the testator) and the Imāms, who are like the earth. This is the separation of heaven and earth.[^cite-49]

When Allah's revelation descended and established the Prophet in prophethood, a like power dwelt with the testator in potentiality. The Prophet then actualized this power through his knowledge, establishing the testator in his station. The Prophet said: "I and ʿAlī are from one light."[^cite-50] Thus ʿAlī's light was within Muḥammad's light; the heavens and earth were joined. When the Prophet proclaimed on the day of Ghadīr: "Of whomsoever I am the *mawlā* (master), ʿAlī is his *mawlā*"—and appointed ʿAlī his successor—ʿAlī's light was manifested, and heaven separated from earth.[^cite-50]

Thereafter the heavens showered upon believers spiritual and intellectual rain from Allah's Word, causing the earth to flourish and become a source of life for the human soul. The Prophets were first to silence themselves in potentiality; upon receiving revelation, they became active, performing their functions. Abu Lahab, Abu Jahl, Abu Sufyan, Muʿāwiya, Ibn Khattab, and Abu Bakr—all witnessed these events with their own eyes. Allah says: "When We established ʿAlī as the successor and separated the testator's station (the earth) from the guardian's station (the heavens), did not these disbelievers perceive?"[^cite-51]

## "There is no god except Him; all things will perish except His Face"

Everything whose existence is eternal and established throughout all time is itself God, Most High. Whatever is subject to corruption and capable of perishing cannot be Allah. The Qur'an affirms this principle when it states:

> ⟪quran 28:88⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَلَا تَدْعُ مَعَ ٱللَّهِ إِلَهًا آخَرَ ۘ لَآ إِلَهَ إِلَّا هُوَ ۚ كُلُّ شَىْءٍ هَالِكٌ إِلَّا وَجْهَهُۥ ۚ لَهُ ٱلْحُكْمُ وَإِلَيْهِ تُرْجَعُونَ ‎⟫

What does "the face of God" (wajh) signify in this verse? The face of an object denotes that aspect by which it becomes known and recognized—the means through which its true reality is disclosed and apprehended. The religion that is imperishable, that endures eternally unchanging throughout all ages, is the Din al-Mateen (the firm religion) through which humanity has attained knowledge of divine unity (tawḥīd, the affirmation of God's oneness). This same religion has been present in all eras and conveyed by all the prophets in succession, until the final messenger brought it in the form of Islam. Because this religion is perfect and immutable, it shall never perish but shall remain from everlasting to everlasting.[^cite-49]

The prophets of Allah—from Adam through the end of time—have all invited humanity to this singular religion. A profound mystery lies concealed within the seven letters of *Lā ilāha illā al-Muhammad* (the Islamic testimony of faith), each corresponding to one of the prophets:

| 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 | 5 | 6 | 7 |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| A | L | A | Q | L | A | M |
| Adam | Noah | Abraham | Moses | Jesus | Muhammad | The Eternal One |

Islam itself constitutes the cause (sabab) through which Allah became known to humanity. Yet inasmuch as the knowledge and understanding of Islam were transmitted to the Ummah (the community of believers) through the Messenger of Allah—peace and blessings upon him—the Messenger himself was the vehicle of divine knowledge. By this principle, the Messenger's successor (khalīfah, the caliph) and rightful representative, the true heir to the Message, is likewise called the cause of Allah (sabab Allāh), for he stands as the guide bringing truth and knowledge, inviting people to the recognition of divine unity.

Just as the face of an object grants us knowledge of its essential reality, so too do the saints of Allah (awliyāʾ Allāh) grant us true knowledge of divine unity (tawḥīd). In this capacity, they deserve to be called the causes of Allah.[^cite-50]

There exists no path to comprehending the true essence of the Creator, nor is such direct comprehension possible—for the Creator is necessary existence, whilst man is contingent. No connection exists between these two orders. Yet God, in His mercy, bestowed upon man, the noblest of creatures, the blessing of knowing His face, that is, the perfect religion and the knowledge of the Blessed One (al-Mubayyinah). Through this knowledge, man became blessed with proximity to the saints, the prophets, the angels, the divine throne, the divine footstool, Paradise, and all the spiritual dimensions and mysteries of God.

It is through this veil and these divine secrets that certain attributes of Allah come to be known. This is why the Messenger of Allah—may peace and blessings be upon him—said: "Whoever comes to know his own soul (nafs) comes to know his Lord."[^cite-51] Since the soul itself is a spiritual being, one who does not comprehend the creature cannot comprehend the Creator. The creature is accessible to human perception and thus easier to understand.

It has been demonstrated that religion and its teachings constitute the cause of Allah (sabab Allāh)—imperishable, incorruptible, and free from all deterioration. Only the person bound to this eternal remainder shall be merged into it and shall endure forever. In reference to this eternal permanence, Allah declares:

> ⟪quran 4:69⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَمَن يُطِعِ ٱللَّهَ وَٱلرَّسُولَ فَأُو۟لَٓئِكَ مَعَ ٱلَّذِينَ أَنْعَمَ ٱللَّهُ عَلَيْهِم مِّنَ ٱلنَّبِيِّۦنَ وَٱلصِّدِّيقِينَ وَٱلشُّهَدَآءِ وَٱلصَّلِحِينَ ۚ وَحَسُنَ أُو۟لَٓئِكَ رَفِيقًۭا ‎⟫

A righteous person is one who is free from all corruption and wrongdoing. In the exegesis of this verse, the Prophet, the truthful one (al-Siddīq), the martyr, and the righteous are all mentioned as companions in the path of divine truth.

### Scientific and Practical Devotion

The firm religion consists of two acts of devotion: scientific worship and practical worship. The latter is fulfilled through the actions prescribed by the divine law (Sharīʿah). Just as the human being is composed of two elements—the soul (nafs) and the body—so too does religion encompass two dimensions. From the philosophical and mystical perspective, the soul represents the sum of the noble faculties of the body, the abstract and the immaterial. Thinking, comprehension, and the acquisition of knowledge are functions of the soul, not of the body. For this reason, the work of knowing the testimony of divine unity (shahādat) belongs to the soul, not the body. This devotion is knowledge, a matter pertaining to the human soul, which by its nobility, virtue, and excellence deserves the acquisition of knowledge.

The body, by contrast, is itself inert and devoid of the capacity to comprehend and acquire knowledge. Therefore, it has been made obligatory upon the body to perform practical worship—a discipline that subdues and refines the bodily nature—so that the body becomes worthy of the worship of knowledge by attaining perfection, piety, and obedience in bodily discipline.[^cite-52]

## "And each moves in an orbit"

The Qur'an is often called the heart of divine revelation. The heart of Surah Yā-Sīn is constituted by this very verse, for it occupies the central position of the chapter—not precisely at its geometric center, but displaced slightly, just as the human heart lies not at the midpoint of the chest but somewhat to the left.

A remarkable property distinguishes this verse: whether recited forward or in reverse, it maintains its harmony and meaning intact.

The term *falak* (orbit) denotes the form or shape of a circle. No celestial body can escape the bounds of its designated orbit. Similarly, every rank within the daʿwa (the divine summons or invitation to faith)—whether makāsir (the great ones), mazūn (the commissioned ones), dāʿī (the summoner), or others—possesses a defined sphere within which it conducts its functions. It is forbidden for any person to exceed the authority of his station. Should anyone transgress these boundaries, the system of the daʿwa would be disrupted, just as the cosmos itself would fall into chaos if the planets abandoned their orbits and the gravitational harmony were lost.[^cite-53]

## "Say: I ask of you no reward except love for my kinfolk"

There came a time when the people of Medina approached the Prophet—may peace and blessings be upon him—bearing bags laden with jewels. Many of them offered substantial portions of their wealth to the Lord of the Universe, saying, "You have caused us great hardship; you have delivered us from the Fire and shown us the path to Paradise. We were divided among ourselves, yet you have made us brothers. Accept these offerings as compensation for your blessed service." At that moment, the Prophet remained silent. Then this verse descended from Allah, proclaiming: "O Prophet, tell them that I ask of you only one reward for your prophethood—that you should love those who are bound to me by kinship."

In this verse, Allah addresses the Prophet, saying: "Tell the people, 'I do not ask of you any reward for my prophethood except that you should love those who are my relatives (Ahl al-Bayt, the people of my house).'" Where there is love, obedience naturally follows; where there is obedience, devotion emerges. This verse alludes to the same love that the Prophet confirmed on the day of Ghadir Khumm when he declared concerning ʿAlī: "Of whomsoever I am master, so too is ʿAlī his master."

### The Significance of This Request

There was a time when the unbelievers of Mecca brought before the Prophet—may peace and blessings be upon him—the mountain of Abū Qubays heaped with gold, offering him riches and power on the condition that he would abandon his mission. He rejected all these gifts utterly and declared, "Even if the sun were placed in my right hand and the moon in my left, I would not abandon the message of Allah." If a person has been offered such tremendous wealth without requesting it and has spurned it entirely, then when that same person asks for something, it must be of utmost significance. And what did he ask for? Only the love of the Ahl al-Bayt. This demonstrates that the love of the People of the House surpasses all the wealth, beauty, and dominion of this world.

### The Reason for the Creation of the Universe: Love of the Five Pure Ones

The very reason for the creation of this vast universe is the love of the Panjtan Pāk (the Five Pure Ones). Every being creates and adorns according to his nature; in the same way, Allah has created this boundless universe out of love for these five sanctified souls. He declared: "I have not raised the heavens, I have not spread out the earth, I have not kindled the light of the sun and moon, I have not set in motion the revolving celestial spheres, I have not created the flowing seas and the ships that sail upon them—all except through love for these five pure beings enveloped in the cloak, purified by divine grace."

This universe of breathtaking beauty was brought into existence solely through love for the Ahl al-Bayt, as testified by the words of Allah: "Were it not for you, O Muhammad, I would not have created the heavens." The Messenger of Allah—may peace and blessings be upon him—declared: "Allah created my light first; then He created the light of all created things." He further said, "ʿAlī and I are of one light (Annā wa ʿAlī min Nūr Wāḥid)."[^cite-54] From this shared light, he continued, "came forth the earth and the heavens; then from our light came forth the light of beauty and grace, from which the sun and moon were formed." When the light of our master Fāṭimah al-Zahrāʾ—may peace be upon her—was brought forth, she became known as Mashiyyat Allāh (the Will of Allah) precisely because she embodies the very will and intention of the Almighty.

It is by divine grace alone that Allah has created all things for these five holy souls. Yet how dare we, insignificant servants of dust and clay, presume to create anything out of love for the Panjtan? We ourselves are creatures; what could we possibly do? Man is created weak by nature. We can only express our love by holding fast to the strong rope of Allah and loving the Ahl al-Bayt of the Prophet with all our hearts. The Prophet—may peace and blessings be upon him—said: "Adorn your gatherings with the remembrance of ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib, for to remember ʿAlī is to remember me, and to remember me is to remember Allah, and to remember Allah is worship itself."[^cite-55] It is as though the mention of ʿAlī has become a form of devotion to God. Elsewhere he proclaimed:

> O ʿAlī! Allah has given His prophets and messengers superiority over His angels, and He has given me superiority over all the prophets and messengers. After me, O ʿAlī, this excellence belongs to you and to the Imāms who will come from your line. The angels who bear the divine throne and those who circle it in glorification all ask forgiveness for those who believe in our guardianship (wilāyah).

### The Reason for the Request

This is no trivial matter, but a question of recompense. Whoever fails to honor the prophethood of the Messenger insults his very mission, and on the Day of Resurrection, he shall surely be questioned about it. It is notable that such a request is made only when people do not give without being asked. This demonstrates that when the Messenger of Allah completed his proclamation, he waited for the people to render their tribute of their own accord. Yet the Messenger—may peace and blessings be upon him—despaired, and Allah commanded him to request the reward due for his prophethood.

A second reason for this request was this: the Prophet, through his prophetic foresight and divine support, beheld the future and saw ʿAlī meeting martyrdom, the door falling upon Fāṭimah, al-Ḥasan poisoned, and al-Ḥusayn beheaded. He thus said to the people, "If you would render anything in return for my prophethood, show mercy to my family and do not cause them harm." This command was issued by Allah so that the persecution of the Ahl al-Bayt might be stayed.[^cite-56]

### Payment of Recompense Distinguishes the Lawful from the Forbidden

Nothing is permissible without recompense and compensation. For instance, if a person prays in stolen garments or clothes not properly sewn, can his prayer be valid and accepted? Now consider: what happens to one's prayer if the person who delivered this prayer-message has not been compensated? Every ordinance of divine law is part of the proclamation of the Messenger of Allah. If the messenger is not rewarded, no act of the Sharīʿah will be acceptable.

### The Debate of Imam Jaʿfar al-Ṣādiq Concerning Truth

One day, Imām Jaʿfar al-Ṣādiq—peace be upon him—passed through a marketplace. He observed a man who stole two pomegranates from one shop and then two loaves of bread from another. The man then proceeded to a street corner where he gave all four items to a poor, needy person.

The Imām questioned him about this action. The man replied, "O son of the Messenger of Allah, surely you know that in Allah's law, the reward of one good deed is ten good deeds, while the sin of one evil deed is one sin. I have stolen four things, so four sins have been written in my record. Then I gave these four things to a poor person, and forty good deeds have been written for me. Thus, out of forty good deeds, four sins are subtracted, leaving me with thirty-six good deeds."

The Imām said, "O man, may your mother lose you! You are heedless of Allah's Book and its commands. Your reckoning is false! The correct accounting is this: You stole four items, and your sins increased fourfold. You did not compensate the owner of the property and therefore misappropriated it. Then you gave what was not yours to give—a poor person whom you had no right to grant anything. Thus four further sins were written against you, and you have not earned a single good deed; rather, your deeds have been multiplied eightfold in evil."

Here lies a matter of profound concern: If the Messenger of Allah—may peace and blessings be upon him—prays and performs other acts of the Sharīʿah without rendering the compensation due for his prophethood, will he be rewarded or will he incur sin?

In His manifest Book, Allah has commanded the Muslims to pray, give the alms-tax (zakāt), fast, perform pilgrimage, and undertake struggle in His way (jihād). Each of these acts possesses a discrete nature; that is, if someone prays but does not perform pilgrimage, he receives the reward of prayer, even if questioned about pilgrimage. Yet when it comes to love for the Ahl al-Bayt, it is bound to recompense. If the Messenger does not receive compensation for his prophethood, no act of the Sharīʿah derived from the Messenger will bear reward. Therefore, it is absolutely impermissible to use any property without payment.

### "Light" Refers to the Five Pure Ones

Allah declares in the Qur'an:

> ⟪quran 5:15⟫
> ⟪ar:‏يَٓأَهْلَ ٱلْكِتَبِ قَدْ جَآءَكُمْ رَسُولُنَا يُبَيِّنُ لَكُمْ كَثِيرًۭا مِّمَّا كُنتُمْ تُخْفُونَ مِنَ ٱلْكِتَبِ وَيَعْفُوا۟ عَن كَثِيرٍۢ ۚ قَدْ جَآءَكُم مِّنَ ٱللَّهِ نُورٌۭ وَكِتَبٌۭ مُّبِينٌۭ ‎⟫

If one understands the word *nūr* (light) in this verse to refer to the Qur'an, as many commentators claim, then why does Allah mention the Book separately after mentioning it within this very verse? This inconsistency reveals the truth: the light (*nūr*) is something distinct, and the Revealed Book is something else entirely. The first verse mentions the Messenger and the Light; the second verse mentions the Light and the Book—showing that a third entity exists besides the Light, the Messenger, and the Book; something that is neither messenger nor book.

The Messenger of Allah—may peace and blessings be upon him—declared: "ʿAlī and I are of one light (Annā wa ʿAlī min Nūr Wāḥid)." This demonstrates that ʿAlī—peace be upon him—is a light. The Messenger said, "Fāṭimah is of me," implying that if the Messenger is a light, then Fāṭimah too is a light. He said concerning al-Ḥasan and al-Ḥusayn: "Their flesh is my flesh, and their blood is my blood," proving that these two grandsons are also sharers in the light of the Prophet.

### Kinship

There exist two kinds of kinship. One is permanent, the other temporary. Temporary kinship includes the relationship between husband and wife; as long as both remain together, the rights of kinship endure, but upon divorce, all kinship ceases. Similarly, the relationship between a dwelling and its occupant is temporary, as are all friendships—bonds that can be severed tomorrow.

Permanent kinship comprises the relationships of mother, father, sibling, brother, sister, aunt, and uncle. In the Messenger's request—may peace and blessings be upon him—it is necessary that we seek out his eternal relatives: those bound to him by permanent ties of kinship, those who are the people of purity and sanctity, connected to the Messenger through bonds that transcend time itself.

Shortly after the revelation of this verse, someone asked the Messenger of Allah—may peace and blessings be upon him—"O Messenger of Allah! Who accompanied you to the mubāhalah (the prayer of imprecation), and who are those about whom this verse was revealed?" He replied, "We are Fāṭimah, myself, ʿAlī, al-Ḥasan, and al-Ḥusayn." It is as though Fāṭimah al-Zahrāʾ became the axis around which all relations revolve—now as a daughter, now as a wife, now as a mother.

### When Is Recompense Demanded?

Religion has always been Islam in the sight of Allah. The Prophet did not bring any new religion; rather, it is the same din (religion) that commenced with Adam. Over the passage of ages, the laws underwent refinement and codification, yet the name of Islam remained constant from its inception and shall remain so until the end. With this word, the name of the Messenger became bound: sometimes Adam, the Messenger of Allah; sometimes Noah, the Prophet of Allah; sometimes Abraham, the Friend of Allah (Khalīl Allāh); sometimes Moses, the Interlocutor of Allah (Kalīm Allāh); sometimes Jesus, the Spirit of Allah (Rūḥ Allāh). The final addition to this word was Muhammad, the Messenger of Allah—but it could not be perfected without ʿAlī, the Guardian of Allah (Walī Allāh). This is the ʿAlī who placed his hand upon his breast and declared, "Here are the learned ones," and who proclaimed from the pulpit, "Ask of me before you lose me."

Before the Messenger of Allah, one hundred and twenty-four thousand prophets came, yet none asked for recompense of their prophethood. Only the Messenger of Allah demanded recompense from his community. This is a matter worthy of contemplation and reflection. A worker requests wages only upon completing his labor; while the work remains unfinished, no compensation is sought. Throughout the ages of antiquity, the religion of Islam continued to unfold and progress; therefore, no preceding prophet asked for recompense. In the time of the Messenger of Allah, however, the religion reached its perfection; its laws were codified and sealed. When he proclaimed at Ghadir Khumm, "Of whomsoever I am the master, so too is ʿAlī his master," Allah revealed this verse, and it shall shine eternally: "This day have I perfected your religion for you and completed My favor upon you, and have chosen for you Islam as your din (way of life)."[^cite-57] At that moment, the work of his prophethood was complete, and he requested his recompense. All the prophets participated in bringing Islam to its ultimate fulfillment, and all endured trials and tribulations. Therefore, the Prophet's request for recompense was in truth a request for reward for himself and for the tireless efforts of all the prophets.

### The Distinction Between Relatives and Community

The one who issues a command and the one to whom the command is addressed are different entities. Allah is the Commander; the Messenger is the one commanded by Allah; the Messenger commands the community. Allah cannot be a messenger, and a messenger cannot be Allah. Similarly, the community is the entity obligated to give the recompense to the relatives of the Prophet, while the relatives are the recipients. The community is one thing; the relatives are another. The community cannot become relatives, nor can relatives become prophets. Here, the community is commanded to show love for the relatives as compensation, because the message of prophethood was directed to the community and not to the relatives themselves. Had the message been addressed to the relatives, they too would have come under this obligation and been asked for recompense.

### The People's Understanding of This Verse

Wages are demanded from those who have provided labor. The mission of prophethood was not entrusted to the Prophet by the community; rather, it was given to him by Allah, who sent him as a messenger. Therefore, only Allah can compensate the Messenger of Allah. What right has the community? What obligation does it bear to reward the Messenger? The community stands in need of the intercession of the Messenger of Allah; what recompense, then, could it possibly offer? Thus it became clear that it is impossible for the community to compensate the Messenger. The Qur'an itself declares that the reward of the Messenger is with Allah alone:

> ⟪quran 34:47⟫
> ⟪ar:‏قُلْ مَا سَأَلْتُكُم مِّنْ أَجْرٍۢ فَهُوَ لَكُمْۖ إِنْ أَجْرِىَ إِلَّا عَلَى ٱللَّهِۖ وَهُوَ عَلَىٰ كُلِّ شَىْءٍۢ شَهِيدٌۭ ‎⟫

A certain faction among the Ahl al-Ẓāhir (the literalists) claimed that this verse abrogates the previous one, arguing: "In this verse, Allah Almighty says that He Himself will reward the Messenger for his prophethood. Therefore, Muslims need only treat their relatives and kinfolk well." This interpretation cannot be correct under any circumstances, for there are many in the community whose relatives and kinfolk are unbelievers. How could it be possible for Allah to reward the Messenger for love of the disbelievers?

This second verse does not abrogate the first; rather, it supports and clarifies it. The true meaning is this: the community shall be the sole beneficiary of the reward that the Messenger requested from the community in the form of love for his Ahl al-Bayt. Neither the Prophet nor the Ahl al-Bayt shall benefit from this recompense, for the Prophet and his Ahl al-Bayt are rich in divine knowledge, while the community is poor. Paradise cannot be attained without their guidance, and whoever abandons the Ahl al-Bayt finds Paradise distant and Hell near. Allah alone is the one who rewards the Messenger and the Ahl al-Bayt.[^cite-58]

### Conclusion

Just as every sūrah of the Qur'an is blessed and worthy of respect—whether it be a lengthy sūrah like Sūrat al-Baqarah or a brief one like Sūrat al-Kawthar—so too is every member of the Ahl al-Bayt beloved and honored, whether one be a figure of lesser prominence like Muslim ibn ʿAqīl or ʿAnī, Ḥazrat al-Qāsim and al-ʿAbbās, or one of the greater lights like Imām al-Ḥasan and Imām al-Ḥusayn. The love and guardianship of all of them have been enjoined upon us by the Messenger of Allah.

### The True Benefit of Recompense for Prophethood

The recompense for the prophethood is love for the Imāms of the guardianship (wilāyah) of Amīr al-Muʾminīn and his descendants—without which the bounties of the Qur'an cannot benefit the heart. The Qur'an has reached all Muslims; why, then, has faith not entered their hearts? It is known that only those who render this recompense of prophethood and practice the guardianship (wilāyah) of Amīr al-Muʾminīn and the Ahl al-Bayt—peace be upon them all—will find faith entering their hearts. Those who neglect this guardianship cannot achieve true faith.[^cite-59]

## "That Allah may forgive you your sins of the past and the future"

When Sūrat al-Fatḥ was revealed, the Prophet—may peace and blessings be upon him—experienced great joy, for within this chapter lie many wonders. Reciting this sūrah every morning carries tremendous virtue. The sūrah contains mention of *al-Fatḥ al-Mubīn* (the clear victory), which represents the outward manifestation of prophethood through which Allah granted him manifest triumph. Yet this verse also indicates that Allah forgave the Messenger for all the sins that preceded and would follow.

In elucidating the truths of this verse, the Prophet declares: "Muhammad is exalted, supreme, and protected from error—free from all sin and transgression." The "Muhammad" mentioned in this verse is one of the *ḥudūd* (the limits or ranks of the spiritual hierarchy of the daʿwa), who appears as the veil of the historical Muhammad—may peace and blessings be upon him. Such veils sometimes are subject to error.

### The Facts

The veils that take the form of the boundaries of the daʿwa, as they progress through the ranks, do not fall within the sphere of infallibility (ʿiṣmah). They are liable to make mistakes. However, those veils that are constituted by the remembrance of the divine (dhikr) are within the realm of infallibility and are free from all error.

### His Previous Sin

His prior sin was an inclination to place Abū Bakr within the ranks of the inner daʿwa (the esoteric summons) so that both the exoteric and esoteric dimensions would be preserved. Yet it was the divine decree that Abū Bakr should find no place even in the inner daʿwa. Therefore, Gabriel came with an admonition from Allah to the Prophet. Yet at that moment, the Messenger of Allah—may peace and blessings be upon him—was satisfied. Though his inclination toward Abū Bakr had been established, because your inclination was not in disobedience to Allah but arose solely from your desire to preserve the daʿwa, it became in the sight of Allah a sin to be forgiven.

### A Profound Lesson

Here lies a great lesson for the believers: The Messenger of Allah—may peace and blessings be upon him—who proclaimed the command of Allah for twenty-three years, enduring all manner of hardship, the beloved of Allah, is told by his Lord, "If you associate anyone with the status of ʿAlī, we shall render null all your deeds!" The matter of concern for the believers is this: if they add the love of the enemies of ʿAlī to their love of ʿAlī, what will Allah do to their actions? (May Allah protect us!)

### And What Is Delayed

His final sin was the delay in announcing the testament concerning ʿAlī. When he returned from ʿArafāt after completing the pilgrimage, revelations began descending upon him so heavily that his camel would kneel repeatedly under their weight. That revelation concerned ʿAlī's testament. The command had already come: "Convey what has been sent down to you," yet the Prophet was seeking an opportune moment for its proclamation.

Because of this delay, the moment passed as he departed from the valley of ʿArafāt. When he reached Ghadir Khumm, Allah commanded him most sternly:

> ⟪quran 48:2⟫
> ⟪ar:‏لِّيَغْفِرَ لَكَ ٱللَّهُ مَا تَقَدَّمَ مِن ذَنۢبِكَ وَمَا تَأَخَّرَ وَيُتِمَّ نِعْمَتَهُۥ عَلَيْكَ وَيَهْدِيكَ صِرَطًۭا مُّسْتَقِيمًۭا ‎⟫

The verse that became manifest was: "Convey what has been revealed to you from your Lord concerning ʿAlī—your Lord." But the hypocrites later expunged ʿAlī's name from this verse.

### A Statement of Gravity

The Messenger whom Allah honored throughout his life with titles of tenderness—sometimes *al-Muzammil* (the cloaked one), sometimes *al-Muddaththir* (the covered one), sometimes *Ṭā-Hā*, sometimes *Yā-Sīn*—is addressed here with the formality "O Messenger!" This was an extraordinarily severe communication from Allah to His Messenger.

As soon as this verse was revealed and the glad tidings of Allah's protection of his din came to him, he set the people at ease, called back those who had gone ahead, and waited for those who lagged behind, gathering them together. Describing the significance of this gathering, the Dāʿīs (the Summoners) say: "At that moment, all the preceding prophets and the spiritual forms of all previous nations assembled at that place, and all the spiritual forms that would come after him waited in gathering until the Day of Judgment."

When the seventh hour arrived, he proclaimed ʿAlī's testament and declared: "Of whomsoever I am the master, so too is ʿAlī his master (Man Kuntu Mawlāhu, fa-ʿAlī Mawlāhu)." He took from all those present—and from the generations past and future—an oath of allegiance to ʿAlī's guardianship. Yet this delay in doing so was a sin in the sight of Allah. In this verse, Allah forgives the veil of Muhammad for these two transgressions.[^cite-60]

## "And We are nearer to him than his jugular vein"

> ⟪quran 50:16⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَلَقَدْ خَلَقْنَا ٱلْإِنسَنَ وَنَعْلَمُ مَا تُوَسْوِسُ بِهِۦ نَفْسُهُۥ ۖ وَنَحْنُ أَقْرَبُ إِلَيْهِ مِنْ حَبْلِ ٱلْوَرِيدِ ‎⟫

How can a Being who exists beyond the limits of time and space, who is infinitely transcendent, remote from all notions of proximity or distance, be described as near to the jugular vein of a human neck? If this proximity refers to the divine essence itself, surely the singular form (*ʿayn*) would have been more fitting than the plural (*naḥnu*)! What is the virtue of employing the plural pronoun?

Some explain this as a rhetorical convention: a single sovereign, when addressing his people, speaks of himself in the plural, saying "We command" or "We decree." Yet this explanation casts a shadow upon the doctrine of divine unity—a grave impropriety!

The great master Syadnā Ghulām Ḥusayn Ṣāḥib would often relate that Sulṭān al-ʿĀrifīn (the Master of the Knowers of God) has said, "Whoever among you comes to know his own soul comes to know his Lord." Our common understanding posits that the self (nafs) is singular, yet if we contemplate deeply, we immediately perceive three dimensions within ourselves. The lowest manifestation of the soul is its vegetative aspect, evident in plants. Beyond this is a higher manifestation—the sensitive soul, manifest in animals. Transcending both of these is the rational soul, the faculty of human consciousness. No, more precisely: man is the *manifestation* of these three souls united. It is this integrated human being who attains knowledge of the Creator and grasps the secrets of the cosmos; this is why man is called the noblest of creatures. Addressing this human being, the Almighty asks, "Where do you wander in search of truth, away from yourself?" You yourself are brimming with mysteries. As the sacred tradition teaches, "Man is My secret, and I am his secret."[^cite-61] The Prophet—may peace and blessings be upon him—declared in the *ḥadīth qudsī* (the sacred utterance from the Lord): "Understand the allusion of this verse!"

The three letters of *naḥn* (we/us) hold profound meaning:

- **Nūn (N)** = *Nāmiyah* (the vegetative soul)  
- **Hāʾ (H)** = *Ḥassāsah* (the sensitive soul)  
- **Nūn (N)** = *Nāṭiqah* (the rational soul)

| N | H | N |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Nāmiyah | Ḥassāsah | Nāṭiqah |

Understand these three dimensions of the self! The plural pronoun *naḥn* (we) refers to these three souls, which are closer to this human being than his jugular vein. Comprehend the faculties of the soul, grasp their proximity, and then the soul of the human shall understand the soul of the world—the principle of closeness. You will discover that the soul is closer to human consciousness than even Allah! And once you have known them, you shall declare in humility, "I have not fulfilled the right of knowing You," understanding truly that the inability to perceive is itself the highest knowledge.[^cite-62]

## None Shall Touch It Save the Purified

Allah says in the Holy Qur'an:

> ⟪quran 56:77⟫
> ⟪ar:‏إِنَّهُۥ لَقُرْآنٌۭ كَرِيمٌۭ ‎⟫
> *That (this) is indeed an honourable recital (the Noble Quran).*
>
> ⟪quran 56:78⟫
> ⟪ar:‏فِى كِتَبٍۢ مَّكْنُونٍۢ ‎⟫
> *In a Book well-guarded (with Allah in the heaven i.e. Al-Lauh Al-Mahfuz).*
>
> ⟪quran 56:79⟫
> ⟪ar:‏لَّا يَمَسُّهُۥٓ إِلَّا ٱلْمُطَهَّرُونَ ‎⟫
> *Which (that Book with Allah) none can touch but the purified (i.e. the angels).*

In these verses, Allah declares that none shall touch the preserved Qur'an save those who are purified. On the surface, this declaration appears to present a difficulty: disbelievers also handle the Qur'an with their hands. This apparent contradiction reveals that "touching" (*al-mass*) here does not signify outward, physical contact but rather an inward, spiritual apprehension—the grasping of meaning through esoteric exegesis (taʾwīl). [^cite-62]

### The Body of the Qur'an: Letters and Words

The topics of the Qur'an rest upon two dimensions, one external and one internal—much as the world divides into this life and the Hereafter, or as the human person comprises body and soul. The external dimension is a place of constant change and transformation; the internal dimension maintains a stable and unified state. Just as the body undergoes perpetual modification—youth yielding to strength, strength to weakness, health alternating with illness, and the seasons succeeding one another—so too does the Qur'an, embodied in letters and words, manifest profound variations. Although it is a book of guidance, its outward appearances present seeming contradictions and perplexities. Allah says at the Qur'an's beginning:

> ⟪quran 2:1⟫
> ⟪ar:‏الم ‎⟫

In Arabic, *dhālik* (that) refers to what is absent and distant, while *hādhā* (this) denotes what is present and near. When the reader holds the Qur'an in hand, the book is immediately before him; yet Allah employs the distant form. The verse should read, "This is the Book of which there is no doubt," if ordinary proximity were intended. Yet in the very first statement, Allah affirms the Book's certainty and its guidance for humankind—and this opening invites the reader to wonder: what does Allah mean by employing the sign of distance? [^cite-63]

### Apparent Contradictions Concerning Divine Vision

As the reader advances through the text, one verse establishes an understanding, and then another forces the imagination toward its opposite. For example, one reads:

> ⟪quran 75:22⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وُجُوهٌۭ يَوْمَئِذٍۢ نَّاضِرَةٌۭ ‎⟫
> *Faces, that Day, will be radiant.*

From this verse, one forms the notion that God may be seen. Yet when one recites:

> ⟪quran 6:103⟫
> ⟪ar:‏لَا تُدْرِكُهُ ٱلْأَبْصَرُ ۖ ‎⟫
> *No vision can grasp Him.*

One is left in perplexity. In one place, Allah states that certain faces shall behold Him; in another, that sight cannot encompass Him. Which understanding is correct? [^cite-64]

### The Question of Human Will

Similarly, the matter of human will (*irādah*) presents seeming contradiction. When the reader encounters:

> ⟪quran 18:29⟫
> ⟪ar:‏فَمَن شَآءَ فَلْيُؤْمِن وَمَن شَآءَ فَلْيَكْفُرْ ‎⟫
> *Whoever wills may believe, and whoever wills may disbelieve.*

One forms the conviction that faith and disbelief rest upon human volition and fall within human control. Yet immediately upon reading:

> ⟪quran 76:30⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَمَا تَشَآءُونَ إِلَّآ أَن يَشَآءَ ٱللَّهُ ‎⟫
> *You will not, unless Allah wills.*

And even more plainly:

> ⟪quran 81:29⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَمَا تَشَآءُونَ إِلَّآ أَن يَشَآءَ ٱللَّهُ رَبُّ ٱلْعَـٰلَمِينَ ‎⟫
> *You will not, unless Allah, Lord of all the worlds, wills.*

One begins to think that human will possesses no reality whatsoever—that whatever exists is Allah's will alone. Then what meaning does human volition possess?

### Dispute Concerning the Qur'an as Allah's Word

Likewise, the Qur'an is the Word of Allah. As one recites, one encounters the verse:

> ⟪quran 26:192⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَإِنَّهُۥ لَتَنزِيلُ رَبِّ ٱلْعَـٰلَمِينَ ‎⟫
> *And it is, in truth, the revelation of the Lord of the worlds.*

From this one establishes the conviction that the Qur'an is undoubtedly Allah's word, revealed to the heart of His Messenger. Yet upon reading further:

> ⟪quran 69:40⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَإِنَّهُۥ لَقَوْلُ رَسُولٍۢ كَرِيمٍۢ ‎⟫
> *And it is the word of a noble messenger.*

One becomes troubled and uncertain: is this the Qur'an, or the utterance of the Messenger of Allah?

### The Order of Creation: Heavens and Earth

In the same manner, those who ponder the creation of the heavens and earth grow anxious about their sequence: which was created first? For two apparently contradictory verses address this question. In one, Allah says:

> ⟪quran 2:29⟫
> ⟪ar:‏هُوَ ٱلَّذِى خَلَقَ لَكُم مَّا فِى ٱلْأَرْضِ جَمِيعًۭا ثُمَّ ٱسْتَوَىٰٓ إِلَى ٱلسَّمَآءِ ‎⟫
> *It is He Who created for you all that is on earth; then turned to the heaven and fashioned it.*

This verse states clearly that He created the earth first, then the heavens. But in another place He says:

> ⟪quran 79:27⟫
> ⟪ar:‏أَأَنتُمْ أَشَدُّ خَلْقًا أَمِ ٱلسَّمَآءُ ۚ بَنَىٰهَا ‎⟫
> *Are you harder to create, or is the heaven? He built it.*

Here the heavens are mentioned as created first. Which of the two is correct?

Beyond these instances, many other verses display such seeming contradiction and disagreement, leading inevitably to divergence in understanding and causing a person to doubt his religion. For this reason, one of our preachers lifted the Qur'an above his head and exclaimed: "By Allah! This book has led whole nations astray!" [^cite-65]

### The Spirit of the Qur'an: Meaning and Soul

Yet unlike the Qur'an's letter and words, there exists a soul (*rūḥ*), a meaning that does not submit to manifold influences as the human soul does, but rather abides in eternal constancy. All agree that the soul constitutes the true reality in the human being and possesses the excellence. A scholar or physician, however highly esteemed in the world, possesses neither learning nor healing when the soul separates from the body. A living human being holds infinite worth; the dead are worthless. The real value resides not in the body but in the soul.

The same truth applies to the Qur'an. Its words dwell in the palace of the body, being a book of written letters. Yet the Ahl al-Bayt (the People of the House)—the spiritual descendants of the Prophet—are the soul of the Qur'an, revealing the hidden meaning (*bāṭin*) concealed within these words. Should the soul be separated from the Qur'an, it becomes meaningless and dead. For this reason, the Commander of the Faithful, ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib, called the Qur'an "a mute speaker and the Ahl al-Bayt a speaking silence." The Qur'an is the letter; the Ahl al-Bayt embody its meaning. Allah named the Qur'an *dhikr* (remembrance) and called the Ahl al-Bayt *ahl al-dhikr* (the people of remembrance), saying:

> ⟪quran 21:7⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَمَآ أَرْسَلْنَا قَبْلَكَ إِلَّا رِجَالًۭا نُّوحِىٓ إِلَيْهِمْ ۖ فَسْـَٔلُوٓا۟ أَهْلَ ٱلذِّكْرِ إِن كُنتُمْ لَا تَعْلَمُونَ ‎⟫
> *Ask the people of remembrance if you do not know.* [^cite-66]

If you read the Qur'an, you become Muslim; if you understand it, you become a believer. The Prophet (peace and blessings be upon him) declared: "I leave among you two precious things. One is the Book of Allah; the other is my Itrat—my Ahl al-Bayt. As long as you hold fast to both, you shall never go astray. There shall be no separation between them until they meet me at the Pool of Kawthar." He further said: "There shall never be division between the Qur'an and the Ahl al-Bayt, between the Book and the People of the Book, between remembrance and the people of remembrance." If the Qur'an exists in every age, so too must the Ahl al-Bayt exist in every age. If the Qur'an is guidance, then the Ahl al-Bayt likewise embody guidance. Were one sufficient alone, there would be no need to link it with the other. Allah Himself explains this conjunction in His Clear Book. On one hand, He says of the Book: "There is nothing dry or wet that is not in the Book." On the other hand, He says to the Imam:

> ⟪quran 36:12⟫
> ⟪ar:‏إِنَّا نَحْنُ نُحْىِ ٱلْمَوْتَىٰ وَنَكْتُبُ مَا قَدَّمُوا۟ وَآثَرَهُمْۚ وَكُلَّ شَىْءٍ أَحْصَيْنَهُ فِىٓ إِمَامٍۢ مُّبِينٍۢ ‎⟫
> *We shall bring the dead to life and record all that they send forward and all they leave behind. All things We have preserved in a Clear Imam.* [^cite-67]

Thus, touching in this verse means understanding the meaning of the Qur'an. It is meritorious to touch with outer purity (ablution), though not obligatory. In this verse, Allah declares that none shall comprehend the true meaning of the Qur'an except the pure and purified servants—that is, only the Imams of the Ahl al-Bayt can attain the hidden meaning (*bāṭin*) behind the Book's words. These pure servants are those to whom Allah sent down the verse of purification, which names the Ahl al-Bayt:

> ⟪quran 33:33⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَقَرْنَ فِى بُيُوتِكُنَّ وَلَا تَبَرَّجْنَ تَبَرُّجَ ٱلْجَهِلِيَّةِ ٱلْأُولَىٰۖ وَأَقِمْنَ ٱلصَّلَوٰةَ وَآتِينَ ٱلزَّكَوٰةَ وَأَطِعْنَ ٱللَّهَ وَرَسُولَهُۚۥٓ إِنَّمَا يُرِيدُ ٱللَّهُ لِيُذْهِبَ عَنكُمُ ٱلرِّجْسَ أَهْلَ ٱلْبَيْتِ وَيُطَهِّرَكُمْ تَطْهِيرًۭا ‎⟫
> *Allah intends but to remove from you uncleanness, O people of the house, and to purify you with a thorough purification.* [^cite-68]

Regarding these people, the Qur'an declares: "Allah has intended to purify them utterly from all defilement and to make them pure as they deserve to be." Therefore, if you wish to understand the hidden and manifest meanings (*ẓāhir wa-bāṭin*) of this Book, ask the Clear Imam—the Imām al-Mubīn. The foremost of this Ahl al-Bayt is the Commander of the Faithful, ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib, who said from the pulpit: "Ask before you lose me! He who remains attached solely to the Qur'an's outward form shall stumble perpetually and never find guidance."

*(From the Assemblies of al-Muʾayyad, as transmitted by Sayyidnā al-Muʾayyad al-Shīrāzī)*

## By the Dawn and the Ten Nights

> ⟪quran 89:1⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَٱلْفَجْرِ ‎⟫
> *By the dawn;*
>
> ⟪quran 89:2⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَلَيَالٍ عَشْرٍۢ ‎⟫
> *By the ten nights.*
>
> ⟪quran 89:3⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَٱلشَّفْعِ وَٱلْوَتْرِ ‎⟫
> *And by the even and the odd.*
>
> ⟪quran 89:4⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَٱلَّيْلِ إِذَا يَسْرِ ‎⟫
> *And by the night when it departs.*

The al-Fajr (the Dawn) at the beginning of this chapter indicates conjunction. Al-Fajr refers to those forms from the past that have progressed through the ranks of existence and now appear in the final configuration (Qiyāmat al-Qāʾim).

*Waliyal ʿAshar* (the Ten Nights) represent the ten stages through which forms progress and attain human status. These ten levels are:

| 1 | Mineral strength | 6 | The Power of Honour |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 2 | Organic Power | 7 | Power of Sovereignty |
| 3 | The Power of Animal Rage | 8 | The Power of the Holy (*Qudsiyyah al-ʿUlya*) |
| 4 | The Power of Humanity (two aspects: lower and rational) | 9 | The Power of the Hereafter |
| 5 | The Power of Wisdom | 10 | The Power of Humankind |

*Al-Shafaʿ* (the Even) signifies the soul that is the origin of the universe—the principle by which form and substance interconnect and depend upon one another. Form depends upon matter; matter equally depends upon form for its manifestation.

*Al-Witr* (the Odd) designates the separation of matter from the senses—the degree of holiness. At that moment, all duality is perfected.

*Wa-al-Layl idhā Yasrī* (the Night When It Departs) denotes the transmission of forms toward development, such that the form reaches its primary perfection (kamāl awwal). At this juncture, they have attained primary perfection and must now ascend toward secondary perfection (kamāl thānī) through their own exaltation.

## Your Lord Will Give You, and You Will Be Well-Pleased

Allah declares concerning the days after the Messenger of Allah (peace and blessings be upon him):

> ⟪quran 93:5⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَلَسَوْفَ يُعْطِيكَ رَبُّكَ فَتَرْضَىٰٓ ‎⟫
> *And your Lord will give you, and you will be well-pleased.* [^cite-69]

We are concerned about how to please our Lord; yet for the Messenger of Allah, it is the Lord Himself who is concerned with the Messenger's pleasure.

There exists a subtle refinement in this verse, illustrated by the following example: when we hire a labourer and agree upon a wage of two hundred rupees, and upon completion of the work we give him the sum, if he regards us with dissatisfaction—wishing for more—we bestow additional money as a gift from our grace, so that he may be pleased. When we observe his satisfaction, we cease our giving. That is, his satisfaction and contentment restrain our gift.

A similar subtlety inheres in this verse: the blessing of Allah ceases where the Messenger of Allah becomes satisfied. In other words, the Messenger shall never be satisfied, and therefore the process of Allah's bestowal shall never cease. This is why Allah says in the Qur'an:

> ⟪quran 33:56⟫
> ⟪ar:‏إِنَّ ٱللَّهَ وَمَلَٓئِكَتَهُۥ يُصَلُّونَ عَلَى ٱلنَّبِىِّۚ يَٓأَيُّهَا ٱلَّذِينَ آمَنُوا۟ صَلُّوا۟ عَلَيْهِ وَسَلِّمُوا۟ تَسْلِيمًا ‎⟫
> *Allah and His angels send blessings upon the Prophet. O you who believe, send blessings and peace upon him.* [^cite-70]

This forms a continuity in the chain: the blessing shall never cease upon him. That is, Allah does not cease to bestow blessings, the angels do not cease to pray blessings, and the believers should not cease to invoke blessings. Thus the stream of divine favour flows eternally uninterrupted.

*(From the Lessons of Tuḥfat al-Sairfī—meaning the letters from al-Muʿjam al-Nawādir—as transmitted by Sayyidnā Ḥasan Khān ibn ʿAbd al-Malik)* [^cite-71]

## No Sin Upon the Believers Who Do Good Deeds

Allah declares in the Holy Qur'an:

> ⟪quran 5:93⟫
> ⟪ar:‏لَيْسَ عَلَى ٱلَّذِينَ آمَنُوا۟ وَعَمِلُوا۟ ٱلصَّـٰلِحَتِ جُنَاحٌۭ فِيمَا طَعِمُوٓا۟ إِذَا مَا ٱتَّقَوا۟ وَّآمَنُوا۟ وَعَمِلُوا۟ ٱلصَّـٰلِحَتِ ثُمَّ ٱتَّقَوا۟ وَّآمَنُوا۟ ثُمَّ ٱتَّقَوا۟ وَّأَحْسَنُوا۟ ۗ وَٱللَّهُ يُحِبُّ ٱلْمُحْسِنِينَ ‎⟫
> *There is no sin upon the believers who do good deeds in what they have eaten, so long as they fear Allah and believe and do righteous deeds, then fear Allah and believe, then fear Allah and do good. And Allah loves those who do good.*

It is narrated from the Commander of the Faithful (may Allah be pleased with him) that when Allah forbade the consumption of intoxicants (*um al-Khabāʾith*, the mother of impurities), the Messenger of Allah condemned the drinker in detail—indeed, the sellers, buyers, bearers, those who guide others to the tavern, and those who drink. At that time, many people had embraced Islam but continued to consume alcohol, not deeming it forbidden, and they passed away before the prohibition was revealed. After Allah's prohibition of alcohol, the Companions of the Prophet came to him and said: "O Messenger of Allah, you have spoken of the impurity of intoxicants. Tell us: what of our brothers who drank and consumed much of it, and died in that state—before it was forbidden to them?" [^cite-72]

This verse was revealed in response to that very question. Thus Allah pardoned those who drank *um al-Khabāʾith* yet were believers and doers of good—those who committed no corruption or sedition in religion, who were true lovers of the Prophet, and whose sole reason for drinking was that it lay pleasingly upon their palate and it was their beloved drink. Moreover, it had not yet been formally forbidden. Allah accepted their faith, piety, and benevolence, and gave them the glad tidings that Allah loves those who excel in goodness.

In conclusion, we entreat Allah Almighty to protect us from error and oversight, and to grant us and all believers success in word and deed through the Messenger of Allah, Muhammad (peace and blessings be upon him).

Praise be to Allah, who has opened to us the paths of guidance through following the Imāms and has saved us, by their blessings, from wandering and going astray. May Allah Almighty bestow blessings and prayers upon Muḥammad (peace be upon him), who was clothed in the finest raiment and wrapped in the most blessed mantle—he upon whom revelation descended and whom the angels aided—and upon his successor, who on the night of migration rested upon the Prophet's bed, preserved the Prophet's life, and sacrificed his own. His name is ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib (peace be upon him), foremost in obedience to his Lord, surpassed by none. And may peace be upon the pure Imāms whose lovers have become righteous in religion, in this world, and in the Hereafter, while their enemies have become wretched. And may peace be upon our Master, Imām Ṭayyib Abū al-Qāsim, Commander of the Believers, who stands at the threshold of the third cycle of Imāms. May Allah Almighty send blessings and peace upon all of them until the Day of Judgment. Allah suffices us; He is the excellent Guardian, the excellent Master, and the excellent Helper.

*(From Risāla 20, *Qawal wa-al-Jawāb bi-al-Ṭawīl*, as transmitted by Sayyidnā ʿAlī ibn Sayyidnā Sulaymān—may Allah be pleased with him)*

## The Hereafter Is Better for You Than the First

Allah declares (interpreting the meaning):

> ⟪quran 93:4⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَلَلْآخِرَةُ خَيْرٌۭ لَّكَ مِنَ ٱلْأُولَىٰ ‎⟫
> *And the Hereafter is better for you than the first.* [^cite-73]

That is, what is to come surpasses what has passed. In one sense, the Messenger of Allah (peace and blessings be upon him) was assured by this verse: Those who came to you before—ʿĀʾisha and Ḥafṣa—do not grieve over them, for those who shall come to you—Umm Salamah—are superior to them. She shall serve you abundantly and shall be a source of peace to your heart.

In a higher sense, these two verses indicate that the virtues of the Master of the Universe shall be transformed after two cycles (*dawr*), and he shall emerge as the Qāʾim—the Triumphant Restorer. Furthermore, it is intimated that the *ʿAyn al-ʿUlya* (the higher essence) shall be superior in rank to the *ʿAyn* (the essence). The *Mīm* of the Ākhirah (the Hereafter), which shall manifest before the establishment of the Mahdī, shall be higher than the *Mīm* of the *Awwal* (the first), by which the seven *Aqlāl* (the seven intellects) shall come under the dominion of His obedience. [^cite-74]

The series of Imāms who preceded the Prophet (peace and blessings be upon him) was called the *Aba* (the fathers); the series of Imāms after him until the Day of Judgment is called the *Abnāʾ* (the sons). Therefore, the meaning of "And the Hereafter is better for you than the first" is that the Imāms of the period of the Qāʾim—the *Abnāʾ*—shall surpass the Imāms of the period of Ibrāhīm—the *Abā*.

In yet another sense, the final seventy years of this cycle surpass the earlier sixty-eight years of the same cycle. This is because the glory of the Qāʾim, who appears at the end of this seventy, shall be capable of integrating all the forms of former revelation (*kashf*) and the seventy—which the Qur'an designates as the *Mīqāt* (the appointed time):

> ⟪quran 7:143⟫
> ⟪ar:‏فَلَمَّا تَجَلَّىٰ رَبُّهُۥ لِلْجَبَلِ جَعَلَهُۥ دَكًّۭا وَخَرَّ مُوسَىٰ صَاعِقًۭا ۚ فَلَمَّآ أَفَاقَ قَالَ سُبْحَنَٰكَ تُبْتُ إِلَيْكَ وَأَنَا۟ أَوَّلُ ٱلْمُؤْمِنِينَ ‎⟫

He is called *Mālik Yawm al-Dīn* (Master of the Day of Judgment) in Sūrat al-Fātiḥa. If a world exists in the worlds, it is the distant religion (*dīn*). That is, after this final seventy, the era of revelation that begins shall be possessed by the Qāʾim ʿAlī—*Dhikr al-Salām* (the Remembrance of Peace)! Thus shall your sovereignty continue through your son. [^cite-75]

*(From the Lessons of the Prophetic Tradition, as transmitted by Sayyidnā Ḥusayn ibn Jadīn)*

*(From the Lessons in the City of Allah, as transmitted by Mullā Ghulām Ḥusayn)*

## Piety Is Not to Enter Houses from Behind Them

Allah declares (interpreting the meaning):

> ⟪quran 2:189⟫
> ⟪ar:‏ يَسْـَٔلُونَكَ عَنِ ٱلْأَهِلَّةِ ۖ قُلْ هِىَ مَوَقِيتُ لِلنَّاسِ وَٱلْحَجِّ ۗ وَلَيْسَ ٱلْبِرُّ بِأَن تَأْتُوا۟ ٱلْبُيُوتَ مِن ظُهُورِهَا وَلَكِنَّ ٱلْبِرَّ مَنِ ٱتَّقَىٰ ۗ وَأْتُوا۟ ٱلْبُيُوتَ مِنْ أَبْوَبِهَا ۚ وَٱتَّقُوا۟ ٱللَّهَ لَعَلَّكُمْ تُفْلِحُونَ ‎⟫
> *They ask you about the new moons. Say: These are signs to mark fixed periods of time for mankind and for the pilgrimage. It is not piety that you enter houses from behind them, but piety is the quality of one who fears Allah. So enter houses through their proper doors, and fear Allah, so that you may prosper.*

It is well known that both the scholar and the ignorant person enter their homes through the doors. Yet it seems strange—indeed, useless and unnecessary—that the Qur'an, the Book of Guidance, should exhort and explain such a commonplace matter. This apparent triviality signals that "house" (*bayt*) and "door" (*bāb*) here do not refer to structures of brick and mortar, but rather denote spiritual realities: certain individuals who are the living houses and gates of Allah. The Prophet (peace and blessings be upon him) declared: ⟪ar-quote:أنا مدینۃ العلم و علی بابھا۔ فمن اراد العلم فلیات الباب⟫ "I am the city of knowledge, and ʿAlī is its gate. Whoever seeks knowledge must come through the gate." [^cite-76]

Thus it became evident that there existed but one gate—⟪ar:'باب علی'⟫ (the Gate of ʿAlī)—to enter the city of knowledge, which stands in contrast to the gate of Moses, called ⟪ar:'باب حطۃ'⟫ (the Gate of Humility), as Allah said:

> ⟪quran 2:58⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَإِذْ قُلْنَا ٱدْخُلُوا۟ هَذِهِ ٱلْقَرْيَةَ فَكُلُوا۟ مِنْهَا حَيْثُ شِئْتُمْ رَغَدًۭا وَٱدْخُلُوا۟ ٱلْبَابَ سُجَّدًۭا وَقُولُوا۟ حِطَّةٌۭ نَّغْفِرْ لَكُمْ خَطَيَكُمْ ۚ وَسَنَزِيدُ ٱلْمُحْسِنِينَ ‎⟫
> *And when We said: Enter this town and eat freely therein where you will, and enter the gate prostrating, and say, "Forgive us." We shall forgive you your sins and increase the reward of those who do good.* [^cite-77]

Just as the people of Moses refused to enter the gate—that is, they denied their successor Aaron and perverted the word ⟪ar:حطۃ⟫ into ⟪ar:حنطۃ⟫ (a word of mockery)—so too were they punished. Allah declares in this verse that the righteous and the God-fearing are those who enter the house (*bayt*) through the gate (*bāb*), not by breaching the wall. That is, whosoever acknowledges the declaration ⟪ar:'علی ولی اللہ'⟫ ("ʿAlī is the *walī* [guardian] of Allah")—affirming the *wilāyat* (guardianship) of ʿAlī as ⟪ar:'باب مدینہ العلم'⟫ (the Gate of the City of Knowledge)—speaks the truth! If this were not the meaning of the verse, what virtue and piety could arise from entering a house of brick and mortar through its door? [^cite-78]

Furthermore, the word ⟪ar:'بر'⟫ (piety) at the beginning of this verse and the word ⟪ar:'تقوی'⟫ (God-consciousness) in the middle are terms unrelated to one who enters through the outward door. The supreme piety, ⟪ar:'بر الوالدین'⟫ (*birr al-wālidayn*, kindness to parents), refers to spiritual parenthood. The religious parents are Muḥammad and ʿAlī, as the Prophet declared: ⟪ar-quote:أنا و أنت یا علی أبوین المومنین⟫ "You and I, O ʿAlī, are the parents of the believers." The Imām (peace be upon him) said: "God-consciousness (*taqwā*) is my religion and the religion of my father." He who lacks God-consciousness is indeed godless.

From this interpretation, it becomes luminously clear that true piety and righteousness (*birr*) belongs to one who enters the house of the Sharīʿah of Muḥammad through attachment to the *wilāyat* (guardianship) of ʿAlī—not to one who obeys the ordinances of the Law while rejecting ʿAlī or adhering to another as the fourth Caliph. Such a person resembles one who scales the wall rather than entering through the gate.

## The Six Days of Creation and Allah's Establishment Upon the Throne

Allah says in His Clear Book: "He created the universe in six days and then established Himself upon the Throne (on the seventh day)." What we reckon as a day is the Earth's complete orbit around the Sun. Yet the question arises: when Allah created the universe, neither sun nor earth existed. What then does the divine "day" signify? And why did He rest upon the Throne on the final day, when He Himself declares in His Book:

> ⟪quran 50:15⟫
> ⟪ar:‏أَفَعَيِينَا بِٱلْخَلْقِ ٱلْأَوَّلِ ۚ بَلْ هُمْ فِى لَبْسٍۢ مِّنْ خَلْقٍۢ جَدِيدٍۢ ‎⟫
> *Were We wearied by the first creation? Nay, they are in doubt about the new creation.*

And in the verse of the Throne (*Āyat al-Kursī*), He states:

> ⟪quran 2:255⟫
> ⟪ar:‏ٱللَّهُ لَآ إِلَهَ إِلَّا هُوَ ٱلْحَىُّ ٱلْقَيُّومُ ۚ لَا تَأْخُذُهُۥ سِنَةٌۭ وَلَا نَوْمٌۭ ۚ ‎⟫
> *Allah! There is no god but He, the Living, the Self-Sustaining. Neither slumber overtakes Him nor sleep.* [^cite-79]

The wise say: "The six days of Allah are the six words He has sent forth—the six prophets (nāṭiq, the speakers), and the six dimensions, for the human being is encompassed by six dimensions, with the seventh being the human himself, the essence of all; for without him, whose dimensions would there be?" [^cite-80]

| 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 | 5 | 6 | 7 |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Above | Below | Right | Left | Forward | Backward | Humanity |

Thus, because of the six dimensions surrounding the human form, Allah Almighty sent six messengers to mankind.

In the pattern of spiritual actualization, Adam descended from the upper dimension of humanity; Nūḥ came from the left dimension of humankind; Ibrāhīm from the rear dimension; Mūsā from the lower dimension (which faces Adam); ʿĪsā from the right dimension (which faces Nūḥ); and Muḥammad Muṣṭafā from the forward dimension (which is opposite to Ibrāhīm). [^cite-81]

All six prophets—these sacred messengers—came from the six dimensions of humanity and bore to people the message of deeds, promising them the recompense of those deeds. When six prophets arrived from six parts of the world to tell them of their obligations, this proves conclusively that no other prophet shall come after the Messenger of Allah. If anyone thereafter comes, it is He—the Qāʾim (the Triumphant Restorer)—who shall reward them and recompense them.

*The Seventh Dimension and Human Reality*

Just as the human being is encompassed by six dimensions—with himself as the seventh—the six dimensions testify to humanity's existence and acknowledge that the human exists, which is why we also exist. Similarly, Allah has sent six points (*nuqṭa*, the prophets) and their guardians (*wilāyat*) to guide the people, which point toward the existence and manifestation of the Qāʾim. They testify that had the manifestation of the Qāʾim not been the ultimate purpose, we would not have appeared in this world. [^cite-82]

When these utterances of Allah (peace be upon them) came, they spoke to the people various teachings, the inner meaning of which constituted an individual manifestation of the Sharīʿah—just as the walnut differs in form from the apple. Though both are fruits, the walnut possesses meanings not found in the apple. Therefore, when Allah sent a messenger to the people, He sent with each an interpreter (*asās*, foundation) who explained the esoteric meaning of his outward actions, so that it could not be said: "We were not given knowledge of the inward reality."

These messengers—the prophets or speakers (*nuṭaqāʾ*)—conveyed the outward teachings, while those who explained their meanings were the foundations (*asāsiyyūn*) or witnesses (*shuhudāʾ*). Thus Ādam (peace be upon him) was succeeded by Mawlānā Shīsh; Nūḥ by Mawlānā Sām; Hārūn became the foundation of Mūsā; Shimʿūn (*Shamʿūn*) the successor of ʿĪsā; and Mawlānā ʿAlī al-Murtaḍā the successor of Muḥammad Muṣṭafā (peace and blessings be upon them).

*The Relationship Between Speaker and Foundation*

During the lifetime of the *Nāṭiq* (the Speaker/Prophet), his successor is designated *Asās* (Foundation); after the death of the *Nāṭiq*, he becomes the *Wāṣī* (Executor/Testamentary Guardian). Mawlānā ʿAlī was the *Asās* of the Messenger of Allah during his life and became the *Wāṣī* (the supreme executor of the Prophet's legacy) after his death. [^cite-83]

*The Seven Days of the Week*

It is related that the Messenger of Allah commenced the creation of the universe on Sunday, completed it on Friday, and rested on Saturday. Moreover, Allah Almighty declares in the Holy Qur'an:

> ⟪quran 3:184⟫
> ⟪ar:‏فَإِن كَذَّبُوكَ فَقَدْ كُذِّبَ رُسُلٌۭ مِّن قَبْلِكَ جَآءُو بِٱلْبَيِّنَتِ وَٱلزُّبُرِ وَٱلْكِتَبِ ٱلْمُنِيرِ ‎⟫
> *If they deny you, so were messengers denied before you, who came with clear proofs, scriptures, and the enlightening Scripture.* [^cite-84]

In Arabic, the word *yawm* (day) encompasses both daylight and night. Only the daylight portion is called *nahār*, and only the night is called *layl*. Thus when Allah uses the word *yawm* in this verse, it signifies the complete cycle of day and night.

Just as there are seven days in a week—with six days interspersed between successive weeks—so too did all these *nuqat* (prophetic points) appear in the interval between two cycles, like these six days:

| Saturday | Sunday | Monday | Tuesday | Wednesday | Thursday | Friday | Saturday |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Qāʾim of Fatarah | Adam | Noah | Abraham | Moses | Jesus | Muhammad | Qāʾim of Satrah |
| | Shīsh | Sām | Ismāʿīl | Aaron | Shimʿūn | ʿAlī | Mahdī |

Likewise, within each period, there exist seven positions between the two prophets, each explaining the inward meaning and outward actions:

| Saturday | Sunday | Monday | Tuesday | Wednesday | Thursday | Friday | Saturday | Sunday |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Speaker | Establishment | Guardian | Witnessing | Substitution | Pilgrimage | Seclusion | Ascetic | Speaker |

This is because the law of Adam was advanced and perfected by Moses; the law of Noah was advanced by Jesus; the law of Abraham was advanced by Muḥammad Muṣṭafā. For this reason, the invocation of blessings (*durūd*) is sent equally upon Muḥammad and upon Abraham and his family:

By invoking the names of both in the *Durūd*, the believer prays: "O Allah, join the temple of Abraham and the family of Abraham in the same manner that You have joined the temple of Muḥammad and the family of Muḥammad." [^cite-85]
