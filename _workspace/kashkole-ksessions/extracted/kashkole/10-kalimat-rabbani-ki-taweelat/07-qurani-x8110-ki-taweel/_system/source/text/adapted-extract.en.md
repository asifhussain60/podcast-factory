## Sura al-Nās (The People)

The interpretation of Sura al-Nās proceeds from an understanding of refuge and protection as fundamental spiritual principles. When the Qur'an opens with "Say: I seek refuge in the Lord of mankind," the words themselves bear layers of meaning accessible through esoteric exegesis (taʾwīl). The term *awādha* (refuge) derives from protection; one who seeks refuge from Satan invokes the formula "I seek refuge in God from Satan the accursed," yet here a different preposition appears—one indicating shelter beneath authority and guidance.

The letter *bāʾ* (in the phrase "by the Lord") represents the Prophet Muhammad, peace be upon him; the point of that letter signifies Amīr al-Muʾminīn ʿAlī, peace be upon him. Thus, the supplicant draws protection under Muhammad and ʿAlī, peace be upon them both, from the false beliefs and malice of those who, though appearing as friends, work in secret to mislead believers.[^cite-1] The "people" (*al-nās*) denotes those indifferent to the summons toward truth, while "their Lord" refers to those who nurture and educate them—that is, the one from whom the God-loving people receive spiritual cultivation.

In another dimension, *Malik al-Nās* (King of the People) embodies the very essence of the Messenger of God, who served as Imām during the Prophet's lifetime. The *Mustawda* (the Trustee) signifies the divine custodian, while the people of the country represent those under his care. When the sūra declares "Refuge in the true God of mankind," it points toward the station of the *Qāʾim* (the Establisher, the final Imam), whose station represents the highest invocation of the divine name.[^cite-2]

The "evil of the sneaking whisperer" (*sharr al-waswāsa al-khannās*) refers to those hidden enemies—the hypocrites and the wicked whose doubts and insinuations lodge secretly in believers' hearts, seeking their misguidance. Though such adversaries pose as allies, they inflict the gravest harm. Whether these tempters emerge from the ranks of the *jinn* (the unseen intelligences) or from humankind, refuge is sought from their designs.[^cite-3]

## Sura al-Takāthur (The Rivalry in Abundance)

The true interpretation of Sura al-Takāthur emerged when a scholar of the *Ahl al-Bayt* (the People of the House) corrected a superficial reading. A conventional exegete had explained the verse as a rebuke for worldly abundance and luxury making one heedless of God's remembrance—a reading so shallow that it overlooked a fundamental distinction. The scholar interrupted: if people are buried by force, not by choice, how can they be said to "visit" the graves? The Arabic term *ziyārah* signifies a voluntary meeting; one visits and then departs, whereas the buried do not return. This correction silenced the former interpreter and moved him to seek the deeper meaning.

Man becomes immersed in greed, anger, lust, desire, and prolonged hope—so entirely caught up in the multitude of worldly pursuits that he forgets himself and neglects all attention to the Hereafter. The person brought to the tomb experiences death, which halts all movement; the corpse is washed, dressed in a strange shroud, and buried in the grave. The earth consumes the beauty of the body and disturbs its collection, even destroying its very marks. No one can rescue him from these horrific and agonizing things; none can forestall them.

Yet contrasting this compulsory physical death stands a voluntary and spiritual death. The Prophet, peace be upon him, declared: "Die before you die." After one accepts this spiritual death, the heart is lifted from all worldly pleasures. Through the water of religious knowledge, impurity is purged; through the hands, the clean white garment of piety is donned.[^cite-4] The Prophet also taught: "The grave is either one of the wells of Paradise or one of the pits of Hellfire." Those who love the Guardian of God attach themselves to Him willingly and are made so eager for the Hereafter that they regard all worldly pleasures as nothing. They become ready to bear every trouble; while yet in this world, they are no longer of this world. God educates and trains them for the Hereafter such that thousands of noble qualities develop within them day by day.

The grave—which is the sanctuary of the *Riyāḍ al-Janna* (the Gardens of Paradise)—stands as the supreme boundary for every believer after apparent death. Whoever loves ʿAlī and his descendants will be with them after death; whoever is their enemy will share their fate. This is why the Prophet, peace be upon him, said: "The Resurrection accompanies love of God. Whomever a person loves, he shall be gathered with them on the Day of Judgment."

Knowledge comprises two kinds. The first is auditory knowledge ('*ilm al-samāʿ*)—that which we have learned from the Prophets and their successors through the Book of God. This rests not on direct observation but solely on hearing and report; the believer accepts the Prophets and God's Book as true, and no intimacy surpasses that trust. The second knowledge derives from the sciences of divine unity (*Tawhīd*) and understanding of Origin and Return.

Hell too is witnessed twice, corresponding to these two modes of knowledge. First, the believer comes to know disbelievers and polytheists and identifies those whom God has cursed—this is knowledge by report. Second, after death, he beholds these hells with his own eyes and knows that Hell is as real as Heaven. What was transmitted to him proves true. Today, Hell is glimpsed beyond the veil; tomorrow, when the curtain lifts and facts become plain, the faces of Hell will be manifest, and eye-witness certainty will settle upon him.[^cite-5]

The blessings about which God will inquire are the sweet nectar—knowledge and wisdom—that flows from the tongues of the Prophets and God's saints. God will ask: "Why did you abandon Our saints and take up with Our enemies? Why did you turn from the springs of wisdom? Why did you forsake the waters of *Kawther* and the Water of Life to drink the scalding water of Hell that burns the bowels and tears them to shreds?"

When Mawlānā Ḥasan al-Ḥākim Bāmarullāh, the sixteenth Imām, was asked about *Na'īm* (divine bliss), the Messenger of God replied that *Na'īm* is the cool water given to people on an intensely hot day. God has made His blessings known to His servants; it would be unworthy of His glory to withhold them. When God announces a blessing, why would He question it on that day? Because God has made the fulfillment of duties itself a blessing that will be questioned. He has bestowed this blessing; now it is your duty to fulfill it.

Those who excel in duties and have been made noble are clothed in obedience. These duties were brought to demonstrate obedience. *Taʿat al-Nadāmah* (obedience born of regret) marks the turning point. Amīr al-Muʾminīn ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib and his descendants, the Imāms of their age, are *al-Na'īm* (the divine blessing); those who stand against them and usurp their rights are *al-Jahīm* (Hellfire).[^cite-6]

## Sura al-Tīn (The Fig)

A companion of Mawlānā Imām Jaʿfar al-Ṣādiq narrated that while reciting the Qur'an, he pondered the oaths in Sura al-Walīn and found them bewildering. The mention of such seemingly trivial things troubled him; doubts arose in his heart. After days of reflection, unable to find peace, he approached Mawlānā Imām and confessed: "My parents are sacrificed for you! A whisper has arisen in my heart which I have come to dispel. God swears by figs and olives—but what virtue lies in such ordinary fruits, eaten and discarded or rotting away? An oath by something great brings meaning; swearing by trifles renders the oath meaningless. What quality distinguishes these two fruits that the Almighty chose them?"

Mawlānā Imām replied: "Surely you have posed a most important question. I shall answer you, but take heed: do not divulge this wisdom to the unworthy."

He then asked: "What do you take these two things to be?" The companion answered: "They are both fruits." The Imām said: "Indeed, they are fruits, yet herein lies their entire world. God distinguished these two fruits with His transcendence and immanence—with the heavens and the earth. He caused them to grow through His knowledge and wisdom; He cast upon them the shadow of His Throne. These two fruits are in truth Adam and Noah.

"Why," asked the companion, "did God compare them to fruits? Are they not consumed and digested, passing as waste?" The Imām replied: "Do you think the fruits of your sight are those alone which are consumed and digested and come out as refuse? No, no! These are the fruits of Paradise—no matter how much you take from them, they never diminish. They resemble a lamp; by its light you may kindle lamp after lamp, and it sustains no loss. What is the fruit of a tree? Its essence! Both of these are the essence of the world![^cite-7]

"As to why Adam is likened to the fig and Noah to the olive: every fruit ordinarily grows with leaves and buds on the bush, then flowers form, and flowers become fruit. But the fig appears directly upon the branches as fruit. Similarly, every human ordinarily requires a womb and gestation before birth. Contrary to these natural principles, Adam, peace be upon him, emerged from dust itself. Like the fig, he was created without preceding conception. Therefore, God likened him to the fig.

"Moreover, beyond Adam, the appearance of the fig also alludes to the *Mahdī* (the Guided One at the Resurrection), for they too shall appear suddenly, without prior signs.[^cite-8]

"As for why Noah is compared to the olive: the olive fruit is consumed in small quantities; its fruit—that is, oil—is mostly used. The purpose of the olive, as it were, is its oil. Likewise, Noah's summons (the olive) is the essence of Abraham, who appeared among Noah's descendants. For this reason, Abraham's call spread and flourished more than Noah's. The word 'olive,' together with Noah's oath, carries implicit and hidden meaning."

Regarding "Ṭūr Sīnīn" (Mount Sinai): The companion asked: "What does the type of Mount Sinai signify?" The Imām answered: "Tur Sinai is the place where Moses, peace be upon him, supplicated and received prophethood. A secret lies hidden within it—like the olive—which is Jesus Christ. God says (in interpretation):

⟪quran 28:29⟫

"Jesus Christ is the genealogy that originated from Mount Sinai, from which the religion of Moses emerged. A subtle meaning appears in this verse when God says that this tree 'yields oil.' No tree produces oil upon the surface; oil emerges only when the fruit is crushed into essence. Similarly, that tree is the essence of Jesus Christ. By 'yielding oil,' it signifies that Jesus Christ presented the truths, meanings, and summaries of the Torah revealed to Moses. In this verse, God makes a subtle reference to these truths and subtleties.[^cite-9]

"God exalted Jesus, peace be upon him. Jesus is both a tree in one sense and a Word (*kalimah*) in another, and God says (in interpretation):

⟪quran 24:35⟫

"God compared one to the other because of the great strength between them—the Word of Ṭayyibah (the Pure) and the Tree of Ṭayyibah."

Regarding "Bilād al-Amīn" (the Secure City): The companion said: "From your continuation, it seems clear that Bilād al-Amīn refers to the Holy Prophet, peace and blessings be upon him." The Imām replied: "Yes! Bilād al-Amīn—Mecca—is the first Qiblah of God, canceling all other directions of prayer. It was the first house built upon earth; the first Imām was born there, as God Himself says:

⟪quran 3:96⟫

"God made this house the dwelling of the final Prophet. This type too contains a secret like the previous two. For both the Messenger of God and the Day of Resurrection were sent together; there is no gap between them in time. Therefore, the Messenger of God, peace and blessings be upon him, joined the fingers of both his hands and said: 'Prophethood and Resurrection have been sent together.' God made pilgrimage obligatory upon His first house and made it a sign for His final Prophet—itself a sign of the Resurrection.[^cite-10]

"Whoever performs pilgrimage sincerely undertakes true pilgrimage, accepts God's call, and attains the rank of piety. He shall walk with the inhabitants of Paradise, to whom the angels will bring glad tidings:

⟪quran 41:30⟫

**Why God Conceals These Oaths in Allegories**

Upon hearing this commentary, the companion exclaimed: "By God! You have shown this oath of God to be immense!" He then asked: "O Master, one question remains: why did God conceal these great oaths in allegories instead of explaining them plainly so that all people could grasp them easily?"

Mawlānā Imām said: "In these words lies the hidden wisdom of God—the concealing of this great oath within the grain inside the husk and the fruit within thick skin. The purpose is that insects should not spoil the fruit, and that only the noblest of God's creatures—humans—should receive it, and none else. Similarly, the reason for concealing meaning within apparent words is that these true sciences should not reach the hands of the undeserving and the incompetent (who resemble insects), mixing their opinions and conjectures with them and creating distortion. Rather, the believers—those with insight and knowledge—should benefit from them. Thus the virtue of those who seek knowledge becomes evident to the ignorant. God says (in interpretation):

⟪quran 2:26⟫

## Sura al-Nāziʿāt (Those Who Tear Out)

Just as human organs are assigned different functions, so too the servants of the *daʿwa* (the summons, the divine invitation) have been entrusted with various invitational services. Before bread is eaten, it comes to hand; the hand delivers it to the mouth. The teeth chew; the tongue salivates; the jaw supports; the throat swallows. The same principle governs developments in the *Daʿwa al-Hādiyya* (the Rightly-Guided Summons). The organs of religion—the servants of religion—each perform their own work. The believer who accepts the summons goes to the *Mustajīb* (the Responder), who in the example of a tooth cuts away and removes the immorality of his beliefs. The *Mazūnīn* (those given permission) provide him knowledge and understanding (the saliva), so that gradually he acquires spiritual and intellectual nourishment.[^cite-11]

The Qur'an indicates this through specific verses. The angels *Al-Nāziʿāt* are those who bring out deeply. The *Nāshiṭāt* are those who bind the knots. The *al-Sābihāt* are those who swim. The *al-Sābiqāt* are those who move forward or increase. *al-Mudabbirāt* (the Regulators) are the wise ordering force. Here are the five stages of spiritual discernment.

For this reason, one of Mawlānā ʿAlī's titles is *al-ʿArīḍ al-Bāṭin* (He of the Unseen Depths). Its apparent meaning is a person whose head is bald and whose belly protrudes outward. The true and intrinsic meaning is that there is no position above you (*ʿarzah*) and that you are the greatest scholar of inner knowledge (*al-bāṭin*).[^cite-12]

*Nāziʿāt* refers to the *Muqassirīn* (the Extractors), whose function is to draw forth the hidden scholarly contents from the depths of apparent rulings and verses. *Nāshiṭāt* refers to the *Mazūnīn* who take a vow (also called the *ʿaqīdīn*—the Pledged Ones)—those about to make a commitment. *Al-Sābihāt* are the *Duʿāt* (Summoners) who plumb the subjects of knowledge. *Al-Sābiqāt* are the *Duʿāʾ* (the Invitation) itself—those who have increased in insight go forward in ranks. The *Mudabbirāt* (the Regulators) are responsible for the Imām's execution of authority. And the *Faʿʿāl limā turīd* (the Author of all He wills) is the Imām.

| Extractors | Pledged Ones | Swimmers | Precedents | Regulators |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Muqassirīn | Mazūnīn | Duʿāt | Daʿwa | Imām |

These ranks have always existed. The Prophet, peace be upon him, too came from heaven. In the Prophet's time, there were scholars and other noble figures from whom he learned, and he said: "I have taken from five, given to five, and for me and my Lord, there are five." This too indicates the orientation of religion.[^cite-13]

Similarly, Jesus declared that whoever does not have two births cannot enter the kingdom of Heaven. By the second birth is meant entry into the age of the noble and advancement in the rank of knowledge. Degrees grow from the acquisition of inner knowledge. When a seed or grain is sown, how the gardener or farmer protects it! Proper space, proper water, proper air, proper fertilizer—all are cared for. In the same manner, the cultivators and gardeners of the faith are tending and cultivating their believers, seeking their progress and eternal felicity.

## Sura al-Qadr (The Night of Decree)

The example of *Laylat al-Qadr* (the Night of Decree) is Mawlānā Fāṭimah al-Zahrāʾ, peace be upon her; and in certain senses, Mawlānā Muḥammad ibn Aḥmad ibn Ṭayyab, the twenty-third Imām. In another dimension, Mawlānā ʿAlī, peace be upon him, also embodies this night.

In the interpretive circle of Mawlānā Fāṭimah, *al-Qadr* (the Decree, the Might) refers to her father, Muḥammad Muṣṭafā, peace and blessings be upon him. In the interpretive circle of Mawlānā Muḥammad ibn Aḥmad ibn Ṭayyab, *al-Qadr* refers to his father, Mawlānā Aḥmad, peace be upon him, author of the *Alif*—a collection of one thousand years of the Qur'an. This is why a thousand is mentioned in this Sura. In fact, this signifies that the spiritual treasures were given to Mawlānā Aḥmad from the station of Muḥammad, and alludes to the gifts that Muḥammad Muṣṭafā bestowed upon Mawlānā Fāṭimah as a gift.[^cite-14]

The Messenger of God, peace and blessings be upon him, did not openly reveal the virtue of Mawlānā Fāṭimah because she dwelt in the palace of the night. This is the twenty-second night and lies at the heart of the whole year, just as Sura *Yā-Sīn* is the heart of the Qur'an. In another circle, *Layl* (the Night) is Mawlānā Fāṭimah, and *al-Qadr* is Mawlānā ʿAlī.

We sent him down during the time of darkness—the seventy days. And what do you know of the station of Fāṭimah daughter of Muḥammad, peace be upon him? The testimony of Mawlānā Muḥammad ibn Aḥmad is greater than a thousand testimonies.

Yet there is a difference between Fāṭimah and this testimony. Here the month refers to the Imām and his thirty witnesses. There are thirty days in a month; each day is a witness. Just as the month gathers the whole thirty days, the Imām gathers all thirty Witnesses (*Ḥujjaj*). That is, Fāṭimah is greater than a thousand such witnesses. In it, the day is the Imām and the night is the Imām's witness.[^cite-15]

The ultimate motivation for this night is the great Trustee (*Muḥtasib*) of the Universe, who does so at the behest of the *Ashir* (the Tenth). This process of handing the decrees from top to bottom has always continued and shall continue eternally. Each passes to the next; the last to the first. This continuous process persists throughout the night—throughout the seventy periods. It does not take long to sleep; delay serves only waste.

The first is the great proof of the Mighty One. Mawlānā Aḥmad received a collection from the Holy Prophet. Apart from this, every Muḥammad always receives from Muḥammad, and every ʿAlī from ʿAlī.[^cite-17]

*Peace* is greeting from all sides. *Salām* refers to the decrees themselves. That is, by the command of the *Ashir*, the decrees are sent throughout the night—throughout all periods—until the time of *Fajr* arrives. That is, the work of sending decrees shall continue until the coming of the *Qāʾim* (the Establisher, the final Imām). In another sense, this process continues until Mawlānā attaches to Fāṭimah's Trustee (*Muḥtasib*), Aḥmad the Guided (*Maḥmūd*), and obtains her succession (*Khilāfah*).
