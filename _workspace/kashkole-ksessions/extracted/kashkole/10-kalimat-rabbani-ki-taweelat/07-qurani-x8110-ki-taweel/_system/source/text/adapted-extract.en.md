## Sūrat al-Nās

The opening of this sūra is an address from God to the Prophet, commanding him to seek refuge — *aʿūdhu*, "I take shelter." The form of this refuge deserves close attention. When the Qurʾān ordinarily commands recourse against Satan, it employs the preposition *min* ("from"), as in *aʿūdhu bi-Llāhi mina al-shayṭāni al-rajīm*. In Sūrat al-Nās, however, the preposition is *bi-* — "by," "through," "under the shelter of." According to the taʾwīl (esoteric exegesis) of the daʿwa (the summons), the letter *bāʾ* itself signifies the Holy Prophet, and the dot beneath it signifies Amīr al-Muʾminīn ʿAlī. To say *aʿūdhu bi-rabbi al-nās* is therefore to enter beneath the shelter of Muḥammad and ʿAlī, away from the errors of false belief and from the malice of their adversaries.

In this reading, *al-nās* — "the people" — does not denote humanity at large but those who remain indifferent to the call to truth. *Rabb al-nās* signifies the one who nurtures and trains them. The clause therefore means: I take refuge with the one through whom the lovers of religion are formed.

*Malik al-nās* — "King of mankind" — designates the inner reality of the Messenger of Allah, for he is the Imām of the Prophet in the order of intellectual descent. Here *rabb al-nās* points to the Mustawdaʿ (the entrusted, deposited bearer), and *malik al-nās* to the Mustaqarr (the settled, permanent bearer of the rank).

*Ilāh al-nās* — "God of mankind" — names the refuge of the true God of the believing community, which is the station of the Qāʾim (the one who shall arise). Throughout the sūra the name *Allāh* belongs to the highest station alone.

| --- | --- | --- |
| Mustawdaʿ | Mustaqarr | Qāʾim |

The shelter sought is *min sharri al-waswāsi al-khannās alladhī yuwaswisu fī ṣudūri al-nās*. The *khannās* — "the one who slinks away" — refers to invisible figures: the hypocrites and the corrupt, who covertly cast doubts into the hearts of believers in order to mislead them. They wear the face of friendship while inflicting the most lasting harm. From these the believer seeks shelter.

*Mina al-jinnati wa-l-nās* — "whether from the jinn or from mankind" — indicates that this corruption may issue from the hidden agents as readily as from the visible ones.

## Sūrat al-Takāthur

An outward-facing scholar — a recognized exegete of the Holy Qurʾān — was once delivering his commentary on Sūrat al-Takāthur to his circle. By chance, a scholar from the Ahl al-Bayt passed by and sat down to listen. The commentator was explaining: "God says that the abundance of family and luxury has made you rebellious against His obedience and has erased His remembrance from your hearts. You did not turn to God even when you forgot yourselves — until you visited the graves. There you saw that neither children, nor great households, nor wealth, nor rank availed you."

The Ahl al-Bayt scholar interrupted him. "O man, speak the truth. God is true, His word is true, and He loves the truthful. Tell me — do people go to the graves of their own will and are then buried there, or are they carried away by force when death overtakes them?" The commentator answered, "Of course they are carried by force — no one is buried in joy." The scholar replied, "Then I see that they did not *visit* the graves, for the meaning of *ziyāra* is to meet, and a visit is an act of free will, not of compulsion. The visitor, moreover, returns from his visit; the one buried in the grave does not." This small objection turned the commentator's face yellow, and he fell silent. Afterward the man followed the scholar and said, "O shaykh, you have called me out with the tongue of truth, and I have heard you. I was in confusion myself — you have startled me. Now tell me what the taʾwīl of this sūra truly is." Upon receiving his pledge, the scholar gave him the following commentary[^cite-1].

The person who is carried to the tomb is taken there by death, which arrests his every motion. His body is bathed and clothed in unfamiliar garments — the shroud — and laid in the grave, which devours the beauty of his body, disperses his composure, and erases his very traces. From these terrors and pains no one can save him, and no one can stay them.

Against this forced death stands another — a voluntary and spiritual dying, of which the Prophet said: "Die before you die." When a person accepts this spiritual death, his heart is lifted from every worldly pleasure. He is bathed in the water of religious knowledge, which purifies the soil of his doubt, and clothed by his own hands in the pure garment of piety. The Prophet also said: "The grave is either one of the gardens of Paradise or one of the pits of Hell." Its guardians of Paradise are the Friends of God; its pit of Hell is their enemy. If a person attaches himself by his own choice to the Friend of God and takes refuge in him, the Friend so kindles his longing for the world to come that he comes to regard all the pleasures of this world as nothing and grows ready to bear every affliction it may impose. While still in this world he no longer belongs to it; the Friend educates and trains him for the Hereafter, so that thousands of qualities flower in him day by day.

### A turn

The grave that is one of the gardens of paradise — the Riyāḍ al-Janna — is the highest station to which every believer ascends after his outward death. The enemies of ʿAlī and his descendants, together with those who love them, will likewise be joined to them after death. As the Prophet said: "Resurrection is according to love." Whomever a person loves, with him will be his end.

As God says, the grain that issues from the earth and divides into many shoots will yield for the worshipper a reward multiplied many times over:

The mention of seeing the Fire is twofold, as the two modes of knowledge are twofold. The first sight of Hell is the recognition of disbelievers and polytheists in this world, the identification of those whom God has cursed. The second is after death, when the believer will see these hells with his own eye and see that Hell is as real as Heaven, and know that what was said was not in error. The blessings concerning which God will inquire are the sweet draught — the knowledge and wisdom that flow from the tongues of the Prophets and the Friends of God. God will ask, "Why did you leave Our Friends and turn to Our enemies? Why did you turn away from the words of wisdom? Why did you abandon the water of al-Kawthar and the water of life and seek instead the boiling water of Hell that scorches the entrails and tears them to pieces?"

When Mawlānā al-Ḥasan al-Ḥākim bi-Amr Allāh (the sixteenth Imām) was questioned about *al-naʿīm*, he answered with the Prophet's reply: that *al-naʿīm* is the cool water given to thirsty travellers on a day of fierce heat. God has explained His blessings to His servants, and it would be against His glory to withhold them. Yet if God has announced a blessing, why does He still question concerning it on that Day? Because the question is about the duties of gratitude that the blessing imposes. He has bestowed the gift; it remains for us to fulfill its claims.

The duty made noblest, and ennobled by His decree, is our obedience. These duties have been instituted that we might show obedience — and so the act of *ṭāʿa* (obedience) becomes, in truth, an expression of *nadāma* (regret): regret for what was not given when it should have been.

A companion of Mawlānā Imām Jaʿfar al-Ṣādiq relates: While reading the Qurʾān, I pondered the oaths at the head of Sūrat al-Tīn and found them strange. It seemed to me curious that God should swear by such modest objects, and doubts began to gather in my heart. I weighed the question for several days, but found no rest. Compelled at last, I came to the Imām and said: "May my parents be ransomed for you. A whisper has risen in my heart, and I have come to dispel it. God in His word swears by figs and olives, and I cannot understand it. What greatness lies in swearing by such trifling things — things that are eaten and thrown away, or pass out as waste, or rot? An oath is sworn by something exalted; an oath upon trifles seems empty. What virtue lies in these two fruits, that the All-Mighty has sworn by them?"

Mawlānā the Imām said, "You have asked a great question, and I shall answer it. But take care that you do not speak the meaning of wisdom to those unworthy of it[^cite-2]."

### Al-Tīn wa-l-Zaytūn (the fig and the olive)

#### A turn

**Al-Tīn (the fig).** "Tell me," he said, "what do you suppose these two are?" I answered that they are both fruits. He said, "Indeed they are both fruits — but in these two fruits lies an entire world. God has distinguished them by His height and His depth, the heavens and the earth; He has caused them to grow with His knowledge and wisdom, and has cast over them the shadow of His Throne. These two fruits are in truth Ādam and Nūḥ."

"And why," I asked, "did God liken them to fruits? Are they eaten and digested?" He said, "Do you take them to be like the fruits of your common sight, eaten and passed out as waste? No — these are the fruits of Paradise. However much you take of them, they are not exhausted. They are like a lamp from which you may light as many lamps as you wish, with no diminishment of the first. Consider too what the fruit of any bush is: it is the very essence of the bush. These two are the essence of the world."

"And what," I asked, "is the reason that Ādam is likened to the fig, and Nūḥ to the olive?" He said, "Most fruit-bearing plants first send forth leaves and buds, then flowers, and only then assume the form of fruit. But the fig appears directly upon the branch already as fruit. So too every human birth requires a pregnancy and its stages — yet Ādam, against these natural principles, came forth from the dust itself, fashioned without antecedent like the fig upon its branch. For this God likened him to the fig."

#### A further turn

Besides Ādam, the appearance of the fig in this manner also signifies the Mahdī (the Qāʾim), for he too shall appear suddenly without prior signs.

#### Al-Zaytūn (the olive)

"And why is Nūḥ likened to the olive?" He said, "Because the olive itself is eaten only in small portions; its real fruit — the oil — is what is most widely used. The purpose of the olive is its oil. Likewise the daʿwa of Nūḥ — whose likeness is the olive — finds its essence in Ibrāhīm, who appeared from the descendants of Nūḥ. For this reason the daʿwa of Ibrāhīm spread further and flourished more than that of Nūḥ. The mention of the olive alongside Nūḥ's oath therefore carries within it a hidden meaning."

#### On the veiling of these meanings in ciphers

When I had heard this commentary, I said, "By God, you have shown the greatness of this oath of God. There remains, my lord, but one question: why did God keep these great oaths within these veils, and not declare them openly in plain language, so that all might understand?"

## Surat al-Nāziʿāt

Just as the bodily organs are each assigned to distinct tasks, so too the servants of the daʿwa (the summons / the invitation) have been entrusted with distinct invitational offices. A morsel of bread arrives at the hand before it is eaten; the hand's task is to deliver it to the mouth. The teeth then chew, the tongue moistens with saliva, the jaws sustain the mastication, and the throat conveys the morsel downward. The same patterning governs progress within the Daʿwa Hādiya (the rightly-guiding summons). The organs of religion — that is, the servants of religion — each perform their proper work. The believer who responds to the summons first approaches the mustajīb (the respondent), who stands in the place of the tooth: he severs and removes the corruptions of belief. The maʾdhūnūn (the licensed Summoners; sing. maʾdhūn) then furnish him with knowledge — the saliva of the analogy — and by these graded stages he gradually acquires spiritual nourishment.[^cite-3]

The nāziʿāt are the angels who extract with utmost force from the depths. The nāshiṭāt are the angels of binding. The sābiḥāt are those who swim. The sābiqāt — the precedents — are those who race forward, that is, who advance in rank. The mudabbirāt are those entrusted with executing the affair. These are the five graded stations of inner reflection.

### Branch

For this reason, one of the titles of Mawlānā ʿAlī is al-Aṣlaʿ al-Baṭīn. Its outward sense names a man whose head is bare of hair and whose belly is enlarged. Its principal and intrinsic meaning is that there is no rank above him (aṣlaʿ — the topmost, beyond which none stands) and that he is the greatest scholar of the inner knowledge (al-baṭin — the hidden).

Nāziʿāt refers to the mukāsir, whose function is to draw out the hidden scholarly contents from beneath the apparent rulings and verses. Nāshiṭāt refers to the maʾdhūnūn, who bind by oath the aspirants — the ʿahd-takers, those on the threshold of contracting the covenant. Sābiḥāt refers to the duʿāt (Summoners) who swim in the seas of knowledge. Sābiqāt refers to the dāʿī al-duʿāt (the chief of the Summoners), who, by virtue of his greater insight, advances in rank within the order. The mudabbirāt are those entrusted with executing the Imam's affair, and the master of that office is the Bāb.[^cite-4]

| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| mukāsir | maʾdhūnūn | duʿāt | dāʿī al-duʿāt | Bāb |

These rites have ever been transmissions from above. The Prophet (peace and blessings be upon him) likewise received from on high. In the time of the Holy Prophet there were monks and other figures of station from whom the Prophet (peace and blessings be upon him) drew instruction, and he said: "I have taken from five, given to five, and to me and to my Lord, there are five." This too points to the directionality of religious orientation.

In the same vein, Jesus says that whoever is not born twice cannot enter the kingdom of heaven. By the second birth is intended entry into the era of the noble and advancement in the ranks of knowledge. The ranks grow through the acquisition of inner knowledge. When a seed or grain is sown, how meticulously the gardener or the farmer protects it — proper soil, proper water, proper air, proper fertiliser — each is attended to. So too do the patrons and cultivators of the daʿwa tend their believers as their orchard, seeking our progress and our eternal felicity.

(*Nukhbat al-Kalām*) Sayyidnā Ghulām Ḥussain (may God be pleased with him)

## Surat al-Qadr

The exemplar of Laylat al-Qadr is Mawlātnā Fāṭima al-Zahrāʾ (peace be upon her), and in another sense Mawlānā Muḥammad b. Aḥmad b. Ṭayyib, the twenty-third Imam. In yet another sense, Mawlānā ʿAlī (peace be upon him) is also an embodiment of this Night.

In the interpretive circle of Mawlātnā Fāṭima, al-Qadr refers to her father, Muḥammad Muṣṭafā (peace be upon him); in the interpretive circle of Mawlānā Muḥammad b. Aḥmad b. Ṭayyib, al-Qadr refers to his father, Mawlānā Aḥmad (peace be upon him), the author of the Alif (a compendium spanning a thousand years of the Sura). It is for this reason that a thousand is mentioned in this Sura.

In substance, the verse signifies that spiritual treasures were transmitted to Mawlānā Aḥmad from the station of Muḥammad, and it alludes to the gifts which Muḥammad Muṣṭafā (peace be upon him) bestowed upon Mawlātnā Fāṭima.[^cite-5]

| Layla | Qadr |
| --- | --- |
| Mawlātnā Fāṭima | Muḥammad Muṣṭafā (peace be upon him) |
| Mawlānā Aḥmad | Mawlānā Muḥammad b. Aḥmad |
| Mawlātnā Fāṭima | ʿAlī (peace be upon him) |

The reason for this is that the Messenger of Allāh (peace and blessings be upon him) did not openly disclose Mawlātnā Fāṭima's true rank, for she was within the inner sanctum of the Night. This is the twenty-second night, lying at the heart of the entire year, just as Sūra Yā Sīn is the heart of the Qurʾān. In another interpretive circle, Layla is Mawlātnā Fāṭima and al-Qadr is Mawlānā ʿAlī.

"We sent it down" — that is, in the time of darkness (the seventy days). "And what shall make you know what the Night of Qadr is?" The verse points to Mawlātnā Fāṭima bint Muḥammad (peace be upon her). The proof of Mawlānā Muḥammad b. Aḥmad is better than a thousand proofs.

Yet there is a distinction between the case of Fāṭima and the present argument. Here "month" (shahr) signifies the Imam together with his thirty (30) ḥujjas (proofs); a month contains thirty days, each day standing for a proof. Just as the month gathers the entire thirty days, so the Imam gathers the entire thirty ḥujjas. That is, Fāṭima is better than a thousand such proofs. In this construal, the day stands for the Imam and the night for the Imam's ḥujja.

The chief mover of this Night is the Great Muḥtaqib of the Universe, who acts at the behest of al-Ashar. Salām is the greeting extended from all sides. Here Salām refers to the trumpets.
