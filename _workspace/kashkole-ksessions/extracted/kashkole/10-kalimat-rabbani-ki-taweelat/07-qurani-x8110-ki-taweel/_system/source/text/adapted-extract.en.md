# Interpretation of Quranic Suras

## Sūrat al-Nās (The People)

The Prophet, peace be upon him, addresses the believers in this sūrah, calling them to seek refuge in their Lord. The word "refuge" (awuz) carries profound significance in Islamic practice. When one seeks protection from Satan, the Qur'ān employs the formula "I seek refuge in God from Satan the accursed" (Aʿūdhu billāhi min al-shaytāni al-rajīm). Here, however, the preposition *bi-* (through/by means of) signals a different mode of seeking shelter: not mere escape, but spiritual attachment.

The Prophet himself embodies this refuge. The letter *Bā* represents the Holy Prophet Muḥammad, peace be upon him, while the point beneath it (*nuqta*) signifies Amīr al-Muʾminīn ʿAlī, peace be upon him. Thus, to seek refuge *bi-* them is to place oneself under their protection and guidance—to come under the guardianship of both the Messenger and the Imām, shielding oneself from the corrupting whispers of Satan, whether in the form of false belief or the usurping caliphs who deviated from the true path.[^cite-1]

The term "people" (*nās*) in this context does not denote mere populations. Rather, it refers to those who have turned away from the summons to truth—those indifferent to the *daʿwa* (the summons, the invitation). Their "Lord" (*Rabb*) is not simply a distant creator, but the one who nurtures, sustains, and spiritually trains them. To seek refuge in one's Lord is to seek refuge in the Guardian from whom all who love the religion receive their sustenance and instruction.

Three interpretive layers unfold across the sūrah's epithets:

**Rabb al-Nās** (Lord of the People): This refers to the one in whom resides the essence of the Messenger of God, for the Prophet himself was the Imām of all prophets. The *Mustawda* (the Trustee, the Hidden Imām) represents this nurturing, invisible guidance. The "people of the country" (*ahlu al-balad*) signify those who have been entrusted to this Trustee's care.

**Malik al-Nās** (King of the People): The kingship of the people is vested in the one whose reality is the Messenger of God, since he alone bore the seal of prophethood and the station of ultimate authority over the faithful.

**Ilāh al-Nās** (God of the People): The true God of mankind is none other than the One in whose worship lies the ultimate station—that of the *Qāʾim* (the Establisher, the eschatological Imām). Throughout this entire sūrah, the word "God" (*Allāh*) signifies the highest divine station.

The figure of "the Whisperer, the Slinking One" (*al-waswāsa al-khannās*) represents those hidden agents of corruption—the hypocrites and the wicked—who cast doubts and suspicions into the hearts of believers under the guise of friendship. They steal into the breasts of mankind, sowing discord and leading astray. These are the very enemies from whom the believer must seek refuge: not through mere words, but through heartfelt attachment to the true guardians.

Whether these whisperers come from the realm of jinn or from among humankind matters little; what matters is that the believer recognizes them and turns instead toward the Trustworthy Guardian, placing all trust in the one whose nature is divine guidance.

| Title | Interpretation | Corresponding Realm |
|-------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Rabb (Lord) | The Trustee / Hidden Guardian | The Unseen Reality |
| Malik (King) | The Manifest Authority | The Visible Order |
| Ilāh (God) | The Station of Ultimate Worship | The Highest Truth |

## Sūrat al-Takāthur (The Abundance, The Rivalry)

An interpreter of the Qur'ān once expounded this sūrah in his circle, declaring that the abundance of family, wealth, and worldly luxury has distracted people from obedience to God and caused them to forget His remembrance. "Only when you visit the graves," he said, "do you recognize that neither children nor possessions nor status avail you anything." 

A scholar from among the Ahl al-Bayt (the Family of the Prophet) was listening. He rose and gently corrected the interpreter: "O my brother, speak the truth! God is the Truth, and truth is His word. Tell me—do people go to their graves willingly, of their own volition? Or are they, when death comes, seized and carried away by force?"

The interpreter replied, "Of course they are carried away by force. No one is buried with joy."

"Then," said the scholar, "how can the verse say 'you visited the graves'? The word *ziyārah* (visit) implies a voluntary meeting, an act of free will—not compulsion. A visitor chooses to go and then chooses to return. But the one buried in the grave does not return. There is a profound difference."[^cite-2] The questioner's insight struck the first interpreter silent. His face paled, and he confessed his confusion. Moved by this intervention grounded in truth, the interpreter approached the scholar afterward and said, "By God, you have called out to me with the tongue of truth! I was indeed bewildered. I now see the matter clearly. But tell me—what is the true interpretation of this sūrah?"

The scholar then unveiled the deeper wisdom:

**The Outward Death and Worldly Distraction**

Man becomes so immersed in greed, covetousness, anger, lust, and endless desires—always chasing the improvement of worldly life—that he forgets himself entirely. He neglects all thought of the Hereafter, enslaved to the multiplication of possessions and status. In this state, he is as the living dead.[^cite-3]

**The Forced Descent to the Grave**

When death comes, a person is seized by the angel of death. His movements cease. He is then washed in ritual ablution, dressed in the shroud—that strange, seamless garment—and placed in the grave. There, the soil consumes the beauty of his form; it disturbs the arrangement of his body; it destroys the very marks of his identity. No one can rescue him from this horror, nor can anyone halt its progression.

**The Spiritual Death: Dying Before Death**

Yet there exists a counterpoint to this forced, physical death—a voluntary, spiritual death. The Prophet, peace be upon him, taught: *"Die before you die."* The believer who embraces this spiritual dying finds his heart lifted from all worldly pleasures. He purifies himself through the waters of religious knowledge, washing away the impurities of doubt. With his own hands, he dons the pure white garment of piety (*taqwā*).

The Prophet also said: "The grave is either a garden of Paradise or a pit of Hell." For those who have oriented themselves toward the Guardian of Allah—the true Imām—the grave becomes a place of peace. And for those who have made themselves enemies of the Imāms, the grave becomes a place of torment.

If a person, of his own accord, attaches himself to the Guardian of God and takes refuge in Him, that Guardian will cultivate within him such profound longing for the Hereafter that all worldly pleasure becomes as nothing. He will be prepared to bear every trial and hardship. Even while still in this world, he will belong no longer to this world. The Guardian will train and nurture him for the life to come, developing within him, day by day, thousands of spiritual qualities and perfections.

**The Inner Grave: The Shrine of Paradise**

The grave—understood in its inner, esoteric sense—is the sanctuary of the Gardens of Paradise (*Riyāḍ al-Janna*). It is the highest boundary that every true believer reaches after his apparent death. Yet those who are enemies of ʿAlī and his descendants will face a different reality. "With whomever a person sides in love, with that one he will be resurrected."[^cite-4] This is why the Prophet declared: "The Resurrection follows love." Whoever you love, you will be gathered with on that Day.

Thus, when you have attached yourself to the inner grave through spiritual death—when you have severed your ties to this world and pierced the heavens from this very place, drawn toward the pleasure of God—then the outward, forced death will harm you not at all. For you will already have transcended the world.

**Two Kinds of Knowledge**

The sūrah speaks twice of "knowing" and twice of "seeing" Hell, mirroring two fundamental modes of understanding:

*First Knowledge* (*ʿilm al-samāʿ wa-l-akhbār*): This is knowledge acquired through report and hearsay—what we have learned from the Prophets and their successors, from the Book of God. It is not based on direct observation, but on acceptance of what we have heard. We have received the Prophets and the Scripture as true authorities, and our knowledge rests on this foundation alone.

*Second Knowledge* (*ʿilm al-tawhīd wa-l-mabdaʾ wa-l-muʿād*): This is the knowledge derived from *taʾwīl* (esoteric exegesis)—understanding the origins and the return, the principles of divine unity and the knowledge of God's oneness.

**Two Modes of Seeing Hell**

Similarly, the vision of Hell appears in two stages:

*First Sight*: The first way one comes to perceive Hell is through recognizing disbelievers and polytheists—those whom God has cursed. This sight is veiled knowledge, apprehended through the veil.

*Second Sight*: The second vision comes after death, when one sees Hell itself with certainty of eye (*ʿayn al-yaqīn*)—the kind of seeing that leaves no room for doubt. What was spoken before is now manifest: Hell is as real as Paradise. The certainty that comes from direct witness is absolute.

**The Inquiry Concerning Blessing**

On that Day, God will ask: "On this day, you shall be questioned about the blessing." What blessing is this? It is the sweet, life-giving nectar—the knowledge and wisdom that flows from the lips of the Prophets and the friends of God. God will ask: "Why did you abandon Our saints and turn instead to Our enemies? Why did you turn away from the words of wisdom? Why did you forsake the Water of Kawthar, the Water of Life, and instead drink the scalding waters of Hell that burn the intestines and tear them asunder?"

When Mawlānā al-Ḥasan al-Ḥakīm—may God bless him—the sixteenth Imām, was asked about *naʿīm* (blessing), he replied that the Messenger of God explained: "Blessing is the cold water that will be given to people on the Day when heat is most intense."[^cite-5] God has explained His blessings upon His servants. It would contradict God's glory to withhold blessings from those He loves.

But why would God ask about a blessing He Himself has bestowed? The answer is profound: God has described the duties *toward* the blessing as themselves a blessing to be questioned about. He has given the blessing; now it is your obligation to fulfill the duties it entails. *Taʾāt al-nadāmah*—obedience here carries the weight of responsibility, even the flavor of regret for past negligence.

**The True Blessing and True Hell**

In truth, Amīr al-Muʾminīn ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib and his descendants—the Imāms of each age—are themselves *al-naʿīm* (the Blessing). And all those who stand in opposition to them, who usurp their rights, are themselves Hell. This is the intimate meaning the interpreter finally understood, drawn from the *Majālis al-Muʾayyada* (the Sessions of al-Muʾayyad al-Shīrāzī).

## Sūrat al-Tīn (The Fig)

A companion of Mawlānā Imām Jaʿfar al-Ṣādiq, peace be upon him, once sat pondering the oaths in Sūrat al-Tīn as he recited the Qur'ān. The oaths struck him as strange. How could God swear by such ordinary things—figs and olives, fruits eaten and discarded, things that rot and become waste? The oath takes its weight and majesty from the greatness of the thing sworn upon. Yet here God swears by trivial fruits. His doubts multiplied over several days until, unable to settle his mind, he came to Mawlānā Imām and said:

"My parents are a ransom for you! A whisper has stirred in my heart, and I come to dispel it. God swears by figs and olives in His Word. What virtue lies in such commonplace things, eaten and thrown away, or emerging as waste? When one takes an oath by something, should it not be by something great? What quality do these two fruits possess that the Almighty chose them for His oath?"

Mawlānā Imām, peace be upon him, replied: "Truly, you have asked a question of great importance. I will answer you, but take care never to divulge the meaning of wisdom to the unworthy."

He then asked: "Tell me—what do you suppose these two things are?"

"They are fruits," the companion replied.

"Yes, you are right. They are fruits. But the affair does not end there. God has distinguished these two fruits with His height and depth, the heavens and the earth. He has given them growth through His knowledge and wisdom, and He has cast upon them the shadow of His Throne. These two fruits are, in truth, *Adam and Noah*, peace be upon them."

"But why," asked the companion, "did God compare them to fruits? Are they eaten and digested?"

"Ah," said the Imām, "what you perceive as fruits that are eaten and digested, producing waste—that is not the reality here. These two are the fruits of Paradise. No matter how much you take from them, they do not diminish. They are like a lamp from which you light another lamp without the first being lessened. They are the essence—the very fruit, the innermost nature—of the world."

**The Fig: Adam**

"Why, then, does God liken Adam to the fig, and Noah to the olive?" asked the companion.

The Imām explained: "Consider the nature of fruit-bearing. Ordinarily, a tree produces leaves, then buds, then flowers, which in turn become fruit. But the fig appears differently. It comes forth directly from the branches in the form of fruit, without the usual progression of buds and blossoms. Similarly, every human being requires a period of gestation and birth, proceeding through the natural stages of conception and development. Yet Adam, peace be upon him, emerged from dust itself, without precedent, without those ordinary stages. Like the fig, he was created suddenly, without the usual prerequisites. Therefore, God likened him to the fig."

The Imām continued: "Beyond Adam himself, the fig also hints at the Mahdī—the Resurrection—for he too will appear suddenly, without warning signs, when the time comes."

**The Olive: Noah**

"And why Noah as the olive?" asked the companion.

"Because," replied the Imām, "the olive fruit is consumed in small quantities, yet its essence—its oil—comprises the greater portion of its use. It is as though the olive's purpose is concentrated in its oil. In the same way, Noah's invitation (*daʿwa*, the summons) is subtle and hidden, but its essential fruit is *Ibrāhīm* (Abraham), peace be upon him, who appeared from Noah's descendants. Abraham's message spread and flourished far more abundantly than Noah's. The word 'olive,' paired with Noah's oath, carries an implicit, hidden meaning."

**Mount Sinai and Jesus Christ**

"And what of 'Ṭūr Sīnīn' (Mount Sinai)?" asked the companion.

The Imām replied: "Ṭūr Sīnīn is the place where Moses, peace be upon him, supplicated and received prophethood. A secret is hidden there, subtle as the olive's oil—and that secret is *Jesus Christ*."[^cite-6]

God says in His Book—the Qur'ān declares—that this tree (the olive) produces oil. Yet in the natural world, no tree produces oil on its surface. Oil emerges only when the fruit is pressed and ground. In the same manner, this tree in the verse is the essence of Jesus Christ. When the verse speaks of it "yielding oil," it signifies that Jesus Christ was the one who extracted and presented the inward truths, the meanings, the essential summaries of the *Tawrāt* (the Torah), which had been revealed to Moses. God made a special allusion in this verse to these profound truths and subtleties.

God exalted Jesus, peace be upon him, to the highest station. Jesus is described as both a tree and a word—God Himself says: "His example is as that of a tree with a good root and branches reaching to the sky." God likened the two because of the great correspondence between them: the word of *Ṭayyiba* (the pure, the good) and the tree of *Ṭayyiba* (the good tree).

**The Secure City: Mecca**

"And 'Balad al-Amīn' (the Secure City)?" asked the companion.

"From your context, it is clear," he replied, "that Balad al-Amīn refers to the Holy Prophet, peace and blessings be upon him."

"Yes," affirmed the Imām. "Balad al-Amīn—Mecca—is the first *Qibla* (direction of prayer) of God, the one that cancelled all other Qiblas. It is the first building erected on earth (where the first Imām was born), as God Himself declares in His Book. God made this House the dwelling of the Last Prophet. This symbol too conceals a secret, as did the previous ones."

"Why is this secret important?" asked the companion.

"Because," said the Imām, "both the Messenger of God and the Day of Judgment were sent at one and the same time. There is no gap between them. Therefore, the Messenger of God, upon him be blessings and peace, joined the fingers of both his hands together and said: 'Prophethood and Resurrection have been sent as twins.'"[^cite-7]

God made pilgrimage to this House an obligation for all, and made it a sign for His Last Prophet—itself a sign of the Resurrection. Therefore, whoever performs the pilgrimage with sincerity is truly making pilgrimage; he accepts the call of God; he attains the station of *taqwā* (God-consciousness). He walks among the inhabitants of Paradise, to whom the angels give glad tidings: "Enter Paradise in peace and safety."

**The Wisdom of Concealment**

When the companion heard this comprehensive interpretation, he exclaimed: "By God! You have established the grandeur of God's oath. But one question remains: Why did God conceal these great oaths in hidden symbols rather than explain them in clear and manifest terms so that all people might understand them easily?"

The Imām replied: "In these verses lies the wisdom of God—to keep great truths hidden within the grain, concealed within thick skins and husks. And what is the purpose? It is that insects should not spoil the fruit, and that the noblest of God's creatures—human beings—alone should receive it. In the same way, the reason for concealing meaning within apparent words is that these true sciences should not reach the hands of the undeserving and the incompetent (who are like insects) and corrupt them with their conjectures and opinions. Rather, the believers—those with insight and knowledge—should benefit from them. And so that the excellence of those who seek knowledge becomes manifest to the ignorant. God says in His Qur'ān: "He raises in degrees those who believe among you and those who have been given knowledge."

| Verse / Oath | Outward Hint | Hidden Inner Meaning |
|--------------|--------------|----------------------|
| The Fig | Adam, peace be upon him | The Mahdī (the Establisher); the Resurrection |
| The Olive | Noah, peace be upon him | Ibrāhīm (Abraham); the spread of the message |
| Mount Sinai | Mūsā (Moses); the Torah | Jesus Christ; the inward truths of revelation |
| The Secure City | Muḥammad, peace and blessings be upon him | The Qāʾim ʿAlī; the Resurrection |

## Sūrat al-Nāziʿāt (Those Who Tear Out)

Just as the human body has various organs, each assigned a specific function, so too the servants of the *daʿwa* (the summons, the invitation) have been entrusted with distinct invitational services. Consider the act of eating: the hand brings bread to the mouth; the teeth bite and tear; the tongue moves and moistens; the jaw supports; the throat swallows. Each performs its appointed task. So too in the sacred calling of the Dīn—religion itself.

The *Mustajīb* (the Responder, one who has accepted the summons) who enters the path of faith is like the teeth: he cuts away and removes the impurities of false belief. The *Mazūnīn* (the Authorizers, the Speakers)—those entrusted with knowledge—provide him with *ʿilm* (knowledge) and *maʿrifa* (gnosis), as the tongue provides saliva. In this way, the believer gradually acquires spiritual nourishment and advancement.

| Element | Function in Da'wa |
|---------|------------------|
| The Nawāla (the Portion) | Knowledge of the Mustajīb |
| The Dante (the Bite) | The Mazūnīn's role |
| The Glaze (Moisture) | The teaching and nurture |

The Qur'ān alludes to this in verses that speak of the ranks and stations within the *daʿwa*: "The angels who tear out the souls" (*al-nāziʿāt*) refer to the *Mukaṣṣar* (those who carry out extraction)—those who extract and bring forth the deepest knowledge from within the apparent meanings of rulings and verses. "Those who gently draw out" (*al-nāshiṭāt*) are the *Mazūnīn* who take the oath (also called the *ʿAqīdīn*, the Committed Ones)—they bind believers to the covenant. "Those who swim along" (*al-sābihāt*) are those who flow through the depths of knowledge. "Those who race ahead" (*al-sābiqāt*) are the messengers (*duʿāt*) who have increased in insight and move forward, advancing others in their ranks. "Those who manage all affairs" (*al-mudbbirāt*) are the Imāms who bear responsibility for executing divine command. And the "Master of the Command" (*Mālik al-amr*) is the Imām himself.[^cite-8]

| The Five Ranks | Corresponding Stations in Da'wa |
|---|---|
| Those who tear out (al-Nāziʿāt) | The Mukaṣṣar |
| Those who gently draw (al-Nāshiṭāt) | The Mazūnīn and the Aqīdīn |
| Those who swim (al-Sābihāt) | The Da'wa; those immersed in knowledge |
| Those who race (al-Sābiqāt) | The messengers (Duʿāt) advancing others |
| Those who manage (al-Mudbbirāt) | The Imāms; the Executor of Command |

These ranks have existed always, throughout time. The Prophet, peace be upon him, himself descended from the heavens (from the higher stations) to bring the message to mankind. In the Prophet's own time, there were learned monks and other elevated souls from whom he received knowledge and understanding. The Prophet himself said: "I have taken from five and given to five, and between me and my Lord there are five"—an allusion to this structure of religious transmission.

Similarly, Jesus said: "Unless you are born again, you cannot enter the kingdom of heaven." By this "second birth" is meant entry into the realm of the noble, the ascent through degrees of knowledge. These degrees grow from the acquisition of inner (*bāṭin*) knowledge. When a seed is sown, the farmer tends it with great care—providing proper space, proper water, proper air, proper nourishment. In the same way, the founders and cultivators of the *daʿwa* are gardeners of the soul, nurturing their believers and seeking their progress and eternal happiness.

## Sūrat al-Qadr (The Night of Power / Decree)

⟪quran 97:1⟫
⟪ar:‏إِنَّآ أَنزَلْنَهُ فِى لَيْلَةِ ٱلْقَدْرِ ‎⟫

The example of *Laylat al-Qadr* (the Night of Decree, the Night of Power) is *Mawlātnā Fāṭima al-Zahrāʾ* (Our Lady Fāṭima the Radiant), peace be upon her. In another sense, it is *Mawlānā Muḥammad ibn Aḥmad ibn Ṭayyib*, the twenty-third Imām. In yet another sense, *Mawlānā ʿAlī*, peace be upon him, is also the embodiment of this night.

In the circle of interpretation surrounding Mawlānā Fāṭima, *al-Qadr* (the Decree, the Power) refers to her father, Muḥammad Muṣṭafā (the Chosen One), peace and blessings be upon him. In the circle of *Mawlānā Muḥammad ibn Aḥmad ibn Ṭayyib*, *al-Qadr* refers to his father, *Mawlānā Aḥmad*, peace be upon him, who authored the *Alif*—a collection spanning a thousand years of *Surat* (verses and teachings). This is why a thousand is mentioned in this sūrah.[^cite-9]

In truth, the spiritual treasures given to Mawlānā Aḥmad came from the Station of Muḥammad. They refer to the gifts that Muḥammad Muṣṭafā, peace be upon him, bestowed upon Mawlānā Fāṭima as an inheritance of wisdom.

| Title (Laylah) | Corresponding to (al-Qadr) |
|---|---|
| Mawlātnā Fāṭima | Muḥammad Muṣṭafā, peace be upon him |
| Mawlānā Aḥmad | Mawlānā Muḥammad ibn Aḥmad |
| Mawlātnā Fāṭima | ʿAlī, peace be upon him |

This is because the Messenger of God, peace and blessings be upon him, did not fully reveal the station and virtue of Mawlānā Fāṭima publicly—she dwelt in the palace of the night, veiled from common sight. This night is the twenty-second night of Ramaḍān and lies at the heart of the entire month, just as Sūrat Yāsīn is the heart of the Qur'ān. In another circle of interpretation, *Layl* (the Night) is Mawlānā Fāṭima, and *al-Qadr* (the Decree) is Mawlānā ʿAlī.

| Circle | Laylah (Night) | Al-Qadr (Decree) |
|---|---|---|
| Small Circle | Mawlānā Aḥmad ibn Ṭayyib | Muḥammad Muṣṭafā, peace be upon him |
| Large Circle | Mawlānā Fāṭima | Mawlānā ʿAlī |

We sent it down in the night of darkness (the nights of concealment). And what do you know of the majesty of Fāṭima bint Muḥammad, peace be upon her? The *ḥujja* (the proof, the argument) of Mawlānā Muḥammad ibn Aḥmad is indeed better than a thousand arguments.[^cite-10]

Yet there is a subtle distinction. Here, the month refers to the Imām and his thirty proofs (*ḥujaj*). A month contains thirty days; each day is a proof. Just as the month gathers all thirty days within itself, the Imām gathers all thirty proofs within his station. That is to say, Fāṭima is more excellent than a thousand such proofs. In this verse, the day represents the Imām, and the night represents the proof of the Imām.

| The Month | The Night of Decree |
|---|---|
| The Thirty Proofs (Ḥujaj) | The Station of Fāṭima |

**The Descent of the Angels**

On this night, the angels descend with the command of their Lord. The descendants of those angels—the Imāms—are of their spiritual lineage. There are two types of angels:

| The Cherubim (al-Karūbiyyūn) | The Intimate Ones (al-Muqarrabūn) |
|---|---|
| Nāsūt (Humanity, the human dimension) | Lāhūt (Divinity, the divine dimension) |

On this night, the components of both *Nāsūt* and *Lāhūt* descend—the human and the divine aspects of the Imāmate.

| Command of the Lord | Spirit | The Angels | Peace |
|---|---|---|---|
| The Tenth Rank | The Very Essence (*ʿAyn al-ʿAzīma*) | The Nasūtī and Lāhūtī Angels | Assignment / Ascension over the mountains |

The driving force behind this night is the great superintendent of the universe—the one who carries out this descent at the command of the divine will. This process of transmission of authority from above to below has always been occurring and will continue eternally. Each transmits to the one below; the first transmits to the last. This process continues throughout the night (throughout all periods of concealment). It does not take long to sleep; delays are only for the sake of allowing things to unfold.

The first of the great proofs receives a collection from the Holy Prophet, peace and blessings be upon him. Apart from this, every Muḥammad always receives from Muḥammad, and every ʿAlī from ʿAlī.

*Salām* (Peace) is a greeting from all sides and directions. *Salām* refers to the transmissions (*surrat*, the proclamations). That is, by the command of divine will, the proclamations are sent throughout the night (all the periods of concealment) until the time of *al-Fajr* (the Dawn) arrives—which is the coming of the *Qāʾim* (the Establisher, the Imām of the Resurrection).[^cite-11] In another sense, this process will continue until *Mawlānā* attaches to the *Muḥtaj* (the Dependent, the one in need) of Fāṭima—until *Aḥmad* (the Praised One) unites with the Station of *Mīm* and obtains her caliphate.

| The Dawn (al-Fajr) | The Qāʾim in His Glory (al-Sulṭān) |
|---|---|
| The End of Concealment | The Manifest Authority of the Resurrection |
