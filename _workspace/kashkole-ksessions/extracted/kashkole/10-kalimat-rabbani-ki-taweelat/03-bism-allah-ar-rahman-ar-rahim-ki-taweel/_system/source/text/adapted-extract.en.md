## The Eminence of Bismillah—the Rahman and Al-Rahim

⟪ar:بسم اللہ الرحمن الرحیم⟫ There exists no word more exalted and noble with which to commence a book or any undertaking. Even in the age of ignorance, the Arabs invoked ⟪ar:'بسم اللہ'⟫ in opening their discourse and compositions; yet the addition of ⟪ar:'الرحمن الرحیم'⟫ bestows upon it an unparalleled radiance and splendor. Though practitioners of other faiths similarly employ sacred formulas in their tongues—the Zoroastrians reciting "Oka Nama na Dard," the peoples of India invoking "Hari Om"—the eloquence and inimitable grace of the Qur'anic ⟪ar:بسم اللہ الرحمن الرحیم⟫ surpasses all comparison.[^cite-1]

According to the teachings of ⟪ar:ائمہ طاہرین صلی اللہ علیھم⟫ the Imams of the Purified Household (upon them be peace), the utterance ⟪ar:بسم اللہ الرحمن الرحیم⟫ functions as the very key to the Qur'an itself. Without it, the Qur'an cannot properly commence; without it, no surah (chapter) may stand complete. The Ahl al-Bayt (the Household of the Prophet) have accorded such profound significance to this phrase that they have declared: no surah possesses validity in prayer unless ⟪ar:بسم اللہ الرحمن الرحیم⟫ precedes it. It is therefore commanded that in prayers wherein recitation is performed aloud, ⟪ar:بسم اللہ الرحمن الرحیم⟫ itself must be recited aloud. In such matters, *taqiyya* (dissimulation for protection of faith) is utterly impermissible.[^cite-2]

Within this blessed phrase dwell three Names of Allah: ⟪ar:اسم (اللہ، رحمن اور رحیم)⟫ In it are Names of two distinct orders. One category comprises those constituted of letters that possess mortality and temporal limitation; the other comprises those that are ⟪ar:'حی'⟫ eternal and ⟪ar:'ناطق'⟫ articulate. These refer to the Wise and the Noble—to the Prophets, the Successors (*Awsiyāʾ*), and the Imams, who are themselves the Names of Allah (*Asmāʾ Allāh*), the signs of the divine reality and the guides to true Tawhīd (divine unity), the bridge between servant and Lord. Allah Himself declares (in the meaning of the revelation):

> ⟪quran 7:180⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَلِلَّهِ ٱلْأَسْمَآءُ ٱلْحُسْنَىٰ فَٱدْعُوهُ بِهَا ۖ وَذَرُوا۟ ٱلَّذِينَ يُلْحِدُونَ فِىٓ أَسْمَٓئِهِۦ ۚ سَيُجْزَوْنَ مَا كَانُوا۟ يَعْمَلُونَ ‎⟫
> 
> *And to Allah belong the Most Beautiful Names; invoke Him by them, and leave the company of those who pervert His Names. They shall soon be requited for what they do.*

Through His Names—through those who embody and manifest them—one attains perfect and genuine tawhīd (monotheism). Conversely, one must shun those who commit *ilḥād* (deviation), namely those who profess belief in the Messenger yet turn away from his bequest and testament, and those who deny the Imam's authority and supplication. These are they who distort the Names of Allah.

It is recorded that ʿAbdullāh ibn ʿAbbās once inquired of Amīr al-Muʾminīn (the Commander of the Faithful) regarding the mysteries concealed within the cosmos and the aftermath of creation. The Amīr sat and began expounding upon ⟪ar:بسم اللہ الرحمن الرحیم⟫ until the dawn prayer approached; as he continued discoursing upon the mysteries of the "Bā" (the letter B) alone, time drew to its close. Then the Amīr declared: "O ʿAbdullāh ibn ʿAbbās! If you should ask further, I could discourse only upon the secrets of the ⟪ar:' ب'⟫."[^cite-3]

Amīr al-Muʾminīn ⟪ar:أمیر المؤمنین⟫ has stated: "The virtues contained within ⟪ar:بسم اللہ الرحمن الرحیم⟫ are so vast that were one to compose treatises upon them, seventy camels laden with scrolls would scarcely suffice to bear them—and the camel is the mightiest of all beasts of burden."[^cite-4]

It is moreover reported that this phrase was inscribed upon the great portal of Pharaoh's palace. By virtue of its presence, Pharaoh remained secure within his walls, and divine punishment did not descend upon him. Yet the moment he emerged beyond the palace gates, the waters engulfed him entirely.

⟪ar:بسم اللہ الرحمن الرحیم،⟫ The whole Qur'an may be understood as a commentary upon ⟪ar:سورۃ الفاتحۃ⟫ the Chapter of the Opening, and the Opening itself comprises an essence distilled from all ⟪ar:صحف و کتب⟫ the heavenly scriptures and divine revelations that preceded it. In like manner, ⟪ar:بسم اللہ الرحمن الرحیم⟫ stands as the concentrated kernel from which all spiritual meaning unfolds.

## The Meanings Within Bismillah—the Rahman and Al-Rahim

**Bismillāh** (Beginning in the Name of Allah)

This phrase attests to a fundamental metaphysical truth: that the Creator has brought forth from absolute nothingness (*ʿadam*) all eternal beings (*al-muḥakkamīyāt*) into manifest existence. The secret of this reality is enshrined within the word ⟪ar:اللہ⟫ itself. Allah is a Name unlike all others—it is not composed from other words. The name "scholar" derives from "knowledge"; "ruler" derives from "wisdom"; yet ⟪ar:اللہ⟫ stands unique, uncompounded, irreducible to any other term. This singularity of the Name reveals that Allah alone is singular in His essence, possessing no partner or peer. The fact that the divine Name is not conjugated with any other word signifies that He has created the spiritual beings (*al-kāʾināt al-rūḥāniyya*) from absolute non-existence—from that which ⟪ar:الٰہیت⟫ (divinity) itself transcends.

**Al-Raḥmān** (The All-Merciful)

This Name is constituted from the root word ⟪ar:رحم⟫ (mercy, compassion), indicating that Allah Almighty has drawn all creatures forth from ruin and desolation and enveloped them in His tender mercies. ⟪ar:رحمٰن⟫ is a Name peculiar to God, one whose divine reality is universally common to all creatures. It is not predicated of any being save Allah, for it denotes "He who sustains the world." Allah's ⟪ar:رحمانیت⟫ (merciful providence) extends equally to the obedient and the disobedient alike; He grants sustenance impartially to all. 

Allah is ⟪ar:رحمٰن⟫—He who provides food and drink, clothing and shelter, all outward blessings. In another sense, the Name refers to the rational principle, for the term ⟪ar:تنزیل⟫ (the descending revelation, the outward text) points to the apparent and exoteric dimension. Thus the Merciful One sustains both the visible and the intelligible realms.

**Al-Raḥīm** (The Compassionate)

⟪ar:رَحِیم⟫ is a Name of Allah whose singular reality is nevertheless particularized in its manifestation to creatures. It is a Name applied both to God and to such creatures as exhibit extreme tenderness and forgiveness. Allah's ⟪ar:رَحِیمِیَت⟫ (compassionate mercy) extends only to those who obey Him, not to those who persist in disobedience; for He has shown us mercy through His Prophets and their Messengers, fulfilling both our corporeal needs as Merciful One and our spiritual nourishment as Compassionate Guide.

In another sense, Al-Raḥīm denotes the foundational principle (*al-asās*) that interprets and expounds the esoteric meaning underlying the apparent words of revelation. The Name "Raḥīm" itself derives from the root of mercy, and it refers preeminently to the Messenger of Allah, whose teachings constitute a mercy for our souls (*al-raḥma li-arwāḥinā*), through which we cultivate our inner nature and elevate ourselves from the material realm to the spiritual worlds.

⟪ar:رحمٰن و رحیم⟫ Both are attributes of Allah; yet ⟪ar:رحیم⟫ contains within itself a greater fullness than ⟪ar:رحمٰن⟫, for ⟪ar:رحمٰن⟫ is exclusive to Allah. That is, the very existence of the dual attributes ⟪ar:رحمٰن و رحیم⟫ declares the existence of God. ⟪ar:رحمن⟫ denotes ⟪ar:باب باطن⟫ the esoteric gateway, while ⟪ar:رحیم⟫ denotes ⟪ar:باب ظاہر⟫ the exoteric gateway. Both are divine attributes that manifest as the very essence of the Imam (upon him be peace).

## The Ordering of Letters in Bismillah

That the three letters of ⟪ar:بسم⟫ precede the four letters of ⟪ar:اﷲ⟫ in ⟪ar:بسم اﷲ⟫ serves as evidence that the four principles of faith (*al-arkaān al-arbaʿa*)—⟪ar:عقل کل، نفس کل، ناطق، اساس⟫ (the Universal Intellect, the Universal Soul, the Speaker, and the Foundation)—can be comprehended only by means of three intermediaries: the *dāʿī* (the Summoner), the *ḥujja* (the Proof), and the Imam.[^cite-5]

There are also three ritual elements in purification (*al-ṭahāra*): the ablution of hands, the rinsing of the mouth, and the pouring of water into the nostrils, which precede the four prescribed washings: the washing of the face, the washing of the hands and forearms, the wiping of the head, and the anointing of the feet.

⟪ar:بسم اﷲ⟫ The seven letters are followed by twelve letters in ⟪ar:الرحمٰن الرحیم⟫, which signify the twelve *ḥujjas* (Proofs) who appear sequentially after the seven Imams and receive spiritual sustenance from them, transmitting divine blessings to all people.

## What Is Meant by "The Name of Allah"?

Bismillāh literally means "In the Name of Allah." Yet a question arises: if ⟪ar:اللہ⟫ is itself the Name of the Self-Existent Creator, what purpose is served by commencing specifically with the Name? If both expressions conveyed the same meaning, would not "Bi-llāh" ("In Allah") have sufficed, without the interpolation of "Ism" ("Name")? Or would it not have been more fitting to invoke one of the Ninety-Nine Names of Allah?

The answer lies in understanding that a name serves as an identifier, a means by which one is known and recognized. Our own identity inheres in our name. In the same way, the particular Name of Allah through which He becomes known to His creation in any given epoch is the blessed person of Muḥammad Mustafā (peace and blessings be upon him) in his time. After him, his successor, ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib (upon him be peace), was the Name of Allah (*ism Allāh*) to his community. In like manner, every Imam of the Age in his era is the true Name of God through which divine knowledge becomes accessible. Allah Himself clarifies this truth:

> ⟪quran 6:121⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَلَا تَأْكُلُوا۟ مِمَّا لَمْ يُذْكَرِ ٱسْمُ ٱللَّهِ عَلَيْهِ وَإِنَّهُۥ لَفِسْقٌۭ ۗ وَإِنَّ ٱلشَّيَطِينَ لَيُوحُونَ إِلَىٰٓ أَوْلِيَآئِهِمْ لِيُجَدِلُوكُمْ ۖ وَإِنْ أَطَعْتُمُوهُمْ إِنَّكُمْ لَمُشْرِكُونَ ‎⟫
> 
> *Eat not of that whereon the Name of Allah hath not been invoked; for indeed it is transgression. And the devils do inspire their friends to contend with you; and if you obey them, you will become polytheists.*

In the exegesis of this verse, the Prophet forbade the consumption of certain animals even if the divine Name were recited over them—they would remain unlawful. In this case, the greatest Name of Allah is the Messenger himself, for whatever has been forbidden by his command cannot be rendered permissible through the invocation of any other apparent divine Name. Likewise, the successors of the Messenger, who have inherited his commandments, are themselves the Names of Allah; whatever they permit becomes lawful, and whatever they declare unlawful becomes unlawful. 

The interpretation of slaughter (*dhabaḥ*) pertains to the taking of pledges and vows; the interpretation of "food" (*ṭaʿām*) refers to the acquisition of knowledge. Thus the verse commands: "Do not acquire knowledge from a person who has not taken the covenant of the Imam of the Age; do not receive from him esoteric wisdom and spiritual realities unless he stands in the circle of divine guidance."

## The Four Gateways of Bismillah

Four words comprise this invocation—Bismillāh, Allah, Al-Raḥmān, Al-Raḥīm—and these signify the four principles of the faith. Two are spiritual in character and two are physical: the spiritual limits are ⟪ar:عقل کل اور نفس کل⟫ the Universal Intellect and Universal Soul; the physical limits of each age are ⟪ar:ناطق⟫ the Speaker and ⟪ar:اساس⟫ the Foundation.

**Ten Essences: The Ten Lights of the Cosmos**

This phrase is composed of ten individual letters (*jawāhir*), of which five signify the spiritual limits—⟪ar:عقل، نفس، جد، فتح، خیال⟫ (the Intellect, the Soul, Grandeur, Opening, Imagination)—and five denote the physical limits—⟪ar:ناطق، اساس، امام، داعی، حجت⟫ (the Speaker, the Foundation, the Imam, the Summoner, the Proof).

**Nine Stanzas: The Invocation in Nine Verses**

⟪ar:بسم اللہ الرحمن الرحیم⟫ There are nine stanzas of recitation, which signify the two physical limits—⟪ar:ناطق⟫ the Speaker and ⟪ar:اساس⟫ the Foundation—and the seven ⟪ar:صاحبان اسبوع⟫ lords of the cycle, the seventh Imam who gathers the seven Imams of his week into perfected unity. These Imams dwell in the palace of Imams, for upon their completion comes the end of one cycle and the commencement of another—much as a pen is lifted from paper and then descends again. This cyclical pattern mirrors the spiritual cosmos, which has two leaders—⟪ar:عقل اول اور عقل ثانی⟫ the First and Second Intellects—from whom emanate seven intellects in the process of their summons (*daʿwa*).

**Nineteen Letters: The Divine Manifestation**

The complete letters number nineteen: *Bismi-Allāhi-r-Raḥmāni-r-Raḥīm* (B.S.M.A.L.L.H. + N.A.R.H.I.M.), comprising the seven *dāʿīs* (Summoners) and their twelve *ḥujjas* (Proofs). In reference to these nineteen bearers of divine authority, Allah declares in ⟪ar:سورۃ المدثر⟫ the Chapter of the Enveloped One (interpretation of the verse): "There are nineteen guardians over the Fire."

## The Esoteric Interpretation of the Four Words of Bismillah

⟪ar:بسم اﷲ الرحمٰن الرحیم⟫ Four words point to Allah ⟪ar:أسمآء الحسنی⟫ and His Most Beautiful Names. The first two words—⟪ar:بسم⟫ and ⟪ar:اللہ⟫—refer to ⟪ar:عقل کل،نفس کل⟫ the Universal Intellect and Universal Soul, or alternatively to ⟪ar:لوح و قلم⟫ the Tablet and the Pen, or to ⟪ar:عقل اول اور مبنعث اول⟫ the First Intellect and the First Emanator (*al-Munbaʿith al-Awwal*). The latter two words—⟪ar:الرحمن اور الرحیم⟫—refer to the two physical limits, namely ⟪ar:ناطق واساس⟫ the Speaker (the Prophet) and the Foundation (the Imam or Bāb), each of whom is the Prophet and each of whom is the custodian of the faith.

A word (*kalima*) is formed by the joining of meaningful elements with that which carries meaning. These four spiritual and physical limits are termed "meaningful words" because each possesses perfection within its own essence and sphere, yet each requires endorsement from an authority above it to discharge its functions. However, those beings higher in the hierarchy surpass those below them in perfection and have no need of lower sanction. There is also a subtle principle: every lower being appears as "meaningless" compared to ⟪ar:مُحمِل⟫ the one above it, yet becomes "meaningful" when measured against those beneath it. For instance, the Universal Soul and Universal Intellect appear ⟪ar:نفس کل، عقل کل⟫ as meaningless compared to each other, yet the ⟪ar:ناطق⟫ Speaker becomes meaningful in comparison to them. This principle obtains throughout the spiritual and physical orders of divine guidance.

⟪ar:بسم اﷲ الرحمٰن الرحیم⟫ In the divine utterance, "Allah" is the essence and soul of all existence. Were there no Allah, ⟪ar:الرحمٰن الرحیم⟫ then His attributes would become bereft of meaning, and Bismillāh itself would lose all significance. Similarly, ⟪ar:عقل اول⟫ the First Intellect stands as the essence of all worlds, both spiritual and physical—the counterpart to Allah in the created order—without which no other being could subsist. That is, if ⟪ar:عقل اول توحید مبدع تعالی⟫ the First Intellect had not proclaimed unity (*tawhīd*) with respect to the Originator, Most High (*Mubdiʿ Taʿālā*), then all beings would remain suspended in meaninglessness.

## The Quintessence of Nineteen in Bismillah

⟪ar:بسم اﷲ الرحمٰن الرحیم⟫ The invocation consists of nineteen letters, divided into two portions: seven letters in ⟪ar:بسم اللہ⟫ and twelve in ⟪ar:الرحمن الرحیم⟫.

Moreover, should one count the diacritical marks—the vowel points, the *tashdīd* (doubling), the *maddāt* (lengthening)—along with the letter intervals, the total again yields nineteen.

There are one hundred and fourteen surahs in the entire Qur'an. ⟪ar:بسم اللہ الرحمن الرحیم⟫ appears before all surahs save one: ⟪ar:سورۃ التوبۃ⟫ the Chapter of Repentance. Yet this apparent deficiency is rectified by Allah in ⟪ar:سورۃ النمل⟫ the Chapter of the Ant, at verse 30, wherein Allah declares (interpretation of the meaning):

> ⟪quran 27:30⟫
> ⟪ar:‏إِنَّهُۥ مِن سُلَيْمَنَ وَإِنَّهُۥ بِسْمِ ٱللَّهِ ٱلرَّحْمَنِ ٱلرَّحِيمِ ‎⟫
> 
> *Behold! It is from Solomon, and it reads: "In the Name of Allah, the Most Merciful, the Compassionate."*

Through this divine artistry, the total count of ⟪ar:بسم اللہ الرحمٰن الرحیم⟫ throughout the Qur'an achieves perfect equality with the number of surahs—one hundred and fourteen. When one divides 114 by 19, the result is 6, with no remainder. This corresponds to the six days of creation and signifies the six levels of intellect and their corresponding six foundations.

(114 ÷ 19 = 6)

⟪ar:سورۃ التوبۃ⟫ stands as the ninth surah lacking ⟪ar:بسم اللہ الرحمٰن الرحیم⟫, while ⟪ar:سورۃ النمل⟫ is the twenty-seventh. When the odd numbers between 9 and 27 are summed—⟪ar:سورۃ النمل، سورۃ التوبۃ⟫—the total is 342, which equals precisely 19 × 18. Furthermore, should one count the occurrences of each word within the entire Qur'an, the tallies divide evenly by nineteen:

## The Panjtan as Means Within Bismillah

⟪ar:بسم اﷲ الرحمن الرحیم⟫ One of the most sublime virtues of this phrase is that it embodies the source of the Five Radiant Ones (*Panjtan*), concerning whom Allah declares (interpretation of the meaning):

> ⟪quran 5:35⟫
> ⟪ar:‏يَٓأَيُّهَا ٱلَّذِينَ آمَنُوا۟ ٱتَّقُوا۟ ٱللَّهَ وَٱبْتَغُوٓا۟ إِلَيْهِ ٱلْوَسِيلَةَ وَجَهِدُوا۟ فِى سَبِيلِهِۦ لَعَلَّكُمْ تُفْلِحُونَ ‎⟫
> 
> *O you who believe! Fear Allah and seek the means of approach to Him, and strive hard in His Cause as much as you can, that you may be successful.*

The subtlety of this verse lies in its command: seek the means, not create the means, as the transggressors have done. That is, Allah Himself has established all means; we are but to discover and employ them. In this realm of causes (*dār al-asbāb*), all possibility flows through means: one requires parents to come into the world, a teacher to acquire knowledge. Allah has furnished means for every matter—both spiritual and temporal.

In the same manner, there are five luminous beings (*kāʾināt nūrāniyya*) through whom approach to Allah becomes possible. They are: ⟪ar:محمد، علی، فاطمۃ، حسن، حسین⟫ Muḥammad, ʿAlī, Fāṭima, al-Ḥasan, and al-Ḥusayn. We require means because we lack the capacity to reach Allah directly. To connect with the Divine, we need mediators who relate to us (in the physical world) and relate to Allah (in the spiritual realm)—who are like us, yet not entirely, and who resemble Him, yet not perfectly. For this reason, at the commencement of every action we are commanded to declare ⟪ar:بسم اللہ الرحمن الرحیم⟫; for without the mediation of these beings, every action we perform is rendered unto other than Allah.[^cite-6]

Thus it became manifest that ⟪ar:بسم اللہ الرحمٰن الرحیم⟫ is inherently blessed, containing within it the light of those luminous beings who illuminate this phrase. Whenever and wherever it is recited, its radiance exerts a transformative influence. This phrase was inscribed upon the portal of Pharaoh's palace; while he dwelt within, divine punishment remained suspended, yet the moment he emerged, the waters overwhelmed him entirely.

## Drawing the Right Hand Across the Face While Reciting Bismillah

⟪ar:بسم اﷲ الرحمن الرحیم⟫ This phrase is constituted of ten individual letters (*jawāhir*). Among these ten, five are ⟪ar:مکرر⟫ repeating letters (appearing multiple times), and five are ⟪ar:غیر مکرر⟫ non-repeating letters (appearing once):

The practice of drawing the five fingers of the right hand across the face while reciting ⟪ar:بسم اللہ الرحمن الرحیم⟫ symbolizes the connection between the five physical limits and the five spiritual limits, from which they ever receive sustenance and blessing. The gesture of drawing the hand across the face signifies our acknowledgment that we have derived all existence from the Originating Source.

The left hand belongs to the right hand in principle; the right foot corresponds to the left hand; the left foot corresponds to the right foot. Although the right hand is the most excellent of all these limbs, these four together cannot equal the five senses of the face, for all the higher faculties—sight, smell, taste—are concentrated in the face.

The five limits ⟪ar:ناطق، اساس، امام، حجت، داعی⟫ of the physical realm recur in every age in the very manner that the five repeating letters recur; they are therefore like the five fingers of the right hand. ## The Tradition of Reciting Bismillah Over Water

It is narrated in the hadith that on the Day of Resurrection, Hell will roar like a lion and growl like a dragon. ⟪ar:رسول اﷲ (ﷺ)⟫ The Messenger of Allah (may peace and blessings be upon him) will come bearing a vessel of water. He will recite ⟪ar:بسم اﷲ الرحمن الرحیم⟫ over that water and blow upon it. Then, pouring the water into Hell, its fires will be immediately extinguished and its voice silenced. Hell will cry out in submission to the command of Allah Almighty: "O Fire, what has befallen you that you have become mute and have no cry?" Hell will reply: "O Lord! I have no strength against Your Name."[^cite-7]

The esoteric interpretation is this: the hardened opposition to truth and the enemies of the Ahl al-Bayt (the Household of the Prophet) are, in reality, an image of Hell—that which burns its followers in the fire of doubt and suspicion. When they are asked a question to which they have no answer, they roar and threaten by means of outward power and the severity of their tongues or invocation of divine punishment. Yet when they encounter one who is fortified with the Divine Name, their voices become muted and all their verbal strength is weakened.

⟪ar:رسول اﷲ (ﷺ)⟫ Alternatively: the Imam of the Age (upon him be peace) is the true ⟪ar:أسم اﷲ⟫ Name of Allah. The vessel of water upon which the divine Name is recited—⟪ar:بسم اﷲ⟫ that is, one who has taken the covenant of the Imam of the Age—is the true Mustajīb, the respondent. This respondent is a humble disciple of the Imam and appears as a drop of water compared to the boundless ocean of knowledge. Yet when this disciple engages in dialogue with the apparent scholar (Hell) and poses a simple inquiry while seeking arguments, that apparent scholar becomes humbled and rendered incapable of argumentation. His voice grows dull and all his verbal power withers away.[^cite-8]

## The Exemplars of Bismillah: Adam and Solomon

We recite ⟪ar:بسم اللہ الرحمن الرحیم⟫ at the commencement of every endeavor. In like manner, ⟪ar:سیدنا سلیمان بن حسن⟫ Sayyidnā Sulaymān ibn al-Ḥasan, being the inaugurator of his era, is likewise comprehended within this significance.

### Prophet Solomon

⟪ar:بسم اللہ الرحمن الرحیم⟫ "I begin with the Name of Allah, the Most Merciful, the Compassionate." Yet the name is one thing, and Allah is another. If Allah is He whose attributes are ⟪ar:رحمن و رحیم⟫ the Merciful and the Compassionate, then who is His Name? The Prophets of each age are the Names of Allah (*asmāʾ Allāh*) in their respective times, through whom Allah becomes known, just as man is known through his name. Thus ⟪ar:نبی سلیمان⟫ Prophet Solomon declared unto his people (interpretation of the meaning):

"I am the bearer of the Name of Allah, the Most Merciful, the Compassionate. Through me alone may Allah be known."

That is, confess to me, for I am the ⟪ar:رحمن و رحیم⟫ Name of Allah—the All-Merciful and the Compassionate. Only through me may the true knowledge of Allah (the Imam of the Age) be attained.

### Surah 114 and the Number 19

There are 114 chapters in the Qur'an, of which 113 commence with ⟪ar:بسم اللہ الرحمن الرحیم⟫. One alone—⟪ar:سورۃ التوبۃ⟫ the Chapter of Repentance—lacks this opening. The number 114 divides perfectly by the number ⟪ar:بسم اللہ الرحمن الرحیم⟫ 19 with no remainder: 114 ÷ 19 = 6. The Qur'an thus comprises six complete groups of nineteen chapters each. In other words, ⟪ar:بسم اللہ الرحمن الرحیم⟫ the opening phrase encapsulates the essential structure of the entire Qur'an!

## Bismillah and the Guardianship of Ali

⟪ar:حضرت علی ابن ابی طالب بسم اﷲ الرحمن الرحیم⟫ The Commander of the Faithful spoke of Bismillah, saying that all the treasures of Islamic wisdom and knowledge have been condensed into the heavenly scriptures, and the summary of these scriptures is found in the Qur'an and ⟪ar:صحف ابراھیم، توریت، زبور، انجیل⟫ the Sacred Books of Abraham, the Torah, the Psalms, and the Gospel. The Holy Qur'an itself is summed in ⟪ar:سورۃ الفاتحۃ⟫ the Chapter of the Opening. ⟪ar:سورۃ الفاتحۃ⟫ The Opening is itself condensed in ⟪ar:بسم اﷲ الرحمن الرحیم⟫ Bismillah—the Raḥmān and Al-Raḥīm. ⟪ar:بسم اﷲ⟫ Bismillah is summed in ⟪ar:بسم اﷲ⟫ itself. And the essence of Bismillah ⟪ar:بسم⟫ lies in the letter "Bā" (B), and the essence of the Bā resides in its point. ⟪ar:' أنا نقطۃ التحت البآء'⟫ Thus did Ali declare: "I am the point beneath the Bā."[^cite-9]

This signifies that the entire Qur'an has been concentrated ⟪ar:سورۃ الفاتحۃ⟫ into the Opening. Within these seven verses, Allah has summarized the complete Qur'an. ⟪ar:اَلحَمْدُ لِلّٰہِ رَبِّ اَلعَالَمِیْنَ⟫ By commencing "Praise be to Allah, Lord of All Worlds," I have described ⟪ar:مبدا⟫ the principle of faith. ⟪ar:الرَّحْمٰنِ الرَّحِیْم⟫ By invoking "the Most Merciful, the Compassionate," I have described the attributes of Allah. ⟪ar:مَالِکِ یَوْمِ الدِّیْن⟫ By naming Him "Master of the Day of Judgment," I have affirmed belief in resurrection. ⟪ar:اِیَّاکَ نَعبُدُوَاِیَّاکَ نَسْتَعِیْنَ⟫ By saying "You alone do we worship; You alone we seek aid from," I have ⟪ar:عبودیت⟫ professed servitude. ⟪ar:اِھدِنَا الصِّرَاطَ المُسْتَقِیْمَ⟫ By imploring "Guide us to the Straight Path," I am seeking the way. ⟪ar:صِرَاطَ الَّذِیْنَ اَنْعَمْتَ عَلَیْھِمْ⟫ By naming "those upon whom You have bestowed favor," I maintain justice. ⟪ar:غَیْرِ الْمَغْضُوْبِ عَلَیْھِمْ وَلاَ الضَّالِّیْنَ⟫ And by saying "not those upon whom rests Your wrath, nor the astray," I have questioned regarding sins.[^cite-10]

In the commentary upon the Opening, it is stated: "There is no prayer without the Opening." The Opening itself comprises seven verses, corresponding to the seven letters of Bismillah. For this reason, the virtue of the Opening was not diminished, for ⟪ar:سورۃ الفاتحۃ⟫ its effect derives directly from ⟪ar:بسم اللہ⟫ Bismillah.

### The Guardianship of Ali

⟪ar:بسم اللہ الرحمن الرحیم⟫ Even were one to remove the dotted letters (⟪ar:ب ن ی⟫), Bismillah could still be read. But if the letter ⟪ar:ب⟫ (the Bā)—the first letter—were removed, the phrase would become unpronounceable and lose all meaning.

Every action is initiated ⟪ar:بسم اللہ الرحمن الرحیم⟫ with the letter Bā; if it is absent, the action loses all importance and becomes void. By calling the letter Bā the "point beneath the Bā," Ali is pointing out that every prescribed action (*ʿamal taklīfī*) is futile and devoid of meaning without his guardianship (*wilāya*), just as Bismillah becomes meaningless when its opening letter is removed. Moreover, just as every action begins with the letter Bā and its meaning derives from the point beneath that Bā, so too does religion commence with the guardianship of ⟪ar:امیر المؤمنین⟫ Amīr al-Muʾminīn, without which all other actions are rendered null.

In other words, if you deny the guardianship presented in the Qur'an, the entire Qur'an will be suspended from your understanding. You cannot fathom the Qur'an until you develop intimate knowledge through my guardianship, for I am the essence of all the meanings the Qur'an contains, just as the whole Qur'an is embodied in Bismillah![^cite-11]

Our entire faith rests upon the guardianship of Mawlānā ʿAlī. ⟪ar:حجاب علی ابن ابی طالب⟫ If guardianship is absent, no other devotion will avail us on the Day of Judgment.

### The Black Stone

In the same way, Allah Almighty made the entire earth the source of sacred places. Then He gathered the quintessence of their blessings in ⟪ar:مکۃ المعظمۃ⟫ the Holy City of Mecca. From Mecca, He drew forth the essence and concentrated it in ⟪ar:مسجد الحرام⟫ the Sacred Mosque. From the Mosque, He extracted the summary and placed it within ⟪ar:کعبۃ اللہ⟫ the House of Allah. From the House, He drew out its essence and placed it in ⟪ar:رکن اسعد⟫ the Corner of Fortune. And from that Corner, He gathered the ultimate essence into ⟪ar:حجر اسود⟫ the Black Stone—that sacred point ⟪ar:نقطۃ التحت البآء⟫ beneath the Bā. ⟪ar:کعبۃ اللہ⟫ Thus the key to all divine actions and blessings is secured within this point.

### The Believer

Another interpretation: Allah created man ⟪ar:اشرف المخلوقات⟫ as the noblest of all creatures in this world. He then condensed their essence, creating the community of ⟪ar:موالید ثلاثۃ⟫ three generations. He made their summary ⟪ar:أمت آدم⟫ the Community of Adam, and from that ⟪ar:أمت نوح⟫ created the Community of Noah. From the Community of Noah, ⟪ar:أمت أبراھیمؑ⟫ He generated the Community of Abraham. From Abraham's community, He brought forth ⟪ar:أمت موسیٰ⟫ the Community of Moses. From Moses' community, ⟪ar:أمت عیسیٰ⟫ emerged the Community of Jesus. And from Jesus' community came ⟪ar:أمت مسلمۃ⟫ the Muslim Community, the final and most complete. The essence of the entire Muslim Community is ⟪ar:مومنین (سلیمانی)⟫ "the Believers"—specifically, the followers of the faith of the Imam—which alone among the seventy-three sects is saved. It is this reality that stands opposite ⟪ar:نقطۃ التحت البآء⟫ to the point beneath the Bā, for salvation is possible only through becoming a true ⟪ar:مؤمن⟫ Believer; otherwise, all purpose of creation would be lost. The ⟪ar:ب⟫ (the Bā) is therefore the essential marker in ⟪ar:بسم اللہ الرحمن الرحیم⟫.

## The Letter Bā: Upon It Bismillah Is Constructed

The entire Qur'an is constituted of twenty-eight ⟪ar:حروف معجم⟫ letters of the alphabet. The Opening, ⟪ar:سورۃ الفاتحۃ⟫ which is a summary of the Qur'an, is composed of twenty-one letters. The ⟪ar:مقطعات، بسم اللہ الرحمن الرحیم⟫ *muqaṭṭaʿāt* (the mysterious abbreviated letters that head certain surahs) along with Bismillah—the Raḥmān and Al-Raḥīm—comprise a count of nineteen.

The distinction is that the Qur'anic ⟪ar:بسم اللہ الرحمن الرحیم⟫ phrase did not enter into the surahs themselves, but the *muqaṭṭaʿāt* entered the chapters of the Qur'an as their preface precisely because of the blessings they confer.

| | **Muqaṭṭaʿāt** | **Bismillāh—Al-Raḥmān Al-Raḥīm** |
|---|---|---|
| Constituent Letters | 14 Letters of the Alphabet | 19 Letters of the Alphabet |
| Luminous Quality | All Luminous Letters | Contains One Dark Letter (**B**) |

⟪ar:مقطعات، بسم اللہ الرحمن الرحیم⟫ The *muqaṭṭaʿāt* are elevated above Bismillah because they contain no dark letters, whereas ⟪ar:بسم اللہ الرحمن الرحیم⟫ within the nineteen letters a dark letter (the Bā) has been inserted. Yet although the Bā belongs among the dark letters (*ḥurūf ẓalmāniyya*), it is their leader and chief, the closest of them to the luminous letters.
