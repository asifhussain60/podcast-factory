# Interpretation of Bismillah al-Rahman al-Rahim

## The Excellence of Bismillah al-Rahman al-Rahim

⟪ar:بسم اللہ الرحمن الرحیم⟫ There exists no word more exalted and noble with which to commence a book or undertaking. Even in the age of ignorance (jāhiliyya), the Arabs invoked ⟪ar:'بسم اللہ'⟫ at the opening of discourse and action; yet the addition of ⟪ar:'الرحمن الرحیم'⟫ invested the formula with unsurpassed luminosity. Other religious traditions have likewise adopted solemn utterances—the Zoroastrians spoke 'Oka Nama na Dard,' the peoples of India recited 'Hari Om'—but none attains the rhetorical magnificence and spiritual resonance of the Qur'anic ⟪ar:بسم اللہ الرحمن الرحیم⟫.[^cite-1]

According to the Imams of the Ahl al-Bayt (the Family of the Prophet, upon them be peace), this invocation serves as the very key to the Qur'an itself, without which neither the Book nor any of its surahs (chapters) may properly commence. So profound is their reverence for this formula that they teach: no sura is accepted in prayer unless preceded by ⟪ar:بسم اللہ الرحمن الرحیم⟫. In prayers where recitation is performed aloud, this opening must likewise be pronounced aloud; in such circumstances, *taqiyya* (precautionary concealment of faith) is utterly forbidden.[^cite-2]

Within this sublime utterance dwell three Divine Names: Allah, al-Rahman, and al-Rahim. These Names are of two orders. The first comprises those constituted of letters subject to mortality; the second consists of those that are living and speaking—namely, the Aqil (the Intellectual Principle) and the Sharif (the Exalted)—which are the Prophets, the Awsiyāʾ (Successors), and the Imāms. These latter are themselves the Names of Allah, the signs of the Hereafter, the guides to true monotheism (tawḥīd), and the bridge between the servant and the Divine. Allah Most High declares:

> ⟪quran 7:180⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَلِلَّهِ ٱلْأَسْمَآءُ ٱلْحُسْنَىٰ فَٱدْعُوهُ بِهَا ۖ وَذَرُوا۟ ٱلَّذِينَ يُلْحِدُونَ فِىٓ أَسْمَٓئِهِۦ ۚ سَيُجْزَوْنَ مَا كَانُوا۟ يَعْمَلُونَ ‎⟫
> *And (all) the Most Beautiful Names belong to Allah, so call on Him by them, and leave the company of those who belie or deny (or utter impious speech against) His Names. They will be requited for what they used to do.*
> — **Quran 7:180**

By the Names of Allah we attain perfect and true knowledge of His oneness; through these Names we turn from those who deny them—those, that is, who acknowledge the Prophet yet reject his will and testament, and who disavow the Imam's authority in prayer.[^cite-3]

A celebrated tradition recounts that ʿAbdullāh ibn ʿAbbās once posed a question to the Commander of the Faithful regarding the mysteries latent in ⟪ar:بسم اللہ الرحمن الرحیم⟫. Ali sat and expounded upon it until the hour of dawn prayer, continuing to speak of the profundity hidden in the letter ⟪ar:'ب'⟫. At last he declared: "O ibn ʿAbbās, were you to ask further, I could speak only of this single letter ⟪ar:'ب'⟫!"[^cite-4] 

The Commander of the Faithful has testified: "The virtues contained within ⟪ar:بسم اللہ الرحمن الرحیم⟫ are so vast that were one to write of them, seventy camels would be laden with manuscripts; yet this formula is the most weighty of utterances."

It is related that this invocation was inscribed upon the great door of Pharaoh's palace. By virtue of this writing, so long as Pharaoh remained within that palace, he was shielded from divine wrath; yet the moment he departed, the river engulfed him. The entire Qur'ān is a commentary upon Sūrat al-Fātiḥa (the Chapter of Opening), and Sūrat al-Fātiḥa itself is the summary of ⟪ar:بسم اللہ الرحمن الرحیم⟫, which encompasses the spiritual essence of all revealed scriptures that preceded it.

## The Meanings of Bismillah al-Rahman al-Rahim

### Bismillah: Beginning in the Name of Allah

This formula furnishes incontestable proof that the Creator brought forth all eternal beings from non-existence into existence. The secret of this reality lies concealed within the Name "Allah" itself—a word composed of no other root, standing unique and irreducible. The term "scholar" derives from knowledge; "ruler" emerges from wisdom; yet "Allah" contains no precedent, no component. In its uniqueness lies the testimony that Allah alone is, without partner, and that His creation of all spiritual beings proceeded from absolute nothingness (without any prerequisite), an attribute peculiar to His Divinity (ilāhiyya).

### Al-Rahman: The All-Merciful (Exterior Mercy)

This Name derives from ⟪ar:رحم⟫, signifying that Allah Most High has drawn us forth from ruin and bestowed upon us His mercy. Al-Rahman is a Name whose reality is exclusively Allah's, yet which extends to all creation. It cannot be applied to any save Allah, for it denotes "He who sustains the worlds." The Mercy of al-Raḥmān encompasses all—obedient and disobedient alike—for He sustains both with equal provision.[^cite-5]

Allah is He who grants us sustenance, drink, raiment, and all outward provisions. In another sense, it refers to the Intellect, for the word points to *tanzīl* (manifest revelation)—the apparent dimension of Divine speech.

### Al-Rahim: The Merciful (Interior Mercy)

⟪ar:رَحِیم⟫ is a Name whose reality, though held in common with creatures, bears particular application. It may be applied both to Allah and to any creature possessed of extraordinary compassion. Yet Allah's *Raḥīmiyya* pertains only to the obedient; it does not extend to the disobedient. For the obedient, He has opened mercy through His Prophets and Messengers. He has fulfilled our bodily needs as al-Raḥmān; as al-Raḥīm, He has shown mercy by nourishing our inner and spiritual essence.[^cite-6]

In another sense, this Name denotes *ta'wīl* (esoteric exegesis)—that which interprets and unveils the inner meaning of the apparent words of revelation. The Name al-Raḥīm derives from mercy (raḥm) and refers to the Messenger of Allah, whose teachings are a healing mercy for the souls, through which we perfect our spiritual faculties and elevate ourselves from the material to the spiritual realm.

| **Aspect** | **Al-Rahman** | **Al-Rahim** |
|-----------|---------------|-------------|
| **Root** | Womb (raḥm) | Womb (raḥm) |
| **Bearer** | Allah | The Messenger of Allah |
| **Reference** | The Apparent (words) | The Inner (meanings) |
| **Sphere** | The Speaker | The Interpreter |

Both ⟪ar:رحمٰن و رحیم⟫ are Divine Attributes, al-Raḥīm being more comprehensive than al-Raḥmān, for al-Raḥmān is exclusive to Allah alone. The existence of these two Attributes demonstrates the existence of Allah. ⟪ar:رحمن⟫ denotes the ⟪ar:باب ظاہر⟫ (the Outer Gate), while ⟪ar:رحیم⟫ denotes ⟪ar:باب باطن⟫ (the Inner Gate). Both are Divine Attributes, and their combined essence is the Imam in this age.

| **Principle** | **Al-Rahman** | **Al-Rahim** |
|---------------|---------------|-------------|
| **Correlate** | The Inner Gate | The Outer Gate |
| **Manifestation** | In the Imam | In the Imam |

## The Ordering of Letters in Bismillah

⟪ar:بسم⟫ The fact that the three letters of ⟪ar:بسم⟫ precede the four letters of ⟪ar:اﷲ⟫ serves as evidence that the four principles of faith—⟪ar:عقل کل، نفس کل، ناطق، اساس⟫ (the Universal Intellect, the Universal Soul, the Speaker, and the Foundation)—can be known only through three limits: the *dāʿī* (the Summoner), the *Ḥujja* (the Proof), and the Imām (the Leader).

Consider too the three phases of ritual purification (*wuḍūʾ*): ablution, rinsing, and pouring water into the nostrils—which precede the four obligatory washings: of the face, the hands, the wiping of the head, and the anointing of the feet.

| **Letter** | **B** | **S** | **M** | **A** | **L** | **L** | **H** |
|-----------|-------|-------|-------|-------|-------|-------|-------|
| **Principle** | Dāʿī | Ḥujja | Imām | *ʿAql Awwal* | *Nafs* | Speaker | Foundation |
| **Ablution** | Invocation | Purification | Intention | Face | Hands | Head | Feet |

The seven letters ⟪ar:بسم اﷲ⟫ are followed by twelve letters ⟪ar:الرحمٰن الرحیم⟫, which represent the twelve *Ḥujjas* (Proofs) who succeed the seven Imāms and receive spiritual sustenance from them, transmitting it to the people.[^cite-7]

## What Is Meant by "In the Name of Allah"?

Bismillah literally signifies "in the name of Allah." Yet a question arises: if "Allah" is already the Name of the Self-Subsisting Creator, why must we begin with His Name separately? If both conveyed identical meaning, would not "Balaah" suffice, or would not another of the Ninety-Nine Names be more fitting?

The truth lies herein: a name is that which identifies and makes known. Our very personhood derives from our name. Likewise, the particular Name of Allah by which He became known in each age is the blessed essence of Muḥammad al-Muṣṭafā (peace be upon him) in his time. After him, his successor ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib became the Name of Allah in his epoch. In like manner, every Imām of the Age is the true Name of Allah in his time. Expounding this truth, Allah declares:

> ⟪quran 6:121⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَلَا تَأْكُلُوا۟ مِمَّا لَمْ يُذْكَرِ ٱسْمُ ٱللَّهِ عَلَيْهِ وَإِنَّهُۥ لَفِسْقٌۭ ۗ وَإِنَّ ٱلشَّيَطِينَ لَيُوحُونَ إِلَىٰٓ أَوْلِيَآئِهِمْ لِيُجَدِلُوكُمْ ۖ وَإِنْ أَطَعْتُمُوهُمْ إِنَّكُمْ لَمُشْرِكُونَ ‎⟫
> *Eat not (O believers) of that (meat) on which Allah's Name has not been pronounced (at the time of the slaughtering of the animal), for sure it is Fisq (a sin and disobedience of Allah). And certainly, the Shayatin (devils) do inspire their friends (from mankind) to dispute with you, and if you obey them [by making Al-Maytatah (a dead animal) legal by eating it], then you would indeed be Mushrikun (polytheists).*
> — **Quran 6:121**

In the exegesis of this verse, the Prophet taught that things He forbade remain forbidden even if the Name of Allah be recited over them. Herein lies the great significance: the true Name of Allah is the Messenger himself, for what He prohibited according to His command remains unlawful regardless of any other apparent Divine Name invoked. Thus the Successors of the Messenger—ʿAlī and those Imāms who follow his ordinance—are themselves the Names of Allah, and whatsoever they declare lawful becomes lawful; whatsoever they declare unlawful becomes unlawful.[^cite-8]

The slaughter (*dhabīḥa*) signifies the taking of covenant; food (*ṭaʿām*) represents the acquisition of knowledge. Allah thus commands: "Do not acquire knowledge from one who has not taken the covenant of the Imām of the Age; do not share your learning, interpretations, and inner truths with him who stands outside the sacred bond."

## The Four Gates of Bismillah

Four words comprise this invocation: ⟪ar:بسم⟫, ⟪ar:اللہ⟫, ⟪ar:الرحمن⟫, ⟪ar:الرحیم⟫—signifying the four principles of religion. Of these, two are spiritual: the Individual and the Spiritual; the other two are physical: ⟪ar:الرحمن الرحیم⟫. The spiritual limits are ⟪ar:عقل کل اور نفس کل⟫ (the Universal Intellect and the Universal Soul). The physical limits are ⟪ar:ناطق⟫ and ⟪ar:اساس⟫ (the Speaker and the Foundation) of each age.

**Ten Essences: The Essence of the Universe**

This formula comprises ten essential letters, five of which signify spiritual limits: ⟪ar:عقل، نفس، جد، فتح، خیال⟫ (Intellect, Soul, Grandeur, Opening, Imagination). The remaining five denote physical limits: ⟪ar:ناطق، اساس، امام، داعی، حجت⟫ (the Speaker, Foundation, Imām, Summoner, and Proof).

**Nine Verses: Bismillah al-Rahman al-Rahim**

⟪ar:بسم اللہ الرحمن الرحیم⟫ contains nine letters that signify the physical limits ⟪ar:ناطق⟫ and ⟪ar:اساس⟫ of K, together with the seven ⟪ar:صاحبان اسبوع⟫ (Lords of the Week)—the seven Imāms of an age, crowned by the seventh Imām who gathers all seven within himself. These Imāms dwell in the palace of Imāmate; after them, one cycle concludes and another begins, as the pen rises from the page, then descends again. This pattern mirrors the spiritual world, which comprises two leaders—⟪ar:عقل اول اور عقل ثانی⟫ (the First and Second Intellects)—and seven intellects emanating from the Second Intellect's invitation.

**Nineteen Total Letters: B-S-M-A-L-H-M-N-A-R-H-E-M**

These nineteen beings comprise seven *dāʿīs* (Summoners) and their twelve *Ḥujjas* (Proofs). Referencing this cosmic assembly of nineteen, Allah states in Sūrat al-Muddaththir:

> ⟪ar-quote:عَلَیْھَا تِسْعَۃ عَشَرٍ⟫
> *Over it are nineteen.*

## Esoteric Exegesis of the Four Words of Bismillah

⟪ar:بسم اﷲ الرحمٰن الرحیم⟫ Four words point to the ⟪ar:أسمآء الحسنی⟫ (Most Beautiful Names). The first two—⟪ar:بسم⟫ and ⟪ar:اللہ⟫—refer to the spiritual limits, that is, ⟪ar:عقل کل،نفس کل⟫ (the Universal Intellect and Universal Soul), or ⟪ar:لوح و قلم⟫ (the Tablet and the Pen), or ⟪ar:عقل اول اور مبنعث اول⟫ (the First Intellect and the First Emanator). The latter two—⟪ar:الرحمن اور الرحیم⟫—denote the two physical limits: ⟪ar:ناطق واساس⟫ (the Speaker and the Foundation), the Prophet and his successor, or the Imām and the *Bāb* (Gate).[^cite-9]

A word (*kalima*) is formed when a meaningful letter joins with other meaningful letters. These four spiritual and physical limits are termed "meaningful" because each is perfect in its essence and sphere, requiring only endorsement from a principle above them to discharge their functions. The higher beings, conversely, require nothing from those below. Yet a subtle truth inheres: each lower principle is meaningless compared to the one above it, while remaining significant in relation to those beneath. Thus ⟪ar:نفس کل، عقل کل⟫ is meaningless in comparison to it; yet ⟪ar:ناطق⟫ is meaningful in relation to it. This principle of graduated significance extends throughout the entire chain of spiritual and physical manifestation.

In Allah's utterance, "Allah" is the essence and soul of all existence. Without Allah, the Attributes ⟪ar:الرحمٰن الرحیم⟫ become orphaned and Bismillah loses all meaning. Likewise, the essence of all worlds—spiritual and physical—is opposed to Allah and is ⟪ar:عقل اول⟫ (the First Intellect), without which nothing else could exist. Should the First Intellect not emanate (*inbaʿatha*) in recognition of ⟪ar:عقل اول توحید مبدع تعالی⟫ (the origination of the Originator, Most High), all beings would remain devoid of meaning.

| **Principle** | **Bismillah** | **Allah** | **Al-Rahman** | **Al-Rahim** |
|--------------|---------------|-----------|--------------|-------------|
| **Nature** | Composite | Additive | Composite | Descriptive |
| **Type** | Noun | Compound Noun | Noun | Adjective |
| **Intellect** | Universal Soul | Universal Wisdom | The Speaker | The Foundation |
| **Spiritual** | The Tablet | The Pen | Prophet | Guardian |
| **Emanation** | First Emanation | First Intellect | Imām | *Bāb* |

**Examples from the Circle of Imāmate**

| **Principle** | **Bismillah** | **Allah** | **Al-Rahman** | **Al-Rahim** |
|--------------|---------------|-----------|--------------|-------------|
| **Station** | Imām's Son | Imām | Inner Gate | Outer Gate |
| **Level** | Nasūt (Humanity) | Lāhūt (Divinity) | Gate of Evolution | Gate of Manifestation |
| **Example** | Muḥammad b. Ismāʿīl | ʿAyn al-ʿAẓīma | Imām Aḥmad | Imām ʿAlī al-Ṭayyib |
| **Alternate** | Imām ʿAlī al-Ṭayyib | Muḥammad b. Ismāʿīl | Imām al-Maʿād | Imām al-Maʿād |

From the First Intellect, the Divine principle ⟪ar:لا الہ الا اللہ مبدع تعالی⟫ (There is no god but Allah, the Originator, Most High) unfolds. From this emerges the circle of ⟪ar:بسم اللہ الرحمٰن الرحیم⟫, and from this circle emanates ⟪ar:عقل اول⟫ (the First Intellect).

## The Essence of Nineteen in Bismillah

⟪ar:بسم اﷲ الرحمٰن الرحیم⟫ This invocation comprises nineteen letters, divisible into two parts: seven letters ⟪ar:بسم اللہ⟫ and twelve ⟪ar:الرحمن الرحیم⟫.

Were one to count, alongside the letters themselves, the diacritical marks—the *zabar* (fatha), *zer* (kasra), *pesh* (damma), *mad* (lengthening), *tashdīd* (doubling)—and the spaces between letters, the total would again yield nineteen.[^cite-10]

The entire Qur'ān comprises one hundred fourteen surahs. ⟪ar:بسم اﷲ الرحمٰن الرحیم⟫ appears at the beginning of all surahs save one; it is absent from ⟪ar:سورۃ التوبۃ⟫. Yet Allah compensated for this deficiency in ⟪ar:سورۃ النمل⟫, verse 30, where the formula is explicitly mentioned. Thus the total occurrences of ⟪ar:بسم اللہ الرحمٰن الرحیم⟫ throughout the entire Qur'ān equals the number of surahs: one hundred fourteen.

Dividing 114 by 19 yields 6, corresponding to the six days of creation and the six Intellects and their six Foundations:

**(114 ÷ 19 = 6)**

⟪ar:سورۃ التوبۃ⟫ is the ninth surah, lacking ⟪ar:بسم اللہ الرحمٰن الرحیم⟫. ⟪ar:سورۃ النمل⟫ is the twenty-seventh surah. Adding the odd numbers from nine to twenty-seven yields 342, which equals (19 × 18).[^cite-11] When the count of each word in the entire Qur'ān is examined, each such count divides evenly by nineteen.

| **Word** | **Count in Qur'ān** | **Division by 19** |
|----------|-------------------|------------------|
| Bismillah | 19 | 19 ÷ 19 = 1 |
| Allah | 2,698 | 2,698 ÷ 19 = 142 |
| Al-Rahman | 57 | 57 ÷ 19 = 3 |
| Al-Rahim | 114 | 114 ÷ 19 = 6 |

Alternatively, they represent the twelve pristine editors descended from ⟪ar:آدم ابدائیۃ⟫ (the original Adam).[^cite-12]

## The Five Pure Ones as the Means in Bismillah

⟪ar:بسم اﷲ الرحمن الرحیم⟫ One of the most precious virtues of this formula lies in its being the means to the Five Pure Ones, concerning whom Allah declares:

> ⟪quran 5:35⟫
> ⟪ar:‏يَٓأَيُّهَا ٱلَّذِينَ آمَنُوا۟ ٱتَّقُوا۟ ٱللَّهَ وَٱبْتَغُوٓا۟ إِلَيْهِ ٱلْوَسِيلَةَ وَجَهِدُوا۟ فِى سَبِيلِهِۦ لَعَلَّكُمْ تُفْلِحُونَ ‎⟫
> *O you who believe! Do your duty to Allah and fear Him. Seek the means of approach to Him, and strive hard in His Cause as much as you can. So that you may be successful.*
> — **Quran 5:35**

The subtlety of this verse merits careful attention. Allah commands the faithful to "seek" the means, not to create it—a principle contrary to the path of the ungodly. Allah Himself has provided the means; we are commanded only to discover and employ them. In this world of causality (⟪ar:دار الأسباب⟫), all affairs depend upon means. To come into existence, one requires the means of parents; to acquire knowledge, the means of a teacher. Thus has Allah provided means for all the religious and worldly concerns of creation.

Likewise, five luminous beings exist through whom one may approach Allah. ⟪ar:محمد، علی، فاطمۃ، حسن، حسین⟫ These are the Five Pure Ones. We require their means because we lack direct capacity to approach Allah ourselves. Therefore, to connect with the Divine, we need those who are related to us in the physical world and to Allah in the spiritual—who are like us in some respects yet utterly unlike us in others. For this reason it is commanded to begin every action with ⟪ar:بسم اللہ الرحمن الرحیم⟫; without the means of these Five, whatever we undertake will be directed toward other than Allah.[^cite-13]

Thus ⟪ar:بسم اللہ الرحمٰن الرحیم⟫ became blessed, containing within it the radiance of beings who illuminate this invocation. Wherever and whenever it is recited, it bears positive influence. This formula was inscribed upon Pharaoh's palace door, and so long as he remained within, divine punishment was withheld; yet upon emerging, the waters consumed him. The radiance of the Five Pure Ones is the light of the righteous, for this formula contains nineteen letters, and the names of the Five Pure Ones likewise total nineteen letters:

| **Bismillah** | **Al-Rahman** | **Al-Rahim** |
|--------------|--------------|-------------|
| 7 letters | 5 letters | 7 letters |
| **Muḥammad** | **ʿAlī** | **Fāṭima** | **Ḥasan** | **Ḥusayn** |
| 4 letters | 3 letters | 5 letters | 3 letters | 4 letters |
| **Total: 7 + 12 = 19** | | | | **Total: 19** |

## Wiping the Face While Reciting Bismillah

⟪ar:بسم اﷲ الرحمن الرحیم⟫ This formula consists of ten individual letters. Of these, five are ⟪ar:مکرر⟫ (recurring)—appearing more than once—and five are ⟪ar:غیر مکرر⟫ (non-recurring)—appearing but once.

⟪ar:ب س م ا ل ل ہ ا ل ر ح م ن ا ل ر ح ی م⟫

| **Recurring** | **A** | **L** | **H** | **R** | **M** |

## The Tradition of Blowing Upon Water While Reciting Bismillah

It is narrated in *Ḥadīth* that on the Day of Resurrection, Hellfire shall roar like a ferocious lion and shriek like a dragon. The Messenger of Allah will come bearing a cup of water and recite ⟪ar:بسم اﷲ الرحمن الرحیم⟫ over it, then breathe upon it. He will pour the water into Hell, and its flames will be extinguished at once. A voice will call out by Allah's command: "O Fire! What has befallen thee that thou hast been silenced and become powerless?" Hell shall reply: "O Lord! I have no strength against Thy Name."[^cite-16]

The esoteric meaning of this hadith is that the path of falsehood and the enemies of the Ahl al-Bayt constitute a living hell that ignites its followers in flames of doubt and suspicion. When they are confronted with questions to which they have no answer, they roar threateningly, attempting to intimidate through the power of their tongues or threats of divine punishment. Yet they are silenced.

Alternatively: the Imām of the Age and the Messenger of Allah are the true ⟪ar:أسم اﷲ⟫ (Names of Allah). The cup of water upon which ⟪ar:بسم اﷲ⟫ is recited—that is, upon which the covenant of the Imām of the Age has been taken—represents a humble seeker of the Imām, small as a cup of water compared to the ocean of knowledge. Yet when this seeker contends with the apparent scholar (the "Hell") and poses a straightforward question while seeking truth, that scholar becomes abased and loses all power of argument. His voice grows hollow, and all his verbal force is diminished.

## Bismillah and the Wilāya of ʿAlī

⟪ar:حضرت علی ابن ابی طالب بسم اﷲ الرحمن الرحیم⟫ The Commander of the Faithful declares regarding this formula that all treasures of Islamic wisdom and knowledge have been condensed within the revealed scriptures.
