## Love of Ali as a Virtue Against Which No Evil Prevails

The Messenger of Allah (peace and blessings of Allah be upon him) has transmitted the hadith: "The love and *wilāyat* (spiritual guardianship) of Ali is such a virtue that no evil can harm it." A question arises concerning this utterance: if it is taken at face value, then righteous deeds lose all value. One who loves Ali would remain virtuous even after committing grave sins and escape punishment—a notion that contradicts justice itself. Such a belief cannot belong to the believers; it is a corruption born of misunderstanding. Therefore, clarification is essential.

In the science of *ta'wīl* (esoteric exegesis), good deeds refer to the Imams—they who invite souls toward Paradise. Conversely, evil deeds are embodied in the false Imams, those who mislead and seduce, inviting believers toward actions that culminate in the Fire.

| Favors of Truth | Imams of Misguidance |
|---|---|
| The Imams of Guidance | The False Leaders |

The Prophet's (peace be upon him) meaning becomes clear: whoever loves Mawlana Ali in the sense of recognizing his station as *khalīfa* (successor), who believes in his *Imāma* (spiritual authority), and obeys him—for love without obedience is hollow and meaningless—such a person is liberated from the dominion of the false Imam and shielded from the evil of misguidance. Henceforth, no whisper of falsehood can divert him from the *wilāya* and obedience of the true Imam.[^cite-1]

This is the authentic meaning of the hadith, not the perversion that the *Ghāliya* (extremists) introduced, bestowing upon Mawlana Amīr al-Mu'minīn ⟪ar:امیر المومنین علی ابن ابی طالب علیہ السلام⟫ the rank of divinity itself. On the one hand they blasphemed, burning in their own ignorance; on the other, they opened wide the door to sin and transgression. They became, in truth, the worst enemies of Ali. By this evil doctrine, they annulled the law of the Prophet and, in annulling his law, they annulled his prophethood and his standing as messenger. They cast him down from the highest pinnacle to the depths. Having rejected prophecy and the *Sharīʿa* through their corrupt belief, how could the love of Ali—which depends upon obedience to the law—remain in their hearts?

The Messenger of Allah declared: "O Ali! Your friend is my friend, and your enemy is my enemy. Therefore, those who turned their backs upon the *Sharīʿa* and rejected its rulings became hostile to Ali and his foes became the enemies of the Messenger and of Allah. Such a person cannot claim to be a lover of Ali."[^cite-2]

*(Risālat 20, Qaul wa-l-Jawāb bi-l-Tawīl) — Sayyidinā ʿAlī ibn Sayyidinā Sulaymān (may Allah be pleased with him)*

## The Declaration: "I am the First and I am the Last"

### Sharīʿa-based Reasoning

⟪ar:### شرعی استدلال:

امیر المومنین علی ابن ابی طالب علیہ السلام⟫

The statement ⟪ar:'أنا الاول و أنا الآخر'⟫ is not a claim to divinity but rather an utterance of *ʿubūdiyya* (servanthood) and *kalimatu-Llāh* (the Word of God). The words of Ali are a source of guidance and healing for the believers, just as Allah Most High declares (interpretation of the meaning):

> ⟪quran 41:44⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَلَوْ جَعَلْنَهُ قُرْآنًا أَعْجَمِيًّۭا لَّقَالُوا۟ لَوْلَا فُصِّلَتْ آيَتُهُۥٓ ۖ آ۬عْجَمِىٌّۭ وَعَرَبِىٌّۭ ۗ قُلْ هُوَ لِلَّذِينَ آمَنُوا۟ هُدًۭى وَشِفَآءٌۭ ۖ وَٱلَّذِينَ لَا يُؤْمِنُونَ فِىٓ آذَانِهِمْ وَقْرٌۭ وَهُوَ عَلَيْهِمْ عَمًى ۚ أُو۟لَٓئِكَ يُنَادَوْنَ مِن مَّكَانٍۭ بَعِيدٍۢ ‎⟫

The first principle is this: ⟪ar:کلمۃ اللہ (لا الہ الا اللہ)⟫—the Word of God—is the primordial light from which all creation was brought forth. This word precedes all else and has no predecessor; it is the "last" in that nothing follows it. The radiance of this utterance is eternal and indestructible, shining first and last. As Allah declares (interpretation of the meaning):

> ⟪quran 39:69⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَأَشْرَقَتِ ٱلْأَرْضُ بِنُورِ رَبِّهَا وَوُضِعَ ٱلْكِتَبُ وَجِآءَ بِٱلنَّبِيِّنَ وَٱلشُّهَدَآءِ وَقُضِىَ بَيْنَهُم بِٱلْحَقِّ وَهُمْ لَا يُظْلَمُونَ ‎⟫

Without recitation of the word of sincerity—⟪ar:'لا الہ الا اللہ محمد رسول اللہ'⟫—the felicity of the hereafter cannot be attained, for this word has become the foundation of Islam. It is Islam's opening proclamation, without precedent. No sect of Islam disputes this. Yet there are two modes of recitation: one merely verbal, without understanding or belief; the other uttered with full comprehension and certitude. The Prophet (peace and blessings of Allah be upon him) said: "I have been commanded to fight mankind until they pronounce ⟪ar:لا الہ الا اللہ محمد رسول اللہ⟫. When they speak this Kalima, their blood and wealth are protected from me, save what Islamic law demands; their reckoning is with Allah."[^cite-3]

By this hadith, the Messenger guarantees protection of life and property to those who merely voice the word without inward belief—but not salvation in the hereafter. Yet in explaining its grandeur, he declared on another occasion: "Whosoever recites ⟪ar:کلمہ لا الہ الا اللہ⟫ with sincerity enters Paradise." When asked, "What is this sincerity?" he replied: "To understand its spiritual and physical dimensions and to fulfill the rights they entail."[^cite-4]

So noble and exalted is this Kalima that it is inseparably bound to the *Wāsī* (the Executor—Ali, peace be upon him). There can be no proper understanding of *tawḥīd* (divine unity), *tanzīh* (purification of God from deficiency), and *tajrīd* (absolute negation of peers)—⟪ar:توحید⟫⟪ar:تنزیہ⟫ ⟪ar:تجرید⟫—without learning from the Treasury of Knowledge, for through it one distinguishes between the *awliyāʾ Allāh* (the friends of God) and the ⟪ar:'فاروق اعظم'⟫ ⟪ar:'حدود اللہ'⟫ ⟪ar:'اولیا شیطان'⟫ servants of Satan. Whoever seeks to understand sincerity elsewhere, abandoning the Prophet's Teaching, shall go astray and find no guidance. He shall fall into association of partners with God (*shirk*) and become mired in doubt, severed from both terminals of this word and cut off from the means of salvation. Amīr al-Mu'minīn is first and last in this sense: the beginning of knowledge of the sincere utterance originates in him, and its culminating perfection is likewise bound to his essence. ⟪ar:أنا الاول و أنا الآخر⟫ That is to say: knowledge of the word upon which Islam and faith depend is attained from me alone, and after such knowledge is acquired, salvation necessarily flows through my love and guardianship. The origin and terminus of the believer's soul are bound to me. This is what we mean when we say: Ali ⟪ar:'ھوالاول ھو الآخر'⟫.[^cite-5]

There is a well-known hadith: when Adam was in Paradise, he beheld written upon the Throne the words ⟪ar:'لا الہ الا اللہ محمد رسول اللہ ایدتہ بعلی'⟫—"There is no god but Allah, Muhammad is the Messenger of Allah, supported by Ali." Hence, in God's sight, they are first in rank and standing, and last in terminating prophethood and the executorship.

It is established that since primordial times, the end of prophethood and the testament has been proclaimed. Adam himself and all messengers who followed carried this news. Thus they are first and last in manifestation.

The Prophet narrated: "Allah created souls from bodies six thousand years before bodies." Since souls precede bodies in creation and excel them in nobility, and since none equals these two in prophethood and executorship, he said: "In terms of nobility." ⟪ar:أنا الاول و أنا الآخر⟫

### Rational Argument from Human Constitution

Man is first as *nafs* (the selfhood) and last as *jism* (body). Here is the exegesis: the human being at the stage of *nutfa* (sperm-drop) resembles plants, for it possesses only the *quwwat al-nāmiya* (vegetative power). As development proceeds, he acquires the *quwwat al-ḥiss* (sensory faculty) and resembles animals, producing substances of sensation and suffering. After birth, he becomes rational, distinct from animals through intellect, and these stages are the pathway to understanding his origin—a truth accessible in no other way. These degrees demonstrate that the plant precedes the animal in form, and the animal form precedes the human; the human form is perfection itself, superior to all prior forms, and thus capable of receiving influence from the intellectual and spiritual forces—forces wholly independent of ⟪ar:مزاجات طبیعیۃ⟫ (natural temperaments). Man acquired his share of intellect and spirit from the world of souls and through unification ⟪ar:'اول'⟫ with that world. He stands as ⟪ar:قامت الفیۃ⟫ (the upright form), capable of receiving signs from the spiritual realm. That is, man is spiritually ⟪ar:'آخر'⟫ first and physically last.

Thus man is like a circle whose ends intertwine, encompassing heavens, earth, and all between. By his knowledge he holds dominion over all; his judgment enforces itself over them all. He brings every species of animal into service, making them servants to his purposes. When rational nature itself reveals that every human is both first and last, how much more so the Absolute Man—the Perfect Man—from whose prophethood and executorship all times draw their closure? He is both first and last.[^cite-6]

### Rational Argument from Mathematical Knowledge

All of Allah's creation—the intelligible world and the natural world—depends upon calculation (*ḥisāb*), and true reckoning encompasses all, as Allah declares (interpretation of the meaning):

> ⟪quran 10:5⟫
> ⟪ar:‏هُوَ ٱلَّذِى جَعَلَ ٱلشَّمْسَ ضِيَآءًۭ وَٱلْقَمَرَ نُورًۭا وَقَدَّرَهُۥ مَنَازِلَ لِتَعْلَمُوا۟ عَدَدَ ٱلسِّنِينَ وَٱلْحِسَابَ ۚ مَا خَلَقَ ٱللَّهُ ذَلِكَ إِلَّا بِٱلْحَقِّ ۚ يُفَصِّلُ ٱلْآيَتِ لِقَوْمٍۢ يَعْلَمُونَ ‎⟫

There are four orders of number:

| Units | Tens | Hundreds | Thousands |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | 10 | 100 | 1000 |

These four categories represent the hierarchy of the created and the eternal worlds, the ⟪ar:حدود اربعہ⟫ (four limits). Arithmetic begins with "one" and terminates with "thousand"; all numbers beyond are repetitions of these. Thus calculation has two terminals: one is the beginning, the other the end. The first vertex is "one," from which all numbers originate and develop. The second is "thousand," the terminus. No number save one merits the name "number," for it is the first of all—the ⟪ar:'علت مطلق'⟫ (absolute cause). Similarly, no number save thousand deserves the appellation, for it is the last of all—the ⟪ar:'معلول مطلق'⟫ (absolute effect). All numbers between are both cause and effect, standing as father and son. Two relates to one as son to father; three is two's son and four is three's father, and so forth. Yet there is one number—father of all with no father—and a thousand is the number of all that is offspring, having no offspring. This state reveals that one is the first with no first before it, and thousand is the last with no end after it. These two terminals rest in the hands of Ali (peace be upon him), as we have said of the sincere utterance: both its terminals are Ali. Whosoever is deprived of witnessing this word in this world is deprived of its reward.[^cite-7]

### The First Intellection and the Last Action

There is a celebrated Wisdom-saying: the first in thought and intention, the last in deed and manifestation. Its meaning: when one intends to build a house, he first conceives it mentally, then unfolds it upon paper, then slowly completes it according to the design. His first thought appears last in actuality. Between conception and manifestation, countless forms arise and vanish. ⟪ar:امیر المومنین علی علیہ السلام⟫ Such was the station of Ali (may Allah have exalted him) in Allah's knowledge: his form appeared first in creation. Thus he is, in this sense as well, ⟪ar:امیر المومنین اول الفکر و آخر العمل⟫—the Commander of the Faithful, first in thought and last in action. And your statement is absolutely just: ⟪ar:'أنا الاول و أنا الآخر'⟫.[^cite-8]

## The Declaration: "I am the Manifest and I am the Hidden"

Allah is both outward and inward. Yet this does not mean that the outward and inward are His attributes—nor are they among His Most Beautiful Names. Rather, these two attributes belong to His creation, which He has brought forth by His power and which lie within His dominion and possession. From the foundation of our praise comes this principle: the outward and inward are not attributes of ⟪ar:باری البرایا رب العالمین⟫ (the Creator of all creatures, Lord of all worlds), but rather attributes of His creatures. For we observe that the heavens—creations of Allah—manifest themselves through their actions and effects, yet remain hidden from perception. Similarly, the functions of our souls are evident, but their essences are concealed. Our movements demonstrate a force within the body that produces them, yet we know not what it is nor where it dwells. Thus the soul is both manifest and hidden; and when we find these attributes in Allah's creation, we must necessarily separate Allah from the attributes of creatures. True *tawḥīd* (divine unity) is the purification of ⟪ar:باری سبحانہ⟫ (the Creator, Exalted be He) from all physical and spiritual attributes.[^cite-9]

⟪ar:أمیر المؤمنین علی ابن ابی طالب⟫ They too are creations of Allah. Since the manifest and hidden are attributes of creatures, we understand that in saying ⟪ar:'أنا الظاھر و أنا الباطن'⟫ you have not claimed Lordship, but rather made clear a particular state and station. You said: "I am manifest to those of the hereafter who perceive My station and know My true standing. Yet I am among the people of this world, those ignorant of My station, who know not My reality. I am as a stranger among them." The Amīr al-Mu'minīn similarly described the believer's condition when he said: "The believer is unknown in this world, yet well-known in the highest assembly."[^cite-10] That is, he is hidden to this world and manifest to those of the hereafter.

The people of truth recognize that the Prophet's station (peace be upon him) is as the ⟪ar:'لوح'⟫ (Preserved Tablet) stands to the ⟪ar:'قلم'⟫ (Pen). It is well known that in this world, whatever particles and objects the Pen inscribes upon the Preserved Tablet appear here; what is not inscribed does not appear. Similarly, the names of the Prophet (Ali) not inscribed upon this Tablet are unworthy of salvation in the hereafter. The people of truth understood Ali's station—to them he is manifest; to his enemies he is hidden, for they neither knew him nor recognized his true reality. The Prophet (peace and blessings of Allah be upon him) declared: "Blessed is he who saw me; blessed is he who saw one who saw me; blessed is he who saw one who saw one who saw me."[^cite-11]

If the Messenger meant seeing with outward eyes, then both friends and enemies, believers and polytheists alike, would be included. Indeed, all the disbelievers and idolaters of Mecca—particularly ⟪ar:ابو لھب⟫ and ⟪ar:ابو جہل⟫—saw him clearly from childhood through prophethood and beyond. Yet sincere believers born later heard only his name and mention. Would ⟪ar:ابولھب⟫ be more blessed than them?

Manifestly, the hadith speaks of ⟪ar:'رویت نفسانی'⟫ (spiritual vision). Allah Most High declares (interpretation of the meaning):

> ⟪quran 7:198⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَإِن تَدْعُوهُمْ إِلَى ٱلْهُدَىٰ لَا يَسْمَعُوا۟ ۖ وَتَرَىٰهُمْ يَنظُرُونَ إِلَيْكَ وَهُمْ لَا يُبْصِرُونَ ‎⟫

The verb ⟪ar:'ترا'⟫ means "to see," ⟪ar:'نظر'⟫ also means "to see," and ⟪ar:'بصر'⟫ likewise means "to see." Yet the three terms carry wholly distinct and different meanings. Here, "seeing" denotes perception of the soul—perception through the eye of insight—and knowledge of the Messenger's station. This true knowledge was attained only by those whom the Messenger himself testified to, as he declared: "None has known me with right knowledge save Allah and you, O Ali."[^cite-12] You are the custodians of the Messenger's secrets and bearers of the divine trust. Thus ⟪ar:'طوبی لمن رأنی'⟫ refers to Ali (peace be upon him). In this way, ⟪ar:طوبی لمن رای من رأنی⟫ we mean the Imam (peace be upon him), who is Allah's successor and caliph upon earth. The third category comprises all believers who have acquired knowledge of *sharīʿa* (revealed law) and *tarīqa* (the spiritual path)—⟪ar:ائمہ علیھم السلام⟫ the Imams (peace be upon them)—and those who follow them and hold them as guides and leaders of their time, in accordance with Allah's words (interpretation of the meaning):

> ⟪quran 13:7⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَيَقُولُ ٱلَّذِينَ كَفَرُوا۟ لَوْلَآ أُنزِلَ عَلَيْهِ آيَةٌۭ مِّن رَّبِّهِۦٓ ۗ إِنَّمَآ أَنتَ مُنذِرٌۭ ۖ وَلِكُلِّ قَوْمٍ هَادٍ ‎⟫

This proves that prophet and messenger are manifest in their bodies—seen by every enemy and friend alike—yet hidden in their station and greatness, unknown to any.[^cite-13]

### Second Argument

Among the ignorant, a scholar has no worth. A scholar stands among them as a wise man among children and the mad. Those who know him revere and respect him; those who do not ignore him. Thus he is manifest in appearance among them, yet hidden in grace and knowledge. So too ⟪ar:'ظاہر'⟫ are you manifest in body to those who encounter you, yet ⟪ar:'باطن'⟫ hidden in your station from those who deny you.

### Third Argument

Allah said to His Messenger (peace and blessings of Allah be upon him):

> ⟪quran 93:7⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَوَجَدَكَ ضَآلًّۭا فَهَدَىٰ ‎⟫

The exegetes have offered many interpretations of this verse according to their opinions. Some said ⟪ar:'ضال'⟫ means "the astray"; others, "the unguided"; still others, "the unwary" (in devotion to Allah). Yet the Infallible Imams clarify that this ⟪ar:ضال⟫ carries the same meaning as the wise men's phrase: "Wisdom is the lost treasure of the believer." The ignorant Arabs of the Prophet's time were in the condition of precious essence among beasts—none was aware of your worth. You were ⟪ar:'ضال'⟫ (hidden) among them. Thus Allah instructed these ignorant Arabs to understand your worth. Otherwise, the Messenger was guided from childhood, as he said: "I was a prophet when Adam was between water and clay." In this verse, the conscience does not refer to the Messenger but to the people. Allah guided the people to the greatness of the Messenger. Thus it becomes clear: the difference between manifest and hidden is that his greatness was concealed among them until Allah made it manifest. ⟪ar:'فھدی'⟫[^cite-14]

## The Comprehensive Declaration: "I am the First and the Last, the Manifest and the Hidden"

The point reaches a station so elevated and rare that none other has attained it. From the emergence of Saradeep came the Point (400), and thereafter began the first round of Point's unveiling. ⟪ar:جثہ ابدائیہ⟫ This emanation possesses the highest vertex, ⟪ar:'عین عظیمہ'⟫ (the Mighty Eye), and ⟪ar:'عین عظیمۃ'⟫ no other being of its rank was born throughout the entire cycle except the one who arrives at it. Since the point of the Resurrection (*Qāʾim al-Qiyāma*) has not yet risen—and until the establishment of that point—the Mighty Eye remains supreme. The union of ⟪ar:عقل عاشر (جثہ ابدائیہ)⟫ the Tenth Intellect with the ⟪ar:قائم القیامۃ (قائم دور)⟫ Mighty Eye was complete, receiving substance from the Tenth Intellect without material intermediary—content that shall never terminate. From the emergence of this embodied principle to the unveiling of the Mighty Eye (excepting the coming Point of the Resurrection), no such divine support was revealed to any as to the Mighty Eye. The support for the *Qāʾim* will exceed that of the Mighty Eye, for the Mighty Eye is a part of the remembrance of the *Qāʾim*; the totality exceeds the part. Similarly, all Imams are partial in comparison to the Mighty Eye, while the Mighty Eye is total in comparison to them. Likewise, the *Sunna* (way) of the Resurrection is part of the station of this emanated principle. By this condition, countless miracles were unveiled from the veil of the Mighty Eye. Witnessing these miracles, many Shi'ites addressed him as ⟪ar:'الہ'⟫ (a deity), and he compiled sermons of the Master of All Existence—⟪ar:شموس الزاہرۃ⟫ (the Shining Suns)—a compilation made by ⟪ar:سیدنا ابراھیم بن حسین الحامدی⟫ Sayyidinā Ibrāhīm ibn al-Ḥusayn al-Ḥāmidī. These are sermons that have not reached the masses, preserved only in our canonical texts. Mawlana Ali declared in his sermon: ⟪ar:'اَناَ اَلاَوَّل وَاَناَ الآخر اَناَ الباَطِن وَاَناَ الظَّاہِر'⟫ "I am the First and I am the Last; I am the Hidden and I am the Manifest."[^cite-15]

### I am the First and I am the Last

Those who speak thus are indeed the greatest ones speaking through one of their sublime veils. You are first among all periods—first in the sequence of all times that have unfolded in the cycles. And in this dimension you are also the last, for none stands above you in rank until the coming Resurrection. Your declaration means: the first human born of God bore the name ⟪ar:'علی'⟫ (Ali), and the last who shall appear will also bear the name ⟪ar:'علی'⟫. Likewise, the names of all eternal portions of the vessels at the beginning of the cycles and periods shall be ⟪ar:'علی'⟫, and all the *Qāʾim*, ages, periods, and cycles at the cycle's end shall bear the name ⟪ar:'علی'⟫.[^cite-16]

### I am the Manifest and I am the Hidden

Your statement that you are hidden means your temple comprises the totality of all ⟪ar:صورات لطیفۃ⟫ (subtle forms) and ⟪ar:نفوس جوہریۃ⟫ (essential souls) of the periods. It may not be visible, yet its effects are manifest; therefore you are hidden. At the Gate of Gates of an Imam, all forms congregate. The Mighty Eye made this its chapter, and all the forms gathered around ⟪ar:'میم'⟫ (Meem, the letter M—representing the Prophet's name). From them the Mighty Eye received the collection of forms.

To say you are manifest means you have manifested to yourself, and the actions of all saints—wrought outwardly and through the sciences of *taʾwīl* and ⟪ar:معجزات الہیۃ⟫ (divine miracles) and ⟪ar:حکمت الہیۃ⟫ (divine wisdom)—were connected to their respective Imams and ultimately joined to you. He said: "I caused the death of Adam, Noah, Abraham, Moses, and Jesus; I made prophets and sent messengers." The veil of the Mighty Eye that uttered these words is itself one of the forms you possess. In another sense, it is a proclamation of nine thousand years (three thousand years of age and six thousand years in seventy-year units) sent forth by the Mighty Eye—and all these figures are beings of the highest rank.

In yet another sense: all Imams are a part of Ali, and Ali himself is their totality. That is, all Imams who have appeared or shall be revealed are Ali in a partial manifestation (hidden), and when Ali ibn Abī Ṭālib himself appears or shall be revealed, they appear as a whole (manifest). From beginning to end, Ali is Ali—or rather, Ali is the imprint upon all periods and cycles.[^cite-17]

## "I am the Knower of All Things"

Imam Ali (peace be upon him) declared: "I am the Knower of all things." ⟪ar:سیدنا مؤید⟫ The common understanding is that no creature has the right to make such a grand claim. In response, several arguments and proofs are advanced.

The first argument concerns ⟪ar:'علم'⟫ (knowledge)—a state that grants superiority to one being over others. For example, all humans are equal physically, whether small or large, ignorant or learned. Yet when a person becomes acquainted with knowledge and skills, reads the Qur'an, learns *tawḥīd*, learns to write, and acquires crafts useful for religion and worldly life, he inevitably attains honor and superiority over all his peers. Knowledge was thus a blessing and a miracle for him.[^cite-18]

Now, though impossible, let us hypothetically conceive of Allah as mere Essence. In our imagining, He would surely be a Perfect Essence whose like is unmatched. If He is perfect, it is useless to attribute knowledge to Him, for He needs not this virtue; giving it to Him does not increase His greatness, for He is already perfect. Conversely, if we deem His Essence imperfect and assume He gradually needs the attributes of All-Knowing, Almighty, and the like, this is the greatest defect for Allah's Essence—that His perfection depends upon knowledge of things external to Him, unrelated to His Essence itself, and thus non-existent to Him. This proves that knowledge is not an attribute exclusive to Allah.

Secondly, our souls attain nobility only through the knowledge of *tawḥīd*, for this knowledge is the highest and best. Yet we cannot say of Allah that He became knowing by acquiring knowledge of His creatures and deeds. Creatures are base and inferior. It is no honor for the superior to acquire the basest knowledge; rather, it is disgrace.

Thirdly, knowledge is defined as: the true form of the known enters the soul of the knower. This occurs in two ways: either the knower acquires knowledge of the known through acquisition, or the form of the known exists in the knower's self along with the known's existence. If we assume the imprints of the form cause the self-realization of the knower, this signifies a defect perfected by knowledge's acquisition. This cannot apply to Allah, for it implies He was first defective, then became knowing—first ignorant, then informed. If we say the form of the known existed in His knowledge from eternity, then the world and all therein become eternal with Allah's eternity, which we know is impossible, for the world is contingent—it did not always exist but was created.

Fourthly, knowledge encompasses the world's minor professions and crafts—farming, dyeing, tailoring. And these base industries are included in the verse: "He is the Knower of all things." Is Allah aware of the knowledge of coloring, sewing, and cultivating? If yes, it adds nothing to His greatness. If no, the verse becomes void.

Fifthly, some sciences and industries are base, and ignorance of them is preferable to knowledge. For example, deceit, betrayal, trickery, and those who practice them—the deceitful, traitors—are condemned. The knowledge of wine-making and pocket-cutting is undesirable. Does Allah know of these? Does such knowledge add glory to Him? Do humans ever appreciate these things? Allah has also mentioned His ignorance of much:

> ⟪quran 10:18⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَيَعْبُدُونَ مِن دُونِ ٱللَّهِ مَا لَا يَضُرُّهُمْ وَلَا يَنفَعُهُمْ وَيَقُولُونَ هَٓؤُلَآءِ شُفَعَٓؤُنَا عِندَ ٱللَّهِ ۚ قُلْ أَتُنَبِّـُٔونَ ٱللَّهُ بِمَا لَا يَعْلَمُ فِى ٱلسَّمَوَتِ وَلَا فِى ٱلْأَرْضِ ۚ سُبْحَنَهُۥ وَتَعَلَىٰ عَمَّا يُشْرِكُونَ ‎⟫[^cite-19]

From all these proofs, it is clear that ⟪ar:ھو بکل شئی علیم⟫—the verse "He is the Knower of all things"—does not refer to Allah, but to His chosen servants. The Prophet (peace and blessings of Allah be upon him) praised the Qur'an, saying: "Its exterior is elegant and its interior is profound; its wonders never cease, its marvels never end, and only through it are darknesses unveiled."[^cite-20]

The phrase "I am the Knower of all things" is as if the earth (could it speak) would say: "I am the Knower of all that exists upon me—in valleys, mountains, minerals, and animals. I am familiar with all physical substances and instruments of creation and annihilation." Would such a claim be wrong? Since the Amīr al-Mu'minīn is likewise the earth of all that is created for the hereafter, no one else knows every chapter and verse as you do. Nothing is hidden from you; no secret or mystery escapes you. For this reason, he said: "By Allah, though all veils were lifted from my face, my certainty would not increase."[^cite-21] The claim of the Amīr al-Mu'minīn—"I am the Knower of all things"—refers to this knowledge, which contains not the slightest trace of lordship.

## "I am He Who Raised Its Heavens and I am He Who Spread Its Earth"

When we contemplate human powers and capabilities, we see that Allah has granted him certain faculties and mighty powers resembling those of Allah. For example, man, through his psychic and intellectual powers—mere effects—extracts springs from the earth, pours water upon its surface, makes his fields verdant and flourishing, draws water from the depths to give life to man and beast, and from it grows grain providing sustenance. In modern times, he extracts oil, travels by land, sea, and air. If such are the powers of man, what can be said of the Amīr al-Mu'minīn's powers, whose station is totality in relation to each component?

We now establish the *Sharīʿa* argument alongside this rational proof, from the Messenger's own words: "Allah moved the Pen upon the Tablet and wrote upon it all that is created and revealed in the heavens, the earth, and between them." Therefore, what the Lawgiver calls the Tablet is ⟪ar:نفس کلیہ⟫ (the Universal Soul). What the Pen inscribed upon the Tablet shone forth and appeared to the people of the world. Thus the comparison between soul and soul is clear.[^cite-22]

The second *Sharīʿa* argument: the birthplace of both Prophet and Executor for the hereafter is the same as the Tablet and Pen for this worldly renaissance. Just as what the Pen writes upon the Tablet manifests in this world—nothing more, nothing less—so in the hereafter shall appear those forms attached to the Prophet and having taken refuge in the domain of Ali. None other can appear. Just as the physical heavens, earth, and mountains were inscribed beforehand upon the Tablet, so are the images of the hereafter's heavens, earth, and mountains.

His heavens are the ⟪ar:'وصی'⟫ (Executors) and the ⟪ar:'ائمۃ الھداہ'⟫ (Imams of Guidance), who rank higher than all humanity through divine support. From them, the human soul gains knowledge's sustenance, as bodies gain sustenance from the apparent heavens. On the other hand, they are the ⟪ar:ائمہ ھداۃ⟫ (the Guiding Imams)—stars and luminaries of the spiritual world who illuminate and brighten the human soul with their light and radiance, guiding it from error, as the stars of the outer heavens guide our bodies through darkness.

In the same way, these heavens—the Imams' realm—have their earth: the *Ḥujja* (Proof), *Naqīb* (Overseer), and *Dāʿī* (Summoner), who, through knowledge gained from them, produce all colorful, visible flowers and useful fruits, as the earth absorbs celestial substance and yields flowers and fruits. Similarly, the mountains of this spiritual earth are the strong members of their *daʿwa* (the summons) whose faith never wavers, as the outer mountains stand firm. Finally, the cultivation of this spiritual earth is those who accept the invitation—like the outer plants and trees, dwelling with varied knowledge and deeds.[^cite-23]

## Every Stone and Pebble Bears Witness to My Prophethood

The Messenger of Allah (peace and blessings of Allah be upon him) declared: "Every stone and pebble bears witness to my prophethood." This appears contrary to observation. How can this be confirmed?

Every thing—whether stone or pebble, and all persons and creatures—is encompassed by six dimensions of the body. There are no exceptions or exclusions from them. Every body is enclosed within these six dimensions. These six dimensions refer to the sixth prophet, the sixth era of Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him). The structure and existence of all things testify that nothing can exist beyond six limits. If religion is compared to the human body, it too is confined to six periods or six prophets.

| 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 | 5 | 6 |

| Forward | Backward | Right | Left | Top | Bottom |

There are seven days in a week, and each prophet is analogous to a day. Our Prophet (peace and blessings of Allah be upon him) is the sixth prophet, epitomizing Friday:

| 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 | 5 | 6 |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Adam | Noah | Abraham | Moses | Jesus | Muhammad |

## Whoever Loses Three Children Shall Be Shielded from the Fire

The Messenger of Allah (peace and blessings of Allah be upon him) declared: "Whoever has three of his children die and remains patient, asking for reward, shall be shielded from the Fire by them." One asked: "And he who has lost two children—shall he also receive this benefit?" The Prophet replied: "Yes, and why not?"

On the surface, this passage seems perplexing. If taken at face value, then any of the seventy-three sects of the community whose children die would be saved from hellfire. Yet another hadith states that of seventy-three sects, only one is saved; seventy-two are in the Fire. The Prophet declared: "My community will divide into seventy-three sects, of which only one is saved, and seventy-two are of the Fire." If the Prophet's words are accepted as true, there would be no distinction between the obedient and disobedient—both would be equal and deserve the same reward.

In truth, "children" here means spiritual children, not physical offspring. In *bāṭin* (esoteric) interpretation, "death" means the elevation of a believer from one station to a higher one. That is, to raise one's spiritual level and rank—as Sayyidinā al-Qāḍī al-Nuʿmān explained in his *Taʾwīl al-Daʿāʾim*. "Fire" refers to doubts and suspicions that ignite hearts, consuming faith to ashes and destroying souls.[^cite-28]

The hadith's meaning is this: when the *dāʿī al-muṭlaq* (the Absolute Summoner)—the spiritual father of believers—elevates one of his children (the believers) to the rank of *muqaddas*, then promotes the second (previously a *muʿaddhan*) to the rank of *maʿdhūn al-muṭlaq*, and then elevates the third (previously a *muʿaddhan*) to the rank of *dāʿī*, they themselves are freed from the burden of service. He appoints these three to answer the enemies' objections—objections that are collections of doubts—and retires from his own direct service. The responsibility of refuting falsehood no longer rests on the *dāʿī al-muṭlaq*. The duty of extinguishing and cooling the fire that enemies kindle in believers' hearts falls to the *dāʿī*, the *maʿdhūn*, and the *muqaddas*.

The people of falsehood are called "Fire" (*al-Nār*) because they are the hellfire that invites others toward the actual Fire. Similarly, the invitation of guidance is the potential Paradise, inviting toward actual Paradise. If the *dāʿī* cannot elevate three people to these three ranks, and can only raise two—one to *maʿdhūn al-muṭlaq* and another to *muqaddas*—then both suffice to confront the people of falsehood and give befitting answers to their weak objections. Both are enough; the *dāʿī* need not worry.[^cite-29]

*(Risālat 20, Qaul wa-l-Jawāb bi-l-Tawīl) — Sayyidinā ʿAlī ibn Sayyidinā Sulaymān (may Allah be pleased with him)*

## The Believer Eats in One Belly; The Disbeliever in Seven

The Messenger of Allah (peace and blessings of Allah be upon him) declared: "The believer eats in one belly; the disbeliever eats in seven bellies."[^cite-30] How is this possible? All humans possess the same intestinal system. There is no more or less. If it refers to eating little or much, we observe that many believers eat more than disbelievers.

Here too, the outward meaning is not intended. Rather, the purpose is this: a believer who accepts the invitation of the true Imam (peace be upon him) and enters the realm of faith with covenant and contract receives the sublime meanings and precious wisdom of the Holy Qur'an's words. Though the amount is small, he is satisfied with it. He needs no other sciences here and there. His heart and mind attain satisfaction and contentment through understanding the true meaning, and he becomes satiated. His hunger ceases.

Conversely, if a disbeliever or hypocrite seeks to understand the Qur'an to satisfy his spiritual hunger, he turns to self-styled scholars and false caliphs who interpret it with false meanings that fail to satisfy his spiritual appetite. Instead, they kindle hunger further. As a result, he remains perpetually hungry. This is the disbeliever's eating—much and insatiable—compared to the believer's eating—little yet content.[^cite-31]

At the surface level: the believer eats little, and the disbeliever and hypocrite eat much. The believer eats within the bounds of pure *Sharīʿa* and refrains from mingling the lawful and unlawful. The disbeliever keeps consuming without distinction between *ḥalāl* (permissible) and *ḥarām* (forbidden). Second, the believer eats at appointed times, refraining from untimely eating and drinking so his stomach remains pure. He eats to live and worship. Conversely, the disbeliever and hypocrite live to eat.[^cite-32]

## The Intention of the Believer Is Better Than His Deed

This hadith possesses clarity in its transmission: "The intention of the believer is better than his deed, and the intention of the disbeliever is worse than his deed." To grasp its meaning requires careful reflection.

When a believer intends to perform a righteous religious act yet finds himself constrained by circumstance and rendered unable to execute it, his reward remains secure. He shall receive the full recompense of his intention. Indeed, according to the prophetic tradition, the sincere intention of the believer becomes more beneficial than the outward action itself. [^cite-18]

Similarly, when a disbeliever harbors malevolent intention—especially to harm the awliyāʾ (friends and protectors) of Allah—and is subsequently prevented by compulsion from fulfilling that intention, his evil intention nonetheless exceeds his evil deeds in gravity. Punishment corresponds to the sincerity and depth of intention.

A hasty blow struck in anger or excitement does not carry the weight of carefully planned malice. Should a person fail in his design today, he will attempt again when opportunity arises, as did the hypocrites in the Prophet's ⟪ar:رسول اللہ صلی اللہ علیہ و آلہ و سلم⟫ own time. The intention reveals the trajectory of the soul.

### Wilāyat as the Counterpart of Intention

Faith comprises three elements:

| Confirmation by the Heart | Speech by the Tongue | Action by the Limbs |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Intention from the heart | Verbal affirmation | Physical conduct |
| Spiritual dimension | Spiritual manifestation | Physical manifestation |
| Potential | Actualized | Actualized |

Verbal confession represents a spiritual act, while action of hand and foot constitutes a physical expression. Both are inherently unified in intention and are manifested through tongue, hands, and feet. For this reason, the Prophet ⟪ar:رسول اللہ صلی اللہ علیہ و آلہ و سلم⟫ declared: "The intention of a believer surpasses his action, for Islam and faith are both founded upon intention and both exist through genuine faith." A modest action performed with pure intention exceeds abundant action devoid of sincere purpose. [^cite-19]

In the same manner, among the seven supplications (*duʿāʾ*) of Islam, the supplication of wilāyat (allegiance to the Imam) stands opposed to intention, while the remaining six supplications correspond to action. The supplication of wilāyat encompasses the remaining six with overwhelming power. For this reason, all other supplications prove ineffective without wilāyat. It forms the foundation upon which all religious practice rests.

## The Believer Is Like the Bee: He Consumes What Is Good and Produces What Is Good

The hadith states with clarity: "The believer is like a bee—he consumes what is good and brings forth what is good." This appears at first paradoxical, for although the bee ingests nectar, it expels waste from its body, a foul and malodorous substance. What, then, does this hadith signify?

The Prophet's ⟪ar:رسول اللہ صلی اللہ علیہ و آلہ و سلم⟫ words do not refer to outward sustenance, but to interior realities. The comparison of the believer to a bee should be understood thus: the bee draws the sweet juice from flowers and transforms it, expelling it as pure honey. In the same way, by "consuming what is good," the Prophet means the acquisition of righteous knowledge. The believer obtains knowledge that is correct from the people of truth, knowledge that brings peace and tranquility to heart and mind. He embodies these sciences throughout his life and ultimately manifests them in a form most noble and illuminating. At death, this subtle form separates from his body and ascends to his destination. This alone is the meaning of consuming what is good and producing what is good.

In another sense, the believer who acquires righteous knowledge discloses it to the world in the form of exemplary character. He learns beneficial sciences from his elders and imparts the same to others, so that they too may undergo spiritual formation. Just as bees benefit others through the production of honey, so too does the believer benefit humanity through the manifestation of virtue. [^cite-20]

## The First Thing Weighed in the Balance Is Good Character

The Holy Prophet ⟪ar:رسول اللہ صلی اللہ علیہ و آلہ و سلم⟫ declared: "The first thing weighed in the balance is good character (*akhlāq al-ḥasana*)." What does this signify?

The scales of divine justice point to the Imam of every age, for he alone gives true measure to every deed. Allah states:

> ⟪quran 17:71⟫
> ⟪ar:‏يَوْمَ نَدْعُوا۟ كُلَّ أُنَاسٍۭ بِإِمَمِهِمْ ۖ فَمَنْ أُوتِىَ كِتَبَهُۥ بِيَمِينِهِۦ فَأُو۟لَٓئِكَ يَقْرَءُونَ كِتَبَهُمْ وَلَا يُظْلَمُونَ فَتِيلًۭا ‎⟫

The Imam (al-ʿIṣma) stands as the scale (*al-mīzān*) by which Allah will reward and punish each person for his actions. In another place, Allah declares that on the Day of Resurrection He shall establish a scale with two pans—one in the east and one in the west. This signifies that the Imam stands holding both arms of the Sharīʿa: the outward and the inward wisdom. He exhorts his followers to remain connected to both dimensions. The outward law (action) is the east; the inner reality (knowledge) is the west.

| Outward (Mashriq) | Inward (Maghrub) |
| --- | --- |
| Action | Knowledge |

Thus on that Day it becomes manifest who has remained balanced in both matters and who has favored one while neglecting the other. Whoever embraces one and abandons the other shall be among those whose deeds are made light. Of such a person, the Almighty declares:

> ⟪quran 101:8⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَأَمَّا مَنْ خَفَّتْ مَوَزِينُهُۥ ‎⟫

> ⟪quran 101:9⟫
> ⟪ar:‏فَأُمُّهُۥ هَاوِيَةٌۭ ‎⟫

### The Meaning of Good Character

*Ḥasana* (goodness, beauty) refers to the true Imam, for he invites toward Paradise. Thus "good character" (*al-khuluq al-ḥasan*) alludes to the wilāyat (allegiance) and obedience (*tāʿa*) of Maulana Ali (ʿalayhi al-salām), which constitutes the very center of religion. Without wilāyat, nothing endures. All actions and acts of worship depend upon this wilāyat. Whoever disobeys the direction of the Imam and his followers finds his worship rejected. Of such people it is said in the criterion of the wise:

The testimony of the Imam Jaʿfar al-Ṣādiq (ʿalayhi al-salām) stands decisive: "Without our wilāyat, even if a person circumambulates between the Black Stone and the Station of Abraham night and day, Allah will not accept any of his deeds." This demonstrates that wilāyat forms the foundation of religion, upon which all Sharʿī worship turns. Without it, all endeavor becomes futile.

## Whoever Performs Many Prayers at Night Shall Have a Beautiful Face by Day

The hadith of the Prophet ⟪ar:رسول اللہ صلی اللہ علیہ و آلہ و سلم⟫ states: "Whoever prays abundantly at night shall have a beautiful countenance by day." Yet observation seems to contradict this. The winds of the morning weary the face of one who has kept vigil through the night. At dawn such a person feels exhausted and lacks inclination for work. By contrast, one who sleeps soundly awakens refreshed, with radiance visible upon his countenance. How shall we reconcile this with the hadith?

For those who penetrate to the inner meanings, the true prayer (*ṣalāt al-bāṭin*) refers to sincere service rendered to the true Imam (ʿalayhi al-salām). Connection with the Imam constitutes genuine prayer. The night symbolizes the period of occultation (*ghaybat*). Whoever devotes himself sincerely to the Imam's daʿwa (summons/invitation) during this period of concealment shall find his hereafter (*ākhira*) beautified. That is, in the period of unveiling (*kashf*), he shall witness the beauty of that spiritual form, and shall participate in the gathering of the Qa'im—which is the most magnificent form. Even in outward appearance, the beauty of the countenance becomes a reward for such service, conferring an additional benefit.

## Whoever Builds a Mosque, Even as Small as a Bird's Nest, Allah Shall Build for Him a House in Paradise

The Messenger of Allah ⟪ar:رسول اللہ صلی اللہ علیہ و آلہ و سلم⟫ brought glad tidings: "Whoever builds a mosque, even if it be as small as a bird's nest, Allah shall build for him a house in Paradise." How can this be? What benefit flows from such a small place? How could prayers, recitations, and devotions be performed therein? And if prayer cannot occur, in what sense could it be called a mosque? Such puzzlement merits clarification.

In the interpretive sense (*taʾwīl*), the bird (*ṭāʾir*) refers to the prayer-form (*ṣalāt*). Higher-ranked prayers resemble large birds, while lower-ranked prayers resemble smaller birds. The mosque signifies the rank and station of prayers.

With this understanding, consider the hadith afresh: whoever prepares a person capable of religious service, however humble his capacity, accomplishes something noble. If he trains an ordinary servant of the daʿwa, yet enables that servant to undertake scholarly service within the daʿwa al-Hādiyya (the Rightly-Guiding Invitation), then Allah shall grant him an exalted station in Paradise—specifically, in the divine realm (*Lāhūt al-Imāmī*), which is the true paradise. The daʿwa al-Hādiyya constitutes the paradise of truth, and the divine realm represents the Imam's paradise—the true Paradise itself. [^cite-21]

## There Is No Trial After Me More Harmful to Men Than Women

The Prophet ⟪ar:رسول اللہ صلی اللہ علیہ و آلہ و سلم⟫ declared: "There is no trial (*fitna*) after me that harms men more than women." Yet this seems paradoxical. Did not the Prophet himself strongly encourage marriage, saying, "Marry women who are fertile and loving, so that I may be proud of your numbers before the prophets"? How could the Messenger of Allah encourage men toward marriage while simultaneously warning them of women as a peril? [^cite-22]

In the interpretive sense, "men" (*rijāl*) refers to scholars and teachers—those called the "beneficiaries" (*al-mufīdūn*). "Women" (*nisāʾ*) denotes learners or disciples—those called the "beneficiaries" (*al-mustafīdūn*). The Prophet here refers to the Imams, who instruct the Ummah in the sciences of the Sharīʿa, taʾwīl (esoteric exegesis), and reality (*haqīqa*). They are supreme; all others are beneath them and stand in need of their knowledge and guidance, while they themselves require nothing from anyone. Allah declares:

> ⟪quran 3:28⟫
> ⟪ar:‏لَّا يَتَّخِذِ ٱلْمُؤْمِنُونَ ٱلْكَفِرِينَ أَوْلِيَآءَ مِن دُونِ ٱلْمُؤْمِنِينَ ۖ وَمَن يَفْعَلْ ذَلِكَ فَلَيْسَ مِنَ ٱللَّهِ فِى شَىْءٍ إِلَّآ أَن تَتَّقُوا۟ مِنْهُمْ تُقَىٰةًۭ ۗ وَيُحَذِّرُكُمُ ٱللَّهُ نَفْسَهُۥ ۗ وَإِلَى ٱللَّهِ ٱلْمَصِيرُ ‎⟫

By this decree, the Imams (ʿalayhim al-salām) are the men (*rijāl*), and all others are those inferior, weaker, and subordinate to them.

## The Hour Shall Not Come Until Men Are Few and Women Are Many

The Prophet Ṣādiq (ʿalayhi al-salām) prophesied: "The Hour shall not come until men are reduced in number and women become abundant." What is the meaning of this saying?

The coming of the Hour (*qiyāmat*) refers to the appearance of Maulana al-Qāʾim (ʿalayhi al-salām). The Messenger of Allah ⟪ar:رسول اللہ صلی اللہ علیہ و آلہ و سلم⟫ indicates that the Qa'im will emerge when the scholars (*rijāl*) of the Prophet's religion—those of the daʿwa al-Hādiyya of the rank of the Mufīdūn—have diminished for some reason. Either the Imams' daʿwa shall be concealed through extreme taqiyya (*dissimulation*), preventing open service to the cause, or the scholars shall decline through the daʿwa's suppression and hardship, their successors becoming fewer. And al-Nisāʾ (women)—those of falsehood who constitute trials—shall prevail. Their numbers shall multiply; their mischief shall become unbearable. At such a critical juncture, the Qāʾim ʿalā Dhikr al-Islām (the Upholder of Islam's Remembrance) shall suddenly appear.

## Anoint Yourselves with Earth, for Truly Earth Is Kind to You

The Prophet ⟪ar:رسول اللہ صلی اللہ علیہ و آلہ و سلم⟫ said: "Anoint the earth (for tayammum, purification by earth)—truly the earth is kind to you." What does this signify? The Prophet gave a general command, yet made no distinction regarding holy and unholy earth. Yet surely it is not permissible to perform tayammum with unclean or defiled soil. What good, then, does earth represent?

Here the earth symbolizes the Imam of the Age—the center of truth. To anoint oneself is to maintain connection with him and to pledge allegiance by extending one's hand, just as one who performs tayammum touches the earth and places his hand upon it, and the dust that clings to his hand brings purification. What greater excellence or goodness could exist than to purify the impure, rendering such a one worthy of prayer and deserving entry into Paradise? Without connection to this Imam's daʿwa, it is impossible to attain such purification.

In another sense, "earth" refers to the proof (*ḥujjat*) of Imam al-Zaman (ʿalayhi al-salām). This means: whoever anoints himself with the proof of Imam al-Zaman—whoever develops a spiritual bond with him and derives from his blessings of knowledge and sanctity—has attained supreme goodness beyond all other goods. Therefore, both the daʿwa al-Hādiyya and the Proof of Imam al-Zaman are earth. The question of purity and impurity does not arise; both are pure. [^cite-23]

## The Fewest Inhabitants of Paradise Shall Be Women

The Prophet ⟪ar:رسول اللہ صلی اللہ علیہ و آلہ و سلم⟫ declared: "The fewest among the inhabitants of Paradise shall be women." What does this mean?

Here, Paradise (*Jannah*) refers to the daʿwa al-Hādiyya, which is Paradise itself (*Jannah al-Quwwā*). Women (*al-Nisāʾ*) denotes the group of beneficiaries, that is, disciples. In the same way, men (*al-Rijāl*) signify the Mufīdūn (teachers).

The meaning of this hadith is that among the ranks of daʿwa, those in the upper limits—the Mufīdūn—perform the duties of the daʿwa. They remain devoted to teaching and learning, combating the enemies of the Invitation through scholarly argument. In the daʿwa of Imam al-Zaman (the Paradise of the Imam), there are many who serve the cause of the Hādiyya. Yet there are fewer who occupy the ranks of women—those unable to benefit others through knowledge, but who are themselves in need of benefit. The saying thus means: "In the Imam's temple, there shall be fewer beneficiaries and more benefactors." [^cite-24]

## The Punishment of This Ummah Has Been Placed in This World

The Messenger of Allah ⟪ar:رسول اللہ صلی اللہ علیہ و آلہ و سلم⟫ said: "The punishment of this Ummah is in this world." This statement appears to negate the punishment of the Hereafter. Yet the Qur'an contains many promises and warnings regarding the Hereafter. How can this hadith be reconciled with Qur'ānic verses? The Prophet himself forbade acceptance of hadīths presented in isolation and commanded: "Whatever command or report is conveyed to you in my name, measure it against the verses of the Qur'ān. If it accords with the Qur'ān, accept it. If it contradicts the Qur'ān, reject it, for I speak nothing contrary to the Qur'ān." How, then, shall we establish harmony between this hadīth and the Qur'ān?

### The True Meaning of This Ummah

In this hadīth, "Ummah" refers to the true community that follows in the Prophet's footsteps—a succession of Imams descended from him. This Ummah itself walks in guidance and guides others. Among them are well-known personalities, foremost among whom is Maulana Amīr al-Muʾminīn ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib, the successor of the Prophet, and then the Infallible Imams who are his descendants.

The meaning now becomes clear: this true Ummah of the Prophet ⟪ar:رسول اللہ صلی اللہ علیہ و آلہ و سلم⟫ has endured immense hardship and affliction from enemies. Their rights were usurped; they were slain; their women widowed and children orphaned. They were expelled from the battles of Badr and Hunayn, Khaybar and Khandaq, from Jamal, Siffīn, Nahrawān, and Karbalāʾ. The hatred concealed in the hearts of those who embraced Islam through hypocrisy was revealed when your descendants—your true Ummah—were martyred and imprisoned, their hearts thereby cooled. The Messenger of Allah ⟪ar:رسول اللہ صلی اللہ علیہ و آلہ و سلم⟫ proclaimed: "What has befallen my Ummah in this world has sufficed. Now my Ummah shall not face punishment in the Hereafter." [^cite-25]

### The Reason for People's Confusion

Through the knowledge of taʾwīl obtained from the elders of the daʿwa al-Hādiyya, one can establish proper harmony between hadīths and Qur'ānic verses. Those who abandoned the Imams and relied upon their own opinions and conjectures were led further astray. For example, a group called the ʿAbdiyya—followers of one ʿAbdullah, a fierce enemy of the true Imams—compiled hadīths and compared them with Qur'ānic verses, yet reached erroneous conclusions. They portrayed Allah as an embodied human form with hands, feet, face, and other limbs, basing this on the hadīth: "Allah created Adam in His image." They said: "Allah states:

> ⟪quran 28:88⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَلَا تَدْعُ مَعَ ٱللَّهِ إِلَهًا آخَرَ ۘ لَآ إِلَهَ إِلَّا هُوَ ۚ كُلُّ شَىْءٍ هَالِكٌ إِلَّا وَجْهَهُۥ ۚ لَهُ ٱلْحُكْمُ وَإِلَيْهِ تُرْجَعُونَ ‎⟫

This proves that Allah has a face." They continued: "Allah says:

This proves Allah has hands." And further: "Allah declares:

> ⟪quran 39:56⟫

This proves that Allah possesses a side (*janb*)." In this manner, by assembling these limbs, they fashioned a corporeal form and body for Allah.

Such reasoning ignores the immense difficulty it creates: Allah becomes embodied, resembling a creature. The Creator becomes as the created. The Creator is transformed into a creature bearing the defects inherent in creaturely existence. Those who abandon the Imams (ʿalayhim al-salām), who derive no benefit from their knowledge of taʾwīl, but rather rely upon opinion and conjecture, are similarly led astray and cast aspersions upon the unity and holiness of the Almighty. May Allah preserve us from such misguidance! [^cite-26]

## Whoever Knows His Soul Has Known His Lord

This hadīth belongs among those specific statements for which the Prophet ⟪ar:رسول اللہ صلی اللہ علیہ و آلہ و سلم⟫ declared: "I have been sent with comprehensive speech and brief words of wisdom." When someone asked him to make known Allah, he replied: "Whoever knows his own soul has thereby come to know his Lord." On another occasion he said: "The one among you most knowledgeable of his own soul is the one most knowledgeable of his Lord."

The Messenger of Allah thus made clear that knowledge of the Lord becomes impossible except through knowledge of the soul. How can we know the Essence that created our souls, bestowed upon us intellect, using that very intellect? If man lacks the capacity to comprehend fully his own self, how can he attain knowledge of his Lord who created him?

It is therefore impossible to know Him! One can only confess His existence. Knowledge and recognition always flow from the higher to the lower. Three realities are mentioned in this hadīth:

| Intelligence (*ʿAql*) | Soul (*Nafs*) | Lord (*Rabb*) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Intellect | Spirit | The Divine |

In this hadīth, ⟪ar:مَن⟫ (who, he who) refers to the one possessing intellect who strives to recognize the inner capacities of the soul and through those capacities moves toward knowledge and recognition of his Lord.

### For the People of the Outward

For those focused on the literal meaning (*Ẓāhir*), the hadīth signifies this: when a person contemplates his own birth and observes how Allah created for him from clay—from the ⟪ar:اجزاء غذائیۃ⟫ (nutritious elements) and ⟪ar:سلالۃ⟫ (pure extraction) and ⟪ar:نطفۃ⟫ (drop of sperm)—then transformed it into ⟪ar:عَلَقَۃ⟫ (clot) and then into ⟪ar:مُضغَۃ⟫ (chewed lump) and then into ⟪ar:عظام⟫ (bones), over which He placed ⟪ar:لحم⟫ (flesh), and breathed into it the Spirit, and brought it forth upon the face of the earth, and made it the ruler of all things therein—when he sees how Allah nourished him with His bounties and gave him to drink, he realizes that he did not bring himself into existence. There must be another who created him from nothingness. Reflecting further, he observes that at birth he was exceedingly weak. Gradually he gained strength, then old age came upon him, as Allah declares:

> ⟪quran 30:54⟫
> ⟪ar:‏ ٱللَّهُ ٱلَّذِى خَلَقَكُم مِّن ضَعْفٍۢ ثُمَّ جَعَلَ مِنۢ بَعْدِ ضَعْفٍۢ قُوَّةًۭ ثُمَّ جَعَلَ مِنۢ بَعْدِ قُوَّةٍۢ ضَعْفًۭا وَشَيْبَةًۭ ۚ يَخْلُقُ مَا يَشَآءُ ۖ وَهُوَ ٱلْعَلِيمُ ٱلْقَدِيرُ ‎⟫

In another place, the Qur'ān relates:

> ⟪quran 18:37⟫

### Direct and Indirect Creation

Those who contemplate inner realities recognize that man is not a direct creation of Allah. The Creator transcends dependence on time and place in any action. Similarly, He is not dependent on ⟪ar:عناصر اربع⟫ (the four elements: fire, air, water, earth). It is only man who requires all manner of tools and conditions to create or produce something. Not so the Creator! He is free and pure from all such dependencies. To attribute such things to Allah in the creation of man would be to characterize Him as weak, incapable, and limited—like a human being. Does Allah, like man, require paper, ink, pen, and hand to write? Does He require soil, womb, and nine months to create humanity? Such a belief contradicts the true concept of divine unity.

We therefore say that the human being, who emerges gradually from clay and soil, is not a direct creation of Allah but an indirect one. The evidence is clear: man is created in the world where he dwells and sustains himself. Man depends upon this world for survival and existence. Yet this world does not itself depend upon all the things and conditions necessary for man's origin. This fact establishes a clear distinction between man and world. The world itself requires neither tools nor time nor space for its own creation; it is directly a creation of Allah. Man, being created from the ⟪ar:عناصر اربع⟫ (four elements) of this world, is therefore an indirect creation. Allah declared regarding the world's creation:

> ⟪quran 16:40⟫
> ⟪ar:‏إِنَّمَا قَوْلُنَا لِشَىْءٍ إِذَآ أَرَدْنَهُ أَن نَّقُولَ لَهُۥ كُن فَيَكُونُ ‎⟫

Regarding the birth of man, He states:

> ⟪quran 10:3⟫
> ⟪ar:‏إِنَّ رَبَّكُمُ ٱللَّهُ ٱلَّذِى خَلَقَ ٱلسَّمَوَتِ وَٱلْأَرْضَ فِى سِتَّةِ أَيَّامٍۢ ثُمَّ ٱسْتَوَىٰ عَلَى ٱلْعَرْشِ ۖ يُدَبِّرُ ٱلْأَمْرَ ۖ مَا مِن شَفِيعٍ إِلَّا مِنۢ بَعْدِ إِذْنِهِۦ ۚ ذَلِكُمُ ٱللَّهُ رَبُّكُمْ فَٱعْبُدُوهُ ۚ أَفَلَا تَذَكَّرُونَ ‎⟫

### Constituent Parts Versus Effects

⟪ar:سیدنا مؤید اعلی اﷲ قدسہ⟫ explaining this hadīth further, states: "Man is divided into two aspects. One is body; the other is soul. The body comprises four elements: fire, air, water, earth. He draws his earthy part from sustenance, his aerial part from breath through the nostrils, his fiery part from the warmth generated in the body, and his watery part from moisture. 

Beyond this dense physical body exists a subtle reality through which man encompasses heavens and earth within the center of his consciousness and thoughts, without physical distress or corporeal movement. This force is commonly called the ⟪ar:نفس ناطقۃ⟫ (rational soul). Through this rational soul, he is distinguished as human—⟪ar:اشرف المخلوقات⟫ (the most noble of creations)—and separated from ⟪ar:نفس حیوانیۃ⟫ (animal soul).

Beyond this, there exists within the self another reality, more subtle still, which is intellect (*ʿaql*), through which man discerns good from evil. Logic demands that, like the body, there exist a center of soul and intellect from which they derive their relationship. We call this center the ⟪ar:نفس کلیۃ⟫ (universal soul) and ⟪ar:عقل کلیۃ⟫ (universal intellect), for all the particular souls and intellects of this physical world are partial and derive their powers from this center.

In this manner, we establish that man is, in his bodily aspect, a child of the natural world, and in his soul and intellect, a child of another realm. Thus man is the offspring of two worlds." [^cite-27]

| Body | Soul | Intellect |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Four Elements (Fire, Air, Water, Soil) | Universal Soul (*Nafs Kaliyya*) | Universal Intellect (*ʿAql Kaliyya*) |

We observe that the portion man receives from earth is infinitesimal—impossible to relate to the earth's totality. What proportion exists between human soil and terrestrial soil? What relation between the air man breathes and the atmosphere? What ratio between bodily heat and cosmic fire? What comparison between bodily moisture and aqueous spheres? No meaningful proportion can be established. **The four constitutive elements of the human body are parts of those ⟪ar:عناصر اربع⟫, not effects thereof.**

Turning then to ⟪ar:عالم نفس کلی⟫ (the realm of universal soul) and ⟪ar:عقل کلی⟫ (universal intellect), we see that the human ⟪ar:نفس اور عقل⟫ (soul and intellect) are both partial, and their effect upon ⟪ar:عالم کل⟫ (the universal realm) is not partial but nothing! **We thus conclude that no relation whatsoever can be established between human ⟪ar:نفس و عقل⟫ (soul and intellect) and ⟪ar:عالم جسم⟫ (the physical world) or ⟪ar:عالم نفس و عقل⟫ (the realm of soul and intellect), despite infinite effort.**

When man is born of ⟪ar:عالم نفس و عقل⟫ and yet cannot establish relation with it despite that connection, how then shall he attain the knowledge and capacity to comprehend the reality of divine unity (*tawḥīd*) from the ⟪ar:مبدِع تعالی⟫ (Originator, Most High), with whom no relation exists whatsoever?

Thus man recognizes that what he deemed monotheism is but a mirage. He then understands the truth of ⟪ar:مولانا علی⟫'s saying: ⟪ar-quote:اَلعِجزُ عَن دَرَکِ اِلأدرَاکِہِ اِدراک⟫ (inability to comprehend comprehension itself is comprehension), for such incapacity is itself true realization regarding the ⟪ar:مبد تعالی⟫. Thus he confirms the Prophet's declaration: "When he cannot know his own soul, how can he know his Lord?" [^cite-28]

## The Grave: Either a Garden of Paradise or a Pit of Hell

The Prophet ⟪ar:رسول اللہ صلی اللہ علیہ و آلہ و سلم⟫ declared: "The grave is either one of the gardens of Paradise or one of the pits of Hell." A second hadīth states: "After death there is no leisure for acquiring knowledge, and there is no home after this world save Paradise or Hell." A third proclaims: "Whoever dies shall have his resurrection established."

These three hadīths appear to contradict the verses of the Qur'ān, which speaks of a Day of Reckoning. We understand that the Day of Known Judgment refers to Maulana al-Qāʾim al-Qiyāma, the Upholder whose blessed hand shall execute justice—sending the people of Paradise to Paradise and the people of Hell to Hell. Yet in Surah al-Muʾminūn, Allah says that punishment and reward do not come immediately after death but only after a long period. How then shall we establish harmony between the hadīths and the Qur'ānic verses?

### The Truth of the Hadīths

The words of the Messenger of Allah are indeed true and not contrary to the Qur'ān, provided that one refers to the people of remembrance and the possessors of knowledge and seeks their guidance in interpreting the meaning.

#### The Garden or Pit of the Grave

When a believer dies, his form departs from his physical body. At that moment, he becomes connected and merged with his superior. The two unite in such manner that no distinction remains between them. Now, in the interpretive sense, the true grave of the believer is the higher state (*ḥadd ʿālī*) of the one with whom he is merged—the one with whom he becomes unified, just as the outward body becomes one with the grave in which it is buried. Thus, the soul in the Most High state—which is the spiritual grave of the believer—becomes one of the treasures of Paradise after death. For upon arriving there, the believer's soul savors the fragrance of comfort, tranquility, contentment, and knowledge. This is the first step or station in the ascent toward the Paradise of Eternity (*Jannah al-ʿAdn*, also understood as *Lāhūt al-Imāmī*, the divine realm of the Imam).

By this same principle, the grave becomes a pit of Hell when the enemy of Ahl al-Bayt (ʿalayhim al-salām) or a disbeliever dies. The evil, impure, and filthy conception he has fashioned through enmity and unlawful deeds separates from his body at death, wandering for a time before settling in foul, fetid, horrible, and terrifying places. This is the first gate of Hell toward which he is directed, where he shall suffer. His sensuality spreads through his depraved form; the parts of his body rot and disperse in such evil places, where fires perpetually burn. Such a painful place of torment becomes his grave—one of the pits of Hell.

These two states—wherein the believer attains every comfort and tranquility, and wherein the disbeliever suffers punishment from his corrupt form and soul in painful unclean places after bodily dispersal—constitute their *Barzakh* until the Day of Resurrection, when they shall be raised. Then, on the Day of Judgment—when the Qāʾim of the Remembrance appears, peace be upon him—the final reckoning shall come. [^cite-29]

#### Death and Resurrection

The meaning of the second and third hadīths is thus: the death of every person constitutes a partial resurrection for him. Many of the events that shall occur at the Day of Great Judgment transpire immediately after death, leaving only the final complete resurrection on the Day of Judgment itself.

The believer perceives a beautiful and noble form—the manifestation of his righteous deeds—and feels joy after death in contemplating his life. Similarly, the disbeliever witnesses the horror of his evil actions. As Amīr al-Muʾminīn said to Ḥarīth al-Hamdānī: "O Ḥarīth! I swear that whoever dies shall surely encounter me. For the believer, I am a herald of glad tidings; for the disbeliever, I am a messenger of ill news." Both believers and disbelievers witness their time and reflect upon what shall occur at the Great Reckoning.

The disbeliever moreover realizes that the door of repentance has closed. Allah can no longer be appeased through seeking forgiveness. He shall receive no pardon and must now endure whatever punishment is decreed. The Prophet ⟪ar:رسول اللہ صلی اللہ علیہ و آلہ و سلم⟫ thereby indicated that there is no third dwelling after death save Paradise and Hell.

*(Risalat 20, Qawal Wal-Jawab Baltaweel) Syedna Ali ibn Sayyiduna Sulaiman (may Allah be pleased with him)*

## Between My Grave and My Pulpit Is a Garden of Paradise

The hadīth states clearly: "Between my grave and my pulpit is a garden from the gardens of Paradise." What is signified here?

In explanation, the Duʿāt indicate that the grave is the place that conceals the entire person. The pulpit, by contrast, is the elevated place in the mosque from which the Imam (ʿalayhi al-salām) openly delivers the sermon and address. The pulpit of the mosque stands in a high and conspicuous place so that when the Imam ascends it, all listeners can see him clearly. In essence, the grave's function is concealment; the pulpit's function is manifestation.

In this sense, the grave of Muḥammad al-Muṣṭafā ⟪ar:رسول اللہ صلی اللہ علیہ و آلہ و سلم⟫ is in truth Maulana ʿAlī (ʿalayhi al-salām), for all the knowledge and wisdom of the Prophet was buried and hidden within him. ʿAlī concealed all the Prophet's sciences and wisdom in his breast. The pulpit of the Prophet, conversely, refers to the blessed personality of al-Qāʾim ʿalā Dhikrih (ʿalayhi al-salām), who, upon his appearance, shall publicly broadcast the sciences that ʿAlī preserved in secrecy.

The Paradise between grave and pulpit refers to the succession of Imams (ʿalayhim al-salām) between ʿAlī and the Qāʾim. They constitute the Paradise of the believer's form and soul, gardens where trees bear the fruits of wisdom and where the fragrance of knowledge's flowers pervades the air. [^cite-30]

## Adhere to the Qur'ān, for It Is the Intercessor Whose Intercession Is Accepted

Concerning the excellence of the Qur'ān, the Prophet ⟪ar:رسول اللہ صلی اللہ علیہ و آلہ و سلم⟫ said:

The Qur'ān is the Word of Allah, the Intercessor whose intercession is accepted, the witness whose testimony is true, and the authenticator of the Imamate. He said: "Verily, truthfulness, deeds, rewards, and judgments with justice."

Recite the Qur'ān and follow it. It is the intercessor, and its intercession shall be heard. It is the witness, and its testimony is truthful. Whoever makes it go before him (or takes it as his Imam), it shall lead him to Paradise. Whoever makes it go behind him shall be led to Hell. The Qur'ān is a guide to the best path of Allah. Whoever speaks according to the Qur'ān speaks truth. Whoever acts upon it shall be rewarded. Whoever judges according to it shall have judged with justice.

Does this hadīth then refer to ʿAlī (ʿalayhi al-salām)?

### The Qur'ān and ʿAlī

Surely the hadīth refers to the Qur'ān itself! This does not diminish its value, prestige, virtue, nobility, and magnificence. These remain secure. At the Battle of Siffīn, the Holy Prophet ⟪ar:رسول اللہ صلی اللہ علیہ و آلہ و سلم⟫ recited:

This hadīth also refers to the Imam of all times, who is the speaking manifestation of the Book of the Prophet. All these individuals are the speaking guides (*natiq*) of their respective ages. They are the witnesses whose testimony has been verified! Whoever takes them as his Imam shall be guided toward Paradise. Whoever disbelieves in them shall be sent toward Hell. Whoever acknowledges their Imamate speaks truth and follows their command, earning great reward. Whoever judges according to the Sharīʿa of their grandfather, the Messenger of Allah ⟪ar:رسول اللہ صلی اللہ علیہ و آلہ و سلم⟫, shall have judged with justice; otherwise, he shall have acted with injustice. ## The Likeness of My Ahl al-Bayt to Noah's Ark

The Prophet ⟪ar:رسول اﷲ (ﷺ)⟫ likened his Ahl al-Bayt (the Prophet's Household) to Noah's Ark, declaring: "The likeness of my Ahl al-Bayt is like Noah's Ark—whoever embarks upon it is saved, and whoever remains behind is drowned and perishes." This comparison carries profound significance: it reveals that the Prophet foresaw peril ahead, for otherwise the metaphor of a vessel navigating storm-tossed waters would hold no meaning.[^cite-25]

All narratives preserved in the Qur'an serve a single, unified purpose: to demonstrate that what has occurred in former times shall recur in latter ages. Indeed, after the Messenger of Allāh ⟪ar:رسول اﷲ (ﷺ)⟫, those very events reappeared, though clothed in different garments. In the story of Noah's Ark we discern a pattern repeated: after the Prophet's departure, a tempest of innovations (*bida*, singular *bidʿa*) arose within the body of Islām, submerging the souls of the Muslim *Umma* (community) beneath waves of doubt and bewilderment. Yet in the midst of such turbulent waters, the Prophet had prepared the vessel of the Ahl al-Bayt to afford refuge from the storm. Just as few—scarcely eighty—souls boarded Noah's Ark, so too are those who join the fellowship of the Ahl al-Bayt few in number:

> ⟪quran 11:40⟫
> ⟪ar:‏حَتَّىٰٓ إِذَا جَآءَ أَمْرُنَا وَفَارَ ٱلتَّنُّورُ قُلْنَا ٱحْمِلْ فِيهَا مِن كُلٍّۢ زَوْجَيْنِ ٱثْنَيْنِ وَأَهْلَكَ إِلَّا مَن سَبَقَ عَلَيْهِ ٱلْقَوْلُ وَمَنْ آمَنَ ۚ وَمَآ آمَنَ مَعَهُۥٓ إِلَّا قَلِيلٌۭ ‎⟫

Yet in the sight of Allāh, the weight of these few transcends all earthly measure. Each is accounted an entire *Umma* (community), and those deemed humble in our reckoning are, in His wisdom, magnified. Concerning certain believers with Noah (peace be upon him):

> ⟪quran 11:48⟫
> ⟪ar:‏قِيلَ يَنُوحُ ٱهْبِطْ بِسَلَمٍۢ مِّنَّا وَبَرَكَتٍ عَلَيْكَ وَعَلَىٰٓ أُمَمٍۢ مِّمَّن مَّعَكَ ۚ وَأُمَمٌۭ سَنُمَتِّعُهُمْ ثُمَّ يَمَسُّهُم مِّنَّا عَذَابٌ أَلِيمٌۭ ‎⟫

In like manner, the Prophet Ibrāhīm (Abraham), though standing alone, was designated by the term "*Umma*":

> ⟪quran 16:120⟫
> ⟪ar:‏إِنَّ إِبْرَهِيمَ كَانَ أُمَّةًۭ قَانِتًۭا لِّلَّهِ حَنِيفًۭا وَلَمْ يَكُ مِنَ ٱلْمُشْرِكِينَ ‎⟫

These figures all possessed a gathering of believers; thus they merit the designation *Umma*.[^cite-26]

In brief: multitudes were submerged beneath the flood of innovations and doubts. Yet the few who boarded the Prophet's vessel—the Ahl al-Bayt—were preserved from error and attached themselves to the Family of the Prophet. Despite their scarcity, these souls constitute the true *Ahl al-Sunna wa-l-Jamāʿa* (the People of the Sunna and the Community) according to Qur'ānic testimony, for they stand united in doctrine and practice.

### The Reason for the Shortness of Truth

Imām ʿAlī (peace be upon him) was once asked, "Who are the Ahl al-Sunna wa-l-Jamāʿa?" He replied: "Myself and my companions—however few they may number." When pressed further—"And who are the people of innovation and division?"—he answered: "My opponents, though they exceed in multitude." Allāh has condemned such numerical abundance:

> ⟪quran 6:116⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَإِن تُطِعْ أَكْثَرَ مَن فِى ٱلْأَرْضِ يُضِلُّوكَ عَن سَبِيلِ ٱللَّهِ ۚ إِن يَتَّبِعُونَ إِلَّا ٱلظَّنَّ وَإِنْ هُمْ إِلَّا يَخْرُصُونَ ‎⟫

and again:

> ⟪quran 43:78⟫
> ⟪ar:‏لَقَدْ جِئْنَكُم بِٱلْحَقِّ وَلَكِنَّ أَكْثَرَكُمْ لِلْحَقِّ كَرِهُونَ ‎⟫

Yet Allāh praises the sparse companions of Truth repeatedly:

> ⟪quran 34:13⟫
> ⟪ar:‏يَعْمَلُونَ لَهُۥ مَا يَشَآءُ مِن مَّحَرِيبَ وَتَمَثِيلَ وَجِفَانٍۢ كَٱلْجَوَابِ وَقُدُورٍۢ رَّاسِيَتٍۚ ٱعْمَلُوٓا۟ آلَ دَاوُۥدَ شُكْرًۭاۚ وَقَلِيلٌۭ مِّنْ عِبَادِىَ ٱلشَّكُورُ ‎⟫

The essence of all things is ever rare and concentrated. Among humankind, the learned and wise are few whilst the ignorant and debased abound. Among the stars of heaven, seven shine forth whilst countless others remain unnumbered. Precious gems lie sparse within the earth's stones, whilst common pebbles blanket the ground. Similarly, prophets and saints appear seldom amongst human populations, whilst the multitude consists of the heedless and foolish.[^cite-27]

### Interpretation of Taking Couples in the Ark

A subtle dimension emerges in Noah's narrative when Allāh commanded him whilst aboard the Ark:

> ⟪quran 11:40⟫
> ⟪ar:‏حَتَّىٰٓ إِذَا جَآءَ أَمْرُنَا وَفَارَ ٱلتَّنُّورُ قُلْنَا ٱحْمِلْ فِيهَا مِن كُلٍّۢ زَوْجَيْنِ ٱثْنَيْنِ وَأَهْلَكَ إِلَّا مَن سَبَقَ عَلَيْهِ ٱلْقَوْلُ وَمَنْ آمَنَ ۚ وَمَآ آمَنَ مَعَهُۥٓ إِلَّا قَلِيلٌۭ ‎⟫

In this vessel of the Ahl al-Bayt, such meanings unfold: the Ahl al-Bayt related every outward command of the Prophet to its inward reality, and perceived in each parable a reflection of deeper truths. They furnished each word with meaning and each noun with a name; they fashioned pairs (*azwāj*, singular *zawj*) within all practices, beliefs, and devotions—one dwelling in the palace of the body, the other in the palace of the soul. Outwardly there are words; inwardly, their taʾwīl (esoteric exegesis) and true reality. Action bereft of knowledge is a body without soul; knowledge devoid of action is a soul without body—neither can endure.[^cite-28]

Then Allāh declares concerning Noah's Ark:

> ⟪quran 11:42⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَهِىَ تَجْرِى بِهِمْ فِى مَوْجٍۢ كَٱلْجِبَالِ وَنَادَىٰ نُوحٌ ٱبْنَهُۥ وَكَانَ فِى مَعْزِلٍۢ يَبُنَىَّ ٱرْكَب مَّعَنَا وَلَا تَكُن مَّعَ ٱلْكَفِرِينَ ‎⟫

The same holds true of the Ahl al-Bayt: these Imāms of the Ahl al-Bayt (themselves the vessel) continue to guide the believers who traverse in their wake, steering them through the straight path of salvation whilst confronting the tremendous, misguided waves of innovation that assault them from without, and shielding them from all perils.

They deceive the people by presenting themselves garbed in the guise of devils masquerading as scholars. These caliphs are effigies without soul and devoid of life—into them has not been breathed the spirit of true eternal existence; that is, the Messenger of Allāh ⟪ar:رسول اﷲ (ﷺ)⟫ has not sealed them with his divinely appointed authority.

All these Imāms are holy souls whose covenant with the Lord of the Worlds is perpetually renewed through the Holy Spirit.[^cite-29]

## The Tradition of Reciting the Basmalah Over Water

It is related in hadith that on the Day of Resurrection, Hell shall roar and bellow like a lion and dragon. ⟪ar:رسول اﷲ (ﷺ)⟫ They shall bring forth a cup of water and recite upon it ⟪ar:بسم اﷲ الرحمن الرحیم⟫ and blow into it. When they pour this water into Hell, its fire shall be at once extinguished, and its voice silenced. Then it shall be called out by the decree of Allāh the Exalted: "O Fire! What has befallen you that you have grown quiet and become cowed?" Hell shall reply: "O Lord! I possess no power against Your name."

The taʾwīl (esoteric interpretation) of this hadith unfolds thus: the faction of falsehood and those hostile to the Ahl al-Bayt are in reality an exemplar of Hell itself, burning its followers in flames of doubt and suspicion. When they question it with inquiries to which it can offer no reply, it roars before them in threat, attempting to silence them through the outward force of tongues or fearful invocation of Allāh's punishment. Yet even this thunder is but hollow show.

⟪ar:رسول اﷲ (ﷺ)⟫ Or, alternatively: Imām al-Zamān (the Imam of the Age), peace be upon him, is the true *Asm* Allāh (the Name of God) ⟪ar:أسم اﷲ⟫, and the cup of water upon which the Name is recited ⟪ar:بسم اﷲ⟫ represents the covenant-keeper who has sworn allegiance to Imām al-Zamān. This seeker (*mustajibb*, one who answers the call) is a humble disciple of the Imām, and like a cup of water compared to an infinite ocean of knowledge. Yet when this seeker questions an apparent scholar (the figure of Hell) and poses even a simple query in pursuit of rational argument, that scholar becomes abased and loses all capacity to counter him. His voice grows faint, and all his verbal prowess drains away.[^cite-30]
