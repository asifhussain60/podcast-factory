# Interpretation of the Hadiths of the Prophet (peace be upon him)

## The Love of Ali as a Virtue that No Evil Can Diminish

The Messenger of Allah (peace and blessings of God be upon him) declares: "The love and *wilāya* (guardianship) of Ali constitutes such a virtue that no evil can harm one who possesses it." A fundamental difficulty arises in interpretation. If taken at face value, this saying renders all good deeds meaningless. A lover of Ali would remain righteous even after committing grievous sins and would escape punishment—a doctrine wholly at odds with divine justice. Such a belief belongs to the unbelievers, not to those who truly submit to God. How, then, must we understand this hadith?

In the technical language of *taʾwīl* (esoteric exegesis), the term "good deeds" refers not to external actions but to the Imams (peace be upon them), for they alone are the guides who lead souls toward Paradise.[^cite-1] Conversely, "evil deeds" signify the Imams of *al-Dhalāl* (misguidance), those false leaders who mislead and direct souls toward the Fire. The hadith thus acquires its true meaning: whoever loves Maulānā Ali in the proper sense—recognizing his station as the successor (*khalīfa*) to the Prophet, believing firmly in his Imamate, and obeying him in all matters—becomes liberated from the grasp of false imams and protected from spiritual corruption and error. The words of no false guide can then swerve such a person from the *wilāya* and obedience owed to the true Imam.

This authentic meaning stands in sharp contrast to the perverse doctrine of the *Ghāliyn* (extremists), who grossly misinterpret the tradition to attribute divine attributes to Maulānā Amīr al-Muʾminīn ʿAlī ibn Abī Tālib (peace be upon him). On one hand, they kindle divine wrath and consign themselves to hellfire; on the other, they open the door to license for all manner of transgression and vice. Truly, such people are among the worst enemies of ʿAlī, for by their impious belief they nullify the Law of the Prophet (peace and blessings of God be upon him), and in nullifying the Law, they nullify his Prophethood itself and cast down his rank. How, then, can any love of ʿAlī endure in hearts that reject the Sharīʿa and its ordinances?

The Messenger of Allah taught with clarity: "O ʿAlī! Your friend is my friend, and your enemy is my enemy."[^cite-2] All who turn their backs upon the Law and reject the rulings of the Sharīʿa thereby become enemies of ʿAlī and foes of the Messenger and of God. Such a person cannot truthfully be called a lover of ʿAlī.

(Risālat 20, Qaul wa-l-Jawāb bi-l-Tawīl — Sayyidnā ʿAlī ibn Sayyidunā Sulaymān, may God be pleased with him)

## I Am the First and I Am the Last

⟪ar:امیر المومنین علی ابن ابی طالب علیہ السلام⟫ The statement ⟪ar:'أنا الاول و أنا الآخر'⟫ presents no claim whatsoever to divinity, but rather expresses ⟪ar:'عبدیت'⟫ (perfect servitude to God). The words of ʿAlī constitute a fountain of guidance and healing for the believers, as the Qur'ān itself affirms:

> ⟪quran 41:44⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَلَوْ جَعَلْنَهُ قُرْآنًا أَعْجَمِيًّۭا لَّقَالُوا۟ لَوْلَا فُصِّلَتْ آيَتُهۥٓ ۖ أَعْجَمِىٌّۭ وَعَرَبِىٌّۭ ۗ قُلْ هُوَ لِلَّذِينَ آمَنُوا۟ هُدًۭى وَشِفَآءٌۭ ۖ وَٱلَّذِينَ لَا يُؤْمِنُونَ فِىٓ آذَانِهِمْ وَقْرٌۭ وَهُوَ عَلَيْهِمْ عَمًى ۚ أُو۟لَٓئِكَ يُنَادَوْنَ مِن مَّكَانٍۭ بَعِيدٍۢ ‎⟫
> *And if We had made it a Qur'ān in a foreign tongue, they would have said: "Why are not its verses expounded? What! A foreign scripture and an Arabic Messenger?" Say: "It is a guidance and a healing for those who believe; and those who do not believe are deaf in their ears, and it is blindness for them. These are they who are called from a place far away."*

The ⟪ar:کلمۃ اللہ⟫ (Word of God)—the testimony *Lā ilāha illā Allāh* (there is no god but God)—is that primordial light from which all creation emanated. This Word of God has no precedent before it, and it is likewise the *end* beyond which no other end exists. Its light is eternal and incorruptible, shining first in creation and will shine at the last. As God says:

> ⟪quran 39:69⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَأَشْرَقَتِ ٱلْأَرْضُ بِنُورِ رَبِّهَا وَوُضِعَ ٱلْكِتَبُ وَجِآءَ بِٱلنَّبِيِّنَ وَٱلشُّهَدَآءِ وَقُضِىَ بَيْنَهُم بِٱلْحَقِّ وَهُمْ لَا يُظْلَمُونَ ‎⟫
> *And the earth will shine with the light of its Lord, and the Book will be set forth, and the prophets and the witnesses will be brought forward, and judgment will be made between them with justice, and they will not be wronged.*

Without recitation of the Word of sincere declaration—⟪ar:'لا الهه الا اللہ محمد رسول اللہ'⟫ (there is no god but God; Muhammad is the Messenger of God)—the felicity of the Hereafter cannot be attained, for this Word has become the cornerstone of Islamic faith itself. It is the first utterance of Islam, without precedent in any law, and none among the communities of Islam dispute its necessity. Yet there exist two modes of reciting this *Kalima* (sacred formula): one is utterance alone, without understanding or inner conviction; the other is utterance joined with knowledge and heartfelt belief. The Prophet (peace and blessings of God be upon him) said:

⟪ar-quote:أمرت ان اقاتل الناس حتی یقولوا لا الہ الا اللہ محمد رسول اللہ۔ فأذا قالوھا عصموا منی دمائھم و اموالھم الابحقھا و حسابھم علی اللہ⟫

"I have been commanded to fight the people until they recite the *Kalima*. When they utter it, their blood and wealth are protected from me, save for what is lawfully due, and their reckoning belongs to God alone."

By this hadith, the Messenger of Allah (peace and blessings of God be upon him) guaranteed only the protection of life and property to those who pronounce the Word without inner belief—not salvation in the Hereafter. Yet in another place, explaining the majesty of this Word, he said:

⟪ar-quote:من قال لا الہ الا اللہ مخلصا دخل الجنۃ⟫

"Whoever recites it with sincere dedication shall enter Paradise." When asked what sincerity means, he replied:

⟪ar-quote:معرفت حدودھا و ادا حقوقھا⟫

"To know the spiritual and physical bounds that this Word signifies and to fulfill their rights."[^cite-3]

Such a noble *Kalima* is so intimately bound to the *Wasī* (the Trustee, ʿAlī) that correct knowledge of *tawḥīd* (divine unity), *tanzīh* (purification of God from all imperfection), and *tajrīd* (divesting God of all limitation) cannot be attained save through learning from the repository of the Prophet's knowledge. Only thus can one distinguish between the *awliyāʾ* (saints) and the *auliyāʾ al-Shaytān* (allies of Satan), between the *ḥudūd* (limits or bounds) of God and those who transgress them. Whoever seeks to understand the truth of sincere faith from another source, abandoning the Prophet's treasury of knowledge, shall surely go astray and find no guidance. Such a one falls into *shirk* (association of partners with God) and dwells in doubt, severed from both poles of this Word and cut off from the means of salvation. Amīr al-Muʾminīn is thus the first and the last in this sense: the beginning of knowledge of the Word of sincere faith originates from him, understanding it depends upon him, and ultimate salvation is likewise bound to his essence. The origin and consummation of the believer's spiritual journey are inseparable from ʿAlī himself. We therefore say that ʿAlī is ⟪ar:'هو الاول هو الآخر'⟫ (he is the First, he is the Last).[^cite-4]

There exists a renowned hadith of the Prophet: when Adam (peace be upon him) stood in Paradise, he beheld written upon the Throne the words ⟪ar:'لا الهه الا اللہ محمد رسول اللہ ایدتہ بعلی'⟫ (There is no god but God; Muhammad is the Messenger of God, strengthened by ʿAlī). Thus, according to this hadith, they possess primacy in the sight of God in regard to their exalted rank and station, and they represent the consummation in that the Prophethood and the Testament (*wilāya*) come to their culmination in them.

Since the end of Prophethood and the completion of the Testament have been announced from the beginning of creation, Adam himself and all the Messengers who came after him continued to proclaim this *nubʾ al-ʿaẓīm* (tremendous news). In this respect, ʿAlī is both first and last as manifest principles. The hadith further relates that God created souls six thousand years before bodies; since souls precede bodies in creation and surpass them in nobility, ʿAlī and his successors likewise surpass all others in the orders of Prophethood and Testament. Therefore, the statement ⟪ar:أنا الاول و أنا الآخر⟫ refers to their nobility and preeminence.

### Rational Reasoning from Human Stature

Man emerges as the first in regard to his self and essence, yet the last in regard to his bodily form. The explanation lies in this: the human being in the stage of the ⟪ar:نطفہ⟫ (sperm-drop) resembles the plant, possessing only the ⟪ar:قوت نامیۃ⟫ (vegetative power) with nothing beyond it at that moment. As development proceeds, he acquires the ⟪ar:قوت حس⟫ (sensory power) and comes to resemble an animal, generating sensation and the capacity to perceive pleasure and pain. After birth, he becomes rational and distinct from animals through intellect and reason. These successive degrees constitute the very means by which one comprehends his origins—a comprehension unattainable by any other path. They demonstrate that the plant precedes the animal in sequence, the animal form precedes the human form, and the human form, being the most perfect of all preceding forms, proves capable of receiving the influence of intellect and reason from the spiritual world. Yet these faculties remain unbound by material constitution. Man attains his share of intellect through union with the world of spirit by means of these very powers; as his *nafs* (soul) becomes ⟪ar:'اول'⟫ (first)—capable of accepting the signs of the spiritual realm—he becomes ⟪ar:'آخر'⟫ (last) in the material order. Thus man resembles a circle whose two ends interlock, encompassing the heavens and earth and all between them. Through knowledge, man exercises dominion over all creation and enforces his judgment upon it, subjugating all manner of beasts and rendering them servants to his purposes. If such is the rational station of every human being—to be both first and last—then the absolute and perfect Man, the one whose Prophethood and Testament brought creation to its culmination, must likewise be both first and last in the highest degree.

### Rational Reasoning from the Science of Number

All of God's creation, whether of the intellect or of nature, depends upon reckoning and enumeration. True reckoning encompasses all things, as God declares:

> ⟪quran 10:5⟫
> ⟪ar:‏هُوَ ٱلَّذِى جَعَلَ ٱلشَّمْسَ ضِيَآءًۭ وَٱلْقَمَرَ نُورًۭا وَقَدَّرَهُۥ مَنَازِلَ لِتَعْلَمُوا۟ عَدَدَ ٱلسِّنِينَ وَٱلْحِسَابَ ۚ مَا خَلَقَ ٱللَّهُ ذَلِكَ إِلَّا بِٱلْحَقِّ ۚ يُفَصِّلُ ٱلْآيَتِ لِقَوْمٍۢ يَعْلَمُونَ ‎⟫
> *It is He who made the sun a light and the moon a light, and determined its stations, that you might know the number of years and the reckoning. God created all this in truth. He distinguishes His verses for a people who understand.*

Four categories of number constitute the hierarchy of both the created realm and the eternal order, marked by four ⟪ar:حدود اربعہ⟫ (bounds or limits): the unit (1), the ten (10), the hundred (100), and the thousand (1000). The science of arithmetic begins with "one," from which all numbers originate and develop, and concludes with "thousand," after which all numbers merely repeat these fundamental categories. Thus reckoning possesses two poles: one is the beginning, and one is the end. The first vertex, "one," is the source from which numbers emanate; the second, "thousand," is their terminus. No number other than one deserves the title of true cause (⟪ar:'علت مطلق'⟫), being first of all. Similarly, no number save thousand merits the name of absolute effect (⟪ar:'معلول مطلق'⟫), being the end of all. The numbers between them are each both cause and effect, standing in a father-and-son relation: two relates to one as son to father, three to two as son, four to three as father, and so forth. Yet there exists a number that is father of all with no father—one—and a number that is son of all with no progeny—thousand. This condition mirrors the cosmic order: one represents what is first without precedent, and thousand represents what is last without succession. These two poles rest in the hands of ʿAlī (peace be upon him), just as both poles of the Word of sincere faith belong to him. Whosoever is deprived of belief in the Word and the Imamate in this world is deprived of its reward.

### The First Thought and the Last Deed

There exists a celebrated saying in wisdom: ⟪ar-quote:أول الفکر و آخر العمل⟫ (the first in thought, the last in deed)—meaning that thought and intention come first, while action and outward manifestation come last. The analogy is instructive: when a man intends to build a house, he first conceives it in his mind, then spreads the design upon paper, and finally executes the plan gradually. His first thought appears as the final structure. Between conception and completion, countless forms and details arise and pass away. ⟪ar:امیر المومنین علی علیہ السلام⟫ Such sublime rank holds before God that in the divine knowledge, the form of ʿAlī (whom God created) was the first to appear in the world. Thus in this respect as well, ⟪ar:امیر المومنین اول الفکر و آخر العمل⟫ (Amīr al-Muʾminīn is the first thought and the last deed), and his declaration ⟪ar:'أنا الاول و أنا الآخر'⟫ stands completely justified.

## I Am the Manifest and I Am the Hidden

God is both *ẓāhir* (manifest, outward) and *bāṭin* (hidden, inward)—yet this does not signify that these are attributes of God Himself or His beautiful Names. Rather, these two designations belong to His creation, which He has fashioned by His power and held within His dominion.[^cite-5] The preamble to our praise makes this clear: the manifest and the hidden are not attributes of ⟪ar:باری البرایا رب العالمین⟫ (the Creator, Lord of all beings), but rather attributes of creation. We observe that these heavens, though fashioned by God, are manifest in their effects and actions yet concealed from direct sight. Similarly, the functions of our souls are evident, yet their essence remains veiled. Our movements attest to a force within the body that produces them, yet we neither know what it is nor where it dwells. It thus becomes evident that the soul is both manifest and hidden. When we discover these attributes in God's creation, we necessarily distinguish God from the attributes of creatures, for true *tawḥīd* (divine unity) consists in purifying ⟪ar:باری سبحانہ⟫ (the Creator, may He be exalted) of all material and spiritual attributes.

⟪ar:أمیر المؤمنین علی ابن ابی طالب⟫ Now, ʿAlī too is a creation of God. Since manifestation and hiddenness are attributes of creatures, his statement ⟪ar:'أنا الظاھر و أنا الباطن'⟫ claims no lordship whatsoever, but rather clarifies a particular aspect of his station and rank. He meant: "I am manifest to those among the people of the Hereafter who understand my true worth and recognize my exalted station, yet I remain hidden among the worldly and the ignorant who are unaware of my station and status—as if I were a stranger among them." Amīr al-Muʾminīn has likewise described the believer's condition in these words:

⟪ar-quote:المؤمن مجھول فی الدنیا و معروف فی الملاء الأعلی⟫

"The believer is unknown in this world but well known in the highest assembly."[^cite-6] That is, he is hidden to the worldly, yet manifest to those in the Hereafter.

The people of truth recognize that the Prophet's (peace and blessings of God be upon him) station bears the same relation to ʿAlī as that between the ⟪ar:'قلم'⟫ (Pen) and the ⟪ar:'لوح'⟫ (Tablet). It is well known that whatever the Pen has inscribed upon the Preserved Tablet manifests in this world, while what is not recorded there does not appear here. Similarly, those whose names are not inscribed in this Tablet are unworthy of salvation in the Hereafter. The people of truth came to understand ʿAlī's station through the Prophet, and thus he is manifest to them, while remaining hidden from the eyes of his enemies—truly hidden—for they neither know nor recognize him in his reality. The Prophet (peace and blessings of God be upon him) declared:

⟪ar-quote:طوبیٰ لمن رأنی ؛ و طوبی لمن رای من رأنی ؛ و طوبی لمن رای من رأی من رأنی⟫

"Glad tidings to those who have seen me; glad tidings to those who have seen those who have seen me; glad tidings to those who have seen those who have seen those who have seen me."[^cite-7]

If the Messenger of Allah meant seeing with the outer eye alone, then both friends and enemies, Muslims and idolaters alike, would be included in this vision. Indeed, the disbelievers and polytheists of Mecca, especially ⟪ar:ابو لھب⟫ and ⟪ar:ابو جہل⟫, saw him fully from childhood through the period of Prophethood and afterward. Yet sincere believers born in later generations heard only his name and mention. Can ⟪ar:ابولھب⟫ truly be more blessed than the faithful? The Prophet must have meant something other than mere physical sight. What he indicated by ⟪ar:'رویت نفسانی'⟫ (spiritual vision) is explained by the Qur'ān:

> ⟪quran 7:198⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَإِن تَدْعُوهُمْ إِلَى ٱلْهُدَىٰ لَا يَسْمَعُوا۟ ۖ وَتَرَىٰهُمْ يَنظُرُونَ إِلَيْكَ وَهُمْ لَا يُبْصِرُونَ ‎⟫
> *And if you call them to guidance, they do not hear; and you see them looking toward you, yet they do not see.*

The terms ⟪ar:'ترا'⟫ (to see), ⟪ar:'نظر'⟫ (to look), and ⟪ar:'بصر'⟫ (to perceive) all mean "to see," yet their meanings differ profoundly from one another. It is proven here that "to see" refers to spiritual vision—seeing through the eye of insight and comprehending the Prophet's true station. This knowledge was granted only through the Prophet himself, as his own hadith attests:

⟪ar-quote:ما عرفنی حق المعرفۃ الاَّ اللہ و أنت یا علی⟫

"None has known me rightly save God and you, O ʿAlī."[^cite-8] You are the keepers of the Prophet's secrets and bearers of his trust. Thus ⟪ar:'طوبی لمن رأنی'⟫ refers to ʿAlī (peace be upon him). In this sense, ⟪ar:طوبی لمن رای من رأنی⟫ denotes the Imam (peace be upon him)—the successor and Caliph of God on earth. The third category comprises all believers who have acquired knowledge of both the *sharīʿa* (divine law) and the *ṭarīqa* (spiritual path), those who follow ⟪ar:ائمہ علیھم السلام⟫ the Imams and regard them as the guides and directorate of their time, as God Himself affirms:

> ⟪quran 13:7⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَيَقُولُ ٱلَّذِينَ كَفَرُوا۟ لَوْلَآ أُنزِلَ عَلَيْهِ آيَةٌۭ مِّن رَّبِّهِۦٓ ۗ إِنَّمَآ أَنتَ مُنذِرٌۭ ۖ وَلِكُلِّ قَوْمٍ هَادٍ ‎⟫
> *And the disbelievers say: "Why is not a sign sent down upon him from his Lord?" You are only a warner, and for every people there is a guide.*

This proves that the Prophet and ʿAlī are manifest in their corporeal forms, which every enemy and friend alike have witnessed, yet hidden in their majesty and true rank, known to none.

### Second Reasoning

Among the ignorant, a scholar possesses no honor. A scholar stands as a bridge between them and wisdom, just as a wise man stands between children and the mad. Those who know him honor and revere him; those who do not pay him no heed. Thus he is ⟪ar:'ظاہر'⟫ (manifest) among them in his appearance, yet ⟪ar:'باطن'⟫ (hidden) in his grace and knowledge.

### Third Reasoning

God said to His Messenger (peace and blessings of God be upon him):

> ⟪quran 93:7⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَوَجَدَكَ ضَآلًّۭا فَهَدَىٰ ‎⟫
> *And He found you unaware and guided you.*

Exegetes have offered diverse explanations of ⟪ar:'ضال'⟫ according to their conjectures. Some understood it as one astray, others as heedless, still others as devoted in the love of God. Yet the infallible Imams affirm that ⟪ar:ضال⟫ carries the same meaning as in the wise saying: ⟪ar-quote:الحکمۃ ضالۃ المؤمن⟫ ("wisdom is the lost treasure of the believer").[^cite-9] The unlettered Arabs among whom the Prophet lived were as beasts unaware of his precious essence—truly lost in their ignorance. You were lost among them (⟪ar:ضال⟫). Thus God instructed these ignorant Arabs to comprehend your worth. The Messenger of Allah was guided from his youth, as he himself proclaimed. In this verse, the conscience and understanding refer not to the Messenger, but to the people—that God Almighty guided the people to recognize the Prophet's greatness. Thus it becomes clear that the difference between manifest and hidden consists in this: his greatness lay concealed among them until God made it manifest.

## I Am the First and the Last, the Manifest and the Hidden—Synthesis

The point (*nuqṭa*) at which this station is attained stands so elevated that no other being has reached it. The Kawr (cycle) of four hundred years emerged from the *jaththa al-ibdāʾiyya* (primordial essence), after which the first round of unveiling began. The *jaththa al-ibdāʾiyya* possesses the highest point ⟪ar:'عین عظیمۃ'⟫ ('Ayn al-ʿAẓīma, the Mighty Eye). No other such person was born during this entire epoch save for the one ⟪ar:قائم القیامۃ (قائم دور)⟫ (Qāʾim al-Qiyāma, the Qa'im of the Resurrection) yet to come. Since the point of the Establishment has not yet appeared, and until it does appear, ʿAyn al-ʿAẓīma shall remain supreme.

The union of ⟪ar:عقل عاشر (جثہ ابدائیہ)⟫ (the Tenth Intellect, the primordial essence) with ʿAyn al-ʿAẓīma was complete—that is, the supreme point received emanation from the Tenth Intellect without any intermediate transmission, content infinite and imperishable.[^cite-10] From the appearance of *jaththa al-ibdāʾiyya* until the emergence of ʿAyn al-ʿAẓīma (excluding the future Qa'im al-Qiyāma), no such support was revealed to any other being as was granted to ʿAyn al-ʿAẓīma. The support granted to Qa'im al-Qiyāma will exceed even that of ʿAyn al-ʿAẓīma, for ʿAyn al-ʿAẓīma is a part of the remembrance (*dhikr*) of Qa'im, while all the Imams are partial in comparison to ʿAyn al-ʿAẓīma, and ʿAyn al-ʿAẓīma is whole when set beside them. The Sunnah of Qa'im al-Qiyāma is likewise a part of the station of *jaththa al-ibdāʾiyya*. By virtue of this station, miracles issued forth from the veil of ʿAyn al-ʿAẓīma without number. Witnessing these wonders, many Shi'as beheld him and saw in him what moved them to address him as divine. The great master ⟪ar:سیدنا ابراھیم بن حسین الحامدی⟫ (Sayyidnā Ibrāhīm ibn al-Ḥusayn al-Hāmidī) compiled sermons bearing ⟪ar:'الہ'⟫ (divine radiance) from the ⟪ar:شموس الزاہرۃ⟫ (suns of clarity)—discourses that remain exclusive to our sacred books and have not reached the general masses. In his sermon, Maulānā ʿAlī proclaimed:

⟪ar-quote:اَناَ اَلاَوَّل وَاَناَ الآخر اَناَ الباَطِن وَاَناَ الظَّاہِر⟫

"I am the First and I am the Last, I am the Inward and I am the Outward."[^cite-11]

### I Am the First and I Am the Last

Those who utter this statement are in truth the supreme ones speaking through one of their lofty veils. You are the first of all epochs—first in the sequence of all times that have transpired in the cycles. And in this dimension you are likewise the end, for there is none above you in order until the coming of the Resurrection. Your meaning is this: the first human being born of God bore the name ⟪ar:'علی'⟫, and the final person to appear shall bear the name ⟪ar:'علی'⟫. Similarly, the names of all the eternal aspects at the beginning of the *Akwār* (cycles) and *Anwār* (lights) shall be ⟪ar:'علی'⟫, as shall the names of all the *Qāʾims*, epochs, periods, and lights at the end of ages.

### I Am the Manifest and I Am the Subtle

Your statement that you are inward signifies that your form is constituted from the sum of all ⟪ar:صورات لطیفۃ⟫ (subtle forms) and ⟪ar:نفوس جوہریۃ⟫ (essential souls) of the epochs. It may not be visible to the senses, yet its effects are plainly evident; therefore you are hidden. In the Gate of Gates (*Bāb al-Abwāb*) of each Imam, a gathering of all forms assembles. ʿAyn al-ʿAẓīma made it his chamber; thus all the forms gathered around him, and from them ʿAyn al-ʿAẓīma received the collection of all forms.

And to say that you are manifest means that ⟪ar:معجزات الہیۃ⟫ (divine miracles) have been manifested through you, and the deeds of all the saints, which were wrought in the outer realm and in the sciences of *taʾwīl* (esoteric interpretation) and ⟪ar:حکمت الہیۃ⟫ (divine wisdom), were connected with their respective Imams and ultimately joined to you. He declared: "I caused the death of Adam, Noah, Abraham, Moses, and Jesus; I made prophets and sent messengers." The veil of ʿAyn al-ʿAẓīma that uttered these words is in reality one of the forms you possess. In another sense, it represents a declaration spanning nine thousand years (three thousand of the age and six thousand of the seventieth epoch) that emanated from ʿAyn al-ʿAẓīma, and all these figures are beings of the highest rank.

In yet another sense, all Imams are partial in ʿAlī, and ʿAlī himself is their totality. That is, all Imams who have appeared or will be revealed are ʿAlī in a partial manifestation (hidden), and when ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib himself appears or shall be revealed, they will appear in their wholeness (manifest). From beginning to end, ʿAlī is ʿAlī—the imprint upon all cycles and epochs.

## I Am the Knower of All Things

Imām ʿAlī (peace be upon him) declared: ⟪ar-quote:أنا بکل شئی علیم⟫ ("I am the Knower of all things"). The common understanding recoils from any creature making such a claim. Yet ⟪ar:سیدنا مؤید⟫ (our Master al-Muʾayyad) offers several reasoned arguments in response.

First, knowledge constitutes a state that grants superiority to the knower over other beings. While all humans are physically equal—whether tall or short, ignorant or unlettered—the moment one acquires knowledge and skill, reads the Qur'ān, learns *tawḥīd*, learns writing, and gains crafts useful for both religion and worldly life, he inevitably attains honor and excellence beyond all his peers. Knowledge thus becomes a blessing and mark of distinction for him.

Now, if we were to posit God as a mere Essence—a hypothesis impossible yet useful for argument—we would certainly conceive Him as a perfect Essence without peer. If He is perfect, it would be pointless to attribute knowledge to Him, for He requires no such virtue, and attributing it adds nothing to His greatness, as He is already perfect. Yet if we conceive His Essence as imperfect and imagine it gradually acquiring attributes—omniscience, almightiness, and the like—we commit the gravest defect and dishonor to God's very Essence. An essence that becomes perfect only through knowledge of creatures external to itself, entities unrelated to His nature but rather non-existent in themselves, is thereby shown to be dependent. This proves that knowledge cannot be an attribute reserved solely for God.

Second, our souls attain nobility when we acquire knowledge of *tawḥīd*, the highest and best form of understanding. How could we say of God that He became wise by acquiring knowledge of His creatures, by knowing their deeds and actions? Creatures are inferior and lowly. It brings no honor to a superior being to acquire such base knowledge; rather, it constitutes diminishment.

Third, the definition of knowledge entails that the true form of the known enters the soul of the knower. This occurs in two ways: either the world acquires knowledge of the known through acquisition, or the form of the known already exists in the self along with its own existence. If we suppose that the impressions of the known arise from the self-realization of the world, this signals a defect that becomes perfected through knowledge—an attribute that cannot apply to God, for it would mean He was first deficient and then acquired knowledge, first ignorant and then became knowing. But if we assert that the form of the known exists eternally in His knowledge, then the world and all within it would likewise be eternal with God's eternity, which we know is impossible, since the world is *ḥādith* (contingent, created at a point in time).

Fourth, knowledge encompasses even lowly professions and crafts such as farming, dyeing, and tailoring. These inferior trades are included in the verse "He is the Knower of all things." Does God then possess knowledge of how to dye cloth or sew? If we say yes, it adds nothing to His greatness; if we say no, the verse becomes invalid.

Fifth, some forms of knowledge and craft are evil, and not to know them is better than to know them. Deceit, betrayal, treachery, and the sciences of such vices are rightly condemned. Similarly, knowledge of winemaking and pickpocketing are undesirable—does God possess such knowledge? Does it add glory to Him? Do humans ever value and praise such things? God has also declared His non-knowledge of many matters. He says:

> ⟪quran 10:18⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَيَعْبُدُونَ مِن دُونِ ٱللَّهِ مَا لَا يَضُرُّهُمْ وَلَا يَنفَعُهُمْ وَيَقُولُونَ هَٓؤُلَآءِ شُفَعَٓؤُنَا عِندَ ٱللَّهِ ۚ قُلْ أَتُنَبِّـُٔونَ ٱللَّهَ بِمَا لَا يَعْلَمُ فِى ٱلسَّمَوتِ وَلَا فِى ٱلْأَرْضِ ۚ سُبْحَنَهُۥ وَتَعَلَىٰ عَمَّا يُشْرِكُونَ ‎⟫
> *And they worship beside God that which neither harms them nor profits them, and they say: "These are our intercessors with God." Say: "Do you inform God of that which He knows not in the heavens or on earth?" Glorified is He and exalted above what they associate with Him!*

From all these proofs, it becomes manifest that the verse ⟪ar:ھو بکل شئی علیم⟫ does not refer to God, but to His chosen servants. The Prophet (peace and blessings of God be upon him) praised the Qur'ān, saying:

⟪ar-quote:ان القرآن ظاھره انيق و باطنہ عميق لا تفني عجائبہ و لا تنقضي غرائبہ و لا تکشفُ الظلمات الا بہ⟫

"The Qur'ān is beautiful on the surface and profound in depth. Its wonders never perish, its marvels never cease, and darkness is dispelled only through it."[^cite-12]

⟪ar-quote:أنا بکل شئی علیم⟫ It is as if the earth (were it to speak) would say: "I am the knower and understander of all that exists upon land—in valleys, mountains, minerals, and animals. I am familiar with all physical instruments and the instruments of creation and trial." Would such a claim be false? Since Amīr al-Muʾminīn is likewise the ground of all things created for the Hereafter, no one else is as thoroughly familiar with every aspect and detail as you are. Nothing there escapes you; no secret and no mystery is hidden from you. For this reason, he said:

⟪ar-quote:واللہ لو کشف الغطاء ما ازددت یقینا⟫

## I Am He Who Elevated Its Heavens and I Am He Who Spread Out Its Earth

When we consider the powers and faculties granted to humanity, we observe that God has bestowed upon man certain powers analogous to His own cosmic dominion. By means of his psychic and intellectual capacities—which are themselves mere effects—man draws forth springs from the earth and spreads water upon its surface, making his fields verdant and flourishing. He brings forth water from the depths and grants life to man and beast; from this water emerges grain that sustains them all. In our age, he extracts oil from the earth and travels by land, sea, and air. If such are the powers of man, what might be said of the powers of Amīr al-Muʾminīn, whose station stands to each component as the whole stands to its parts?

We shall now establish the legal argument (*sharʿī*) alongside this rational reasoning, drawing from the words of the Prophet (peace and blessings of God be upon him). The Prophet said: "God has moved the Pen upon the Tablet and written upon it all that is created and all that shall be revealed in the heavens, the earth, and all between them." The one whom the legislator calls the Tablet is known in philosophy as the ⟪ar:'نفس کلیۃ'⟫ (universal soul). The inscription upon the Tablet became established and shone forth, appearing to the people of the world. Thus the comparison between the soul and the soul stands clear.[^cite-14]

The second legal argument concerns the station of both the Prophet and his successor (*waṣī*) for the Hereafter: just as the Tablet and the Pen are to this worldly creation, so are they to the creation of the next life. Whatever the Pen writes upon the Tablet shall appear in this world, nothing more and nothing less. Similarly, in the Hereafter, only those forms shall manifest who are bound to the Prophet and have taken refuge in ʿAlī's domain. None else shall be able to appear in this world save them. Just as the physical heavens, earth, and mountains were inscribed in the Tablet from the beginning, so likewise are the images of the heavens, earth, and mountains of the Hereafter inscribed in ʿAlī, the ⟪ar:'وصی'⟫ (Trustee).

His heavens—the ⟪ar:'ائمۃ الھداہ'⟫ (Guides of Guidance)—stand elevated above all humanity through the power of divine support. From them, the human soul draws its sustenance of knowledge, just as bodies draw their sustenance from the outward heavens. The ⟪ar:ائمہ ھداۃ⟫ stars and luminaries of the spiritual world illuminate the human soul with their light and radiance, guiding it from error, just as the outer stars guide our bodies through darkness.

In the same manner, the earth of this spiritual realm consists of their successors—the *Ḥujja*, *Naqīb*, and *Dāʿī*, who by drawing knowledge from the Guides produce all manner of colorful, visible flowers and useful fruits, just as the earth absorbs celestial substance and brings forth flowers and fruits. The mountains of this spiritual earth are the stalwart members of their vocation whose beliefs never waver, just as outward mountains stand firm in place. And finally, the cultivation of this spiritual earth comprises those who accept the invitation and become like outer plants and trees, living in the knowledge and actions they have received.

## Whoever Has Three Deceased Children Shall Be Protected from the Fire

The Messenger of Allah (peace and blessings of God be upon him) said: "When the da'i al-mutlaq is asked about one whose three children have died and he remains patient, seeking reward from God, those deceased children will shield him from the Fire. One asked further: 'Will the father of two deceased children also receive this benefit?' The Prophet replied: 'Yes, indeed.'"

This passage appears strange on the surface. If its literal meaning is accepted, any of the seventy-three sects of the Ummah whose children die would be saved from Hell. Yet according to another hadith, only one sect escapes; the other seventy-two are in the Fire. The Prophet declared: "My Ummah shall divide into seventy-three sects. Of these, only one sect is saved, and seventy-two are destined for Hell." If the Prophet's words are true, there would be no distinction between the obedient and disobedient—both would merit equal reward.

In reality, "children" here refer not to physical offspring, but to spiritual children—the disciples and believers elevated by the da'i. In *taʾwīl* (esoteric exegesis), "death" signifies the elevation of a believer from a lower to a higher station—an exaltation of rank, as Sayyidnā al-Qāḍī al-Nuʿmān explains in his *Taʾwīl al-Daʿāʾim*.[^cite-17] "Fire" represents the doubts and confusions that kindle in hearts, consuming faith to ash and destroying souls.

The true meaning is this: when the *dāʿī al-muṭlaq* (the absolute Summoner), who is the spiritual father of believers, elevates one disciple to the rank of *Maqṣūr*, promotes a second (previously at a lower rank) to *Māʾdhūn al-muṭlaq* (the absolute Authorization), and elevates a third to the rank of *Dāʿī* of the inner circle, then they themselves are freed from the burden of direct preaching. He establishes these three to answer the objections of enemies—objections that are a mixture of doubts—and withdraws from his own active role. The responsibility for extinguishing the fire kindled by enemies in believers' hearts falls upon these three representatives: the *Dāʾī*, the *Māʾdhūn*, and the *Maqṣūr*.

The people of falsehood are called "al-Nār" (the Fire) because they are themselves the Hell that invites others to actual Hell, just as the invitation to guidance is potential Paradise that invites toward actual Paradise. Should the *dāʿī* be unable to elevate three persons to these three ranks and instead raise only two—making one an absolute authority and another a *Maqṣūr*—both remain sufficient to confront the enemies of truth and refute their weak objections. The absolute *dāʿī* need not be burdened with this task.

(Risālat 20, Qaul wa-l-Jawāb bi-l-Tawīl — Sayyidnā ʿAlī ibn Sayyidunā Sulaymān, may God be pleased with him)

## The Believer Eats in One Stomach and the Disbeliever Eats in Seven

The Messenger of Allah (peace and blessings of God be upon him) said: "The believer eats in one intestine and the disbeliever eats in seven intestines." How is this possible? All human beings possess the same digestive organs in their bellies—neither more nor fewer. If the saying alludes to eating little or much, we observe that most believers eat less than many disbelievers.

Here too, the apparent meaning is not intended. Rather, the matter concerns spiritual sustenance: when a believer accepts the invitation of the true Imam (peace be upon him) and enters the realm of faith through covenant and pledge, he receives the profound meanings and elevated wisdoms of the Qur'ān's words. Though these meanings are few in number, they satisfy him completely. He requires no turn to other sciences and philosophies. His heart and mind attain peace and contentment through understanding the true meaning, and his spiritual hunger ceases.

By contrast, the disbeliever and hypocrite who seeks to satisfy his spiritual hunger through the Qur'ān turns to self-styled scholars and illegitimate interpreters, who distort the meaning with false interpretations that fail to satisfy his appetite but rather increase his hunger. Thus he eats much yet remains unsatisfied—the eating of the disbeliever, while the believer, eating little, is content. The believer finds sufficiency in little.

Second, the believer eats at appointed times, refraining from eating and drinking at improper hours so that his stomach remains clean. ## The Intention of the Believer is Better than His Deeds

The Hadith declares with clarity: "The intention of the believers is better than his deeds, and the intention of the disbeliever is worse than his deeds." What does this profound utterance signify?

When a believer forms the intention to perform a righteous deed—a deed rooted in religious devotion—yet finds himself constrained by circumstance or compulsion and unable to fulfill it, his reward shall not be forfeited. Rather, the reward of intention itself accrues to him. Indeed, according to hadith transmission, the intention of the believer proves more beneficial than the action itself.[^cite-7] The inner resolve, grounded in sincere faith, possesses a dignity that transcends the external act.

Conversely, if a disbeliever harbors the intention to commit evil—especially to inflict harm upon the saints of Allah—and circumstance prevents him from executing this malice, his evil intention remains graver than the evil deed itself would have been. Punishment is meted according to intentions, not merely according to deeds accomplished. A blow delivered in a fit of passion or excitement carries less severity than wrongdoing undertaken with deliberate premeditation. If such a person cannot realize his designs today, he will seize opportunity tomorrow, as the hypocrites of the Prophet's time demonstrated through their persistent treachery.

### The Three Foundations of Faith

Faith comprises three inseparable elements:

|---|---|---|

Yet both are inherently conjoined in intention and brought to fruition through tongue and limbs. Thus the Prophet declared: "The intention of a believer surpasses his action, for both Islam and faith are rooted in intention and both exist through true faith." A modest deed performed with sincere intention proves superior and more precious than abundant action performed without genuine purpose.

## The Believer is Like the Bee: He Consumes What is Pure and Produces What is Pure

The authentic hadith states that "the believer is like a bee, who consumes what is pure and brings forth what is pure." At first hearing, this utterance appears only partially true: although the bee consumes the finest substances from flowers, its body expels waste—a foul and malodorous thing. What can this teaching signify?

The Prophet's hadith does not refer to outward, corporeal food, but rather to spiritual and intellectual matters. The example of the believer resembles the bee that draws the juice of flowers and transforms it into sweet honey through its own being. By "good food," the Prophet refers to acquiring righteous knowledge (*ʿilm ṣāliḥ*, sound learning).[^cite-8] The believer acquires knowledge that is true from the authentic people of truth—knowledge that brings peace and tranquility to heart and mind—and he translates these sciences into practice throughout his life. In the end, he transmits them in the form of noble teaching that illumines and magnifies the path of faith. At death, this subtle form separates from him and reaches its proper limits.

In another sense, the hadith signifies that a believer who acquires righteous knowledge manifests it to the world through a pure and exemplary character. He learns beneficial sciences from his elders and teaches those same sciences to others, so they too may be spiritually formed. Just as the bee brings benefit to others through the production of honey, the believer disseminates knowledge and goodness.

## The Punishment of This Community Is in This World

The Messenger of Allah declared: "The punishment of this Community is in this world." At first hearing, this hadith appears to nullify all punishment in the Hereafter. The Prophet himself forbade acceptance of hadiths in such cases and instructed us: "Whatever command or news is related in my name, judge it by placing it against the verses of the Qur'an. If the hadith accords with the Qur'an, accept it. If it contradicts, reject it, for I cannot speak against the Qur'an." The question thus becomes: how shall we establish compatibility between this hadith and the Qur'an, and by what means shall we verify its meaning?

### The True Meaning of "This Community"

In this hadith, the word "Community" (*Ummah*) refers not to all who claim Islam, but to the true Community that follows in the Prophet's footsteps—namely, the succession of Imams from his descendants. This Community guides itself and guides others, standing in the middle of humanity as exemplars. Among them are certain well-known personalities: Maulana Amīr al-Muʾminīn ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib, successor to the Prophet, and then the Infallible Imams descended from him.

The meaning is thus clarified: this Community of the Prophet has endured immense suffering and hardship from enemies. Their rights were usurped, they were slain, their women widowed, their children orphaned. They were expelled from battles—Badr and Hunayn, Khyber and the Trench, Jamal, Ṣiffīn, Nahrawān, and Karbalāʾ. The hidden hatred of those who embraced Islam through hypocrisy was revealed through the martyrdom and imprisonment of the Prophet's descendants—his true Community—thereby cooling the hearts of the hypocrites. The Messenger said: "The suffering that has befallen my Community in this world has been completed. Now they shall not suffer punishment in the Hereafter."

### The Reason for People's Confusion

With knowledge obtained from the elders of the daʿwa al-hādiyyah through *taʾwīl* (esoteric interpretation), harmony between hadiths and Qur'anic verses can be correctly established. Otherwise, those who rely upon their own opinions and conjectures become further astray. A group called the ʿAbd Allāhiyyah—followers of a man named ʿAbd Allāh and fierce enemies of the true Imams—compiled hadiths and attempted to reconcile them with Qur'anic verses according to their own conjecture. They fashioned an idol of Allah, embodying Him as a human form with hands, feet, and face. They cited the hadith: "Allah created Adam in His own image," and then marshaled verses to support their heresy:

Allah says: "Every soul shall taste the sweetness of facing Allah"—thus proving the face of Allah is that which is seen. He says: "On the Day when the soul shall say..."—proving the soul of Allah. He says: "The hand of Allah is above their hands"—proving Allah possesses hands. By collecting these attributes, they constructed an idol—a body and form for Allah—as if the Creator resembled creation, as if the Eternal became temporal, the Creator became creature, inheriting all the defects of creatures.

Thus those who abandon the Imams, who do not benefit from them in the knowledge of *taʾwīl*, and who rely upon their own conjectures, become similarly misled, attributing deficiency to the Oneness and Holiness of the Almighty. May Allah preserve us from such unbelief! May the mercy of the Prophet Muhammad bless us.

## He Who Knows Himself Has Known His Lord

This hadith belongs to those specific teachings for which the Prophet said: "I have been sent with comprehensive words and gems of wisdom." Thus the Messenger made clear that knowledge of the Lord cannot be attained save through knowledge of the soul. Yet how can we, with the same intellect that created our own souls, comprehend the Essence who bestowed intellect upon us? If man lacks the capacity to fully understand his own self, how can he fathom his Lord, the Creator of the soul? The Originator, Most High (*Mubdiʿ Taʿālā*) remains beyond complete comprehension. Man exists within this world and depends upon it for survival; yet the world itself does not depend upon the tools and materials required for human birth. ### Parts and Effects Versus the Whole

In explaining the hadith "He who knows his own soul has known his Lord," al-Muʾayyad Bi-llāh states: "The human being comprises two parts: body and soul. Physically, he is composed of four elements—fire, air, water, and earth—deriving his earthen part from soil, his aerial part from the atmosphere, his fiery part from bodily heat, and his aqueous part from water."

Beyond this gross physicality, there exists a subtle dimension through which man encompasses the heavens and earth within his consciousness, without bodily movement or physical hardship. This subtle faculty is called the rational soul (*nafs nāṭiqah*, the articulate soul), which distinguishes humanity from mere animals and renders him the noblest of creatures.

Yet there exists another dimension still more subtle—the intellect (*ʿaql*), by which one discriminates between good and evil. Just as the body must have a center from which its various parts proceed, similarly the soul and intellect must have their center and source. We call this center the universal soul (*nafs kulliyyah*) and the universal intellect (*ʿaql kulliyyah*), for all individual souls and intellects of this physical realm are partial—they derive their powers from this universal center.

Thus we have demonstrated that man is the child of two worlds: in his bodily aspect, the child of nature; in his soul and intellect, the child of another realm—the universal soul and universal intellect. Yet observe: the portion man receives from the earth is so infinitesimal that no meaningful relationship can be established between his earthly component and the whole earth. What comparison exists between the soil of man and the soil of the terrestrial sphere? Similarly, what proportion obtains between the air he breathes and the atmospheric sphere, or between his bodily heat and the sphere of fire, or between his water and the hydrosphere? No true comparison is possible.

| *Jism* (Body) | *Nafs* (Soul) | *ʿAql* (Intellect) |
|---|---|---|
| The Four Elements (Fire, Air, Water, Earth) | Universal Soul | Universal Intellect |

**This crucial truth must be grasped: the four natures comprising the human body are not effects derived from their respective spheres, but only minute particles thereof.**

When we turn to examine the other realm, we observe that the human soul and intellect are partial entities, yet their relationship to the universal soul and universal intellect is not mere participation—it is pure effect, not part. We thus conclude: despite infinite effort, we cannot establish a meaningful relationship between human soul and intellect and the natural world.

If man is born of other realms—the realm of universal soul and intellect—and yet cannot establish his relationship to them despite his connection to them, how then can he grasp the reality of the Originator, Most High, with whom no relationship whatsoever can be established?

Man thus comes to recognize that what he deemed knowledge of God is but a mirage. He then comprehends the profound truth in Maulana ʿAlī's saying: "Inability to grasp comprehension is itself comprehension"—that is, his inability to comprehend is his true realization of God's transcendence. Thus the Prophet's word is confirmed: "He who knows not his own soul—how shall he know his Lord?"

## The Grave: A Garden of Paradise or a Pit of Hell

The Prophet declared: "The grave is either one of the gardens of Paradise or one of the pits of Hell." A second hadith states: "After death there is no ease and no home save Paradise or Hell." A third teaches: "Whoever dies shall experience resurrection." Yet these hadiths appear to contradict the Qur'anic verses. Allah says in His Book that judgment occurs on the Day of Resurrection, when the Qa'im shall render account. In Surah al-Muʾminun, Allah says that the soul rises in stages and is returned to the body.

From these verses, we understand that punishment and reward do not occur immediately upon death, but only after a prolonged period. How then shall we reconcile the hadiths with the Qur'anic verses?

The words of the Messenger are indeed true and do not contradict the Qur'an, provided we consult the people of remembrance and the people of knowledge, seeking from them the true meaning and interpretation.

### The Garden or the Pit

When a believer dies and his soul separates from the corpse, it becomes connected with and merges into his celestial superior—the lofty archetype. The two unite in such a manner that no distinction remains. In the esoteric sense, the true grave of the believer is the *ḥadd ʿālī* (upper limit, the exalted realm) into which he merged and with which he became one—just as the earthly body, when buried, becomes indistinguishable from the grave. Thus the soul of the Exalted One, which is the spiritual grave of the believer, becomes one of the treasures of Paradise after death. Upon reaching there, the believer's soul receives the fragrance of comfort, tranquility, contentment, and knowledge. This constitutes the first step toward attaining *Jannah* al-ʿIdn (*Paradise Eternal*, the *Lahūt al-Imāmī*, the divine realm of the Imam).

Conversely, when an enemy of the Ahl al-Bayt or a disbeliever dies, the evil and impure form—the dark concept he fashioned through enmity and transgression—separates from his body. He wanders for a time, then settles in filthy, foul, and horrifying places. This is the first gate of Hell toward which he is directed, where he suffers torment. His sensuality disperses into his evil form; the parts of his body decompose and scatter in places of perpetual fire. Such a terrible and tormented place becomes his grave—one of the pits of Hell.

These two realms—comfort and blessing for the believer, torment and filth for the disbeliever—constitute their *Barzakh* (the intermediate realm) between death and the Day of Resurrection, where they remain until the Day of Judgment, when the Qa'im al-Dhikr appears.

### Death and Resurrection

Such is the meaning of the second and third hadiths. The "resurrection" (*qiyāmah*) of the one who dies signifies that each person's death is a partial resurrection. Certain events destined for the Day of Judgment occur immediately after death, as the body's elements begin to dissolve and a reckoning is rendered.

The believer beholds a beautiful form—the beautiful visage of his righteous deeds—and rejoices. The disbeliever witnesses the horror of his evil deeds. As Amīr al-Muʾminīn said to Ḥārith al-Hamdānī: "O Ḥārith! Whoever dies shall surely see me. For the believer, I bear glad tidings; for the disbeliever, I am a messenger of doom." Both witness their state and ponder what shall transpire on the Day of Resurrection. The disbeliever feels acutely that the door of repentance has closed eternally; Allah cannot be appeased through supplication; no pardon remains. He must endure whatever punishment awaits. Thus the Prophet indicated that no third home exists after death except Paradise and Hell.

## Between My Grave and My Pulpit: A Garden of Paradise

The hadith declares that "between my grave and my pulpit is one of the gardens of Paradise." What is its meaning?

In explanation, the scholars say: the grave is that which conceals and covers the entirety of a person's form. The pulpit, by contrast, is the place in the mosque from which the Imam openly delivers his sermon. It stands elevated and clear so that all may see and hear. The grave's function is concealment; the pulpit's function is revelation.

In this esoteric sense, the grave of Muḥammad Muṣṭafā is none other than Maulana ʿAlī, for all the knowledge and wisdom of the Prophet were entrusted to and concealed within him. ʿAlī safeguarded all the sciences within his breast. The pulpit of the Prophet refers to the blessed personality of the Qa'im ʿAlī Dhikr (may peace be upon him), for when he appears, he shall openly proclaim the sciences that ʿAlī kept hidden.

The condition of the Qa'im's time shall be such that knowledge and truth are broadcast openly from the pulpits. The Paradise between his grave and pulpit is the succession of Imams between ʿAlī and the Qa'im—the Imams of Purification (*al-Tāhirīn*). They are the Paradise of the believer's soul and form, the gardens wherein trees are laden with the fruits of wisdom and the fragrance of knowledge's flowers is ever present.

## The Qur'an: The Intercessor Whose Intercession Prevails

Regarding the excellence of the Qur'an, the Prophet declared: "The Qur'an is the Word of Allah—the intercessor whose intercession is accepted, the witness whose testimony is true, the authenticator of the Imamate. Yet does not this verse also point toward ʿAlī?

Indeed, this verse refers to the Qur'an itself! Such reference does not diminish the value, prestige, virtue, and nobility of the Qur'an—it remains supreme. Yet the Prophet, reciting this verse in the Battle of Ṣiffīn, said: "O Book of Allah! Speak!" When the Qur'an gave no answer, he declared: "I am the speaking Qur'an, and this is the silent Qur'an. Whoever acknowledges their Imamate speaks truth; whoever follows their command receives reward. Whoever judges according to the Shariah of their grandfather, the Messenger of Allah, renders justice; otherwise, he commits injustice.

## The Likeness of My Ahl al-Bayt to Noah's Ark

The Prophet, peace and blessings of God be upon him, drew a profound analogy between his Ahl al-Bayt (the members of his household) and Noah's Ark, declaring: "The likeness of my Ahl al-Bayt is like Noah's Ark—whosoever embarks upon it shall be saved, and whosoever turns away shall be drowned and perish." This parable reveals the Prophet's concern for the spiritual welfare of the Muslim community, for an analogy to a vessel would be meaningless were there no storm to navigate. All narratives preserved in the Qur'an serve a singular purpose: to demonstrate that events witnessed in earlier generations recur in succeeding ages, though often in altered form.[^cite-13] Indeed, after the Prophet's departure, the great tempests of *bid'a* (innovations) and doctrinal deviation swept across the Islamic community, drowning the souls of believers in seas of doubt and bewilderment. Against such turbulent waves, the Prophet established the Ahl al-Bayt as the vessel of salvation.

As Noah's Ark carried but few souls—the Qur'an specifies eighty believers—so too does the path of the Ahl al-Bayt remain traversed by a minority of the Muslim *umma* (community). Yet God has elevated the station of this faithful remnant, naming each of them an *umma* (a people, a community) unto themselves. Regarding the believers who accompanied Noah, the Qur'an testifies:

> ⟪quran 11:40⟫
> ⟪ar:‏حَتَّىٰٓ إِذَا جَآءَ أَمْرُنَا وَفَارَ ٱلتَّنُّورُ قُلْنَا ٱحْمِلْ فِيهَا مِن كُلٍّۢ زَوْجَيْنِ ٱثْنَيْنِ وَأَهْلَكَ إِلَّا مَن سَبَقَ عَلَيْهِ ٱلْقَوْلُ وَمَنْ آمَنَ ۚ وَمَآ آمَنَ مَعَهُۥٓ إِلَّا قَلِيلٌۭ ‎⟫
> *So it was till Our Command came and the oven gushed forth. We said: Carry in the Ark a pair of every kind, and thy family, except those against whom the Word hath gone forth, and those who believe. And there believed not with him save a few.*

Similarly, the Prophet Abraham, though solitary in his righteousness, was honored with the appellation *umma*:

> ⟪quran 16:120⟫
> ⟪ar:‏إِنَّ إِبْرَهِيمَ كَانَ أُمَّةًۭ قَانِتًۭا لِّلَّهِ حَنِيفًۭا وَلَمْ يَكُ مِنَ ٱلْمُشْرِكِينَ ‎⟫
> *Lo! Abraham was a nation obedient to Allah, by nature upright, and he was not of the idolaters.*

In truth, each of these solitary figures stood at the center of a gathered community of believers, and thus they merited the designation *umma*. A multitude of believers were drowned in the tempest of *bid'a* and uncertainty; yet those few who boarded the Ark of the Prophet's family were delivered from error and bound themselves to the household of the Prophet. Though small in number, these adherents constitute the true *Ahl al-Sunna wa-l-Jama'a* (the People of the Sunna and the Community) according to Qur'anic testimony, for they stand united in their convictions.[^cite-14]

### The Reason for the Scarcity of Truth

When questioned, Imam ʿAlī, peace be upon him, declared: "Who are the People of the Sunna and the Community? I and my companions, though few we be. Who are the people of innovation and discord? My adversaries, though numerous." God has reproved such multitudes:

> ⟪quran 6:116⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَإِن تُطِعْ أَكْثَرَ مَن فِى ٱلْأَرْضِ يُضِلُّوكَ عَن سَبِيلِ ٱللَّهِ ۚ إِن يَتَّبِعُونَ إِلَّا ٱلظَّنَّ وَإِنْ هُمْ إِلَّا يَخْرُصُونَ ‎⟫
> *If thou obey most of those on earth, they will mislead thee far from Allah's way. They follow nothing but an opinion, and they do but guess.*

> ⟪quran 43:78⟫
> ⟪ar:‏لَقَدْ جِئْنَكُم بِٱلْحَقِّ وَلَكِنَّ أَكْثَرَكُمْ لِلْحَقِّ كَرِهُونَ ‎⟫
> *Verily We have brought the truth unto you, but most of you are averse to the truth.*

Conversely, God extols the faithful few who cleave to truth:

> ⟪quran 34:13⟫
> ⟪ar:‏يَعْمَلُونَ لَهُۥ مَا يَشَآءُ مِن مَّحَرِيبَ وَتَمَثِيلَ وَجِفَانٍۢ كَٱلْجَوَابِ وَقُدُورٍۢ رَّاسِيَتٍۚ ٱعْمَلُوٓا۟ آلَ دَاوُۥدَ شُكْرًۭاۚ وَقَلِيلٌۭ مِّنْ عِبَادِىَ ٱلشَّكُورُ ‎⟫
> *They made for him all that he willed: synagogues and basins like reservoirs and cauldrons fixed in their places. Give thanks, O House of David! Few of My servants are thankful.*

The quintessence of all things is invariably scarce and refined. Among humankind, the wise and learned are few, whilst the ignorant and debased are many. Of the celestial bodies, seven are reckoned as planets, whilst countless are the stars unmeasured. In the earth's stones, precious gems are rare, whilst common pebbles abound. So too are prophets and saints few among humanity, whilst the foolish and heedless are legion.[^cite-15]

### The Interpretation of the Pairs in the Ark

A subtle teaching emerges from Noah's narrative. When God commanded Noah within the Ark:

> ⟪quran 11:40⟫
> ⟪ar:‏حَتَّىٰٓ إِذَا جَآءَ أَمْرُنَا وَفَارَ ٱلتَّنُّورُ قُلْنَا ٱحْمِلْ فِيهَا مِن كُلٍّۢ زَوْجَيْنِ ٱثْنَيْنِ وَأَهْلَكَ ‎⟫
> *Carry in it a pair of every kind.*

The Ahl al-Bayt, inheriting the Prophet's wisdom, perceived within this command a deeper meaning: they related every outward ordinance of the Prophet to its inner reality (*bāṭin*), and beheld in every parable an exemplum of esoteric truth. To each utterance they affixed a significance; to every noun they bound a name. In like manner, they paired all acts of worship, commandments, and beliefs—one dimension residing in the temple of the body, the other in the sanctuary of the soul. There are words in the apparent sense, whilst within them dwells their *taʾwīl* (esoteric exegesis) and essential reality. Action devoid of meaning is a body without soul; knowledge bereft of practice is a soul without body—both states are untenable and ruinous.[^cite-16]

Thereafter, God declared concerning Noah's Ark:

> ⟪quran 11:42⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَهِىَ تَجْرِى بِهِمْ فِى مَوْجٍۢ كَٱلْجِبَالِ وَنَادَىٰ نُوحٌ ٱبْنَهُۥ وَكَانَ فِى مَعْزِلٍۢ يَبُنَىَّ ٱرْكَب مَّعَنَا وَلَا تَكُن مَّعَ ٱلْكَفِرِينَ ‎⟫
> *So it sailed with them amid waves like mountains, and Noah cried out to his son who was standing apart: O my son! Embark with us and be not with the disbelievers.*

So it is with the Ahl al-Bayt. These Imams of the Prophet's household—sailing eternally through trials and tribulations—continue to guide the believers who follow them toward deliverance, confronting the fearsome surges of *bid'a* (innovation) and shielding them from every peril and misguidance.

*(Majalis Mu'ayyidah [The Sessions of al-Muʾayyad], Seventh Session, al-Muʾayyad al-Shīrāzī)*

## The Hadith of Animating Images and Statues

The Prophet, peace and blessings of God be upon him, declared: "On the Day of Resurrection, those who fashion images and idols shall be brought before God Most High and commanded: 'Breathe spirit into these creatures of yours!'" These sculptured forms and lifeless effigies represent, in their esoteric dimension, the Imams of falsehood and unjust usurpers of the caliphate—those who stand in opposition to God's appointed Imam upon the earth, deceiving the people by presenting themselves as learned guides whilst bearing the countenance of Satan. These false caliphs are indeed as soulless and lifeless statues into which the spirit of true and eternal life has never been breathed—that is to say, the Messenger of God, upon whom be peace, has never invested them with legitimate caliphate through divine inscription and designation.

The Messenger of God was the first in his age to have received the True Spirit within himself. After him, Maulana ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib inherited the Prophet's manifest words and spiritual authority (*tawqīf*), transmitted by the Prophet's decree. ʿAlī breathed the soul into al-Ḥasan, and al-Ḥasan into al-Ḥusayn, and this sacred transmission continues through every succeeding Imam until the Day of Judgment. All these Imams are holy souls (*arwāḥ muqaddasa*) whose bond with the Lord of all Worlds is perpetually sustained through the Holy Spirit.

## The Hadith of Reciting the Basmala over Water

It is narrated in the tradition that upon the Day of Resurrection, Hell shall roar as a ferocious lion and shriek as a dragon. ⟪ar:رسول اﷲ (ﷺ)⟫ The righteous shall approach bearing a cup of water, and upon it they shall recite ⟪ar:بسم اﷲ الرحمن الرحیم⟫ (In the name of God, the Merciful, the Compassionate) and blow upon it. Then they shall pour the water into Hell, and immediately its fire shall be quenched, and its voice shall fall silent. Hell shall be called out to by the command of God Most High: "O Fire, what hath befallen thee that thou hast become silent and hath been subdued?" Fire shall answer: "O Lord! I possess no power against Thy name."

The esoteric interpretation of this hadith is as follows: the company of falsehood and the enemies of the Ahl al-Bayt represent, in truth, an image of Hell itself—burning their followers in the flames of doubt and suspicion. When they are questioned by those seeking answers, and presented with queries to which they cannot respond, Hell roars and threatens them with the outward power of its tongue or with divine punishment. Yet when silenced by true knowledge, its voice becomes extinguished.

⟪ar:رسول اﷲ (ﷺ)⟫ Alternatively, Imam al-Zamān (the Imam of the Age) represents the true *Asm Allāh* (the Name of God), whilst the cup of water upon which the divine name is recited—⟪ar:بسم اﷲ⟫—signifies one who has entered into covenant with Imam al-Zamān. Such a *mustajīb* (one who has responded to the divine call) is a humble disciple of the Imam, comparably insignificant as a cup of water before the ocean of divine knowledge. Yet when this respondent engages in disputation with the apparent scholar (representing Hell) and poses even a simple question whilst earnestly seeking proofs, that false scholar becomes humbled and is rendered incapable of counter-argument. His voice grows feeble, and all his rhetorical strength is diminished.[^cite-17]
