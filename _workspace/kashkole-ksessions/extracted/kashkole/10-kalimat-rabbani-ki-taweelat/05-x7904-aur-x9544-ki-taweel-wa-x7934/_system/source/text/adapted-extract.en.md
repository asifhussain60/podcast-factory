## The Wisdom of Diacritical Marks

On the occasion of the Urs of ⟪ar:الحد الفاضل سید عبد اللّٰہ بن ھبۃ اﷲ⟫, a gathering in Hyderabad was addressed with discourse on the significance of Arabic diacritical marks (aʿrāb, إعراب), woven together with reflections on the conditions of pilgrimage. The marks themselves—⟪ar:اعراب⟫, ⟪ar:تشدید⟫ (intensification), and ⟪ar:جزم⟫ (cessation)—constitute far more than ornamental signs: they transform meaning itself. A single word may yield entirely distinct senses depending upon which diacritical form governs it.[^cite-1] The discourse that unfolded that day kindled profound contemplation among the assembled believers. Drawing upon the knowledge transmitted by venerable teachers—⟪ar:مولوی محمد عباس صاحب⟫ and the illustrious guides ⟪ar:سیدنا حسین بن اسماعیل⟫ and ⟪ar:مولانا ضیاء علی بن اسحاق ضیائی صاحب⟫—these few points and allusions have been set forth. Whatever merit they possess flows entirely from the blessing of those benefactors; any shortcoming remains the writer's own, and for this he seeks God's forgiveness.

⟪ar:مولوی عبد اللہ صاحب⟫ declared: "⟪ar:أعراب⟫ *is* the soul of the Arabic language." These marks alter both sense and significance. The three principal diacritical forms are called "movements" (ḥarkāt):

> **⟪ar:اعراب⟫ In rank of eloquence, the preposition stands highest; the diacritic (zabar) occupies the middle; and the subjoined mark (zaīr) the lowest.**
>
> **Concerning the sentence, it is said: "The tongue ceases when it arrives thereat; the mark describes the rounded form of the throat."**

The Brotherhood affirms: Arabic is the language of the Qur'an—the most beloved and lucid of all tongues in God's sight, which is why it is called ⟪ar:عربی مبین⟫ (the Manifest Arabic). Outwardly, the Arabic language displays the wisdom of its diacritical system and its nobility; inwardly, it conceals ⟪ar:رموز وأسرار وغوامض⟫ (symbols, mysteries, and subtleties). Its grammatical principles are more comprehensive, more intricate, and more stable than those of other languages. Within its matrix lies extraordinary uniqueness: letters may be dotted or undotted; some form circles (⟪ar:ن ق ض⟫), others do not (⟪ar:ا م⟫). A division exists between solar letters (⟪ar:شمسی حروف⟫) and lunar letters (⟪ar:قمری حروف⟫). God has invested delicacy in the outer forms of these letters as well as in their inward meanings.

## Solar and Lunar Letters

### Solar Letters

When a letter is preceded by the definite article (⟪ar:ال⟫) and the accompanying lām (the initial *l*) merges with the solar letter's sound, that letter is called a solar letter (⟪ar:شمسی حروف⟫). In the word ⟪ar:الشَّمسُ⟫ (the sun), for example, the *shīn* absorbs the *lām*'s articulation directly, and the *lām*'s existence passes undetected.

### Lunar Letters

When a letter is preceded by the definite article (⟪ar:ال⟫) and the *lām* remains distinctly pronounced, that letter is called a lunar letter (⟪ar:قمری حروف⟫). In ⟪ar:القمر⟫ (the moon), both the *qāf* and the *lām* retain their full phonetic presence.

| Solar Letters | Lunar Letters |
| --- | --- |
| The *lām*'s existence is concealed when heard. | The *lām*'s presence is known when pronounced. |
| The Sun | The Moon |

## Luminous and Shadowed Letters

As is well known among scholars, the twenty-eight letters of the Arabic alphabet are themselves esoteric letters (ḥurūf bāṭina), their proverbs pointing to specific individuals. In this sense, the 28 letters divide into four categories:

All these letters signify the physical and the spiritual realms alike. Even at their outward level, their importance shines throughout the pages of the Qur'an. Surah al-Ḥamd (Surah 1) contains 21 letters of the first three types and omits entirely the seven letters of the "oppressive heaviness."[^cite-2]

The luminous letters alone—⟪ar:نورانی لطیفۃ⟫ (the luminous subtle) and ⟪ar:نورانی خفیفۃ⟫ (the luminous light)—employ only the alphabet's own forms; they do not resort to the oppressive letters. Beyond this, three letters stand apart (⟪ar:نون، میم، واو⟫): their names remain identical even when their phonetic forms are inverted.

## Diacritical Letters and the Word of Sincerity

Regarding these letters, it has been said that they signify the twenty-eight letters of ⟪ar:کلمہ شہادت⟫ (the Declaration of Witness). The imperative is to fulfill the rights of these letters and to know the spiritual bounds (ḥudūd rūḥāniyya, ⟪ar:حدود روحانی⟫) they represent.

## The Four Letters of the Qur'an

⟪ar:قرآن مجید⟫ Although voluminous and not composed in the mother tongue of its most diverse bearers, the Qur'an remains a text preserved in thousands of hearts. Not a single secular book can be committed to memory across such breadth; yet the Qur'an's miracle manifests in the fact that its memorizers number in the thousands across the world.[^cite-3] The manner of its recitation, the art of its chanting—all have become refined disciplines. A second wonder: in many places, an incorrect diacritical reading renders the meaning into disbelief (kufr). May God preserve us from such error.

If one examines the word ⟪ar:قرآن⟫ itself, one discovers two dotted letters—*qāf* (ق), which also takes circular form—and two undotted letters—*rā* (ر), which do not. By numerical reckoning, the letter *nūn* appears 50 times and *qāf* 100 times in the Qur'an, each with profound significance. These meanings have been explained in the writings of ⟪ar:مولانا حضرت اکرام علی صاحب مقدس⟫. The meaning of the word "Qur'an" itself, viewed through the lens of letters, has been expounded by ⟪ar:حضرت مولانا اسحاق صاحب⟫ in his work ⟪ar:فیض المفید⟫. The virtues and excellences of these letters have been described by ⟪ar:دعات عظام⟫ (the great summoners) and ⟪ar:مناصیب کرام⟫ (the noble deputies) in their own compositions, which overflow with treasures concealed within the ⟪ar:دعوت ھادیۃ⟫ (the guided summons).

### *Risalat Hikmat al-ʿArab*

The Qur'an is composed of four letters. Two of them are conjoined (⟪ar:قر⟫), and two are separated (⟪ar:آن⟫). The word ⟪ar:قرآن⟫ derives from ⟪ar:قرین⟫ (a companion or mate), signifying pairing. The Qur'an must traverse four stations of its own letters to reach humanity: two should function like ⟪ar:قر⟫—inseparable from one another—and two like ⟪ar:آن⟫—broad and distinct. These paired letters (⟪ar:قر⟫) correspond to the speaker (al-nāṭiq, ⟪ar:ناطق⟫) and the foundation (al-asās, ⟪ar:اساس⟫), who remain always in communion. The single letters (⟪ar:آن⟫), by contrast, exemplify intellect and soul (⟪ar:عقل و نفس⟫)—the intellect (al-ʿaql, ⟪ar:عقل⟫) and soul (al-nafs, ⟪ar:نفس⟫)—which derive their support from the speaker and foundation. Just as the two conjoined letters of ⟪ar:قر⟫ are perfected together with the two single letters of ⟪ar:آن⟫, so are the speaker and foundation perfected alongside the intellect and soul (⟪ar:ناطق اور اساس، عقل اور نفس⟫).

| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Dotted Letter | Undotted Letter | Undotted Letter | Dotted Letter |
| Circular | Unrounded | Unrounded | Circular |
| Speaker | Foundation | Intellect | Soul |

### *Alif* (The Letter A)

The letter alif (⟪ar:الف⟫) signifies the universal intellect (al-ʿaql al-kull, ⟪ar:عقل کل⟫) because it stands apart from all other letters in such a way that when the writer's pen reaches it, the sequence of writing breaks. In composition, other letters connect with *alif* from below, yet *alif* does not join with other letters from below. Similarly, all beings connect to their superior principle, the First Intellect (al-ʿaql al-awwal, ⟪ar:عقل اول⟫); yet the First Intellect connects to no creature above it. In terms of numerical reckoning (ḥisāb al-jummal, ⟪ar:حساب جمل⟫), *alif*'s value is one, signifying that the First Intellect is the cause of all existence and the wellspring of their becoming—just as the numeral one is the cause of all other numbers.[^cite-4]

### *Nun* (The Letter N)

*Nun* (⟪ar:نون⟫) symbolizes the universal soul (al-nafs al-kull, ⟪ar:نفس کل⟫) because it is a stroke in which one end wishes to reach the other but cannot. The universal soul finds itself in such a state: upon perceiving the universal intellect, it is seized with longing and moves toward it, yet it cannot attain its rank nor ever reach it. In pilgrimage (*ḥajj*), both the beginning and the end are marked by *nun*—an indication that every terminus echoes its beginning. *Nun* thus embodies the eternal return.

### *Qaf* and *Ra* (The Letters Q and R)

The first two of these four letters are *qāf* (⟪ar:ق⟫), which signifies the foundation (⟪ar:اساس⟫), and *rā* (⟪ar:ر⟫), which signifies the speaker (⟪ar:ناطق⟫). Through the foundation, the believer finds the path to knowing the speaker. By the science of numerical values (ʿilm al-ʿadad, ⟪ar:علم العدد⟫), *qāf* equals 100 and *rā* equals 200. The speaker is master of both revelation and interpretation (al-tanzīl wa-l-taʾwīl, ⟪ar:تنزیل و تاویل⟫); the foundation is master of interpretation alone (al-taʾwīl, ⟪ar:تاویل⟫). Religious scholars carry the rank of the speaker, and the foundation carries the rank of the soul.

| --- | --- |
| 200 | 100 |
| Revelation and Interpretation | Interpretation |
| Speaker | Foundation |

### Knowledge and Practice

The paired letters (⟪ar:قر⟫) of ⟪ar:قرآن⟫ exemplify knowledge (ʿilm, ⟪ar:علم⟫), which forms part of the composite body (al-jism al-murakkab, ⟪ar:جسم مرکب⟫). The single letters (⟪ar:آن⟫) exemplify practice (ʿamal, ⟪ar:عمل⟫), which constitutes the simple soul (al-nafs al-basīṭa, ⟪ar:بسیط نفس⟫). Without knowledge, practice becomes mere animal behavior; without practice, knowledge becomes angelic abstraction. Knowledge united with practice belongs to humanity alone, for humans partner with animals in bodily form (⟪ar:جسم⟫) and equal the angels in soul (⟪ar:نفس⟫). Thus can a human ascend from animal to angelic ranks through the integration of knowledge and practice (⟪ar:علم و عمل⟫).

The four letters of the Qur'an differ as do the actions of all the prophets—actions bound to the body, since all human bodies are distinct. Yet the meaning of God's books and the interpretation of the prophets' laws remain one: a parable of the human spirit, and the soul knows no alteration.

| S | T | A | N |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Practice | Revelation | Knowledge | Soul |
| Body | Soul | Being | Soul |

## The Vowel Letters: Alif, Waw, and Ya

The Brotherhood affirms that diacritical movement (ḥaraka, ⟪ar:حرکت⟫) is called ⟪ar:أعراب⟫, and it comprises three types expressed through vowel letters (ḥurūf al-ʿilla, ⟪ar:حروف علت⟫):

| Waw (Before) | Alif (Zabar) | Ya (Below) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| The prepositioned mark has the sound of *u* | Zabar carries the sound of *a* | The below-mark carries the sound of *i* |

As evident from the forms of these three letters and the arrangement of their phonetic values, the prepositioned mark appears only above the letter. In like manner, the vowel mark ascends upward, while zabar (the high mark) always stands beneath the letter.

## The Grammatical Rules of Arabic

Among the manifold characteristics of the Arabic language, one stands paramount. Whereas other languages employ only two categories in their rules—singular (wāḥid, ⟪ar:واحد⟫) and plural (jamʿ, ⟪ar:جمع⟫)—Arabic, the very tongue in which ⟪ar:قرآن مجید⟫ was revealed, maintains three: the singular (⟪ar:واحد⟫), the dual (tathníya, ⟪ar:تثنیۃ⟫), and the plural (⟪ar:جمع⟫). This structure unveils a spiritual reality. The First Intellect (⟪ar:عقل اول⟫) accomplished the initial unification (tawḥīd rabb al-ʿālamīn, ⟪ar:توحید رب العالمین⟫). Those who followed, beholding the First Intellect, comprised two beings called the Emanators (al-munbaʿithūn, ⟪ar:منبعثین⟫). Thereafter, seven intellects advanced, forming seven celestial circles. The union of these two emanators and their subsequent ascent became the cause—unique among the world's languages—for Arabic alone to establish the dual form after the singular and before the plural.

| One | Trinity | Plural |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Singular | Dual | Plural |
| First Intellect | The Emanators | Seven Intellects |

Viewed from another perspective, Arabic grammar recognizes two sentence types:

| Nominal Sentence | Verbal Sentence |
| --- | --- | --- |
| *Jumulat al-asmiyya* | *Jumulat al-fiʿliyya* |

According to grammatical convention, the nominal sentence (jumulat al-asmiyya, ⟪ar:جملۃ اسمیۃ⟫) unites a subject (mubtadaʾ, ⟪ar:مبتدا⟫) and a predicate (khabar, ⟪ar:خبر⟫), both in the state of nominative case (rafi') or nunation (tanwīn, ⟪ar:تنوین⟫). These are beings who have not undergone bodily descent (ḥubūṭ al-jismānī, ⟪ar:جسمانی ھبوط⟫) and who possess knowledge of the Divine Names (asmāʾ al-ḥusnā, ⟪ar:أسمآء حسنی⟫). The second type, the verbal sentence (⟪ar:جملہ فعلیۃ⟫), contains a verb, an agent, and an object. For example:

> ⟪ar-quote:ضَرَبَ مَحمُودٌ حَامِداً مِن الخَشَبِ⟫ *(Mahmud struck Hamid with a stick.)*

In this sentence, the verb stands in the nominative (rifi'), the agent in the accusative (naṣb), and the object in the genitive case (jarr). This structure indicates the physical world, where beings manifest in nominative form (the remorseful, ⟪ar:نادمین⟫), in the accusative position (the confused and bewildered, ⟪ar:شاک و متحیرین⟫), and in the genitive (the stubborn and arrogant, ⟪ar:مصر و مستکبرین⟫).

| Nominative | Accusative | Genitive |
| --- | --- | --- |
| The Remorseful | The Confused & Bewildered | The Stubborn & Arrogant |

## Arabic Grammar and the Spiritual Realm

The Arabic language comprises twenty-eight diacritical letters (ḥurūf al-muʿjam, ⟪ar:حروف معجم⟫). They are termed "diacritical" because individually they are meaningless and inert—mere forms lacking sense—unless a speaker articulates them. When two or more letters unite in a specific order, meaning emerges. In the interpretation of the Qur'an, the diacritical letters represent believers who have recently entered the summons of faith through covenant with the Prophet. As they become obedient to their Lord, blessings kindle within them and their forms begin to grow noble—much as letters, when combined, produce meaning. Once they are accepted into the spiritual assembly, they rise beyond the alphabet.[^cite-5]

Amir al-Muʾminīn taught the grammatical rules of Arabic, saying: "First, understand that there are twenty-eight letters called diacritical letters (ḥurūf al-muʿjam). These twenty-eight originally consisted of twenty-seven people (the original Imams) and the Prophet himself. In like manner, in the circle of each intellect, a seat of twenty-eight letters is established. Even in the Imam's circle—which numbers a thousand—when grouped further, only twenty-eight remain. These twenty-eight letters were the primordial origins (aṣūl) from which the alphabet issued materially into this world."

These twenty-eight letters formed some meaningful words and some without meaning. Meaningful words are called nouns (asmaʾ); meaningless ones are called obscure (majhūl). Meaningful words fall into three classes: (1) nouns, (2) verbs, and (3) particles. These correspond to the three sacred letters of divine unity (a, lam, ha):

The scholars explain that names are the nourishments which the Creator fashioned in a state of primary perfection (kamāl awwal, ⟪ar:کمال اول⟫). Verbs are the deeds of those who have become the Word—those who attained the Oneness of the Creator and reached secondary perfection (kamāl thānī, ⟪ar:کمال ثانی⟫)—while the disbeliever becomes a meaningless word. Nouns, verbs, and particles (the self, unity, and the Word-form of light) remain related.

| --- | --- | --- |
| The Self | Unification | The Word (Light Form) |
| The Self | Disbelief | The Word (Dark Form) |

Maulana Ali declares:

> *Every nominative is raised, every accusative is attributed, and every genitive is bound.*

In the world of light, one who first deliberately surpassed himself through his own deeds became a verb—present and elevated. One upon whom this deed's influence fell, who witnessed it and strove to emulate it, was attributed—it came upon him. And whosoever was guided rightly was bound in submission.

| Active | Passive | Recipient |
| Nominative (Self-Transcendence) | Accusative (Passivity) | Genitive (Bindedness) |
| An agent performing an act | A patient receiving an act | A noun undergoing an action |

The eighty-eight Arabic grammatical marks are diacritics placed upon the twenty-eight letters. The letters differ; the diacritics differ. The twenty-eight letters that issued materially into this world were the eternal essences. These twenty-eight letters correspond to the twenty-eight letters in the phrase *Lā ilāha illā Allāh* (There is no god save God). Twenty-eight letters were born in the world of light; the Arabs (the spiritual ranks) were born in the world of intention.

### Clarification

These twenty-eight letters were employed with diacritics in the world of intention. In the spiritual world, they existed without diacritics. In the world of intention, the letter that stood foremost in accepting the summons—the one that immediately exercised its authority without delay—received the prepositioned mark. A circle was drawn above the letter, then elongated. For the letter that delayed, the circle faded, leaving only the line of zabar. When he fell from the zabar state, he descended into subservience.

That is, the cessation state did not persist but merged with another similar letter, one bent (cessation) and one straight (diacritical). Two emanations at once—one arriving at truth, the other going astray. |

## Prayer and Diacritical Marks

I return to my theme: what appears outwardly in Arabic as the nominative case (marfūʿ), God has also preserved inwardly in reality. Consider the condition of prayer (ṣalāt), that essential pillar of the faith. The standing posture corresponds to the nominative case, the bowing (rukūʿ) to zabar, and the prostration (sajda) to the below-mark.

## Diacritical Marks: The Spiritual, Celestial, and Physical Realms

If we consider the physical world, the celestial realm, and the spiritual world in light of the origin and persistence of cosmic knowledge, we discern that the spiritual world contains ten circles stretching from the First Intellect (al-ʿaql al-awwal, ⟪ar:عقل اول⟫) to the lowest intellect, all attaining and having attained perfection.

Below the spiritual world lies the celestial realm, called the world of the heart (ʿālam al-tamathul, ⟪ar:عالم روحانی⟫)—the world of the veiled ones (al-muḥtajibūn) and the world of the soul. Below that stretches the physical realm, founded upon the repentant (al-nādimīn) and the seekers of forgiveness (al-mustaghfirīn), whom God has granted places in the heavens. We may call this the realm of heavens and universes. Below it comes the sublunar world—from the lunar sphere to Earth's surface—called the world of the constrained (muqayyada). The heavens exert influence over all beneath them; they are agents upon the world and its inhabitants, and we humans stand beneath these heavens.

| Nominative | Zabar | Below |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Celestial Realm | Physical Realm | Physical World |

## Diacritical Marks: The Veiled and the Veil

Through God's aid and the support of the Companions of the Age and the elders of faith, I declare: if we regard the celestial realm as an active field, then the physical world and its affairs constitute a passive field by comparison. Should the celestial realm assume the nominative state, the physical world bears its subjugated form. That is, in the spiritual realm, they exist in a state of presence; they are in a state of relief. Whenever movement occurs at any point within them, their veil (ḥijāb, ⟪ar:حجاب⟫) manifests in this world. The one who dwells in the spiritual world occupies the station of presence (ḥāḍir). The Imams who manifest here occupy the zabar state, and then the appearance of the lower veil in the islands takes the zabar form. The prepositioned mark of the veiled one shows his greatness; the below-mark of the lower veil indicates his inferior station.

For another reason, the Imams of the Prophet's chain stand in the prophetic rank; the Imams of the foundation chain take the zabar form; and the believers who come in the seventy ages assume the below form.

| Nominative | Zabar | Below |
| --- | --- | --- |
| The Veiled | High Veil of the Gentlemen | Lower Veil of the Islands |
| The Imams | The Imams | Believers |

## Diacritical Marks: The Three Channels

In the Ḥanīf faith, after the unification (tawḥīd) of the Lord of the Worlds, the most precious and delicate branch is the knowledge of the Prophet (peace be upon him) and the knowledge of ʿAlī the Great. I beg God's forgiveness for my sins and testify that the Triple Channel (thulāthiyyat al-majārī) is a noble and exalted theme. Maulana Amir al-Muʾminīn ʿAlī (may peace be upon him) is the supreme of souls in the world of spirits, his station without peer. Yet in this age, especially in this final seventy, his veil was the lowest of his veils. The Prophet, conversely, whose middle veil descended into this final period of the seventy, thereby became superior to the veil of ʿAlī. For this reason, Maulana ʿAlī recited the words of the Prophet; yet he occupies a very lofty position as one veiled. And there came a moment when these two personalities stood together.

### *Maddā* (Lengthening)

*Maddā* refers to those who radiate luminosity; these exist in diverse forms. Sometimes lengthening occurs within a word; sometimes at its end. In this sense, a sound equivalent to two or four letter-values must be drawn. The clause drawn to the equivalent of two letters refers to the effulgence of the divine realm (Lāhūt) upon the human realm (Nasūt). That drawn to the equivalent of four letters indicates the veil's support by the veiled one (muḥtajab), or that the illumination of the divine self upon the divine presence descends upon the human presence.

### *Jazm* (Cessation) and *Tashdīd* (Intensification)

Cessation (jazm, ⟪ar:جزم⟫) is the place where the tongue stops; it is a crooked form of zabar. Intensification (tashdīd, ⟪ar:تشدید⟫) is that in which the middle letter is pronounced twice, producing speech's intensity. As in the verse: "O Messenger of God, convey what has been sent down to you."[^cite-6] Both *bulgh* (convey) and *tashdīd* appear, indicating both command and intensity. In this admonishing verse, God said to the Prophet: "O Messenger, convey what has been sent down to you, lest your prophethood be harmed." Thus the tashdīd signals this command's severity; the cessation (jāzm) signals the prohibition that non-compliance brings consequence.

## The Letter Alif

The nobility of the alphabet stands supreme among all letters; it is a letter unique in its form. In the spiritual world, the First Intellect bears the rank of *alif* due to its uniqueness. In the physical world, the Imam of the Age possesses the rank of the eternal *alif*; due to his uniqueness, he is not even enumerated among the Imams. In this age, Amir al-Muʾminīn stands as the counterpart of *alif*—he surpasses all the Imams. In every age, the Imam of the time holds the rank of *alif*, for he surpasses all his peers.

### The Greatness of the Believer

Of the twenty-eight letters, *alif* alone possesses a unique form. No other letter shares this configuration. Only the letter *yāʾ* (ي) carries in its phonetic tone a sound resembling *alif*.

### Commentary

From the luminous letters, only one meaningful sentence can be formed:

*Ṣirāt-i ʿAlī Ḥaqq Namsak*

These words function as oaths, signifying that God has sworn by the seven luminous intellects and the seven physical intellects.

### *Naqqa* (The Seven Physical Letters)

The bearers of *naqqa* (the luminous heavy letters) who support the *naqqa* (the dark letters) possess great power. For this reason, the words of the Speakers (*nutaqāʾ*) hold such efficacy that their utterances ennoble the souls of peoples. The "infallibility" (ʿiṣma) that arises within the logic—the fact that they do not err in speech—derives from these scholars' support. Thus we find no incident in the biography of the Messenger of God (peace be upon him) wherein he spoke and then retracted his words.

The sustenance that descends from these scholars feeds the logics. Just as we constantly need outward sustenance—without which the body weakens—so do these logics perpetually receive inward sustenance to maintain their spiritual powers. For this reason, they expend their entire lives with great caution, their sustenance determined by the measure of their piety. Thus one of the titles of Amir al-Muʾminīn is Imam al-Muttaqīn (Imam of the God-fearing).

### The Twenty-Ninth (29) *Muqaṭṭaʿa*: *ʿAm*

[Image reference: 013]

The disconnected letters appear in twenty-nine (29) of the 114 surahs of the Qur'an. Besides these, another *muqaṭṭaʿa* appears in similar form in Surah al-Nabaʾ to complete the count of thirty. It is not a true *muqaṭṭaʿa*; it is like a *muqaṭṭaʿa*.

[Image reference: 014]

In its original sequence, the point of *qāf* (q) is the twenty-eighth, and the point of *nūn* (n) is the twenty-ninth. But *ʿam* (the interrogative) between *q* and *n* became the equivalent of a *muqaṭṭaʿa*, entering to complete the count of thirty. This altered the order: *ʿam* became the twenty-ninth *muqaṭṭaʿa*, and *n* became the thirtieth.

In reality, it is not *ʿam* alone but *ʿan-mā*: "What are they questioning one another about?" That great news, concerning which these people dispute, is truly the Qāʾim ʿAlī of remembered mention. All believe in him; all dispute regarding him. In part, that great news is Amir al-Muʾminīn ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib.

The Twenty-Ninth (29) *Muqaṭṭaʿa*: a *muqaṭṭaʿa* that is not itself a *muqaṭṭaʿa*, yet functions as one—it always breaks the sequence preceding it, and the thirtieth establishes that sequence. The thirtieth appears in Surah al-Nūn, which is the letter *nūn* itself, the foundation of all disconnected letters.

[Image reference: 015]

In the same way, *Bismillāh ar-raḥmān ar-raḥīm* (In the name of God, the Most Gracious, the Dispenser of Grace), which opens every surah save Surah al-Barāʾa, finds its completion in the thirtieth verse of Surah an-Naml:

Amir al-Muʾminīn stands as the thirtieth in rank—that is, when Imam Ismāʿīl ibn Ibrāhīm began a new succession of Imamate, ʿAlī was the thirtieth, and the Messenger of God (peace be upon him) was the twenty-ninth who broke the preceding chain. Note: In this reckoning, not all Imams are included, but a special group has been arranged similar to the month of Rajab, in which the Messenger is the twenty-ninth and ʿAlī the thirtieth.

The days of Rajab end with Imam al-Ḥasan and Maulana Hāshim ibn Muḥammad (the thirtieth Imam). Similarly, the twenty-eighth *dāʿī* in the summons is Syedna Jaʿfar ibn Syedna Sulaymān. ## Nine Diacritical Marks of the First Half

Among the seventeen surahs comprising the first half of the Qur'an, nine bear the disjoined letters—al-Muqaṭṭaʿāt (the Disjoined Letters). These nine surahs signify the *quorum* (mutawāda), denoting the cyclic forms established across nine periods spanning 9,000 years: three millennia preceding Adam, six millennia from Adam's time until the Prophet Jesus, and one thousand years constituting the prophetic era of Muḥammad. Each thousand-year cycle represents a discrete cosmological age. The chronological distribution appears thus:

| The Pre-Adamite Period | From Adam to Jesus | The Muhammadan Age |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 3,000 years | 5,000 years | 1,000 year |

The Qur'an makes oblique reference to the three-thousand-year epoch preceding creation through its sacred verses, whilst the five-thousand-year period spanning from Adam to Jesus receives explicit mention in the following:

> ⟪quran 3:124⟫
> ⟪ar:‏إِذْ تَقُولُ لِلْمُؤْمِنِينَ أَلَن يَكْفِيَكُمْ أَن يُمِدَّكُمْ رَبُّكُم بِثَلَثَةِ آلَفٍۢ مِّنَ ٱلْمَلَٓئِكَةِ مُنزَلِينَ ‎⟫
> *(Remember) when you said to the believers, "Is it not enough for you that your Lord should help you with three thousand angels sent down?"*

> ⟪quran 3:125⟫
> ⟪ar:‏بَلَىٰٓ ۚ إِن تَصْبِرُوا۟ وَتَتَّقُوا۟ وَيَأْتُوكُم مِّن فَوْرِهِمْ هَذَا يُمْدِدْكُمْ رَبُّكُم بِخَمْسَةِ آلَفٍۢ مِّنَ ٱلْمَلَٓئِكَةِ مُسَوِّمِينَ ‎⟫
> *Yes, if you hold steadfast to patience and piety, and they come upon you suddenly, your Lord will reinforce you with five thousand angels bearing distinctive marks.*

These angelic manifestations embody the spiritual sustenance afforded to the faithful across the cosmic ages. During the Battle of Badr, the Prophet (s) was aided by such celestial forces; similarly, when Imām al-Ḥusayn (a) heard the celestial trumpet on the Day of ʿĀshūrāʾ, he perceived that same eightfold thousand-year resonance echoing from the spiritual realm.[^cite-8]

### The Tenth Surah: Surah al-Nahl

Following these nine disjoined-letter surahs, the tenth surah—Surah al-Nahl (Chapter 16)—occupies the first half without interruption, though it bears no diacritical mark. Its opening verse announces:

> ⟪quran 16:1⟫
> ⟪ar:‏أَتَىٰٓ أَمْرُ ٱللَّهِ فَلَا تَسْتَعْجِلُوهُ ۚ سُبْحَنَهۥ وَتَعَلَىٰ عَمَّا يُشْرِكُونَ ‎⟫
> *The command of Allah has come; do not seek to hasten it. Glory be to Him, and exalted above what they associate with Him.*

The divine command (*Amr Allāh*) here signifies the station of the Amīr al-Muʾminīn (the Commander of the Faithful), who embodies the authority established by the Originator, Most High (*Mubdiʿ Taʿālā*). The verse counsels restraint: one must not precipitate the manifestation of the Amīr al-Muʾminīn in this era. His *ẓuhūr* (apparition) remains partial, for he appears as one facet of the Qāʾim (the Establisher), whose complete revelation awaits the future age. In another sense, the passage forbids precipitating divine punishment upon the multitude—a prerogative reserved for the Hujjat (the Proof) when the eschatological hour arrives.[^cite-9] The verse then declares:

> He who comes after is greater than what people suppose of him, for his station encompasses the highest spiritual realities, which pertain to the Hereafter itself.

This passage applies in part to ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib, yet in its entirety it refers to the Qāʾim ʿAlī Dhikr al-Salām (the Establisher, whose mention be exalted).[^cite-10]

### The Night Prayer Rak'ahs

Just as the first half comprises seventeen surahs, of which nine bear disjoined letters, the daily ritual prayer consists of seventeen *rak'ahs* (bowing cycles) in total, each corresponding to the wholeness of the Qur'anic revelation. For this reason, nine *rak'ahs* are designated for the night prayers:

| ʿIshāʾ | Maghrib | Fajr |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 4 | 3 | 2 |

| **Ishāʾ (Night)** | **Maghrib (Evening)** | **Fajr (Dawn)** |
| 4 | 3 | 2 |

The sum yields nine, mirroring the nine disjoined-letter chapters of the Qur'an's first division.

### Nine Disjoined Letters and Twenty-Nine Qur'ānic Letters

The nine diacritical surahs comprising the first half are composed of twenty-nine distinct disjoined letters in total. These twenty-nine letters signify that Muḥammad, the Prophet (s), stands as the twenty-ninth Imam within the hierarchical sequence of the Imāms established after Ismāʿīl ibn Ibrāhīm (the Friend of Allah). He completes a full cycle of the Imamate. ## Twenty Disjoined Letters of the Second Half

The twenty surahs bearing diacritical marks in the second half of the Qur'an correspond to the twenty Imāms extending from Imām al-Ḥasan (a) to Imām Mawlānā al-Manṣūr. From the twenty-first Imām onward—Imām Mawlānā al-Ṭayyib—the era of the Ṭayyibī succession and the age of concealment (*sattar kālī*) commenced, initiating a new cosmic cycle wherein the Proof remains hidden from ordinary perception yet continues to exercise spiritual governance over the faithful.

### Twenty Disjoined Letters and Forty-Nine Qur'ānic Letters

The second half of the Qur'an contains twenty surahs bearing disjoined letters, which collectively comprise forty-nine distinct letter-combinations. These forty-nine letters signify the forty-nine Imāms of the prophetic age, distributed across seven *asbāʿ* (weeks), with each week containing seven Imāms. In an esoteric sense, these forty-nine also represent the forty-nine *daʿwas* (summonses) that emerged during the era of *kashf* (unveiling), the final and fiftieth *daʿwa* embodying the comprehensive establishment of all prior knowledge.[^cite-12]

The Qur'an contains twenty-nine surahs with diacritical marks, a number that indicates the Prophet (s) himself, for he is the twenty-ninth Imām in the sequence of the Imamate. The entire *Anwār* (lights, illuminations) of the Qur'an descended upon his noble heart; the finest of these are verses composed of letters of radiance, and the Prophet himself—the twenty-ninth in order—stands as the very vessel of their transmission. For this reason, the descent of the Kaʿbah was ordained to occur on the twenty-ninth of Dhū al-Qaʿdah, eternally linking the Prophet to this sacred numeral.

### The Twenty-Ninth and Thirtieth Letters: The Completion and Transcendence

The letter-combination that completes the sequence of these twenty-nine is not itself a disjoined letter (*muqaṭṭaʿ*), but rather the dyadic letters *ʿAyn-Mīm* (ع-م), which resemble the disjoined letters whilst transcending their categorical definition. Just as the fiftieth and fifty-first Imāms lie beyond the seven-week cycle of forty-nine, standing as their culmination and transcendence, so too do these final letters break the genre of their predecessors. The *Qāʾim* (the Establisher) himself similarly transcends the sequential order of the Imāms, appearing at the termination of one age and the inauguration of the next. These letters are *ʿAyn* and *Mīm*—*ʿAlī* and *Muḥammad*—whose meanings pervade and illuminate all others.[^cite-13]

The meanings of all other disjoined-letter combinations remain concealed in their esoteric depths, yet the dyadic *ʿAyn-Mīm* possesses transparent meaning: it denotes the eternal union of *ʿAlī* and *Muḥammad*, whose radiances are never severed from one another.

### Meaningless and Meaningful Letters

There exist two categories of Qur'ānic letter-combinations: those bearing explicit meaning and those devoid of it. In each thousand-year cycle of the age of Revelation (*dawr al-nutq*, the era of prophecy), a *Qāʾim* appears who gathers the knowledge and spiritual luminosity accumulated throughout that millennium. With the advent of *kashf* (the age of unveiling), which begins through the son of Mawlānā al-Qāʾim, a new Imāmic succession emerges. Yet Mawlānā al-Qāʾim himself is not numbered among this new order, just as ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib, though supreme in rank, is not reckoned as the first of the hidden Imāms—that distinction belongs to al-Ḥasan. The disjoined letters are unique to the age of Revelation and have no counterpart in the age of Unveiling.

The forty-nine Qāʾims who appear during *kashf* correspond inversely to the forty-nine disjoined-letter combinations, which remain semantically opaque. Those who emerge after them—the fiftieth and fifty-first establishments—correspond to the transparent letters *Mīm* and *ʿAyn*, which together form the dyadic *ʿAyn-Mīm*, as exemplified in Surah 78:

> ⟪quran 78:1⟫
> ⟪ar:‏عَم يَتَسَآءَلُونَ ‎⟫
> *What are they questioning one another about?*

No age has ever been devoid of *Muḥammad* and *ʿAlī*, for they repeat and manifest themselves perpetually across all cycles. In the letters *ʿAyn-Mīm*, when reversed, one obtains *Mīm-ʿAyn* (*Maʿ*), signifying *together*—an eternal testimony that *Muḥammad* and *ʿAlī* shall forever remain inseparable.[^cite-14]

### The Integration and Meaning-Bestowal

The significance becomes manifest when the forty-nine Imāms merge and unite with *Mīm* and *ʿAyn*: only then do they acquire full semantic resonance. When the believers become absorbed into the *Haikāl* (the sacred temple, the spiritual form) of the Imām's son, they too attain meaning and become the *Nafs Mutamīmah* (the perfected self)—the self endowed with understanding and purpose. In other words, so long as the disjoined letters remain in proximity to the higher *ḥadds* (limits, thresholds) of divine reality, they remain semantically vacant; only upon their integration into the *Haikāl* (the divine form) do these letters acquire meaning and potency.

The principles of *Nāsūt* (human materiality) and *Lāhūt* (divine transcendence) stand in opposition. Although the disjoined letters represent the finest letters of the entire Qur'an and constitute the quintessence of Qur'ānic revelation, they remain meaningless until the one who receives them—the Qāʾim—integrates them and arranges them in their proper order, bestowing upon them the light of comprehension.

### Surahs 113 and 114: The Muʿawwidhāt (The Two Takings of Refuge)

Surahs al-Falaq (Chapter 113) and al-Nās (Chapter 114)—known collectively as the *Muʿawwidhāt* or *Muʿawwidhatayn* (The Two Surahs of Refuge)—signify the argument of the *Qāʾim* (a) and the testimonies supporting his station. Both surahs open with the letter *Qāf* (*q*), which denotes *Qāʾim* (the Establisher); the letter *Lām* (*l*), whose numerical value is thirty, corresponds to the thirty Imāms preceding him, extending from Imām al-Ṭayyib (the twenty-first in the manifest count) to the *Qāʾim* (the fifty-first in the comprehensive reckoning).

These two closing surahs occupy the final position of the Qur'an, just as the *Qāʾim* and his proofs appear at the culmination of the prophetic age. Together with the twenty preceding disjoined-letter surahs, they form a complete set of twenty-two surahs, each corresponding to one of the twenty-two Imāms, including both the Established (*Qāʾim*) and the Hujjah (the Proof).

## The Enumeration of Disjoined Letters in the Qur'an

### [5] Al-Raḥmān (Five Instances / Fifty Clean Ones)

The verse *"Alif-Lām-Mīm"* appears five times throughout the Qur'an, signifying the five foundational *Niqābāt* (the Five Pillars of Guardianship) and the five *Anbiyāʾ Akhirīn* (the final five prophets bearing comprehensive authority). *Muḥammad* (s) parallels *Ādam*, *Fāṭimah* parallels *Nūḥ*, *ʿAlī* parallels *Ibrāhīm*, *al-Ḥasan* parallels *Mūsā*, and *al-Ḥusayn* parallels *ʿĪsā*—the *Anwār* (spiritual lights) of these Five Purified Ones stand in homologous relation with the lights of the five prophetic witnesses.[^cite-17]

### The Five Prophetic Cycles and Their Recurrence

The *fā'ilah* (the deed or manifestation) of Adam's era has recurred in the Prophet *Muḥammad*; that of Nūḥ in *Fāṭimah*; that of *Ibrāhīm* in *ʿAlī*; and so forth. The transgression of Cain (*Qābīl*) recurred in Abū Bakr, and the obstinacy of Adam was repeated in Abū Bakr as well. Yet the rank of the *Panjtān Pāk* (the Five Purified Ones) transcends all rational categorization.

### [8] Alif-Lām-Mīm (Eight Forms of the Unveiling)

The letters *Alif-Lām-Mīm* (*A-L-M*) appear eight times, signifying the eight permanent *ṣuwar* (forms, archetypes) of the age of *kashf* (unveiling).

#### [1] Khā-Ḥā (The Messenger)

This diacritical mark appears once and denotes the Messenger of Allah (s), who gathered within himself the five letters of the Qur'an, just as this verse itself contains five letters. He likewise assembled the five *Āthār* (traces, essences) constituting the *Khamsat al-Āthār* (the Five Sacred Traces).[^cite-18]

#### [2] Ṭā-Hā (The Qāʾim of the Resurrection)

*Ṭā-Hā* signifies a fixed (*mutamakkina*) station. The numerical value of *Ṭāʾ* is nine, denoting the stable sum of nine thousand years—spanning from three millennia pre-Adamite through six millennia under ʿAlī's guardianship in the age of Concealment. Beyond this, the *Qāʾim* has integrated the entire *Panjtān Pāk* (Five Purified Ones) into his luminous being.

#### [3] Ṭā-Sīn (Three Fixed Forms)

During the pre-Adamite epoch, three established forms appeared. At the 48,000-year juncture, a fixed form emerged that gathered the accumulated knowledge of the thousand-year cycles preceding it; at the 49,000-year mark, another gathered what came before; and at the 50,000-year threshold, a final form completed the integration—each successively encompassing all prior wisdom.

#### [7] Ḥā-Mīm (The Week of Completeness)

These seven disjoined-letter combinations—*Ḥā-Mīm* (ح-م)—correspond to the Imāms of the first week of the Prophet's successors.

##### The First Two: Imām al-Ḥasan and Imām al-Ḥusayn

The initial two *Ḥā-Mīm* combinations appear in the same *āya* (verse-section), signifying *Imām al-Ḥasan* and *Imām al-Ḥusayn*, who according to the Messenger of Allah stood in parity of spiritual station: "*Al-Ḥasan wa-l-Ḥusayn, Imāmān aw lam yaqūmā*" (The Ḥasan and Ḥusayn are Imāms whether they stand or do not stand). The Prophet's *aḥādīth* (sayings) on the excellence of his grandsons are beyond enumeration, yet none elevated *Imām al-Ḥusayn* above *Imām al-Ḥasan*; rather, he always mentioned al-Ḥasan first, then al-Ḥusayn. All subsequent Imāms are designated *Ḥasanī* (descendants of al-Ḥasan) and *Ḥusaynī* (descendants of al-Ḥusayn). In the sacred *Haikāl* (temple) of *Imām al-Ḥusayn*, *Imām al-Ḥasan* occupies the position of the heart—for the heart itself is woven from the temple of al-Ḥasan.[^cite-19]

##### Ḥā-Mīm-ʿAyn: Imām ʿAlī Zayn al-ʿĀbidīn

*Ḥā-Mīm-ʿAyn* denotes *Imām ʿAlī Zayn al-ʿĀbidīn*, whose spiritual station differs altogether from that of all other Imāms. The rewards he accumulated have been matched by none before him and shall be matched by none hereafter. The luminous lamp that Ḥuzayfah al-Yamānī beheld radiating from the head of ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib at the Battle of Ṣiffīn appeared again only upon the head of *Imām ʿAlī Zayn al-ʿĀbidīn*. The four stations (*maqāmāt*: Prophethood, Guardianship, Imamate, and the Will) remained distinct through all the preceding Imāms, yet when they converged in ʿAlī Zayn al-ʿĀbidīn, all four merged into one; thereafter they never again separated until the Day of Judgment. This is why he is called *Ādam al-Awliyāʾ* (the Adam of the Friends of God).[^cite-20]

**Eschatological Station:** On the Day of Judgment, *Imām ʿAlī Zayn al-ʿĀbidīn* will break through the ranks of all the Imāms, the righteous, the pious, and the martyrs, advancing to receive the full reward from his Lord.

##### The Final Four: The Later Imāms of the Week

The final four *Ḥā-Mīm* combinations signify *Imām Muḥammad al-Bāqir*, *Imām Jaʿfar al-Ṣādiq*, *Imām Ismāʿīl*, and *Imām Muḥammad ibn Ismāʿīl* (a).

##### The Exemplary Companions: Instances of Entire Epochs

The first two *Ḥā-Mīm* combinations (appearing in surahs 40 and 41), which share a single introductory verse, correspond to the first two supreme companions and successors—*Muḥammad ibn Ismāʿīl* and *Maʿad al-Muʿizz*. It was during the reign of *Mawlānā Maʿad al-Muʿizz* that *Daʿāʾim al-Islām* (The Foundations of Islam) was compiled as a complete legal and spiritual code.

He gathered not only his own week but also the preceding week, and he possessed the gathering of the *Anwār Khamsa* (the Five Lights), to which the five letters of *al-Ṭayyib* correspond. Among the seven weeks spanning seven thousand years, there exist four Imāms of supreme grandeur, and their collective essence is encapsulated in Surah *Qul Huwa Allāh al-Aḥad* (Say, "He is Allah, the One and Unique"). ## The Gathering of the Five Luminous Lights

On the night of the twenty-seventh of Rajab, when the Messenger of Allah (s) received divine grace (*raḥma*) in a manner unique to him, all the prophets—numbering one hundred and twenty-four thousand—assembled within his sacred sanctuary and became unified under the leadership of the five supreme prophetic witnesses. In the terminology of *taʿwīl* (esoteric exegesis), this gathering is designated as the *Anwār Khamsa* (the Five Luminous Lights).

These five supreme lights were first concentrated wholly within *Muḥammad Muṣtafā* (the Chosen One, s). After his death, these five *Anwār* passed in their completeness to *ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib*, who became their sole bearer and transmitter. Thereafter, they underwent division between *Imām al-Ḥasan* and *Imām al-Ḥusayn*. Subsequently, they reconverged and were gathered again in *Imām ʿAlī Zayn al-ʿĀbidīn*. Following him, *Imām ʿAlī al-Ṭayyib* reunited this sacred gathering within himself. After his concealment, these *Anwār* shall be gathered once more only in the *Qāʾim ʿAlī Dhikr al-Salām*. It is this luminance that Ḥuzayfah al-Yamānī perceived radiating from *Mawlānā ʿAlī's* head at the Battle of Ṣiffīn—a lamp whose light shall rekindle at the appearance of the *Qāʾim*. The passage of *Ḥā-Mīm-ʿAyn* is the Qur'ānic testimony to these five transcendent verses and their perpetual cycles.[^cite-24]

### The Comprehensive Manifestation of the Five Luminous Traces

The gathering of the *Anwār Khamsa* in each of its four chief concentrations represents the quintessence of all prophetic guidance and divine illumination transmitted to humanity. These are the Five Pure Ones (*Panjtān Pāk*) and their representatives across the ages, whose light never dims and whose testimony endures unto the Day of Resurrection.

## Letters of Light and Darkness in the Names of the Natqs (The Speakers)

There are six points distributed throughout the seventy epochs of the age of Concealment; the seventh stands established as the beginning of the age of Unveiling. In contrast to these six lower points stands the soul (*al-nafs*, the innermost essence). The nomenclature of the seven prophetic speakers (*Nāṭiq*, the speakers) is composed of *ḥurūf nūrāniyya* (letters of light) and *ḥurūf ẓulmaniyya* (letters of darkness or oppression). The letters of the *Qāʾim*, however, consist purely of *ḥurūf nūrāniyya* (letters of light), for he stands as the soul in antithesis to the *Nāṭiq*.

### The Seven Prophetic Speakers and Their Luminous and Dark Letters

#### Ādam (a.s.)

*Ādam*'s name contains both illuminated letters and letters of obscuration, reflecting the dual nature of his prophetic station.

#### Nūḥ (a.s.)

*Nūḥ* (Noah) likewise bears in his name the mingled signatures of light and shadow.

#### Ibrāhīm (a.s.)

*Ibrāhīm* (Abraham) exemplifies the balance of luminous and dark letters in the nomenclature of the *Nāṭiqs*.

#### Mūsā (a.s.)

*Mūsā* (Moses) embodies this duality in the letters of his blessed name.

#### ʿĪsā (a.s.)

*ʿĪsā* (Jesus), among all the *Nāṭiqs*, bears the name that carries both radiances and shadows.

#### Muḥammad (s)

The Prophet *Muḥammad*, the Seal of the Prophets, bears in his name the integration of all prior luminous and dark letters, transcending their opposition through the perfection of his station.

#### ʿAlī (a.s.)

*ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib* stands in a station wholly unique: his name contains no letters of oppression or darkness whatsoever. This signifies that his being is a *ḥulāṣa* (quintessence, distillation) of the very essence of Adam's nature. When an ordinary human dies, the soul departs and the body decays into foulness; yet these luminous speakers transcend such ordinary dissolution. *ʿAlī* and the *Qāʾim* alone remain forever incorruptible and pure.

#### Al-Qāʾim ʿAlī Dhikr al-Salām

The *Qāʾim* too possesses a name composed solely of *ḥurūf nūrāniyya*, bearing no letters of darkness.

### The Luminous and Dark Letters: Their Spiritual Significance

The letters composing the names of the *Nāṭiqs*—letters of light—number ten, corresponding to the *ʿAshr al-ʿUqūl* (the Ten Intellects, the ten spiritual limits—*Arbāb al-Taʾyīd*, those who give support). These ten spiritual realities sustain and uphold each prophetic speaker. In contrast, three letters of shadow appear in the nomenclature of the *Nāṭiqs*—corresponding to the three positions of *Imām* (the Guide), *Ḥujjah* (the Proof), and *Dāʿī* (the Summoner) present in every epoch, by whom divine guidance is transmitted to humanity.

### The Purification of the Qāʾim

No letters of darkness appear in the names of the two supreme figures—the *Nāṭiq* and the *Qāʾim*—because they have been purified and refined; their bodies have become the dwelling-place (*maḥallis*) wherein the *Anwār* (luminous lights) take manifest form. As Allah declares in the Qur'an:

> The essence and meaning attributed to the Nāṭiqs is said to belong to them wholly, yet in part to the *Nāṭiq* himself alone.

In another context, Allah states:

> These verses refer to the *Hijāb* (the veil)—those souls who conceal the divine realities within their veiled and corporeal frames. One example is ʿAbdullāh ibn Rawāḥah, who served as the *Hijāb* (the concealing veil) of the Prophet (s). Another is Yūsuf al-Najjār, who became the veil protecting ʿĪsā (Jesus). Three distinct veils operated in the concealment of ʿĪsā's physicality. *Sayyidnā Jaʿfar ibn Sulaymān* expressed this mystery in his verse:

> *"Await the completion of the age; be thou humble and contrite, that thy being may merge into the Gatherer of all souls."*

The *Hijāb* functions as the intermediary through which divine realities, too subtle and radiant for ordinary human perception, are filtered and made accessible to the faithful—a veil that protects rather than obscures, revealing through its very concealment.[^cite-25]

## The Wisdom of Nineteen in the Disjoined Letters

A group of Jews posed to the Messenger of Allah (s) a question concerning the number of angels who stand guard over Hell, and he answered by extending the fingers of both his hands. Upon a second inquiry, he closed the thumb of his right hand and displayed the remaining fingers, thereby indicating the number nineteen. Whereupon they began to mock, declaring, "We number in the thousands, yet you claim only nineteen guardian angels!" One wrestler among them declared, "I shall overturn one of these upon my back and dispatch two more." At that moment, the following verse descended from Allah:

> These divine servants stand firm in their station, and no increase of numbers enhances their power. The nineteen represent the complete spectrum of divine ordering and the perfection of God's design.

*Maʿmūn al-Ṣaqal*, a celebrated calligrapher and scholar of the Arabic letters, established that the foundational letters of the Arabic alphabet number nineteen; by the addition of one, two, or three diacritical dots upon these base letters, all twenty-eight letters of the complete alphabet are generated.

In Surah al-Jinn (Chapter 72), Verse 28, Allah declares:

> *"And He has counted all things in number."*

When one adds together the surah's number (72) and the verse's number (28)—i.e., *7 + 2 + 2 + 8*—the sum yields precisely nineteen. Furthermore, in another verse:

> *"We shall not cease to show them Our signs in the earth and in their own souls until they know that it is the truth from their Lord."*

The total count of this verse's letters also yields nineteen.

### The Word "Unique" (al-Wāḥid): Nineteen Instances

The word *wāḥid* (singular, unique, one), which epitomizes the absolute monotheistic declaration of Allah's oneness, appears precisely nineteen times throughout the Qur'an. This number itself is nineteen.

The supreme word of the Qur'an describing divine uniqueness and singularity appears in Surah 19: *"There is no deity save Allah."* This is the central testimony to God's absolute oneness, appearing with perfect correspondence to the surah number itself.

From these fourteen letters, only a single meaningful sentence can be composed: *"Ṣirāṭ-ʿAlī Ḥaqq Namuskā"* (The Path of ʿAlī is Truth; grasp it). This affirms the cosmic reality and the centrality of ʿAlī to the Qur'anic revelation and the divine order.[^cite-26]

The first surah in which a *muqaṭṭaʿ* appears is Surah al-Baqarah (Chapter 2); the final surah bearing a disjoined letter is Surah al-Nūn (Chapter 68). Between these two surahs, there are thirty-eight surahs that do not contain disjoined letters—and thirty-eight equals twice nineteen. If one examines the internal structure of these intervals, one discovers that the disjoined-letter surahs contain between one and five letters each.

### The Most Frequently Occurring Letters

Alif, Lām, and Mīm are the letters appearing with the highest frequency throughout the Qur'an, and they form the opening declaration of six major surahs.

### The Twenty-Eight Letters and Their Configurations

The complete enumeration of the fourteen disjoined letters, their fourteen distinct combinations, and their distribution across twenty-nine surahs—each representing a unique configuration—demonstrates the mathematical perfection underlying the Qur'anic text and its correspondence to the spiritual realities of the Imamate and divine governance.

### The First Verse: Nineteen Letters

The first verse ever revealed to the Prophet—from Surah al-ʿAlaq (Chapter 96)—contains precisely nineteen letters:

> ⟪quran 96:1⟫
> ⟪ar:اقرأ باسم ربك الذي خلق‎⟫
> *"Read! In the name of thy Lord who created..."*

All five verses of this opening surah contain exactly nineteen letters each. Across these nineteen verses, a total of seventy-six letters appear, which when divided by nineteen yields a quotient of four with a remainder of zero—a perfect correspondence (76 ÷ 19 = 4).

Surah al-ʿAlaq occupies the position of the ninety-sixth surah in the canonical Qur'anic order; when numbered from the end of the Qur'an in reverse (Surahs 114, 113, 112... down to 96), this surah becomes precisely the nineteenth surah from the terminus.

### The Mathematical Structure of the Entire Qur'an

The Qur'an contains a total of 6,346 verses, a number equivalent to *19 × 334*. The word *Qur'ān* (the Recitation) appears fifty-seven times throughout the entire text—and *57 = 19 × 3*.

The entire architecture of the Qur'an, from the numerical value of its letters to the distribution of its surahs and verses, manifests perfect harmony and demonstrates that this sacred text is the unmistakable word of the All-Knowing, All-Wise Creator—a revelation whose internal structure mirrors the eternal order of divine governance through the Imamate and testifies eternally to the truth of ʿAlī's primacy and the perfection of the divine guide.[^cite-27]
