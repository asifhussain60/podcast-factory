## The Wisdom of Iʿrāb

On the occasion of the Urs of ⟪ar:سیدنا علی بن حسین (قس )⟫, held at the residence of ⟪ar:الحد الفاضل سید عبد اللّٰہ بن ھبۃ اﷲ⟫ in Hyderabad, the speaker drew the attention of the Majlis to the importance of iʿrāb (the system of vocalic case-endings) in Arabic, taking it up alongside an exposition of the conditions of the Hajj. Iʿrāb, ⟪ar:اعراب⟫, he reminded us, is no mere decoration of the script. Even the smaller marks of ⟪ar:تشدید، جزم⟫ are instruments by which sense is altered, sometimes reversed altogether. That day's address opened a long horizon of thought for the present writer, a humble servant attached to the noble ⟪ar:دعوت ھادیۃ سلیمانیۃ⟫ (the Hādiya Sulaymāniyya daʿwa, the summons).

## Solar and Lunar Letters

### Solar Letters

When the definite article of ⟪ar:معرفۃ⟫ (definiteness)—namely (⟪ar:ال⟫)—precedes one of the ⟪ar:شمسی حروف⟫, the lām of the article is not articulated; the article is heard only as a doubling of the following consonant. Thus in ⟪ar:الشَّمسُ⟫ the alif slips directly into a doubled shīn, and the existence of the lām is not registered by the ear.

### Lunar Letters

When ⟪ar:معرفۃ⟫ (⟪ar:ال⟫) precedes one of the ⟪ar:قمری حروف⟫, the lām is fully pronounced. Thus in ⟪ar:القمر⟫ the presence of both alif and lām is plainly indicated.

| Solar Letters | Lunar Letters |
| --- | --- |
| The lām is concealed at the moment of articulation; its presence is felt only by the doubling it leaves behind. | The lām is plainly heard; both letters of the article keep their independent voices. |
| ⟪ar:الشَّمسُ⟫ (the sun) | ⟪ar:القمر⟫ (the moon) |

## The Four Letters of the Qurʾān

Although ⟪ar:قرآن مجید⟫ is a voluminous book, and although Arabic is not the mother tongue of most of its readers, it has been preserved in countless hearts. No other book is so widely committed to memory. Two pages of an ordinary work are not so easily learned by heart, yet the miracle of the Qurʾān is that its ḥuffāẓ stand in their thousands throughout the world. The manner of its writing, the manner of its recitation, the modulation of its tones—all have become disciplines unto themselves. A second sign is this: there are many passages in which a single misplaced ⟪ar:أعراب⟫ turns affirmation into unbelief. May God protect us from such a slip.

Consider the word ⟪ar:قرآن⟫. It contains two dotted letters (the two qāfs), each drawn as a circle, and two undotted letters (the two rāʾs), neither of them in circular form. By the science of numerical reckoning the nūn stands at fifty and the qāf at one hundred; many meanings flow from this, as ⟪ar:مولانا حضرت اکرام علی صاحب مقدس⟫ has set out at length in his book. The semantic riches of these letters have likewise been expounded by ⟪ar:حضرت مولانا اسحاق صاحب⟫ in his ⟪ar:فیض المفید⟫. Their finer points have been described by the ⟪ar:دعات عظام اور مناصیب کرام⟫ (eminent duʿāt and noble dignitaries) in their own treatises, works belonging to the treasury of ⟪ar:دعوت ھادیۃ⟫.

*Risāla on the Wisdom of Arabic*

The word ⟪ar:قرآن⟫ is composed of four letters. Two of them are joined to one another (⟪ar:قر⟫); the other two stand apart (⟪ar:آن⟫). The very name ⟪ar:قرآن⟫ derives from ⟪ar:قرین⟫, meaning "companion." The teaching is plain: the Qurʾān, taʾwīl-wise, must pass through its four ranks before it reaches the people. Two of them (⟪ar:قر)⟫ remain conjoined as one inseparable pair; the other two (⟪ar:آن⟫) stand apart at full openness. The two conjoined letters signify the two wings of the ⟪ar:ناطق⟫ (Speaking Prophet) and the ⟪ar:اساس⟫ (Foundation, the Imam who carries the inner sense); these are never far from one another. The two separated letters of (⟪ar:آن⟫) are emblems of ⟪ar:عقل و نفس⟫ (Intellect and Soul). Just as the ⟪ar:ناطق اور اساس⟫ draw their support from above, so the two simple letters of (⟪ar:آن⟫) stand for the higher principles from which the ⟪ar:ناطق اور اساس، عقل اور نفس⟫ together receive their sustenance.

### Alif

The argument of the alif is that it points to ⟪ar:عقل کل⟫ (the Universal Intellect), for the alif stands aloof from every other letter in such a way that when the scribe reaches it, the running line of his script is broken. In ordinary writing other letters join the alif from below, but the alif itself does not connect downwards into a following letter. So too with all beings: they are connected upwards to the ⟪ar:عقل اول⟫, while the ⟪ar:عقل اول⟫ has no creature above it to which it could be connected. By ⟪ar:حساب جمل⟫ (the abjad reckoning) the numerical value of alif is one, the very sign that the ⟪ar:عقل اول⟫ is the principle of every existent and the source of its coming-to-be—just as the number one is the principle from which every other number proceeds.[^cite-1]

### Nūn

⟪ar:نون، نفس کل⟫: the nūn is the argument for the Universal Soul. It is a line that bends toward closure but does not quite meet itself. So too ⟪ar:نفس کل⟫: when it beheld ⟪ar:عقل کل⟫ it was overcome with longing and turned toward it, but it could not attain that station and shall never attain it. The very shape of the ⟪ar:نون⟫ is a glyph of yearning that never closes. So also: when the rites of Hajj are performed, both the beginning and the end of its name are nūn—an argument that every ending will at last answer to its beginning.

### Qāf and Rāʾ

The first of these four letters is ⟪ar:ق⟫, which serves as the sign for the ⟪ar:اساس⟫, because it is through the Asās that the believer finds the road to knowing the ⟪ar:ناطق⟫; and ⟪ar:ر⟫ points back to the ⟪ar:ناطق⟫. By ⟪ar:علم العدد⟫ (the science of number), ⟪ar:ق⟫ stands at one hundred and ⟪ar:ر⟫ at two hundred. The Nāṭiq is the master of ⟪ar:ناطق، تنزیل و تاویل⟫ (revelation and esoteric interpretation), while the Asās holds the rank of ⟪ar:اساس، تاویل⟫ (interpretation alone). In the order of religion, the ⟪ar:ناطق⟫ bears the active role and the ⟪ar:اساس⟫ the receptive.

| Tanzīl and Taʾwīl | Taʾwīl |

### Knowledge and Practice

In ⟪ar:قرآن⟫, the joined pair (⟪ar:قر⟫) is the figure of ⟪ar:عمل⟫ (practice), which belongs to ⟪ar:جسم مرکب⟫ (the composite body), and the open pair (alif and nūn) is the figure of ⟪ar:علم⟫ (knowledge), which belongs to ⟪ar:بسیط نفس⟫ (the simple soul). Practice without knowledge is the lot of the beasts; knowledge without practice is the lot of the angels; knowledge and practice together are the portion of the human being, who shares ⟪ar:جسم⟫ with the animals and is the equal of the angels in respect of ⟪ar:نفس⟫. By the union of ⟪ar:علم و عمل⟫ a human being can rise from the rank of the beasts toward the rank of the angels.

The four letters of ⟪ar:قرآن⟫ differ from one another, as the legislations (sharāʾiʿ) of all the prophets differ; for these laws touch the body, and bodies differ from one another. But the inward meaning of the divine books and the taʾwīl of the prophets' laws is one—as the human spirit is one, and the spirit is not subject to alteration.

## Letters of Weakness: Alif, Wāw, Yāʾ

To the brethren—may God guide them: ⟪ar:أعراب⟫ is also called ⟪ar:حرکت⟫ (motion). It is of three kinds, each expressed through one of the ⟪ar:حروف علت⟫ (the letters of weakness, the vowel letters):

| Wāw (ḍamma) | Alif (fatḥa) | Yāʾ (kasra) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| The ḍamma carries the sound of wāw. | The fatḥa carries the sound of alif. | The kasra carries the sound of yāʾ. |

As is plain from the shapes of the three vowels and the way their corresponding letters are written, the wāw appears only above the letter—so the ḍamma is set above; the alif lies flat across—so the fatḥa rides as a horizontal stroke; the yāʾ descends below—so the kasra is set beneath.

## The Rules of Arabic

The Arabic tongue has many distinctive features. One of them is this: every other language of the world recognises only two grammatical numbers, ⟪ar:واحد اور جمع⟫ (singular and plural), but the language in which ⟪ar:قرآن مجید⟫ was revealed admits three: ⟪ar:واحد⟫ (singular), ⟪ar:تثنیۃ⟫ (dual), and ⟪ar:جمع⟫ (plural). This points to the order of ⟪ar:عالم روحانی⟫: the one who first proclaimed ⟪ar:توحید رب العالمین⟫ (the absolute unity of the Lord of the Worlds) was the ⟪ar:عقل اول⟫, alone in its station. Those who then beheld the ⟪ar:عقل اول⟫ and joined its proclamation of ⟪ar:توحید رب العالمین⟫ were two beings, called the ⟪ar:منبعثین⟫ (the two Emanators, al-Munbaʿithūn). After them, seven further intellects unfolded and seven cycles took shape. Thus the singular, the dual, and the plural in Arabic correspond to that first ⟪ar:عقل اول⟫, to the two ⟪ar:منبعث⟫ who arose with it, and to the seven intellects that followed; ⟪ar:تثنیۃ⟫ came between ⟪ar:واحد⟫ and ⟪ar:جمع⟫ in this language alone because that twofold emanation has no parallel outside the spiritual order.[^cite-2]

| --- | --- | --- |
| First Intellect | The Two Emanators (al-Munbaʿithūn) | The Seven Intellects |

Looked at from another angle, the ⟪ar:عربی قواعد⟫ recognise two kinds of sentence:

A nominal sentence is, by the grammarians' rule, the union of ⟪ar:جملۃ اسمیۃ مبتدا⟫ (subject) and ⟪ar:خبر⟫ (predicate); both stand either in the state of rafʿ or in the state of ⟪ar:تنوین⟫ (the doubled-vowel ending). The figural meaning is this: these are the beings who have not been subjected to ⟪ar:جسمانی ھبوط⟫ (descent into corporeal existence), and they hold the rank of the ⟪ar:أسمآء حسنی⟫ (the most beautiful Names). The second kind, ⟪ar:جملہ فعلیۃ⟫, is built upon a verb, an agent, and an object. For example:

⟪ar-quote:ضَرَبَ مَحمُودٌ حَامِداً مِن الخَشَبِ⟫ ("Maḥmūd struck Ḥāmid with the rod").

In this sentence the verb takes its proper form, the agent stands in rafʿ, the object stands in naṣb, and the final ⟪ar:أسم⟫ governed by the preposition stands in jarr. This is a sign of the corporeal world: that there are also among us human beings set in the form of ⟪ar:قامت الفیۃ⟫ (the upright alif-form, the standing body), and they fall into three classes—those in the rank of rafʿ, namely the ⟪ar:نادمین⟫ (the repentant); those in the rank of naṣb, namely the ⟪ar:شاک و متحیرین⟫ (the doubters and the bewildered); and those in the rank of jarr, namely the ⟪ar:مصر و مستکبرین⟫ (the obstinate and the haughty).

## The Rules of Arabic and the Spiritual World

The Arabic tongue possesses twenty-eight letters of muʿjam (the unpointed alphabet considered apart from any vowelling). They are called "dumb" because, in isolation, each is mute and bears no sense of itself unless a speaker draws it into utterance. Only when two or more letters are joined in a particular order does meaning come to be. In the taʾwīl of the Qurʾān, the letters of the muʿjam are those believers who have just entered the daʿwa through the prophetic covenant. As they grow obedient to their Lord, a blessing takes root in them, and their forms begin to be ennobled—exactly as the joining of letters yields significant speech. As soon as the surrounding rank receives them, they rise above the bare alphabet.

The Amīr al-Muʾminīn taught the rules of Arabic grammar in this very vein. He said: "First understand that there are twenty-eight letters; these are called the letters of muʿjam." The first community he addressed was likewise twenty-eight souls. So too, in every circle of an intellect, twenty-eight ranks are seated. Even the circle of the Imam, peace be upon him, is said to number a thousand; but when they are gathered into their proper order, they are still twenty-eight. These twenty-eight letters were the spiritual originals of the alphabets which materially issued forth into this world in alphabetic form.

These twenty-eight letters yield up some combinations that bear meaning and others that do not. Combinations that bear meaning are called kalima ("a word"); combinations without meaning are called majhūl ("unknown"). The meaningful words are themselves of three kinds: (1) nouns, (2) verbs, and (3) particles. These three categories answer to the three jewels of the formula of unity—alif, lām, hāʾ:

The duʿāt have explained that the nouns are the substances which the Originator brought into being in the state of perfection. The verbs are the actions of those who become a "Word": whoever has attained the unity of the Originator and reached his completion has become a meaningful word, while whoever has fallen into denial has become a meaningless one. The particles, which connect nouns to verbs, link the self to its actions.

| Noun | Verb | Particle |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Self | Tawḥīd | Kalima (the luminous form) |
| Self | Kufr | Majhūl (the dark form) |

Mawlānā ʿAlī, peace be upon him, has said:

> Every subject is raised, every object is brought under, and every governed noun is restrained.

In the world of light, the one who first put his own being forward by deliberate action became a "verb" (raised up)—that is, he became "present." The one upon whom the verb's action fell, who beheld him and strove to become like him, was made "object"—the action descended upon him. And whoever was driven on to the straight path was "governed" by it.

| Alif | Lām | Hāʾ |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Active | Acted Upon | The Bond Between |
| Marfūʿ (raised beyond the self) | Manṣūb (acted upon) | Majrūr (drawn along) |
| The One who moves outward | The one who receives the action | The one who is taken in by it |

The Arabic system of vocalisation places diacritics above the letters of the muʿjam. The letters are one thing; the diacritics are another. The twenty-eight letters which were issued materially into this world are the eternal originals. The number twenty-eight is no accident, for it is the count of letters within the phrase *Lā ilāha illā Allāh*. Twenty-eight letters were born in the world of light, and their Arabic equivalents were born in the world of intentions.

*A clarification:*

These twenty-eight letters appear in the world of intentions clothed in diacritics; in the spiritual realm they bear no diacritics. In the world of intentions, the letter that was foremost in accepting the daʿwa—the one that exercised its prerogative without delay—received the ḍamma upon itself (the wāw drawn in miniature, then extended). The letter that delayed received only the stroke of the fatḥa. And when it fell further from the rank of fatḥa, it came down to the rank of kasra.

## Prayer and Iʿrāb

Returning to my theme: what appears outwardly in Arabic at the level of the marfūʿ has been given by God an inward counterpart in reality. ## Iʿrāb: The Spiritual, Celestial, and Corporeal Worlds

If we consider the corporeal world, the world of life, and the spiritual world in the light of the origin and continuance of the knowledge of being, we find that the spiritual world is the realm in which ten intellect-circles unfold, from the First Intellect down through the lower intellects, and in which every one of them attains its perfection.

Below the spiritual world lies the world of life, which is also called the world of the heart or al-tamūr; this is the world of the muḥtajibīn (those who are veiled), and it is the world of the soul in this manner. Below that begins the world of the jirmāniyyāt (the celestial bodies), founded upon the nādimīn (the repentant) and the mustaghfirīn (those who seek forgiveness); to them God has granted a station in the heavens, and one may name this the world of the spheres and the celestial orbs. Beneath that we call the world of the jusmāniyyāt—the realm of bodies, extending from beneath the sphere of the moon (the world of the ummāt) down to the surface of our earth—the world of bodily fragmentation. The heavens act, and the beings who live beneath them are acted upon; we ourselves are human beings standing under those heavens.

| Ḍamma | Fatḥa | Kasra |

| World of Life (rūḥānī) | Celestial World (jirmānī) | Corporeal World (jusmānī) |

## Iʿrāb: The Veiled and the Veiling

With the help of God and through the support of the Companions of the Age and the elders of the religion, I venture this: if the world of life is taken as an active field, then this world and what lies between are passive in comparison. If the world of life occupies the subjective state, this world holds the lower, objective state. In the spiritual realm the higher ranks stand in repose; whenever a movement issues from any of those stations, its veil-form appears in the world below. The one who abides in the spiritual realm holds the rank of ḍamma; the Imam who descends into manifestation holds the rank of fatḥa; and the lesser veil that appears upon the islands of the daʿwa is in the rank of kasra. The ḍamma over the muḥtajab (the veiled one, written with mīm) declares his eminence, and the kasra beneath the veil-bearer (written with hāʾ) marks the diminished rank of the veiling form.

For another reason as well: the Imams of the descending chain (al-istiqāṭa) stand in the rank of the Prophet's marker; the Imams of the ascending chain (al-istiʿtidāʾ) stand in the rank of fatḥa; and the believers who attend upon the Seventy Ages stand in the rank of kasra.

## Correspondences of the Diacritical Marks in the Various Spheres

If one extends the inquiry across the concentric circles of being, the three Arabic diacritics — pesh (ḍamma, the raised mark), zabar (fatḥa, the opening mark), and zir (kasra, the lowering mark) — disclose a rich web of resemblance, since each register of creation rehearses the same triadic geometry.

- In the order of souls, the rational soul (al-nafs al-nāṭiqa) stands in the station of pesh; the sensitive soul (al-nafs al-ḥassāsa) in that of zabar; and the vegetative soul (al-nafs al-nāmiya) in that of zir.
- In the human realm (al-nāsūt), the special nāsūt — that which is joined to the higher world — corresponds to pesh; the shadow nāsūt (nāsūt al-ẓilli) to zabar; and the subordinate nāsūt (nāsūt al-tābiʿ) to zir.
- In the orders of knowledge, the science of realities (ʿilm al-ḥaqāʾiq) sits in the seat of pesh; the science of esoteric exegesis (taʾwīl) in that of zabar; and the science of revealed Law (ʿilm al-sharīʿa) in that of zir.
- In the triad of obedience (al-ṭāʿāt), obedience to God belongs to pesh, obedience to the Messenger to zabar, and obedience to the lawful authorities (ūlū al-amr) to zir — exactly as the Qurʾān arranges them.
- In the season of devotion, the month of Ramaḍān is set against pesh, the month of Rajab al-murajjab against zabar, and the month of Shaʿbān al-karīm against zir.
- The same proportion governs the three stages of fermentation: cream (zubd), leaven (khamīr), and the raw matter beneath them.
- The ranks of the Imams divide similarly into the settled Imam (al-mustaqarr), the entrusted Imam (al-mustawdaʿ), and the believers below them — corresponding to pesh, zabar, and zir respectively.[^cite-1]
- Among the human dispositions, the repentant and those who seek forgiveness occupy pesh; the contrite and the purified occupy zabar; and the rebellious and the arrogant occupy zir.
- Minerals, plants, and animals are likewise opposed: animals at pesh, plants at zabar, and minerals at zir.
- Among the stations of pilgrimage, ʿArafāt belongs to pesh, Minā to zabar, and Muzdalifa to zir.
- If one turns to the heavens, the stars (al-kawākib) display the subtleties of spirituality in the same triadic order: shells (aṣdāf), the subtle (al-laṭīf), and the body of the star itself — by fraction, opening, and elevation.
- Among the foundational ranks of the daʿwa, the three vowels distinguish the Speaker-Prophet (nāṭiq), his Foundation (asās), and the Imam.
- Among the festivals, the Day of Ghadīr stands at pesh, the Feast of Sacrifice (ʿīd al-aḍḥā) at zabar, and the Feast of Fast-Breaking (ʿīd al-fiṭr) at zir.

## The Diacritics as Three Channels

In the Ḥanīf religion — that pure monotheism which is the patrimony of Abraham — after the affirmation of the Oneness of the Lord of the Worlds, no chapter is finer or more delicate than knowledge of the noble Prophet (peace be upon him and his family) and of ʿAlī, the most exalted. Seeking the forgiveness of God Most High for every shortcoming, we say: the doctrine of the three channels (al-majārī al-thalātha) is itself a chapter of high and noble subtlety.

In truth, Mawlānā Amīr al-Muʾminīn ʿAlī (peace be upon him) is the greatest of souls in the world of souls, a station that admits of no partner. That is his condition in the spiritual realm. But in this world, and especially in the present cycle, his veil was the lowest of his veils. The Prophet (peace and blessings be upon him), whose veil is intermediate, came into the world at the end of the cycle, and only at that moment did the Prophet's veil become outwardly superior to ʿAlī's. For this reason, ʿAlī uttered words of obedience and submission to the Messenger — though in himself he stood at a very high station as one veiled (muḥtajab). Yet there came a moment when these two personages stood together in equal majesty.[^cite-2]

The diacritic pesh (signifying elevation) marks the high station of ʿAyn — that is to say, of ʿAlī — in the realm of isolation (al-infirād). The mark of zabar — opening and equality — denotes the station in which the two are equal, namely the station of spiritual imamate (al-imāma al-rūḥāniyya), where Muḥammad and ʿAlī stand together as best of the best. To this station the Prophet referred when he said, "*Anā wa-ʿAlī min nūrin wāḥidin* — I and ʿAlī are of one light." And then there is the descending mark of zir — the station of ʿAlī's outward veiling beneath the Prophet, where ʿAlī said, "I am one of the servants of Muḥammad, I am one of the servants of Muḥammad."

| Pesh | Zabar | Zir |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Isolation (al-infirād) | Spiritual imamate (al-imāma al-rūḥāniyya) | The veil of ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib |

Thus, O brotherhood of the faithful, Mawlānā ʿAlī (may God ennoble his countenance) is in one place raised, in another set level, and in a third placed below. These stations are so refined that none can grasp them save those whom God permits. Here the common believers fall short of true monotheism and cannot ascend to such heights. May God forgive our sins and grant us understanding of these subtleties. For all of this is by the regard and grace of the people of the age (ahl al-zamān) — that in this dark world of the cycle of concealment, the seeker may yet receive light from the moons and stars of religion even when the spiritual sun is veiled from his sight. May God guide us to the straight path.

## Madd, Jazm, Tashdīd, and Tanwīn

### Madd (the extended vowel)

The madd is a sign of prolongation — a radiance carried by certain vowels — and it is of several kinds. Sometimes it falls within a single word, sometimes at the word's close, and in either case the sound is drawn out to the length of two letters or of four. The madd extended to the measure of two letters indicates the confirmation of the divine realm (lāhūt) upon the human realm (nāsūt). The madd extended to the measure of four letters signals that the veil itself is supported by the One Veiled (al-muḥtajab) — the great ʿAyn — or that the illumination of the directing intellects (al-ʿushshār al-mudabbira al-ḥākima) is irradiating the great ʿAyn.

| Madd of two letters | Madd of four letters (terminal) |
| --- | --- |
| The shining of lāhūt upon nāsūt | The radiance of the veil |

### Maqṣūra (the abbreviated alif written as yāʾ, the standing zabar)

This minor form of the letter signals the partial infallibility of the limits (al-ḥudūd) — that is, of those ranks immediately beneath the Imams.

### Jazm and tashdīd

Jazm is the place where the tongue is brought to a halt; it is, as it were, the bent or arrested form of zabar. Tashdīd is the doubling of the median letter, pronounced twice and producing the intensity (shidda) of speech. Both appear together in the Qurʾānic command, "*O Messenger, convey what has been sent down to you*" (5:67), where the imperative *balligh* bears tashdīd and the prohibition *lā taḥzan* (and what follows) carries jazm. In that admonitory verse, God says to His Messenger Muḥammad: "Convey what is in your heart, lest your prophethood be diminished." The tashdīd signals the severity of the command; the jazm signals the prohibition — *if you do not do so*, what is dreaded will come to pass.

| Maqṣūra | Jazm | Tashdīd |
| --- | --- | --- |
| The limits below the Imams (partial infallibility) | Halt | The intensity of speech |

| Double zir (kasratān) | Indicates that at times the high prophets and certain Imams are overwhelmed by multiplicity, even though they possess every faculty. |

## The Letter Alif

Of all the letters of the alphabet, the alif holds the highest nobility, for it is unique in its very form — a single, undivided stroke that admits no compounding. In the spiritual world, the First Intellect (al-ʿAql al-Awwal) holds the rank of the alif, since its uniqueness is unmatched by any other emanation.[^cite-4] In the physical world, the human body holds the station of the eternal alif: by reason of its singularity it is not even counted among the order of the Imams. The Intellect is the first counter (al-ʿādd al-awwal) and is not itself enclosed within number; in the physical realm, likewise, the body is the eternal counter, and there is no number above it (so far as the Imams are concerned). In the present age, the Commander of the Faithful corresponds to the alif, being the one above all the Imams. In every age, the Imam of the time stands in the order of the alif, for he is superior to all the sons of his generation.

### The Greatness of the Believer

Of the twenty-eight letters of the alphabet, the alif possesses the most singular form. No other letter shares that form; only the final letter (yāʾ) carries, in its phonetic quality, the resonance of an alif.

Mawlānā ʿAlī (peace be upon him) says: "Do you reckon yourself a small body, when within you the greater world is folded?" That is to say: this day you appear as a sapling, but on the day of resurrection you will become a flourishing tree, and from there you will ascend to the spiritual world. In the span of a single great cycle — that is, three hundred and sixty turnings — the believer's preceptor (tāli) and senior (sābiq) will rise to the place of the heart, and the heart will come to the place of the Speaker-Prophet (nāṭiq). To him the Qurʾān addresses the verse, "And you are the highest, if you are believers" (3:139).

### A Single Sentence Out of the Fourteen Luminous Letters

If one assembles a meaningful sentence from the fourteen letters of light, only one such sentence emerges:

**Ṣirāṭ ʿAlī Ḥaqq Numsikuhu** — "The path of ʿAlī is true; we hold fast to it."

These words function as an oath: Allāh swears by the seven luminous intellects (ʿuqūl nūrāniyya) and by the seven corporeal intellects (ʿuqūl jismāniyya). The taʾwīl (esoteric exegesis) of the cipher rests on this twofold septenary structure — a doctrine that recurs across the Fatimid corpus, where the heavenly hierarchy is repeatedly mapped against the terrestrial daʿwa (the summons).[^cite-5]

### The Nuṭaqāʾ (the Seven Corporeal Letters)

The mumiddūn (sustainers) of the nuṭaqāʾ — the letters of nūrāniyya thaqīla — who underpin the nuṭaqāʾ (the letters of darkness) are exceedingly potent. For this reason the speech of a nāṭiq (Speaker-Prophet) carries an effect that ennobles the bearing of those who hear it. The aura of inerrancy that surrounds his utterance — that he never errs in what he says — derives from the corroboration of these higher intellects. We accordingly find not a single episode in the biography of the Messenger of Allāh (ṣ) in which he spoke and afterward retracted.

The support that descends from these intellects is the very sustenance of the nuṭaqāʾ. Just as we are perpetually in need of outward sustenance — without which the body weakens — so these speakers continually receive an inward sustenance by which their spiritual faculties are upheld. They therefore live their entire lives with the utmost care, since their provision is calibrated to the quality of their taqwā (God-conscious restraint). It is for this reason that one of the titles of Amīr al-Muʾminīn is *Imām al-Muttaqīn* (Imām of the God-wary).[^cite-6]

In the original sequence, the *qāf* (q) is the twenty-eighth and the *nūn* (n) is the twenty-ninth. But the *ʿam* lying between *qāf* and *nūn* came to be reckoned in the manner of a muqaṭṭaʿ and entered the count in order to complete the number thirty. This rearrangement made *ʿam* the twenty-ninth and *nūn* the thirtieth.

The form is in fact not *ʿam* but *ʿan-mā* — "about what are these people asking you?" That great tiding (al-nabaʾ al-ʿaẓīm) over which they disagree is, in truth, the Qāʾim — upon him be peace. All believe in him, yet differ among themselves. In partial measure, that great tiding is Amīr al-Muʾminīn ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib.

The twenty-ninth, then, is a quasi-muqaṭṭaʿ — formally a muqaṭṭaʿ yet not one in essence. The twenty-ninth always breaks the sequence that preceded it; the thirtieth founds a new sequence. ### The Significance of Thirty

The number thirty carries a particular weight: it betokens perfection and completion. Wherever completion arises, it does so from a thirtieth verse or a thirtieth person. There are thirty days in the month of Allāh. So too, the thirtieth among the Imāms or among the duʿāt holds an especial station. The thirtieth Imām is Mawlānā Hāshim ibn Nazār (ʿa) ibn Maʿad ibn Ḥusayn; with his arrival a particular grouping of Imāms reached its term. The thirtieth functions as a kind of qāʾim, while the twenty-ninth stands at the station of the Mahdī and is often a mustawdaʿ (an entrusted custodian, not a successor).

Sayyidnā ʿAlī ibn Sulaymān is the thirtieth dāʿī in the line of the daʿwa. Whoever occupies the thirtieth rank inherits great powers, just as Sayyidnā ʿAlī received an abundance of material and supernatural support from his Imām. The cadence of the thirtieth is distinct — as is the cadence of the thirtieth verse of the twenty-seventh sūra, which is distinct precisely because it fulfils the meaning of *Bismi-llāhi al-Raḥmāni al-Raḥīm*.

### The Significance of Thirty-One: The Inception of a New Era

After thirty, a fresh era and series commences — just as we have only thirty days in a month, and the thirty-first becomes the first of the next. The thirty-first is therefore singular: it inaugurates a new dispensation. Imām Ismāʿīl ibn Ibrāhīm Khalīl Allāh (ʿa) is the twenty-ninth and ʿAlī the thirtieth. After him, a new era of the Imāmate opens with Imām al-Ḥasan (ʿa).

In parallel, the duʿāt are organized into a discrete grouping that terminates with the thirtieth dāʿī (Sayyidnā ʿAlī ibn Sayyidnā Sulaymān); thereafter the very mode of the daʿwa shifts entirely — from the thirty-first dāʿī, Sayyidnā Shaykh Ibrāhīm ibn Muḥammad al-Fahd, the cadence of the summons is altered.

## The Twenty Muqaṭṭaʿāt of the Second Half

The twenty sūras of the second half [marked by muqaṭṭaʿāt] refer to the period of the Prophet (ṣ). These twenty sūras correspond to the twenty Imāms running from Imām al-Ḥasan (ʿa) to Imām Mawlānā al-Manṣūr. With the twenty-first Imām, Mawlānā Imām al-Ṭayyib, the Ṭayyibī era began — the period of the satr al-kullī, the comprehensive concealment.

These forty-nine letters point to the forty-nine Imāms of the prophetic era, distributed across seven weeks. In another register, they refer to the forty-nine ranks that came forth in the period of kashf (disclosure), to be completed by a comprehensive fiftieth — the established cycle.

In the Qurʾān there are twenty-nine muqaṭṭaʿ openings, and this count itself indicates the Prophet (ṣ), since he is the twenty-ninth Imām in the order. The entire luminous content of the Qurʾān was revealed upon the heart of the Prophet — the choicest of which are the verses composed of the letters of light, and the twenty-ninth corresponds to the Messenger of Allāh himself who is twenty-ninth in sequence. ### Meaningless Letters and Meaningful Letters

There are two species of words: those bearing manifest meaning, and those without. In the dawr al-kashf (era of disclosure), every thousand years a Qāʾim arises who gathers the assembly of that millennium. The era of kashf begins with the son of Mawlānā Qāʾim, who is the first Imām. Mawlānā Qāʾim is not himself counted within this series, just as ʿAlī is not reckoned the first Imām: al-Ḥasan is. They do not belong to the period of revelation but to the period of disclosure alone. The forty-nine qāʾims who appear in the period of kashf correspond to the muqaṭṭaʿāt letters that bear no manifest meaning. Those who succeed them — the fiftieth (*mīm*) and the fifty-first (*ʿayn*) — correspond to the letters bearing meaning, just as is the case with the twenty-ninth opening, *ʿam*.

No age has ever been bereft of Muḥammad and ʿAlī. They recur throughout time. In the cipher *ʿam*, if one inverts the order, the form becomes *maʿ* — "together." Muḥammad and ʿAlī are always together.

This means that when the forty-nine Imāms are joined to *mīm* and *ʿayn*, they acquire meaning — just as believers acquire meaning when they merge into the temple (*haykal*) of the son of the Imām, which is termed the *nafs mutamimma*, the self that has been made complete. So long as the surrāt (the lower ranks) remain in proximity to the ḥadd ʿālī (the upper limit) without integration, they are meaningless letters; but when they are absorbed into the *haykal sharīf*, those same meaningless letters become meaningful. *Nāsūt* (the human realm) and *lāhūt* (the divine realm) are opposites: although the muqaṭṭaʿāt are the choicest of the Qurʾān's letters and constitute its very essence, they remain meaningless until one who receives and integrates them aligns them rightly. Meaning cannot arise in them until such an integrator confers it.[^cite-10]

The Muʿawwidhatān close the Qurʾān, just as the Qāʾim and his ḥujjas close the cycle.

## The Enumeration of the Disjoined Letters (al-muqaṭṭaʿāt) in the Qurʾān

### Alif-Lām-Mīm — five occurrences (the five nuṭaqāʾ)

*Taʾwīl (esoteric exegesis):*

These letters appear in five settings throughout the Qurʾān, and they signify the five Speaker-Prophets (nuṭaqāʾ — the Speaker-Prophets who bring a new revealed dispensation) together with the five Pure Ones (Panjtan-i Pāk: Muḥammad, ʿAlī, Fāṭima, al-Ḥasan, al-Ḥusayn). Muḥammad (peace be upon him) corresponds to Ādam, Fāṭima to Nūḥ, ʿAlī to Ibrāhīm, al-Ḥasan to Mūsā, and al-Ḥusayn to ʿĪsā. That is, the lights (anwār) of these five nuṭaqāʾ stand adjacent to the lights of the Panjtan.[^cite-11]

*Ḥaqāʾiq (the inner facts):*

The trace of Ādam recurs in the Prophet; the trace of Nūḥ in Fāṭima; the trace of Ibrāhīm in ʿAlī; and so onward through the series. The trace of Qābīl recurs in Abū Bakr — that is, the stubbornness of Qābīl is reproduced in Abū Bakr. The rank (martaba) of the Panjtan is higher than the rank of every Speaker-Prophet (nāṭiq).

### Alif-Lām-Mīm-family — eight occurrences (the eight fixed forms of the dawr al-kashf)

*Taʾwīl:*

When the same letter-group is reckoned in its wider distribution, it amounts to eight, and these eight point to the eight enduring forms (al-ṣuwar al-thābita) of the period of unveiling (dawr al-kashf).

### Kāf-Hā-Yā-ʿAyn-Ṣād — one occurrence (Muḥammad)

*Taʾwīl:*

This verse has come a single time, and it points to the Messenger of Allāh (peace be upon him), for he gathered five orders within himself, just as five letters are gathered in this single muqaṭṭaʿa. He also gathered in himself the five lights of Khamsat al-Athar (the Five of Trace) — a collection of five hierarchic ranks.

### Ḥā-Mīm — seven occurrences (the Imāms of the first asbūʿ)

*The septenary cycle (asbūʿ) of the Prophet:*

These seven occurrences of Ḥā-Mīm refer to the Imāms of the first septenary cycle (asbūʿ — a seven-Imām dispensation) following the era of the Prophet.

*The first two Ḥā-Mīms (Imām al-Ḥasan and Imām al-Ḥusayn):*

The first two sūras opening with Ḥā-Mīm fall within the same juzʾ, and they point to Imām al-Ḥasan and Imām al-Ḥusayn, who, according to the saying of the Messenger of Allāh, stand together in a single rank: "al-Ḥasan wa al-Ḥusayn imāmāni ḥaqqan — al-Ḥasan and al-Ḥusayn are both Imāms in truth, whether they rise to claim it or remain at peace." Innumerable traditions are reported from the Prophet in praise of his two grandsons, yet in none of them did he ever set al-Ḥusayn ahead of al-Ḥasan; he always named al-Ḥasan first, and then al-Ḥusayn. Every imamate that follows is either Ḥasanī or Ḥusaynī. Within the temple of Imām al-Ḥusayn, Imām al-Ḥasan occupies the place of the heart — that is, the heart of the temple itself is made of al-Ḥasan.

*Ḥā-Mīm-ʿAyn-Sīn-Qāf (Imām ʿAlī Zayn al-ʿĀbidīn):*

The composite Ḥā-Mīm-ʿAyn-Sīn-Qāf (Q 42:1–2) points to Imām ʿAlī Zayn al-ʿĀbidīn, whose station is set apart from that of all the other Imāms. The merits gathered in him had not been gathered in any before him, nor will they be gathered in any after him. For this reason, the lamp that Ḥudhayfa al-Yamān beheld upon the head of ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib at the Battle of Ṣiffīn was beheld again only upon the head of Imām ʿAlī Zayn al-ʿĀbidīn. The four ranks (Prophethood, Messengership, Imamate, and Waṣīyya) had earlier been distributed across distinct persons; but when they passed to ʿAlī Zayn al-ʿĀbidīn, all four converged in him, and thereafter were never again divided until the Day of Resurrection. For this reason he is called "Ādam al-Ābāʾ" (the Ādam of the Fathers).

*Ḥaqāʾiq:*

On the Day of Resurrection, Imām ʿAlī Zayn al-ʿĀbidīn will pass through the ranks of the Prophets, the Imāms, the truthful, the righteous, and the martyrs, and shall receive the reward of his Lord.

*The last four Ḥā-Mīm chapters:*

The last four occurrences of Ḥā-Mīm refer to Imām Muḥammad al-Bāqir, Imām Jaʿfar al-Ṣādiq, Imām Ismāʿīl, and Imām Muḥammad ibn Ismāʿīl (peace be upon them all).

*Parallels in the era of completion (the Companions of the Septenaries):*

The first two Ḥā-Mīms of this second reading (Q 40 and Q 41), falling within a single juzʾ, point to the first two Companions of the Septenary in the era of disclosure — Muḥammad ibn Ismāʿīl and Maʿadd al-Muʿizz. (It was during the reign of Mawlānā Maʿadd al-Muʿizz that *Daʿāʾim al-Islām* was composed by al-Qāḍī al-Nuʿmān.)[^cite-12]

Ḥā-Mīm-ʿAyn-Sīn-Qāf in this register refers to the Master of the Martyrs (Sayyid al-Shuhadāʾ). All the Imāms of his septenary were martyred, and the master of that septenary is Imām ʿAlī al-Ṭayyib. He gathered into himself not only his own septenary but also the septenary that preceded him; and he too partook of the gathering of the Anwār Khamsa, to which the five letters of "al-Ṭayyib" themselves point. This is the fourth occasion on which the Anwār Khamsa converged. He received the temple of the Imamate from his father when he was only three days old. ## The Assembly of the Five Lights (Anwār Khamsa)

On the day in Rajab when the Holy Prophet received a special blessing, all the prophets — one hundred and twenty-four thousand of them — gathered in his sacred temple, and he became the convergent point of the five major prophets. In the technical vocabulary of this discourse, this gathering is called the Anwār Khamsa (the Five Lights).[^cite-13]

These five lights were first gathered in Muḥammad al-Muṣṭafā (peace be upon him). After his passing, they came in their entirety to ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib. Thereafter, the Anwār Khamsa were divided between Imām al-Ḥasan and Imām al-Ḥusayn. They then converged once more and were gathered in Imām ʿAlī Zayn al-ʿĀbidīn. After him, Imām ʿAlī al-Ṭayyib brought this assembly together within himself. After him, this assembly will be reconstituted only at al-Qāʾim ʿalā dhikrihi al-salām. This is the very lamp that Ḥudhayfa al-Yamān saw shining upon the head of Mawlānā ʿAlī at Ṣiffīn. The composite muqaṭṭaʿa Ḥā-Mīm-ʿAyn-Sīn-Qāf points to these five lights.

## The Wisdom of Nineteen in the Muqaṭṭaʿāt (the Disjointed Letters)

A delegation of Jews once approached the Prophet to inquire how many angels are appointed over the fire of Hell. He answered first by raising the fingers of both his hands, and then a second time by folding the thumb of his right hand and displaying the remaining digits — nineteen in all. The sum of these figures is striking: 14 (letters) + 14 (modes of combination) + 29 (sūras) yields 57, which is precisely 19 × 3. And from these fourteen letters, taken together, only a single meaningful Arabic sentence may be composed: *Ṣirāṭ ʿAlī ḥaqq numsikuhu* — "ʿAlī's path is the truth; we hold fast to it." Here the arithmetic of the muqaṭṭaʿāt discloses, by its own inner logic, a confession of walāya — the doctrine of allegiance to ʿAlī and his progeny that the Fatimid daʿwa (the summons) holds to be the crowning pillar of religion.[^cite-16]

The first sūra in which the muqaṭṭaʿāt appear is Sūrat al-Baqara (2), and the last is Sūrat al-Qalam (68). Between these two stand thirty-eight sūras that contain no muqaṭṭaʿāt — and thirty-eight is itself twice nineteen. The letter-clusters themselves range in length from one letter to five.

All the verses of Sūrat al-ʿAlaq together number nineteen; within those nineteen verses are seventy-six letters, themselves divisible by nineteen (19 × 4 = 76).

Sūrat al-ʿAlaq is the ninety-sixth sūra of the Qurʾān; counted from the end of the muṣḥaf, it stands nineteenth in order (114, 113, 112 … 96).

Beyond these correspondences, the Qurʾān as a whole contains 6,346 verses — equal to 19 × 334. And the word *qurʾān* itself recurs throughout the muṣḥaf precisely fifty-seven times — which is 19 × 3.
