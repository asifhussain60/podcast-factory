## The Wisdom of Diacritical Marks

⟪ar:سیدنا علی بن حسین (قس )⟫ On the occasion of the *Urs* (commemorative gathering) of ⟪ar:الحد الفاضل سید عبد اللّٰہ بن ھبۃ اﷲ⟫ in Hyderabad, the venerable master explained the profound importance of Arabic diacritical marks (*iʿrāb*, the system of grammatical inflection) alongside the spiritual conditions of pilgrimage during that assembly. ⟪ar:اعراب⟫ These marks—which regulate the grammatical case of words—are compared to the psalms of the soul. When combined with emphatic doubling (⟪ar:تشدید، جزم⟫) or suppression of vowels, the very meaning and semantic weight of words transforms entirely. In other words, the discourse of that day opened a flood of contemplative insight for the humble listener. The master proved a worthy successor to the great scholarly tradition of ⟪ar:دعوت ھادیۃ سلیمانیۃ⟫ (the Sulaymani summons), following in the footsteps of luminaries such as ⟪ar:مولوی محمد عباس صاحب⟫. The profound doctrinal concepts—those which ⟪ar:سیدنا علی (قس)⟫ and the saint embodied in their spiritual effulgences—and the wisdom that ⟪ar:سیدنا حسین بن اسماعیل (عطا اﷲ بقاہ)⟫ and ⟪ar:مولانا ضیاء علی بن اسحاق ضیائی صاحب⟫ imparted through their preachings inspired the author to set down these scattered reflections and allusions. Should any good be found herein, it is entirely the grace transmitted through those benefactors; should there be error or excess, may the Almighty grant forgiveness and mercy through His Messenger, upon whom be peace and blessings.

⟪ar:مولوی عبد اللہ صاحب⟫ declared: "⟪ar:أعراب⟫ Diacritical inflection is the very soul of the Arabic tongue. It is through these marks that meaning and semantic significance transform." ⟪ar:أعراب⟫ These marks are termed "movements" (*ḥarakāt*, vowel signs), and they are threefold:

> **⟪ar:اعراب⟫ The Upper Position (Rafiʿ — nominative case), the Middle Position (Nasb — accusative case), and the Lower Position (Jarr — genitive case)**

Concerning the sentence structure, the master stated: "The tongue comes to rest when it reaches the end, and the sentence closes in rounded completion."

The brotherhood of the faithful thus reflects: Arabic is the language of the Qur'ān, and it stands as the most beloved and luminous speech in the sight of the Divine. Hence it is called ⟪ar:عربی مبین⟫ (the clear Arabic tongue). The outward eloquence of the Arabic language, the profound wisdom embedded in its diacritical system, and its majestic grandeur all reveal themselves through their inward dimensions—the ⟪ar:رموز وأسرار وغوامض⟫ (symbols, secrets, and subtle mysteries). The grammatical rules of this language are broader, more intricate, and more stable than those of other tongues, and within it dwells a distinctive character. Letters are either dotted or undotted; some are rounded in shape (⟪ar:ن ق ض⟫) while others are not (⟪ar:ا م⟫). There exists a fundamental division ⟪ar:شمسی حروف اور قمری حروف⟫ (between solar and lunar letters). The Almighty has placed delicate refinement in the outward form of these letters as well as in their concealed meanings.[^cite-1]

## Solar and Lunar Letters

### Solar Letters

The letters that are preceded by the definite article (⟪ar:معرفۃ⟫) through (⟪ar:ال⟫) are termed ⟪ar:شمسی حروف⟫ (solar letters) when the *lām* is assimilated and pronounced with the sound of the following consonant. For example, in ⟪ar:الشَّمسُ⟫ (the sun), the *lām* merges directly with the *shīn*, disappearing into it, and one perceives no separate articulation of the definite article.

### Lunar Letters

The letters that are preceded by the definite article (⟪ar:معرفۃ⟫) through (⟪ar:ال⟫) are called ⟪ar:قمری حروف⟫ (lunar letters) when the *lām* is pronounced distinctly and clearly. For example, in ⟪ar:القمر⟫ (the moon), both the *alif*-*lām* and the *qāf* are articulated as separate entities.

| Solar Letters | Lunar Letters |
| --- | --- |
| The *lām* is concealed when assimilated to the following letter. | The *lām* is heard distinctly. |
| The Sun | The Moon |

## Luminous and Dark Letters

Moreover, as all are aware, the twenty-eight letters of the Arabic alphabet are themselves esoteric letters whose mysteries and proverbs point to specific spiritual entities and stations. In this regard, the twenty-eight letters are subdivided into four categories. All these letters signify both the physical and the spiritual realms; yet even upon the surface, their significance remains indelibly marked throughout the pages of the Qur'ān.[^cite-2] The opening chapter, *Sūrat al-Ḥamd* (the Chapter of Praise), contains twenty-one letters of the first three categories and does not include the seven letters classified as oppressive (*saqlīyyah*).

The luminous letters (⟪ar:نورانی لطیفۃ اور نورانی خفیفۃ⟫) consist only of letters of the alphabet, and they do not employ the oppressive consonants. Furthermore, there exist three letters (⟪ar:نون، میم، واو⟫) whose phonetic identity remains unchanged even when inverted or reversed.

## The Four Letters of the Qur'ān

⟪ar:قرآن مجید⟫ Although it is a voluminous scripture and Arabic is not the mother tongue of many, it remains a book preserved in the hearts of thousands upon thousands. Not even two pages of an ordinary work can be easily committed to memory through oral transmission, yet the miracle of the Qur'ān lies precisely in this: its memorizers (*ḥuffāẓ*) exist in thousands throughout the world. The manner of its recitation, the modes of its chanting, the art of its delivery—all have become perfected sciences. A second remarkable characteristic of the Qur'ān is that in many of its passages, should ⟪ar:أعراب⟫ the diacritical marks be read incorrectly, the meaning itself becomes a statement of disbelief. May the Almighty preserve us from such an error.

Examine the word ⟪ar:قرآن⟫ itself: it contains two dotted letters—*qāf* (⟪ar:ق⟫)—which are also round in form, and two letterless letters—*rāʾ* (⟪ar:ر⟫)—which lack that rounded shape. In numerical enumeration (*ḥisāb al-jumal*, the science of letter values), the *nūn* bears the value of fifty and the *qāf* the value of one hundred. Many meanings are contained within this arrangement, which ⟪ar:مولانا حضرت اکرام علی صاحب مقدس⟫ has expounded in his own writings. Furthermore, the meaning of the word Qur'ān itself, analyzed through the lens of its constituent letters, ⟪ar:حضرت مولانا اسحاق صاحب⟫ has explained in his work ⟪ar:فیض المفید⟫ (the Outpouring of Beneficence). The merits and spiritual virtues of these letters have been described by other ⟪ar:دعات عظام اور مناصیب کرام⟫ (eminent Summoners and honored authorities) in their respective treatises, and their works ⟪ar:دعوت ھادیۃ⟫ (the summons to guidance) are repositories of hidden treasures.

### The Qur'ān: A Treatise on the Wisdom of Arabic

The Qur'ān is composed of four letters. Two of them are adjacent and connected (⟪ar:قر⟫), and two are separate and distinct (⟪ar:آن⟫). The word ⟪ar:قرآن⟫ is derived from the root ⟪ar:قرین⟫, which signifies "companion" or "one who is paired." It is necessary that the Qur'ān should pass through four distinct modes and reach humanity in this manner: two of its letters should resemble one another in their pairing (⟪ar:قر⟫), and two should remain broad and separate, as wide as are (⟪ar:آن⟫). These two compound letters embody the ⟪ar:ناطق⟫ (the Speaker, the prophetic figure) and the ⟪ar:اساس⟫ (the Foundation, the Imam-designate), and they remain eternally inseparable, always drawing near to one another. Similarly, there exist two simple letters (⟪ar:آن⟫) that exemplify ⟪ar:عقل و نفس⟫ (intellect and soul), and they derive their sustenance from the ⟪ar:ناطق اور اساس⟫ (Speaker and Foundation). Just as the two compound letters of (⟪ar:قر⟫) attain their perfection together with the two exalted letters of (⟪ar:آن⟫), so too they achieve their completion through this same reciprocal support—for ⟪ar:ناطق اور اساس، عقل اور نفس⟫ (the Speaker and Foundation, intellect and soul) are mutually dependent and complementary.[^cite-4]

### On *Alif* (A):

The argument for *alif* is that it stands separate from all other letters in such a manner that when the writer reaches it, the continuity of writing is broken. In conventional writing, all other letters connect to *alif* from below, yet *alif* does not join to any other letter from above. In the same manner, all beings are connected to their superior principle—the ⟪ar:عقل کل⟫ (the Universal Intellect)—yet ⟪ar:عقل اول⟫ (the First Intellect) remains unconnected to any other created being from above. According to ⟪ar:حساب جمل⟫ (the enumeration of letter-values), the numerical value of *alif* is one, signifying that ⟪ar:عقل اول⟫ it is the cause of all existence and the originating principle of all things, just as the number one in counting is the generative cause of all other numbers.

### On *Nūn* (N):

The argument for *nūn* is that it is a line wherein one end seeks to connect with the other yet fails to do so. It exists in a condition such that when ⟪ar:نفس کل⟫ (the Universal Soul) perceived it, it was repelled and moved away, yet could not attain ⟪ar:عقل کل⟫ to its station and shall never reach it. In the same manner, when pilgrimage is performed, both the beginning and the end of the pilgrimage encounter ⟪ar:نون⟫; this symbolizes that every end shall eternally mirror its beginning.

### On *Qāf* and *Rāʾ* (QR):

The first of these four letters is (⟪ar:ق⟫), which serves as the argument for the ⟪ar:اساس⟫ (Foundation), for through this letter the believer discovers the path to knowledge of him; and (⟪ar:ر⟫), which signifies the ⟪ar:ناطق⟫ (Speaker). According to ⟪ar:علم العدد⟫ (the science of numerology), ⟪ar:ق⟫ (qāf) bears the value of one hundred and (⟪ar:ر⟫) *rāʾ* bears the value of two hundred. The ⟪ar:ناطق⟫ (Speaker) possesses both ranks, while the ⟪ar:اساس⟫ (Foundation) possesses an analogous station. Religious scholars among the faithful bear the rank of femininity (*unūthah*), and the Foundation has the rank of femininity ⟪ar:ناطق، تنزیل و تاویل⟫ (the Speaker: revelation and esoteric interpretation), ⟪ar:اساس، تاویل⟫ (the Foundation: esoteric interpretation).

### Science and Practice:

⟪ar:قرآن⟫ The pairing (⟪ar:قر⟫) exemplifies ⟪ar:عمل⟫ (action/practice), which forms part of the ⟪ar:جسم مرکب⟫ (composite body), while (⟪ar:آن⟫) exemplifies ⟪ar:علم⟫ (knowledge), which forms part of the ⟪ar:بسیط نفس⟫ (simple soul). The four letters of the Qur'ān differ one from another, just as the actions of all the prophets differ—for actions are obligatory upon the body, and all human bodies are distinct from one another. Yet the meaning of God's books and the interpretation of the prophets' laws remain identical; for they are a parable of the human spirit, and the soul does not alter.

## The Letters of Weakness: Alif, Wāw, and Yāʾ

The brotherhood of the faithful explains: ⟪ar:أعراب⟫ (diacritical marks) are termed ⟪ar:حرکت⟫ (vowel movements), and these are of three kinds, expressed through the ⟪ar:حروف علت⟫ (weak or vowel letters):

| Wāw (before) | Alif (Zabar) | Yāʾ (below) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| The prelude carries the sound of *ḍammah* (u) | Zabar bears the sound of *fatḥah* (a) | Below carries the sound of *kasrah* (i) |

As is evident from the forms of these three letters and the set of their vowel sounds, the prelude (*ḍammah*) appears only above the letter. In the same manner, the zabar (nominative case) will always appear above the letter, while the *kasrah* (diacritic below) constantly appears below.

## The Rules of the Arabic Language

While the Arabic language possesses many distinguishing characteristics, one stands paramount: whereas any other language in the world contains only two divisions in its grammatical rules, the Arabic language—that in which the Qur'ān has been revealed—possesses three divisions: ⟪ar:واحد⟫ (singular), ⟪ar:تثنیۃ⟫ (dual), and ⟪ar:جمع⟫ (plural). This indicates that in the ⟪ar:عالم روحانی⟫ (spiritual world), the one who first brought forth the ⟪ar:عقل اول⟫ (First Intellect) through the ⟪ar:توحید رب العالمین⟫ (attestation of the Lord of the Worlds) established the singular; those who witnessed and emulated it after perceiving the ⟪ar:عقل اول⟫ were two beings designated as ⟪ar:منبعثین⟫ (the Two Emanators). Thereafter, the seven intellects proceeded forth, and seven celestial spheres were constituted. It is precisely because the pairing of the two ⟪ar:منبعث⟫ (Emanators) and their emergence thereafter became the generative reason that, uniquely among the languages of the world, the Arabic language alone established the grammatical ⟪ar:تثنیۃ⟫ (dual) between the ⟪ar:واحد⟫ (singular) and ⟪ar:جمع⟫ (plural).[^cite-5]

According to the rules, a nominal sentence ⟪ar:جملۃ اسمیۃ مبتدا⟫ is constituted by the combination of the subject (mubtadaʾ) and the ⟪ar:خبر⟫ (predicate), and both must be in the state of the nominative case or in the state of the ⟪ar:تنوین⟫ (indefiniteness). That is to say, these are beings who have not yet undertaken corporeal descent, and they constitute those who possess a place in the ⟪ar:أسمآء حسنی⟫ (Divine Names). The second type is the ⟪ar:جملہ فعلیۃ⟫ (verbal sentence), which contains three elements: a verb, a subject, and an object. For example:

⟪ar-quote:ضَرَبَ مَحمُودٌ حَامِداً مِن الخَشَبِ⟫ *(Mahmūd struck Ḥāmid with a stick)*

In this sentence, the verb stands in the nominative state (*marfūʿ*), the object is in the accusative state (*mansūb*), and the instrument-phrase ends in the genitive state (*majrūr*)—all of which point to the material world, wherein human beings exist in the form of those raised up (*marfūʿ*, which are ⟪ar:نادمین⟫—the remorseful), those affected (*mansūb*, which are ⟪ar:شاک و متحیرین⟫—the doubtful and perplexed), and those estranged (*majrūr*, which are ⟪ar:مصر و مستکبرین⟫—those who are wilful and arrogant).

## The Rules of Arabic and the Spiritual World

The Arabic language possesses twenty-eight letters of the *muʿjam* (dumb or unsorted letters). They are named "dumb" because they are devoid of independent meaning and remain mute in their isolated state unless their speaker appears. Only when two or more letters combine in a specific order does meaning emerge. In the exegesis of the Qur'ān, the letters of the *muʿjam* represent those believers who have newly entered into the covenant and summons (*daʿwa*) of faith through the Prophetic Covenant. When they become obedient to their Lord, blessings manifest within them, and their form begins its spiritual refinement—a transformation akin to the combination of letters generating meaning. As soon as they are recognized by their sphere, they ascend beyond the letter-stage.

Amīr al-Muʾminīn (the Commander of the Faithful) instructed him in the rules of Arabic grammar, saying: "First, understand that there are twenty-eight letters, called the letters of *muʿjam*." The first creation was twenty-eight persons (twenty-seven souls and one guide). In the same manner, a circle of twenty-eight letters was established within every intellect. Even the circle of the Imām (*qawṣ al-Imām*) is said to number a thousand, yet when compressed further, it yields precisely twenty-eight. These twenty-eight letters were the essences that materially issued forth in this world in alphabetic form.

The twenty-eight letters of the Arabic alphabet are diacritics superimposed upon the *muʿjam*. The letters differ, and the diacritics differ. The twenty-eight letters that materially issued into this world were the eternal ones. These twenty-eight letters exist because there is a radiance of twenty-eight in the attestation *Lā ilāha illā-llāh* (There is no god but God). Twenty-eight letters were born in the world of light. The Arabs (in the world of intentions) were born in the world of souls.

### Expression

These twenty-eight letters were employed with diacritics in the world of intentions. In the spiritual realm, these were letters without diacritics. In the world of intentions, the letter that stood foremost in accepting the summons—that is, the letter that immediately exercised its authority without delay—came to carry that letter's mark. When the prelude (*ḍammah*) circle was drawn, then extended lengthwise, only the line (*zabar*) remained upon the letter that delayed. When it fell from the state of *zabar*, it entered into a state of *jarr* (subjection).

## Diacritical Marks: The Spiritual, Celestial, and Material Worlds

If we examine the material world, the celestial world, and the spiritual world in light of the origin and comprehension of worldly knowledge, we shall find that the spiritual world is that wherein ten spheres exist—from the First Intellect to the Intellect of the Moon—and all attain perfection, all having achieved their completion.

The world beneath the spiritual realm is the celestial world, called the world of heart or divine intention (*tamthīl*), which is the world of the *muḥtajab* (the veiled ones), and it is the world of the soul in this manner. After that, the material world begins, which is based upon the *nādimīn* (the remorseful) and the *mustaghfirīn* (those who seek forgiveness), to whom the Almighty has granted residence in the heavens. We may refer to this world—the world of the heavens and the celestial spheres. Below it lies the realm extending from below the surface of the moon (*subterre*) to the surface of this Earth, which is the world of the *majrūr* (those in genitive state). The heavens and the celestial spheres are efficacious and thus influence the world and those who dwell therein; we humans stand beneath these heavens.

## Diacritical Marks: The Veiled and the Veils

With the aid of the Almighty and through the support of the Companions of the Age and the dignitaries of the faith, I declare: if we regard the celestial world as an active field, then this material realm and its veils shall be a passive field in comparison. That is, if the celestial world has assumed the nominative state (*marfūʿ*), then it possesses the genitive state (*majrūr*) below it. In other words, in the spiritual domain, they exist in a state of being, that is, in a state of relief from veil. Whenever a movement arises at any point, its veil appears in this world. That is, the one in the spiritual world stands in the position of *pesh* (the upper mark). The Imāms who manifest here are in the state of *zabar* (the middle mark), and then the appearance of the inferior veil in the islands is in the state of *jarr* (the lower mark). Lengthening (*maddah*) refers to those who bear a luminescence, and these are of varied kinds. ## The Letter Alif

The nobility of the letter *alif* is supreme among all letters, and it is unique in its form. In the spiritual world, the First Intellect (*al-ʿAql al-Awwal*, the First Intellect) bears the station of *alif* because of its uniqueness. In the same manner, in the physical world, the *Jāthhā* (the Primordial Reality) holds the station of Eternal *Alif*, which by virtue of its uniqueness has not even been enumerated in the sequence of Imāms. The First Intellect is the "One Who Counts" and is without limit, while in the physical world the body is the eternal *Alif* and bears no number (Imāms). In this era, the Commander of the Faithful is the counterpart of *Alif*, standing superior to all the Imāms. In every age, the Imām of the time occupies the category of *Alif*, for he is sovereign over all the sons of his dispensation.

### The Greatness of the Believer

Of the twenty-eight letters of the alphabet, the most singular in form is *alif*. This form does not appear in any other letter. Only the final letter *yāʾ* (or *wāw*) bears a phonetic tone with the pronunciation of *alif*.

> *Yes, if you hold on to patience and piety, and the enemy comes rushing at you; your Lord will help you with five thousand angels having marks of distinction.*

The Messenger (upon him be peace) was aided in the Battle of Badr by divine forces corresponding to these nine thousand years of spiritual cycles. Similarly, when Imam al-Ḥusayn (upon him be peace) heard the celestial trumpet on the day of ʿAshūrāʾ, it resounded with the same cosmic sound corresponding to eight thousand years of sacred time.[^cite-2]

### The Tenth Surah: Surah al-Nahl

After these nine surahs of the Qur'an, the tenth surah—Surah al-Nahl—stands apart, though it belongs to the first half. Its opening verse declares:

> ⟪quran 16:1⟫
> ⟪ar:‏أَتَىٰٓ أَمْرُ ٱللَّهِ فَلَا تَسْتَعْجِلُوهُ ۚ سُبْحَنَهُۥ وَتَعَلَىٰ عَمَّا يُشْرِكُونَ ‎⟫
> *The command of Allāh has come, do not seek to hasten it. Glory be to Him, and exalted above what they associate with Him.*

This verse embodies the essence of the divine command (Amr Allāh) of the Commander of the Faithful (Amīr al-Muʾminīn). The discourse here cautions against premature expectation of their manifestation. The term "appearance" (ẓuhūr) alludes to the Qāʾim of the age (al-Qāʾim ʿAlī Dhikr al-Salām), who is inseparably linked to the Commander of the Faithful as one who constitutes his full station. It is therefore declared that the manifestation of the Commander of the Faithful in this era is partial; do not hasten for their complete appearance. In another sense, one is commanded not to rush toward the punishment of the multitudes—this is the work of divine establishment, and when they appear in their fullness, they shall execute it.[^cite-3] The verse continues:

The essence of the Qāʾim (the Divine Establishment who is about to appear) transcends all that people associate with him. That transcendence resides in the spiritual heights—the eschatological realm (al-ākhira). This verse pertains partially to ʿAlī and entirely to the Qāʾim.

### The Nine Diacriticals and Twenty-Nine Letters

The nine diacritical surahs of the first half encompass twenty-nine (29) distinct letters. This number signifies that Muḥammad is the twenty-ninth Imam in the succession of those established after Ismāʿīl ibn Ibrāhīm Khalīl Allāh. He completes the grouping of Imams. This is affirmed by the fact that the Kaʿba was revealed on the twenty-ninth day of Dhū al-Qaʿda. ʿAlī ranks as the thirtieth (30th) in this order and holds the station of the Qāʾim (al-Qāʾim al-Qiyāma). The twenty-ninth is always the Mahdī, and the thirtieth is the Mahdī in their fullness.

When the obligatory and supererogatory night prayers are combined, they total twenty-nine rakʿāt, corresponding to the prophetic presence and the blessed structure of sacred time.[^cite-4]

## The Twenty Diacriticals of the Second Half

The twenty diacritical surahs in the second half of the Qur'an correspond to the period of the Prophet (upon him be peace). These twenty surahs represent twenty Imams, extending from Imam al-Ḥasan (upon him be peace) to Imam Mawlānā al-Mansūr. With the twenty-first Imam, Mawlānā Imam al-Ṭayyib (upon him be peace), the era of the Ṭayyibī Ismāʿīliyya and the period of occultation (al-sattar al-kālī, the greatest concealment) commenced.

### The Twenty Diacriticals and Forty-Nine Letters

In the second half of the Qur'an, there are twenty diacritical surahs that together contain forty-nine (49) distinct letters. These forty-nine letters refer to the forty-nine Imams of the Prophetic age, ordered in seven cycles (al-asābiʿ, the weeks). In another interpretation, they allude to the forty-nine ranks that appeared during the period of revelation (kashf), which shall culminate in a comprehensive fiftieth—the perfected and established form.

The Qur'an contains twenty-nine (29) diacritical surahs in total, and this number indicates the Prophet (upon him be peace), for he is the twenty-ninth Imam in the succession of Imams. The whole lights (anwār) of the Qur'an were revealed upon the heart of the Prophet, the most excellent of which are those verses composed of radiant letters. The Prophet himself (upon him be peace)—the twenty-ninth in rank—corresponds to this numerical designation. For this reason, the revelation of the Kaʿba was ordained on the twenty-ninth of Dhū al-Qaʿda.

The form that completes this succession of twenty-nine is not itself a diacritical sign, but rather a word—the conjunction ʿAm (ع and م)—that resembles a diacritical without being one. Just as the fiftieth and fifty-first of the weeks of Imams stand similar to yet apart from the preceding forty-nine, so too does this thirtieth letter—which is twenty-ninth in order—break the pattern of earlier verses. The Qāʾim likewise transcends the pattern of Imams, arriving as the crowning light. These are the letters ʿAyn and Mīm, representing ʿAlī and Muḥammad respectively. The meanings of all other diacriticals remain veiled, yet the letter ʿAm bears manifest meaning.[^cite-5]

### Meaningless and Meaningful Letters

There exist two categories of diacritical letters: those that are meaningful, and those that are meaningless. In each age of revelation, there appears every thousand years a Qāʾim (the Divine Establishment) who gathers the community of that millennium. The era of revelation (kashf) shall commence with the son of Mawlānā al-Qāʾim, who shall be the first Imam of that age. Mawlānā al-Qāʾim himself does not fall within this succession—just as ʿAlī is not the first Imam, but rather al-Ḥasan is the first—for he exists only in the age of concealment (dawr al-satr), not in the age of revelation.

The forty-nine ranks that appear during the revelatory age stand in opposition to the meaningless diacritical letters. Those appearing after shall correspond to the fiftieth (Mīm) and fifty-first (ʿAyn), who embody the meaningful letters—as with the twenty-ninth letter ʿAm itself.

No era has ever been void of Muḥammad and ʿAlī. They are perpetually renewed! Within the letter ʿAm lies a profound secret: when inverted, it becomes mā, signifying "togetherness." Thus Muḥammad and ʿAlī shall forever remain inseparable.

When these forty-nine Imams merge with Mīm and ʿAyn, they attain meaning. As believers merge into the sanctified temple (haikāl) of the Imam's successor, they too become meaningful—a state termed "the self made meaningful" (al-nafs al-mutammima). So long as the diacritical letters remain near the celestial limit (al-ḥadd al-ʿālī), they remain meaningless; but when merged into the Holy Sanctuary, these letters of obscurity become radiant with meaning. Humanity (nāsūt) and divinity (lāhūt) are opposite poles. Though the diacriticals constitute the essence and treasure of the entire Qur'an, they remain unveiling until the one who accepts them, and the one who gathers and orders them aright, brings them forth.

### Surahs Twenty-One and Twenty-Two: The Protective Surahs

Surah al-Muʿawwidhatayn (Surahs al-Falaq and al-Nās)—the two protective surahs—signify the argument of the Qāʾim (al-Qāʾim ʿAlī Dhikr al-Salām) and their proofs (al-ḥujaj). Both surahs begin with the letter Qāf. Qāf refers to the Qāʾim, while Lām (whose numerical value is 30) refers to the thirty Imams who preceded him. The Protective Surahs appear at the Qur'an's conclusion, just as the Qāʾim and their arguments conclude the age.

| Letter | Refers to |

| Qāf | Qāʾim ʿAlī Dhikr al-Salām |
| Lām | Thirty Imams (from Imam al-Ṭayyib [21st] to the Qāʾim [51st]) |

Twenty-two surahs—those bearing diacritical marks together with the Protective Surahs—correspond to twenty-two Imams, including the Manifest Imam and the Concealed Guardian. These two beings, the Mahdī of the Final Hour and the Qāʾim, are termed the "Lord of Mankind" (Rabb al-Nās, the Gatherer of the Assembly) and the "Lord of Daybreak" (Rabb al-Falaq, the Lord of the Righteous).

The sky is termed "daybreak" (al-falaq)—that moment when the previous day concludes with the sun's departure and a new day begins. Likewise, the age of concealment shall end with the light of the Qāʾim (as the sun), and the age of revelation (kashf) shall commence as a new dawn. The proof of Mīm awwala (Fāṭima) appears afterward, yet the proof of the Qāʾim appears first. For this reason, the Blessed Prophet (upon him be peace) established twenty-two as the number of obligatory prayers daily.[^cite-6] This signified that after twenty Imams, the Imamate would enter concealment, and at its conclusion—after two further stages—the Imam would manifest anew.

The twenty-second surah of the Qur'an is Surah al-Ḥajj, which contains two prostrations (sajdāt), inspiring the believers to perform the inward pilgrimage (al-ḥajj al-bāṭin) alongside the outward pilgrimage (al-ḥajj al-ẓāhir).

## The Enumeration of Diacriticals in the Qur'an

### [Rāʾ] Five Manifestations and the Fifty Purified Forms

These verses containing the letter Rāʾ appear five times throughout the Qur'an, signifying five luminous emanations (al-anwār al-khamsā) and their five purified stations. Muḥammad (upon him be peace) corresponds to Adam in the pattern of sanctity; Fāṭima corresponds to Noah; ʿAlī corresponds to Abraham; al-Ḥasan corresponds to Moses; and al-Ḥusayn corresponds to Jesus. Thus the lights of these five prophets are adjacent to the lights of the Panjatān (the Five Pure Ones).[^cite-7]

**The Essential Reality (al-Ḥaqīqa):**

The trace of Adam has been repeated in the Prophet (upon him be peace); the trace of Noah in Fāṭima; the trace of Abraham in ʿAlī; and so forth. The trace of Qābīl has been repeated in Abū Bakr. The obstinacy of Adam was renewed in Abū Bakr. The station of the Panjatān transcends all intellectual reasoning.

### [Alif, Lām, Mīm] Eight Eternal Forms of the Revelatory Age

The letter Alif, Lām, Mīm (Alm) alludes to eight eternal forms (al-ṣuwar al-qāʾima) of the age of revelation (kashf).

#### [Khāʾ] The Prophet Muḥammad

This verse appears once, referring to the Messenger of Allāh (upon him be peace), for he gathered the five letters of the Qur'an—just as this verse contains five letters. Furthermore, he gathered the Panjatān al-ʿAthar (the Five Purified Ones of Trace), a collection representing five sacred stations.[^cite-8]

#### [Ṭāʾ, Hāʾ] The Qāʾim of the Resurrection

This indicates a station of establishment. The letter Ṭāʾ has a numerical value of 9, signifying a perfected span of nine thousand years—encompassing the three thousand years of the primordial age and the six thousand years of the historical cycle of ʿAlī (upon him be peace). Beyond this, the Qāʾim has unified the congregation of the Panjatān al-Ṭāhira (the Five Purified Ones).

#### [Ṭāʾ, Sīn] Three Established Forms

During the primordial age (al-fitr), three established forms appeared. At 48,000 years, a perfected form manifested that unified the community of the preceding thousand years. At 49,000 years, a perfected form gathered the thousand years before it. At 50,000 years, a perfected form completed the thousand-year cycle preceding it.

#### [Hāʾ, Mīm] The Week of the Mighty Ones

These seven diacriticals correspond to the Imams of the first week of the Prophetic age.

**The First Two Hāʾ-Mīms: Imams al-Ḥasan and al-Ḥusayn**

The first two surahs beginning with Hāʾ-Mīm stand in the same verse-group and refer to Imam al-Ḥasan and Imam al-Ḥusayn (upon them both be peace). According to the Messenger of Allāh (upon him be peace), they stood simultaneously: "Al-Ḥasan and al-Ḥusayn are the two Imams of truth." Though countless ḥadīths praise the Prophet's grandsons, none elevates al-Ḥusayn over al-Ḥasan. The Prophet always named al-Ḥasan first, then al-Ḥusayn. All successors are designated as Ḥasanī and Ḥusaynī. In the sanctuary of Imam al-Ḥusayn, Imam al-Ḥasan dwells in the station of the heart—the heart is constituted from his sanctuary.[^cite-9]

**Hāʾ-Mīm-ʿAyn-Sīn: Imam ʿAlī Zayn al-ʿĀbidīn**

This diacritical refers to Imam ʿAlī Zayn al-ʿĀbidīn (upon him be peace), whose station differs from that of all other Imams. The spiritual rewards (thawāb) accumulated in his person surpass what any have accumulated before or shall accumulate after. Therefore, the lamp that Khuzayfa al-Yamānī beheld shining upon the head of ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib at the Battle of Ṣiffīn appeared again only upon the head of Imam ʿAlī Zayn al-ʿĀbidīn. The four stations—prophethood (nubuwwa), guardianship (walāya), Imamate (imāma), and succession (waṣāya)—were distinct in former ages; but when ʿAlī came to Zayn al-ʿĀbidīn, all four converged and never separated again until the Day of Judgment. For this reason, he is called Adam of the Fathers (Ādam al-Abāʾ).

**The Reality:**

On the Day of Judgment, Imam ʿAlī Zayn al-ʿĀbidīn (upon him be peace) shall break through the ranks of the Imams, the righteous, the pious, and the martyrs, and receive the reward from his Lord.

**The Final Four Hāʾ-Mīms**

These four surahs refer to Imam Muḥammad al-Bāqir, Imam Jaʿfar al-Ṣādiq, Imam Ismāʿīl, and Imam Muḥammad ibn Ismāʿīl (upon them all be peace).

#### The Exemplars of the Entire Age: The Companions of the Week

The first two Hāʾ-Mīms (surahs 40 and 41), appearing in the same verse-group, correspond to the first two companions of al-Asbuʿ: Muḥammad ibn Ismāʿīl and Maʿadd al-Muʿizz. (The Daʿāʾim al-Islām was composed during the age of Mawlānā Maʿadd al-Muʿizz.)

Hāʾ-Mīm-ʿAyn-Sīn refers to the Lord of the Martyrs. All Imams of this week suffered martyrdom, and the lord of this week is Imam ʿAlī al-Ṭayyib (upon him be peace). He gathered not only his own week but also the prior week within his blessed person. He possessed the gathering of the Five Purified Lights, to which the five letters of al-Ṭayyib allude. This is the fourth place where the Lights of the Panjatān converged. He received the Imamate's sanctuary (haikāl) from his father when only three days old. His original title (apart from mystical knowledge, maʿrifa) stands as a profound designation:[^cite-10]

The number of al-Ṭayyib is twenty-one, for he is the twenty-first Imam and the gatherer of the twenty-one Imams preceding him.

The final four Hāʾ-Mīms allude to the remaining four weeks, corresponding to the twenty-eighth, thirty-fifth, forty-second, and forty-ninth stations.

#### Surah al-Ikhlāṣ and the Masters of the Weeks

**The Reality:**

There dwells a supreme greatness in the station of four Imams within these seven weeks, and this is referenced in Surah Qul Huwa Allāh al-Aḥad. Though the verses and surah numbers are incalculable, their gatherings are appointed. No such community was born in all the 7,000 years. There are four summaries of the 7,000 years, and the essence of the entire era is established—similar to the whole of Surah Qul Huwa Allāh al-Aḥad. Four exalted gatherings exist within this station: Muḥammad ibn Ismāʿīl, ʿAlī al-Ṭayyib, Aḥmad ibn Muḥammad, and Abū al-Qāʾim. The highest of these four is Abū al-Qāʾim (the Divine Establishment), who is supreme.

These letters—ʿAm—resemble diacriticals yet are not diacriticals. They signify that ʿAlī and Muḥammad are not Imams but stand as transcendent to Imamate itself (far greater than the Imamate). This verse indicates that when ages and cycles reach their end, a resplendent form shall appear bearing the name ʿAlī. As ʿAlī advances, he shall transform into the eternal form—the abiding station. Thus shall ʿAlī progress and gather the entire congregation at the epoch's conclusion, while Ādam becomes eternal, receiving the caliphate of the Hidden Master. Mīm shall be the caliph beneath ʿAlī. Mīm and ʿAyn shall never be separated!

#### [Yāʾ, Sīn] The Heart

This indicates one who shall manifest in the heart of every age—within the cosmic cycle and the sacred sanctuary. The Messenger of Allāh (upon him be peace) appeared in the heart of this age, and thus was called "Yāsīn." Surah Yāsīn appears in the twenty-third unit (juzʾ) of the Qur'an, and Laylat al-Qadr manifests in the heart of Ramadan—the twenty-third night. The month of Ramadan refers to the Prophet (upon him be peace), while the twenty-third night (Laylat al-Qadr) signifies the twenty-third Imam among his descendants:[^cite-13]

If the Qur'an is divided into seven parts, Surah Yāsīn stands at the beginning of the sixth part. When a human is born, the body's height equals seven hand-spans. The heart emerges at the beginning of the sixth span. Similarly, if the stations of the first thirty days (the first thirty Imams) are divided into seven parts (approximately four Imams per part), then Imam al-Ṭayyib occupies the beginning of the sixth part—placing him in the station of the heart, Yāsīn, or Laylat al-Qadr. Though Imam al-Ṭayyib is the twenty-first Imam, if the Prophet and ʿAlī are counted together (beginning from the Prophet rather than al-Ḥasan), then Imam al-Ṭayyib becomes the twenty-third and assumes the position of heart or Yāsīn. The twenty-ninth Imam (Mīm) and the thirtieth Imam (ʿAyn) are unified in rank and thus are arranged in this station.

## The Gathering of the Five Purified Lights

On the night of Rajab, when the Blessed Prophet (upon him be peace) received a singular grace, all the prophets—numbering 124,000—gathered within his sacred sanctuary and became united under the station of the five prophets. In the sacred language of the tradition, this gathering is termed the Lights of the Panjatān (Anwār Khamsā)!

## The Wisdom of Nineteen in the Diacriticals

A group of Jews questioned the Blessed Prophet (upon him be peace) regarding the number of angels appointed over Hell. He answered by raising the fingers of both hands; when asked a second time, he closed the thumb of his right hand and indicated the remaining fingers of both hands—nineteen angels in total. At this, they began to mock, saying, "There are thousands of us, yet you claim only nineteen!" One of their wrestlers declared, "On the seventeenth, I shall lift you upon my shoulders and you shall see two." At that moment, Allāh revealed the following verse:[^cite-16]

This verse establishes the authority of divine wisdom against human mockery.

The renowned calligrapher Maʾmūn Saqal has documented that the letters of the Arabic alphabet exist in nineteen (19) basic forms. By adding one, two, or three diacritical marks above or below these forms, they generate the complete twenty-eight (28) letters of the Arabic script.

In verse 28 of Surah al-Jinn (chapter 72), Allāh declares:

> *And I have been commanded to count all things in number.*

If one adds the surah number (72) and its verse number (28), the sum yields nineteen: 7+2+2+8 = 19. The verse that follows contains this declaration:

> *We shall show them Our signs on the horizons and within their own souls until they recognize that it is the truth from their Lord.*

The total letter-count of this verse likewise equals nineteen.[^cite-17]

### The Word "Alone" (al-Wāḥid)

Throughout the Qur'an, the term "singular" or "alone" (wāḥid / wāḥida) appears nineteen times—its number mirrors its frequency.

The most vital word in the Qur'an, which celebrates the absolute oneness of Allāh, is the proclamation "There is no god but Allāh"—appearing in nineteen surahs.

### The Most Common Letters

Alif, Lām, and Mīm are the most frequently occurring letters in Arabic. Their prominence throughout the Qur'an and the surahs named for them reflect their cosmic significance. The letters ʿAyn and Ghayn, Ḥāʾ and Khāʾ, ṭāʾ and ẓāʾ, and their correlates—all appear with specific numerical patterns echoing the sacred mathematics of nineteen.

### The First Surah Revealed: Surah al-ʿAlaq

The first surah revealed to the Messenger of Allāh (upon him be peace) was Surah al-ʿAlaq (Surah 96), which comprises nineteen verses. Each verse bears the name and number nineteen. Within these nineteen verses stand a total of seventy-six (76) letters: 76 ÷ 19 = 4, with a remainder permitting further subdivision.

Surah al-ʿAlaq stands as the ninety-sixth surah in the compiled Qur'an. When counted from the end of the scripture (from surah 114 backward), it becomes the nineteenth surah: (114, 113, 112... 96).

### The Total Verses and Occurrences

The Qur'an contains 6,346 verses in total, which equals 19 × 334. The word "Qur'an" (al-Qurʾān) appears fifty-seven times throughout the entire scripture—equivalent to 19 × 3. These numerical patterns confirm the inviolable design (al-taṣmīm al-ilāhī) woven throughout scripture, each reflecting the supreme wisdom and the inimitable nature of the divine word.
