## An Account of Ghusl (Ritual Bathing)

The Qur'ān states concerning ritual purity, and the Prophet (peace and blessings of Allāh be upon him) is reported to have said: "If a person does not intend the ghusl (major ritual bathing) of janāba while performing ghusl, then he should perform ghusl ten times, for such bathing will not suffice him."[^cite-1]

⟪ar:آئمہ طاہرین علیہم السلام⟫The Imāms of the Prophet's Household (upon them be peace) have expounded the proper method of ritual bathing. One should first perform ablution (wuḍūʾ) to prepare oneself, then remove all traces of major impurity. Water should be poured over the entire body, reaching every part that the hands can access. Every limb without exception must be thoroughly washed. The hands should be spread across the body, the hair should be thoroughly soaked, and the skin should be cleansed completely. As with ablution, there is no prescribed minimum quantity of water for ghusl—rather, the intention and thoroughness of purification constitute its essence.

The sharīʿa encompasses nineteen forms of ghusl, mirroring the nineteen angels appointed over Hell. Of these, seven are obligatory (*wājiba*) and twelve are recommended (*sunna*). The obligatory baths address the following conditions:

| 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 | 5 | 6 | 7 |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Janāba | Menstruation | Apostasy | Loss of consciousness | Marital relations outside covenant | Major impurity | Death |

The twelve recommended baths (*ghusl al-sunna*) include:

| 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 | 5 | 6 |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Friday | ʿĪd al-Fiṭr | ʿĪd al-Aḍḥā | Iḥrām (pilgrimage consecration) | Entry to Mecca | Entry to the Kaʿba |
| 7 | 8 | 9 | 10 | 11 | 12 |
| ʿArafāt | Entry to Medina | 19th of Rajab | 21st of Rajab | 23rd of Rajab | Ghusl of the deceased |

⟪ar:مولانا علی علیہ السلام⟫The Commander of the Faithful relates in his *Kitāb al-Arbaʿīn* (Book of Forty Etiquettes) that believers should never sleep in a state of spiritual heedlessness or without ritual purification. Should water be unavailable, one should perform *tayammum* (dry ablution) instead. This is essential because when a believer sleeps, his soul ascends to the heavens where the Almighty beholds him with mercy. If death approaches, Allāh transforms the soul into a more radiant form; if life continues, the protective angel returns the soul safely to the body.[^cite-2]

## Ṭahāra on the Day of Friday Congregational Prayer

Among the twelve recommended baths, the ghusl of Friday (*ghusl al-Jumu'a*) holds preeminence by reason of its spiritual nobility and becomes the foremost of all supererogatory purifications. The ghusl of the deceased stands as the final of the seven obligatory baths, while the Friday ghusl serves as the first of the recommended baths—a threshold that marks the intimate relationship between the two worlds and the union of obligation and merit. Friday's bath thus functions as a boundary between obligation and recommendation, just as in the metaphysical hierarchy (*ʿālam al-Almāniyya*) there exists a realm between the celestial spheres and the physical world, distinguished by wondrous miracles.[^cite-3]

The righteous servants of Allāh perform the Friday ghusl with particular intention. Over the course of a week, imperfections inevitably accumulate within the soul and body of every person. This bath serves to dissolve those deficiencies. The saints therefore pronounce the following intention:

⟪ar:اَللّٰھُمَّ اِنِّی نَوَیتُ ھَذَا وُضُوءِی وَ طَھُورِی لِغُسلِ یوم الجُمُعَۃ لأِزاَلَتِ جَمِیعِ النَّجاَساَتِ وَالأَدِراَنِ (کچرا، میل کچیل) فِی جِسمِی وَ فُضلاَ تِی وَ قَلبِی وَ ذَھنِی وَ فِطرِی وَ رِیحِیاَتِی وَ صُورَتِی وَ نَفسِی وَ جَمِیعِ الاَھوَالِ فِی الدُّنِیَا وَ الآخِرَۃِ وَالظَّاھِرَۃِ وَالباَطِنَۃِ لِصَلوٰاۃِ الظُّھرِ وَالعَصرِ وَلِکُلِّ صَلوٰاۃٍ اُصَلِّیھَا⟫

A subtle question arises: why does this intention mention the removal of all impurities—external and internal—when the one bathing has committed neither sexual transgression (*iḥtilām*) nor major impurity (*janāba*) nor is in a state of menstruation? The answer lies in the nature of spiritual deficiency itself. All such imperfections arise not from explicit transgression but from weakness of conviction, negligence in worship, heedlessness regarding knowledge, and defects in devotion to the Imāms. When a person has failed to cultivate the realm of knowledge and wisdom, has neglected the embellishment of moral character, and has not made contemplation the sanctuary of the heart, these very absences generate subtle defilements in the soul and body. Over time, these accumulate into obscurations that obstruct sincere worship. The water of the Friday bath purifies both the manifest and the hidden dimensions of the human being from such outer and inner contaminations.

**The Virtue of the Sixth Hour**

The Friday ghusl confers immense spiritual benefit and advantage. Friday itself corresponds to the sixth day, and the sixth (*al-sādis*) signifies the Natiq (the speaking prophet, Muḥammad al-Muṣṭafā, upon him be peace). On this day, the spiritual powers of the sixth hour are greatly amplified. When the bath is performed at this moment, it activates two distinct dimensions within the human constitution: one removes filth from the body, while the other awakens in the person an ardent desire for knowledge.

| **Benefit** | **Effect** |
| --- | --- |
| Purity from Impurity | Purification of the physical vessel |
| Quickening of the Desire for Knowledge | Illumination of the intellect |

**The Trimming of Nails**

The believer should trim the nails during this sacred time as well—both the external nails of the fingers and toes, and the "nails" of the personality and character. Nails constitute the outermost extremities of the body where dirt naturally accumulates beneath them; they signify external beliefs and tendencies that exceed their proper bounds. To cut them is to sever oneself from all such misaligned convictions.

Moreover, nails can inflict harm upon others. For this reason, along with the trimming of external nails, one must also trim the "nails" of one's inner self—those sharp impulses and harmful habits—so as never to cause spiritual pain to another soul. It is recommended that nails be trimmed on Thursday in preparation for Friday's rites.

## Ghusl al-Janāba and Iḥtilām (Major Ritual Bathing and Wet Dreams)

*Janāba* literally means "to be distant" or "to stand apart." In Arabic usage, remoteness is termed *al-janab*, and one who is estranged is *al-jānib*—one who has withdrawn from kinship and lineage. In the sharīʿa, janāba denotes a state in which the believer has become distant from the obedience of the Messenger of Allāh (may Allāh's peace be upon him) and the guidance of the Imāms, rendering his acts of worship void and worthless.

The conditions requiring obligatory ghusl al-janāba are specified as follows:

| **Condition** | **Ruling** |
| --- | --- |
| 1 | When seminal fluid emerges from the back (whether through sexual excitement or involuntarily), ghusl becomes obligatory. A person must bathe when exposed to an emission capable of generating a person like himself. The entire body, comprising seven major organs, must be washed thoroughly. Marital intercourse performed after proper marriage is permissible (*ḥalāl*); intercourse without the marriage contract is forbidden (*ḥarām*). |
| 2 | Ghusl becomes obligatory when the two circumcised parts meet—that is, when the male organ enters the female organ—even if ejaculation has not occurred. In this case, both man and woman must perform ghusl. |
| 3 | If a person undergoes an involuntary emission during sleep (*iḥtilām*, wet dream), ghusl is obligatory, yet there is no sin upon him because the act was not intentional. Should masturbation occur deliberately, it constitutes a grave sin. |

*Janāba* occurs when the sperm of a man unites with that of a woman within her; *infertility* occurs when sperm is emitted but does not enter the woman. The tradition states: "Whosoever does not perform the ghusl of janāba is forbidden to sleep, to eat, to drink, or even to breathe until he has purified himself through ghusl."[^cite-4] For this reason, one should hasten to perform the ablution and ghusl when janāba occurs—unless the married couple intend to have further relations, in which case they may delay the ghusl until after the final intercourse.

**Symbolic Interpretation of Ghusl al-Janāba**

An example drawn from the spiritual sciences: when a dāʿī (summoner) teaches a *mustajīb* (respondent), and knowledge flows from the dāʿī to the *mustajīb*, both must then ascribe that knowledge entirely to its true Source—the Imām al-Zamān (may Allāh hasten his return)—and distance themselves utterly from claiming ownership of that wisdom. Just as water is applied to every part of the body during ghusl, so too the light of true knowledge must reach every faculty of the soul.

**(1) The Emergence of Words:**

When something emerges from the mouth of a dāʿī that possesses the power to generate spiritual life in the *mustajīb*, it is obligatory upon the *mustajīb* to immediately disclaim any ownership of that knowledge and to attribute all wisdom to the Seven Imāms, who correspond to the seven organs of the body. The example of one who collects knowledge lawfully is of one who speaks religious truth only with the permission of Imām al-Zamān; the example of one who commits forbidden fornication is of one who speaks of religious matters without such permission.

In another interpretation, ghusl also signifies that all doubts and confusion must be purified through the knowledge of the Imām, for doubts arise only through the deficiency of proper understanding and incomplete knowledge.

The preacher of Yemen, Sayyidunā ʿAlī ibn Sayyidunā Sulaymān, responded to a question thus: "If a person in a state of janāba and a corpse are in one place, and water suffices only to perform ghusl for one of them, then the person in janāba should bathe with that water, and the corpse should be purified through *tayammum*."

It is forbidden to cut the hair or nails during menstruation or the state of janāba.

(Kitāb Asʿāf al-Ṭālib fī Jāmiʿ al-Muttalib)

**(2) Bath at the Meeting of Circumcision:**

The preacher of truth is exemplified by a man, and the woman who receives knowledge exemplifies the *mustajīb*. Both must perform ghusl—physically and spiritually. That is, both teacher and learner must attribute all knowledge to the Imāms. The interpretation of ejaculation without penetration is this: even if scholarly discourse has occurred, if the true inner meaning has not been conveyed to the listener or remains uncomprehended, both must perform spiritual ghusl—that is, return all knowledge to the Imām.

**(3) Iḥtilām (Wet Dream):**

One who experiences iḥtilām is like a person who unintentionally and through error speaks something concerning the esoteric interpretation (*taʾwīl*), and then startles, realizing that what I have uttered concerns matters of esoteric meaning. It remains obligatory for such a person to attribute this to the Imāms. Conversely, one who deliberately derives taʾwīl through conjecture is like one who willfully takes pleasure in interpreting the divine commandments from his own understanding, a practice he does not possess the authority to exercise. Forced transgression leads to both external and internal maladies.

When a believer enters a state requiring ghusl, his connection with the Nāṭiq (the speaking Imām) is severed because the original purity has been lost, whereupon the angels withdraw from him. If the believer does not immediately perform ghusl, the devils approach him and inflict him with trials, inciting him toward sin. Proper and swift bathing restores the bond with the guardian angel, and the believer returns to the shelter and protection of the angelic order.

## The Water for Wuḍūʾ and Ghusl

The water employed at the time of ablution possesses a most special quality, distinguished by three aspects of transformation:

| **Aspect** | **Characteristic** |
| --- | --- |
| 1 | Which flows through the channel |
| 2 | Which contacts the limbs and enters the body through the pores |
| 3 | Which transmutes through intention and utterance |

The realities (*ḥaqāʾiq*) of these three waters are fundamentally distinct. As soon as a believer commences ablution by reciting "Bismillāh al-Raḥmān al-Raḥīm" (In the Name of Allāh, the Compassionate, the Merciful), the essence of that water and the quality of the breath change profoundly. Components of life begin to enter the breath. The bodies of the Imāms are constituted of pure life-force (*ḥayāt*), and all that they consume throughout their lives partakes of this essence. The believer receives nourishment from the Imām in three specific moments:

| **Moment** | **Circumstance** |
| --- | --- |
| 1 | At the time of ablution |
| 2 | At the time of *Suhūr* (pre-dawn meal during fasting) |
| 3 | At the time of *Ifṭār* (breaking of the fast) |

Similarly, every drop of water contains three distinct components. One component is characterized by physical hygiene, active in all forms of bodily washing—of the body, garments, utensils, and surroundings. Another component serves to quench thirst and refresh the physical constitution. The third component is reserved for ablution and spiritual purification. The aspect of water that becomes efficacious depends entirely upon the intention (*niyya*) of the person performing the act.

When a person forms the intention to perform ablution or ghusl and recites "Bismillāh al-Raḥmān al-Raḥīm," the water activates according to that very intention.

## The Virtues of the Worlds of Islām and Īmān (Symbolism)

*Wuḍūʾ* (ablution) signifies the act of departing from the evils of the world of Islām (*ʿālam al-Islām*) and entering into the world of faith (*ʿālam al-Īmān*). After entry into the world of faith, the greater purification is called *ghusl*, by means of which the believer progresses toward the most exalted stations. In performing ablution, the believer's virtuous deeds from prior periods spent in the world of Islām become manifest in the form of water. When a person performs ghusl, the virtues accumulated through faithful obedience in the world of faith become manifest as waters of greater radiance.

| **Ablution** | **Ghusl** |
| --- | --- |
| Manifestation of the Virtues of the Islamic World | Manifestation of the Virtues of the World of Faith |

**The Virtues of the Islamic World:**

We are bound by the cycles of time and epoch. In ages past, we performed acts of goodness toward those who did not yet believe, acts which we have long forgotten—yet the cosmic order (*ṭibāʿ*) has preserved the record of every virtue. The one to whom we showed kindness fell into darkness because of his own evil deeds and corrupt thoughts; yet through the wilāya (guardianship) of Muḥammad and ʿAlī, we returned to human dignity and form. Nevertheless, a trace of that goodness remained inscribed in the cosmic order. Allāh now brings before us the fruits of that ancient charity in the form of water, and we are purified by it.[^cite-5] In this way, those virtues render service to us: they wash away our physical impurities and repay the goodness we once extended. Allāh holds account of every particle and atom, and therefore it has been said that "Allāh is Swift in His Reckoning."

The water of ablution changes in quality according to the water of the ghusl toward which it prepares. The water for obligatory ghusl differs from the water for recommended ghusl. Since ablution is a prerequisite for ghusl, the water of the preparatory ablution itself transforms according to the intention with which the believer approaches the ghusl. The interpretation is this: the greatness of the prophetic bounds (*ḥudūd al-nabī*) is measured according to his Imām. If the Imām possesses a rank of supreme elevation, his prophetic bounds will be correspondingly exalted and majestic.

## Menstruation and the Ghusl al-Ḥayḍ

A woman's menstruation (*ḥayḍ*) is blood that emerges from her own flesh. This unclean blood flows from her body once every lunar month, except when she becomes pregnant. When the sperm of a man unites with the sperm of a woman and pregnancy is established, the pregnancy absorbs that menstrual blood back into itself. But if the two sperm do not combine in conception, the blood becomes impure and flows forth from the woman's body.

Compared with the bodies of humans and animals, their females possess a greater share of fluid capacity. This is because the female body is constituted to knead and complete the form of the human or animal body. When a man's sperm joins with a woman's sperm in her womb, they become unified. And because life-force (*ḥayāt*) dwells within these two commingled sperm, the developing form requires constant nourishment. The secretions accumulated in the woman's body continuously reach the forming sperm as food, enabling it to grow until the unified form of man and woman—which was hidden and latent in their individual sperm—emerges by divine decree into manifest existence.

Conversely, when menstrual blood accumulates without a receptacle to absorb and contain it, it begins to flow forth from the front passage, becoming extremely foul and rank. For this reason, women are forbidden to pray, to recite the Qur'ān, to enter the mosque, and to fast during these days. Moreover, their husbands are not permitted to have intercourse with them until the bleeding ceases. At that time, the woman must perform the ghusl al-ḥayḍ and resume her prayers. There is no obligation to make up the prayers missed during menstruation, but she must make up the fasts she has omitted.

**Symbolic Interpretation:**

In the spiritual teaching, the *mustajīb* (the respondent, the one who hearkens) is exemplified by woman, and the *dāʿī* (the summoner, the one who calls) is exemplified by man. The self of the *mustajīb*, owing to the absence of any true form of knowledge, is inherently weak. Because of this weakness, the *mustajīb's* soul will accept any form of knowledge indiscriminately—whether from truth or falsehood. This ugliness of the *mustajīb* arises from ignorance of his eternal nature and spiritual provenance.

When the *mustajīb* cannot find a qualified dāʿī from whom to learn, he attempts to create his own form of knowledge and to forge solutions to his perplexities through his own intellect alone. He searches and reasons with his unaided power, and this causes disturbed thoughts and doubts to accumulate within him. Because he has not gradually acquired correct understanding from a guide of truth, but has formed conceptions solely through his own reasoning and conjecture, these self-made concepts do not crystallize into genuine knowledge. Instead, his ideas scatter and dissipate. The insignificant and disordered thoughts of such a *mustajīb* resemble menstrual blood: they accumulate, yet because they are weak and watery, lacking solidity, they are easily broken apart by the slightest argument and flow away.

That is, when a woman does not receive the sperm of a man, her power of retention is lost. Just as menstrual blood is foul and malodorous on the outward level, so too the self-made thoughts of a *mustajīb* are impure because they originate from him alone. These thoughts cause him mental anguish and pain, just as menstrual blood causes physical distress and unpleasantness.

When the sperm is fertilized in the womb, menstrual blood ceases to flow, and the menstrual substance becomes food for the developing form. The interpretation is this: the outer knowledge (*ʿilm al-ẓāhir*) of the *mustajīb* is symbolized by the woman's sperm, and the inner knowledge (*ʿilm al-bāṭin*) of the dāʿī is symbolized by the man's sperm. When these two—external and internal knowledge—unite, they establish a firm and solid form of knowledge in the soul of the *mustajīb*. Thereafter, if any doubt arises—which belongs to the sphere of blood—that established form of knowledge absorbs and obliterates the doubt. And as the doubts dissipate (absorbed into the unified knowledge-sperm), the cognitive form becomes ever more stable and complete. Just as the physical form of the child is perfected through menstrual blood, so the spiritual form of the *mustajīb* is perfected through the inner knowledge of the dāʿī. Once that form is established, it shall never be lost.[^cite-6]

A woman cannot resume prayer until she completes the ghusl al-ḥayḍ—that is, until the *mustajīb* becomes disillusioned with his corrupt and false thoughts, breaks his false ego, and admits that he lacks the capacity to answer his own questions and fulfill his intellectual longings without the guidance of a dāʿī. His disillusionment with his self-created doubts constitutes the cessation of menstruation. When the *mustajīb* becomes disillusioned with himself and recognizes that his self-derived knowledge is flawed, he should then enter the era of the Imām—an epoch which is symbolized by the menstrual ghusl.

The mosque is an exemplar of the dāʿī (the preacher of truth), and the Qur'ān is an exemplar of the Imām. During menstruation, a woman cannot enter the mosque or recite the Qur'ān. Similarly, the *mustajīb* who seeks to forge his own path apart from a qualified guide is not brought to the dāʿī or to the Imām.

There is no obligation to make up prayers missed during menstruation. Its interpretation is this: the *mustajīb* should not take the covenant (*bayʿa*) of discipleship until he has become disillusioned with his weak and conjecture-tainted knowledge, and one should not teach him the inner sciences (*ʿulūm al-bāṭina*) until that disillusionment is complete. Once he has taken the covenant, he may join the gathering of knowledge and perfect himself in all that follows; yet he cannot rectify the past, for that time has passed. Therefore, the menstruating woman is not required to make up the prayers she missed; she is obliged only to pray after performing the ghusl.

Although a menstruating woman incurs no obligation to make up the prayers she has missed, she is required to make up the fasts she has omitted. The interpretation is this: fasting (*ṣawm*) exemplifies the concealment of the hierarchical degrees of religion (Muḥammad, ʿAlī, Imām al-Zamān, and the other Imāms) and the hiding of these mysteries from the unworthy. When the *mustajīb* was impure and surrounded by doubts, he could not comprehend the bounds (*ḥudūd*) of religion. At that time, it was not permissible for him to remain silent; rather, he was obligated to seek the truth. But once he has taken the covenant (the ghusl of menstruation) and has acquired correct knowledge of the hierarchy of the bounds of religion, it now becomes his obligation to conceal their ranks from the undeserving—something he could not have done before. This is the interpretation of fasting.

The interpretation of the rule that a man should not have intercourse with a woman during menstruation is this: the dāʿī should not engage in scholarly discourse with the *mustajīb*, and should not teach him, until the *mustajīb's* doubts and false thoughts have been purified—for at that time he lacks the capacity to receive and absorb the teachings. Once menstruation has ceased, the dāʿī may then educate the *mustajīb*, and the knowledge will benefit him indeed.

## Why Was Menstruation Created? (Symbolic Explanation)

Allāh has granted menstruation to women in order to make manifest the distinction between the imperfect and the perfect. No matter how ardently a woman strives, she cannot pray for thirty days of the month. Yet a man, who is perfect in comparison, can pray for all thirty days.[^cite-7]

Women are naturally inclined toward exposing inner states due to the nature of their intent and temperament. Therefore, they were forbidden to conceal themselves from the gaze of the undeserving and unworthy. Menstrual blood represents doubts and confusions. Just as a woman cannot stop her menstruation even if she wishes to, so too the *mustajīb* cannot hide or contain his doubts—despite his best efforts, they express themselves and become evident. But when a woman becomes pregnant, her sperm absorbs that very blood and draws strength from it. In the same way, when the knowledge of the *mustajīb* crystallizes into a true conception of reality, that conception absorbs all his doubts and annihilates them. The more that scholarly conception shatters and disperses doubts, the greater becomes the perfection and stability in the faith of the *mustajīb*. A single true cognitive conception overcomes countless doubts, and what was once a worthless sperm emerges as a perfected human being of wisdom and understanding.

The phenomena of menstruation are intimately connected with the spiritual world that manifested itself among the prophets. The second prophet assumed an equality with fancy and imagination (*al-wahm*), which stands opposite to menstruation. Through this false assumption, he attempted to make himself equal to the first source of emanation, which resulted in evil. In like manner, every woman desires to be equal to a man. When the second one repents and acknowledges the greatness and supreme superiority of the bounds of the first, his evil is removed from him and he returns to the sanctuary of the man. The perfection of woman lies in becoming man—that is, in transcending the limitations of lesser knowledge and ascending to the fullness of true understanding.
