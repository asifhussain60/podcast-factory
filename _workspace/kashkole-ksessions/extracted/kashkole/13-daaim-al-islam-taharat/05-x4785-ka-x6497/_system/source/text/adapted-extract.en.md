## An Account of Ghusl (Ritual Bathing)

God Most High declares in His revealed word the necessity of ritual purity. It is related from the Commander of the Faithful, ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib, that the Prophet—upon whom be peace and blessings—said: "Whoever performs ghusl (ritual bathing) without first forming the intention of ablution for the sake of removing major ritual impurity does so ten times over in futility; such a ghusl shall avail him nothing." The Imāms of the Pure Household taught that one must first perform wuḍūʾ (ablution), then remove any external impurity, and thereafter pour water upon the entire body until every part—reached by the hands and encompassed by sight—has been touched. The hands themselves must traverse the body; the hair must be thoroughly soaked; and the flesh must be cleansed. As in ablution, there exists no prescribed minimum quantity of water for ghusl.[^cite-1]

The Sacred Law has established nineteen categories of ritual bathing—a number that mirrors the nineteen keepers of the Fire—comprising seven obligatory baths and twelve Sunnaic (recommended) baths. The obligatory ablutions are these:

The Commander of the Faithful has transmitted in his *Four Hundred Maxims of Conduct* that no believer should surrender to sleep in a state of major impurity, nor without having performed ablution. Should water be unavailable, one must perform tayammum (dry ablution). This is because the soul ascends during sleep to the celestial realms, where the Almighty regards it and bestows His grace upon it. Should death approach in that hour, He transforms it into a fairer form; should life be ordained, He returns it to the body under the guardianship of the faithful angel.[^cite-2]

## Ṭahāra on the Day of Friday Congregational Prayer

The ghusl of Friday holds primacy among the twelve Sunnaic baths by virtue of its nobility and rank. It occupies a unique station: the bathing of the deceased stands as the final obligatory ghusl, while the Friday bath stands foremost among the Sunnaic ablutions. This correspondence reveals a profound relation—the Friday ghusl forms a threshold between obligation and recommendation, just as in the cosmos there exists a boundary between the celestial realm, the intermediate sphere of life and soul, and the physical world, wherein dwell wonders beyond reckoning.[^cite-3]

The saints of God approach the Friday ghusl with the following intention:

⟪ar:اَللّٰھُمَّ اِنِّی نَوَیتُ ھَذَا وُضُوءِی وَ طَھُورِی لِغُسلِ یوم الجُمُعَۃ لأِزاَلَتِ جَمِیعِ النَّجاَساَتِ وَالأَدِراَنِ فِی جِسمِی وَ فُضلاَ تِی وَ قَلبِی وَ ذَھنِی وَ فِطرِی وَ رِیحِیاَتِی وَ صُورَتِی وَ نَفسِی وَ جَمِیعِ الاَھوَالِ فِی الدُّنِیَا وَ الآخِرَۃِ وَالظَّاھِرَۃِ وَالباَطِنَۃِ لِصَلوٰاۃِ الظُّھرِ وَالعَصرِ وَلِکُلِّ صَلوٰاۃٍ اُصَلِّیھَا⟫

*O God, I have resolved to perform this ablution and purification for the ghusl of Friday, to remove all filth and soiling in my body, my failings, my heart, my intellect, my constitution, my appetites, my outward form, my soul, and all terrors of this world and the next, both outward and inward, for the midday and afternoon prayers and for every prayer I shall perform.*

One may ask: why does this invocation speak of removing all impurities when the one who performs this bath is neither in a state of major impurity from intercourse, nor afflicted by involuntary emission, nor menstruating? The answer lies in understanding that such defects arise from three sources: insufficient clarity in faith, sloth in worship, and disregard of knowledge. They arise too from deficiency in the love of the Imāms. Since this person has not inhabited the world of knowledge and wisdom, has not adorned the gathering of virtue, and has not placed contemplation at the center of devotion, all such lapses create imperfections in soul and body which, over time, harden into actual filths and obstruct the prayer. The water of Friday's ghusl removes these hidden and manifest impurities from both body and soul.

**The Virtue of the Sixth Hour**

The Friday ghusl confers extraordinary benefit and efficacy. Friday is itself the sixth day; the sixth corresponds to the Nāṭiq (the speaking prophetic authority)—Muḥammad Muṣṭafā, upon whom be peace. On this day the powers of the sixth hour are greatly magnified. The bath performed at this moment illuminates two realities in the human form: one purifies the body of external filth, while the other kindles in the heart a longing for knowledge.

| **Purification from Filth** | **Awakening of the Desire for Knowledge** |

**Trimming of the Nails**

The fingernails and toenails should be trimmed at this time as well. Nails are the outermost boundaries of the body, where decay gathers; they signify outward beliefs and inclinations contrary to truth. To trim them is to distance oneself from all such false doctrines. Moreover, nails can wound another; thus, alongside the trimming of bodily nails, one must trim the spiritual nails of one's character, lest one cause spiritual hurt to others. Whoever experiences disturbance should trim his nails on Thursday.

## Ghusl al-Janāba and Iḥtilām (Major Ritual Bathing and Wet Dreams)

*Janāba* signifies estrangement and distance. The Arab calls the stranger *gharīb* (the foreign one), and the foreign *ghurba* (foreignness). One who is far removed from the lineage and household of the Messenger of God and from obedience to the Imāms—his deeds are void and without effect. The cases obligating ghusl are these:

| **1. Emission of Procreative Fluid** | When water emerges from the back (in sexual intercourse), ghusl becomes obligatory. A man is bound to bathe when exposed to fluid capable of producing life. At such time he must wash the entire body—which comprises seven organs. Marital intercourse is lawful; intercourse outside marriage is forbidden. |
| --- | --- |
| **2. Meeting of the Circumcised Parts** | Ghusl is obligatory when the two circumcised members meet—that is, when the male member enters the female—even without ejaculation. Both man and woman must then perform ghusl. |
| **3. Iḥtilām (Involuntary Emission in Sleep)** | When one experiences involuntary emission in a dream, ghusl becomes obligatory, yet no sin attaches to him because he acted without intention. Should the act be intentional (masturbation), it becomes sinful. |

Major ritual impurity occurs when a man's procreative fluid enters his wife; infertility results when the fluid is emitted but does not enter. It is said that whoever neglects the ghusl of janāba is forbidden to sleep, to eat, to drink, or even to breathe until ghusl is performed. One should therefore hasten to purify oneself when obligatory, unless husband and wife intend further intercourse.[^cite-4]

**Example and Inner Meaning**

An example from the spiritual path: when a teacher imparts knowledge to a student, both must then completely attribute this knowledge to the Owner of Truth—the Imām al-Zamān (may peace be upon him)—and distance themselves from the claim of having originated it. As water is applied to every limb, so must spiritual understanding be traced back to its true Source among the Seven Imāms, who represent the seven organs of the body.

**(1) Emission of the Tongue**

When something proceeds from the mouth of a dāʿī (summoner to faith) that can bring about the spiritual birth of a mustajīb (respondent), it becomes obligatory for the mustajīb to relinquish all claim to that knowledge and to attribute it to the seven Imāms. The example of one who acquires knowledge lawfully is one who speaks of divine matters by the permission of the Imām al-Zamān; the example of one who commits forbidden intercourse (spiritual fornication) is one who speaks of religion without authorization.

In another reading, ghusl signifies the removal of all doubts and suspicions through the knowledge of the Imām, for doubts arise only from false learning and vain conjecture.[^cite-5]

The preacher of Yemen, Sayyidunā ʿAlī ibn Sayyidunā Sulaymān, was asked: "If a person in major impurity and a corpse are in one place, and sufficient water exists for ghusl of only one, whom should be bathed?" He answered: "The one in janāba should perform ghusl with that water; the corpse should be given tayammum."

It is forbidden to cut hair or trim nails during menstruation.

**(2) Bath at the Meeting of Circumcision**

The example of the dāʿī (preacher of truth) is that of a man; the example of the mustajīb (female learner) is that of a woman. Both are obliged to perform ghusl outwardly and inwardly—that is, both teacher and student must attribute the knowledge to the Imāms. The meaning of emission without intercourse is this: even if a scholarly matter has been stated, should the listener have failed to grasp its true reality or benefit from it, both must perform a spiritual ghusl—returning that knowledge to the Imām.

**(3) Iḥtilām (Wet Dream)**

The example of iḥtilām is one who unintentionally and by error speaks something of taʾwīl (esoteric exegesis), then is startled, thinking: "What have I said of interpretation?" Such a one too is obliged to attribute it to the Imāms. The example of intentional transgression is one who deliberately derives taʾwīl from himself through conjecture and speculation, finding pleasure in interpreting the commandments of the Law through his own opinion—which is forbidden. Such forced perversion leads to external and internal disease.[^cite-6]

When a believer is obliged to perform ghusl, his relationship with the Nāṭiq is severed (for the reality has been displaced), upon which the angels withdraw from him. Should he delay in bathing, the devils approach and afflict him, inciting him to further sin. Proper ghusl restores his connection with the angel, and the believer is again sheltered under angelic protection.

## The Water for Wuḍūʾ and Ghusl

The water used in ablution possesses extraordinary qualities, and water manifests in three distinct forms:

| **1. Running Water** | **2. Water Striking the Limbs** | **3. Water Entering Through the Pores** |

Each of these waters operates according to distinct principles. When a believer begins ablution by pronouncing *Bismillāh ar-Raḥmān ar-Raḥīm* ("In the Name of God, the Merciful, the Compassionate"), both the water and the breath undergo transformation. Respiratory vitality enters the breath. The bodies of the Imāms are constituted of pure life; all they consume throughout their days becomes life itself. The believer receives the sustenance of the Imāms at three moments:[^cite-7]

| **During Ablution** | **At the Time of the Pre-Dawn Meal (Saḥrī)** | **At the Time of Breaking Fast (Iftār)** |

Likewise, each drop of water contains three components. One component manifests the quality of physical cleansing, serving all manner of washing—of body, garments, and vessels. One component quenches thirst. The third is employed for ablution and spiritual purification. According to the intention a person brings, that component of the water becomes operative.

## The Virtues of the Worlds of Islām and Īmān (Symbolism)

Wuḍūʾ is the name given to emerging from the evils of the world of Islām and entering the world of Īmān; after entering the world of Īmān, what follows is called ghusl, whereby the believer ascends to ever-higher stations. In ablution, the parts of the body are washed such that water strives to exit through the very pores of the skin. When one performs ablution, the good deeds accomplished in previous times while dwelling in the world of Islām manifest themselves as water. When one performs ghusl, the virtues earned while in the world of Īmān appear as water.

| **Ablution** | **Ghusl** |
| --- | --- |
| **The Virtues of the World of Islām** | **The Virtues of the World of Īmān** |

**The Virtues of the World of Islām**

We are bound by the limits of time and circumstance. In ages past, we performed acts of kindness toward those outside the faith, deeds we have since forgotten; yet the natural order has preserved the record of this goodness. The one to whom we showed kindness fell into darkness through his own evil deeds and corrupt thoughts. But we ourselves returned to human dignity because of the love of Muḥammad and ʿAlī. Nature, however, retained an account of our goodness. God Most High brings forth to us the fruit of that virtue as water, and we bathe ourselves in it. Thus those virtues benefit us in two ways: they wash away our physical defilements and repay our earlier kindness. God possesses the reckoning of every atom—and thus it is said that God is swift in reckoning![^cite-8]

**The Virtues of the World of Īmān**

The virtues of the world of faith are the knowledge a believer has acquired and followed in obedience to the elders. These virtues appear as water on the nights of soul and enter the body through ghusl, becoming part of one's very substance, ascending to the highest stations.

**The Keepers of These Waters**

The Islamic water is overseen by Fāṭima al-Zahrāʾ; the water of faith is overseen by the Imām al-Zamān and ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib. Pure water bears within it the knowledge of the elders. In the world of faith there flows the mother of living water—waters rising from the depths—commingled with the waters of this transient realm. When one performs ablution, connection with these waters is established by pronouncing *Bismillāh ar-Raḥmān ar-Raḥīm* and allowing the water to touch the body.

**The Measure of Water for Ablution and Bathing**

The water of ablution is transformed according to the water of ghusl. The water of obligatory ghusl differs from the water of the Sunnaic ghusl. Since ablution must precede ghusl, the water of preliminary ablution likewise transforms according to the intention with which one approaches ghusl. The inner meaning is this: the rank of the limits (ḥudūd, the sacred boundaries) of the Prophet is proportionate to his Imām. Should the Imām be of exalted rank, his limits shall be equally great.

## Menstruation and the Ghusl al-Ḥayḍ

A woman's menstrual blood is blood that flows from her own flesh. This unclean blood emerges from her body each month, save when she becomes pregnant. When a man's procreative fluid meets a woman's and conception takes place, the pregnancy absorbs the blood, drawing it back into the body. But when the two fluids do not unite, the blood becomes unclean and flows forth from the woman.

Compared to the bodies of humans and animals, the female form contains a higher proportion of fluid. This is by divine design, for the woman's body is fashioned to knead and perfect the human body. When a man's procreative seed enters a woman's womb and unites with her seed, they become one. Because life dwells within these two combined seeds, they require sustenance, which comes from the secretions accumulated in the woman's body. These secretions continuously reach the developing seed, nourishing it to grow until the form of this union—which was latent and potential in their seeds—manifests according to God's wisdom and knowledge.[^cite-9]

Conversely, when menstrual blood accumulates without a recipient to receive it and transform it, it begins to flow forth from the vagina, becoming extremely foul and malodorous. Women are forbidden to pray, to recite the Qurʾān, to enter the mosque, and to fast during these days. Their husbands are not permitted to lie with them until the bleeding ceases. Upon cessation, the woman must perform ghusl al-ḥayḍ and resume her prayers. No makeup is required for prayers missed during menstruation, but she must make up the fasts she has foregone.

**Inner Meaning and Spiritual Interpretation**

In the spiritual order, the mustajīb (respondent) dwells in the world of the dāʿī (summoner), and the dāʿī dwells in the world of the Nāṭiq (speaker-prophet). The self of the mustajīb, owing to the absence of any formed knowledge, remains weak. In this weakness, the soul accepts any knowledge whatsoever—whether from truth or falsehood. This defilement of the respondent arises from ignorance of his eternal reality. When the mustajīb finds no one from whom to learn, he attempts to forge a knowledge-form of his own, seeking solutions through his own powers. He searches and reasons with his individual understanding, thereby accumulating confused thoughts and doubts. Since he has not gradually received proper education from one of the People of Truth, but has formed concepts solely through his own reasoning and speculation, these concepts yield him no solid knowledge; rather, his notions scatter and fragment. Thus the insignificant, disorderly thoughts of the mustajīb resemble menstrual blood: they accumulate, yet being weak and unstable, they shatter at the slightest contrary argument and flow away.

When a woman does not receive the man's seed, her power of retention is lost. Just as menstrual blood is externally unclean and foul-smelling, so too the self-generated thoughts of the mustajīb are unclean, for they spring from himself alone. Such thoughts cause him mental anguish and pain, even as he is sickened by the sight and odor of blood.[^cite-10]

When the man's seed is received in the womb and conception occurs, the menstrual blood ceases to flow, becoming nourishment for the seed. The inner meaning is this: the woman's sperm corresponds to the outer knowledge of the mustajīb; the man's sperm corresponds to the inner knowledge of the dāʿī. When these two—outer and inner knowledge—are united, they establish within the mustajīb's self a solid form of knowledge. Thereafter, should any doubt arise (such doubts belonging to the realm of blood), this knowledge-form absorbs and erases it. As his doubts dissipate and are absorbed into the seed, his cognitive form grows ever more stable and complete. Just as the physical form of the child is perfected through menstrual blood, so the spiritual form of the mustajīb is perfected through the inner knowledge of the dāʿī; and once that form is established, it is never lost.

A woman cannot resume prayer until she has completed ghusl al-ḥayḍ—that is, until the mustajīb has been disabused of his corrupted thoughts, has broken his ego, and has admitted that he lacks the power to answer his questions without a guide, nor to fulfill his intellectual longings alone. His despair in his own doubts marks the cessation of menstruation. When the mustajīb becomes disillusioned with himself and recognizes the flaws in his self-generated knowledge, he must then enter the era of the Imām—an example of ghusl al-ḥayḍ.

The mosque is the example of the dāʿī; the Qurʾān is the example of the Imām. During menstruation, a woman cannot enter the mosque or recite the Qurʾān. Thus, the mustajīb who tries to find a way by his own efforts is not brought to the dāʿī or the Imām.

There is no obligation to make up prayers missed during the days of menstruation. The inner meaning is this: the mustajīb should not take the oath of allegiance (bayʿa) until he has despaired of his weak and conjecture-tainted knowledge, and should not hear the inner sciences. Once he has taken the oath, he may join the gathering of knowledge and improve the rest of his life, but he cannot undo the past, for time has passed. For this reason, the menstruating woman is not required to repeat the prayers of previous days; rather, she is obliged only to pray after ghusl al-ḥayḍ.

Although a menstruating woman is not required to make up missed prayers, she is obliged to make up the fasts she has foregone. The inner meaning is that fasting exemplifies the concealment of the hierarchies of religion—of Muḥammad, ʿAlī, and the Imām al-Zamān—and their safeguarding from the undeserving. When the mustajīb was impure and surrounded by doubts, he could not comprehend the bounds of religion; it was not then permissible for him to remain silent, but rather to seek the truth. But once he has taken the oath (ghusl al-ḥayḍ) and has acquired true knowledge of the sacred boundaries, it becomes obligatory for him to conceal their ranks from the unworthy—a concealment he could not perform before. This is the meaning of fasting.

The prohibition against intercourse during menstruation signifies this: the dāʿī should not engage in learned discourse with the mustajīb until the latter's doubts and false notions are clarified, nor should the dāʿī teach him, for the mustajīb lacks the capacity to receive at that time. Once menstruation ceases, the dāʿī may educate the mustajīb with benefit.

## Why Was Menstruation Created? (Symbolic Explanation)

God Most High made menstruation available to women in order to make manifest the distinction between imperfection and perfection. However strenuously a woman endeavors, she cannot pray for the full thirty days of a month; but a man, in his comparative perfection, can pray all thirty days. Women are inclined toward incompleteness by the nature of their creation; they were therefore forbidden to veil themselves from the eyes of the undeserving. Menstrual blood signifies doubts and suspicions. Just as a woman cannot stop her menstruation even by her utmost effort—it flows forth despite herself—so too the mustajīb cannot conceal his doubts; they burst forth despite his striving. But when a woman becomes pregnant, her procreative seed absorbs that same blood and draws strength from it. Likewise, when the mustajīb receives a knowledge-concept from the dāʿī, that concept absorbs all his doubts and eliminates them. The more that knowledge-form breaks and disperses doubts, the more stability and perfection grow in the mustajīb's faith. A single knowledge-concept overcomes manifold doubts; and that precious seed which possessed no standing becomes a perfect human being.

The matters of menstruation relate to the spiritual order that appeared among the prophets. The second prophet (Satan) assumed an equality with the first source, which is the opposite of menstruation. In his attempt to make himself equal to the First Intellect (al-ʿAql al-Awwal), he brought about evil. Similarly, every woman desires equality with a man. When the second man repented and acknowledged the greatness and superiority of his bounds, his evil was stripped from him and he returned to the realm of the man. The perfection of woman is to become man.[^cite-11]
