## An Account of Ghusl (Ritual Bathing)

God Most High declares in His Book that ritual bathing (ghusl) stands as a foundational ordinance of spiritual and physical purification. It is narrated that ⟪ar:مولانا علی علیہ السلام⟫ transmits from the Prophet—upon him be the peace and blessings of God—these words: "Should a person neglect to form the intention for ghusl al-janāba (major ritual bathing following seminal emission) whilst performing ritual ablution, he must repeat the ghusl ten times; otherwise, that single ablution shall not suffice for him." This teaching establishes that intention (niyya) constitutes the innermost foundation of all purificatory acts.

⟪ar:آئمہ طاہرین علیہم السلام⟫ The Imāms, the Purified Ones, have clarified the proper method of ghusl: one must first perform the preliminary ablution (wuḍūʾ), then cleanse away any visible impurity. Thereafter, water must be poured over the entire body such that every surface receives the stream—from the crown of the head to the soles of the feet. The hands are spread across the body; the hair is soaked; the skin is scrubbed and purified. Just as in ablution, no fixed quantity of water is prescribed, for the intention of the heart determines the sufficiency of the act.[^cite-1]

The Sharīʿa encompasses nineteen baths, corresponding to the nineteen angels who guard the Fire. Among these, seven are obligatory (farḍ) and twelve are recommended (sunna). The seven obligatory baths are:

| 1 | Janābat (seminal emission) |
| --- | --- |
| 2 | Menstruation |
| 3 | Recovery from unbelief |
| 4 | Regaining consciousness |
| 5 | *[term obscured in source]* |
| 6 | Major impurity (ḥadath) |
| 7 | Ritual bathing of the deceased |

The twelve sunna baths comprise those of Friday; the two ʿīds (Fiṭr and Adhā); entry into the sacred state (iḥrām); admission to Mecca; approach to Mount ʿArafāt; arrival in Medina; the nineteenth, twenty-first, and twenty-third days of the sacred month; and the bathing of the dead in circumstances other than the obligatory ghusl al-mayyit.

The Commander of the Faithful (Amīr al-Muʾminīn), ⟪ar:مولانا علی علیہ السلام⟫, declares in his *Four Hundred Etiquettes* that believers must never surrender themselves to sleep in a state of impurity (janāba) or without performing ablution. Should water be unavailable, one performs the dry ablution (tayammum). This is because the soul of the faithful ascends toward the heavens during sleep, and the Almighty regards it with loving attention. Should death be decreed, He transforms it into a form of perfect beauty; if life is prolonged, He returns it to the body under the guardianship of the faithful angel.[^cite-2]

## Ṭahāra on the Day of Friday Congregational Prayer

The ghusl of Friday (ghusl al-Jumu'a), by virtue of its supreme rank among the twelve recommended baths, stands as their leader. It is the first of the sunna baths just as the ritual bathing of the deceased is the last among the obligatory baths. This position reflects a profound correspondence: the Friday ghusl serves as a bridge between obligatory and recommended purifications, mirroring the relationship between the terrestrial world and the celestial realms. In the world of knowledge, there exists a similitude—a boundary line separating the heavens, the world of soul (ʿālam al-nafs), and the physical world, each constituting a station of wondrous signs.[^cite-3]

### The Intention of the Saints of God for Ghusl al-Jumu'a

Throughout a single week, the soul and body of a believer accumulate subtle defects and obscurations. For this reason, the wise among God's servants recite the following intention while performing the Friday ghusl:

⟪ar:اَللّٰھُمَّ اِنِّی نَوَیتُ ھَذَا وُضُوءِی وَ طَھُورِی لِغُسلِ یوم الجُمُعَۃ لأِزاَلَتِ جَمِیعِ النَّجاَساَتِ وَالأَدِراَنِ (کچرا، میل کچیل) فِی جِسمِی وَ فُضلاَ تِی وَ قَلبِی وَ ذَھنِی وَ فِطرِی وَ رِیحِیاَتِی وَ صُورَتِی وَ نَفسِی وَ جَمِیعِ الاَھوَالِ فِی الدُّنِیَا وَ الآخِرَۃِ وَالظَّاھِرَۃِ وَالباَطِنَۃِ لِصَلوٰاۃِ الظُّھرِ وَالعَصرِ وَلِکُلِّ صَلوٰاۃٍ اُصَلِّیھَا⟫

A subtle question arises: why does this intention invoke the removal of all impurities when the one performing the ghusl is neither experiencing seminal emission, nor janāba, nor menstruation? The answer unfolds thus: all these defects arise from weakness in faith (īmān), negligence in worship, indifference to knowledge, and corruption in love for the Imāms. Such a person has not inhabited the world of wisdom, has not adorned the assembly of virtues, nor has he established contemplation in his innermost sanctuary. Consequently, these failures generate defects in both soul and body, which over time harden into spiritual filth and obstruct the path of worship. The water of Friday's ghusl washes away all these outward and inward stains from body and soul alike, restoring the believer to a state of radiant purity.[^cite-4]

### The Virtue of the Sixth Hour

The Friday ghusl carries immense spiritual benefit. Friday itself is the sixth day of the week, and the sixth signifies the *Nāṭiq* (the Speaker, Muhammad Muṣṭafā, upon him be peace). On this day, the powers of the sixth hour are intensely magnified. The ghusl performed at this moment awakens two essential states within the human form: first, the removal of external and internal impurities; second, the kindling of hunger for knowledge.

| Effect | Benefit |
| --- | --- |
| Purification from filth | Awakening the desire for knowledge |

### The Trimming of Nails

Nails should be trimmed at the time of the Friday ghusl. Both the visible nails of the hands and feet, as well as the hidden aspects of the self, require pruning. Nails are the outermost extremities of the body where impurity collects; they signify external attachments and beliefs that obscure the truth. To cut them is to sever oneself from all such false tendencies. Moreover, nails can wound another person; therefore, alongside the trimming of physical nails, one must also refine the "nails" of one's inner being—those sharp and harmful impulses—so as never to inflict spiritual pain upon others. Whoever finds himself vexed or troubled should cut his nails on Thursday, thereby removing from himself the source of discord before the arrival of Friday.[^cite-5]

## Ghusl al-Janāba and Iḥtilām (Major Ritual Bathing and Wet Dreams)

The term janāba carries the meaning of estrangement or distance. In the language of the Arabs, one who is far removed from a lineage is called "alienated" (*ghārīb*). In the spiritual sense, janāba refers to one who has distanced himself from the obedience of the Messenger of God and the *wilāya* (guardianship) of the Imāms—an alienation that renders all deeds void and without merit.

Ghusl al-janāba becomes obligatory in the following circumstances:

| Case | Condition |
| --- | --- |
| 1 | When seminal fluid exits the body (whether through intercourse or involuntary emission), ghusl becomes mandatory. It is obligatory to wash the entire body when a person is exposed to water capable of producing another being. In such a state, the whole body—consisting of seven primary members—must be thoroughly cleansed. Intercourse within lawful marriage is permissible; that outside marriage is forbidden. |
| 2 | Ghusl becomes obligatory when the two circumcised areas meet—that is, when the male member enters the female passage—even if seminal emission does not occur. In this case, both man and woman must perform ghusl. |
| 3 | Should a person experience involuntary emission during sleep (iḥtilām), ghusl is obligatory upon him, yet no sin attaches to him, for the act was not intentional. However, should one deliberately engage in self-pollution, he incurs sin. |

Janābat occurs when the man's sperm enters the wife's womb; infertility (or non-consummation) occurs when sperm is emitted but does not enter the woman. It is said that whoever neglects the ghusl al-janāba is forbidden to sleep, eat, drink, or even breathe the air until he has purified himself. For this reason, one should hasten to perform ghusl when it becomes obligatory, unless husband and wife intend to renew their intimacy.

### The Esoteric Interpretation

An example of ghusl al-janāba in the spiritual sense: when a knowledgeable preacher (dāʿī) imparts learning to a seeker (mustajīb), both must afterward wholly attribute this knowledge to the Source of Truth—the Imām al-Zamān—and distance themselves from any claim of personal authorship. As water is applied to every part of the body, so too must knowledge be attributed to all the spiritual faculties represented by the seven Imāms, who correspond to the seven members of the body.

The one who speaks with the permission of the Imām is like one who lawfully gathers sustenance; the one who speaks religious truths without his permission is like one committing forbidden intercourse. In another sense, ghusl teaches that all doubts and suspicions must be washed away through the knowledge of the Imām—for doubts arise only from ignorance and lack of access to truth.[^cite-6]

When a knowledgeable speaker addresses an ignorant seeker, and something proceeds from the speaker's mouth that can spiritually generate life in the seeker, the speaker must distance himself from any claim to that knowledge and attribute the wisdom entirely to the seven Imāms. The seeker, in turn, must renounce all conjecture and personal opinion, recognizing that true knowledge flows from the Imāms alone.

### Response of a Yemenite Preacher

It is related that a preacher from Yemen (Sayyidunā ʿAlī ibn Sulaymān) was asked: "If a man in a state of janāba and a corpse are together in a place where water suffices for the ghusl of only one, who should perform ghusl?" He replied: "The one in janāba should bathe, and the deceased should undergo dry ablution (tayammum), for the restoration of the living takes precedence."

It is forbidden to cut hair or nails during menstruation. (*Kitāb Aʿsāf al-Ṭālib fī Jāmiʿ al-Muttālib*)

### The Meeting of the Circumcised Areas

When the male and female organs meet in lawful union, both partners must perform ghusl—both outwardly and inwardly. That is, both teacher and learner must attribute all knowledge to the Imāms. The case in which seminal fluid is emitted but true consummation has not occurred—meaning that although scholarly discourse has taken place, the profound truth has not been revealed to the listener, or comprehension has not occurred—requires both to perform a spiritual ghusl: that is, to return all knowledge to the Imām and acknowledge its true source.

### Iḥtilām (Involuntary Emission)

The one who experiences iḥtilām is like a person who unintentionally speaks of esoteric exegesis (taʾwīl) and then is startled, exclaiming: "What I have uttered concerns the hidden meanings!" Such a person must also attribute this to the Imāms. By contrast, one who deliberately derives private interpretations through mere conjecture—taking pleasure in deriving meanings from his own intellect without the Imām's permission—commits a violation. Forced repression of such illicit interpretation leads to external and internal maladies.

When a believer is obliged to perform ghusl, his relationship with the *Nāṭiq* (the Speaker, i.e., the Messenger) is temporarily severed because the real bond has been broken by impurity. On account of this severance, the angels withdraw from him. If the believer does not hasten to bathe, the devils draw near and afflict him, inciting him to further sin. Proper and timely ghusl restores the connection with the angel, and the believer once more dwells under angelic protection.[^cite-7]

## The Water for Wuḍūʾ and Ghusl

The water employed in ablution possesses singular properties. There exist three distinct forms of water:

| Form | Characteristic |
| --- | --- |
| 1 | Water that flows forth from the source |
| 2 | Water that strikes the limbs |
| 3 | Water that enters the body through the pores |

Each of these waters manifests different properties and effects. The moment a believer begins ablution by pronouncing "Bismillāh ar-Raḥmān ar-Raḥīm" (In the Name of God, the Merciful, the Compassionate), the water of his ablution and his own breath undergo transformation. The components of life itself begin to enter into his respiration. The bodies of the Imāms are composed of pure life (*ḥayāt*), and all that they consume throughout their existence partakes of this essence. A believer receives nourishment from the Imāms' life-force in three moments:

| Moment | Occasion |
| --- | --- |
| 1 | At the time of ablution |
| 2 | At the time of the pre-dawn meal (suhūr) during the fast |
| 3 | At the time of breaking the fast (iftār) |

Similarly, each drop of water contains three components. One component manifests the property of physical cleansing, serving all forms of bodily ablution—whether of the body, clothing, or vessels. One component satisfies thirst. The third component is reserved for ablution and spiritual purification. According to the intention (*niyya*) a person forms, that particular component becomes active and efficacious.

When a person forms the intention to perform ablution or ghusl and pronounces "Bismillāh ar-Raḥmān ar-Raḥīm," the water must be engaged at that very moment. The components governing inner purity become activated and perform their function of purifying the soul of the believer. These components are fashioned from the knowledge of the Imāms and serve to cleanse the soul.[^cite-8]

The acts of ablution constitute an illustration of self-refinement. From the *Standing Place* of the Imāms, their attention descends upon us, and this attention begins a transformation within our souls.

| Component | Function |
| --- | --- |
| Physical Hygiene | Cleansing the outward body |
| Quenching Thirst | Sustaining the physical form |
| Ablution and Ghusl | Purifying the soul |

## The Virtues of the Worlds of Islām and Īmān (Symbolism)

Wuḍūʾ is the name given to the act of departing from the evils of the Islamic world (ʿālam al-islām) and entering the realm of faith (ʿālam al-īmān). After one has entered the world of faith, the subsequent purification is termed ghusl, whereby a believer may ascend to ever higher stations. In the prayer of the saints, they beseech God that the organs of the body be washed so thoroughly that water strives to emerge from the very pores. When a person performs ablution, his virtuous deeds accumulated in previous times while dwelling in the Islamic world manifest themselves in the form of water. When a person performs ghusl, his virtuous deeds accumulated during his time in the world of faith manifest themselves as water.

| Rite | Symbolism |
| --- | --- |
| Ablution | The Virtues of the Islamic World |
| Ghusl | The Virtues of the World of Faith |

### The Virtues of the Islamic World

We are bound by periods and cycles. In the past, we performed acts of goodness toward those who dwelt in unbelief—deeds now forgotten by us, yet preserved by the nature of all things. Those recipients of our kindness, having fallen into darkness through their own evil deeds and corrupt thoughts, have lost all benefit from our generosity. Yet by the *wilāya* of Muḥammad and ʿAlī, we ourselves have returned to the stature of true humanity. The account of that goodness, however, remains inscribed in nature's ledger. God brings forth to us their condensed essence in the form of water, and we perform ablution with it. In this way, those virtues work for our benefit: they remove our physical impurities and repay the debt of our past goodness. God possesses the reckoning of every particle, and thus it has been said that "God is Swift in His Reckoning."[^cite-9]

### The Virtues of the World of Faith

The virtues of the world of faith refer to the knowledge a believer has acquired through the guidance and obedience of the Imāms. These virtues appear in the form of water during the nights and, through ghusl, enter his body, becoming integrated within it and ascending to the pinnacle of the letters (*ḥurūf*)—the divine symbols.

**Facts:**

The Islamic water is administered by Fāṭima, the Mistress; the water of faith is administered by the Imām al-Zamān and ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib. Pure water contains components of the knowledge of the Imāms. In the world of life, there is the Mother of Waters (Umm al-Māʾ)—the Rising Water—which mingles with the waters of this earthly realm. During ablution, connection with this water is established by pronouncing "Bismillāh ar-Raḥmān ar-Raḥīm" and by the touch of the one performing ablution upon his own body.[^cite-10]

### The Measure of Water for Ablution and Bathing

The water of ablution changes according to the water of ghusl. The water of obligatory ghusl differs from that of supererogatory ghusl. Since it is necessary to perform ablution before ghusl, the water of the pre-ghusl ablution likewise changes according to the intention (*niyya*) with which the believer intends the ghusl itself. The inner meaning is this: the magnitude of the Prophet's limits (*ḥudūd*) corresponds to the rank of his Imām. Should the Imām be of exalted station, then his limits shall likewise possess proportionate greatness of rank.

| Element | Correspondence |
| --- | --- |
| Ablution | The Limits (ḥudūd) |
| Ghusl | The Imām |

## Menstruation and the Ghusl al-Ḥayḍ

Menstruation in a woman is blood that issues from her own flesh. This unclean blood flows from her body each month, save when she becomes pregnant. When the sperm of a man unites with the sperm of a woman and pregnancy occurs, the pregnancy absorbs that blood back into itself. But if the two sperms do not combine, the blood becomes unclean and flows outward from the woman's body.

Compared to the bodies of humans and animals, the female body possesses a greater proportion of fluids. This is because the female body is formed to knead and complete the human or animal form. When a man's sperm joins with a woman's sperm in the womb, they become unified. Due to the life force residing in these two merged sperms, they require nourishment. The secretions present in the woman's body accumulate and continuously reach the sperm, becoming food for its growth within the womb. In this manner, the powerful bond between man and woman, which was latent in their respective sperms, is manifest as a formed being emerging from Divine Will and Knowledge.

Conversely, if menstrual blood accumulates without a receiver—nothing to absorb and arrest it—it begins to flow outward from the vagina and becomes extremely foul and fetid. During these days, women are prohibited from prayer, recitation of the Qur'ān, entry into the mosque, and fasting. Their husbands are forbidden from intimate relations with them until the bleeding ceases. Thereafter, the woman must perform the menstrual ghusl and resume her prayers. There is no obligation to make up the prayers missed during menstruation, yet she must make up the fasts she omitted.

### Esoteric Interpretation

In the spiritual science, the seeker (mustajīb) dwells in the domain of the woman, and the knowledgeable preacher (dāʿī) dwells in the domain of the man. The soul of the seeker, owing to the absence of any formed knowledge, is weak. Because of this weakness, his soul accepts any knowledge indiscriminately—whether from truth or falsehood. This moral ugliness of the seeker stems from ignorance of his eternal world. When the seeker finds no suitable teacher from whom to learn, he attempts to construct forms of knowledge by his own effort and to solve problems through his own reasoning. He searches and ponders with his own power alone, thereby accumulating confused and conflicting thoughts and doubts. Since he has not gradually acquired proper education from a source of truth, but has formed concepts solely through his own thinking and conjecture, these concepts do not yield any solid form of knowledge; rather, his ideas remain scattered and incoherent. Thus, the insignificant and disordered thoughts of such a seeker resemble menstrual blood: they accumulate, yet because they are weak and cannot solidify, they shatter at the slightest objection and flow out.[^cite-11]

Just as a woman loses her capacity for retention when she does not receive a man's sperm, so the seeker's power of comprehension fails when he lacks a true teacher. As menstrual blood is ritually impure and foul-smelling on the surface, so too are self-generated thoughts impure, being products of the self alone. These thoughts cause the seeker mental distress and pain, just as menstrual flow causes a woman physical discomfort and shame.

When the sperm becomes fertilized in the womb, menstrual blood ceases to flow, and the sperm becomes nourishment. The spiritual meaning is this: the woman's sperm corresponds to the external knowledge of the seeker, and the man's sperm corresponds to the inner knowledge of the preacher. When these two—the external and inner sciences—unite, they establish a solid form of knowledge within the seeker's soul. Thereafter, if any doubt arises (which belongs to the domain of blood), this form of knowledge absorbs it, erasing the doubt entirely. As the seeker's suspicions dissipate and are absorbed into the sperm, his cognitive form becomes ever more stable and complete. Just as the physical form of the child is perfected through menstrual blood, so the spiritual form of the seeker is perfected through the inner knowledge of the preacher, and this form, once established, is never lost.[^cite-12]

A woman cannot resume prayer until she has completed her menstrual ghusl. The inner meaning: the seeker cannot return to service until he has become disillusioned with his immoral thoughts, has broken his ego, and has admitted that he lacks the strength to answer his questions without a guide and to fulfill his intellectual needs without help. His disillusionment with his own doubts represents the cessation of menstruation. When the seeker becomes disillusioned with himself and recognizes the flaw in his personal knowledge, he must enter the era of the Imām (the Imām of the Age), which is the spiritual equivalent of the menstrual ghusl.

The mosque symbolizes the preacher of truth, and the Qur'ān symbolizes the Imām. During menstruation, a woman cannot enter the mosque or recite the Qur'ān. Spiritually, the seeker who attempts to find a path through his own efforts is not brought to the preacher or to the Imām.

There is no obligation to make up prayers missed during menstruation. The interpretation: the seeker should not give the pledge (bayʿa) until he has been disillusioned with his weak and conjecture-tainted knowledge, and until then, he should not be instructed in the inner sciences. Once he has taken the pledge, he may join the assembly of knowledge and perfect the rest of his life; yet he cannot undo the past, for the time is gone. For this reason, the menstruating woman is not required to repeat the prayers of the past; she is obliged only to perform prayers after her ghusl is complete.

Although a menstruating woman need not make up missed prayers, she must make up all missed fasts. The spiritual significance: fasting symbolizes the concealment of the grades and stations of religion (Muḥammad, ʿAlī, the Imām al-Zamān, etc.) and their protection from the unworthy. When the seeker was impure and surrounded by doubts, he could not grasp the true limits (*ḥudūd*) of religion. At that time, it was not permissible for him to remain silent; rather, he had to demand knowledge of the truth. But once he has taken the pledge (performed the menstrual ghusl) and has acquired correct knowledge of the ranks and limits of religion, it now becomes his duty to conceal these ranks from the undeserving—something he could not have done before. This is the spiritual meaning of fasting.[^cite-13]

The prohibition against a man having intimate relations with a woman during menstruation signifies this: the preacher should not engage in scholarly discourse with the seeker until the seeker's doubts and false conceptions have been purified, and should not impart knowledge to him, for the seeker lacks the capacity to absorb at that time. Once menstruation has ceased, the preacher may teach the seeker, which will also bring benefit to him.

## Why Was Menstruation Created? (Symbolic Explanation)

God decreed menstruation for women in order to make manifest the distinction between the imperfect and the perfect. No matter how earnestly a woman strives, she cannot pray for thirty days in a month. Yet a man, in his comparative perfection, is able to pray all thirty days. Women possess a natural inclination toward concealment and modesty; therefore, they were forbidden to expose themselves to the eyes of the uninitiated. Menstrual blood is the symbol of doubt and confusion.

Just as a woman cannot arrest her menstruation even if she wills it with all her effort, so too the seeker cannot suppress his doubts and suspicions, despite striving, and perforce expresses them. But when a woman becomes pregnant, her sperm absorbs the menstrual blood and draws strength from it. In the same manner, when the seeker receives the knowledge of a true teacher and forms a concept of truth, that concept absorbs all his doubts and annihilates them. The more a scholarly conception breaks through and dispels doubt, the greater the perfection and stability that emerges in the seeker's faith. One true conception overcomes countless doubts, and that precious embryonic knowledge, which possessed no status, emerges as a perfected human being.[^cite-14]

The question of menstruation relates to the spiritual realm that manifested among the prophets. The second emanation (al-munbaʿith al-thānī) assumed an equality with the *wahm* (conjecture)—the opposite of menstruation—and sought to make itself equal to the First Intellect (al-ʿaql al-awwal), which led to its fall into error. In the same way, every woman desires equality with a man. When the second being repented and acknowledged the greatness and superiority of the limits of the first, its error was removed, and it returned to the domain of the man. The perfection of woman lies in her becoming a man—that is, in her ascent to the station of the perfect and masculine principle of knowledge and truth.[^cite-15]
