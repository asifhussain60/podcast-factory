# The Marriage of the Outer and the Inner

## Behind Every Appearance There Is an Inner Reality

The terms *zāhir* (the outer) and *bātin* (the inner) derive from Arabic: *ẓāhara* (ظَہَرَ, to appear) and *baṭana* (بَطَنْ, to contain within). The vital organs of the human body reside not upon the back but within the belly; what is truly real is internal, while the external merely points toward it. Just as the peel envelops the fruit, the outer form enshrouds the inner essence. The realm of spirit (*ʿālam rūḥānī*, العالم الروحاني) precedes the realm of body (*ʿālam jasmanī*, العالم الجسماني), just as the self precedes the body.[^cite-1] The outward perpetually gestures toward the inward—as example does to exemplified, as intellected form does to intellect. For this reason intention precedes action: a person's deeds mirror their inner resolve. Intention constitutes the inner dimension; action expresses it outwardly.

Every tangible thing in this world manifests both outer and inner faces. What we perceive through the five senses (*ḥawās khamsah*, حواس خمسۃ)—the tangible forms—reveals only the surface. True knowledge (*ʿilm*, علم) and discernment grasp what the outer senses cannot: an object's properties, benefits, and harms. The outward dominates the inner; the visible conceals the hidden—as the body masks the soul, as words conceal their meanings. The outer and inner are called each other's spouses and kin. Allah declares:

> ⟪quran 51:49⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَمِن كُلِّ شَىْءٍ خَلَقْنَا زَوْجَيْنِ لَعَلَّكُمْ تَذَكَّرُونَ ‎⟫
> *And of everything We have created pairs, that you may remember (the Grace of Allah).*

All created things must therefore be paired according to this verse, so that through this pairing Allah's unity (*tawḥīd*, توحید) becomes manifest and distinguishable. Allah alone (*aḥad*, أحد) stands without counterpart; all else besides Him must couple.[^cite-2]

### The Pairing of Outer and Inner, and the Oneness of God

Externally a human appears singular; yet inwardly he is a compound of body and soul. Body constitutes the outer; soul, the inner. Each of these two, examined closely, itself divides into two: the body comprises cold and dryness (*barāda* and *yubūsa*, برودت و یبوست); the soul comprises heat and moisture (*ḥarāra* and *ruṭūba*, حرارت و رطوبت). When the soul departs, the body becomes cold and dry.

| Body | Spirit |
| --- | --- |
| Cold + Dryness | Heat + Moisture |

Consider all things in creation: each possesses a mate. The material world divides into three substances—solid, liquid, and gaseous. Each state then subdivides into two: matter and form (*mādda* and *ṣūra*, مادہ و صورت), or essence and attributes (*dhāt* and *ṣifāt*, ذات و صفات). Nothing in existence lacks this duality: each thing is either hot or cold, dry or moist, present or absent, large or small, frozen or fluid, living or dead, dense or subtle, heavy or light. The Almighty transcends all such pairs. He cannot be called existent or non-existent, many or few, lacking or complete. His existence is proven by His signs and vestiges; He remains separate from all creation. As Allah says:

> ⟪quran 42:11⟫
> ⟪ar:‏فَاطِرُ ٱلسَّمَوَتِ وَٱلْأَرْضِ ۚ جَعَلَ لَكُم مِّنْ أَنفُسِكُمْ أَزْوَجًۭا وَمِنَ ٱلْأَنْعَمِ أَزْوَجًۭا ۖ يَذْرَؤُكُمْ فِيهِ ۚ لَيْسَ كَمِثْلِهۦ شَىْءٌۭ ۖ وَهُوَ ٱلسَّمِيعُ ٱلْبَصِيرُ ‎⟫
> *The Creator of the heavens and the earth. He has made for you mates from yourselves, and for the cattle (also) mates. By this means He creates you (in the wombs). There is nothing like unto Him, and He is the All-Hearer, the All-Seer.*

In the first verse Allah declares that all things possess a spouse; in this verse He proclaims that nothing resembles Him. Thus is the divine unity established through the principle of pairing itself.

## The Significance of Example and Exemplified

Those from among the people of truth (*ahl al-ḥaqq*) who follow the Shariah (*sharīʿa*, شریعت) by understanding both the example (*mithāl*, مثال) and the exemplified (*mumthal*, ممثول)—comprehending the correspondence between parable and reality—these rank among the foremost and the last (*sābiqīn*, سابقین).[^cite-3] Those who, lacking this knowledge, merely adhere to the law constitute the followers and the later generations (*tābiʿīn wa-lāḥiqīn*, تابعین و لاحقین). The outer rests upon the inner, from which the outer derives its benefit; the inner is established by the outer, from which the inner itself becomes beneficial. Allah says:

> ⟪quran 6:120⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَذَرُوا۟ ظَهِرَ ٱلْإِثْمِ وَبَاطِنَهُۥٓ ۚ إِنَّ ٱلَّذِينَ يَكْسِبُونَ ٱلْإِثْمَ سَيُجْزَوْنَ بِمَا كَانُوا۟ يَقْتَرِفُونَ ‎⟫
> *Leave (O mankind, all kinds of) sin, open and secret. Verily, those who commit sin will get due recompense for that which they used to commit.*

The two forms of worship remain inseparable. Each commandment of the Shariah and its inner meaning together constitute a whole:

| Religious Act | Outer Form | Inner Meaning |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Purity | Ritual ablution | Cleansing the heart of attachment to Satan's whispers through knowledge of the Imam |
| Prayer | Standing in ritual prayer | Remaining steadfast in the Imam's summons (*daʿwa*, دعوۃ) |
| Alms-tax | Giving of wealth | Bestowing esoteric knowledge (*ʿilm*, علم) upon the deserving |
| Fasting | Abstaining from food and drink | Concealing knowledge from the unworthy |
| Pilgrimage | Journey to the House of Allah | Preparing one's form to join the inner congregation of the Imam's circle |
| Spiritual struggle | Outward jihad | Removing the evils arising from heed to the self's tyranny; acting with thought, word, and deed in alignment with truth |

## The Relationship Between Example and Exemplified

A special relationship always obtains between the example and the exemplified: the exemplified must possess the same qualities as the example. This correspondence between parable and reality is called *taʾwīl* (esoteric exegesis, تاویل). All the prophets therefore established examples for their laws, permitting one to move from the visible example to the inner exemplified truth. Noah, upon him be peace, made the ark his example. Abraham made the building (*kaʿba*, کعبہ). Jesus made the cross. Moses—and Muhammad the Seal of Prophets (*muḥammad muṣṭafā*, محمد مصطفی)—made the testimony of faith (*kalima lā ilāha illā-llāh*, کلمہ لا الہ الا اﷲ) their example for their Shariah.[^cite-4] The example represents the apparent form; the exemplified constitutes its hidden truth. The apparent ever points toward its inner self.

Water exemplifies knowledge. In worldly terms, all the physical properties of water appear in its parable:

| Water (Example) | Knowledge (ʿIlm) |
| --- | --- |
| Water flows perpetually from high to low | Knowledge descends always from the higher to the lower; if received from one's equal, it is mere information, not knowledge |
| Water cleanses the body | Knowledge purifies the self |
| Water assumes the shape of its vessel | Knowledge conforms to the one who receives it; from a good person it yields benefit; from a bad person, harm |
| Water sustains all creatures | Knowledge sustains all souls |
| Water remains pure as it flows; if stagnant, it spoils | Knowledge remains uncorrupted as it passes from transmitter to transmitter; if it halts with someone, doubt and confusion arise |
| Water changes state by degree of subtlety and density (ice, liquid, vapor) | Knowledge exists in degrees of refinement |
| Water purifies but does not purify itself | The outer law purifies; inner knowledge cannot perfect itself alone |

Water comprises two elements—hydrogen and oxygen—in the proportion H₂O. Knowledge similarly comprises two things: the sayings of the Prophet (upon him be peace) and the wisdom of the Commander of the Faithful. In inheritance law, the male heir receives two shares and the female one. Thus hydrogen dwells in the male's abode and oxygen in the female's. In religion, man exemplifies the Messenger because his knowledge is doubled; the example of ʿAlī resembles woman, whose knowledge is half that of the Messenger.

| H₂ | O |
| --- | --- |
| Sayings of the Messenger of Allah | The Wisdom of Maulānā ʿAlī |
| Man | Woman |

Behind every outward example in creation must stand an inner exemplified reality in faith. All commandments of the Shariah bind the body; knowledge of their inner meanings binds the soul.

## The Supreme Example

Allah declares in the Qur'an:

> ⟪quran 16:60⟫
> ⟪ar:‏لِلَّذِينَ لَا يُؤْمِنُونَ بِٱلْآخِرَةِ مَثَلُ ٱلسَّوْءِ ۖ وَلِلَّهِ ٱلْمَثَلُ ٱلْأَعْلَىٰ ۚ وَهُوَ ٱلْعَزِيزُ ٱلْحَكِيمُ ‎⟫
> *For those who believe not in the Hereafter is an evil description, and for Allah is the highest description. And He is the All-Mighty, the All-Wise.*

Allah is an invisible self, possessing no physical form. To imagine God as anthropomorphic is idolatry. Since Allah Himself is the Supreme Example (*al-mithāl al-aʿlā*, المثل الأعلی), it becomes necessary to establish for Him an example. Each circle—each rank of existence—has a chief (*rafīq*, رفیق). We call it *al-mithāl al-aʿlā* because we cannot fathom His majesty; we lack capacity to comprehend His reality fully. We merely acknowledge this chief's existence and proceed through knowledge toward understanding via his limits. Whatever we wonder about is called by His name. In this verse, the chief of each circle is called the Supreme Example. This holds true because, although they are not Allah, they possess all His powers.[^cite-5]

In the circles of believers, Allah appears through the Imam (*imām*, امام). In the circles of the Imamate itself, Allah appears through ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib (*mawlānā ʿAlī*, مولانا علی ابن ابی طالب). To understand ʿAlī, one must first understand the Imam, for the Imam exemplifies Allah in his circle, and ʿAlī exemplifies the Imam:

| Level of Understanding | Allah's Example | Allah Himself |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Imam's circle | ʿAlī | Supreme Reality |
| Believer's circle | Imam | ʿAlī |
| Summoner's circle | Daʿī (Summoner) | Imam |
| Unbeliever's circle | Believer | Daʿī |

The testimony of faith (*kalima lā ilāha illā-llāh*) represents another circle, wherein Allah remains the Supreme Example.

## The Outer and Inner Meanings of the Qur'an

Allah Almighty frequently mentions the outer and inner in the Qur'an, saying: "Allah has bestowed upon you His blessings, both apparent and hidden."

> ⟪quran 31:20⟫
> ⟪ar:‏أَلَمْ تَرَوْا۟ أَنَّ ٱللَّهَ سَخَّرَ لَكُم مَّا فِى ٱلسَّمَوَتِ وَمَا فِى ٱلْأَرْضِ وَأَسْبَغَ عَلَيْكُمْ نِعَمَهُۥ ظَهِرَةًۭ وَبَاطِنَةًۭ ۗ وَمِنَ ٱلنَّاسِ مَن يُجَدِلُ فِى ٱللَّهِ بِغَيْرِ عِلْمٍۢ وَلَا هُدًۭى وَلَا كِتَبٍۢ مُّنِيرٍۢ ‎⟫
> *See you not (O men) that Allah has subjected for you whatsoever is in the heavens and whatsoever is in the earth, and has completed and perfected His Graces upon you, (both) apparent and hidden? Yet of mankind is he who disputes about Allah without knowledge or guidance or a Book giving light!*

Then He says: "On that Day you shall be questioned about these blessings."

> ⟪quran 102:8⟫
> ⟪ar:‏ثُمَّ لَتُسْـَٔلُنَّ يَوْمَئِذٍ عَنِ ٱلنَّعِيمِ ‎⟫
> *Then, on that Day, you shall be asked about the delight (you indulged in, in this world)!*

This verse establishes that inner blessings exist as counterparts to outer blessings. These inner blessings are the Imams (*aʾimmat*, ائمہ); one shall be questioned regarding them—why were they denied? The proof lies in sacred numerology: the numerical value of *al-naʿīm* (the Bliss, النعیم) equals that of *sulaymānī* (Sulaimani, سلیمانی), both summing to 201, just as the numerical value of *Rabb* (Lord, رب) equals that of *Muḥammad + ʿAlī*, both 202.[^cite-6]

| Al-Bliss | Sulaimani |
| --- | --- |
| 201 | 201 |
| Lord | Muhammad + Ali |
| 202 | 202 |

The Messenger of Allah declared: "No verse of the Qur'an has been revealed to me without outer and inner meanings." Elsewhere He says: "Abandon both the outer and the inner sin!"

> ⟪quran 6:120⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَذَرُوا۟ ظَهِرَ ٱلْإِثْمِ وَبَاطِنَهُۥٓ ۚ إِنَّ ٱلَّذِينَ يَكْسِبُونَ ٱلْإِثْمَ سَيُجْزَوْنَ بِمَا كَانُوا۟ يَقْتَرِفُونَ ‎⟫
> *Leave (O mankind, all kinds of) sin, open and secret. Verily, those who commit sin will get due recompense for that which they used to commit.*

One must know the inner sin alongside the outer; whoever remains ignorant of inner transgression shall surely fall into it and deserve punishment in the afterlife. If outer sin means ingratitude for outward blessings, inner sin signifies denial of the inner blessing—the Imam. Then Allah declares:

> ⟪quran 3:7⟫
> ⟪ar:‏هُوَ ٱلَّذِىٓ أَنزَلَ عَلَيْكَ ٱلْكِتَبَ مِنْهُ آيَتٌۭ مُّحْكَمَتٌ هُنَّ أُمُّ ٱلْكِتَبِ وَأُخَرُ مُتَشَبِهَتٌۭ ۖ فَأَمَّا ٱلَّذِينَ فِى قُلُوبِهِمْ زَيْغٌۭ فَيَتَّبِعُونَ مَا تَشَبَّهَ مِنْهُ ٱبْتِغَآءَ ٱلْفِتْنَةِ وَٱبْتِغَآءَ تَأْوِيلِهِۦ ۗ وَمَا يَعْلَمُ تَأْوِيلَهُۥٓ إِلَّا ٱللَّهُ وَٱلرَّسُخُونَ فِى ٱلْعِلْمِ ۗ يَقُولُونَ آمَنَّا بِهِۦ كُلٌّۭ مِّنْ عِندِ رَبِّنَا ۗ وَمَا يَذَّكَّرُ إِلَّآ أُو۟لُوا۟ ٱلْأَلْبَبِ ‎⟫
> *It is He Who has sent down to you (Muhammad PBUH) the Book (this Quran). In it are Verses that are entirely clear, they are the foundations of the Book; and others not entirely clear. So as for those in whose hearts there is a deviation (from the truth) they follow that which is not entirely clear thereof, seeking Al-Fitnah (polytheism and trials, etc.), and seeking for its hidden meanings, but none knows its hidden meanings save Allah. And those who are firmly grounded in knowledge say: "We believe in it; the whole of it (clear and unclear Verses) are from our Lord." And none receive admonition except men of understanding.*

The Qur'an contains verses that are entirely clear—the established verses (*muḥkamāt*, محکمات), which form the foundation of the Scripture—and others whose meanings remain ambiguous to most—the allegorical verses (*mutashābihāt*, متشابھات). Their hidden meanings reside within the veils of language. Concerning these verses, Allah revealed: "None knows their true interpretation save Allah and those firmly rooted in knowledge (*al-rāsikhūn fī al-ʿilm*, الراسخون فی العلم)." These are the Imams of the People of the House (*ahl al-bayt*, أهل البيت).[^cite-7]

This stands among the supreme miracles of the Qur'an: though it possesses one apparent meaning, countless hidden meanings dwell within. Just as none but Muḥammad Muṣṭafā could expound the Qur'an's outward sense, none but the Imams of the House possess authority to unveil the inner meanings of these verses. They disclose these meanings to the deserving according to their capacity and withhold them from the unworthy, for this knowledge has been granted them by Allah:

> ⟪quran 38:39⟫
> ⟪ar:‏هَذَا عَطَآؤُنَا فَٱمْنُنْ أَوْ أَمْسِكْ بِغَيْرِ حِسَابٍۢ ‎⟫
> *[Saying of Allah to Sulaiman (Solomon)]: "This is Our gift, so spend you or withhold, no account will be asked."*

## Auspicious and Inauspicious Days

*(no content in source)*

Among the 354 days of the year, certain days and nights are exalted for believers. These apparent days correlate with the celestial spheres' rotation and the cycle of sunrise and sunset. The outer days possess no knowledge of themselves; they have no sense of their own fortune or misfortune, being compelled in their motion, not self-governing. Since they recognize not their own well-being, how could they bestow it upon others? Whence arises the virtue that some days are called "Days of Allah" and honored, while others are deemed ominous and accursed?

The nobility of these outer days proves itself only when joined with the inner days—when we understand that Allah's chosen ones (Prophets and Imams) are themselves the true Days of Allah, alive and sovereign, self-knowing. They themselves are auspicious (*saʿd*, سعد), understanding their own greatness. The outer fortunate days are fortunate because they point toward these inner fortunate beings. The outer days are examples; the inner beings are the exemplified. Similarly, inauspicious days signify unfortunate and ill-fated souls.

## The Pairing of the Year's Months

As with all creation, Allah created the months of the year in pairs. Each of the twelve months pairs with another: Muḥarram (the first month, thirty days) with Shawwāl (the tenth month, twenty-nine days); Ṣafar (the second, twenty-nine days) with Rajab (the seventh, thirty days), and so forth. The perfect months of thirty days represent the masculine principle (*soul*, nafs, روح); the imperfect months of twenty-nine represent the feminine principle (*body*, jism, جسم). The first day of each pair falls on the same weekday.

| Month | Days | First Day | Spouse Month | Days | First Day |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Muḥarram (1) | 30 | Sunday | Shawwāl (10) | 29 | Sunday |
| Ṣafar (2) | 29 | Tuesday | Rajab (7) | 30 | Tuesday |
| Rabīʿ al-Awwal (3) | 30 | Wednesday | Dhū al-Ḥijja (12) | 29 | Wednesday |
| Rabīʿ al-Ākhir (4) | 29 | Friday | Ramaḍān (9) | 30 | Friday |
| Jumādā al-Awwal (5) | 30 | Thursday | Shaʿbān (8) | 29 | Thursday |
| Jumādā al-Ākhir (6) | 29 | Monday | Dhū al-Qaʿda (11) | 30 | Monday |

### Jumādā al-Awwal and Shaʿbān al-Karīm

Among all month-pairs, two stand unique: Jumādā al-Awwal and Shaʿbān al-Karīm do not share the same first day with any other month, nor with each other. Though paired, they remain incompatible in this respect. Shaʿbān refers to the month of the Messenger of Allah; Jumādā al-Awwal refers to the month of our Lady Fāṭima (upon whom be peace)—her martyrdom occurred in Jumādā. The non-coincidence of their first days symbolizes that these two exalted beings are not found in any given moment throughout the year—they transcend ordinary temporal succession.

| Shaʿbān al-Karīm | Jumādā al-Awwal |
| --- | --- |
| The Messenger of Allah | Lady Fāṭima |

### Jumādā al-Awwal, Jumādā al-Ākhir, and Dhū al-Qaʿda

The fifth month is Jumādā al-Awwal; the sixth is Jumādā al-Ākhir. When five and six are summed, eleven emerges—the number of Dhū al-Qaʿda. The number eleven equals the numerical value of *hū* (He, هو), that mysterious name before which all intellects stand bewildered, unable to comprehend its majesty. The Messenger of Allah is the Eleventh Imam; the Mahdī (upon him be peace) is the Imam after him. The fact that these two months' first days do not coincide with all others points to a special affinity between them. Just as the Messenger brought Islam after the age of ignorance, so when the sixth Imam was born, the Mahdī established the completed Shariah of Islam.

| 5 | + | 6 | = | 11 |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Jumādā al-Awwal | | Jumādā al-Ākhir | | Dhū al-Qaʿda |
| ه (H) | | و (W) | | ي (Y) |
| | | Muḥammad Muṣṭafā | | Imām Mahdī |

When the twelve months divide in half, six remain in each half. The first half ends with Jumādā al-Ākhir (sixth month); the second begins with Rajab. These two—masculine (*hā*, ه) and feminine (*wāw*, و)—pair with each other through the letter *yā* (*y*, ي), which bridges them numerically: 5 + 6 = 11.

## The Superior and Inferior Regard in the Year

Of the 354 days in a year, one hundred possess special spiritual significance. Among these, thirty-six stand supreme. There exist two categories: the Superior Regard (*naẓar ashraf*, نظر اشرف) and the Inferior Regard (*naẓar adūn*, نظر ادون), each containing eighteen days.

Seventeen days fall fixed on this calendar, yet the date of each year shifts. This indicates that the divine authority (*ʿayn ʿaẓīma*, عین عظیمۃ) continually transforms. The Friday (*yawm al-jumʿa*, يوم الجمعۃ) signifies the veil of the Messenger through day; the night signifies the veil through the letter *M* of "Mary" (*Maryam*, مريم). ## The Two Dimensions of the Shariah

The Commander of the Faithful, ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib, declared: "Acquire understanding of the religion first; afterward, proceed to commerce." The world and religion are not separate entities but two forms of a single reality—religion constitutes the inner; the world constitutes the outer. The spiritual dimension of faith manifests only through material actions in the world, and religion's ultimate destination is reached only by walking the path of the world. For this reason, one must come to me and fulfill human needs for the establishment of the law.

Most imagine that only the prescribed acts (*aʿmāl sharʿiyya*, أعمال شریعت)—prayer, fasting, pilgrimage, struggle—constitute religious deed. This misconception errs fundamentally. Everything performed by Allah's command and His Messenger's guidance constitutes religious act: eating, drinking, sleeping, spending time with family, friends, and relatives. Even the etiquette of the toilet and the practice of marriage form part of the religion. The sole condition is that worldly work be undertaken from a religious perspective, aimed at obtaining Allah's pleasure.

Human soul and body are two forms of a single essence. The intellect (*ʿaql*, عقل) and the self (*nafs*, نفس) represent the subtle form; the body (*jism*, جسم) represents the dense form. Similarly, the subtle form of religion is called knowledge (*ʿilm*, علم); its dense form is called action (*ʿamal*, عمل). Allah divided His Shariah into two parts: one concerning the outward actions, which He entrusted to the Messenger of Allah; the other concerning the knowledge of interpretation, which He entrusted to ʿAlī. Yet both constitute two forms of the same light, as Muḥammad and ʿAlī are two parts of one luminescence. He rendered knowledge obligatory on the soul because it is subtle; He rendered action obligatory on the body because it is dense.

| | Subtle | Dense |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Religion | Knowledge | Action |
| Human Being | Soul | Body |

For this reason we intone in prayer: "I bring the subtle face-to-face with the subtle, and the dense face-to-face with the dense." That is, I draw the spiritual Kaʿba from my subtle self toward the physical Kaʿba through my dense body.[^cite-8]

The Messenger of Allah taught: "Should you desire success, take both religion and the world together, and balance them!" This is the meaning of taking knowledge and action jointly—the *mizān* (balance, میزان), the name of *taqwā* (God-consciousness, تقوی).

## The Speakers and the Difference Among Their Laws

Describing this philosophy, it is said: "From Adam to the Messenger of Allah, there have been six Speakers (*nāṭiq*, ناطق). The common understanding differs greatly from the true conception. People suppose that each prophet abrogated the law of his predecessor and instituted his own. The true understanding is that each Speaker preserved the Shariah of his predecessor and added his own Shariah to it, adapting it to changing circumstances and the needs arising in his era.

Adam brought forth the principle of Divine Guardianship (*wilāya*, ولایت). Noah, who came after him, preserved Adam's law and added purification (*ṭahāra*, طہارت) to it. In reality, as time advanced, wilāya addressed the problems that arose in purification. Noah's Shariah is in truth the interpretation of Adam's wilāya. Abraham came after him, preserving Noah's law and adding prayer (*ṣalāt*, صلوۃ) to it. Abraham's law represents the interpretation of Adam's wilāya and Noah's purification. Moses came after him, preserving Abraham's law and adding the alms-tax (*zakāt*, زکوۃ) to it. Moses' law constitutes the interpretation of wilāya, purification, and prayer. Jesus came after them, preserving Moses' law and adding fasting (*ṣawm*, صوم) to it. Jesus' law represents the interpretation of wilāya, purification, prayer, and zakat. Finally, Muḥammad Muṣṭafā, the Seal of the Prophets and Messengers (*khātim al-anbiyāʾ wa-l-mursalīn*, خاتم الانبیاء و المرسلین), came preserving Jesus' law and adding the pilgrimage (*ḥajj*, حج) to it. The Messenger's law is the interpretation of all five preceding Shariʿas—wilāya, purification, prayer, zakat, and fasting.

The Supreme Speaker learned the Shariah of the five preceding Speakers through five masters. Having mastered both the outer and inner aspects of their laws, he then instituted his Shariah, incorporating the outer and inner origins of all previous Shariʿas. As the Sixth Great Speaker, he established such a complete law that when the Seventh Speaker—our Master Muḥammad ibn Ismāʿīl—came, he did not codify his own Shariah but preserved and carried forward that of the Messenger. When the Qā'im—the Rising One—comes, he shall not alter the Messenger's Shariah but shall bring it to the far reaches of unveiling (*kashf*, کشف).

When the Qā'im arrives, he shall make obligatory upon believers the spiritual struggle (*jihād*, جہاد), which will be the interpretation of all preceding Speakers' laws. At that time, such a Shariah shall be established in which the two parts—action and knowledge—become unified, with no difference between outer and inner. Similarly, at the time of our physical death, religion and the world shall become one; no distinction shall remain between them. Each Speaker implemented the commandments of his Shariah. For this reason, the Commander of the Faithful, ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib, is called the "Partial Rising One" (*qāʾim juzʾī*, قائم جزوی), for he enacted the duty of spiritual struggle in reality—at Jamal, Ṣiffīn, and Nahrawān—and obligated the believers of his time to the same.[^cite-9]

| Abraham | Prayer | Interpretation of Wilāya + Purification |
| Moses | Alms-tax | Interpretation of Wilāya + Purification + Prayer |

## The Speaker and the Codification of the Sacred Law

One of the pillars of faith—the sturdiest of the Shariah's members—is to follow it. By means of the sacred law (*nāmūs*, ناموس), Allah commands and forbids. Its commands and prohibitions (*awāmir wa-nawāḥī*, أوامر و نواحی) rest always upon this foundation. The sacred law is the straight path whose end is Paradise; whoever follows it remains ever distant from transgression.

The most blessed and noble deed in Allah's sight, the most excellent of human acts, is that which only those of specific rank can perform—a rank bordering on angelic station. That is the station of the law-giver (*nāṭiq*, ناطق). In Allah's view, the formulation of the Shariah constitutes the highest and finest deed.[^cite-10]

Those who establish the Shariah, those who follow them, and those who adhere to the Sunnah after hearing the commands of the sacred law—their example resembles the sky, its rain, the earth, and vegetation. The Speaker resembles the word; the followers resemble those who hear the word and retain it, as the earth absorbs rainwater. The result arising from hearing and acting upon this word resembles vegetation—the benefit accruing from devotion and righteous deeds. Allah declares:

> ⟪quran 13:17⟫
> ⟪ar:‏أَنزَلَ مِنَ ٱلسَّمَآءِ مَآءًۭ فَسَالَتْ أَوْدِيَةٌۢ بِقَدَرِهَا فَٱحْتَمَلَ ٱلسَّيْلُ زَبَدًۭا رَّابِيًۭا ۚ وَمِمَّا يُوقِدُونَ عَلَيْهِ فِى ٱلنَّارِ ٱبْتِغَآءَ حِلْيَةٍ أَوْ مَتَعٍۢ زَبَدٌۭ مِّثْلُهُۥ ۚ كَذَلِكَ يَضْرِبُ ٱللَّهُ ٱلْحَقَّ وَٱلْبَطِلَ ۚ فَأَمَّا ٱلزَّبَدُ فَيَذْهَبُ جُفَآءًۭ ۖ وَأَمَّا مَا يَنفَعُ ٱلنَّاسُ فَيَمْكُثُ فِى ٱلْأَرْضِ ۚ كَذَلِكَ يَضْرِبُ ٱللَّهُ ٱلْأَمْثَالَ ‎⟫
> *He sends down water (rain) from the sky, and the valleys flow according to their measure, but the flood bears away the foam that mounts up to the surface, and (also) from that (ore) which they heat in the fire in order to make ornaments or utensils, rises a foam like unto it, thus does Allah (by parables) show forth truth and falsehood. Then, as for the foam it passes away as scum upon the banks, while that which is for the good of mankind remains in the earth. Thus Allah sets forth parables (for the truth and falsehood, i.e. Belief and disbelief).*

The sky represents the Prophet; the water signifies the Qur'an, Allah's word. The valleys receiving this water represent the knowledge of the Qur'an (*ʿilm al-qurʾān*, علم القرآن) spreading to all believers. When the valleys flood, abundant foam rises to the surface—those who seize only the words of the Qur'an, omitting their meaning. They resemble foam, separated from the believers through the hypocrisy, doubt, and confusion of their hearts. Allah then says: "There is another foam arising from the heating of these precious metals in fire." These are the people who, following the commandments of the Shariah, burn their bodies in the fire of the law, removing foam from their souls and bodies through action (*ʿamal*, عمل). Thus Allah distinguishes between truth and falsehood. The foam upon the water's surface dies and gathers at the edges, separating from the water. This foam—knowledge fraught with doubt—resembles a corpse; the dead create nothing. Such barren knowledge cannot fashion the soul. The beneficial things remain within the earth. The deep inner meaning of the Qur'an dwells in the hearts of believers and the righteous, from whom wisdom derives. Thus Allah declares:

> ⟪quran 14:24⟫
> ⟪ar:‏أَلَمْ تَرَ كَيْفَ ضَرَبَ ٱللَّهُ مَثَلًۭا كَلِمَةًۭ طَيِّبَةًۭ كَشَجَرَةٍۢ طَيِّبَةٍ أَصْلُهَا ثَابِتٌۭ وَفَرْعُهَا فِى ٱلسَّمَآءِ ‎⟫
> *See you not how Allah sets forth a parable? - A goodly word as a goodly tree, whose root is firmly fixed, and its branches (reach) to the sky (i.e. very high).*

The parable of this good word of wisdom resembles a good tree whose root is firmly established in the earth and whose branches ascend toward heaven.

## Esoteric Exegesis and the Circles of the Exemplified

The Messenger of Allah declared: "Allah has founded His religion upon the example of His creation, so that one might establish proof of the religion through the world, and of the world through the religion." That is, Allah created the world as example (*mithāl*, مثال) and religion as the exemplified (*mumthal*, ممثول). Understanding the meeting of the exemplified through the example is called *taʾwīl* (esoteric exegesis, تاویل). In other terms, searching for the non-material (*ʿālam ghayri-l-mādda*, غیر مادیت) through the material realm (*ʿālam al-mādda*, مادیت) is interpretation.

Allah created religion first and established the world as its example. The example is visible; the exemplified remains unseen. Yet through the example, the exemplified becomes known—as the soul, though invisible, reveals itself through bodily movement. Were the exemplified not to exist, Allah would not have created its example. Wood exists, therefore a chair may be fashioned; without wood, nothing could be made.

### Why the Circles of Examples Arise

This necessity arises from *tawḥīd* (divine unity, توحید). Since we cannot access the Creator directly, we gain access through His manifestations—the exemplified beings—from which the circles commence. Examples are signs of our humility. Unable to comprehend the Creator, we strive to understand His signs. Once we understand them, we attempt to understand Muḥammad and ʿAlī. Thus does the ascent continue.

When a stone falls into water, concentric circles emanate from the point of impact. Truth—the singular reality—resides at the stone itself. The surrounding circles reveal this single truth's existence; they are called examples of this truth and serve as means to reach it. The outermost circle possesses the least force and greatest diameter. As one moves inward through the circles, the truth's manifestations grow denser and fewer until reaching the central truth (the stone) itself. Beyond this point, one cannot advance.

The circle to which our gaze becomes fixed—and wherein our understanding weakens—becomes truth (*ḥaqq*, حق) for us. All circles beneath it become examples (*mumthal*, ممثول) of that truth for us. Should our sight fix upon the seventh circle, it becomes truth for us, and the sixth circle and all inner circles become examples of that seventh circle. Interpretation is the endeavor to reach truths.

It is said: "Interpretation is a bridge to truths." Through interpretation, one reaches the truths which constitute the true destination. Once the bridge is crossed, it is no longer needed. Similarly, once a thing's truth is known, interpretive language becomes unnecessary.

Just as these circles outwardly acquaint us with the spot where the stone fell, so examples acquaint us with their exemplified. Were there no exemplified, the example would not exist, as without a stone falling into water, no circles would form. Examples are the names of interpretation's circles. *Taʾwīl* is the knowledge of the beings and limits created by the Exalted Creator—knowing their ranks and making them wilāya, and in comparison to them—comprehending the Creator's divine unity—is truth. The further we proceed beyond interpretation's circles, the nearer we draw to the Creator—or Truth.

In the era of the Pure One (*dūr ṭayyib*, دور طیبی), sciences that were truths in the era of the Rising One (*dūr qāʾimī*, دور قائمی) hold the status of interpretation today. What is presently understood is called interpretation; what remains to be understood and pursued is called truth.

True worship consists in humility. There comes a time when, from our humility, we are forced to adopt *sukūt* (silence, سکوت). Where silence begins, examples commence. We cannot reach Allah; therefore, we resort to His signs and Names. Although worship is solely for Allah, we worship Him only through these examples.[^cite-11]
