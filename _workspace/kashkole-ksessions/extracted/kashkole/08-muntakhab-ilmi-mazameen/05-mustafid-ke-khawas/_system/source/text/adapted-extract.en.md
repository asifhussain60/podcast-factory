# Beneficiary Properties

## How the Beneficiary Should Conduct Himself

A beneficiary must first recognize a fundamental truth: without the kindness and guidance of his mentor, he can never advance in the ranks of knowledge. He should acknowledge with full conviction the greatness both of his mentor's useful knowledge and his unshakeable faith. More than this, he must confess that his mentor's usefulness exceeds even that knowledge and faith. Every subordinate, likewise, must remain loyal to the wisdom of his superiors and obedient to their words, accepting what he is told regardless of how strange or paradoxical it may appear to his immediate understanding.

It frequently occurs that a teaching seems profoundly strange or even erroneous to the beneficiary. The reason lies in this: there exists within the beneficiary's intellect a form of knowledge that does not yet exist within him, or there is some deficiency in his grasp of the matter. In such circumstances, the beneficiary should set aside his own belief and, recognizing the intellectual greatness of his mentor whom he has already acknowledged, he should surrender completely to what has been said, accepting this apparently unknowable truth and enduring his own ignorance with patience. Should he fail to do so, doubt will confuse him; but if he perseveres, then one day through the teaching of his mentor he will generate this unknown form within his own soul, and it will become manifest reality. When the beneficiary learns something new from his mentor, he must confess it with his tongue and confirm it in his heart, so that the knowledge received through his ear becomes absorbed into his very thought.[^cite-1]

Just as children revere only their parents, soldiers fear only their commanders, and all men fear the Sultan who holds power over their profits and losses—so too do the angels fear only Allah. In precisely this manner, a beneficiary fears only his own mentor. This fear arises from his dependence upon his mentor for the nourishment of his intellectual form: should that mentor be lost or displeased, all channels of his spiritual development would be severed.

This fear increases along with knowledge. For this reason, the more deeply the angels come to know Allah, the more their fear and their longing increase.[^cite-2] It was for this reason that Mawlānā ʿAlī Zayn al-ʿĀbidīn (the Fifth Imam), when he performed ablution, trembled like a leaf. And when he came to the Black Stone (Ḥajar al-Aswad) in the sacred precinct, he resolved to pronounce the invocation "Labbayk" ("Here I am"). But he trembled with such fear of Allah that he fainted. When he recovered, and the people asked him the reason for this overwhelming fear, he said: "When I was about to say 'Labbayk,' I became afraid that if Allah answered, 'There is no response for you'—what would become of me at that moment?"

When an Arab asked the Prophet Muhammad ⟪ar:رسول اللہ (ﷺ)⟫ (upon him be peace and blessings), "O Messenger of Allah, what is ⟪ar:'أِحسان'⟫ (spiritual excellence)?" he replied: ⟪ar-quote:الأحساَنُ اَنَّ تَعبُدُ اللّٰہ کَأَنَّکَ تَراَہُ فَاألَّم تَکُن تَراَہُ فَاِنَّہُ یَرَاکَ⟫ "Spiritual excellence is that you worship Allah as though you see Him; and if you cannot see Him, know that He sees you."[^cite-3] True observation of the Divine requires that a person see no one in this world except Allah. The observer is called the one who researches all things with utter sincerity. The scholar merely observes; the investigator (muḥaqqiq) perceives from reality itself.

## The Beneficiary's Contemplation and Questions

When something becomes clear to a beneficiary—when it settles securely within his thought and essence—then he should investigate it carefully and ask penetrating questions about its nuances and details. He should seek proofs and arguments from his mentor. That is: first, he must place his trust in his mentor, accept his word, and act upon it. And when that action becomes woven into the fabric of his being, only then should he contemplate it and discover what secrets and mysteries lie hidden behind it. Once he has embarked upon the path of proof and argument, he should not turn back; that is, it would be inappropriate to fall into doubt again after confirmation. He should regard the fine points of everything through the eye of his heart without blind imitation, and strive to attain them through the light of reason. Let him awaken his slumbering soul through these sciences; when he does, his life will become a life of ease and comfort. He will gain knowledge from his Lord such that he can penetrate the heavens themselves and know the kingdom and its secrets within. A sign that a beneficiary is truly investigative is that he never ceases his research.

The beneficiary must not only confirm the knowledge (ḫabar, the report) but also confirm both the informant (muḫbir, the one who conveys) and the station (maqām, the rank) from which he has received it.[^cite-4] That is, in addition to affirming your mentor, you must also testify to the truthfulness and the spiritual station of that mentor.

### Importance of Reflection and Questions

Whatever practice of the Divine Law (Sharīʿa) is undertaken—whether it be prayer, recitation of the Qur'ān, or any other act—it must be performed with complete presence of heart and mind. Mawlānā ʿAlī says: ⟪ar-quote:مَن اَوفاَ اَستَوفاَ⟫ "Whoever fulfills [his obligation] receives fulfillment in return."[^cite-5] That is, the greater one's exertion, the greater the knowledge one attains. The Prophet (upon him be peace and blessings) said: "One hour of your contemplation is more valuable than fifty years of your worship." ʿAlī (upon him be peace) said: "Recite the Qur'ān in such a way that when the verse of glad tidings comes, your heart will be gladdened by it, and when the verse of punishment comes, your body will tremble with fear of Allah (ḫašyat Allāh)." This becomes possible only when the beneficiary's perception penetces the depths of his inner self by piercing through the veils of outward appearance.

Contemplation generates questions, and questions become the means of elevating the beneficiary's knowledge. The Prophet (upon him be peace and blessings) said: ⟪ar-quote:حُسنُ السُّؤاَل نِصفُ الجواب⟫ "A well-posed question is half the answer in itself."[^cite-6] That is, the beneficiary should endeavor to gather information about a matter before asking his question of his mentor, so that the mentor need not exert great effort to provide an answer. Imām Jaʿfar al-Ṣādiq (upon him be peace) said: ⟪ar-quote:سَلْ سَلْ حَتّٰی یُقاَلَّکَ اِنَّکَ لمَجْنُون⟫ "Ask questions, ask questions, until people begin to say of you that you have lost your mind!"[^cite-7]

Once, a person asked Amīr al-Muʾminīn ʿAlī (upon him be peace): "O ʿAlī, what did you do when you were in the company of the Messenger of Allah?" He replied: "When the Messenger of Allah (upon him be peace and blessings) spoke, I would listen in complete silence; and when he fell silent, I would ask him questions."

## The Effect of Knowledge Upon the Beneficiary's Soul

Every creature needs nourishment to survive; for the animal soul (nafs al-ḥassāsa), that nourishment is knowledge. Just as the body grows weak without outward sustenance, so too the animal powers of the soul (nafs al-ḥassāsa) and its celestial dimensions (malakūtiyya) become weak without knowledge, and its carnal and irascible faculties (al-šahwānī wa-l-ġaḍabī) awaken, rendering it susceptible to sin.[^cite-8] The Messenger of Allah (upon him be peace and blessings) said: "If a person is deprived of the company of a scholar for forty days, a traveler comes into his heart and he begins to dare toward his soul's greater sins (kabāʾir)." This is because the company of scholars is a light that weakens his animal powers and instills within him a reverent fear, which increases his desire for knowledge.

The ardor of a beneficiary elevates his knowledge, and ardor does not arise save through the help of Allah. Acquiring knowledge is not an easy task; rather, it is exceedingly difficult. For this reason, the elevation of ranks depends upon knowledge, not upon mere action. In order to acquire knowledge, a person must sacrifice many of his legitimate desires and turn his face away from the pleasures of the world toward the pursuit of knowledge.

## The Pure and the Deficient Beneficiary

A pure beneficiary might occupy himself with worldly affairs, rest, or satisfy himself by expending that time in trivial pursuits. Instead, however, in order to please his Lord and to develop his soul, he binds himself with the bonds of the Hereafter and devotes himself to the worship of knowledge. This act increases the pleasure of Allah and earns the forgiveness and reward of the Divine.

Conversely, there is the deficient beneficiary who acknowledges the greatness of divine sciences and expresses with his tongue his desire to acquire them, yet his actions bear no witness to this passion. Such a person spends his days worshipping his own self through his actions, while expressing (merely outwardly) his passion for knowledge before people. He plots against Allah, and Allah plots against him, binding him all the more firmly in the snares of the world. This is why Allah says (in the interpretation of the meaning):

> ⟪quran 3:54⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَمَكَرُوا۟ وَمَكَرَ ٱللَّهُ ۖ وَٱللَّهُ خَيْرُ ٱلْمَكِرِينَ ‎⟫
> *And they plotted, and Allah plotted. And Allah is the best of plotters.*

Allah is angered with such a beneficiary and says: "You have reserved time in the day for your pleasures and diversions, but you have cast My worship behind you! Therefore I shall not look upon you on the Day of Judgment!" (We seek refuge in Allah from this.) Allah further says (in the interpretation of the meaning):

> ⟪quran 2:9⟫
> ⟪ar:‏يُخَدِعُونَ ٱللَّهَ وَٱلَّذِينَ آمَنُوا۟ وَمَا يَخْدَعُونَ إِلَّآ أَنفُسَهُمْ وَمَا يَشْعُرُونَ ‎⟫
> *They seek to deceive Allah and those who believe, but they deceive only themselves, and they are not aware.*[^cite-9]

These people attempt to deceive others by expressing their passion, but in reality they deceive no one save themselves—yet they are not conscious of it. The example of this is the human body's constant need for physical and material food day and night; if it fails to receive food at any time, it feels the lack, becomes restless and anxious, and dies without it. In the same way, the soul too needs spiritual nourishment—that is, knowledge—but should it fail to acquire knowledge, it does not perceive the ruin and decay of its own being. There is no pain in being deaf to knowledge throughout one's life, for the soul does not display its anguish and restlessness as the body does; thus a person remains heedless and indifferent. The feeling and awareness of this loss arrives only when the hour of descent comes and death approaches to seize the soul.

The wise say that human life passes between movement and stillness. One moves in one's youth, laboring ever harder in order to find peace in old age. That is, today's action leads to tomorrow's tranquility. For this reason the beneficiary moves in this world, suffers, and abandons his desires so that his soul may find peace in the Hereafter. Similarly, the person who fulfills his day's duties well sleeps peacefully at night, with no anxiety about tomorrow, having made proper arrangements. In the same way, a good movement in life brings a happy and peaceful death. And a happy death can be achieved only through knowledge.

Through worldly knowledge, a person lives a successful life in this world and uses it as a means to save himself and his family from poverty and destitution. In the same way, if he wishes to improve his life in the Hereafter, he must acquire the knowledge of the Hereafter. If he does not do so, he will live in poverty and destitution in the Hereafter, dependent upon the favors and blessings of others for all his life.

## Thirst and Fear

Thirst for knowledge increases the beneficiary's demand for knowledge. When his thirst reaches its peak, no force in the world and no preoccupation can stop him from acquiring knowledge. His example is like a man whose physical thirst makes him stand by a river. When he sees the waves of water, he fears in his heart lest the river's waves drown and destroy him, and his worldly life be ruined on account of it. That is, the beneficiary fears that should he engage in religious education, he will be deprived of the pleasures of the world. This fear prevents him from descending into the river.

But when a beneficiary's thirst reaches its peak and his suffering becomes extreme, and he cries out with the thirst (al-Atš al-Atš—the burning thirst), his thirst overcomes his fear and he leaps into the river to save his life. At that moment he does not think of drowning himself, for he is certain that if he does not jump into the water, he will surely perish of thirst. Similarly, the beneficiary who is certain of the destruction of his own soul leaps into the river of monotheism and knowledge without any fear, and no worldly excuse stands in his way. Allah grants this thirsty person to drink from His Pool of Kawthar in such a way that he never becomes thirsty again.

The mentor creates and increases thirst in the beneficiary so that he may come to know himself—so that he might abandon the edge of duality and move toward tomorrow's water, that he might give up the shore of separation and quench his thirst by leaping into the river of unity. And this leap can be taken only when thirst within the beneficiary is greater than fear! Thirst has caused him to suffer, and he cries out, "Water, water!"

### Journey to the Inner Self

Like worldly education, religious education has its own stages. The beneficiary is initially the child of the school and gradually rises through academic ranks, passing through various examinations. In worldly education, it is possible to achieve a certain degree of fulfillment such that the student stops at a particular station and reaches his destination. But on the path of religion, all are travelers and wayfarers, for this path never ends. Every stage is followed by a new stage, and after each level comes another. To continue upon this path, to persevere in prayer and petition, is the true destination. All knowledge and action depend upon intention (nīya). If the intention is sincere and thirst is free from fear, then no obstacle can remain in the way of a beneficiary!

## The Difference Between a Student and a Beneficiary

There is a profound difference between a student and a beneficiary. The student desires only what he does not desire to change within himself; his knowledge brings no benefit to his soul. His example is that of a candle, which derives no benefit from its own light and spends its entire life in darkness beneath the glow it produces. Spirituality cannot be learned or read; spirituality can only be embodied and lived. The student does not wish to undertake the journey of the inner self—he merely wishes to acquire its knowledge. He does not wish to become a cobbler; he merely wishes to know how shoes are made. Knowledge alone! But the beneficiary asks: "How can I transform this knowledge? How can I become a being? How can I descend into essence?" This desire for transformation makes the beneficiary's knowledge beneficial to his soul, and upon his death, that knowledge is transfigured into a noble form.

The true purpose of knowledge is to transform the essence—to transform physicality into spirituality.

## The Beneficiary's Migration

When the Prophet (upon him be peace and blessings) came with the message of Islam and invited the people of Mecca, the Meccans became his enemies and began to oppress him and his Companions. He preached Islam for thirteen years amid great difficulty. When the oppression became excessive, by the command of Allah he departed with his Companions from the Mighty Sanctuary (Makka al-Muʿaẓẓama) to the Illuminated City (al-Madīna al-Munawwara)—an event called the Migration (al-Ḥijra). When he arrived in Medina, the people of Medina treated him with immense love and affection. They sacrificed their lives and wealth for him and offered such an example of devotion as has no parallel in the history of the world. There are two epochs in Islam: one before the Migration and one after it.

Just as the Messenger of Allah (upon him be peace and blessings) left the people of evil and migrated to the people of goodness, so too is it incumbent upon every beneficiary to abandon his evils and migrate toward the good, to leave the darkness of ignorance and incline toward the light of knowledge, and to emerge from the bondage of arrogance and passion into the realm of humility. Through this spiritual migration, he transforms himself, and two epochs are established in his life: one the history in which he was surrounded by darkness, and the other the history after his spiritual exile, in which through knowledge and action he is transformed and attains higher ranks.

He lives the life of this world like a traveler. When a traveler journeys to a place, he believes that after a few days he must depart from this temporary abode and return to his homeland. Therefore he spends those few days in this place with courtesy, performing his necessary tasks. And he departs without taking offense from anyone or causing harm to anyone. The traveler does not quarrel during his travels but remains respectful and courteous among the people. This spiritual migration of the beneficiary requires not a physical journey but a transformation of thoughts and temperaments; he must break the bonds of nature. And this spiritual migration is far more difficult than outward migration.

## The Beneficiary's Question and the Mentor's Answer

When a beneficiary asks a good question, sometimes his intellect supposes it has conceived an excellent question, and he feels pride in his wisdom. Similarly, when a mentor provides a good answer, he may feel pride in his own ability. But in truth, the beneficiary's question arises from the stirring (taḥrīk) of the Imam. That is, when the Imam (upon him be peace) wishes to impart knowledge to someone, he stirs that person to ask. Similarly, when the mentor answers this question, it is the enabling grace (tawfīq) of the Imam (upon him be peace) that allows the answer to be given. In the end, all praise returns to the Imam (upon him be peace), for if it were not for his stirring, no beneficiary would have been able to ask the question, and if it were not for his enabling grace, no one would have been able to give a worthy answer.[^cite-10]

| Element | Agent | Source |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Question | Beneficiary | Imam's Stirring |
| Answer | Mentor | Imam's Enabling Grace |

When a scholarly gathering is established and teaching continues, the gathering is composed of beneficiaries who learn from the mentor. When they wish to ask a question, one of the attendees poses it, and the mentor responds.

When a scholar or preacher asks a question and then answers it himself, that question actually pertains to the context of the verses included in that lesson. The scholar or preacher therefore first poses the question and then provides the answer.

## From Whom Should Questions Be Asked?

Allah says in the Qur'ān:

> ⟪quran 21:7⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَمَآ أَرْسَلْنَا قَبْلَكَ إِلَّا رِجَالًۭا نُّوحِىٓ إِلَيْهِمْ ۖ فَسْـَٔلُوٓا۟ أَهْلَ ٱلذِّكْرِ إِن كُنتُمْ لَا تَعْلَمُونَ ‎⟫
> *And We sent not before you except men to whom We revealed. Ask the people of the Reminder if you do not know.*

This verse refers to the Immaculate Imams (al-Aʾimma al-Ṭāhirīn), and "the Reminder" refers to the word of Allah or of the Messenger (upon him be peace and blessings). By this verse, Allah has divided the world into two parts: one part is the Imams—the people of the Reminder—and the other part is all those who do not know.

| The People of the Reminder | Those Who Do Not Know |
| --- | --- |
| The Immaculate Imams | All others |

Yet Allah does not state anywhere in this verse that the Imams are obligated to answer; rather, He made it obligatory for the world to ask questions, but He did not impose upon the Imams the obligation to answer.

A man once asked Imām al-Ḥusayn (upon him be peace) one hundred questions in a single gathering. The Imām answered all of them immediately and in the exact order in which they had been posed. A true Imam must possess four qualities; otherwise he is not a true Imam.

| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Knowledge | Generosity | Courage | Justice |

## The Great Kindness of My Mentor Upon Me

Every work from beginning to end passes through many stages. The initial stages are called the preliminary aspects (al-awwaliyyāt), and the later stages are called the final aspects (al-uḫrawiyyāt). Efforts and struggles are made at every stage of this work in order to achieve the true purpose of that work. Should that true purpose not be achieved, all effort is wasted.

Consider the farmer who places a grain of wheat into the earth. From that moment until the formation of human nourishment—which is its true purpose—many circumstances pass over this grain. First the grain is buried in the ground; then it breaks through the earth; then it grows slowly and becomes a plant; then it becomes a stalk; then it is reaped and ground into flour; then it is leavened; then it is cooked and becomes bread and reaches humanity; then teeth chew it into food; then the body takes it within itself and transforms it into pure blood; then it takes the form of bodily organs; and finally it becomes part of the human body.

At every one of these degrees and circumstances, the wheat erased its previous form and assumed another. Every transformation required a cause and agent. The grain was dependent upon the farmer until it became a plant, then upon a merchant to sell it, then upon a miller to grind it, then upon fire to cook it, until it reached a human in a state acceptable to him. Only then did it finally become his nourishment. Reaching humanity and becoming his sustenance was the true object of the wheat's development and the final destination of its long journey. But to arrive there, it had to pass through several stages, and required causes to advance through each. The grain itself had no power to develop itself from one level to another.

Now consider: when that grain of wheat reached the human body and became blood, and that blood ascended to the faculty of imagination (quwwat al-mutaḫayyila), it became transformed into a different state. Until that moment it had changed its form, but only outwardly. Now it possessed the ability to absorb the forms and images of all worlds through imagination. Until then it had been absorbed in others; now it developed the power to integrate others into itself.

The esteemed Manṣūb Ḍiyāʾ ʿAlī ibn the blessed Isḥāq explained that the mirror possesses the ability to absorb all shapes that come before it—all those shapes are visible in this single mirror, and this is the state of the greatest reality: that though it is one, it merges many luminous forms within itself. This is the condition of our faculty of imagination—it can simultaneously encompass many different concepts and draw them into its grasp. Thus the grain, imprisoned in the physical world of form in its original state, when its imaginative power is transformed, becomes like a mirror, now able to integrate other forms and different concepts.[^cite-11]

The example of this grain is that of the beneficiary in his initial stage: he is in need of causes, which his mentor provides. The mentor draws him from one level and elevates him to another where he receives yet another mentor. Like the grain of wheat, the beneficiary dies from one stage to the next, achieving a better state through the erasure of his previous condition. The mentor at each level prepares him for the next, until he develops to the point where he now possesses the ability to be a mentor to others. Until then, others had been his limits—his form was absorbed in theirs—but now he himself becomes the limit and can integrate the forms of others into himself.[^cite-12] In this way, growing through his mentor like the grain of wheat, he becomes the nourishment of his true human being—the Imam (upon him be peace)—and reaches his divinity, which is his final and true destination. After reaching this station, his condition does not change again, for he exists now in the divinity of the Imam of the Age in the sphere of the Throne.

The development of this grain of wheat was possible only because it completely surrendered itself to its causes and agents. It accepted quietly the influences of those who acted upon it, whether the burden of being buried in the earth, the harshness of the seasons, the blow of the sickle, the grinding of the mill, the heat of the fire, or the cutting of the teeth. It made no resistance to any of these influences. It erased its own will and adopted the will of its agents; it made itself the object of action, and it was this very status as object that led to its progress. Had it not done so, it would never have risen above its original rank. Similarly, unless the beneficiary surrenders himself to his mentor, does not remove the crown of self-assertion (al-anā) from his head and place it at the feet of his mentor, erases his own being, desires, and hopes, and submits to the strictest commands of his mentor, his development will not be possible. Allah says (in the interpretation of the meaning):

> ⟪quran 7:40⟫
> ⟪ar:‏إِنَّ ٱلَّذِينَ كَذَّبُوا۟ بِـَٔايَتِنَا وَٱسْتَكْبَرُوا۟ عَنْهَا لَا تُفَتَّحُ لَهُمْ أَبْوَبُ ٱلسَّمَآءِ وَلَا يَدْخُلُونَ ٱلْجَنَّةَ حَتَّىٰ يَلِجَ ٱلْجَمَلُ فِى سَمِّ ٱلْخِيَاطِ ۚ وَكَذَلِكَ نَجْزِى ٱلْمُجْرِمِينَ ‎⟫
> *Indeed, those who deny Our signs and are arrogant against them—the gates of heaven shall not be opened for them, nor shall they enter Paradise until the camel passes through the eye of the needle. Thus do We recompense the guilty.*

Note well this additional point: had that grain of wheat reached humanity in its unrefined state, the human would never have taken it into his nourishment and made it his body. In that condition, the existence of the grain and all efforts in its development would have been wasted, for it would not have achieved the true purpose of its creation. It is evident, therefore, that the gratitude owed by the grain to its causes and agents is immense—they give it proper care and oversight, transforming it into a state that renders it acceptable to the human temperament.

In the same way, were our Imam (upon him be peace) to come to him in a state full of sins and defects, the Imam would never draw such a defective form into his divinity. In such a circumstance, the purpose of our creation would be lost, and both our life and death would be ruined. Therefore, it is a supreme blessing to us from our beneficial religion, our prayers, and all the limits of the All-Mighty—that they refine our form and transform it into a state such that our form can merge with that of the Imam (upon him be peace). For this reason the Prophet (upon him be peace and blessings) said: "Were it permissible to prostrate to anyone other than Allah, I would have commanded women to prostrate to their husbands"—that is, I would have commanded the beneficiary to obey what is beneficial to him.

May Allah enable us all to sacrifice our self-esteem, our desires, and our hopes for the sake of our elders and mentors, without whose guidance our salvation is not possible. Āmīn wa-āmīn.
