# Beneficiary Properties

## How a Beneficiary Should Conduct Himself

A beneficiary must first grasp a fundamental truth: without the kindness and favour of his mentor, he will never advance through the ranks of knowledge. He should acknowledge the vastness of his mentor's useful wisdom and his encompassing faith, and confess that the mentor's usefulness surpasses his own in both knowledge and faith. In the same manner, every student must remain steadfast in reverence toward his teacher's knowledge and utterances, accepting what he is instructed without demur, however strange it may initially appear.

It sometimes occurs that a teaching seems peculiar or erroneous to the student. This arises because a form of knowledge—⟪ar:ھیولی علم⟫—exists in the teacher's self that does not yet exist in the student's understanding, or the student harbours a misconception about the matter. In such circumstances, the student should relinquish his own conviction. Acknowledging the intellectual greatness of his mentor—which he has already recognised—he should trust completely in what the mentor has said, affirm this seemingly obscure truth, and endure his present ignorance with patience. Should he fail to do so, doubt will confound him; but if he perseveres, that very knowledge will eventually crystallise within his own soul through the mentor's continued teaching, transforming from abstract notion into living reality. When a student receives new knowledge from his mentor, he should confess it with his tongue and confirm it with his heart, so that what his ear has heard becomes absorbed into his reflection.

Just as children fear only their parents, soldiers fear only their commanders, and all persons fear their Sultan—who holds power over their fortunes—so too do the angels fear only Allah. In the same way, a beneficiary fears only his mentor. This fear derives from the student's dependence upon his mentor for the nourishment of his intellectual form; should the mentor be lost or displeased, all progress in his development will cease.[^cite-1]

This fear increases together with ⟪ar:خشیت اللہ⟫ (fear of God) and knowledge itself. For this reason, the more the angels gain in knowledge of Allah, the more their reverence and solicitude intensifies. For this reason, ⟪ar:مولانا علی زین العابدین⟫ (our Master ʿAlī, Zayn al-ʿĀbidīn) trembled like a leaf when performing ablution. And when he came to the station of the Black Stone—⟪ar:مولانا علی ابن ابی طالب علیہ السلام، حجر اسود⟫ (our Master ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib, upon him be peace, at the Black Stone)—he resolved to utter the words ⟪ar:'لب بیک'⟫ (*"Here I am"*). Yet he trembled with awe of Allah and swooned. When he returned to consciousness, those present asked him the cause of this dread. He replied: "When I was about to say ⟪ar:لب بیک⟫ (*'Here I am'*), I grew fearful: what if Allah were to answer, '*⟪ar:'لا لبیک لَہُ'⟫* (You have no one but Me)?' What would become of me then?"[^cite-2]

⟪ar:رسول اللہ (ﷺ)⟫ (the Messenger of Allah, may God's prayers and peace be upon him) was asked by an Arab, "O Messenger of Allah, what is ⟪ar:' أِحسان '⟫ (kindness)?" He responded with the sacred tradition: *"Kindness is that you worship Allah as though you see Him; and if you do not see Him, know that He sees you."* To witness from the truth means that a person should see no one in this world save Allah. It is said that ⟪ar:'مُحَقِّق'⟫ (the investigator) derives his name from inquiring into all things. The ⟪ar:عالم⟫ (*scholar*) merely observes, while the ⟪ar:محقق⟫ (*investigator*) observes from the very heart of reality.[^cite-3]

## The Reflection and Questions of a Beneficiary

Once a beneficiary has secured trust in his mentor regarding ⟪ar:'فکر'⟫ (thought) and ⟪ar:'ذات'⟫ (essence), he should then investigate and pose questions concerning the nuances and subtle details of that truth, seeking rational arguments and proofs from his mentor. That is to say, he should first place his complete trust in his mentor, accept his utterance, and act upon it. Once that action becomes woven into his very essence, only then should he reflect upon it and uncover what mysteries and secrets lie hidden beneath that action. Once he has set foot upon the path of argument and proof, it is not fitting for him to retreat. Rather, it is altogether unseemly to sow doubt anew after confirmation has been achieved. He should examine the subtleties of all things with the eyes of his heart, without blind imitation of others, and strive to attain understanding through the illumination of reason. Let him awaken his dormant soul from the torpor of these sciences. When he does, his life becomes one of true flourishing. He will possess knowledge from his Lord that pierces the veil of the heavens, revealing the kingdom and its hidden mysteries. The mark of ⟪ar:مستفیدِ محقق⟫ (a beneficiary who investigates) is that he is forever engaged in rigorous inquiry.

The beneficiary must not only affirm the ⟪ar:خَبَر⟫ (knowledge transmitted) but must equally confirm the ⟪ar:مُخبِر⟫ (one who transmits) and must verify the station (*maqām*) from which the ⟪ar:مخبر⟫ (transmitter) has received that ⟪ar:خبر⟫ (knowledge). That is, beyond confirming your mentor alone, you must also remain faithful to the knowledge and faith of that very mentor himself.

### Importance of Reflection and Questions

Whatever practice of the sacred law is undertaken—whether prayer, recitation of the Qur'an, or any other deed—it must be performed with complete presence of heart. Maulana ⟪ar:امیر المومنین علیہ السلام⟫ (Commander of the Faithful, upon him be peace) has said: *"He who is faithful is faithful to him."* That is, the greater one's sincere striving, the greater the knowledge one attains. The Prophet (may God's prayers and peace be upon him) declared: *"One hour of your contemplation surpasses fifty years of your worship."* ʿAlī, upon him be peace, said: *"Recite the Qur'an in such a manner that when a verse of glad tidings comes, your heart becomes joyful and content with it; and when a verse of punishment comes, your body trembles with* ⟪ar:خَشیَتُ اللہ⟫ *(fear of God)."* This becomes possible only when the beneficiary's gaze penetrates the depths of the inner self by piercing through the outer veils.[^cite-4]

Contemplation and questioning together raise inquiry, which becomes a means of elevating the knowledge of the beneficiary. The Prophet (may God's prayers and peace be upon him) said: *"A good question is half the answer in itself."* That is, before asking a question of his mentor, the beneficiary should endeavour to gather information himself, so that the mentor need not expend great effort in answering. ⟪ar:امام جعفر الصادق⟫ (Imam Jaʿfar al-Ṣādiq, upon him be peace) declared: *"Ask questions, ask questions until people begin to say of you that you are mad!"*[^cite-5]

Once, when ⟪ar:امیر المومنین علیہ السلام⟫ (Commander of the Faithful, upon him be peace) was asked, "O ʿAlī, what did you do when you were with the Messenger of Allah?" he answered: "When the Messenger of Allah spoke, I listened in silence; and when he was silent, I posed questions to him."

## The Effect of Knowledge upon the Soul of a Beneficiary

Every creature requires sustenance to endure; just so, ⟪ar:نفس حسیہ⟫ (the sensible soul) requires ⟪ar:'علم'⟫ (knowledge) as its nourishment. Just as the body grows weak without outward sustenance, so too does the ⟪ar:نفس حسیۃ⟫ (sensible soul) weaken without knowledge; its ⟪ar:'ملکوتی'⟫ (celestial, angelic) faculties diminish, while its ⟪ar:'شھوانی و غضبی'⟫ (appetitive and irascible) powers awaken, making it susceptible to sin's attraction. ⟪ar:حضور علیہ السلام⟫ (His Eminence, upon him be peace) said: "If a person lacks the company of a scholar for forty days, then a traveller enters his heart, and he begins to venture toward the great sins (⟪ar:'کبائر'⟫)."[^cite-6] This is because the company of scholars is a light that weakens the ⟪ar:نفس حسیۃ⟫ (sensible soul)'s animal propensities and instils piety within him, thereby increasing his hunger for knowledge.

The desire of a beneficiary leads to the elevation of his knowledge; yet desire itself does not arise save through the aid of Allah. Acquiring knowledge is no trivial matter—it is exceedingly difficult. For this reason, elevation of rank depends upon ⟪ar:'علم'⟫ (knowledge), not upon mere action. To acquire knowledge, a person must surrender many of his permissible desires and turn his face away from the pleasures of the world, orienting himself entirely toward the pursuit of knowledge.

## The Pure and Impure Beneficiary

A sincere beneficiary might employ his time in worldly affairs, rest, or the satisfaction of bodily appetites. Yet instead, in order to please his Lord and to perfect his soul, he binds himself in the bonds of the Hereafter and rises up in the worship of knowledge. This action increases the pleasure of Allah and earns His generous reward.

By contrast, there is the beneficiary who acknowledges the greatness of the divine sciences and expresses his longing to acquire them with his tongue, yet his actions bear no testimony to his passion. Such a ⟪ar:'منافق'⟫ (hypocrite) spends his days worshipping his own ego through his deeds, while proclaiming his apparent passion for knowledge before others. He plots against Allah, and Allah plots against him, binding him all the more tightly in the snares of the world. This is why Allah says (in the interpretation of the meaning):

> ⟪quran 3:54⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَمَكَرُوا۟ وَمَكَرَ ٱللَّهُ ۖ وَٱللَّهُ خَيْرُ ٱلْمَكِرِينَ ‎⟫
> *And they (disbelievers) plotted, and Allah planned. And Allah is the Best of planners.*

Allah is wrathful with such a beneficiary and says: "You have set aside time in your day for your pleasures and desires, yet you have thrust My worship behind you. Therefore, I will not look upon you on the Day of Judgement!" ⟪ar:(نعوذ باﷲ من ذالک)⟫ *(We seek refuge with Allah from such a fate).* Allah also says (in the interpretation of the meaning):

> ⟪quran 2:9⟫
> ⟪ar:‏يُخَدِعُونَ ٱللَّهَ وَٱلَّذِينَ آمَنُوا۟ وَمَا يَخْدَعُونَ إِلَّآ أَنفُسَهُمْ وَمَا يَشْعُرُونَ ‎⟫
> *They (think to) deceive Allah and those who believe, while they only deceive themselves, and perceive (it) not.*

These people deceive others by proclaiming their passion; in truth they deceive none but themselves, yet ⟪ar:'وما یشعرون'⟫ *(they do not perceive it).* Consider the analogy: a man requires physical and material food for his body day and night; if he is deprived of food at any moment, he senses it keenly, grows restless and anxious, and perishes should nourishment be withheld. In exactly the same way, his soul too requires spiritual nourishment—that is, ⟪ar:'علم'⟫ (knowledge). Yet if he acquires no knowledge, he does not feel the ruin and dissolution of his own soul. There is no apparent harm in being deaf to knowledge throughout one's life, for the soul does not manifest its anguish and restlessness as the body does; a person thus remains oblivious and indifferent to it. The feeling and awareness of this loss strikes only when the hour of descent arrives, when death approaches to seize the soul.

The wise say that man's life passes between ⟪ar:'حرکت'⟫ (motion) and stillness. He moves in his youth, labouring and toiling to find repose in his old age. That is, today's action yields tomorrow's peace. For this reason, the beneficiary moves through this world, suffers, and foregoes his desires so that his soul may find tranquility in the Hereafter. Just as one who performs his daily tasks with diligence sleeps peacefully at night—he does not worry about tomorrow because he has made provision for it today—so too does a virtuous life lead to a happy and peaceful death. And a blessed death can only be achieved through knowledge.

Through worldly knowledge, a person lives a successful life in this world and uses it as a means to save himself and his family from poverty and destitution. Similarly, if he wishes to improve his life in the Hereafter, he must acquire the knowledge of the Hereafter. If he does not, he will live a life of spiritual poverty and deprivation in the world to come, dependent upon the favours and blessings of others for all eternity.

## Thirst and Fear

The thirst for knowledge increases the beneficiary's demand for knowledge. When that thirst reaches its zenith, no force in the world, nor any preoccupation, can prevent him from acquiring knowledge. His state resembles that of a man whose physical thirst brings him to the riverbank. Upon seeing the river's waters, fear grips his heart: perhaps the waves will drown him and destroy his worldly life. Similarly, the beneficiary fears that engagement in sacred learning will deprive him of the world's pleasures. This fear prevents him from entering the river.

Yet when a beneficiary's thirst reaches its peak and his anguish grows unbearable, when he cries out in anguish—⟪ar:الأتش الأتش⟫ *(thirst! thirst!)*—his thirst overwhelms his fear, and he leaps into the river to save his life. At that moment he gives no thought to drowning, for he is certain that without jumping into the waters, he will perish of thirst. In precisely the same way, the beneficiary who is assured of his soul's ruin plunges into the river of divine unity and knowledge without any fear, and no worldly excuse stands in his way. Allah then grants this thirsty one to drink from His pool of Kawthar (⟪ar:قوت متخیلۃ⟫) in such abundance that he never thirsts again.

The mentor creates and augments this thirst in the beneficiary so that he may recognize himself, pass beyond the component and move toward tomorrow's waters, relinquishing the duality of limitation and quenching his thirst by plunging into the river of unity. This leap can only be taken when thirst exceeds fear within the beneficiary! The thirst torments him, and he cries out: "Water, water!"

### Journey to the Inner Self

Like worldly education, sacred education has its own ascending degrees. The beneficiary is initially a student at the school's threshold and rises step by step through academic ranks, passing various trials. In secular learning, it is possible to attain a certain degree of perfection where the student halts and reaches his destination. But upon the spiritual path, all are travellers and wayfarers, for this path is without end. Each degree is followed by a new one; after every stage comes another. Continuing upon this path, persisting in supplication and prayer, is the true destination. Knowledge and action both depend upon ⟪ar:'نیت'⟫ (intention). If the intention is sincere and thirst is untainted by fear, then no obstacle can remain in the beneficiary's way.

## The Difference Between Student and Beneficiary

There is a very clear distinction between a student and a beneficiary. The student desires ⟪ar:'علم'⟫ (knowledge) that does not ⟪ar:' تبدیلی'⟫ (transform) him. His knowledge does not benefit his soul. The example is that of a candle, which derives no benefit from the light it sheds and spends its entire life in darkness beneath its own flame. Spirituality cannot be learned or read. Spirituality can only be *embodied*. The student does not wish to undertake the inner journey; he merely wishes to acquire its knowledge. He does not want to become a cobbler; he merely wants to know how shoes are made—knowledge alone! The beneficiary, by contrast, asks: "How can I transform this knowledge? How can I become a being? How can I penetrate to essence?" This desire for transformation makes the beneficiary's knowledge profitable to his soul, and upon his death, that knowledge is transmuted into a noble form.

The true purpose of knowledge is to transform essence—that is, to transmute physicality into spirituality.

## The Migration of the Beneficiary

When the Prophet (upon him be peace) came bearing the message of Islam and invited the people of Mecca to ⟪ar:لا الہ الا اﷲ⟫ *(there is no god but Allah),* the Meccans became his enemies and began to persecute him and his companions. For thirteen years he preached Islam in an atmosphere of extreme hardship. When their oppression became unbearable, by the command of Allah he emigrated with his companions from ⟪ar:مکۃ المعظمۃ⟫ (Mecca the Mighty) to ⟪ar:مدینۃ المنورہ⟫ (Medina the Illuminated). This departure is called ⟪ar:'حجرت'⟫ (the migration, *hijra*). Upon arrival in ⟪ar:مدینہ⟫ (Medina), the ⟪ar:اہل مدینہ⟫ (people of Medina) received him with immense love and affection. They sacrificed their lives and wealth for him, presenting an example of self-sacrifice unparalleled in human history. Islam recognizes two eras: the period before the migration and the period after it.

Just as the Messenger of Allah (may God's prayers and peace be upon him) departed from wicked people and came to righteous ones, so too is it incumbent upon every beneficiary to abandon his evils and migrate toward the good, to leave the darkness of ignorance and incline toward the light of knowledge, and to emerge from the bondage of pride and ego, entering into the realm of humility. Through this migration, he transforms himself, and two eras become established in his life. One is the time in which he was enveloped in darkness; the other is the time after his spiritual migration, in which he is transformed through knowledge and action, rising to ever-higher stations.

He lives the life of this world like a traveller. When a traveller journeys to a place, knowing that after a few days he must depart that temporary abode and return to his homeland, he spends those days behaving courteously, attending to necessary matters, and departing without burdening anyone or causing harm. The traveller does not quarrel during his journey; he remains respectful and dignified among others. This is the spiritual migration of the beneficiary, wherein he need not journey through physical space, but must instead transform his thoughts and temperaments, breaking the bonds of natural inclination. This spiritual migration is far more arduous than outward migration.

## The Question of the Beneficiary and the Answer of the Mentor

When a beneficiary poses a good question, sometimes his intellect takes pride in its own acuity, and he feels he has asked the finest question. Similarly, when a mentor gives an excellent answer, he may grow proud of his capability. In reality, the beneficiary's question arises from the ⟪ar:' تحریک'⟫ (movement, impulsion) of the Imam (upon him be peace). That is, when the Imam wishes to impart certain knowledge to someone, he moves that person to ask a question. Likewise, when the mentor answers, it is truly the ⟪ar:'توفیق'⟫ (grace, success) of the ⟪ar:'امام علیہ السلام '⟫ (Imam, upon him be peace) that enables him to answer. Thus it all returns to the praise of the Imam—that without his ⟪ar:تحریک⟫ (impulsion), no beneficiary could ask a question, and without his ⟪ar:توفیق⟫ (grace), no mentor could provide a worthy answer.[^cite-7]

| Beneficiary | Question | Imam's Impulsion |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Mentor | Answer | Imam's Grace |

When a scholarly gathering is convened and teaching proceeds, the assembly comprises beneficiaries learning from the mentor. When they wish to pose a question, they do so through one of those present, and the mentor answers it.

When a planner or preacher poses a question and then answers it himself, that question concerns the context of the verses included in the lesson. The planner or preacher thus first asks the question before answering it himself.

## To Whom Should Questions Be Addressed?

Allah says in the Qur'an:

> ⟪quran 21:7⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَمَآ أَرْسَلْنَا قَبْلَكَ إِلَّا رِجَالًۭا نُّوحِىٓ إِلَيْهِمْ ۖ فَسْـَٔلُوٓا۟ أَهْلَ ٱلذِّكْرِ إِن كُنتُمْ لَا تَعْلَمُونَ ‎⟫
> *And We sent not before you but men to whom We revealed, so ask the people of the Reminder if you do not know.*

This verse refers to the ⟪ar:ائمہ طاہرین علیھم السلام⟫ (Imams of the Purified Household, upon them be peace), and ⟪ar:'ذکر'⟫ (Remembrance) refers to the word of Allah or the Messenger of Allah (may God's prayers and peace be upon him). With this verse, Allah divides humanity into two parts: the ⟪ar:اَھلَ الذِّکر⟫ (People of Remembrance), who are the Imams, the Prophet's successors; and the ⟪ar:لاَ تَعلَمُون⟫ (those who do not know), meaning the common people.

| Ahl al-Dhikr (People of Remembrance) | Those Who Do Not Know |
| --- | --- |
| The Imams, upon them be peace | The common people |

Yet Allah does not say in this verse that the ⟪ar:اہل ذکر⟫ (People of Remembrance) are obliged to answer, though He has made it obligatory for the world to ask questions—but not for the Imams to answer them.[^cite-8]

A man posed one hundred questions to Imam al-Ḥusayn (upon him be peace) in a single gathering. The Imam answered all of them at once, in the very order in which they had been asked. The true Imam must possess four qualities, otherwise he is no true Imam:

| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Knowledge | Generosity | Courage | Justice |

## The Great Blessing of My Mentor Upon Me

Every deed passes through multiple stages from beginning to completion. The initial stages are called ⟪ar:' أَوَّلِیاَت '⟫ (preliminaries), and the final stages are called ⟪ar:' أُخرَوِیاَت '⟫ (ultimacies). Effort and struggle attend every stage, all directed toward achieving the true purpose of the work. Should that real purpose be lost, all labour is wasted.

The farmer places a grain of wheat into the ground. From that moment until it becomes the food nourishment of man—which is its true purpose—many circumstances pass over this grain. First the grain is buried in the earth, then it breaks through the soil, then it grows slowly and becomes a blade, then a spike, then it is cut down and ground into flour, then it is leavened, then it is baked into bread and reaches a man, then his teeth chew it into sustenance, then his body receives it and it becomes righteous blood, then it takes the form of organs and finally becomes a human being.

At every degree and transformation, the grain of wheat erases its previous form and assumes another. Every change required an agent of change—it was dependent upon the farmer until the grain became a plant; then it needed a buyer to trade it; then it needed a miller to grind it; then it needed a cook to leaven and bake it; then it needed fire to bake it; until at last it reached a man in an acceptable state and finally the man made it part of his body. Reaching man and being incorporated into his nourishment was the grain's true objective and the final destination of its long journey. But to arrive there, it had to traverse many stages and required a motive force to pass through each stage. The grain possessed no ability to develop itself from one level to the next.

But consider now: when that grain of wheat reached the human body and became blood, and when that blood reached ⟪ar:'قُوَّتِ مُتَخَیِّلَۃ '⟫ (the power of imagination) in the brain, it underwent a remarkable transformation. Until now it had merely changed its physical form, but now it acquired the capacity to absorb the forms and images of all worlds through imagination. Until now it had been absorbed by others; but now it developed the power to integrate others into itself.[^cite-9]

⟪ar:حضرت المنصوب ضیاء علی بن حضرت اسحاق صاحب مقدس⟫ explained that a mirror possesses the capacity to reflect all shapes that come before it. All those shapes are visible simultaneously in this single mirror—and this is the most wondrous state, that although the mirror is one, it encompasses countless luminous forms within itself. Such is the condition of ⟪ar:قوت متخیلۃ⟫ (our faculty of imagination)—it can simultaneously encompass many different concepts and bring them into captivity. The grain of wheat, which in its original state was imprisoned in the physical world of form, undergoes a radical transformation through ⟪ar:متخیلۃ⟫ (imagination) and becomes like a mirror, now capable of integrating other forms and diverse concepts!

This grain serves as an example of the beneficiary in his initial stage, when he depends upon an agent—his mentor—to stimulate him. The mentor draws him from one degree and elevates him to another, where he finds a new mentor. Like wheat, the beneficiary "dies" from one grade to the next, becoming better by erasing his previous state. Each mentor prepares him for the next stage, until he develops to the point where he now becomes useful to others. Until now, others were his limits, absorbing his form; but now he himself becomes a limit and integrates the forms of others into himself.

In the same way, as he develops, the beneficiary becomes the food—like a grain of wheat—of his true man, ⟪ar:(امام علیہ السلام)⟫ (the Imam, upon him be peace), and reaches his divinity, which is his final and true destination. Upon reaching this station, his condition undergoes no further change, for he is in the state of human perfection within ⟪ar:لاہوت امام الزمان⟫ (the divinity of the Imam of the Age)—seated upon the ⟪ar:'عرش'⟫ (Throne) of development.

The development of this grain of wheat became possible only because it completely surrendered itself to the agents that transformed it. It accepted without resistance the influences exerted upon it. Whether the burden of being buried in the earth, the harshness of the seasons, the blow of the sickle, the grinding of the mill, the heat of fire, or the crushing between teeth—it resisted none of these. It effaced its will and adopted the will of its agent; it made itself the object of action, and this very passivity led to its elevation. Had it resisted, it would never have risen in degree. In exactly the same way, unless the beneficiary surrenders himself to his mentor, removes the crown of ⟪ar:'أنا'⟫ (ego, "I-ness") from his head and places it at his mentor's feet, erases his being, desires, and hopes, and submits himself to the strictest orders of his mentor, his development will not be possible. Allah says (in the interpretation of the meaning):

> ⟪quran 7:40⟫
> ⟪ar:‏إِنَّ ٱلَّذِينَ كَذَّبُوا۟ بِـَٔايَتِنَا وَٱسْتَكْبَرُوا۟ عَنْهَا لَا تُفَتَّحُ لَهُمْ أَبْوَبُ ٱلسَّمَآءِ وَلَا يَدْخُلُونَ ٱلْجَنَّةَ حَتَّىٰ يَلِجَ ٱلْجَمَلُ فِى سَمِّ ٱلْخِيَاطِ ۚ وَكَذَلِكَ نَجْزِى ٱلْمُجْرِمِينَ ‎⟫
> *Verily, those who deny Our verses and treat them with arrogance—for them the gates of heaven will not be opened, nor will they enter Paradise until the camel passes through the eye of the needle. Thus do We recompense the criminals.*[^cite-10]

There is also another crucial matter: had that grain of wheat reached man in its unrefined state, man would never have brought it into his diet or made it part of his body. In such a condition, the existence of this grain and all efforts in its development would be wasted, for it would not have achieved the true purpose of its creation. It is thus evident that the gratitude owed to its agents is immense—they have carefully nurtured and supervised it and given it a form that makes it acceptable to the human constitution.

In the same way, were our ⟪ar:'صورت'⟫ (form, condition) not refined by the Imam (upon him be peace) in our present state—full of sins and faults—the Imam would never incorporate such a defective form into his divinity. In such a circumstance, the purpose of our creation would be lost, and both our life and death would be wasted. Therefore, it is a tremendous blessing from our generous Imam, our prayers, and all the divine ordinances, that they refine our form and transform it into such a state that our ⟪ar:ھیکل⟫ (structure, frame) is able to merge with that of the Imam (upon him be peace). For this reason, the Prophet (may God's prayers and peace be upon him) said: "Were it permissible to prostrate to any but Allah, I would have commanded my wife to prostrate to her husband"—that is, I would have commanded the beneficiary to obey his mentor.

May Allah enable us all to sacrifice our pride, our desires, and our hopes for the sake of our elders and mentors, without whose guidance our salvation is not possible. Amīn, Amīn.
