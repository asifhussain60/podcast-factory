## The Matter of Retribution

The matter of retribution (qisas, قصاص) encompasses all humanity—disbelievers, believers, prophets, and imams alike. The common understanding holds that life persists so long as it exists in the material sense; yet the Qur'an subverts this notion, testifying instead that life itself depends upon the continuance of retribution. Once qisas ceases, life too must end.

> ⟪quran 2:179⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَلَكُمْ فِى ٱلْقِصَاصِ حَيَوٰةٌۭ يَٓأُو۟لِى ٱلْأَلْبَبِ لَعَلَّكُمْ تَتَّقُونَ ‎⟫
> *And there is (a saving of) life for you in Al-Qisas (the Law of Equality in punishment), O men of understanding, that you may become Al-Muttaqun (the pious - see V.2:2).*
> And for you is life in retribution, O men of understanding, so that you may guard yourselves.
> — **Quran 2:179 (inline widget)**

Throughout Islamic history, the records preserve numerous instances wherein the pious elders, moved by fear of retribution and mindful of divine justice, restrained their own anger and forgave those who had wronged them, offering pardon in the path of Allah.[^cite-1]

### Imam ʿAlī Zayn al-ʿĀbidīn and the Incident of the Horse

⟪ar:مولانا امام علی زین العابدین علیہ السلام⟫Imam ʿAlī Zayn al-ʿĀbidīn (the noble imam known for his devotional practices) possessed a mare upon which he frequently rode, a creature to whom he had shown countless favors throughout her years of service. During a journey, the animal halted and refused to proceed. In his frustration, the Imam raised his hand to strike her with severity, yet arrested the blow before it landed. He then addressed the mare, saying: ⟪ar-quote:لَو لاَ خَوفَ القِصاَصِ لَضَرَبتُکِ⟫ "Had it not been for my fear of retribution, I would have struck you down." It is reported that upon the Imam's passing to the mercy of Allah, the mare came to his dwelling seeking him. When she found him absent, she made her way to his tomb and placed her head upon his grave. At that moment, she uttered a piercing cry and expired.[^cite-2]

### Imam ʿAlī Zayn al-ʿĀbidīn and His Servant

⟪ar:مولانا امام علی زین العابدین علیہ السلام⟫There was a slave entrusted with the supervision of the Imam's household, a duty he failed to discharge faithfully. Thieves gained entry and absconded with goods and ornaments of great value. Upon his return and witnessing the devastation, the Imam's anger kindled. He summoned the servant and rebuked him severely for his negligence, then—moved by fury—struck him across the face. (In the Sharīʿah, such an offense carries the possibility of capital punishment for a slave.) The servant stood in silence, his head bowed in shame.

Yet scarcely had the blow landed when remorse seized the Imam. He turned to the servant and said: "O servant! Forgive me. I raised my hand against you in anger." The servant replied: "O master, you have the right to take my life, and you were just to do so. Truly, I failed grievously in my trust, and I am ashamed beyond measure that you have suffered such loss on account of my negligence." The Imam responded: "I have struck you, and if you wish, you may strike me in retaliation and exact your retribution." The servant demurred: "O master, I dare not! The fault is mine alone; I beg your pardon." Thereupon the Imam declared: "I shall discharge the retribution owed for this blow by dedicating this house to you in the path of Allah."[^cite-3]

## The Saying: "Had It Not Been for You, I Would Not Have Created the Heavens"

In explaining the grandeur of the Almighty, the Seal of the Prophets and Messengers (muḥammad ʿalayhi al-salām, محمد علیہ السلام) spoke: ⟪ar-quote:لو لاک لما خلقت الافلاک⟫ "Had it not been for you, I would not have created the heavens."

The mystics and spiritual scholars elucidate this profound utterance as follows: the veil through which Muḥammad appeared into this world—that luminous veil (al-ḥijāb, الحجاب)—attained a degree of perfection such that when all the antecedent divine logic was gathered within him, the reckoning of all those who existed within that logical order was also consolidated with him. This occurred because he stood as a repository (mustawdaʿ, مستودع) in the manifest stream, whereas others constituted the permanent dwelling place (mustaqarr, مستقر) of the reckoning. Those situated in the order of retribution possess an exalted station, yet none escape the obligation to answer for their deeds. Hence, throughout the preceding dispensations, all the Imams propagated their message in concealment, whilst their prophets and messengers endured suffering on their account, behind the veil.

Allah Most High declares: "O Messenger! Had it not been for you, I would not have created the heavens, for the chapter of retribution commences from the celestial spheres themselves. Through the rotation and friction of the stars, all manifestations and generations arise in this world, and every account is settled. Had there not existed persons among the assembly obligated to discharge qisas, Allah would have had no need to create the heavens at all.[^cite-4]

## The Master Serving His Slave

⟪ar:آدم علیہ السلام⟫⟪ar:رسول اللہ (ﷺ)⟫From the epoch of Adam through the era of prophethood (nubuwwah, نبوۃ), the summons (al-daʿwa, الدعوة) operated in such manner that the Imams remained concealed behind the veil, while ⟪ar:اولاد اسماعیل⟫⟪ar:اولاد اسحاق⟫they served their Lord as both servant and slave. All tribulations and hardships descended upon the Prophets, whilst the Imams conducted their sacred work in tranquility and peace. Yet when the manifestation of the Greatest Speaker (al-nāṭiq al-aʿẓam, الناطق الاعظم)—Muḥammad, the Chosen One, upon whom be peace—took place, this pattern underwent transformation. History testifies that ʿAlī, who is the Permanent Imam (al-imām al-mustaqarr, الامام المستقر), fought with drawn sword, whilst the Messenger of Allah, who is the Trustee Prophet (al-nabī al-mustawdaʿ, النبی المستودع), rested beneath the shade of a tree.

⟪ar:مولانا داعی عبدان⟫In elucidating the inward realities of this epoch, Maulana al-Dāʿī al-ʿAbdān declares that this period exemplifies a master serving his slave. Since all prophets from Adam until the consummation of the Imamate endured suffering and combat at the command of the Imams, divine justice demanded a reckoning with these prophets upon their Imams. ⟪ar:ثلاثہ مجاری⟫One of the three channels (al-mujjarī, المجاری) is that wherein the veil stands subordinate to a higher veil. This occurred because the debts incurred by the permanent Imams (al-mustaqarrīn, المستقرین) in the past had to be settled by the trustee-prophets (al-mustawdiʿīn, المستودعین). Thus ʿAlī, who is the lowest veil of the permanent Imam (al-ḥijāb al-adnā al-mustaqarr, الحجاب الادنى المستقر), was sent into the world to discharge the accounts that remained to be settled—those owed by the permanent Imams to the trustee prophets and messengers.

When the rank of the Messenger of Allah was elevated, and all the prophets and messengers—⟪ar:اولاد اسحاق⟫the descendants of Isaac—were gathered unto him, then the reckoning of all these was gathered with him as well. Similarly, with ʿAlī came the reckoning of ⟪ar:اولاد اسماعیل⟫the Imams and trustees (the descendants of Ishmael). At that juncture, it became obligatory upon ʿAlī to serve the one designated by Allah's justice—obligatory, that is, for the permanent Imam to serve the trustee-prophet. During this period, ʿAlī—keeper of the descendants of Ishmael—served and discharged this reckoning with the keeper of the descendants of Isaac. This is the reason why ʿAlī fought with sword in hand while the Messenger of Allah rested under a shady tree during the combat.[^cite-5]

## The Matter of Divine Decree and Divine Determination

⟪ar:' قضا '⟫The term *qaḍā* signifies judgment, whilst ⟪ar:' قدر '⟫ *qadar* denotes the power and determination that one possesses. In the human condition, cycles (kurrāt, كرّات) occur such that after every thousand years the world of a person is overturned. The good and evil deeds (ḥasanāt and sayyiʾāt, حسنات and السیئات) of one's previous cycle become one's destiny for the present one—whatever righteousness or corruption one accumulated before now constitutes the fate decreed for the current existence. Yet what one performs in this present action becomes one's *qadar*—that dominion over which one exercises complete authority. Each person has been granted full mastery over his deeds: he may choose to perform acts of righteousness and advance in rank, or commit transgressions and fall into degradation. This power of choice is an absolute trust placed in his hands. This *qadar* shall in turn become his *qaḍā* for the cycle to come. Should a person wish to alter his destiny through prayer and the grace of Allah obtained via righteous deeds, such transformation remains possible; yet the power of this alteration rests solely with Allah the Almighty.[^cite-6]

In another sense, the term ⟪ar:' قضا '⟫ *qaḍā* designates contentment with the will of Allah, whilst ⟪ar:' ظن '⟫ *ẓann* (suspicion or judgment) refers to a surmise concerning that which lies beyond Allah's revealed will. *Ẓann* constitutes an opinion—good or bad—regarding something upon which there exists neither clear knowledge nor argumentation. A favorable surmise is called ⟪ar:'حُسنَ الظَّن'⟫ *ḥusn al-ẓann* (good suspicion), whilst ⟪ar:'سُوء الظَّن'⟫ *sūʾ al-ẓann* (evil suspicion) designates the contrary. Allah Most High proclaims:

> ⟪quran 49:12⟫
> ⟪ar:‏يَٓأَيُّهَا ٱلَّذِينَ آمَنُوا۟ ٱجْتَنِبُوا۟ كَثِيرًۭا مِّنَ ٱلظَّنِّ إِنَّ بَعْضَ ٱلظَّنِّ إِثْمٌۭ ۖ وَلَا تَجَسَّسُوا۟ وَلَا يَغْتَب بَّعْضُكُم بَعْضًۭا ۚ أَيُحِبُّ أَحَدُكُمْ أَن يَأْكُلَ لَحْمَ أَخِيهِ مَيْتًۭا فَكَرِهْتُمُوهُ ۚ وَٱتَّقُوا۟ ٱللَّهَ ۚ إِنَّ ٱللَّهَ تَوَّابٌۭ رَّحِيمٌۭ ‎⟫
> *O you who believe! Avoid much suspicions, indeed some suspicions are sins. And spy not, neither backbite one another. Would one of you like to eat the flesh of his dead brother? You would hate it (so hate backbiting) . And fear Allah. Verily, Allah is the One Who accepts repentance, Most Merciful.*
> O you who believe! Avoid much suspicion. Indeed, some suspicions are a sin, and do not gossip against anyone, nor backbite anyone. Would any of you like to eat the flesh of his dead brother, which you hate, and fear Allah? Indeed, Allah is the Acceptor of Repentance, the Merciful.
> — **Quran 49:12 (inline widget)**

Those who attain contentment with ⟪ar:قضاء و قدر⟫ divine decree and determination prefer the sublime to the base. *Qaḍā* is the knowledge of Allah that preceded His creation of all things. For this reason, the prophets and the saints (awliyāʾ, أولیاء) remained content with all circumstances and afflictions ordained by divine wisdom. Allah the Almighty bestows His finest blessings only after the severest trials. The blessing easily obtained is deemed light and insignificant, whilst the blessing received through utmost difficulty and patient endurance attains the highest rank. Thus Allah proclaims:

> ⟪quran 39:10⟫
> ⟪ar:‏قُلْ يَعِبَادِ ٱلَّذِينَ آمَنُوا۟ ٱتَّقُوا۟ رَبَّكُمْ ۚ لِلَّذِينَ أَحْسَنُوا۟ فِى هَذِهِ ٱلدُّنْيَا حَسَنَةٌۭ ۗ وَأَرْضُ ٱللَّهِ وَسِعَةٌ ۗ إِنَّمَا يُوَفَّى ٱلصَّبِرُونَ أَجْرَهُم بِغَيْرِ حِسَابٍۢ ‎⟫
> *Say (O Muhammad PBUH): "O My slaves who believe (in the Oneness of Allah Islamic Monotheism), be afraid of your Lord (Allah) and keep your duty to Him. Good is (the reward) for those who do good in this world, and Allah's earth is spacious (so if you cannot worship Allah at a place, then go to another)! Only those who are patient shall receive their rewards in full, without reckoning." *
> Say, O My servants who have believed, fear your Lord. For those who do good in this world is good, and Allah's earth is spacious. Indeed, those who are patient will be given their reward without reckoning.
> — **Quran 39:10 (inline widget)**

## Prophet Dāwūd and the Judgment of the Hereafter

There exist two categories of judgment: one is ⟪ar:'قضاء الدنیا'⟫ the judgment of this world (*qaḍāʾ al-dunyā*), and the other is ⟪ar:'قضاء الآخرۃ'⟫ the judgment of the hereafter (*qaḍāʾ al-ākhirah*). ⟪ar:قضاء الدنیا⟫ *Qaḍāʾ al-dunyā* is rendered upon the basis of the summons (al-daʿwa, الدعوة) and evidence (bayyināt, بَیِّناَت); ⟪ar:قضاء الآخرۃ⟫ *qaḍāʾ al-ākhirah* constitutes a judgment that Allah alone executes, founded upon His knowledge—a knowledge utterly beyond the comprehension of any creature.

Three categories of judges exist: two shall enter Hell, and one shall enter Paradise. The judge who knowingly renders an unjust decision shall enter Hell. The judge who renders an unjust decision through oversight or error shall likewise enter Hell. Only the judge who renders judgment upon the basis of Allah's commandments and those of His Messenger shall enter Paradise.

⟪ar:امیر المومنین علی علیہ السلام⟫⟪ar:نبی داؤد علیہ السلام⟫It is related that Prophet Dāwūd addressed Allah, saying: "O Allah, I render judgment upon the basis of what I perceive of Your truth, yet I sense that You desire something else whilst I desire another." Allah revealed to Dāwūd: "Whatever judgment you render, do so by oath before the people based upon the evidence before you, and appoint Me as judge over that which you do not perceive." This is because you do not render judgment on the basis of ⟪ar:دعوۃ اور بیانات⟫ the summons and evidence of the hereafter, but rather upon ⟪ar:قضاء الآخرۃ⟫ the judgment of the hereafter alone.

Dāwūd replied: "O my Lord! Inform me how the judgments of the hereafter are rendered." Allah answered: "O Dāwūd, you have requested of Me something I have not disclosed to any of creation." Yet when Dāwūd persisted in his plea, Allah said: "O Dāwūd, I shall show you how I judge the hereafter, but you will find the burden unbearable."

### The Dispute Over a Cow

Two persons appeared at Dāwūd's door, one of whom possessed a cow. The first claimant asserted that the cow was rightfully his; the other man refused, insisting the cow belonged to him and presenting witnesses and evidence that proved his possession. The initial claimant possessed neither evidence nor witnesses to support his claim of ownership. At that moment, Allah revealed to Dāwūd: "My judgment is that you award the cow to the first claimant, even though he presents no proof." Furthermore, "Give him a sword and command him to slay the second man." Dāwūd obeyed Allah's command, yet the reasoning remained opaque to him, and the burden weighed heavily upon his heart. The tribes of Israel, witnessing this judgment, grew disturbed and began to harbor doubts regarding Dāwūd's wisdom.[^cite-7]

### The Theft in the Garden

Presently there arrived an elderly man, leading a young man by the arm. The young man held a vine in his hand. The old man lodged a complaint: "This young man entered my garden, destroyed my cultivation, ate my fruit without permission, and stole these vines." Dāwūd asked the youth, "What say you?" The boy confessed: "The old man speaks truly!" Thereupon Allah revealed to Dāwūd: "Give the young man a sword and command him to slay the old man, then grant him the old man's garden as recompense. Moreover, command him to excavate at a specific location within that garden, where he shall discover forty thousand dirhams. Bestow all this wealth upon him." Dāwūd executed Allah's command, yet his sorrow only deepened. Grievous murmurings arose among the Children of Israel; many began to speak of removing Dāwūd from his office as judge.[^cite-8]

### The Incident of the Slaughtered Bull

Amid these circumstances, a multitude approached the Prophet Dāwūd with designs of conferring with him, when they beheld a man enter a house with a bull tethered outside. The animal broke free, wandered off, and approached another man's dwelling. When the householder emerged, he seized the bull and slaughtered it. He then skinned the animal, divided it into portions of meat, and carried these into his home. Shortly thereafter, the bull's owner came forth seeking his animal, questioned the neighbors, and learned what had transpired. They apprehended the man and brought him before Prophet Dāwūd, all testifying that he had unlawfully slaughtered the bull without permission, dismembered it, and taken the meat into his house. Dāwūd asked the man: "What say you?" The man replied: "O Messenger of Allah! I know not what these people claim. For several days, my household had no food. When I departed my dwelling, I saw a bull standing near my house. I slaughtered it and took its meat to feed my family. Now inform me: what compensation do I owe for this bull?" Allah revealed to Dāwūd: "Command the man who owned the ox to lie upon the ground, and command the other man—he who slaughtered the ox—to slaughter him as he slaughtered the ox." And then, "Hand over all the wealth of the deceased to this other person!"

Dāwūd obeyed Allah's command. Yet his turmoil and anguish knew no bounds, and the people of Israel rose in rebellion and outcry: "Until now we heard only of your unjust judgments, but today we have witnessed your injustice with our own eyes!" Dāwūd replied: "I swear by Allah that whatever I have done springs not from my own will, but from Allah's command." He then withdrew into the mosque's inner chamber (al-miḥrāb, المحراب) and prostrated himself, weeping and supplicating Allah: "Reveal to me the meaning behind these judgments, that I may explain to the Children of Israel the reasons for my verdicts." Witnessing Dāwūd's sighing and anguish, Allah spoke: "O Dāwūd! I forewarned you that you would be unable to bear My judgments. Now I shall disclose to you the wisdom therein.

The man who claimed ownership of the cow—he who possessed all the evidence and witnesses—had in years past murdered a man in secret and seized his cow. A child accompanied the victim and witnessed his father's murder and the thief's theft, yet he lacked witnesses or evidence to prove the crime. But I beheld it all! This very day, the man I commanded you to slay was the orphaned child, and the cow was indeed his father's rightful possession.

The owner of the vineyard had, in former times, slain the father of the young man to rob him of his wealth, and with that ill-gotten money, purchased the orchard. After acquiring the garden, forty thousand dirhams remained, which he concealed in a secret location. None knew of this—not even the young man—nor did anyone know that the old man had murdered his father. But I possess all knowledge, and thus I rendered this judgment founded upon My omniscience.

The owner of the bull had slain the father of the other man and seized much of his wealth. With the profits from this plunder, the man had acquired both the bull and the house. No creature save I knew this truth—and that is why I rendered this judgment according to My knowledge alone."

Allah then declared: "O Dāwūd, these were My three judgments—⟪ar:قضاء الأخرۃ⟫ the judgments of the hereafter. I have reserved all such verdicts for the Day of Reckoning, which only I can execute. Hereafter, do not question Me hastily concerning matters for which I have deferred judgment! Continue to judge among people according to the manner I have prescribed—⟪ar:دعوۃ⟫ ⟪ar:بینات⟫ upon the summons and evidence presented—even if such judgments appear erroneous in your estimation due to your ignorance of the unseen!"[^cite-9]

## Retribution and the Imams' Enforcement of Divine Boundaries

### The Nature of Retribution

There is nothing improper in the matter of retribution within Allah's justice, whether among the believers, the masses, the saints of Allah, the young, or the old. Were forgiveness permissible with anyone, it would surely be with the saints of Allah; yet even they do not stand beyond the reach of qisas. Any act undertaken by mutual consent and in accordance with the Sharīʿah—such as marriage or commerce—does not fall under the purview of retribution. If the will of a person is not engaged in the matter, that act comes within the scope of qisas. When both parties consent to an act, yet that act violates the ordinances of the Sharīʿah, then retribution becomes obligatory upon both. The Imams, executing divine orders, have slain persons, imposed the hudūd (the fixed boundaries of divine law, الحدود), flogged and stoned the guilty. Though all such actions were performed by the Sharīʿah's mandate, they occurred without the consent of those punished. Consequently, retribution became binding upon these Imams, and they necessarily endured tribulations in this world.[^cite-10]

### The Imams' Enactment of Boundaries and Its Recompense

When an Imam or believers obedient to his command slay an individual, impose a ḥadd (boundary), or inflict corporal punishment, the recompense for such action accrues to them—to their luminous bodies (al-hayākil al-nūrāniyyah, ھیاکل نورانیۃ)—and to the souls of those who follow them, thereby elevating their rank. Retribution holds its place, yet they simultaneously receive the reward for this deed, performed as it was at Allah's command. Reward and retribution become intertwined. Through their ordeal and suffering, their retribution is extracted from the body of the Imam and the bodies of those punished; thereafter their reckoning is completed and they are elevated.

It sometimes occurs that an Imam or his obedient followers impose a boundary upon someone, flog or stone them, yet death does not ensue from poison or the sword's stroke—that is, they do not seemingly fall within the scope of qisas. This transpires because at that moment they are completing the qisas by enacting the boundary and clearing their accounts. Consequently, retribution is not obligatory upon the one who enacts the boundary. By imposing boundaries, they cleanse the matters pertaining to ⟪ar:حدود سابقہ⟫ the previous boundaries—not their own. Knowledge of all these subtleties rests solely with Allah. Therefore, the Messenger of Allah proclaimed: "Not a single splinter shall afflict you, nor shall any vein cause confusion, nor shall your foot slip, except on account of your own sin."

When ⟪ar:امیر المؤمنین علی ابن ابی طالب⟫ Amīr al-Muʾminīn ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib would flog someone, for each blow his garment would be altered. In this capacity, all punishments enacted by the Imams are in reality a mercy and blessing upon the sinner—sparing him from the torments (al-dharakāt, الدراكات) that would otherwise befall him after the boundary is imposed.[^cite-11]

## The Imams and Summoners: Foreknowledge and Prophecy

If a person possesses authority and agency, how then can the Summoners (al-duʿāt, الدعات) foretell a person's future? Does such foreknowledge not imply compulsion upon the person?

The Summoner may indeed foretell a person's future because he possesses awareness of that person's power and the circumstances of compulsion under which he operates. That is, he perceives where a person's authority extends and under what conditions he will be constrained by the consequences of his prior deeds. True authority resides in the person's own knowledge of where he is being constrained.

All the stratagems of the Imam are at work. In his era, whose accounts shall be fulfilled by means of qisas, the matter is already settled. Due to a person's past transgressions, he harbors wicked thoughts; and circumstances arise in which these base inclinations may easily find expression and he may readily commit sin. His temperament and disposition not only incline him toward this transgression on account of previous matters, but render him eager for it. In other words, every accessory and condition is made available to him to commit the sin. Yet even in such circumstances, ultimate authority remains with the person to refrain from sin despite his overwhelming desire. However, this refraining becomes exceedingly difficult. Because the Summoner beholds the light of Allah, he becomes aware of all these matters and prophesies accordingly—prophecies that come to pass.

Similarly, when a Summoner foretells that someone shall be slain, he perceives the moment of the victim's death on one hand and reads the killer's malevolent intention on the other. He witnesses how the causes of the murder shall arise for the murderer and how the victim shall meet the killer at the appointed hour, thereby creating the conditions for the killing.[^cite-12]

## The Swine and the Children: A Tale of Retribution and Compassion

⟪ar:امام جعفر الصادق علیہ السلام⟫Once, whilst Imam Jaʿfar al-Ṣādiq traveled with his companions along a path, he beheld a swine being pelted with stones by children, its body covered with wounds and blood. The animal staggered toward a house and collapsed, overcome. After some time, a woman emerged, observed the swine in its dreadful state, and moved by pity, poured a bucket of cold water upon the creature, which revived it and departed.

⟪ar:امام جعفر الصادق علیہ السلام⟫When queried about the inward meaning of this incident, the Imam disclosed: "This swine, a most wicked creature, was formerly a teacher of these very children. In his prior state as a man of great stature, he would oppress and beat them without cause. This day, those children are completing their retribution. And the woman who poured water upon him in compassion was his sister in a previous existence. His family had falsely accused her of adultery; she maintained her innocence, yet none believed her. All her relatives, even her own parents, stood against her—yet her brother alone believed in her innocence, supported her, and defended her. This day, by pouring water upon him, the sister repays her brother's kindness."[^cite-13]

### ʿAlī and the Qur'an

Numerous parallels exist between the Qur'an and the life of Maulana ʿAlī (peace be upon him), for he is a kinsman of the Qur'an in intimate correspondence with it. Just as the sequence of the Qur'an's chapters was rearranged, so too was the order of Maulana ʿAlī and his boundaries altered. And he remained silent regarding both matters. Had he not done so, both the Qur'an and Islam would have perished. He endured immense suffering to preserve the faith. This brave man, against whom warriors ⟪ar:عبد وُد⟫⟪ar:مرحب و عنتر⟫ such as ʿAbd al-Wudd, Marhab, and ʿAntar bound ropes and dragged him through the marketplace, yet he remained silent. The House of Honor was set ablaze before his eyes, and the door was breached, crushing his pregnant wife ⟪ar:مولاتنا فاطمۃ الزھرا⟫⟪ar:مولانا محسن⟫ Maulana Muḥsin within, resulting in miscarriage; yet he maintained silence even over this, all because Allah had commanded his silence. In truth, these were not ⟪ar:مولانا علی علیہ السلام⟫ his own revenges, but rather matters pertaining to his station—matters for which he bore responsibility to settle.[^cite-14]

## "I Have Succeeded, By the Lord of the Kaʿbah"

When ⟪ar:ابن ملجم لعین⟫ Ibn Muljam (the accursed) struck his sword upon the head of ⟪ar:امیر المومنین علی ابن ابی طالب علیہ السلام⟫ Amīr al-Muʾminīn ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib on the nineteenth of ⟪ar:شہراللہ⟫ Shahrallāh (the month of Allah, Ramaḍān), these words escaped his lips: ⟪ar-quote:فُضتُ وَرَبِّ الکَعبَ�ة⟫ "I have succeeded, by the Lord of the Kaʿbah!" After the divine dispensation, no Imam possessed the courage to utter these words with his own tongue. He prays that herein lies the lowest and final veil—⟪ar:'علی ابن ابی طالب'⟫ the one regarding whom he speaks—who declared himself to be ⟪ar:عین عظیمہ⟫ the great eye (the supreme witness). That is to say, this veil confessed that he was the one who ⟪ar:قائم المنتظر علی ذکرہ السلام⟫ would receive the caliphate of the Awaited Establisher—he who is ⟪ar:محتجب (عین اولۃ)⟫ veiled (the eye of the first order) and ⟪ar:محتجب (عین آخرۃ)⟫ veiled (the eye of the final order). Or rather, that he would seize the caliphate of the Establisher, which is in truth his success—the fulfillment of his mission.

| --- | --- |
| The Lord of the Kaʿbah | Ayn al-Ākhirah (the veiled eye of the final order); that is, you received a testament from the hereafter |
| Success / Fulfillment | The attainment of the caliphate of Ayn Ula |

Success bears yet another meaning. ⟪ar:آدم علیہ السلام⟫⟪ar:جاہدین⟫⟪ar:کافرین⟫⟪ar:منافقین⟫⟪ar:قاسطین⟫From the epoch of the Prophet Adam to the final dispensation, innumerable battles were waged wherein believers fought at the command of the Imams and Prophets, slaying disbelievers, hypocrites, rebels, and those who violated their covenant. Moreover, the prophets imposed the divine boundaries of the Sharīʿah upon the people of their times and visited various punishments upon them—flogging, execution, and death. Though all these deeds were performed at Allah's command, because their performance caused anguish to the unbelievers and revealed the corruption of their hearts, retribution was thereby created between the two parties.

The burden of this retribution grew so great that no Imam possessed the capacity to bear it—that is, no Imam had the strength to discharge this qisas. On the night of the nineteenth, when ⟪ar:أمیر المومنین⟫ Amīr al-Muʾminīn entered the mosque of Kūfah, he perceived ⟪ar:ابن ملجم لعین⟫ Ibn Muljam sleeping upon the mosque stairs. He stumbled upon him and awakened him. Thereafter, on that very day, ʿAlī himself called the adhān (the call to prayer), by which all the people of Kūfah were summoned to the mosque.

It is said: "When he stumbled upon Ibn Muljam, all ⟪ar:صورات خبیثہ⟫ those malevolent forms who were to exact retribution from the blood of Ras and Zaynab congregated around him." Moreover, his sword, which he had sheathed a thousand times in the past, was entirely derived ⟪ar:حصالات⟫ from the waste from Ras and Zaynab. From ⟪ar:ابن ملجم لعین⟫ your stumbling upon Ibn Muljam, all ⟪ar:صورات خبیثہ⟫ the other wicked forms were gathered into ⟪ar:حصالات خبیثہ⟫ a single wicked vessel—the sword and poison. On the other hand, by calling the adhān, he aroused and assembled all the people of his congregation who themselves owed retribution. Thus he awakened both parties! One by stumbling, and one by the call to prayer. When ⟪ar:ابن ملجم لعین⟫ Ibn Muljam struck him upon the head, the great retribution uttered by his lips—⟪ar:فضت و رب الکعبۃ⟫ "I have succeeded, by the Lord of the Kaʿbah"—was completed. That is, whatever work I came into this world to perform, I have done, and I have succeeded.

⟪ar:ابن ملجم⟫The sword of Ibn Muljam fell in the place where ⟪ar:عمرو بن عبد وُد⟫ the sword of ʿAmr ibn ʿAbd al-Wudd had struck. This created the shape of two horns upon his head, and he became known as ⟪ar:'ذوالقرنین'⟫ *Dhū al-Qarnayn* (he of the two horns). In his lifetime, he received only two head wounds. The first came when ʿAbd al-Wudd struck him most violently, piercing his shell and wounding his face. ⟪ar:عبد وُد⟫ ʿAbd al-Wudd was clad from head to foot in armor. In return, ʿAlī struck him upon the head with such force that his sword passed through the head and exited, splitting it in two. ʿAbd al-Wudd's sister came weeping and demanded: "Who killed my brother?" When told it was ⟪ar:علی ابن ابی طالب⟫ ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib, she swore by ⟪ar:لات و عُزَّا⟫ the idols al-Lāt and al-ʿUzzā: "Had he been killed by an ordinary man, I would weep and mourn all my life. He was slain by Jarīʾ" (a name of ʿAlī in pre-Islamic times).

Another occurrence transpired when Amīr al-Muʾminīn was struck upon the head by ⟪ar:ابن ملجم لعین⟫ Ibn Muljam. By human reckoning, he should have fainted from the profusion of blood; yet he resisted and remained conscious until the sun rose. Upon witnessing the sunrise, he addressed the sun: "Come ⟪ar:شمس⟫ and bear witness! You have never slumbered before my rising, therefore you shall not sleep this day!"[^cite-15]

## The Martyrdom of Imam al-Ḥusayn

⟪ar:عبد اللہ ابن عباس⟫⟪ar:امام حسین علیہ السلام⟫Regarding Imam al-Ḥusayn, ʿAbdullah ibn ʿAbbās declared: ⟪ar-quote:العراقی لا تطاع والکوفی لا یوفی⟫ "Iraq shall never obey, and the Kūfī shall never prove faithful." Imam al-Ḥusayn, aware of this destiny, deemed it prudent to withdraw. The verse ⟪ar:انا اصابتکم بمصیبتکم قالو انا للّٰہ و انا الیہ راجعون⟫ came to bear upon his heart. He journeyed to the grave of the Prophet and then proceeded to Mecca. On the next day after arriving—⟪ar:۷ ذی الحجہ⟫ the seventh of Dhū al-Ḥijjah—he did not complete the Ḥajj. This was because he anticipated that Yazīd's followers, should they discover the opportunity, would slay him, thereby extinguishing his name and erasing Islam from the earth. Upon departing, he carried with him all the blessings of Allah's House. This year, no pilgrim received the reward of Ḥajj, for he bore away all its blessings. That year was meant to unite both Ḥajj and Jihād. ⟪ar:امام حسین⟫ Imam al-Ḥusayn felt the weight of the burden he was destined to discharge—the settlement of all debts that had accrued since the time of Adam, and the recompense owed to his parents.[^cite-16]

Though the nineteenth day of Allah (referring to ʿAlī) was consummated by the Master of the Cosmos, one final chapter remained, which ⟪ar:امام حسین علیہ السلام⟫ Imam al-Ḥusayn completed at Karbalā. Furthermore, there exists a second perspective: those who had fought at the command of ʿAlī were not all innocent. When they struck their enemies, they exceeded the measure, inflicting two or more blows when one sufficed, thereby creating a new retribution. Since they had fought under ʿAlī's orders, ʿAlī was obliged to settle this account. But ʿAlī was martyred; and as a father's debt passes to his son, ⟪ar:حسین علیہ السلام⟫ al-Ḥusayn completed this installment at Karbalā. Allah Most High declares in the Qur'an:

Thus, when on that final day he was asked why he was being slain, the perpetrators replied in unison: ⟪ar-quote:لِبُغضِ أَبیہ⟫ "Out of hatred for your father." ⟪ar:بدر و حنین و خیبر و خندق⟫ The malevolent forms from Badr, Ḥunayn, Khaybar, and al-Khandaq—⟪ar:صورات خبیثہ⟫ those wicked entities—were crying out with their malice and shrieks. ⟪ar:حصالات خبیثہ⟫ They had emerged from the waste derived from Ras and Zaynab who had been slain, and gathered around Imam al-Ḥusayn and his company of believers who had likewise been killed, so that the reckoning was accomplished. The water was withheld from him and his companions because all the water of the Euphrates River flowed from the waste—the forbidden refuse forbidden to the Ahl al-Bayt. For this reason, not one of his companions received even a single drop. He prays that the final installment of qisas was collected upon his throne. This is why when ⟪ar:شمر لعین⟫ Shimr (the accursed) in his anger attempted to cut al-Ḥusayn's throat, his blade fell short; it seemed as though the sword had grown inert. This occurred because there remained no qisas at that juncture.

The Prophet declared: ⟪ar-quote:یَا حُسَین أَنَّا لَکَ الدَّرَجَۃ لَم تَنَلھاَ اِلاَّ بِالشَّھاَدَۃ⟫ "O al-Ḥusayn, I bestow upon you a rank that shall not be attained save through martyrdom." Al-Ḥusayn responded: ⟪ar-quote:اللّٰھم الجعلنی من الشاکرین⟫ "O Allah, make me among the grateful"—he did not say among the patient ones (al-ṣābirīn). That is, he regarded his martyrdom not as a calamity but as a blessing. The eagerness to attain this rank drew these words from his lips. He was fifty-eight years of age. He endured three days of thirst, ten days of hunger, the searing heat of Iraq, and the terror of a multitude. Yet his fortitude was youthful, sustained by the goal of martyrdom before him.[^cite-17]

The mystics relate that the veils in the realm of Karbalā changed so rapidly regarding ⟪ar:امام حسین علیہ السلام⟫ Imam al-Ḥusayn that their description strains the capacity of words. The concept resembles a long procession in which, one after another, souls stepped forward and completed their narratives. It has been disclosed that retribution was not obligatory upon all his companions. They were all pure and sanctified, yet they were cleansing the people around them. The veils of ⟪ar:امام حسین⟫ Imam al-Ḥusayn, ⟪ar:مولانا عباس⟫ Maulana al-ʿAbbās, ⟪ar:حانی بن عروۃ⟫ Ḥānī ibn ʿUrwah, and ⟪ar:حضرت مسلم بن عقیل⟫ Muslim ibn ʿAqīl were among the honored ones.

### Why Do We Mourn?

Ḥazrat Zia ʿAlī Ṣāḥib was asked: "Since we understand that Imam al-Ḥusayn and his companions did not endure hardship at Karbalā, and all their circumstances pertain to the retribution and purification of their people, why then do we mourn on the day of ʿĀshūrāʾ?" He replied: "We mourn because these people slew him believing him to be al-Ḥusayn!" The very al-Ḥusayn whom the Prophet loved so dearly, who was a piece of Fāṭimah's liver, a fragment of her very being! We mourn the brutal and savage treatment meted out to the Messenger of Allah, that most revered one who suffered so greatly for our salvation and who established the upright religion (al-dīn al-ḥanīf, الحنیف) without which we could never be delivered from ⟪ar:ھیولی عالم⟫ the primordial matter of the world.[^cite-18]
