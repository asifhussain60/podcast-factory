## Mālik ibn al-Ḥārith al-Ashtar al-Nakhaʿī — Companion-General of Imam ʿAlī

A figure of extraordinary courage and martial prowess, Mālik al-Ashtar stood as the most trusted military commander of ⟪ar:علی ابن ابی طالب⟫ ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib. The medieval scholar ⟪ar:علامہ ابن حدید⟫ Ibn Abī al-Ḥadīd observed that one would be justified in claiming no warrior of comparable stature—neither before nor after—had ever arisen in the world. A Yemenite by origin, Mālik received his epithet "al-Ashtar" (*the scarred*) when a lance-blow during the Battle of ⟪ar:یرموک⟫ Yarmouk severed the lower portion of his eye, leaving a visible mark that would distinguish him ever after.

His prowess in battle became legendary. During the Battle of al-Jamal, he single-handedly severed three legs of the rebel camel and forced it to ground; when ⟪ar:عبد اللہ بن زبیر⟫ ʿAbd Allāh ibn al-Zubayr attacked him in fury, Mālik seized him with a spear, hurled him to the earth, and stood over him in victory before releasing him unharmed. Fasting while fighting and having gone two days without food, his strength remained undiminished—a testament to the hardness of his spirit.[^cite-1]

The singular distinction of Mālik's swordplay lay in this: each blow clove his adversary into two perfectly balanced halves. When he once boasted to the Imam of this perfection, ʿAlī replied with serene acknowledgment: "Indeed, my lord—but between your work and mine there stands a manifest difference: when you weigh the two severed portions of any man you have slain, the scales shall always show them equal. When I strike, the balance never wavers." This cryptic saying preserved within the tradition alludes to the Imam's knowledge of the inward reality (al-ḥaqīqa) behind every visible act.[^cite-2]

At ⟪ar:لیلۃ الحریر⟫ Laylat al-Ḥarīr (*Night of Silk*), where 70,000 fell, the armies' confidence collapsed when ⟪ar:عمروبن العاص⟫ ʿAmr ibn al-ʿĀṣ lifted the Qur'ān upon spear-points and called halt to the slaughter. When the Master summoned his general back, Mālik burned with desire to return and complete the victory, yet the Lord withheld him—for the dissemblers had threatened to depose the Imam if the commander were not recalled. When asked later whether he had observed *tasbīḥ* (the glorification of God) that night of silence and blood, Mālik answered without hesitation: "Yes, I glorified Him throughout."

His character extended far beyond the battlefield. Once, passing through the marketplace, he was accosted by a foolish man who ate a date and hurled the refuse at him in mockery. Mālik took no notice and proceeded to the mosque. A bystander, upon learning the offender's target was none other than the Imam's commander-in-chief, rushed to the man and disclosed his error. The fool ran in mortification to the mosque, found Mālik emerging, and threw himself at his feet begging pardon. The general smiled and said with gentle grace: "Brother, I harbor no anger toward you. I entered the mosque to offer prayers and to seek forgiveness on your behalf." Such magnanimity revealed the depth of his spiritual station.[^cite-3]

Mālik harbored a fondness for honey. When ⟪ar:محمد بن ابی بکر⟫ Muhammad ibn Abī Bakr, the Imam's governor of Egypt, summoned him to offer aid, Mālik departed for that province. Yet treachery lay in wait: Mu'āwiya conspired with the tax-collector of Qalzum to poison the general's honey. When the cursed Mu'āwiya learned of the deed, he exulted in impious triumph: "My army lives in honey!"—a boast that revealed the depths of his malice. Upon hearing the news, the usurper uttered words that have echoed across the centuries: "ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib possessed two hands. One"—⟪ar:عمار بن یاسر⟫ ʿAmmār ibn Yāsir—"was severed at Ṣiffīn; the other"—⟪ar:مالک اشتر⟫ Mālik al-Ashtar—"has been severed this day."

When the news of his martyrdom reached the Master of the Universe, ⟪ar:علی ابن ابی طالب⟫ ʿAlī wept with inconsolable sorrow: "The rank of Mālik was as the rank of the Prophet himself. My back has been broken. By God, you comprehend not what this master was! The example of his station is that of a lofty and unshakeable mountain, which no mounted warrior can scale and no bird can reach in its heights." The profound lament of the Imam stands as witness to the spiritual stature of Mālik ibn al-Ḥārith al-Ashtar al-Nakhaʿī. When ⟪ar:اشعس کندی⟫ al-Ashʿath al-Kindī received news of his martyrdom, he declared: "With the death of Mālik al-Ashtar, I now believe—truly believe—in death."

### Mālik's Identity in the Words of His Wife Ḥājira

While Mālik sat in prayer within the mosque, his wife ⟪ar:حاجرۃ⟫ Ḥājira approached with their son ⟪ar:ابراھیم⟫ Ibrāhīm. Perpetually absent from home due to the demands of warfare, Mālik remained unknown to his own child, who asked his mother: "Mother, who is this man?" Ḥājira replied with a portrait drawn from intimate knowledge:

He supports the ⟪ar:دُل دُل⟫ Dul-dul, the horse of the Prophet, and the ⟪ar:ذوالفقار⟫ Dhū-l-Faqār, the sword of the Imam. Very little does he speak, and only as necessity demands. Look into his eyes, my son—poverty is reflected there. You shall find him weeping only in prayer. No peace settles upon him until his vessels are thoroughly cleansed. His home is a horse, a saddle, and a stirrup; he never tires of travelling. His legacy is patience and grief. The valley of his heart is illuminated and inhabited by the light of Imāmate. He moves always in the shadow of ʿAlī and Fāṭima, never asking when or whither. He is a devoted follower of the Imam's way and speech. This is your father, my beloved—this is Mālik al-Ashtar.

Upon hearing this unveiling of his father's essence, Ibrāhīm replied in wonder: "Whenever I have asked, I have received a new answer. If only this man's name were the sea!"

On another occasion, when Ḥājira praised her husband and recalled the saying of ʿAlī—"Mālik is to the Messenger what I am to the Messenger of Allāh"—Mālik fell silent. His wife pressed: "What the Imam did and achieved, let it remain. But I wonder: what am I now, and what can I do for the Imam?" The question drew no reply that night.

Mālik would remain awake throughout the darkness in prayer and spiritual practice. His wife, moved by concern, asked: "O Lord, do you never sleep?" He replied: "While sleep was being apportioned among men, I was engaged in practicing the sword, following the path of ʿAlī, so that I might slay my lower self (*nafs*) as he slew ⟪ar:عمرو ابن عبد ود⟫ ʿAmr ibn ʿAbd Wudd. By the time sleep came round to me, it had already been shattered." Ḥājira asked: "Is the answer to my question a riddle?" Mālik responded with gravity: "I remain awake so that I may hear the sounds of ʿAlī's supplications from the oasis of Kūfa." Moved by his words, Ḥājira said: "Master! You once asked me to explain the meaning of love. I understand your answer now. You told me then that **love is the name of a state—like the state of Mālik in relation to ʿAlī.**"

### In the Court of Mālik al-Ashtar: Mu'āwiya's Encounter

In the court of Mu'āwiya, Mālik's presence commanded such awe that even brave men fell silent. Once, when Mālik al-Ashtar arrived bearing a sealed letter from the Master, ⟪ar:علی ابن ابی طالب⟫ Maulanā ʿAlī, he sat beside the usurper's throne. When Mu'āwiya demanded the letter, Mālik replied with unsheathed defiance: "Descend from your throne and take it from my hand!" The minister ⟪ar:عمرو ابن عاس⟫ ʿAmr ibn al-ʿĀṣ retrieved the sealed missive on Mu'āwiya's behalf.

As Mu'āwiya read the letter, he threatened and boasted: "O general, my army is as numerous as the stars in the heavens!" Mālik answered with crystalline clarity: "My Lord ʿAlī is the sun; when he rises, all stars fade from fear of his light." Mu'āwiya pressed further: "The number of my soldiers rivals the grains of grain scattered upon the earth." Mālik replied: "ʿAlī has a rooster named Mālik. When that rooster is hungry, it devours every grain before it and leaves none remaining." This exchange, uttered in the very court of the usurper while standing alone against him, demonstrates the extraordinary courage and unshakeable faith of Mālik ibn al-Ḥārith al-Ashtar al-Nakhaʿī.

## Uways al-Qaranī — The Yemeni Devotee of the Prophet

A resident of Syria, Ḥaḍrat Uwais al-Qaranī (*Uways of Qarn*) harbored such a consuming love for the Holy Prophet that his heart could scarcely contain it. He yearned with every fiber to behold the Prophet's face, yet his mother—aged and blind—forbade him to journey to Madina. The distance became unbearable torment; he questioned every traveler from Madina about the Prophet's appearance, asking whether his eyebrows were joined or separated, seeking to construct within his mind an image of the Beloved whom he had never seen. When no traveler could answer to his satisfaction, he would close his eyes and invoke an inner vision, declaring: "Yes, my beloved Prophet's eyebrows are surely joined."

When news reached him of the Prophet's martyrdom in the Battle of ⟪ar:احد⟫ Uḥud, Uwais pulled forth all his teeth in an agony of grief. Witnessing such devotion, his mother at last permitted him to journey to Madina, yet urged him to return by evening. He hastened forth, but when he arrived, the Prophet had departed the city. Uwais waited in floods of tears, then returned at nightfall without having beheld the Master. When the Prophet returned to Madina, he said: "I perceive a light shining and I smell a fragrance." Who has come hither? Upon learning it was Uwais, the Prophet affirmed: "Yes, indeed—my father has visited." Yet throughout his life, Uwais al-Qaranī never succeeded in meeting the Holy Prophet face to face.

When someone asked the Messenger of Allāh why Uwais, if his love were so great, never came to meet him, the Prophet replied with profound wisdom: "Two necessities constrain him. His mother is old and blind; service to her is obligatory upon him by divine law. Secondly, he tends camels and sustains himself and his mother from their yield. He cannot abandon these duties to visit me. But know"—and here the Prophet touched the heart of the matter—"that physical meeting is not necessary for true knowledge. The knowledge of the Real is a light that Allāh bestows upon whichever of His servants He wills." He then turned to ⟪ar:علی⟫ ʿAlī and said: "O ʿAlī! You shall meet Uwais, and you shall recognize him by two signs: a light radiating from his palm, and another emanating from his ribs."[^cite-4]

Uways al-Qaranī devoted his existence to worship (ʿibāda). Hours passed in bowing and prostration; he would declare: "My Lord is eternal and infinite. Were it not for the constraints of time and space, my bowings and prostrations would be continuous from eternity to eternity. This is the debt owed to such a Lord." His counsel to others was severe: "Walk the path of Allāh in such solitude that none treads it save you and your Lord. When you pray, enter so deeply into prayer that even an arrow striking you would pass unnoticed."

At the Battle of Ṣiffīn, when the Master of the Universe ⟪ar:علی ابن ابی طالب⟫ ʿAlī awaited reinforcement, he declared: "A thousand horsemen shall come to our aid—not one less, not one more!" As the cavalry approached, Amir al-Mu'minīn observed that one horse was missing. Then, with the certainty of one who beholds the unseen, he said: "Wait—there can be no reduction. He approaches now." From the distance appeared a single mounted figure: Uways al-Qaranī, who had ridden across Syria to pledge his allegiance. When he arrived and clung to the feet of the Master, seeking to swear bay'ā (fealty), the Imam said to him: "O Uways! Your allegiance was already sworn in eternity—you are the eternal lover!" Uwais replied: "O Master, that was an allegiance of life. I now seek allegiance unto death." The Imam gave him glad tidings of martyrdom and showed him the very place in the battlefield where he would fall. In this manner did Uwais al-Qaranī achieve his desire, martyred at Ṣiffīn, his station known only to those possessed of true knowledge (*ahl al-ḥaqāʾiq*).[^cite-5]

## Sayyidnā al-Muʾayyad al-Shīrāzī (may God elevate his rank) — Fatimid Chief Daʿwa Officer

The succession of Summoners (*du'āt*, plural of dāʿī) commenced with Sayyidina Dhuʿayb ibn Mūsa and has continued to the present day, producing fifty-two (52) successive preachers—each authorized by the Imam to proclaim the summons (*daʿwa*, the invitation to the faith). Among these, Sayyidina Hibatallāh ibn Mūsa ibn Dāwūd ibn Ādam al-Shīrāzī al-Sulaymānī earned the distinction of "Father of All Summoners" (Abū al-Duʿāt), while the lady of the succession, known as "Mother of the Summoners" (Umm al-Duʿāt), held complementary spiritual authority.

Sayyidina al-Muʾayyad (*the Aided, the Strengthened*) al-Shīrāzī served as the chief dāʿī during the reign of Maulanā Maʿad al-Mustanṣir bi-Llāh (the 18th Imām), to whom the Imam bestowed upon him the honorific title "al-Muʾayyad"—signifying one upon whom divine assistance rests without intermission. Like Maulanā Salmān al-Fārsī, who was a Persian by origin, al-Muʾayyad was not of Arab birth but rather from ʿAjam (the Persian-speaking lands), yet his spiritual station transcended all ethnic boundaries.

The designation "Sulaymānī" (*of the house and lineage of Sulaymān*) applied to al-Muʾayyad for two reasons. First, it connected him to the household of Sayyidina Sulaymān ibn Ḥasan ibn Yūsuf ibn Sulaymān (may Allāh be pleased with him), from whom the succession of the Hidden Imāms in the era of Occultation (*ʿasr al-Qāʾim*) would emanate. Second, it marked a continuity of spiritual authority reaching back to the prophetic line itself.[^cite-6]

In those early centuries, no written texts preserved the teachings; rather, when one dāʿī departed this life, the Imām would appoint a successor to continue the oral transmission. The continuity rested entirely upon the Imām's choice and guidance. As the age of the Minor Occultation drew toward its close and the Imāms themselves began their complete withdrawal from the visible realm, Sayyidina al-Muʾayyad and Maulanā Ḥurrat al-Mulkah (*the Free Woman of Dominion*, known also as Sayyida ʿArwāḥ bint Aḥmad) labored together to consolidate and formalize the philosophical and spiritual sciences of the Imāmate. Together they prepared Sayyidina Dhuʿayb ibn Mūsa to anchor the chain of succession at the threshold of concealment.

Sayyidina al-Muʾayyad al-Shīrāzī stands as the first dāʿī to formalize the philosophical science of cosmogony (ʿilm al-mabādiʾ, the science of origins) into a systematic doctrine. Before his era, the deeper meanings embedded within the sciences of the Prophets lay concealed within the wisdom (*ḥikma*) of Maulanā ʿAlī and the other Imāms, veiled from the uninitiated.[^cite-7]

Yet despite his exalted station, the Imām subjected al-Muʾayyad to trials so severe that had another soul occupied his place, apostasy would surely have followed. While residing in the same city as the Imām, he was denied the privilege of his Master's presence for five years—a separation orchestrated by his own uncle, a minister in the Imām's court, who jealously guarded his own influence and feared the young preacher's rising eminence.

One day, Sayyidina al-Muʾayyad received a pamphlet bearing the Imām's official seal, bearing this command: "We order you to depart for battle at once!" Al-Muʾayyad perceived the test and wrote upon the reverse: "O Maulanā al-Mustanṣir! If the hands of the believer are severed, there remains no other al-Muʾayyad in your time!" In truth, his treacherous uncle had forged the order, intending to send him to war and secure his martyrdom. Yet al-Muʾayyad, supported by the Imām's invisible aid, recognized the deception and responded with audacity that no other soul would have dared attempt. He himself later testified: "Had I been born in the time of the Prophet Muḥammad, my spiritual station would not have fallen short of that of Salmān al-Fārsī himself."[^cite-8]

## Al-Muʾayyad's Verses to Imām al-Mustanṣir bi-Llāh and the Imām's Reply

Sayyidina al-Muʾayyad endured ceaseless anguish by day and night from the grief of separation from his Master. At last, unable to contain the burning of his heart, he gave voice to his sorrow in verse. The Imām, in response, honored him with a reply in the same poetic form, affirming: "Your station with us is precisely that which Maulanā Salmān al-Fārsī held with the Messenger of Allāh (peace be upon him). The more years We have withheld from you the sight of Our face, the more We have elevated your rank." Indeed, this reply of the Imām functioned as a concealed text proclaiming al-Muʾayyad's exalted degree as *Bāb al-Inbaʿāth* (*the Gate of Emanation*)—a station of cosmic significance.

### Verses of Sayyidina Al-Muʾayyad to the Imām

*I swear by Your grace: though You crown me with the diadem of Khosra,*  
*King of the East, and entrust to me dominion over all creation—*  
*Past ages and those yet to unfold,*  
*I would declare: "This moment, O my Lord—  
*Do not withhold from me this one moment of Your sight!"*  
*For separation from You, even for an instant, ages me a lifetime.*

### The Reply of Maulanā al-Mustanṣir bi-Llāh (upon him be peace)

*O thou whose fame is celebrated throughout the world!*  
*O mountain of knowledge, beneath whose weight knowledge-seekers bow!*  
*We have not closed Our doors to you save for grievous cause,*  
*For Our hearts ache at your sorrow.*  
*We have imposed no restraint upon you—hold fast to Our love,*  
*For the heart's tribulation is the tribulation of the heart.*  
*We did not wish to tell you Our doors were closed,*  
*Yet, as a compassionate father closes the door upon his child for his protection,*  
*So We closed Our doors to you.*  
*Our Shīʿas have lost their way—East and West alike.*  
*We are the Father of the Revelation's Father, the Father of the Prophet.*  
*Impart to them what knowledge you will from Our treasury,*  
*And be unto them as a merciful father.*  
*Though you have arrived at the end of Our feast,*  
*You have taken precedence over all who came before.*  
*There is none in this age like unto you,*  
*Neither shall there be any in ages past or future.*  
*Had you lived in the time of the Prophet Muḥammad,*  
*You would not have been inferior to Salmān al-Fārsī!*[^cite-9]
