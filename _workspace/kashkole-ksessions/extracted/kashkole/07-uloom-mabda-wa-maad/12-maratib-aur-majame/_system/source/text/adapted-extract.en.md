## The Primordial Body and the Virtuous Person

With the assistance of the heavens and the earth, the first human creation and their leader and Imam came into being simultaneously. Twenty-eight sanctified beings were brought forth from pits established at the site of the Baitullah. These individuals epitomized the human race and embodied the essence of both the heavenly realm and the world of nature; they were themselves the ⟪ar:حروف معجم⟫ (letters of the alphabet) and the embodiment of the twenty-eight stages of the lunar cycle.[^cite-1] These pits were arranged in hierarchical order, mirroring how an Imam stands before his followers. The foremost among them was the most subtle, purest, and noblest—one whom the scholars designated ⟪ar:'شخص فاضل'⟫ (the Virtuous Person). This singular individual occupied within humanity the station of precious gems among minerals, of dates among plants, and of the finest qualities among animals. He was ⟪ar:'حجاب آدم ابدائیہ'⟫ (the veil of primordial Adam), surpassing in nobility and excellence the twenty-seven beings born alongside him. The learned have explained their virtues thus: "If those twenty-seven were to dwell in the palace of precious stones, the rest of creation would dwell in the palace of common rock; yet if that Virtuous Person were to dwell in the palace of precious stones, those twenty-seven would be relegated to the palace of rock."

**Facts:**

The first to whom the Emanation was ascribed was the Virtuous Person, called ⟪ar:'کیمروت علیہ السلام'⟫ (Kaymurawt, upon him be peace). [repeated three times in source.]

### Unity of the Virtuous Person

The learned man—this foremost individual—first contemplated himself without intermediary, then his own species, then the heavens above him and beyond, then the flowing waters, and the succession of day and night. He thereby knew that there existed for him and for all creatures and worlds an *Inventor* (Mubdiʿ Taʿālā, the Originator, Most High)—one incommensurate with any of His creations. He negated for himself, for all humankind, and for all existence ⟪ar:صفت الٰہیت⟫ (the attribute of divinity), and bore witness to ⟪ar:الہیت⟫ (divinity) as belonging alone to his Creator, proclaiming ⟪ar:'وحدانیت'⟫ (unity) through ⟪ar:تسبیح و تقدیس⟫ (glorification and sanctification).[^cite-2] He was then joined to ⟪ar:مواد الٰہیۃ⟫ (divine substances) and ⟪ar:انوار شعشعانیۃ⟫ (radiant lights), together with ⟪ar:تائیدات ملکوتیۃ⟫ (lordly confirmations). Through this union, he attained the divine support by which he obtained ⟪ar:علم ماکان سیکون⟫ (knowledge of what was and what shall be) and achieved *kamāl thānī* (secondary perfection). He reached the rank called ⟪ar:(رتبہ وحدت)⟫ (the rank of unity)—the station of al-ʿAql al-Awwal (the First Intellect) in the physical world—and was named ⟪ar:' ابدأ '⟫ (the Beginning), having attained the station of origination in the world of nature and of primordial creation in the realm of spiritual initiation. He then summoned these twenty-seven souls unto Allah through the call of ⟪ar:توحید، تنزیہ اور تجرید⟫ (divine unity, transcendence, and detachment).[^cite-3] They accepted his call with alacrity, binding themselves in covenant. He taught them the distinction between the lawful and the unlawful, prescribed the ordinances of worship, and enjoined them to make these rulings and the Sunnah of his conduct known to the learned.

He then selected sixteen of the twenty-eight for positions within his daʿwa (the summons). Of these, one became his *bāb* (gate), one his *ḥujjat* (proof), one a *dāʿī* (summoner), and one a *dāʿī al-muʿallagh* (the absolute summoner). The remaining twelve he designated as ⟪ar:'حجج لیلی'⟫ (the nocturnal proofs), retaining them in his presence—those who would never turn away from him. The remaining eleven he scattered throughout ⟪ar:'حجج نہاری'⟫ (the diurnal proofs) among the islands, commanding each to go forth to their respective regions and proclaim the faith.

⟪ar:جثہ ابدائیہ⟫ (The Primordial Body) holds the highest rank after the Intellect itself. After this body, ⟪ar:حجج لیلی⟫ (the nocturnal proofs) stand foremost in excellence. By virtue of this pre-eminence, he bore the burden of their guardianship. The boundaries of the diurnal proofs extend from the outward and inward realms equally, whereas ⟪ar:حجج لیلی⟫ (the nocturnal proofs) dwell only within the inward. These twenty-eight beings are those whom ⟪ar:'صاحبان اولو العلم'⟫ (the possessors of knowledge) represent, as the Almighty has declared:

> ⟪quran 3:18⟫
> ⟪ar:‏شَهِدَ ٱللَّهُ أَنَّهُۥ لَآ إِلَهَ إِلَّا هُوَ وَٱلْمَلَٓئِكَةُ وَأُو۟لُوا۟ ٱلْعِلْمِ قَآئِمًۢا بِٱلْقِسْطِ ۚ لَآ إِلَهَ إِلَّا هُوَ ٱلْعَزِيزُ ٱلْحَكِيمُ ‎⟫
> *Allah bears witness that La ilaha illa Huwa (none has the right to be worshipped but He), and the angels, and those having knowledge (also give this witness); (He is always) maintaining His creation in Justice. La ilah illa Huwa (none has the right to be worshipped but He), the All-Mighty, the All-Wise.*

These ⟪ar:اولوالعلم⟫ (possessors of knowledge) are the twenty-eight beings born without intermediary—⟪ar:'جثہ ابدائیہ'⟫ (the primordial bodies)—whom the Almighty has made the vessels of knowledge, and who remain righteous through that knowledge eternally.

During the period of *kashf* (unveiling/revelation), the islands and the honored ones are not separate dwellings, just as in the period of concealment they remain distinct. In purity, subtlety, and knowledge during the age of kashf, the islands stand in the same condition as the honored personages. The distinction here is that ⟪ar:شخص فاضل⟫ (the Virtuous Person) retained ⟪ar:حجج لیلی⟫ (the nocturnal proofs) in his presence and dispatched ⟪ar:حجج نہاری⟫ (the diurnal proofs) to distant regions of the world, far removed from him.

## The Universal Vicegerency of the Virtuous Person

When the Virtuous Person of the Primordial Body commences his summons, his foremost intention is to prepare his successor—his true caliph—who will be his true successor in every respect. The learned person stands in the station of al-ʿAql al-Awwal (the First Intellect) within the natural world; thus his successor stands in the position of al-ʿAql al-Awwal as well. The Primordial Body established that every head of his hierarchy shall always occupy the station of *ibdāʿ* (origination), while his successor shall occupy the position of *khalīfat al-nabī* (the prophet's vicegerent). For example: the Imam occupies the station of origination, and the *bāb* (gate) occupies that of vicegerency. The true successor of origination is called al-Munbaʿith (the Emanator).

| Origination (Ibdāʿ) | Emanation (Inbʿāth) |
| --- | --- |
| First Intellect | First Emanation |
| Primordial Body | True Successor |
| Imam | Gate |

The learned man prescribed the Imamate to his son and passed, together with the twenty-seven, into the presence of the Almighty, attaining the station of intellect and becoming lord of that world—ascending to the rank of the Tenth. He shall hold this station until he produces a caliph like himself. These twenty-eight comprise your gate, who grew and matured with you.

#### (a) The Caliph of (a) is:

Although the learned man appointed his heir-son as caliph in his stead, that caliph does not equal him. He was only partially established in your rank. The partial caliph is partial; the universal caliph is universal. You shall govern until you produce a body like your own—which can occur only at the end of the final cycle. Although many primary bodies and cycles shall intervene, each possessing its own primordial body, all such intermediate caliphs remain your partial vicegerents. The true universal caliph of (a) can only be the (ultimate) Qāʾim.

**Facts:**

The first, standing in the station of (a), ranks highest because of precedence. Yet the vicegerency achieves perfection only through (the ultimate Qāʾim). The caliph is termed *ibn al-tamm* (the perfect son).

When the First [learned man] prepares his caliph, he and those in his circle pass to the Ninth Intellect; the Ninth Intellect and its circle pass to the Eighth; the Eighth and its circle pass to the Seventh; and so forth—each passing downward—until the Third Intellect and its circle reach the realm of the Second Intellect.[^cite-4] There, applause and jubilation echo throughout Paradise (meaning *al-Maʾwā*, the Abode). No rank surpasses this except that of the First Intellect—the realm of unity itself, the station of *Sidrat al-Muntahā* (the Lote-tree of the Boundary). All ten levels thus ascend with the passage of epochs and ages.

Concerning this Paradise, the Prophet (upon him be blessings and peace) declared: "Paradise is such that no eye has beheld it, no ear has heard of it, and no human heart has conceived it." Its vastness, elevation, joy, and grandeur defy complete definition; it may only be described by approximation.

The perfection of the First [learned man] shall be consummated only when he brings forth his universal caliph, the Final Qāʾim. All intermediate stations between them are partial in comparison to these two, yet universal in comparison to those beneath them. The First Cycle possesses ten stations which must be established until the world's end. Each shall manifest one after another in this world, ascending to the First to take their seat with him. When all stations have ascended, their universal caliph, the Final Qāʾim, shall appear and elevate the entire world into the spiritual realm. For this reason, the Almighty declares in the Qurʾān:

## The Ordering of the Primordial Bodies

Imām Jaʿfar al-Ṣādiq, upon him be peace, informed Umm al-Nadā that a great cycle has passed. Since then, ninety-nine primary bodies have appeared. Of the first round of the 400th cycle, seven sevens have been completed. We are approaching the completion of the seventh seven. When the Resurrection arrives, it shall gather the entire cycle (400,000 years).

Thus the 399th cycle has passed, and we now stand within the 400th. The body of the 401st cycle that shall appear thereafter, when assembled, will be the final (400th) body in comprehensive array. Its magnitude shall be so great that it appears in the Kaaba itself. Only the first body ever appeared in the House of Allah before this.

Within these 400 cycles, there are forty distinguished bodies appearing at every ten cycles (at the 40th, 235th, 240th, 410th, and 400th cycles). These ten bodies exceed the others in magnitude, yet none can match the first body of the second cycle. And exceeding even that (the first body of the second cycle) is the one that appeared at the beginning of the first cycle. Its equal shall be only its universal caliph, who appears at the end of the final cycle.

All these spaces are sacred places. At the end of the second cycle, the sovereign who emerges shall gather his second cycle, and his seat shall be in the *ʿAfaq* (Horizon) of the first cycle. Upon arriving there, he shall receive a second throne. When the third cycle begins, the First—the body of the first cycle—shall bestow the final two bodies to the third cycle; that third body becomes the caliph of the second part. This continues until the final cycle is revealed, which becomes the universal caliph of the first cycle.

**Facts:**

The second cycle is preparing to produce a third cycle like itself.

(Lessons from *Anwār al-Ṭāʾifa* — Sayyidinā Muḥammad ibn Ṭāhir)

## The Tenth Horizon

When the son of this learned man (the son of the Virtuous Person) was established in his father's place through explicit designation, he commenced his summons to this world according to his primary plan, calling the people to the unity of Allah in order to liberate and rescue those souls trapped in the torrent of passion and bound in captivity. His secondary strategy was to extract his successor (caliph) from his summons, just as his father had prepared him. When he has prepared his son and completed his service, he shall take his congregation to the *ʿAfaq* (Horizon) of the First. Since the First is himself his father, he will pass from this world and return to his father's *barzakh* (partition between worlds), where he shall have an abode. He shall not attain the seat of the universal Qāʾim; that place belongs only to the founder of the final cycle. Likewise, whoever perishes in your station after you shall have an abode established in the Horizon of the First. The Horizon of the First is the *barzakh* where all such departed souls shall go and rest. For this reason, the Almighty declares (in the meaning of His words):

> ⟪ar:لَعَلِّى أَعْمَلُ صَـٰلِحًۭا فِيمَا تَرَكْتُ ۚ كَلَّآ ۚ إِنَّهَا كَلِمَةٌ هُوَ قَآئِلُهَا ۖ وَمِن وَرَآئِهِم بَرْزَخٌ إِلَىٰ يَوْمِ يُبْعَثُونَ⟫
> *That I may do good in that which I have left behind. By no means! It is but a word that he speaks; and before them is a barrier till the Day of Resurrection.*
> — **Quran 23:100**

#### The Seats of the Tenth Horizon

The Horizon of the Tenth is the *barzakh* for all Imāms and sovereigns. Each successive Qāʾim bestows the names of the Imāms upon the Qāʾim who comes after him. Every *Sabʿa* (seven-stage epoch) receives the form of the six Imāms preceding it (from the Barzakh of the Tenth). Similarly, each Qāʾim of every thousand years (*Ṣāḥib* A) obtains his thousand-year tenure from the Barzakh of the Tenth. Each seven-stage epoch has six seats. When these six seats of a week are aggregated and a seventh arrives, the seventh of the preceding week becomes the seventh of the following week.

One person becomes resident of a seven-stage epoch; the next becomes resident of another. That is, its essential purpose is to prepare the next authority-bearer. Although six Imāms exist between the two, these six Imāms are only the partial caliphs of this *Ṣāḥib al-Asbūʿ* (master of the week). Only the next one is the universal caliph of the first. The same principle applies everywhere.

The partial caliph is partial, and the universal caliph is universal.

## 124,000 Forms

To arrive at the Barzakh of the Tenth, it is necessary to complete the count of 124,000 forms that accumulate over 7,000 years. That is, when the first 7,000 years of the age of *kashf* (unveiling) are completed, 124,000 forms shall have been gathered in the Horizon of the Tenth (near the Barzakh or the Primordial Body).

A 7,000-year Qāʾim possesses six seats. Every thousand years, a Qāʾim manifests who gathers his thousand-year congregation and passes into the Barzakh of the Tenth. When the seventh-millennium Qāʾim appears, he embodies the culmination of his predecessor's summons and is himself the universal caliph. His progenitor is the point (*nuqṭa*) that appeared before the seven thousand years and which accumulated the seven millennia that preceded it.

Only when these 7,000-year deposits are unveiled, and the 124,000 forms accumulated there over the last 7,000 years are given to the Essence of the Qāʾim, shall a human form be constituted from their structure, containing all these forms distributed throughout its parts.[^cite-5] (There shall be 124 noble forms, and 1,000 additional forms for each of these.) It shall become the rank of a body for that essence, and the soul itself shall become the soul of this body. That is, the soul of the soul and the form of the lord affect this essence. With this assemblage, those forms shall now be able to ascend to the highest station (to a degree of the Tenth realm). Together, these two (gathered congregations) become one luminous essence. When they pass away after 7,000 years, they shall enter a state of sacred, abstract, divine intellect along with their entire congregation, distinguishable from those before them only by precedence. ## Grandfather, Opening, and Thought

The Almighty communicates with a human being in three modes, conveying His commands:

| Direct Revelation | Behind the Veil | The Sent Messenger |
| --- | --- | --- |
| By Revelation | Behind Veils | By Sending a Messenger |
| *Jad* (Grandfather) | *Fatḥ* (Opening) | *Khayāl* (Thought) |

#### Ancestor (Revelation)

The first mode is revelation, and this is the station of *jad* (the grandfather/ancestor). This position exists and holds sovereignty at all times and in all ages; it ranks highest among the three stations. Revelation is the word of Allah which emanates from the Holy Spirit and communicates through the ancestor (without intermediary) to the person entrusted with the command. It is the duty of the Almighty to convey the word of the Imām to the lower circles.

#### Opening (From Behind the Veil)

The second mode is speech from behind the veil, and this is the station of *fatḥ* (opening). That is, the commands are disclosed concealed within the veil of words. Just as the ordinances in the Qurʾān are hidden within the veil of expression. For example: explaining the meaning of "knowledge" clothed in the veil of the word "water" is an act of opening. That is, the function of opening is to deliver the inner meaning of Allah's message by clothing it in the garb of outward words. The person receiving the message thereby understands what the message truly signifies.

#### Thought (Messenger)

The third mode is the station of the *malak al-mursāl* (the sent angel) who manifests as a messenger in a form visible to the listener of the message, or whose voice the listener can hear.

The holders of these three stations at each level are called the ancestor, the opening, and the thought of that level, serving as intermediaries between it and its Lord. These three are situated in the realm of the *muḥtajab* (those veiled). Through these three messages, the Imām of Allah (the Exalted) connects with the Imām of the time. At each rank, these three hierarchies operate.

### Facts

When the Prophet (may Allah's blessings be upon him) completed forty years of prophethood, he received the prophethood anew. His muḥtajib attained the station of veil. ### Prayer at the Sixth Hour

The prayer of the Day of Ghadir is offered at the seventh hour because there are six times that signify the Seven Hours. In each case, there is a gathering of fifty-seven thousand years—that is, a gathering of one Kashf and a gathering of Seventy. When the seventy reach this age, the seventh hour shall be fulfilled.

### Man Mullah Fahza ʿAlī Mullah

Although he gave the highest of the two summons to ʿAlī in the half of Shaʿbān, his greatness remained. Because on the night of the twenty-seventh of Rajab, he received a special grace and then attained a special blessing. Because of this, his supremacy was established over all, and all the prophets who were hitherto in their respective temples were placed beneath him. Throughout his life, ʿAlī did not receive this special *nasūt qattl* (manifest destruction). The Prophet (peace be upon him) said, "Man Mullah, Fahza ʿAlī Mullah." The *Nasūt* which is constituted from the boundaries of the special nobles, and which is wrapped in *Lāhūt* (the divine dimension)—that is, Nasūt al-Khāss is so elevated that it resembles *Lāhūt*. This Nasūt was given to the Prophet for the duration of his life, while ʿAlī received it on the Day of Ghadir. Therefore, the Prophet's station was superior to ʿAlī's throughout his life.

### Change of Veil

As soon as the Prophet (peace be upon him) recited the text to Mawlānā ʿAlī, the veils of both underwent transformation—that is, ʿAlī took the veil of the Prophet, and the Prophet took the veil of ʿAlī. The *Nāṭiq* (the Speaker) and *Awsiyāʾ* (the Executors) die through their veils; thus, ʿAlī died during the Prophet's lifetime. The veil of the Prophet's death is actually the veil of ʿAyn. That is, ʿAyn died before *Mīm*.

In fact, this was the reason why ʿAlī appeared weak after the death of the Prophet (peace be upon him), and he had to remain silent for thirty years. This was because the veil of *mīm* was operating there, which was weaker than the veil of ʿAyn. Apart from this, another subtlety is that all the wondrous deeds of Mawlānā ʿAlī—the uprooting of Khaybar, the episode of the Gate of Knowledge, and others—are all found in the life of the Holy Prophet. After his death, no extraordinary deed is attributed to ʿAlī. And that is why Mawlānā ʿAlī threatened divorce to ʿĀʾishah in the Battle of al-Jamal, because the veil of the Prophet was operating there; otherwise, there would be no logic in ʿAlī divorcing the Prophet's wife.

### Delivery of Trusts

At the time of his death, the Prophet (may the blessings of Allah be upon him and his family) entrusted his remnants to ʿAlī in five ways. The last episode was the breath of the Messenger of Allah, which flowed upon ʿAlī's hand; he then wiped it upon his face. It is said that the last breath of the Prophet (may the blessings of Allah be upon him and his family) was an episode of the Islamic summons (*daʿwah*)—which ʿAlī combined with his face, that is, the invitation of faith. Thus, there was a union of Islamic summons and faith.

The *Nāṭiq* dies through his veils—that is, they employ veils for their death and burial. Since the veil was transformed, the Prophet died through the veil. Those who wrapped the Prophet's shroud were ʿAbdullāh ibn Rawāḥah; those who buried him were ʿAbdullāh ibn Masʿūd; and those who remained in the grave were Ubayy ibn Kaʿb.

## The Stations of the Panj Tan (The Five Purified Ones)

Muḥammad Muṣṭafā (may the blessings and peace of Allah be upon him and his family) carried the testimony of the Imam—and the two testimonies: *Ash-hadu an lā ilāha illā-Allāh* (I bear witness that there is no deity except Allah) and *Ash-hadu anna Muḥammadan rasūl-Allāh* (I bear witness that Muhammad is the Messenger of Allah). The people accepted Islam upon him, joined him in struggle, fulfilled the duties (the five daily prayers) and the Sunnah that he established, and adhered to him. All adhered to ʿAlī's manifest guardianship (*wilāyah*) and harbored no enmity toward him. Because of these deeds, the souls of these people became lustrous, their faces brightened, and their spirits glowed. When they died, all of them moved to the temple of Mawlānā Fāṭimah. Thus, the congregation of manifest worshippers and those who recognized ʿAlī's guardianship joined with Mawlānā Fāṭimah, and that is why she is called *Khālisāt al-Jawāhir al-Islāmiyyah* (the Pure Essence of Islamic Jewels)—because the gathering of all Islamic forms assembled with her.[^cite-11]

Mawlānā ʿAlī had a gathering of believers. In the same manner, the affairs of these two (ʿAlī and Fāṭimah) continued until Ḥasan (peace be upon him) emerged from the manifest summons, and Ḥusayn (peace be upon him) from the inner summons. ## The Universal Resurrection (*Baʿath-i Kullī*)

We believe in the resurrection of the dead. When we recite the *Shahādah* (testimony), we say, "O Allah, I am the Messenger of Allah." That is, Allah will send forth those who are in the graves. The meaning of rising from the graves is that, just as Allah created us in this sphere through our parents, He will bring us forth into the next sphere through our parents. The mother's womb is like a grave in which the father buries his seed. The nobility of humankind is that one is born through Mother and Father and enters a state of faith. However, the Messenger of Allah said that one should leave the line of parents and be born of Allah—that is, to join the body. This is called *Baʿath-i Kullī* (the Universal Resurrection), which is the honor of the Prophet. Allah says in Sūrah Yā Sīn:

> ⟪ar:يسٓ ۝ وَٱلْقُرْءَانِ ٱلْحَكِيمِ ۝ إِنَّكَ لَمِنَ ٱلْمُرْسَلِينَ ۝ عَلَىٰ صِرَٰطٍۢ مُّسْتَقِيمٍۢ⟫
> — **Quran 36:1–4**

That is, Allah addressed the Prophet and praised his greatness, then said: "You are upon the Straight Path, which you do not understand." The Straight Path in this verse refers to the path of *Jāthā Ibdāʾiyyah* (the Origination of Forms). That is, your caliphate is established in Origination, meaning that as you progress through the periods, you will join the Originator. The Prophet said in the Qur'ān:

> ⟪ar:إِنِّى تَوَكَّلْتُ عَلَى ٱللَّهِ رَبِّى وَرَبِّكُم ۚ مَّا مِن دَآبَّةٍ إِلَّا هُوَ ءَاخِذٌۢ بِنَاصِيَتِهَآ ۚ إِنَّ رَبِّى عَلَىٰ صِرَٰطٍۢ مُّسْتَقِيمٍۢ⟫

His Lord is Mawlānā Abū Ṭālib (peace be upon him)—that is, he praised his guide and mentor, saying that Mawlānā Abū Ṭālib is also standing upon the same path upon which he will enter the form of the Originator.

When we say in Sūrah al-Fātiḥah, "Ahdnā al-Ṣirāṭ al-Mustaqīm" (Guide us to the Straight Path), the "Straight Path" means that our form should not only be upon the right path but also should traverse it. That is, to move from one limit to another and reach its apex—the Temple of the Imam. Do not become fixed at any limit, but continue growing and moving forward.

## Glorification Before the Throne Verse

When asked what believers do upon entering the Imam's sacred precinct after death, the Prophet (peace and blessings of Allāh be upon him) replied: "They glorify Allāh—praise be to Him—and never cease their tasbīḥ (glorification) even for the blink of an eye." Glorification elevates the soul to a realm of inviolability. Through the sanctification and praise of the angels, an immutable quality becomes established within them: they attain ʿiṣma (protection from error), a perfection wrought by devotion alone. The Qur'ān attests to this:

Tasbīḥ (glorification) and taqdīs (sanctification) signify the attainment of knowledge of the Imam (peace be upon him). ### The Recitation of Āyat al-Kursī

The believer recites Āyat al-Kursī (the Throne Verse) after every obligatory prayer. The essential substance of this verse extends from *Allāhu lā ilāha illā huwa* ("Allāh—there is no god save He") through *al-ʿAlī al-ʿAẓīm* ("the Most High, the Most Great"). Yet before reaching its principal declaration, the Prophet (peace and blessings of Allāh be upon him) prescribed preliminary words bearing specific esoteric significance. The worshipper recites:

**Allāhu Akbar. Allāhu Akbar. Allāhu Akbar. Allāhu Akbar.**

("Allāh is Most Great" — thrice invoked, corresponding to the three grandsons each Imam designates to preserve doctrine.)

**Subḥān Allāh. Al-ḥamdu li-llāh. Lā ilāha illā Allāh. Wa-Allāhu Akbar.**

("Glory be to Allāh. Praise be to Allāh. There is no god but Allāh. And Allāh is Most Great.")

**Wa-lā ḥawla wa-lā quwwata illā bi-Allāh.**

("There is no power nor strength save in Allāh.")

Each formula addresses a distinct metaphysical principle. The triple *Allāhu Akbar* affirms the three modes of human existence (*nasūt*—the corporeal); the remaining phrases embody the stations of transcendence.

#### On *Subḥān Allāh* — Purification Through Negation

*Subḥān Allāh* is the utterance of *tanzīh* (absolute transcendence), acknowledging Allāh's eternal subsistence while purifying Him of all created attributes.[^cite-6] The word *sabḥ* (to swim) conveys the buoyant force of divine greatness: just as water prevents the swimmer from sinking, so does Allāh's immensity preserve the Imam at exalted station perpetually. Each Imam embodies a *lāhūt* (the divine aspect), which is the totality of *suwar* (forms / spiritual configurations) received in succession from his father's *daʿwa* (the summons / the invitation). Not only does each Imam receive this inheritance; he transmits it further, preparing his successor with the same noble and magnificent gathering of spiritual realities.

| **Subḥān** | **Allāh** |
|---|---|
| *Lāhūt* (Divine Aspect) | Imam |

#### On *Al-ḥamdu li-llāh* — Gratitude for Succession

Every Imam's son is destined to become prophet and stands opposite in grace to the believers.[^cite-7] Thus *al-ḥamdu li-llāh* ("Praise be to Allāh") means: Allāh is the present Imam, and *ḥamd* (praise / the lauded one) is his successor son. When this son receives the *lāhūt* from his father and himself becomes Imam (Allāh), the worshipper acknowledges the supreme accomplishment: Imam al-Zamān has prepared his true *khalīfa* (caliph / successor), the paramount task of the office. The worshipper expresses gratitude that the *daʿwa* did not lapse but was carried forward through the Imam's son. Gratitude is fulfilled only when *lāhūt* truly meets *nasūt*—only when the Imam instructs his son in the conduct of Imamate.

| **Al-ḥamdu li-llāh** | Allāh |
|---|---|
| The Imam's Son (*Nasūt*) | Imam |

#### On *Lā ilāha illā Allāh* — The Exception of the Successor

This formula represents the worshipper's acknowledgment that among all the *ḥudūd* (the limits / the ranks of the *daʿwa*), not a single one possessed the capacity to become Imam as advancement proceeded—save the Imam's own son alone. Only he possesses the authority to govern from the celestial to the terrestrial.

| **Lā Ilāha** | **Illā Allāh** |
|---|---|
| No limit qualified | Save the Imam's son |

#### On *Wa-Allāhu Akbar* — The Unsurpassable Grandeur

This affirmation declares that the station of the Imams stands supreme among all celestial ranks; every *Qāʾim* (the Standing One, the resurrector) is bewildered and humbled by the knowledge of Qaʾim al-Muntaẓar (the Awaited Establisher). No Imam possesses the capacity to comprehend the greatness of the Hidden Imam.

#### On *Wa-lā ḥawla wa-lā quwwata illā bi-Allāh al-ʿAlī al-ʿAẓīm*

The word *ḥawl* (inward capacity / the esoteric dimension) and *quwwa* (manifest strength / the exoteric aspect) define the twin spheres within which all creation operates; nothing escapes their governance. In the terminology of the *daʿwa*, *ḥawl* is the inner chapter (*bāb*) that collects the forms of the spiritual guides (*ʿAllāma*, the Learned), while *quwwa* is the manifest chapter that gathers the stations of the missionaries (*duʿāt*, the Summoners). 

The recitation of these words recognizes a fundamental truth: neither the inner gate nor the outer gate possesses sufficient potency to establish the sacred precinct save through the Imam, who is the *khalīfa* (successor) and *ḥijāb* (veil) of *ʿAyn al-ʿAẓīma* (the Essence of Greatness). Without divine permission, no power and no strength avail.[^cite-8] Just as Ḥasan and Ḥusayn cannot exist without ʿAlī al-ʿAẓīm, so too is the order of the cosmos sustained solely by the Hidden Imam's will.

In the realm of Imamate, *ḥawl* denotes *lāhūt* (the divine aspect) and *quwwa* denotes *nasūt* (the human form). The worshipper confesses: there would be no existence of *lāhūt*, nor any existence of *nasūt*, were not the Lord of both aspects—the Imam himself—to sustain them. The rank of *ḥawl* surpasses that of *quwwa*; as day advances, strength gathers near inward capacity in what is called the *tilka āyat al-wāḥida* (this single verse of unified knowledge).

| **Ḥawl** | **Quwwa** | **Allāh al-ʿAlī al-ʿAẓīm** |
|---|---|---|
| *Lāhūt* | *Nasūt* | Imam |
| Inner Gate | Gate of Manifestation | Imam |
| Ḥusayn | Ḥasan | ʿAlī |

When the word is traced to its origin, *Lā ḥawla wa-lā quwwata illā bi-Allāh al-ʿAlī al-ʿAẓīm* means: The Creator of all existence is none but the One beyond the Creator of the Divine Aspect itself—the Essence of Transcendence.

| **Lā Ḥawl** | **Wa-lā Quwwa** | **Illā Allāh al-ʿAlī al-ʿAẓīm** |
|---|---|---|
| Not inwardness | Not strength | Save Allāh, the Transcendent Creator |

Upon reciting these words, a specific spirituality awakens within the devotee. The Prophet (peace and blessings of Allāh be upon him) declared: "The Throne Verse is so glorious that none recites it save (1) the Messenger of Allāh, (2) the Truth-seeker in sincere striving, and (3) the believer whom Allāh has tested with faith."

## The Throne Verse

The verse is called ⟪ar:آیت الکرسی⟫ (the Throne Verse) because it contains the word ⟪ar:کرسی⟫ (*kursī*), from which the verse derives its name. This term appears but once in the Qur'ān, designating a unique and singular reality. Numerous traditions prescribe its recitation according to numerical correspondences: if one recites this verse by the numerical value of *kursī* (290) or by the numerical value of one's own name, great aid descends from Allāh. For instance, if a believer bears the name "ʿAlī," he should recite this verse 110 times—the numerical value of both *lā ilāha illā huwa* (there is no god save He) and ʿAlī.

⟪ar:أمیر المومنین⟫ (Amīr al-Muʾminīn, the Commander of the Faithful) has stated: "No one has truly comprehended this verse save the Messenger of Allāh, the Messenger of Allāh, and the believer whom Allāh has tested with faith." The Prophet (peace and blessings of Allāh be upon him) declared: ⟪ar-quote:فِی کُلِّ حَرفٍ اَلفَ رَحمَۃ وَاَلفَ بَرَکَۃ⟫ ("In every letter of this verse lie a thousand mercies and a thousand blessings").

The scholars of *taʾwīl* (esoteric exegesis) teach that the true substance of Āyat al-Kursī lies in the phrase ⟪ar:'اﷲُ وھو العلی العظیم'⟫ ("Allāh—He is the Most High, the Most Great"). All that follows this declaration forms a ⟪ar:جملۃُ المُعتَرِضَۃ⟫ (parenthetical clause) describing Allāh's attributes. After exhaustive enumeration of these qualities, the verse finally reveals that the One ⟪ar:'اللہ'⟫ possessing them all is precisely the Self that is ⟪ar:علی عظیم⟫—He whose essence is ⟪ar:'عین عظیمۃ'⟫ (the Essence of Greatness itself). In effect, the attributes of this verse constitute the very definition of *ʿAyn al-ʿAẓīma*.

The full verse states:

> ⟪quran 2:255⟫
> ⟪ar:‏ٱللَّهُ لَآ إِلَهَ إِلَّا هُوَ ٱلْحَىُّ ٱلْقَيُّومُ ۚ لَا تَأْخُذُهُۥ سِنَةٌۭ وَلَا نَوْمٌۭ ۚ لَّهُۥ مَا فِى ٱلسَّمَوتِ وَمَا فِى ٱلْأَرْضِ ۗ مَن ذَا ٱلَّذِى يَشْفَعُ عِندَهُۥٓ إِلَّا بِإِذْنِهِۦ ۚ يَعْلَمُ مَا بَيْنَ أَيْدِيهِمْ وَمَا خَلْفَهُمْ ۖ وَلَا يُحِيطُونَ بِشَىْءٍۢ مِّنْ عِلْمِهِۦٓ إِلَّا بِمَا شَآءَ ۚ وَسِعَ كُرْسِيُّهُ ٱلسَّمَوتِ وَٱلْأَرْضَ ۖ وَلَا يَـُٔودُهُۥ حِفْظُهُمَا ۚ وَهُوَ ٱلْعَلِىُّ ٱلْعَظِيمُ ‎⟫
>
> Allāh—there is no god save He—the Living, the Guardian of all that exists. Slumber takes Him not, nor sleep. To Him belongs all that is in the heavens and all that is on earth. Who is he that shall intercede with Him save by His leave? He knows what is before them and what is after them, and they comprehend not anything of His knowledge save that which He wills. His Throne extends over the heavens and the earth, and the preservation of both burdens Him not. And He is the Most High, the Most Great.
>
> — **Qur'ān 2:255**

### The Structure of the Throne Verse

Allāh has established perfect equilibrium within this verse. Nine parts compose it, and the number nine signifies the completion, perfection, and essence of the numerical cycle—just as Ramaḍān, the ninth month, represents the summation of all other months.[^cite-9]

| **(1) Allāhu lā ilāha illā huwa al-ḥayy** | **(2) Lā takhdhuhu sinatun wa-lā nwm** | **(3) Lahu mā fī al-samāwāt wa-mā fī al-arḍ** |
|---|---|---|
| **(4) Man dhā al-ladhī yashfaʿu ʿindahu illā bi-idhnihi** | **(5) Yaʿlamu mā bayna aydīhim wa-mā khalfahum** | **(6) Wa-lā yuḥīṭūn bi-shayʾin min ʿilmihi illā bi-mā shāʾa** |
| **(7) Wasiʿa kursiyyuhu al-samāwāt wa-al-arḍ** | **(8) Wa-lā yuʾūduhu ḥifẓuhumā** | **(9) Wa-huwa al-ʿaliyy al-ʿaẓīm** |

When the reader reaches the midpoint of this verse and contemplates both the portion already recited and the portion yet to come, a profound symmetry becomes apparent: the two halves mirror one another around the central axis. That axis is precisely this phrase: ⟪ar:يَعْلَمُ مَا بَيْنَ أَيْدِيهِمْ وَمَا خَلْفَهُمْ⟫ ("He knows what is before them and what is after them"). This central statement itself explicates both the forward and backward dimensions of the verse, revealing that what has passed behind continues to be encompassed within divine knowledge. This is the form of the *dāʾira* (circle), implying that what appears at the beginning shall recur at the end. Observing the reflection of first and last parts upon the central clause, these divisions align as follows:

(1) There is no god but Allāh. He is the Living, the Originator of all. **↔** (9) And He is the Most High, the Most Great.

(2) He takes neither slumber nor sleep. **↔** (8) And the preservation of both burdens Him not.

(3) To Him belongs all that is in the heavens and the earth. **↔** (7) His Throne extends over the heavens and the earth.

(4) Who can intercede without His permission? **↔** (6) No one can comprehend any of His knowledge save as He wills.

**(5) He knows what is before them and what is after them. — The Eternal Axis.**

#### *Allāhu Lā Ilāha Illā Huwa al-Ḥayy al-Qayyūm*

"There is no god but Allāh. He is the Living, the Guardian of all that exists." The numerical value of *Lā ilāha illā huwa* equals 110—the same as ʿAlī:

| **Lā ilāha illā hu** | **ʿAlī** |
|---|---|
| L(30) + I(1) + A(1) + H(5) + A(1) + I(1) + L(30) + A(1) + H(5) + U(6) | A(70) + L(30) + I(10) |
| **110** | **110** |

The essence of *ʿAyn al-ʿAẓīma* (the Essence of Greatness) is eternal and everlasting. There was no moment in the past when this Essence did not subsist; nor shall there ever be a moment in the future when it ceases to be.

#### *Lā Takhdhahu Sinatun wa-lā Nawm*

"Slumber takes Him not, nor sleep." The heedlessness of our spiritual universe manifests in this world as sleep. Sleep holds profound attraction for humanity; awakening from it to perform night prayer, though difficult, carries incomparable reward. This very difficulty signals that the believer must break free from spiritual negligence and rise to the summons of Imam al-Zamān, abandoning complacency to undertake true spiritual effort (*ijtihād*).

Negligence precedes sleep in the spiritual hierarchy; sleep follows in the material realm. Though slumber and negligence befall every human being, Allāh—exalted be He—is infinitely removed from both. The essence of *ʿAyn al-ʿAẓīma* knows neither negligence in any past epoch nor shall it ever experience heedlessness in any future time.

#### *Lahu Mā Fī al-Samāwāt wa-Mā Fī al-Arḍ*

"To Him belongs all that is in the heavens and all that is on earth." The heavens signify the attributes of the Imams; the earth represents the *ḥujaj* (the proofs / the missionaries) who summon to the Imam. In other words, the totality of all Imams and their proofs—both the hidden (*mustwaḍaʿīn*, the trustees) and the manifest (*mustawdaʿīn*, those who are entrusted)—along with all their congregations shall be gathered within the Great Precinct of *ʿAyn al-ʿAẓīma*. He alone is the true possessor of all these forms of transmission.

#### *Man Dhā al-Ladhī Yashfaʿu ʿIndahu Illā bi-Idhnihi*

"Who can intercede save by His permission?" Shafāʿa (intercession) signifies the seeking of remedy from the seventy afflictions of the spiritual world. In every age, the *wilāya* (guardianship) of *ʿAyn* has been the locus of aid and intercession. It is impossible to escape the prison of this chaotic realm without the protection and love emanating from this source.

#### *Yaʿlamu Mā Bayna Aydīhim wa-Mā Khalfahum*

"He knows what is before them and what is after them, and they comprehend not anything of His knowledge save that which He wills." The Imam is aware of the affairs belonging to each *Imam* and of all the believers surrounding him—their present conditions and their future developments. The Imams have reached their stations through the completion of these very affairs, and they possess full knowledge of what shall transpire in succeeding ages. All Imams derive their gnosis from the grace of *al-ʿAẓīm* (the Mighty One).

#### *Wasiʿa Kursiyyuhu al-Samāwāt wa-al-Arḍ*

"His Throne extends over the heavens and the earth, and the preservation of both burdens Him not." In this verse, the possessive pronoun coupled with *kursī* yields *kursiyyuhu* ("His Throne")—a seat spread across both sky and earth. The *kursī* refers to the essence of the Mighty One who dominates all the Imams and their proofs. When the *Qāʾim ʿAlī* sits upon this throne—a station called *Dhikr al-Islām* (the Remembrance of Submission)—he bears the entire burden of the age's governance, placed upon him by the Hidden Imam. Just as a chair never tires of bearing human weight, so the Mighty Ones never fatigue under the immense responsibility of their age; their effort sustains all without diminishment.

| **Heavens** | **Earth** | **Throne** | **Seated upon the Throne** |
|---|---|---|---|
| Imams | *Ḥujaj* (Proofs) | *ʿAyn al-ʿAẓīma* | *Qāʾim ʿAlī* |

#### *Wa-Huwa al-ʿAliyy al-ʿAẓīm*

"And He is the Most High, the Most Great." Know well: the One (*ʿAlī al-ʿAẓīm*) who possesses all these transcendent and sublime qualities is indeed the Mighty One.

### Āyat al-Kursī Equals Two Chapters

It has been transmitted that Āyat al-Kursī possesses the merit of two entire *sūra* (chapters). The highest *sūra* of the Qur'ān is *Sūrat al-Fātiḥa* (the Opening). The remaining chapters follow in descending order of rank. After *Sūrat al-Fātiḥa*, there comes *Sūrat al-Ikhlāṣ* (the Declaration of Sincerity), and then, in sequence, *Āyat al-Kursī*, which ranks equal to two *sūra*. These two chapters correspond to the two great Imams, Mawlānā ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib and Mawlānā Abū Ṭālib (peace be upon them).

| **Sūrat al-Fātiḥa** | *Qāʾim ʿAlī* |
|---|---|
| **Sūrat al-Ikhlāṣ** | *ʿAyn al-ʿAẓīma* |
| **Āyat al-Kursī** | *Amīr al-Muʾminīn (ʿAlī)* |
| **Sūrat al-Kāfirūn** | *ʿAbdullāh* |
| **Sūrat Yāsīn** | *Fāṭima* |
| **Sūrat al-ʿAlāʾ** | *al-Fāṭir (the Originator)* |

## Al-Islām: The Surrender of Forms

From ⟪ar:سورۃ البقرۃ⟫ (the Chapter of the Cow), ⟪ar:حضرت ابراہیم علیہ السلام⟫ (the Prophet Abraham, upon him be peace) commanded his sons:

> ⟪quran 2:132⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَوَصَّىٰ بِهَآ إِبْرَهِیمُ بَنِيهِ وَيَعْقُوبُ يَبَنِىَّ إِنَّ ٱللَّهَ ٱصْطَفَىٰ لَكُمُ ٱلدِّينَ فَلَا تَمُوتُنَّ إِلَّا وَأَنتُم مُّسْلِمُونَ ‎⟫
>
> And Abraham enjoined upon his sons, and Jacob likewise: "O my sons! Allāh has chosen for you the true religion. Do not die save as Muslims—those who submit."
>
> — **Qur'ān 2:132**

It may seem extraordinary that ⟪ar:ابراھیم علیہ السلام⟫ Abraham addresses his children—who possessed ⟪ar:نبوت (اسحاق)⟫ (prophethood in Isḥāq) and ⟪ar:امامت (اسماعیل)⟫ (Imamate in Ismāʿīl)—commanding them not to depart save in a state of *islām*. Yet this apparent redundancy conceals a profound distinction. There exist two forms of *islām*. The first is that which remains subordinate to *īmān* (faith)—the *islām* through which we enter by utterance of ⟪ar:لا الہ الا اللہ⟫ (there is no god save Allāh). The second *islām* exceeds *īmān* in rank and actualization; it is ⟪ar:تسلیم الصور⟫ (*taslīm al-ṣuwar*, the surrender of forms / the submission of configurations). This is the *islām* of entrusting one's gathered congregation to the true Imams and Prophets.

In ⟪ar:ابراھیم علیہ السلام⟫ this verse, Abraham tells his sons—⟪ar:اسحاق و اسماعیل⟫ Isḥāq and Ismāʿīl—"Do not pass from this world until you hand over the assembly of those gathered near you to the Prophets and Imams." Similarly, the Messenger of Allāh (may Allāh bless him and grant him peace) invited all people to enter the *islām* they themselves had already established—to surrender themselves to the *áslamī ṣūwar* (the Islamic forms), the sacred *haykal* (temple / the form of the faith itself).

We dwell in the vast sphere of *islām*. Within this larger circle exists a smaller one: the circle of *īmān* (faith). At the center of this concentric arrangement lies that which Abraham inspired in his children—the true point of *al-islām* (the perfect submission): to entrust one's affairs entirely to the rightful Master.

```

(Islam); the inner circle labeled ⟪ar:ایمان⟫ (Iman/Faith).
A small red dot at the very center labeled ⟪ar:الأسلام⟫ (al-Aslam—
```

This highest *islām* surpasses *īmān* in rank. The Prophets possess an *islām* wherein they entrust the *suwar* (forms) to their Imam. The only pathway into such submission is through prayer (*ṣalāt*). In other words, *islām* unfolds its development through *īmān*: within *islām*, there are no degrees; within *īmān*, degrees abound. This parallels the state of ⟪ar:کمال اول⟫ (*kamāl awwal*, primary perfection), which knows no ranks or orders. But ⟪ar:کمال ثانی⟫ (*kamāl thānī*, secondary perfection)—the actualized state—immediately establishes ranks and hierarchies.

*Īmān* stands as the intermediary between the two *islām*. The first *islām*, contingent only upon utterance of the *shahāda* (testimony), encompasses all the hypocrites and superficial adherents. The second *islām* demands sincerity and surrender of the innermost self (*ʿubūda*, servitude / worship)—conditions for which ⟪ar:حضرت ابراھیم علیہ السلام⟫ Abraham commanded his children: ⟪ar:نبوت، رسالت، امامت، وصایت⟫ entrust the accounts of *nubuwwa* (prophethood), *risāla* (messengership), *imāma* (imamate), and *waṣāya* (executorship / bequest) to those above you in station.

### Why Do We Purify Ourselves?

We cleanse the self because we must present it to our highest self so that it may accept it. This principle pervades all existence. The *islām* of the mineral is its surrender to the plant's roots. The *islām* of vegetation is its surrender to the animal. It is the duty of ⟪ar:الأسلام⟫ the beast and all lawful creatures to surrender their bodies to humanity, to bow their necks before the knife, so that man may refine them as components of his own being. And it is man's ⟪ar:الاسلام⟫ duty to surrender himself ⟪ar:صورت شریف⟫ (his noble form) to ⟪ar:(تسلیم الصور)⟫ his exalted limit (*ḥadd ʿālī*), for our exalted limit is the master of our perfection. Thus the Qur'ān says: "Wish for death, for it is the key to liberation and the fulfillment of this ⟪ar:الأسلام⟫ aspect of ours." Through death, we present and refine our form. Death itself is the true ⟪ar:الاسلام (تسلیم الصور)⟫ (*islām*) of the believer. The Qur'ān testifies:

> ⟪quran 2:94⟫
> ⟪ar:‏قُلْ إِن كَانَتْ لَكُمُ ٱلدَّارُ ٱلْآخِرَةُ عِندَ ٱللَّهِ خَالِصَةًۭ مِّن دُونِ ٱلنَّاسِ فَتَمَنَّوُا۟ ٱلْمَوْتَ إِن كُنتُمْ صَدِقِينَ ‎⟫
>
> Say: "If the abode of the Hereafter with Allāh is for you alone, to the exclusion of all humankind, then long for death if you are truthful."
>
> — **Qur'ān 2:94**

The Hereafter is indeed the supreme station where our form reaches completion and finds eternal rest. Herein Allāh declares: "Yearn to surrender yourselves to the Throne, that you may attain the Abode of the Hereafter prepared for you." That is, your exalted limit (*ḥadd ʿālī*) belongs to you alone—not to any other believer.

## The Diverse Meanings of *Salām*

### The Greeting of Believers to One Another

The word *islām* derives from *salām*, which signifies peace. The Prophet (peace and blessings of Allāh be upon him) taught: ⟪ar-quote:السلام قبل الکلام⟫ ("Greet before you speak"). When one believer extends *salām* to another, he acknowledges a shared platform of submission—a mutual commitment that neither shall cause harm to the other when present, nor backbite when absent. Peace becomes the foundational language of the *daʿwa*.

### The Greeting of the Rider to the Pedestrian

The Prophet (peace and blessings of Allāh be upon him) declared: "It is obligatory for the rider to greet the pedestrian." Here, the rider represents the *dāʿī* (*the Summoner / missionary of the *daʿwa*), while the pedestrian signifies the believer. In *daʿwa* terminology, the *dāʿī* is metaphorically called a ⟪ar:حمار⟫ (a donkey), for the donkey bears its master's full burden without complaint. Similarly, the *dāʾī* undertakes the burden of training all believers on behalf of the Imam (peace be upon him) without grievance. He gathers all into his congregation, irrespective of their deeds.

### The Greeting of the Prophets

The transmission of the accounts of ⟪ar:مستودعین⟫ (the trustees / the hidden spiritual realities) and ⟪ar:صورات⟫ (the forms / the spiritual configurations) to successors is likewise called *salām*. When the Prophets confer high ranks—⟪ar:نبوت ورسالت⟫ (*nubuwwa* and *risāla*, prophethood and messengership) and ⟪ar:(امامت و وصایت)⟫ (*imāma* and *waṣāya*, imamate and executorship)—upon their successors, this too is a greeting, a peace-giving transfer of sacred trust.

### The Transfer of the Hierarchy

In truth, *salām* signifies the distribution and transmission of *suwar* (the forms / the spiritual configurations). Concerning this, Allāh declares in ⟪ar:سورۃ القدر⟫ (the Chapter of Power):

> ⟪quran 97:5⟫
> ⟪ar:‏سَلَمٌ هِىَ حَتَّىٰ مَطْلَعِ ٱلْفَجْرِ ‎⟫
>
> It is peace—all peace—until the rising of the dawn.
>
> — **Qur'ān 97:5**

⟪ar:لیلۃ القدر⟫ (*Laylat al-Qadr*, the Night of Power) is the night of distributions. Wherever spiritual centers are established and wherever authority (*quwwa*) exists, a succession of distributions (*salām*) issues forth on this blessed night, transmitting the divine forms through the hierarchy of the faithful.
