## The Spiritual Ranks (Marātib Rūḥāniyya)

The Ismaili tradition transmits its understanding of the cosmos not only through discursive prose but through ordered tables of correspondence, in which each visible rank of the daʿwa (the summons / the invitation) is paired with its hidden counterpart in the spiritual world. Two diagrams stand at the head of this chapter; together they map the ten ranks of the hierarchy onto their cosmological aspects, and they should be read as a single composition divided across two sheets.

The first diagram enumerates the ten ranks themselves, ascending from the seeker who has only just answered the call to the final dignity of the wali. The second diagram replaces each named rank with the abstract noun (the *-iyya* form) that describes its function within the cosmos — Imāmat Rūḥāniyya, Nuṭq Ilāhī, and so on — and assigns to each block a named figure from sacred history.[^cite-1]

## The Originative Body (al-Jutha al-Ibdāʿiyya) and the Excellent Person (al-Shakhṣ al-Fāḍil)

When the heavens and the earth were brought into being, the first human creation was brought forth with them, and along with that creation its leader and Imam. At the site of the Bayt Allāh twenty-eight wells were prepared, and from these wells twenty-eight extraordinary persons were drawn forth. These were the choicest of the human race, the very distillation of the heavenly world and of the world of generation, and — corresponding to the ⟪ar:حروف معجم⟫ — they answered to the twenty-eight stations of the moon. The wells were ranged in the same disposition in which an Imam stands with his followers behind him. The foremost of these persons was the most subtle, the most pure, the most noble of them all: the one called ⟪ar:’شخص فاضل‘⟫. Among human beings he was as the surplus gem among gems, as the ruby among minerals, as the date-palm among plants, as the most exalted creature among animals. He was the ⟪ar:’حجاب آدم ابدائیہ‘⟫, and far nobler than the twenty-seven who were born together with him. The learned have rendered the disparity in this image: were the twenty-seven of the substance of ruby, the rest of mankind would be of common stone; and were the Excellent Person himself of the substance of ruby, then those twenty-seven who came forth with him would be of stone.[^cite-2]

**Salient points:**

### The Tawḥīd of the Excellent Person

This learned one first regarded himself, without any external instruction, and then the others of his kind, then the heavens that turned above him, then the waters that flowed, and the alternation of day and night. From this inward observation he came to know that he himself, and his fellow creatures, and all the worlds, had a Mubdiʿ Taʿālā (the Originator, Most High) who was in no way like to any of them. He therefore denied to himself, to the children of his species, and to all that exists, the ⟪ar:صفت الٰہیت⟫, and affirmed the ⟪ar:الہیت⟫ for his Originator alone, bearing witness to ⟪ar:’وحدانیت‘⟫ and offering ⟪ar:تسبیح و تقدیس⟫. He was thereby joined to the ⟪ar:مواد الٰہیۃ⟫, the ⟪ar:انوار شعشعانیۃ⟫, and the ⟪ar:تائیدات ملکوتیۃ⟫. United thus with the support of the Tenth, he attained the ⟪ar:علم ماکان سیکون⟫ and arrived at the kamāl thānī (secondary perfection). He reached the ⟪ar:(رتبہ وحدت)⟫ of al-ʿAql al-Awwal (the First Intellect) within the world of nature, and so was named ⟪ar:’ ابدأ ‘⟫ — that is, he attained the rank of origination within the physical order and the rank of the Tenth within the order of ibdāʿ. He then summoned the twenty-seven to ⟪ar:توحید، تنزیہ اور تجرید⟫. They accepted his summons without hesitation, took the covenant (mīthāq) from his hand, and were instructed in the distinction between the lawful and the forbidden, the prescribed rites of worship, and the duty of conveying these rulings and the marital sunna to the learned among them.[^cite-3]

He then chose sixteen of the twenty-eight to remain within his prophetic establishment: one as his Bāb, one as Ḥujja, one as a preacher (muballigh), and one as Dāʿī al-Balāgh. He left twelve in his presence as the ⟪ar:’حجج لیلی‘⟫, the night-arguments who never quit his side. The remaining eleven he dispersed as the ⟪ar:’حجج نہاری‘⟫, the day-arguments, commanding each to depart to his assigned island and there spread the summons.

The ⟪ar:جثہ ابدائیہ⟫ is, after the man of intellect, the most excellent rank; and after this rank the ⟪ar:حجج لیلی⟫ are the most excellent. By virtue of this excellence he relieved them of distant labour. The day-arguments stand by him in both the outer and inner registers, while the ⟪ar:حجج لیلی⟫ stand by him in the inner only. These twenty-eight are the ⟪ar:’صاحبان اولو العلم‘⟫, of whom Allāh declares:

> ⟪quran 3:18⟫
> ⟪ar:‏شَهِدَ ٱللَّهُ أَنَّهُۥ لَآ إِلَهَ إِلَّا هُوَ وَٱلْمَلَٓئِكَةُ وَأُو۟لُوا۟ ٱلْعِلْمِ قَآئِمًۢا بِٱلْقِسْطِ ۚ لَآ إِلَهَ إِلَّا هُوَ ٱلْعَزِيزُ ٱلْحَكِيمُ ‎⟫
> *Allah bears witness that La ilaha illa Huwa (none has the right to be worshipped but He), and the angels, and those having knowledge (also give this witness); (He is always) maintaining His creation in Justice. La ilah illa Huwa (none has the right to be worshipped but He), the All-Mighty, the All-Wise.*
> Allah, and the angels, and those endowed with knowledge, bear witness: there is no god but He. He is the Guardian of Justice. There is no god but He, the All-mighty, the All-wise.
> — **Quran 3:18 (inline widget)**

These ⟪ar:اولوالعلم⟫ are the twenty-eight born without ordinary procreation, the ⟪ar:’جثہ ابدائیہ‘⟫, who have been constituted possessors of knowledge and, by virtue of that knowledge, remain forever upholders of justice.[^cite-4]

In the period of unveiling (kashf) the islands and the inner presences (ḥaḍarāt) are not separate places, whereas in the period of veiling (satr) they are sundered. With respect to purity, subtlety, and knowledge in the era of unveiling, the islands are as the inner ranks. One of the twelve islands — the one in which the Imam himself is present — is termed ⟪ar:’حضرات‘⟫; the remaining eleven, in which the Imam is not present, are termed ⟪ar:’جزائر‘⟫.

The distinction here amounts to this: the ⟪ar:شخص فاضل⟫ keeps the ⟪ar:حجج لیلی⟫ in his presence and sends the ⟪ar:حجج نہاری⟫ to summon the eleven regions of the world that lie distant from him.

## The Total Vicegerency of the Excellent Person

When the man of the Jutha al-Ibdāʿiyya inaugurates his summons, his first concern is to prepare his vicegerent (khalīfa), who will be his true son. Just as the Excellent Person stands in the place of al-ʿAql al-Awwal within the world of nature, so his son stands in the place of the First Emanator. The Originative Body has established that whoever sits at the head of his circle stands forever in the place of ibdāʿ, while his successor stands in the place of the inbiʿāth — the emanated. So too the Imam is in the place of ibdāʿ and the Bāb is in the place of inbiʿāth. The true son of ibdāʿ is therefore called al-Munbaʿith (the Emanator).

| Ibdāʿ | Inbiʿāth |
| --- | --- |
| First Intellect | First Emanator |
| Jutha Ibdāʿiyya | Son (Vicegerent in act) |
| Imam | Bāb |

The Excellent Person designates the Imamate to his son and then, with the twenty-seven, departs to the House of the Almighty. He attains the rank of intellect and becomes lord of this world; that is, he passes to the rank of the Tenth (al-ʿĀshir). He will hold this rank until he produces another vicegerent in his own image. The twenty-eight include his Bāb who arose alongside him.[^cite-5]

### (a) The Vicegerent of (a) Is:

Although the learned one appoints his descendant son as his vicegerent in his place, that son is not the equal of him. He stands as a partial successor at his post. The vicegerent of a part is partial, and the vicegerent of the whole is whole. He shall manage the affair until he produces a body equal to his own, and that can occur only at the close of the final cycle. Although many cores (kawr) and cycles will intervene, and a Body will arise upon each core, every one of them will be his partial vicegerent. The true vicegerent of (a) can only be (or).

| A. | OR |
| --- | --- |
| The Excellent Person (First Cycle) | Qāʾim Kullī (Qāʾim of the Final Cycle) |

**Salient points:**

The first of these, who stands in the place of (a), is the highest in rank because precedence belongs to him. Yet the vicegerency can only attain its perfection in (or). The vicegerent is called the Son of Completion (Ibn al-Tamām).

When the Tenth (the first mortal Excellent Person) prepares his vicegerent, he and those who belong to his order pass to the rank of the Ninth Intellect; the Ninth Intellect and those of its order pass to the Eighth; the Eighth and its order to the Seventh; the Seventh and its order to the Sixth; the Sixth and its order to the Fifth; the Fifth and its order to the Fourth; the Fourth and its order to the Third; and the Third and its order pass to the realm of the Second Intellect. There the welcome is given, and Paradise is the dwelling of the departed. *Maʾwā* signifies the abode. There is no rank above this save the rank of the First Intellect, which is the realm of unity and the place of the Sidrat al-Muntahā. All the ranks (ten in number) thus advance with the passage of cycles and ages.

It is of this Paradise that the Prophet, peace and blessings be upon him, said: "Paradise is a place no eye has seen, no ear has heard, and no human heart has ever conceived." Its grandeur, elevation, joy and sublimity defeat any definition; it can only be approached by intimation.[^cite-6]

The unfolding of the Tenth (the man of the First Cycle) reaches completion only when he produces his vicegerent, the Qāʾim Kullī (the final Qāʾim of the last cycle). All the dignities lying between them are partial in relation to the two; and the two are wholes in relation to those between. The First Cycle (the Excellent Person) has ten dignities (rutab) to be established before the world is brought to its end. All these dignities will appear in the world one after another and will rise up to the Tenth, taking their seats with him. When all of them have ascended, his Vicegerent Kullī — the standing Qāʾim of the last cycle — will appear and lift the entire world up into the spiritual realm. For this reason Allāh declares in the Qurʾān:

> ⟪ar:إِنَّ ٱلَّذِينَ كَذَّبُوا۟ بِـَٔايَـٰتِنَا وَٱسْتَكْبَرُوا۟ عَنْهَا لَا تُفَتَّحُ لَهُمْ أَبْوَٰبُ ٱلسَّمَآءِ وَلَا يَدْخُلُونَ ٱلْجَنَّةَ حَتَّىٰ يَلِجَ ٱلْجَمَلُ فِى سَمِّ ٱلْخِيَاطِ ۚ وَكَذَٰلِكَ نَجْزِى ٱلْمُجْرِمِينَ⟫

## The Sequence of the Bodies

Imam Jaʿfar al-Ṣādiq told Umm al-Nidāʾ that one great cycle had thus far elapsed. From that point three hundred and ninety-nine cores have passed. Of the first round of the four-hundredth core, seven sevens have already been completed. We are now finishing the seventh seventy. Once the Resurrection appears, it will gather to itself the whole of the period — four hundred thousand years.

Thus three hundred and ninety-nine cores have passed and we stand in the four-hundredth. The body of the four hundred and first core, which is to appear after, will be assembled as the final of the four hundred, and its array will be a comprehensive one. Its grandeur will be such that it shall appear at the Kaʿba itself. Until this hour, only the very first body had appeared in the House of Allāh.[^cite-7]

Within these four hundred cores there are forty distant (faḍilī) bodies that appear at every tenth core. Their magnitude exceeds that of the others; none, however, attains to the first body of the present (second) cycle. And greater still than this — the first body of the second cycle — was the body that appeared at the inauguration of the first cycle. Its equal will be only the vicegerent who shall appear at the end of the final cycle.

The distant bodies are all sacred precincts. At the close of the second cycle, the sovereign who arises will gather his cycle to himself, and his seat shall be in the horizon of the first cycle. Upon arriving there he will receive a second seat. When the third cycle begins, the Tenth — the body of the first cycle — will give to the third cycle the last two bodies, and that third body will be the vicegerent of the second part. This pattern continues until the final cycle is revealed, whose head will be the vicegerent of the first cycle.

**Salient points:**

The second cycle is engaged in preparing a third cycle in its own likeness.

*(Lessons of Anwār al-Ṭāʾifa, Sayyidnā Muḥammad ibn Ṭāhir)*

## The Horizon of the Tenth (Ufq al-ʿĀshir)

When the son of this man — the son of the Fāḍil — is established in his place by explicit designation, he issues his summons to this world in accordance with his father's original plan, calling people to the tawḥīd of Allāh in order to rescue those souls trapped in the river of becoming and held in captivity. His second strategy is to draw forth his successor from his own summons, just as his father had prepared him. When he has prepared his son and brought his service to completion, he carries his assembly up to the horizon of the Tenth. Since the Tenth himself is his father, he passes away and returns to his father's intermediate state (barzakh), where a seat is appointed for him. He does not attain the seat of the Khalīfa Kullī; that station is reserved for the Founder of the final cycle. In like manner, whoever passes away in your place after you has a seat established in the horizon of the Tenth. The horizon of the Tenth is the place of barzakh in which all these departed souls rest. For this reason Allāh declares:[^cite-8]

> ⟪ar:لَعَلِّى أَعْمَلُ صَـٰلِحًۭا فِيمَا تَرَكْتُ ۚ كَلَّآ ۚ إِنَّهَا كَلِمَةٌ هُوَ قَآئِلُهَا ۖ وَمِن وَرَآئِهِم بَرْزَخٌ إِلَىٰ يَوْمِ يُبْعَثُونَ⟫
> That I may do good in that which I have left behind. By no means! It is but a word that he speaks; and before them is a barrier till the Day of Resurrection.

### The Seats of the Horizon of the Tenth

The horizon of the Tenth is the place of barzakh for all the Imams. A Qāʾim transmits the names of the Imams to the Qāʾim who comes after him. Each Ṣāḥib al-Usbūʿ (Master of the Week) receives the forms of the six Imams who preceded him from the barzakh of the Tenth. So too, the Qāʾim of every thousand years (Ṣāḥib Alf) receives his thousand years from the barzakh of the Tenth. Each week (usbūʿ) has six seats; when these six seats of one week are summed, and the seventh arrives, the seventh of the previous week becomes the seventh of the following.

One person becomes the stationer (muqīm) of one week; the next person becomes the stationer of the next. That is, the principal aim is to prepare the next gentleman. Although there are six Imams between the two, those six are the partial vicegerents of this Ṣāḥib al-Usbūʿ. Only the immediate successor is the vicegerent of the first. The same principle applies at every station.

> The vicegerent of the partial is partial, and the vicegerent of the whole is whole.

## One Hundred and Twenty-Four Thousand Forms

To reach the Barzakh ʿĀshir (the Tenth Isthmus, the eschatological threshold of return), the count of one hundred and twenty-four thousand forms (ṣuwar) must be completed across seven thousand years. When the first seven thousand years of the dawr al-kashf (the cycle of unveiling) reach their close, 124,000 forms will have gathered upon the horizon of the ʿĀshir, near the Barzakh — the seat of the gathered congregation.

[diagram: A small purple-on-white numerology display showing the digit sequence '1 2 4 0 0 0' on the top line and the equation '(1 + 2 + 4 + 0 + 0 + 0 = 7)' on the bottom line.]
  (see ../images/007.png)
  *Note:* Numerological / place-value teaching aid. The digits 1, 2, 4 (powers-of-two-style) plus padding zeros sum to 7 — likely referencing a seven-rank structure in the chapter on ranks.

The Qāʾim (the Riser) of the seven-thousand-year period is enthroned upon six prior seats. Every millennium witnesses the manifestation of a Qāʾim who gathers his thousand years and then passes into the Barzakh ʿĀshir. When the seventh millennial Qāʾim manifests, he is in truth the sum of his predecessor's daʿwa (the summons) together with that predecessor's successor. The father of this foundation is the point that appeared before the seven thousand years, the point that gathered into itself the seven thousand years that preceded it.

Only when these seven thousand years are unveiled, and the 124,000 forms accumulated there are conveyed to the temple of the Qāʾim, does the consummation begin. From the architecture of these 124,000 forms a luminous body (haykal) is composed, in which all the forms are distributed across distinct parts — one hundred and twenty-four noble forms, each comprising one thousand. This body assumes the rank of a haykal-temple, and the soul itself becomes the soul of this body. That is, the soul of the soul and the form of the ruling principle act upon this temple. Through this gathering, those forms become capable of ascent to the highest station — a degree of the ʿĀshir realm. Together, these two (the two hundred and twenty-eight chapters) become one temple of light. When they pass at the close of seven thousand years, they stand in the state of the sacred, abstract, divine intellect together with the whole of their congregation. No distinction will remain between them and those who preceded them save in precedence. The Qāʾim takes his entire congregation with him and assumes his seat.

Before he passes, he establishes his seven-thousand-year son as Imām in his place. These sons established as Imāms hold the status of permanent Imāms. The true son of the seven-thousand-year Qāʾim — his successor — appears at the close of the next seven thousand years, and gathers the prior seven thousand into his own sum.

To this the Prophet (peace and blessings be upon him) referred when he said that the Prophets of Allāh number one hundred and twenty-four thousand.

The architecture of the dawr al-kashf — in which prophetic millennia accumulate toward the gathering of the Qāʾim — is developed at length in Fatimid cosmological literature, where the supernal intellects (al-ʿuqūl) and their attendant horizons frame the unfolding of sacred time.[^cite-9]

Allāh says in the Qurʾān:

> ⟪ar:قُلْ إِنَّ ٱلْأَوَّلِينَ وَٱلْـَٔاخِرِينَ لَمَجْمُوعُونَ إِلَىٰ مِيقَـٰتِ يَوْمٍ مَّعْلُومٍ⟫

> ⟪ar:تَعْرُجُ ٱلْمَلَـٰٓئِكَةُ وَٱلرُّوحُ إِلَيْهِ فِى يَوْمٍ كَانَ مِقْدَارُهُۥ خَمْسِينَ أَلْفَ سَنَةٍ⟫

When forty-nine thousand years — seven multiplied by seven thousand — have elapsed, these six millennia are joined together with their temples. Within this seventh established temple the order of the Imāms, of the ḥudūd (the prescribed spiritual ranks), and of the faithful is fixed. The Qāʾim's design will encompass his time of fifty thousand years; thereafter he passes, ascending to the ninth rank in the realm of permanence, while his son assumes his place in the true tenth station.

Assemblies are sundered so that they may meet, and they meet so that they may be sundered again. Compounded things are scattered that they may be added, and added that they may be differentiated. For this reason, the title of the Imām (peace be upon him) is *Ṣāḥib al-jamʿ wa-l-shatt* — the Lord of Gathering and Scattering.

Hence ʿAlī (peace be upon him) said: "Be still, that you may be with the angels." Those who go before await the next to come and to be joined with them.

## The Ancestor, the Opening, and the Imagination

Allāh communicates with the human being and conveys His commands in three modes:

| Eli Waḥy | From Behind the Veil | The Messenger |
| --- | --- | --- |
| By Revelation | From Behind the Veil | By the Sending of a Messenger |
| Jadd | Fatḥ | Khayāl |

### Jadd (revelation)

The first mode is revelation, and it is the station of the jadd (the ancestor). This station endures sovereignly at all times and in every age, and it occupies the highest of the three ranks. Revelation is the word of Allāh which descends from the rūḥ al-quds (the Holy Spirit) and is conveyed, through the jadd alone and without intermediary, to the one charged with the command. It is the duty of the Most High to deliver the Imām's word to the lower circles of the daʿwa.

### Fatḥ (from behind the veil)

The second mode is speech from behind the veil, and it is the station of the fatḥ (the opening). Here the commandments are revealed concealed within the veil of words. So it is with the Qurʾān, in which the commandments are veiled in words — for example, the meaning of "knowledge" set within the veil of the word "water" is the work of the fatḥ. The function of the fatḥ, in other words, is to convey the inner sense of the divine message by dressing it in the garb of outward speech, so that the recipient grasps what the message truly means.

### Khayāl (Messenger)

The third mode is the station of the angel (al-malak al-mursal), who is sent as a messenger in a form visible to the recipient of the message, or whose voice he may hear.

The people of the upper three levels of each station are called the jadd, the fatḥ, and the khayāl of that station. They are the intermediaries between the recipient and his Lord. These three beings dwell in the world of the muḥtajabīn (the veiled ones). Through these three ranks the Imām of Allāh is linked to the Imām of his time. At every level, this triadic hierarchy is at work.[^cite-10]

| Messenger Channel | Mode of Communication | Archangel | Rank |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Khayāl | Through a Messenger | Jibrāʾīl | Station's rank + 1 |
| Fatḥ | From behind the veil | Mīkāʾīl | Station's rank + 2 |
| Jadd | By revelation | Isrāfīl | Station's rank + 3 |

When the Prophet (peace be upon him) held the rank of Nabī, his jadd, fatḥ, and khayāl were distinct personalities who sent down to him the matters of prophethood. When he assumed the rank of Rasūl, his jadd, fatḥ, and khayāl became distinct personalities who began to reveal to him the matters of messengership. When he ascended to the rank of nāṭiq, again his jadd, fatḥ, and khayāl changed accordingly. When he became Imām, his jadd, fatḥ, and khayāl were distinct personalities who began to disclose to him the matters of spiritual imāma. These three ranks operate up to the level of prophethood, and not for the saints of God.

| Peace be upon him | Khayāl | Fatḥ | Jadd |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Muḥammad al-Muṣṭafā (Risāla) | ʿAbdullāh | Abū Ṭālib | ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib |
| Sayyidnā ʿAlī ibn Sulaymān | Muḥammad ibn Fahd | Sayyidnā Jaʿfar | Sayyidnā Sulaymān |

### Two Incidents of Sayyidnā ʿAlī

Sayyidnā ʿAlī relates that when he was summoned before the court of Aurangzeb, who was the governor at that time, the governor — during the audience — signalled to his gatekeeper to lead him out and have him killed. As he was being led out, he beheld a horseman in the likeness of Sayyidnā Muḥammad ibn Fahd come before him and say, "Do not be afraid, and do not grieve. I am with you." At that moment a sudden tumult arose in the market, by which the gatekeeper was parted from him, and he escaped.

When Aurangzeb killed the grandson of Dāwūd ibn Quṭb and left his body to decompose in the marketplace, the people urged Sayyidnā ʿAlī to depart Ahmedabad and seek refuge elsewhere. He told them that he would not leave until his Lord commanded it. On the first day of Rajab, while he was at prayer, he heard an unseen voice ordering him to depart from Ahmedabad. His khayāl had brought him the command.

*(Lessons of Anwār al-Ṭāʾifa, Sayyidnā Muḥammad ibn Ṭāhir.)*

## The Jadd, Fatḥ, and Khayāl of Muḥammad and ʿAlī

When the Prophet (peace be upon him) ascended through the ranks to the rank of ḥijāb (the veil), and when his resident master Maulānā Abū Ṭālib passed away, he became the veil of the intellect of ʿĀshir (al-ʿAql al-ʿĀshir, the Tenth Intellect), and through his jadd, fatḥ, and khayāl became the place upon which the rays of the ʿĀshir descended. These three are spiritual limits (ḥudūd), the spiritual jadd, fatḥ, and khayāl; over against them stand the physical limits — ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib, Abū Ṭālib, ʿAbdullāh.

In the world of the muḥtajabīn (the veiled ones), the Mīm became the veil of the ʿĀshir (the ʿAyn), and the support of ʿĀshir reached the Mīm through his jadd, fatḥ, and khayāl. In the world of the visible ḥijāb, Muḥammad — who is the veil of the Mīm — received his support from his three earthly intermediaries: ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib, Abū Ṭālib, and ʿAbdullāh. In the language of the sharīʿa, these three intermediaries correspond to Jibrāʾīl, Mīkāʾīl, and Isrāfīl.[^cite-11]

### Facts

The gathering of jadd, fatḥ, and khayāl did not take place with ʿAyn al-ʿAẓīma but with the Qāʾim (peace be upon him).

For this reason, ʿAlī kept silence in the presence of the Messenger of Allāh: the Messenger spoke and ʿAlī remained silent. The light of the ʿĀshir, the veiled one, was united with the Prophet (peace be upon him), and the rays of that light came to ʿAlī through the Prophet. Muḥammad received the knowledge of motion and the knowledge of rest — what has come to pass and what is to be — while ḥijāb ʿAlī received only the knowledge of what has been done. The Messenger of Allāh advanced ʿAlī step by step through the ranks of prophethood, and on the Day of Ghadīr he conferred upon him the day's ordinance and led him to the threshold of God's miracle — until Amīr al-Muʾminīn (peace be upon him) himself progressed to the place where the rays of the ʿĀshir descended upon him, and he himself became the veil of the ʿĀshir. Through Muḥammad, the ʿĀshir began to shine upon him, supporting and assisting him; and at that moment ʿAlī too acquired the knowledge of motion — of what has been and what is to be.

|  | Muḥammad | ʿAlī |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Jadd | ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib | ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib |
| Fatḥ | ʿAbdullāh | Abū Ṭālib |
| Khayāl | Abū Ṭālib | Muḥammad |

ʿAbdullāh is called fatḥ because he died before Abū Ṭālib.

In the order of the hierarchy, the fatḥ is Abū Ṭālib, and the khayāl is ʿAbdullāh.

For this reason, ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib, ʿAbdullāh, and Abū Ṭālib did not assemble within the temple of ʿAlī (peace be upon him) but remained upon his horizon, so that these three might become intermediaries between ʿAyn al-ʿAẓīma and ʿAlī (peace be upon him) so long as they were gathered together.

### Facts

Whenever the Imām is established, after his passing his resident master assumes the station of that Imām (the khayāl), and the resident of his resident becomes the fatḥ, and the resident of that one in turn becomes the jadd.

Of the station of ʿAbdullāh it is said:

*ʿAbdullāh and Allāh* — if the dots are removed from these words, the two become one. That is, the station of ʿAbdullāh is with Allāh (Abū Ṭālib).

### The Educational Course of Muḥammad

It is incumbent upon every nāṭiq, every successor, and every Imām to ascend, rank by rank, from the lower to the higher. As Allāh says: "We brought you forth from the wombs of your mothers while you knew nothing."

### The Five Teachers of Muḥammad

## The Islam of Khadīja and ʿAlī

The meaning of Islām is the acceptance of the gathering (jamʿ) — that is, the surrender of one's accumulated gatherings to their masters and the submission to them. Maulātnā Khadīja accepted Islām on a Monday, and ʿAlī accepted Islām on a Tuesday, with a span of five prayers between Khadīja's Islām and ʿAlī's.[^cite-12]

Likewise, the Prophet (peace be upon him) had five teachers, each of whom held the assembled forms of a prior prophetic cycle. As he learned from his teachers and advanced through them, he came to surpass them, until his final teacher was Khadīja al-Kubrā, the chief ḥujja of Imām Abū Ṭālib (peace be upon him), whose rank was far above the Prophet's own.

The Prophet's five teachers:

| 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 | 5 |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Ubayy ibn Kaʿb | Zayd ibn ʿAmr | ʿUmar ibn Nawfal | Misra (Surges) | Khadīja |
| Assembly of Ādam | Assembly of Nūḥ | Assembly of Ibrāhīm | Assembly of Mūsā | Assembly of ʿĪsā |

When the Prophet was given the station of ḥujja, and when prophethood was conferred upon him by Abū Ṭālib (peace be upon him), the conferral was effected through Khadīja al-Kubrā. Khadīja's Islām consisted in her entrusting the Messenger of Allāh with what she possessed of the rank, and in becoming his wife — she became his consort, and the Messenger of Allāh became her husband. At that moment the Prophet's rank stood higher than her own. It was a Monday, at the hour of noon, which is why the first Ẓuhr prayer of Islām was offered on that day.

On the following Tuesday, Khadīja summoned the five men:

| 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 | 5 |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Ubayy ibn Kaʿb | Zayd ibn ʿAmr | ʿUmar ibn Nawfal | Misra (Surges) | the Bohra monk |

She told them: "My master Abū Ṭālib has commanded me to inform you all that ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib shall be the Imām after him, and that the Prophet (peace be upon him) shall be his guardian until he reaches maturity. When the Prophet passes, all that is with him shall pass to ʿAlī."

In other words, Khadīja's Islām consisted in her entrusting the Prophet, on Monday at noon, with the ranks of prophethood and messengership that were his by right. And on the next day, Tuesday at noon, she entrusted the station of ʿAlī's imāma to the Prophet (peace be upon him) — and this is the Islām of ʿAlī: that ʿAlī left his possessions with the Prophet until he attained majority. That entrusting was his acknowledgment of the truth.

| Conferral of Prophethood and Messengership | Conferral of Imāma and Successorship (Waṣiyya) |

### ʿĪsā ibn Maryam

### Facts

After the Prophet, the first to accept Islām was Maulātnā Khadīja. After her, ʿAlī accepted Islām. Between the conversion of Khadīja and that of ʿAlī lay a span of five prayers. In those early days Khadīja was ʿAlī's teacher. For this reason she is in truth named Maryam, and ʿAlī is named ʿĪsā ibn Maryam. Just as Maryam, the mother of ʿĪsā, ranked above ʿĪsā, so too, in those early days, Khadīja ranked above ʿAlī.

## Our Lady Fāṭimah al-Zahrāʾ

She is the abode of the celestial brides in human form, the foundress of *Mishkāt al-Anwār al-Zāhira wa Majmaʿ al-Asrār al-Fāqira wa al-Qindīl al-Nūr fī al-Dunyā wa al-Ākhira* — that is, the Resplendent Niche of Lights, the Confluence of Luminous Mysteries, and the Lamp of Light in this world and the next.

| Mishkāt al-Anwār al-Zāhira | Majmaʿ al-Asrār al-Fāqira |
| --- | --- |
| Anwār (lights) | Asrār (mysteries) |
| The forms gathered into the temple (*haykal*) are called Anwār | After joining the temple, those same forms are called Asrār |

Whenever our Lady Fāṭimah came to him, the Prophet would rise from his place and seat her there. He would kiss her and inhale her fragrance with affection, saying, "O Fāṭimah, I smell from you the perfume of paradise." This was an indication that the Prophet would convene a great assembly and would rise to honour her presence within it. His seating her in his own place signified that wherever the Messenger of God set foot, Fāṭimah too would pass — that is, she would inherit the caliphate of the Prophet.[^cite-13]

⟪ar:****### خلاصۃ الجوھر السلامیۃ:****⟫

Before the proclamation of ʿAlī, those who died in the love of the Prophet entered Islam through him. Bibi Fāṭimah became the gatherer (*jāmiʿa*, the comprehensive one) of all such forms, and for this reason she has been called *Khulāṣat al-Jawhar al-Islāmī* — the essence of the Islamic substance. The most exalted figure within this assembly is Ḥamza. It is related that Fāṭimah would go to visit him; the earliest practice of the *tasbīḥ* (rosary) was likewise transmitted through her, for she kneaded the soil of Ḥamza's grave and fashioned a *tasbīḥ* from it. Ḥamza lies within the Prophet's Mosque.

When Jaʿfar al-Ṭayyār attained martyrdom, his rank rose higher than that of Ḥamza, for his assembly stood with the Messenger of God. The Prophet said, "Jaʿfar flies in paradise with two wings." Those two wings are: (1) Ḥamza, and (2) Abū ʿUbayda al-Ḥārith ibn ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib.

⟪ar:### آیت تطھیر کا درمیانی نقطۃ:⟫

When the companions asked the Prophet, "Who are the Ahl al-Bayt?", he replied: "We are Fāṭimah, her father, her husband, and her two sons — she is Fāṭimah, her father is her father, her husband is her husband, and her sons are her sons." It is as though he placed Fāṭimah at the centre, like the axis of a mill, which indicates her rank. Her station is *ḥujjat baʿlihā wa khalīfat abīhā* — the proof for her husband and the deputy of her father.

### The Caliphate of the Messenger of God

When the Messenger of God was in Masjid al-Fatḥ, Sūrat al-Naṣr was revealed to him, and with it the following verse:

> ⟪ar:فَسَبِّحْ بِحَمْدِ رَبِّكَ وَٱسْتَغْفِرْهُ ۚ إِنَّهُۥ كَانَ تَوَّابًۢا⟫
> *So glorify the praise of your Lord and seek His forgiveness. Verily, He is the One Who accepts repentance.*
> — **Quran 110:3** *(from image; see _provenance.json for validation)*
  (image preserved at .. /images/013.png)
  *Note:* Quran widget image with English (not Urdu) translation — unusual for this chapter. The red underline emphasises the imperative to seek forgiveness.

That is: glorify the praise of your Lord (Abū Ṭālib). The Messenger of God was being informed that the season of gatherings had arrived. The Prophet's boundaries (*ḥudūd*, ranks) had been varied, and each *ḥadd* possessed its own congregation. But now he was being told that it was time to build his own temple, drawing all those assemblies into it. In other words, he was being notified of his imminent passing, and given the indication that the moment had come to clarify the station of ʿAlī. In a *tasbīḥ*, one bead passes to the next; just so, he was being commanded to expound the great rank of ʿAlī and to seek God's forgiveness for whatever oversights had occurred behind his veil.

When this verse was revealed, Fāṭimah perceived its meaning and began to weep. Seeing her tears, the Prophet whispered something into her ear, and she smiled. After his death, ʿĀʾisha asked her about this, and she replied: "The Messenger of God said in my ear that I would be the first of his Ahl al-Bayt to meet him after him, and that I would die seventy nights after him. I rejoiced at this glad tidings."

There were seventy ranks (*ḥudūd*) between the Prophet and Fāṭimah, signalled by these seventy nights. The prayers remain silent as to whether her rank was elevated above that of Ibrāhīm Khalīl Allāh or his above hers — there is a Qur'anic parity (*qirān*) between the two, such that sometimes she advanced and sometimes Ibrāhīm did. The Fatimid Imams were called "Fāṭimī" because outwardly they are the children of Fāṭimah, while inwardly they are all wonderstruck (*ḥayrān*) and absorbed in her contemplation.

### The Contest of Rank between ʿAlī and Fāṭimah

*Ḍāminat* (the guaranteed):

ʿAlī and Fāṭimah once entered into a state of contemplative astonishment, and each began to expound their virtues. In truth, this was an expression of hierarchy. When they came to the Messenger of God for a decision, he said: "You, Fāṭimah, are Laylat al-Qadr, and ʿAlī is the Day of Ghadīr; and the Day of Ghadīr is the most excellent of days, just as Laylat al-Qadr is the most excellent of nights — yet when the two are weighed against each other, the Day of Ghadīr is greater than Laylat al-Qadr." It is for this reason that it has been said: were ʿAlī not present, Fāṭimah would have had no equal (*kufuʾ*).

(Lessons of *Tuḥfat al-Ṣayrafī*, meaning the Letters of *al-Muʿjam al-Nawādir*, by Sayyidunā Ḥusayn ibn ʿAbd al-Malik)

## The Three Ambassadors (Thalāthat Sufarāʾ)

The highest rank in the human body is the heart, then the brain. The faculties of the mind — *mufakkira* (the cogitative faculty), *mutakhayyila* (the imaginative faculty), and *ḥāfiẓa* (the retentive faculty) — stand above both heart and brain. In the standing temple (*haykal qāʾim*), they correspond, respectively, to ʿAyn (the eye, signifying ʿAlī) in the place of the heart and to Mīm (signifying Muḥammad) in the place of the brain; the three faculties of the mind are the three ambassadors — Amīr, ʿImrān, and ʿAbd Allāh. The mind has its own intellect, and the heart has its own.[^cite-14]

| Mufakkira | Mutakhayyila | Ḥāfiẓa | Heart | Brain |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib | Abū Ṭālib | ʿAbd Allāh | ʿAyn | Mīm |

This does not mean that the three ambassadors are higher in rank than ʿAyn, but rather that the assemblies (*majāmiʿ*) of the three are not gathered into ʿAyn. Although ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib possessed the comprehensiveness (*jāmiʿiyya*) of all the Imams from Adam to the Qāʾim, he did not possess the *majmaʿ* (the gathering-place) of the three ambassadors — only their elevation.

To gather (*jamʿ*) means to draw into one's own circle. The three ambassadors stand at the horizon (*ufuq*) but are not drawn into ʿAyn's circle; their *majmaʿ* lies with the Qāʾim. The faculty of any organ does not reside within the organ itself but operates upon its surface; in the same way, these three mental faculties — the three ambassadors — operate upon the surface of the brain and not within its sphere.

The Imam of the present age stands in the rank of our Lord ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib, and yet stands higher than him, for the Imam is *jadd* (the grandfather, the higher principle) of ʿAlī and the ancestor of the Qāʾim.

### The Wiping of the Head (Masḥ al-Raʾs)

It is said in the analogical sciences (*amthāl*) that our Lord ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib stands in the rank of the head. The head is the highest point of the body, and if the head is removed the body is reckoned dead. Thus the place of the head signifies height and importance, and by placing ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib in that station, his eminence is established.

In the same way, the Messenger of God is in the rank of the face, which is part of the head. In ablution, we wash the face but only wipe a small portion of the head. This indicates that complete knowledge (*ʿilm*) of *jadd al-ʿAyn* cannot be attained; the wiping signifies partial knowledge, and the indication is that a partial knowledge of *jadd al-ʿAyn* is sufficient.[^cite-15]

In *tayammum* (dry ablution), the organs of wiping are omitted entirely; only the organs of washing remain. That is, the elevated departs and only the lower remains. The meaning is that when water — that is, knowledge — is absent, even a partial acquaintance with the lower limits suffices for prayer; but when knowledge is attained, complete knowledge becomes obligatory, just as when water is obtained, *tayammum* will no longer suffice for prayer.

(Lessons of the Night of *Hijrah 500*, by Mullā Ghulām Ḥusayn ibn Ḥaḍrat Naṣr Allāh Ṣāḥib)

## Resurrections and Qāʾims (Qiyāmāt wa-Qawāʾim)

There are many resurrections. The first is the lesser resurrection (*qiyāma*). Whoever convenes a gathering of the trumpets is called a Qāʾim — "the one who stands forth." After the lesser resurrection comes the resurrection of the prophet, then of the *nāṭiq* (the speaker-prophet), and then of the chapter (*fāṣil*). All of these are resurrections that gather their respective trumpets.

The Qāʾim is the one who arises from the gathering of the trumpets.

At the end of all of these stands a great resurrection, which is the foundation of all the partial resurrections — and that is the Imam. After this comes a yet greater resurrection, the Great Resurrection (*al-qiyāma al-kubrā*), which is complete in contrast to all the partial resurrections. The Imam has a gathering of believers and limits (*ḥudūd*); the Qāʾim has a gathering of Imams.[^cite-16]

(Lessons of *Anwār al-Ṭāʾifa*, by Sayyidinā Muḥammad ibn Ṭāhir)

## The Qāʾim of the Cycle of Revelation (Qāʾim Dawr al-Kashf)

### The Cycle of Revelation (Dawr al-Kashf)

The body of the first hierarchy (*jathth*) is spread over the whole corps. After the passing of *jathth al-ibdāʾiyya* (the primordial originative hierarchy), his sons became his partial deputies as Imams. They occupy a seat in the horizon (*ufuq*). When the seven thousand years of the cycle of revelation (*dawr al-kashf*, the era of unveiling) are completed, one hundred and twenty-four thousand forms are gathered in the Tenth Horizon (*ufuq al-ʿāshir*), which the deputy of *jathth al-ibdāʾiyya* hands over to the temple of that seventh millennium — that is, their temple is constituted from the forms of the Imams of the past seven thousand years. In the same way, every seven thousand years there appears a Qāʾim who totals up the preceding seven thousand (124,000 forms). When the seventh Qāʾim — he who appears at the close of 49,000 years — arises, his glory is overwhelming. He gathers not only the last seven thousand years of his own series, but also the previous six series (each of seven thousand years) from the Tenth Barzakh (*barzakh al-ʿāshir*, the tenth interworld). The previous six occupy the rank of the body; the seventh is the culmination of the established creation.[^cite-17]

### The Onset of the Cycle of Concealment (Dawr al-Satr)

When the fifty thousand years of the cycle of revelation reach completion, the cycle of concealment (*dawr al-satr*) begins. When the cycle of revelation attains its perfection and the book reaches its consummation, age begins to overtake the cycle until laxity increases and defects arise. The Imams and their summoners withdraw behind the veil; knowledge of realities (*ḥaqāʾiq*) is replaced by knowledge of analogies (*amthāl*). The Imams enter *taqiyya* (precautionary dissimulation), while the power of the worldly authorities grows and they come to dominate the realm.

*Ḥaqāʾiq* (realities):

In the last three thousand years of the cycle of revelation, two kinds of forms appear: forms of revelation, and forms of concealment. The latter are those that will become the seeds of meaning for the cycle of concealment and will manifest in it. In the cycle of revelation there is no place for the *mustawdaʿūn* (those whose station is on deposit); only for the *mustaqirrūn* (those whose station is firmly established).

In this cycle of concealment, Adam appears first, established by the Imam of his time. He summons the people to *tawḥīd* (the affirmation of divine oneness) by his Imam's command and lays down a *sharīʿa* (revealed law). The cycle begins in weakness (old age) and moves toward the strength of a renewed cycle of revelation. Adam stands in the rank of *sulāla* (the original extract). This pattern continues until Adam appoints his successors (Hābīl and Shīth). And thus, within the seven millennia, there appear seven phases corresponding to the stages of foetal formation. Each *nāṭiq* instituted his own *sharīʿa* and its ordinances, embedding within it symbols and allusions through which the believers might attain both spiritual and bodily knowledge.

### Imams of the Cycles of Revelation and Concealment

*Ḥaqāʾiq*:

The Imams of the cycle of concealment are more numerous than those of the cycle of revelation. Moreover, the Imams after the Prophet and ʿAlī are more numerous than those who came before them. The reason is that the earlier Imams operated under a division of ranks: prophethood and its successive offices lay with the prophets, while imamate and the ranks of the *awṣiyāʾ* (trustees) lay with the Imams. But beginning with ʿAlī Zayn al-ʿĀbidīn — son of Imam Ḥusayn — all four ranks were combined in a single person, and from his time onward no such distinction is to be drawn until the Day of Judgement. For this reason, they carry greater weight than the earlier Imams.

(Lessons of *Anwār al-Ṭāʾifa*, by Sayyidinā Muḥammad ibn Ṭāhir)

## The Qāʾim of the Cycles of Revelation and Concealment

When seven thousand years have passed, those who arrive at the cycle of concealment gather the seven thousand years of that cycle. The standing-rank of the *nāṭiq* is that of the close of the era. In addition, they gather the three-thousand-year *fiṭriyya* forms of the *fiṭra* (the primordial period). In this way, a total of ten thousand years — 3,000 of *fiṭra* plus 7,000 of *dawr* — is collected from the horizon of the intellect of the Tenth (*jathth al-ibdāʾiyya*).

Vertical assembly (10,000):

| Period | Doorkeeper |
| --- | --- |
| 3000 | 7000 |

The lower forms of the period were gathered with Imam al-Hind; the higher forms were gathered with our Lord Hāshim. The temple constituted with Hāshim became the daughter of ʿAlī — that is, it became ʿAlī, and ʿAlī became the descendant of Ismāʿīl. For this reason, ʿAlī said: "In every ten thousand years there is one perfect form, and I am that form!" He had gathered the seven thousand years of the cycle of concealment together with the three thousand years of the *fiṭra* period as a part of the established whole.

### The Seven Steps

He is the partial deputy of the Qāʾim of this cycle of concealment, and the complete deputy of the founders of the previous cycle of concealment. The whole assembly is gathered with them, just as the *ḥudūd* of the believers are connected to the Imam through *bāb al-abwāb* (the gate of gates). The entire assembly becomes the body of the founder, and they themselves become the soul of that body. Then the rank of the Qāʾim of this cycle of concealment is elevated, and he totals the gathering of fifty thousand years of the cycle of revelation, becoming master of the entire gathering of fifty-seven thousand. In this way he attains the rank of the trumpet — at once trumpet and trumpeter.

Since there are seven Qāʾims of *dawr al-kashf* and seven Qāʾims of *dawr al-satr* in a single era of four hundred thousand years, seven *fawāṣil* (chapters, dividers) are formed in one era. Since we are seated within the seventh *dawr al-satr* of the first era, six great chapters have already been prepared, and they are called *al-suwar al-sitt* (the Six Forms).

(Lessons of *Anwār al-Ṭāʾifa*, by Sayyidinā Muḥammad ibn Ṭāhir)

### The Importance of the Sixth (Ismāʿīl)

The sixth person in the week stands in an extraordinary state, as in the case of our Lord Ismāʿīl ibn Jaʿfar al-Ṣādiq, who passed during his father's lifetime. The sixth is always Ismāʿīl. Imam Ismāʿīl ibn Ibrāhīm Khalīl Allāh was the sixth Imam of his week; the seventh Imam of that week was his son, Imam Qayṣar. Likewise, Ismāʿīl ibn Manṣūr is the sixth Imam in the week of the caliphs. The name Ismāʿīl is, in truth, a name of ʿAlī. The sixth always passes to the seventh and becomes the structure of the heart. Amīr al-Muʾminīn ʿAlī is the sixth in relation to the Qāʾim.

(Lessons of *Tuḥfat al-Ṣayrafī*, meaning the Letters of *al-Muʿjam al-Nawādir*, by Sayyidunā Ḥusayn ibn ʿAbd al-Malik)

### How the Plateaus of Concealment Become Established

When a Qāʾim of the cycles of revelation and concealment appears, he first becomes the deputy of his father (Abū al-Qāʾim) and receives from him the trace of the imamate. Next, he becomes the deputy of the last *sabʿa* (the set of seven) and gathers them. Next, he attains the rank of the plateau, becoming the deputy of *ṣāḥib al-alif* (the lord of the thousand) and receiving the whole thousand years from him. Next, his rank rises to the level of the cycle of concealment, and he totals the whole of the seventy thousand of that cycle. Finally, his status is elevated yet further, and the gathering of fifty thousand years of revelation from the pre-concealment period is also drawn into him, so that he comes to possess a total of fifty-seven thousand years.[^cite-18]

Then they pass into the rank of *al-ʿaql al-ʿāshir* (the Tenth Intellect) and attain their seat in its horizon. Upon reaching this seat, they begin partial planning for their own period — the coming cycle of revelation and concealment — until they have prepared their universal deputy (*khalīfa kullī*), the founder of the next fifty-seven thousand years.

For this Day, when we perform ablution, we pray: "O God, place my book in my right hand; do not place it in my left, nor from behind my back." The "book" refers to the Imam. On that Day, no believer within their assemblies will be passed over, whether of high or low rank, and it is they who will reward or punish.

There will be no reckoning for those of the cycle of revelation. The reckoning — reward and recompense — pertains only to the cycle of concealment, because the additions appear in this cycle and the retributions are likewise within it. Until the *qiṣāṣ* (just retaliations) have been completed, the cycle will continue to manifest within the cycle of concealment. God forgives His own rights, but only His servants can forgive the rights of His fellow-servants. For this reason the *faqāqa* are dispatched.

*Ḥaqāʾiq*:

*Faqāqa* signifies liberation. The burden of retribution falls upon the self-centred, the self-conscious, and the self-absorbed. Rather than having to exact *qiṣāṣ* from a fellow servant, the person from whom *qiṣāṣ* is owed is rendered, in another form — through the agency of the *faqāqa* — as the same; and the reckoning is thereby completed.

(Lessons of *Anwār al-Ṭāʾifa*, by Sayyidinā Muḥammad ibn Ṭāhir)

### The Greatness of the Cycle of Concealment

*Ḥaqāʾiq*:

Of all the Imams of the cycle of revelation, the Qāʾim who appears every thousand years is equivalent to one Imam of the cycle of concealment. And the seven thousand years of the cycle of revelation are equivalent to the three thresholds of the cycle of concealment — there is a Qur'anic parity (*qirān*) between the two.

When the coming Qāʾim appears, he will first gather the cycle of concealment. Then he will add to it the cycle of revelation — that is, the seventh cycle of concealment together with revelation. The following Qur'anic seats will be set up in his temple at that time.

### The Imams of the Seventh Cycle of Concealment

The glory of the Imams of the seventh cycle of concealment is exceedingly great. For this reason, the standing-rank of this cycle of concealment — by virtue of its yield — stands higher than the standing-rank of the cycle of revelation. The Qāʾim of revelation gathers fifty thousand years, while the Qāʾim of revelation alone gathers only seven thousand; but since the yield of the Qāʾim of revelation is so great, they exceed the number of the Imams.

(Lessons of *Anwār al-Ṭāʾifa*, by Sayyidinā Muḥammad ibn Ṭāhir)

## The Six Mustawdaʿ Imams of the Cycle of Concealment

Between Adam and Noah there stand six great Imams who together gather and embody the whole of Adam's dawr (cycle). These six are the aʾimma proper, and once they have been established in their ranks, Noah—peace be upon him—takes his place in the seventh. The same pattern holds throughout: between Noah and Abraham there are six, with Abraham the seventh; between Abraham and Moses, six, with Moses the seventh; between Moses and Jesus, six, with Jesus the seventh; between Jesus and Muḥammad, six, with Muḥammad the seventh; and after Muḥammad once more six, the seventh of whom is our master Muḥammad b. Ismāʿīl.[^cite-19]

After Mawlānā Muḥammad b. Ismāʿīl come three Imams of concealment—Mawlānā ʿAbd Allāh b. Muḥammad, Mawlānā Aḥmad b. ʿAbd Allāh, and Mawlānā Ḥusayn b. Aḥmad. In their time, each Imam's bāb (gate) was commanded to declare his Imam openly. These three Imams together with their three abwāb make six, and the seventh is Mawlānā al-Mahdī, who occupies the rank of the seventh nāṭiq (speaking prophet).[^cite-20]

The seventh Qāʾim first gathers his own fifty-seven thousand years of kashf and satr, just as each of his predecessors had done within his own sphere. He then ascends to a higher rank and plateau, where he sums into himself the six prior cycles—the sitta ṣuwar (six forms)—so that the entire dawr of four hundred thousand years stands gathered in his person.[^cite-21]

### The Place of the ʿAyn and the Mīm in the Haykal

#### Facts

The hayākil (temples) do not simply sum themselves; rather, the veil itself gathers the forms within its observer. The seventh is composed of ten thousand years (three thousand and seven thousand together). When the Qāʾim appears, he gathers the seven forms from the horizon of the Tenth. He further collects the forms of ancestry, victory, and reflection from that same horizon, and beyond these the form of the seventh kashf—itself the compendium of fifty thousand years of forms—remains. The Qāʾim becomes the heart of this entire haykal and merges it with the world to come. The form of the last, seventh kashf will be the supreme form after the end of the world, and through it the whole congregation will be gathered to the hereafter. At that moment the very hereafter is called qiyāmat al-qiyāmāt (the Resurrection of Resurrections).

The mīm is the heart of the Qāʾim's nāsūt (humanity), and the heart of the Qāʾim's lāhūt (divinity) is the haykal itself. The heart of the first is the temple, and the heart of the last is the temple of the hereafter. When all the gatherings have been brought near the Qāʾim, two points alone remain: the creators of the hereafter, and the mīm of the hereafter, which is the very structure of their hearts. The highest of the forces of this group is the jadd, who is granted the opportunity to attain the vicegerency of the Qāʾim.

After the close of this dawr, when the first kashf and satr of the next cycle is completed—that is, when the eighth kashf and satr in order of counting has run its course—then the vicegerency of Mawlānā ʿAlī b. Abī Ṭālib will descend upon the one who shall be established at that hour. This is the rank known as tasnīm.[^cite-23]

## The Mill of Kashf and Satr

Every mor begins with a kor, every kor begins with a dawr, and every dawr begins always with a remote revelation. After the ṣāḥib alif (master of the letter alif), the period of kashf has potentially commenced. When the Qāʾim ʿalī dhikrihi al-salām comes, he will inaugurate the kashf in its proper sense. The ṣāḥib alif is Mawlānā Aḥmad—the thirty-fifth Imam—in whose time Sayyidnā Sulaymān Ṣāḥib rose to authority. He is presented under the figure of the alif because the alif is a sign of the heart, and his name also appears within the words *huwa* and *manẓar*.

| Alif | Sulaymān |
| --- | --- |
| S | S |
| The heart of the Qurʾān | The heart of the prayers of the deposits |

When the period of a Qāʾim reaches its close, he appoints his son and successor as Imam before his death; that son becomes the first Imam of the kashf to follow. After his own death, the departing Qāʾim ascends with his community to the level of the Tenth Intellect and becomes master of the world of nature in place of the Tenth. The sons of the Qāʾim, as Imams, will arise from the daʿwa (the summons), pass away each in his own time, and enter their father's horizon. In this manner each Imam in turn continues the daʿwa after his father and, upon his death, takes a place upon the horizon of the Tenth together with the congregation of his own age. A rank cannot be elevated until it has established within itself a station equivalent to its own; there is no caliph other than this. When the seventh Imam appoints the seventh Imam after him—the caliph of the whole—he is raised in rank.[^cite-24]

This process continues until the first seven thousand years of the period of kashf are completed; at that point there appears a qāʾim mustaqarr (a permanent establisher) who gathers the Imams of these seven thousand years from the horizon of the Tenth. Another seven thousand years then pass, at whose close a further qāʾim mustaqarr appears and gathers his own period. In this way, throughout the fifty thousand years of kashf, seven such permanent qāʾims arise.

Thereafter the Wise One sends forth the bodies and virtues belonging to the foregoing satr into the next seven-thousand-year period.

### The Emergence of the Virtues of Revelation in the Period of Revelation

First appear the saints of the time of the Prophet—peace be upon him—who have already passed and whose virtues and souls have come to maturity. Next, the souls and bodies of those who have returned to Muḥammad from the chastisement of the lower realm—that is, from the punishments of the borders—are sent forth. This continues for seven thousand years, at whose end a great congregation of seven thousand arises. The first bodies and virtues of Jesus then appear over seven thousand years, ending in his qāʾim; then those of Moses, then of Abraham, then of Noah, and finally of Adam, each within a seven-thousand-year span.[^cite-25]

Thus, when fifty thousand years have run their course (seven thousand × seven, plus a remainder of one thousand), the period of kashf reaches its close, and with it appears a Qāʾim who sums up the six preceding partial qāʾims in addition to his own seven thousand. Within this period of kashf the final three thousand years are five hundred years apart, and after that the satr begins. Even within the satr the Imam continues to come after the father, and the summons to truth continues until the satr is fulfilled; then there appears a permanent establisher who gathers the period of kashf and the period of satr together—the seventh thousand years. He is called the Lord of Ages, for he gathers in himself the four hundred thousand years.

In this manner the alternation of day (kashf) and night (satr) continues across vast ages. A kor measures four hundred thousand mor. One dawr is four hundred thousand years; within one mor there are seven hundred turnings of kashf and satr, and a remainder of a thousand years.

### The Resurrections of the Kor and the Mor

When the principal corps reaches its end, the commander who arises in that hour gathers it entire. The spiritual bonds of the heavens—the cords of gravity—are then loosened, and everything upon the earth (animals, plants, men) perishes and returns to its former state, with administration entrusted to Zuḥal (Saturn). A new birth and a new creation then begin. During that age the harshest winters reign; the earth is covered with ice and freezing rain until the whole earth becomes a single ocean, and the cosmos goes dark. Then creation begins anew, just as it did upon the first cover.

### The Virtues of Eternity

At the close of the cover, the summaries of the inner sanctum ascend toward the sky. Part of this summary is taken up into the Sun, and the rest is distributed within the zodiac. The old things of the sky—the constellations and their hosts—descend toward the earth and merge into its soil. From these summaries the seeds of the next body are formed: the seed of the Imam is produced from the Sun, the seeds of the twenty-seven dignitaries from the rest of the heavenly parts, and the seed of the bāb from the Moon.

From there the world begins anew. Human beings are born from the depths, sons are born to the Imam, and the chain of the daʿwa resumes. When four hundred thousand kors have passed, one mor is complete. The Resurrection of the mor is the supreme Day of Judgement, in which the universes come to their end. In it all the learned are gathered up. The founders of that hour are the noblest in greatness, and they ascend to the spiritual world with the whole congregation of the mor. In the terminology of the daʿwa they are called Sultans, and it is through them that the soul is delivered from this corporeal world.

### Facts

There is one form per forty thousand kors, and there are ten such forms in the body of one mor; that is, for every forty thousand kors there is one such form, and its leader is named the *raʾsa*.

### The Resurrections of Kashf and Satr

This awaited hour is the gathering of four hundred thousand years—seven periods of kashf and seven of satr. The seventh period of kashf is itself gathered by the ʿayn. In truth, the ʿayn gathers the whole dawr, but here we speak only of what is nearest. The whole congregation of this dawr will be brought together and merged into the very first, the very hereafter.

Every seven thousand years of the kashf bring a Resurrection (the appearance of a permanent establisher of kashf), and every ten thousand years of the satr (seven thousand of satr together with three thousand of the resurrection) bring another. They are designated by their divinity, each occupying the place of the instrument for its own sphere. He is the centre, and all the stars wheel around him in astonishment at his great rank.

Every ruler holds the rank of a transmitter who will reckon for the congregation of his own age. Each nāṭiq—Adam, Noah, Abraham, Moses, Jesus, Muḥammad—is a prophet of his own age. Likewise the partial figures who come after them—the bāb, the ḥujja (proof), the dāʿī (Summoner) of the age, and the maʾdhūn—each is established for his own time. All of these partial lists compose a single permanent whole, and they will be gathered in the congregation of the great ones who strike out and exact their recompense:

All the points yet to be unveiled until the Day of Judgement are already gathered with the body of the first mor. Each point has its own appointed period.

The Qāʾim shall appear in this world according to a precedent established in the world of intentions. When those who have accepted his summons within the world of light have themselves appeared, then this point too shall appear, and he will begin his plan. So, for instance, when the second body of eternity appears, those who have accepted its summons in the spiritual world also appear; he begins his plan for his own age, and his plan continues until his full vicegerent—the next jathth al-ibdāʿiyya—is ready. When that caliph stands prepared, he is levelled out of his station.

The first verse declares, "Say: He is Allāh, One." Scripturally, the repetition of the divine name in the second verse is not redundant — it would have sufficed to say simply "He is al-Ṣamad." The deliberate doubling of "Allāh" therefore discloses that the two utterances do not point to the same referent.[^cite-31]

What is signified is the substitution of the very First by the very Last. Qāʾim ʿAlī occupies the station of ʿAyn Ūlā as deputy (waṣī) and successor (khalīfa) to the Dhikr of Islam. He has gathered into himself the forms (ṣuwar) of the fifty-seven thousand years of this period; the minimum count of forms gathered by ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib amounts to ten thousand years. The ten tasbīḥāt of the bayʿa are an index of precisely this gathering. Furthermore, the closing tasbīḥ — that of Muḥammadiyya — is recited three times to mark the three stations of the Qāʾim, and by extension of the Imam of the present age.

## The Division of the Four Ranks

Ādam and Nūḥ were the prophets of their respective ages. When Ibrāhīm — peace be upon him — came, he gathered into himself the deposits of both their cycles, and the four ranks of *nubuwwa* (prophethood), *risāla* (apostleship), *imāma* (imamate) and *waṣāya* (executorship) were assembled within him.

> **Note.** Ibrāhīm — peace be upon him — holds a singular station among the caliphates of the Qāʾim. He is the Prime Minister of the Foundation.

Ibrāhīm distributed the four ranks between his sons. To Isḥāq he assigned nubuwwa and risāla; to Ismāʿīl he committed imāma and waṣāya. The stations of nubuwwa and risāla then passed down through the line of Isḥāq, while the stations of imāma and waṣāya were transmitted through the line of Ismāʿīl. The descendants of Isḥāq became the veils (ḥijāb); all the Imams of the line of Ismāʿīl rested securely beneath that veil, while their veil — the line of Isḥāq — bore the brunt of their enemies and endured affliction in their stead.[^cite-32] So matters proceeded until the four ranks were once again gathered together in Mawlānā Hāshim — peace be upon him — who then transmitted the entire assembly to his son Mawlānā ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib.

Mawlānā ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib appointed two Bābs (Doors) for himself. Through these two Bābs he gathered, by way of the daʿwa (summons), both the souls already departed and the souls then ascending. One of these — the upper Bāb (ḥadd ʿālī) — assembled the affairs of the believers, the elders, and the limits of the learned; the other — the lower Bāb (ḥadd dānī) — assembled the affairs of the *mustajībūn* (respondents to the daʿwa) who had taken the oath of allegiance and had died upon the *walāya*. The upper Bāb was attached to Mawlānā Abū Ṭālib — peace be upon him — and the lower Bāb to Mawlānā ʿAbd Allāh, in which honour ʿAbd Allāh stood very high before Allāh.

Mawlānā ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib made known the recitation of the Qurʾān upon Ḥaḍrat ʿAbd Allāh through the rite of the slaughter — that is, he prepared him for the ranks of nubuwwa and risāla. He then entrusted imāma and waṣāya to Mawlānā Abū Ṭālib and nubuwwa and risāla to Mawlānā ʿAbd Allāh. In this manner both became Imams, of whom Abū Ṭālib was *al-mustaqarr* (the settled) and ʿAbd Allāh *al-mustawdaʿ* (the entrusted).[^cite-33]

When Mawlānā ʿAbd Allāh died during his father's lifetime, the ranks of nubuwwa and risāla reverted to ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib — for if a grandson exists during his grandfather's lifetime and the grandson's father predeceases the grandfather, the inheritance does not pass to the grandson but to the father. ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib accordingly assigned the ranks of nubuwwa and risāla to Mawlānā Abū Ṭālib in his capacity as guardian of Muḥammad — the Messenger of Allāh — which is termed *kafālat ṣughrā* (the Lesser Guardianship, the kafāla of the orphan). Thus all four ranks came to reside in Mawlānā Abū Ṭālib: of two he was the inheritor, and of two he was the guardian.

> **Note.** Mawlānā ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib held all four ranks; Mawlānā Abū Ṭālib also held all four ranks; yet ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib's station was the higher. ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib was the *owner* of all four; Abū Ṭālib was the owner of imāma and waṣāya only — guardian of the other two.

Mawlānā Abū Ṭālib retained all four ranks until the Prophet — peace be upon him — reached maturity. When ʿAlī was born and the Prophet attained forty years, Abū Ṭālib transferred to Muḥammad the ranks of nubuwwa and risāla, which were rightfully his, and entrusted to him as guardian — on behalf of ʿAlī, the true owner — the ranks of imāma and waṣāya. This is termed *kafālat kubrā* (the Greater Guardianship). In this way, the Messenger of Allāh came to encompass all four ranks. He continued thereafter to ascend rank after rank until he reached the point of Allāh and became, in cosmic terms, of the descendants of Isḥāq — that is, he gathered into himself all the prophets.

### The Effect of the Sponsorship

> **Note.** This great sponsorship had such an effect upon him that he surpassed all the *mustawdaʿ* Imams he had received. More than this: he surpassed all the *mustaqarr* Imams (Imams and Prophets) which he had received on behalf of ʿAlī, and he became the deputy of the ʿAyn. The reason: when the daʿwa was established, the first to confess was the Mīm. When ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib — the veil of ʿAyn ʿAẓīma — appeared to this world he said, "I am one of the slaves of Muḥammad." This utterance was made from the vantage of the veil, while in respect of the veiled one (al-muḥtajab), the ʿAyn is higher than the Mīm.

The dignity of the Mīm rose so high that, having attained the ten ranks of nubuwwa and risāla and every station of Imam and waṣī save the rank of ʿAlī himself, he came to surpass them all. Between him and the ʿAyn only three ranks then remained.

### The Unity of Light

Imām ʿAlī — peace be upon him — said: "The Prophet and I are from a single light." Allāh commanded that this light be divided into two parts: of the first half He commanded, "Be Muḥammad"; of the second half He commanded, "Be ʿAlī." The reference is to the daʿwa of the descendants of Isḥāq and the descendants of Ismāʿīl — nubuwwa and risāla on one side, imāma and waṣāya on the other; that is, from these two lights — the mustawdaʿ and the mustaqarr — Allāh fashioned two entities and gathered them again into one establishment.[^cite-34]

And Amīr al-Muʾminīn said: "From this aspect, Muḥammad — peace be upon him — is Yāqūt al-Ṣafrāʾ (the yellow ruby) and I am Yāqūt al-Ḥamrāʾ (the red ruby)." The Messenger of Allāh used to say: "I am from ʿAlī and ʿAlī is from me; none shall discharge my obligation save ʿAlī."

For the death of the Messenger of Allāh was, in inward reality, the death of ʿAlī himself; and the ʿAlī who appeared after the Messenger was, in reality, the veil of the Prophet. In this sense both reports stand fast in their own dimensions.

Imām al-Ḥasan — peace be upon him — held all four ranks: nubuwwa and risāla as their master, and imāma and waṣāya as their trustee. At the time of his death he transmitted all four to Imām al-Ḥusayn. The Qurʾānic principle is that the nearer of kin is the more entitled to inheritance. Imām al-Ḥusayn — peace be upon him — accordingly entrusted all four ranks to Mawlānā Imām ʿAlī Zayn al-ʿĀbidīn. ## The Ascents of the Day of Ghadīr

On the Day of Ghadīr, within the seven hours running from dawn to afternoon, many developments unfolded in the spiritual world. At every successive moment different beings advanced. The veil (ḥijāb) administers these affairs at the discretion of the veiled one (al-muḥtajab).

### The Ascents of ʿAyn ʿAẓīma

It was through the labour of ʿAyn ʿAẓīma's father, ʿImrān (Abū Ṭālib), that ʿAyn ʿAẓīma became singular. On the Day of Ghadīr ʿAyn ʿAẓīma drew to himself a great gathering. The *nāsūt-i kullī* (the universal humanity) was joined to the *lāhūt-i kullī* (the universal divinity). His aim then was to draw out the assemblies still remaining; yet he did not merely draw them in — he gained the upper hand over them. First he crossed the boundaries of the Circle of Kashf (Disclosure), then he surpassed the Three Messengers (Jadd, Fatḥ, Khayāl), and then the Seven Forms (Satta Ṣuwar). Beyond this, he obtained the intellect of the encompassing horizon, which is called the *ʿaql al-muḥīṭ* (the encompassing intellect). For this reason, Mawlānā ʿAlī's rank — modest at first — was raised greatly after Ghadīr.[^cite-36]

**The assemblies received by ʿAyn ʿAẓīma:**
1. Nāsūt khāṣṣ (the particular humanity) — 4. The aspects of the Revelation
2. Nāsūt ʿāmm (the common humanity) — 5. The context of the Seven Seasons
3. Lāhūt kullī (the universal divinity) — 6. The encompassing wisdom

The intellects of Qāʾim al-Kashf, the Three Messengers, and the Seven Forms were not absorbed into ʿAyn al-ʿAẓīma; they came to rest along the line of the circle — that is, they reached only their proper horizon. When the Qiyāma comes, they shall absorb the intellects of Qāʾim al-Kashf, the Three Messengers and the Seven Forms from the horizon of ʿAyn al-ʿAẓīma, and at last shall absorb Qāʾim al-Kashf himself, becoming thereby the senses of the Divine Revelation.

### Prayer at the Seventh Hour

The prayer of the Day of Ghadīr is performed in the seventh hour. This is because the six hours preceding it signify the Seven Horns. In each hour the assembly of fifty-seven thousand years gathers — that is, the assembly of one Kashf and one Satr. When the Satr arrives in this present age, the seventh hour shall be fulfilled.

### Man kuntu Mawlāhu fa-hādhā ʿAlī Mawlāh

Although the Prophet gave the higher of the two invitations to ʿAlī in mid-Shaʿbān, his own supremacy remained intact. For on the night of the twenty-seventh of Rajab he had received a special grace, and a special blessing thereafter. Through this his pre-eminence was established over all, and all the prophets — who until then had stood in their respective temples — were placed beneath him. Throughout his life ʿAlī did not obtain that singular grace and *nāsūt khāṣṣ*. The Prophet — peace be upon him — declared: "Man kuntu mawlāhu fa-hādhā ʿAlī mawlāhu" ("Whoever I am his master, this ʿAlī is his master"). The *nāsūt* that is constituted of the limits of the noble gentlemen, wrapped in the *lāhūt* (al-khāṣṣūt) — that is, the *nāsūt khāṣṣ* so exalted that it resembles the *lāhūt* — was given to the Prophet for life. ʿAlī received it on the Day of Ghadīr. Thus the Prophet's station remained superior to ʿAlī's throughout his lifetime.

### The Exchange of Veils

As soon as the Prophet — peace be upon him — recited the *naṣṣ* (textual designation) upon Mawlānā ʿAlī, the veils of both were exchanged: ʿAlī took on the veil of the Prophet, and the Prophet took on the veil of ʿAlī. The *nāṭiq* (Speaker-Prophet) and the *waṣī* (executor) die through their veils — meaning that ʿAlī died during the Prophet's lifetime. The veil of the Prophet's death was, in inward truth, the veil of the ʿAyn. That is, the ʿAyn died before the Mīm.

This is, in reality, why ʿAlī appeared outwardly weak after the death of the Prophet and was compelled to remain silent for thirty years: it was the *ḥijāb* of the Mīm that was now operative, weaker than the *ḥijāb* of the ʿAyn. A further nuance is that all the marvellous deeds of Mawlānā ʿAlī — the uprooting of the gate of Khaybar, the incident of the Pir ʿIlm, and the like — fall within the Prophet's lifetime. After the Prophet's death no extraordinary deed is attributed to ʿAlī. And this is why Mawlānā ʿAlī threatened to divorce ʿĀʾisha at the Battle of the Camel: it was the *ḥijāb* of the Prophet that was operative there — otherwise there could have been no sense in ʿAlī's divorcing the Prophet's wife.

### The Delivery of the Trusts

At the time of his death, the Prophet — peace be upon him — delivered his remnants to ʿAlī in five ways. The final episode was the Messenger's last breath, which flowed upon ʿAlī's hand and which he wiped across his face. It is said that the final breath of the Prophet was an instance of the Islamic daʿwa, which ʿAlī mingled with his own face — that is, the daʿwa of *īmān*. There was thus a conjunction of the Islamic daʿwa and the daʿwa of īmān.

The *nāṭiq*s die through their veils — that is, they use their veils for their death and burial. Because the veil was exchanged, the Prophet died through that veil. The one who shrouded him was ʿAbd Allāh ibn Rawāḥa; the one who buried him was ʿAbd Allāh ibn Masʿūd; and the one who remained in the grave was Ubayy ibn Kaʿb.

## The Nāṭiq (the Speaking-Prophet) and the Ranks of Divine Speech

Adam's daʿwa (the summons / the invitation) was confined to its outward dimension alone. From his cycle the summons descended to Noah, peace be upon him, who joined to the outward invitation the interpretive invitation grounded in taʾwīl (esoteric exegesis). The summons then reached Abraham, peace be upon him, in whom the two thousand years of Adam and Noah were gathered into a single inheritance. He disclosed the ḥaqāʾiq (the realities) together with the outward and the interpretive invitations alike. Adam and Noah were themselves invitees to Abraham, and all four ranks were assembled in him.[^cite-38] As God says in the Qurʾān:

> ⟪ar:وَإِبْرَٰهِيمَ ٱلَّذِى وَفَّىٰٓ⟫

That is, he attained the three levels of knowledge—tanzīl (revealed text), taʾwīl, and ḥaqīqa (the real).

The emergence of a nāṭiq is required when the virtues poised to manifest in his age demand a recasting of the sharīʿa (sacred law). Each nāṭiq is accompanied by a gathering proper to his era, but that gathering does not stand in the abjadī (alphabetical) configuration; the alphabetical configuration is mentioned only in connection with the Bāb al-Abwāb (the Gate of Gates). The prophets Adam, Noah, Moses, and Jesus belonged to the nāsūt (the bodily-human order) alone. Those who possessed the divine doctrine—Hūd, Abraham, Hāshim, and Muḥammad—stood above the rank of the Imamate, possessors of both lāhūt (the divine) and nāsūt at once. The difference between the divine and the merely Divine-bearing being is great indeed.

When an Imam is born, he is at first a man of innate nāsūtī nature. When through his father's chapter he comes into possession of the haykal (the temple) of the believers, he becomes possessor of divinity as well—that is, the conjunction of lāhūt with nāsūt. ## The Lesser Assembly (Majmaʿ al-Ṣighār)

The Prophet had four children, and all four died in infancy. Yet all four belonged to the people of the hierarchy of prayer; together they form four sites in which the gatherings of various infant collectivities are assembled.

| Ibrāhīm | Qāsim | Ṭayyib | Ṭāhir |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |

To these four are entrusted, by way of example, the children of the prophets who died in infancy and the children of believers who died in infancy. In addition, there are adult believers who, through faults in knowledge and action, did not attain maturity in this knowledge and practice. All such forms, in their gathered groupings, were entrusted to these four for their further training. The aggregate of these gatherings is called the Majmaʿ al-Ṣighār (the Lesser Assembly).

By reason of their imperfections, this Assembly of al-Ṣighār is not incorporated into the temple of the Imam. They are assigned instead to a succession of stations, where their training is advanced; from each they pass to the next. In this graduated manner they continue to be elevated, until at the last they are incorporated into the established structure.

The descendants of all the prophets who died in childhood formed the first Majmaʿ al-Ṣighār. This gathering was first received by Ibrāhīm, son of the Prophet, who died at four years of age. It was next received by Bībī Fāṭima, and then by Imam Muḥammad al-Bāqir, peace be upon him, through Jābir ibn ʿAbd Allāh al-Anṣārī. Thereafter the gathering accrued to Mawlānā ʿAbd Allāh al-Mahdī, peace be upon him; from him it passed to ʿAlī al-Ẓāhir, and from there to Mawlānā Mahdī Akhtar al-Zamān. There they shall complete their training and at last join the established temple.

## The Ranks of the Panjtan

Muḥammad Muṣṭafā, peace and blessings be upon him, was made witness by the Imam through two testimonies (the shahādatayn): the testimony that there is no god but God, and the testimony that Muḥammad is the Messenger of God. In this fashion the Prophet remained with the outward invitation only. The people accepted Islam at his hand, joined him in jihād, fulfilled the duties (the seven prayers) and the sunan he had established, and remained with these. They all adhered to the outward wilāya (guardianship) of ʿAlī and bore him no enmity. By reason of these works, their souls became jewel-like, their faces brightened, and their inmost selves were illumined. When they died, they all passed into the temple of Mawlātnā Fāṭima. Thus the gathering of outward worshippers, and the gathering of the wilāya, were both conjoined in Mawlātnā Fāṭima; and for this reason she is called Khulāṣat al-Jawharāt al-Islāmiyya (the Distillation of the Islamic Jewels), since the gathering of all Islamic forms was assembled in her.[^cite-41]

Mawlānā ʿAlī had a gathering of believers. When the temple of Mawlātnā Fāṭima was completed—she had been gathered into her temple for thirteen years—her father gave her in marriage to Mawlānā ʿAlī, so that these Islamic and faithful forms might be reconciled. In this state the outward daʿwa was issued, and the summons to the inner world continued. The affairs of these two—ʿAlī and Fāṭima—proceeded in this manner until al-Ḥasan, peace be upon him, was born of the outward invitation, and al-Ḥusayn, peace be upon him, came forth from the inward invitation. The outward daʿwa was the episode of the ẓāhir; the inward summons was the episode of the bāṭinī will.

| Invitation of the ẓāhir | Invitation of the inner soul |

| Primary speaker | Installment of the guardian |

The last and highest of all the great stations that the Prophet possessed is the ḥijāb (the veil). Among the hierarchies he held were prophethood and the prayer—which were his own—which he entrusted to al-Ḥasan, peace be upon him, through ʿAlī. He committed the prophethood and the waṣīyya (executorship) to Mawlānā ʿAlī as a trustee for Ḥasan, so that when the latter came of age and completed his education, ʿAlī would entrust them to him in turn.

After the Prophet's passing, ʿAlī remained in all four ranks. That is, the Prophet did not bestow all things upon al-Ḥasan at once. When he said, "Al-Ḥasan wa al-Ḥusayn imāmāni ḥaqq, qāʿidān aw qāʾimān, wa abūhumā khayrun minhumā" (Ḥasan and Ḥusayn are Imams of truth, whether they sit or stand; and their father is better than both of them), he entrusted certain things to Ḥasan and Ḥusayn. For the remaining matters he charged ʿAlī to deliver them to his two sons when they came of age.[^cite-42]

**Ḥaqāʾiq (Realities)**

There are ten levels of the Imamate, the first being the station of istijāba (the Mustajīb, the Respondent), followed by the station of īmān (the Muʾmin, the Believer). In the same fashion the inner ranks of the Imamate also rise by degrees.

ʿAlī then entrusted the prophethood and waṣīyya to al-Ḥusayn. Though al-Ḥasan was Imam before al-Ḥusayn, certain stations of imamate and prophethood were transferred to al-Ḥusayn during al-Ḥasan's lifetime, so that he was, in those respects, Imam even before al-Ḥusayn manifestly assumed it. It is narrated that when the summons of ʿĀshūrāʾ was given, al-Ḥasan came forward before al-Ḥusayn; yet al-Ḥusayn possessed a vision greater than al-Ḥasan's. For this reason the condition of remaining (the abiding station) lay not with al-Ḥasan but with al-Ḥusayn. Thus certain stations of al-Ḥusayn came to be established within al-Ḥasan. These ranks gathered with you shall no longer issue forth from you; and none shall attain them except the Qāʾim. At the end of days all these ranks shall be gathered in the temple of the Qāʾim al-Qiyāma ʿAlī Dhikrā al-Salām, and you shall become the soul (nafs) of that temple. ʿAlī's place shall be that of the heart; the Messenger of God, peace be upon him, shall occupy the place of the head; and the rest—the three ambassadors, Fāṭima, and the Imams—shall be the limbs. The three ambassadors (ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib, Abū Ṭālib, and ʿAbd Allāh) shall stand in the place of the faculties of the intellect.

**The Established Structure**

- Qāʾim ʿAlī Dhikrā al-Salām · Nafs al-Haykal (Soul of the Temple)
- ʿAlī, peace be upon him · Heart of the structure
- Muḥammad, peace and blessings be upon him · Raʾs al-Haykal (Head of the Temple)
- The Imams · Limbs of the structure

These three (ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib, Abū Ṭālib, and ʿAbd Allāh) shall remain in the temple of ʿĀshir and shall not be gathered into the temple of Muḥammad and ʿAlī, for they are ambassadors of God to both. Their gathering shall be held at the time of the Resurrection itself.

(From the *Lessons of Anwār al-Ṭāʾifa* of Sayyidinā Muḥammad ibn Ṭāhir.)

## Imam ʿAlī Zayn al-ʿĀbidīn

When Mawlānā ʿAlī Zayn al-ʿĀbidīn was born, Mawlānā ʿAlī declared: "Anā ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib, hādhā ʿAlī Zayn al-ʿĀbidīn" (I am ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib; this is ʿAlī Zayn al-ʿĀbidīn).

The mother of Mawlānā ʿAlī Zayn al-ʿĀbidīn was Grāmī (Shahr Bānū), a non-Arab (ʿajamī) woman. Since Imam al-Ḥusayn, peace be upon him, was an Arab—that is, he possessed the realities of stability—Imam ʿAlī Zayn al-ʿĀbidīn carried within him a conjunction of Arab and non-Arab, and the two collectivities were joined in him.

- Arab · ʿAjam
- Stable · Storehouse
- Imamate (228 waṣīyyāt) · Prophethood (228 epistles)

**Ādam al-Ābāʾ (Adam of the Fathers)**

When Imam Ibrāhīm, peace be upon him, was granted the four ranks (Prophethood, Apostleship, Imamate, and Will), he became a Ḥijāb-i ʿĀshir (Veil of the Tenth), which is why he was called Khalīl Allāh (the Friend of God). He partitioned these four stations, granting the highest ranks to Mawlānā Ismāʿīl and the lower to Ḥaḍrat Isḥāq. From there these ranks passed through Mawlānā Hāshim and were assembled in Mawlānā ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib, who in turn divided the hierarchies once more. From that time forward the ranks remained divided, until they came to Mawlānā Imam ʿAlī Zayn al-ʿĀbidīn and were again gathered into one. Upon their arrival in him, the condition of these gatherings changed: from his time until the Day of Judgment there will be no further partition of these ranks. Imam ʿAlī Zayn al-ʿĀbidīn was the first to attain this new condition; for this reason he is called Ādam al-Ābāʾ (Adam of the Fathers). From the era of the Imams until the Day of Judgment, the condition of the Imams was altered, and through the consolidation of the four ranks their stations were raised above those of the previous Imams. For this reason it is said in prayer: "Muḥammad wa ʿAlī khayr al-bashar, wa ʿitratuhumā khayr al-ʿitar" (Muḥammad and ʿAlī are the best of mankind, and their progeny is the best of all progeny).[^cite-43]

On the basis of this principle, Rajab al-Ḥarām and Shahr Allāh (the Month of God) are differentiated as day from night.

Though the ḥujaj of the Imams are inferior in rank, the glory of these scholars is exceedingly great. The glory of the Manṣūṣ (the one designated) is itself enhanced—just as the Prophet, peace be upon him, enhanced the glory of the universe. In truth, the principal purpose of the naṣṣ (the explicit designation) is to elevate the Manṣūṣ. In this sense alone, the Ḥujja (the son of the Imam) stands above the Imam.

## The Universal Sending (al-Baʿth al-Kullī)

We affirm the resurrection of the dead, and when we recite the tashahhud we declare, "O Allāh, I am the Messenger of Allāh." That is, Allāh shall raise those who lie in their graves. To be raised from the graves means this: just as Allāh brought us into the present sphere through our parents, so shall He bring us into the next sphere through our parents. The mother's womb is itself a grave, into which the father buries his seed. The dignity of the human person consists in being born of mother and father and brought thereby into the station of faith. The Messenger of Allāh (peace and blessings be upon him), however, taught that one must leave the lineage of natural parents and be born anew of God — that is, to be joined to the spiritual body. This is what is called al-baʿth al-kullī (the universal sending), and it is the distinguishing honour of the Prophet. Allāh, Most High, says in Sūrat Yāsīn:

> ⟪ar:يسٓ ۝ وَٱلْقُرْءَانِ ٱلْحَكِيمِ ۝ إِنَّكَ لَمِنَ ٱلْمُرْسَلِينَ ۝ عَلَىٰ صِرَٰطٍۢ مُّسْتَقِيمٍۢ⟫
> yes. By the Qur'an , I swear by the Wise . Surely you are one of the Messengers . On the Straight Path
> — **Quran 36:1** *(from image; see _provenance.json for validation)*
  (image preserved at .. /images/033.png)
  *Note:* Quran widget image, range 36:1-4. The yellow highlights on verses 3-4 emphasize the prophetic affirmation and the straight-path concept central to the chapter.

Allāh addressed His Prophet, first exalting the grandeur of his rank and then conveying something humble concerning him: that he stands upon a straight path which he himself does not fully apprehend. Having elucidated the lofty qualities of his prophethood, He thus disclosed this modest truth in turn. The "straight path" in this verse therefore refers to the path of the originated bodies (al-ajthāth al-ibdāʿiyya): his caliphate is established within those bodies, and as he ascends in the day of the cycle (yawm al-dawr) he is joined to them. The Prophet himself declares in the Qurʾān:

> ⟪ar:إِنِّى تَوَكَّلْتُ عَلَى ٱللَّهِ رَبِّى وَرَبِّكُم ۚ مَّا مِن دَآبَّةٍ إِلَّا هُوَ ءَاخِذٌۢ بِنَاصِيَتِهَآ ۚ إِنَّ رَبِّى عَلَىٰ صِرَٰطٍۢ مُّسْتَقِيمٍۢ⟫
> I have put my trust in God, my Lord and your Lord. There is not a creature that moves on the earth but He holds its forefingers. Surely my Lord is on a straight path.
> — **Quran 11:56** *(from image; see _provenance.json for validation)*
  (image preserved at .. /images/034.png)
  *Note:* Quran widget image. The 'straight path' theme echoes image 033's verse 36:4.

His "Lord" in this passage is Mawlānā Abū Ṭālib (peace be upon him): the Prophet here is honouring his patron and mentor, affirming that Mawlānā Abū Ṭālib likewise stands upon the very path that will eventually enter the body of the cycle.

### The Straight Path

When in Sūrat al-Fātiḥa we say "ihdinā al-ṣirāṭ al-mustaqīm" (guide us to the straight path), the expression signifies that our being should not merely rest upon the right path but actively traverse it. It signifies reaching from one limit (ḥadd) to another, ascending until one attains the highest extremity, which is the Temple of the Imām. One must never become arrested at any single ḥadd ʿālī (the upper limit) or ḥadd dānī (the lower limit), but continue to grow and ascend.[^cite-44]

*(Lessons of Anwār al-Ṭāʾifa, Sayyidnā Muḥammad b. Ṭāhir)*

### Realities

Every Imām of the first week is equal to a full week (usbūʿ), and the one who gathers them all — Muḥammad b. Ismāʿīl — is the most excellent. The same parallel obtains for the final week, every member of which is likewise equivalent in rank. The first week is called the Muḥammadan period (dawr Muḥammadī) because its Imāms are those who bring the Muḥammadan cycle to completion. The Imāms of the final week are designated the Period of Faithfulness (dawr al-wafāʾ). Of these two weeks, the first is the more excellent. In like manner, the seventh Imām of every remaining week possesses the rank of divine providence.

## Mawlānā Muḥammad b. Ismāʿīl

The chain of truth continued after Imām al-Ḥusayn (peace be upon him) through the week of the atmāʾ until the command reached Mawlānā Muḥammad b. Ismāʿīl (peace be upon him), who was the founder and inaugurator of this period. Through his daʿwa (the summons), he conveyed the commandments of the Sharīʿa of the Prophet (peace and blessings be upon him) to the whole world. He is the Creator (al-mūjid), and he is the Original Disclosure (al-kashf al-aṣlī), because he revealed the inner knowledge (al-ʿilm al-bāṭin). Those who appear at the Resurrection are the creators of the Greater Disclosure (kashf al-kubrā).[^cite-46]

Mawlānā Muḥammad b. Ismāʿīl occupies a most elevated rank. Upon his death he became the caliph of the Tenth (khalīfat al-ʿāshir). He is the Prophet, because he gathered the six who preceded him into himself. He has surpassed the rank of the Seventh Speaker (al-nāṭiq al-sābiʿ) and attained the highest rank of divine unicity (waḥdat ilāhiyya), which is the rank of the Qāʾim. He assembled an exceptionally splendid congregation, namely the congregation of the first week. Each of those Imāms — from al-Ḥasan through to Ismāʿīl — is himself a qāʾim. Therefore, when Muḥammad b. Ismāʿīl gathered them all, he gathered a constellation of qāʾims. The virtue (faḍl) of Mawlānā Muḥammad b. Ismāʿīl arises from several considerations:

| 1 | He is the son of his father, the Imām. |
| --- | --- |
| 2 | He is the gatherer of the cycle (that is, he assembled the seventh of the atmāʾ). |
| 3 | He became the deliverer (mukhalliṣ). |
| 4 | He is the author of the First Disclosure (kashfāt al-awlī): his rank was concealed and shall become manifest in the dawr al-kashf. |
| 5 | He became the Speaker of the Sharīʿa (nāṭiq al-sharīʿa): he abrogated the law of Muḥammad (peace be upon him) and then issued the same law anew. |
| 6 | He is the first of the caliphates of the Qāʾim after ʿAyn and Mīm. |

### The Seventh Speaker: Mawlānā Muḥammad b. Ismāʿīl

If the seventh nāṭiq gathers the previous six into himself, does it follow that Mawlānā Muḥammad b. Ismāʿīl outranks Muḥammad Muṣṭafā (peace and blessings be upon him)?

Muḥammad Muṣṭafā (peace and blessings be upon him) holds the rank of the Sixth Speaker (al-nāṭiq al-sādis), which stands lower than that of the Seventh Speaker. Yet the station of the Mīm reached the veil (ḥijāb) of ʿAyn ʿAẓīma (the Great Source), by which means he became the veil of ʿAyn ʿAẓīma and rose above the rank of the nāṭiq al-sābiʿ. He surpassed all the Imāms until the Day of Judgement and became leader over them all. For this reason his rank stands higher than that of Mawlānā Muḥammad b. Ismāʿīl.

In the same manner, the Qāʾim (peace be upon him) ascended from the point of the nāṭiq al-sābiʿ, took ʿAyn ʿAẓīma as his vizier, and himself became the Qāʾim of the entire cycle. Such is the loftiness of those who dwell in the horizon of the intellect — they are the point. True monotheism is the knowledge of from which point the ascent began and to which point it has risen.

### Realities

Within this period of seventy, in a certain sense the nāṭiq al-sābiʿ is Mawlānā Muḥammad b. Ismāʿīl (peace be upon him), because he is the seventh Imām of the week of the atmāʾ who gathered the six before him (with the exception of Imām al-Ḥusayn (peace be upon him)). The original nāṭiq al-sābiʿ is, in reality, the rank of the Qāʾim (peace be upon him), since he is the gatherer of the six nāṭiqs; but because the station of the Qāʾim was far higher than that of the Seventh Speaker, he conferred the rank of nāṭiq al-sābiʿ upon Mawlānā Muḥammad b. Ismāʿīl (peace be upon him).

### The Imāmic and Qāʾimic Structure

Mawlānā Muḥammad b. Ismāʿīl (through his father Ismāʿīl) inherited the temple of the Imāmate. In addition, as founder of the Prophet's line, he assembled the structure of the Establishment. He conferred the temple of the Imāmate upon his son ʿAbdallāh, and the temple of the Qāʾimate upon his eldest son Yaʿlā, who became the trustee of ʿAbdallāh. Yaʿlā's veil was ʿAbdallāh, who himself was the Imām of the time. Then ʿAbdallāh's veil was established in turn.

## ʿAyn ʿAẓīma (the Great Source)

"I know all things." Your declaration that you are the Knower of all things is so because you have been wholly joined to the Tenth (al-ʿāshir), in whose light you have come to know everything that has been and is yet to come. By virtue of this, the entire world of physical being lies before you like a ball cast upon the ground, no part of which is concealed from you. From the same dimension you also say: "I have a rank that has not descended upon the heart of any mortal, and I possess a limit that none has reached" — meaning, I have gathered such forms whose grandeur no one is capable of comprehending.[^cite-47]

### The Knowledge of the Great One

The creatures know Allāh through outward knowledge (al-ʿilm al-ẓāhir); the people of truth (ahl al-ḥaqq) know Allāh through true knowledge and are monotheists through Him. Yet none has attained to the Divine Knowledge save the Messenger of Allāh and the Imāms from his progeny. The rest know the realities only by way of outward knowledge. When the Prophet disclosed the signs, the proofs, the miracles, and the revelations, some of those who listened to him (the Nāṣirī community) came to recognise the unseen through him: some called you the First Intellect (al-ʿAql al-Awwal), while others spoke of you differently. These subtle secrets were hidden from them, and apart from a few none could know you properly. Such was the knowledge that, after the cycle of eternity until the appearance of the coming Qāʾim al-Qiyāma, your point stands the highest.

### The Power of the Great One (Makūr Aʿẓam)

It is precisely the Great Ones who exist in true reality. Allāh, Most High, says:

## The Three Currents (al-Majārī al-Thalātha)

The three modes of relation between the Messenger of Allāh (peace and blessings be upon him) and ʿAlī b. Abī Ṭālib.

### The First Current

The condition of the first current is that of the Great One in the world of the concealed ones (al-muḥtajibūn), which has risen to the level of the Mīm in the world of light. In this state the Messenger of Allāh (peace and blessings be upon him) says to ʿAlī: "ʿAlī has obtained three things which I myself have not obtained — a father-in-law like me, a bride like Fāṭima, and sons like al-Ḥasan and al-Ḥusayn."

### The Second Current

The second condition obtains in the world of the veil, in which the Prophet (peace and blessings be upon him) joined the index fingers of his right and left hands and declared: "I and ʿAlī are like these two fingers." That he said "I" first signifies that he held the position of the right finger, and that ʿAlī held the position of the left. With reference to this state, we declare in the adhān: "Muḥammad and ʿAlī are the best of mankind."

### The Third Current

In this veiled state, ʿAlī says: "I am one of the slaves of Muḥammad (peace and blessings be upon him) — the slave of slaves of Muḥammad." That is, ʿAlī's veil was lower than that of the Prophet. There is a great glory in this third state. All the earlier prophets rendered great services to their Imāms: the Imāms remained at rest, while the prophets continued to resist adversity and endured hardship. When the time of the Messenger of Allāh arrived, Allāh willed to recompense the services of all these prophets. For this reason He sent ʿAlī as a follower of the Prophet. Hence ʿAlī continued to defend the Prophet (peace and blessings be upon him) with his sword, while the Prophet himself would sit beneath a shady tree during the battles. All the earlier prophets stood with the Prophet, and all the earlier Imāms stood with ʿAlī. ʿAlī repaid the sufferings of all the prophets by suffering in his own person within these battles, thereby granting comfort to the noble lineage (Banū Karīm).[^cite-50]

## The Tasbīḥ that Precedes Ayat al-Kursī

The Prophet (peace and blessings be upon him) was once asked about the believers who, having departed this world, join the Temple of the Imam: what is their occupation upon arrival? He replied that they are engaged in the glorification of God and in praise of Him, and that they do not abandon this tasbīḥ (glorification) for so much as the blink of an eye.

Tasbīḥ leads the soul into the realm of inviolability. It was through their ceaseless glorification and sanctification that the angels acquired the quality by which they can no longer err — that is, they attained ʿiṣma (impeccability). The Qurʾān addresses them in this very condition:

> ⟪ar:يَـٰٓأَيُّهَا ٱلَّذِينَ ءَامَنُوا۟ قُوٓا۟ أَنفُسَكُمْ وَأَهْلِيكُمْ نَارًۭا وَقُودُهَا ٱلنَّاسُ وَٱلْحِجَارَةُ عَلَيْهَا مَلَـٰٓئِكَةٌ غِلَاظٌۭ شِدَادٌۭ لَّا يَعْصُونَ ٱللَّهَ مَآ أَمَرَهُمْ وَيَفْعَلُونَ مَا يُؤْمَرُونَ⟫
> O you who believe! Save yourselves and your families from a Fire whose fuel is men and stones, over which are angels stern and severe, who do not disobey God in what He commands them and do as they are commanded.
> — **Quran 66:6** *(from image; see _provenance.json for validation)*
  (image preserved at .. /images/040.png)
  *Note:* Quran widget image. Yellow highlights emphasise the family-protection imperative and the stern angels who guard the Fire.

In the lexicon of the daʿwa (the summons), tasbīḥ and taqdīs denote the attainment of the knowledge of the Imam. When a believer dies, the ledger of his outward acts is closed, since action belongs to the body; yet his longing for knowledge remains, and in the Temple that longing is met by an unbroken supply of gnosis. The Qurʾān describes their state:[^cite-51]

> ⟪ar:أُو۟لَـٰٓئِكَ هُمُ ٱلْمُؤْمِنُونَ حَقًّۭا ۚ لَّهُمْ دَرَجَـٰتٌ عِندَ رَبِّهِمْ وَمَغْفِرَةٌۭ وَرِزْقٌۭ كَرِيمٌۭ⟫

By this scholarly sustenance, the believers remain occupied in acquiring the knowledge of the Imam.

After every obligatory prayer, the believer recites Ayat al-Kursī. The verse proper runs from "Allāhu lā ilāha illā huwa" to "wa-huwa al-ʿAlī al-ʿAẓīm." Before reciting it, however, the Prophet instituted a set of preparatory words, each carrying a particular meaning. The worshipper says:

*Allāhu Akbar, Allāhu Akbar, Allāhu Akbar, Allāhu Akbar.*

*Subḥān Allāh, al-ḥamdu li-llāh, wa-lā ilāha illā Allāh, wa-Allāhu Akbar.*

*Lā ḥawla wa-lā quwwata illā bi-llāh al-ʿAlī al-ʿAẓīm.*

Each Imam has at least three lineal descendants through whom his line is identified. To affirm the knowledge of this descent, the worshipper says *Allāhu Akbar* three times:

**Subḥān Allāh.**

*Subḥān Allāh* is a formula of tanzīh (transcendence) that affirms the subsistence of God while purifying Him from the attributes proper to creation. The root *sabaḥa* means to float. Just as the buoyancy of water lifts the swimmer rather than letting him sink, so the majesty of Allāh — that is, of the Imam — never permits His descent to a lower plane, but maintains Him always at the highest station.

Every Imam is a Lāhūt, a collected gathering of the spiritual figures (ṣuwar) he has received from his father's daʿwa. More than this, every Imam in turn prepares such a gathering for his son. This Divine Gathering is of immense nobility and grandeur. In acknowledgement of His sovereignty and holiness, the worshipper exclaims, "Subḥān Allāh — how exalted is the glory of God!"[^cite-52]

**Al-ḥamdu li-llāh.**

The son of every Imam — the one destined to become Imam in his turn — is, in his preparatory phase, in the position of a prophet relative to the believers. Thus the formula *al-ḥamdu li-llāh* announces that praise belongs to the present Imam, and that this praise is embodied in his successor-son.

When the son of the Imam (the nāsūtī faqīr) receives Lāhūt from his father, he himself becomes the Imam (Allāh). The worshipper, by uttering this praise, confesses that the Imam of the age has prepared his rightful successor — his most consequential task — and gives thanks that the daʿwa has not lapsed, but has been carried forward through the son of the Imam. He records his gratitude that Lāhūt has met Nāsūt; for unless the Imam prepares his son and entrusts him with the Imamate, the believer's thanksgiving cannot be completed.

**Lā ilāha illā Allāh.**

This is the worshipper's affirmation that no one among the ḥudūd (the limits) of the daʿwa, however advanced, was capable of becoming Imam except the son of the Imam himself. He alone possesses the capacity to govern from the heavens to the earth.

**Wa-Allāhu Akbar.**

This is the worshipper's acknowledgement that the rank of the Imam stands above the rank of all other Imams, and that all the Imams stand in astonishment before his knowledge. No Imam possesses the capacity to encompass the greatness of the Qāʾim al-Muntaẓar (the awaited Riser).

**Lā ḥawla wa-lā quwwata illā bi-llāh al-ʿAlī al-ʿAẓīm.**

The word *ḥawl* signifies might, and *quwwa* signifies strength. Everything in the cosmos turns within the orbit of these two, and nothing is exempt from them. In the terminology of the daʿwa, *ḥawl* is the inner gate that gathers the forms of the noble ones, while *quwwa* is the outer gate that gathers the chapters of the islands (jazāʾir).

These words are pronounced because neither the inner gate nor the outer gate possesses, of itself, the might and strength sufficient to form the Temple. That capacity belongs only to the Imam, who is the caliph and the veil of ʿAyn ʿAẓīma. Without God there is no might and no power; in just this way, the existence of Ḥasan and Ḥusayn is impossible without ʿAlī al-ʿAẓīm — nor can Ḥasan and Ḥusayn together rival ʿAlī al-ʿAẓīm.

Within the order of the Imamate, *ḥawl* denotes Lāhūt and *quwwa* denotes Nāsūt. The one who pronounces this confession of *ḥawl* and *quwwa* confesses, in effect, that there would be no Lāhūt and no Nāsūt were it not for the Master of Lāhūt and Nāsūt.

*Twist.*

The rank of *ḥawl* stands above that of *quwwa*. As the cycle progresses, *quwwa* will be drawn up into *ḥawl* — what is called the *Tilak Āyat Wāḥida*.

If we restore this formula to its essential meaning, "Lā ḥawla wa-lā quwwata illā bi-llāh al-ʿAlī al-ʿAẓīm" affirms that the Originator of the universe is none other than the Originator of the divine essence itself.

## Ayat al-Kursī

This verse is called ⟪ar:آیت الکرسی⟫ because it employs the word ⟪ar:کرسی⟫, which is the verse's organising principle. The word ⟪ar:کرسی⟫ occurs only once in the entire Qurʾān, since it designates a singular place — one that has no parallel. Several traditions surround its recitation: among them, that whoever recites it according to the numerical value of *kursī* (290), or according to the numerical value of his own name, will receive abundant divine aid. For example, one bearing the name ʿAlī (numerical value 110) should recite the verse 110 times.

⟪ar:أمیر المومنین⟫ has said: "None has obeyed this verse save the Messenger of God, the trusted Messenger, and the believer whom God has tested in faith." The Prophet (peace be upon him) has likewise said, ⟪ar-quote:فِی کُلَّ حَرفٍ اَلفَ رَحمَۃ وَاَلفَ بَرَکَۃ⟫ — "In every letter of this verse there are a thousand mercies and a thousand blessings."

The taʾwīl (esoteric exegesis) of the formula teaches that Ayat al-Kursī, in its essential structure, terminates at ⟪ar:’اﷲُ وھو العلی العظیم‘⟫. Everything that intervenes between these two poles is a ⟪ar:جملۃُ المُعتَرِضَۃ⟫ (parenthetical clause) that enumerates the attributes of Allāh. After all these attributes have been declared, the verse finally discloses that the ⟪ar:’اللہ‘⟫ to whom they belong is none other than ⟪ar:علی عظیم⟫ in his own person. In other words, the attributes set out in this verse are the attributes of ⟪ar:’عین عظیمۃ‘⟫.[^cite-53]

> ⟪quran 2:255⟫
> ⟪ar:‏ٱللَّهُ لَآ إِلَهَ إِلَّا هُوَ ٱلْحَىُّ ٱلْقَيُّومُ ۚ لَا تَأْخُذُهُۥ سِنَةٌۭ وَلَا نَوْمٌۭ ۚ لَّهُۥ مَا فِى ٱلسَّمَوَتِ وَمَا فِى ٱلْأَرْضِ ۗ مَن ذَا ٱلَّذِى يَشْفَعُ عِندَهُۥٓ إِلَّا بِإِذْنِهِۦ ۚ يَعْلَمُ مَا بَيْنَ أَيْدِيهِمْ وَمَا خَلْفَهُمْ ۖ وَلَا يُحِيطُونَ بِشَىْءٍۢ مِّنْ عِلْمِهِۦٓ إِلَّا بِمَا شَآءَ ۚ وَسِعَ كُرْسِيُّهُ ٱلسَّمَوَتِ وَٱلْأَرْضَ ۖ وَلَا يَـُٔودُهُۥ حِفْظُهُمَا ۚ وَهُوَ ٱلْعَلِىُّ ٱلْعَظِيمُ ‎⟫
> *Allah! La ilaha illa Huwa (none has the right to be worshipped but He), the Ever Living, the One Who sustains and protects all that exists. Neither slumber, nor sleep overtake Him. To Him belongs whatever is in the heavens and whatever is on earth. Who is he that can intercede with Him except with His Permission? His Kursî extends over the heavens and the earth, and He feels no fatigue in guarding and preserving them. And He is the Most High, the Most Great. [This Verse 2:255 is called Ayat-ul-Kursî.]*
> there is no god but Allah, the Living, the Guardian of all things, and none can uphold His sleep, nor His is sleep, and to Him belongs all that is in the heavens and all that is in the earth. Even though His Throne occupies within Him all that is in the heavens and the earth, and Allah's protection does not avail them at all, and He is the Most Great, the Great.

### The Balance of Ayat al-Kursī

⟪ar:آیت الکرسی⟫ exhibits a perfect symmetry. It consists of nine parts; the number nine indicates the extremity of unity, its perfection, and its essence — just as Ramaḍān, the ninth month, is the recapitulation of the months that precede it.

| (1) Allāhu lā ilāha illā huwa al-Ḥayy al-Qayyūm | (2) Lā taʾkhudhuhu sinatun wa-lā nawm | (3) Lahu mā fī al-samāwāti wa-mā fī al-arḍ |
| --- | --- | --- |
| (4) Man dhā alladhī yashfaʿu ʿindahu illā bi-idhnih | **(5) Yaʿlamu mā bayna aydīhim wa-mā khalfahum** | (6) Wa-lā yuḥīṭūna bi-shayʾin min ʿilmihi illā bi-mā shāʾ |
| (7) Wasiʿa kursiyyuhu al-samāwāti wa-l-arḍ | (8) Wa-lā yaʾūduhu ḥifẓuhumā | (9) Wa-huwa al-ʿAlī al-ʿAẓīm |

When the reciter arrives at the middle of the verse and weighs what has just been read against what remains, he discovers that the two halves mirror one another. The pivot of the verse is ⟪ar:يَعْلَمُ مَا بَيْنَ أَيْدِيهِمْ وَمَا خَلْفَهُمْ⟫ — and this central clause is itself the commentary on the two flanks, declaring that what lies behind is being narrated forward. This is the figure of the *circle*, in which the end recapitulates the beginning. If we reflect the opening and closing parts upon the central pivot, the correspondences emerge in this order:

(1) There is no god but Allāh, the Living, the Sustainer of all — (9) And He is the Most High, the Most Great.
(2) Neither slumber nor sleep overtakes Him — (8) And the guarding of them does not weary Him.
(3) To Him belongs all that is in the heavens and the earth — (7) His Kursī extends over the heavens and the earth.
(4) Who can intercede with Him save by His leave? — (6) None encompasses any of His knowledge save as He wills.
**(5) Yaʿlamu mā bayna aydīhim wa-mā khalfahum** — He knows what is before them and what is behind them.

**Allāhu lā ilāha illā huwa al-Ḥayy al-Qayyūm.**

There is no god but Allāh, the Living, in whom all things subsist.

Allāh, in essence, is the bearer of the formula "lā ilāha illā huwa" — whose numerical value is 110, the same as the value of ʿAlī.[^cite-54]

| Lā ilāha illā huwa | ʿAlī |
| --- | --- |
| Oh my God | ʿAlī |
| 30 + 1 + 1 + 30 + 5 + 1 + 30 + 1 + 5 + 6 | 70 + 30 + 10 |
| 110 | 110 |

The essence of ʿAyn al-ʿAẓīma is eternal and everlasting: there has never been a moment in the past when the Essence did not exist, nor will there ever be a moment in the future when It shall not exist.

**Lā taʾkhudhuhu sinatun wa-lā nawm.**

He is not overtaken by drowsiness, nor by sleep.

The negligence proper to our spiritual world manifests itself in this world as sleep. For just this reason, sleep is dear to us, and rising from it to pray in the night is difficult — though its reward is correspondingly great. The implication is that the believer should break the bond of his own negligence, rise to answer the summons of the Imam of the age, set aside repose, and exert himself in striving.

Negligence comes first, in the spiritual world; sleep follows, in this world. Both are conditions that befall every human being — but Allāh transcends them. The essence of ʿAyn al-ʿAẓīma is touched neither by negligence nor by sleep: It was not heedless in any past moment, nor will It ever be heedless in any moment to come.

**Lahu mā fī al-samāwāti wa-mā fī al-arḍ.**

To Him belongs all that is in the heavens and the earth.

The heavens are the attributes of the Imams; the earth comprises the ḥujjas (proofs) of the Imams who summon to him. In other words, the gathering of all the Imams and their ḥujjas — both *mustaqarr* and *mustawdaʿ* — together with all their congregations, takes place in the Great Temple of ʿAyn al-Aʾimma, and he alone is the true Master of all these orders of being.

**Man dhā alladhī yashfaʿu ʿindahu illā bi-idhnih.**

Who can intercede with Him save by His leave?

Intercession here means seeking deliverance from the seventy maladies of the spiritual world. In every epoch, the wilāya of ʿAyn has been the locus of success and intercession. To escape the prison of this world of disorder without his guardianship and love is impossible.

**Yaʿlamu mā bayna aydīhim wa-mā khalfahum.**

He knows what is before them and what is behind them.

And none has any share in His knowledge save by His permission. He is aware of the affairs of each Imam and of every believer within his circle, including those of past epochs. The Imams have arrived at their station by completing him, and he is aware of their further developments in the cycles to come. All the Imams draw knowledge from the bounty of the Great One.

**Wasiʿa kursiyyuhu al-samāwāti wa-l-arḍ wa-lā yaʾūduhu ḥifẓuhumā.**

His Kursī extends over the heavens and the earth, and the guarding of them does not weary Him.

In this clause the pronominal suffix on "kursī" — "his Kursī" — refers to the Throne extended over heaven and earth. The Kursī signifies the essence of the Great One that presides over all the Imams and their ḥujjas. The one who sits upon the Kursī is Qāʾim ʿAlā Dhikrihi al-Salām. When we sit upon a chair, we lay the full burden of our weight upon it. So too the burden of the entire pattern of an age is placed upon the Great One by Qāʾim ʿAlā Dhikrihi al-Salām. Yet just as the chair never tires of bearing us, the Great Ones never grow weary beneath the responsibility of an entire cycle, nor are these labours difficult for them.

Know, then, that ʿAlī al-ʿAẓīm, the bearer of all these lofty and tremendous attributes, is the Great One himself.

*The realities.*

It has been transmitted that Ayat al-Kursī is equivalent to two sūras. The supreme sūra of the Qurʾān is al-Fātiḥa; the other sūras follow as its caliphate. Surat al-Fātiḥa is followed in order by Surat al-Ikhlāṣ, and after that comes Ayat al-Kursī, which is equivalent to two sūras. ## Al-Islām: Taslīm al-Ṣuwar (Islam as the Surrender of Forms)

In ⟪ar:سورۃ البقرۃ⟫ the Qurʾān preserves the testament of ⟪ar:حضرت ابراہیم علیہ السلام⟫ to his sons:

> ⟪quran 2:132⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَوَصَّىٰ بِهَآ إِبْرَهِیمُ بَنِيهِ وَيَعْقُوبُ يَبَنِىَّ إِنَّ ٱللَّهَ ٱصْطَفَىٰ لَكُمُ ٱلدِّينَ فَلَا تَمُوتُنَّ إِلَّا وَأَنتُم مُّسْلِمُونَ ‎⟫
> *And this (submission to Allah, Islam) was enjoined by Ibrahim (Abraham) upon his sons and by Ya'qub (Jacob), (saying), "O my sons! Allah has chosen for you the (true) religion, then die not except in the Faith of Islam (as Muslims - Islamic Monotheism)." *
> And so did Abraham and Jacob enjoin upon their sons, saying, O my sons, Allah has chosen for you the religion, so do not die except while you are Muslims.
> — **Quran 2:132 (inline widget)**

The instruction seems strange at first reading. Why would ⟪ar:ابراھیم علیہ السلام⟫ exhort sons of his own spiritual stature — children in whom ⟪ar:نبوت (اسحاق)⟫ and ⟪ar:امامت (اسماعیل)⟫ already resided — to "die in Islam"? The riddle resolves once we recognise that Islam operates in two registers. The first is an Islam beneath faith (īmān), entered through the profession of the Word, ⟪ar:لا الہ الا اللہ⟫. The second is an Islam higher than faith: ⟪ar:تسلیم الصور⟫, the surrender of forms. This second Islam is a deed, not merely a confession. Its substance is the handing over of those gathered about oneself to those of higher rank. Addressing ⟪ar:اسحاق و اسماعیل⟫ in this very verse, Ibrāhīm was commanding his sons not to depart this life until they had transferred the assembly entrusted to them into the keeping of the ⟪ar:نبوت و رسالت اور امامت و وصایت⟫ that stood above them. In just the same manner, the Messenger of Allāh (ṣ) summoned all peoples to enter the ⟪ar:اسلامی صورات⟫ — the constituted ⟪ar:ھیکل⟫ — of the Muslim community.[^cite-56]

We dwell within the wider sphere of Islam. Within that larger circle lies a smaller circle, the circle of īmān; and at its very centre stands ⟪ar:’ الاسلام‘⟫, the inmost submission which ⟪ar:ابراھیم خلیل اللہ⟫ breathed into his children. To entrust one's affairs to their rightful owner is the true Islam.

This higher submission — ⟪ar:الأسلام⟫ — stands above faith in rank. The Prophets, as trustees ⟪ar:(مستودعین)⟫, possess an Islam whose very substance is the consignment of their charges to the Imam. ⟪ar:الأسلام⟫ in this sense is entered only through prayer. Islam at this height develops within faith. Islam itself admits no degrees, but faith admits many. The analogy is with the constitution of a polity: in its ⟪ar:کمال اول⟫ (kamāl awwal, primary perfection) no ranks or offices are yet articulated; only with ⟪ar:کمال ثانی⟫ (kamāl thānī, secondary perfection) do the ordered ranks emerge. Between the two Islams lies faith. The first Islam — its sole condition the testimony of the Word — encompasses every nominal adherent and conformist alike. The second imposes a further condition: worship and the recognition of divine authority. This is why ⟪ar:حضرت ابراھیم علیہ السلام⟫ commanded his sons not to die until they had delivered the accounts of their charges to the bearers of ⟪ar:نبوت، رسالت، امامت، وصایت⟫ above them.[^cite-57]

### Why do we cleanse ourselves?

We cleanse the self because it must be presented to our highest self, that it may be received. The same principle runs through all the births of nature. The Islam of the mineral is to surrender itself to the roots of the plant. The Islam of the plant is to surrender itself to the grazing animal. It is the ⟪ar:الأسلام⟫ of the lawful animal to yield its body to the human being, to bend its neck before his knife, so that man may develop it as part of his own substance. So too the ⟪ar:الاسلام⟫ of man is to surrender his ⟪ar:صورت شریف⟫ ⟪ar:(تسلیم الصور)⟫ to his exalted limit, for the ḥadd ʿālī (the upper limit) is the lord of his benefit. This is why the Qurʾān bids us long for death: it is the warrant of our release and the consummation of this aspect of our ⟪ar:الأسلام⟫. Through death we present and complete our form. Death, in truth, is the believer's ⟪ar:الأسلام (تسلیم الصور)⟫. The Qurʾān declares:

> ⟪quran 2:94⟫
> ⟪ar:‏قُلْ إِن كَانَتْ لَكُمُ ٱلدَّارُ ٱلْآخِرَةُ عِندَ ٱللَّهِ خَالِصَةًۭ مِّن دُونِ ٱلنَّاسِ فَتَمَنَّوُا۟ ٱلْمَوْتَ إِن كُنتُمْ صَدِقِينَ ‎⟫
> *Say to (them): "If the home of the Hereafter with Allah is indeed for you specially and not for others, of mankind, then long for death if you are truthful." *
> Say, If the abode of the Hereafter with Allah is for you alone, to the exclusion of all mankind, then long for death, if you are truthful.
> — **Quran 2:94 (inline widget)**

The Hereafter is the highest station at which our form arrives and finds its rest. The verse urges us to submit to that summit through which the abode of the Hereafter, prepared for us alone, may be reached. Our exalted limit is exalted for us — not for any other believer.

(From the *Lessons of Reading al-Anwār* — Mullā Saʿdullāh.)

## Various Interpretations of Salām

### The greeting of believers to one another

The very word "Islam" derives from *salām*, peace. The Prophet (ṣ) said: ⟪ar-quote:السلام قبل الکلام⟫ — "Greeting precedes speech." When a believer extends *salām* to another, he acknowledges that both stand together upon the platform of Islam: in his presence the other will not be harmed, and in his absence he will not be slandered.

### The rider greeting the pedestrian

The Prophet (ṣ) also said that the rider must greet the pedestrian. In taʾwīl (esoteric exegesis), the rider stands for the dāʿī (Summoner) and the pedestrian for the ordinary believer. For this reason, within the daʿwa (the summons), the dāʿī is figuratively styled a donkey — ⟪ar:حمار⟫ — because the donkey bears the full burden of its owner without complaint. So too the Summoner takes upon himself the burden of training all believers for the sake of the Imam, gathering them into his congregation whatever their state or deeds may be.

### The greeting of the Prophets

The handing over of those held in trust — ⟪ar:مستودعین⟫ — together with the ⟪ar:صورات⟫ that they bear, from one rank to the next, is itself a salām. So too the conferral by the Prophets of ⟪ar:نبوت ورسالت⟫ ⟪ar:(امامت و وصایت)⟫ upon their masters is a salām: a greeting that is at once a transmission.

### The transcription of the hierarchy

In its deepest sense salām means transmission — the distribution of the bearers of form along the chain of ḥudūd. Of this Allāh speaks in ⟪ar:سورۃ القدر⟫:

> ⟪quran 97:5⟫
> ⟪ar:‏سَلَمٌ هِىَ حَتَّىٰ مَطْلَعِ ٱلْفَجْرِ ‎⟫
> *Peace! (All that night, there is Peace and Goodness from Allah to His believing slaves) until the appearance of dawn.*
> It is the night of peace until the rising of the morning.
> — **Quran 97:5 (inline widget)**

⟪ar:لیلۃ القدر⟫ is the night of these conveyances. Wherever spiritual centres are established and wherever the powers reside, on this night a series of transmissions is issued.[^cite-58]
