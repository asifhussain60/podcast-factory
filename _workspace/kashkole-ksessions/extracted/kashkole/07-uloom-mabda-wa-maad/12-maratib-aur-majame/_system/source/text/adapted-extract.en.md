## Spiritual Ranks

The structure of the Ismaili spiritual hierarchy comprises ten principal ranks, arranged in successive tiers of sacred authority and cosmic correspondence. These ranks form the backbone of the Ismaili understanding of both the terrestrial community and the celestial order—a unified vision in which the community of believers mirrors the emanation of divine intellect itself.

The first four ranks—the Mustajib (the Answerer), the Mu'min (the Believer), the Mukasir (the Breaker), and the Ma'dhun (the Licenced One)—constitute the realm of those bound by the outward law and the external *daʿwa* (summons). Above them stands the Da'i Mutlaq (the Unrestricted Summoner), who bridges the common believers and the hierarchy of initiated teachers. The next tier comprises the Da'i Balagh (the Summoner of Proclamation), the Hujjat (the Proof), and the Bab (the Gate)—figures who occupy progressively higher stations of esoteric knowledge and cosmic function. At the summit stand the Imamate and the Wilayat (Guardianship), the red-highlighted seats denoting the ultimate poles of spiritual authority in their respective epochs.

Each rank receives its nourishment not through earthly rank alone, but through a corresponding spiritual and cosmological aspect. This correspondence—rendered in the abstract noun forms (-*iyyat*, -hood or -ness)—reveals that the hierarchy is not merely administrative but fundamentally rooted in the unfolding of divine intellect.

To each earthly rank corresponds a celestial reality. The Imam's spiritual station is the *Imamat Ruḥāniyyah* (Spiritual Imamate), while the Natiq (the Speaker, i.e., the Prophet-Legislator) embodies *al-Nutq al-Ilāhī* (Divine Speech itself). The Proof manifests *al-Ḥujjiyyah* (Proofhood), the Veil displays *al-Ḥijābiyyah* (Veiling), and those who receive through angelic visitation embody *al-Khayāliyyah* (the Angelic Form). The Summoner of Proclamation carries *al-Fatḥiyyah* (Opening), while the Grandfather-figure holds *al-Jaddiyyah* (Grandfatherhood in the spiritual sense).[^cite-1] Descent and resurrection, *al-Inzaʿiyyah* and *al-Qaʾīmiyyah*, are followed by the ultimate principle, *al-Jathhiyyah* (the Bodyhood or Substantiality of all forms)—the realm in which all scattered particulars are gathered into their true temple.

## The Primordial Body and the Virtuous Person

With the creation of the heavens and the earth, and alongside the emergence of the first human, there arose also a leader and Imam. In the sacred site of the Baytullāh (the House of God), there were born twenty-eight pits, from each of which issued forth one of the select: twenty-eight souls of surpassing virtue. These were the epitome of the human race, the crystalline essence of both the celestial and terrestrial worlds, and the embodiment of the twenty-eight letters of the alphabet (⟪ar:حروف معجم⟫). They corresponded to the twenty-eight stages of the lunar cycle, arranged in the same hierarchical order as an Imam stands at the head of his community.

The foremost among them was the Shakhṣ al-Fāḍil (the Virtuous Person), possessing a perfection so refined that the scholars have explained the disparity thus: "If the remaining twenty-seven dwelt in the palace of precious stones, the rest of creation would inhabit the palace of common rock; yet were the Virtuous Person to dwell in the palace of precious stones, those twenty-seven would occupy no higher rank than the palace of common stone."[^cite-2] This One was the *Ḥijāb Ādam al-Abdāʾiyyah* (the Veil of Primordial Adam), standing in relation to all others as the intellect stands to the faculties, as the First Intellect (⟪ar:اول عقل⟫) stands to all cosmic intellects below it.

The first of these Twenty-Eight to manifest the Shakhṣ al-Fāḍil was Qaymurath—he who initiated the first peacock.

### The Unity of the Virtuous Person

This learned and luminous being first turned his regard inward, upon himself alone, without intermediary; then he beheld his own kind; then he perceived the heavens above him; then the flowing waters and the cycle of day and night. Through this ordered observation, he attained certainty: there must exist an *Ibdāʿ* (Originator, Most High)—a being unlike any creation, whom he alone could neither equal nor comprehend. He negated from himself all that belongs to creation, and bore witness to his Creator's *Ilāhiyyah* (Divinity) and *Waḥdāniyyah* (Unity, ⟪ar:وحدانیت⟫), offering *Tasbīḥ wa-Taqdīs* (glorification and sanctification). Thus united with the *Mawād al-Ilāhiyyah* (Divine Substance), he became a conduit for the *Anwār al-Shaʿshāʿāniyyah* (Radiant Lights) and *al-Taʾyīdāt al-Malakūtiyyah* (Celestial Confirmations). Through this union, he attained *Kamāl Thānī* (secondary perfection) and came to possess *ʿIlm Mā Kān wa-Yakūn* (Knowledge of All That Is and Shall Be).

He then achieved the station of *Ruttbat al-Waḥdah* (the Rank of Unity) and was named *al-Abdāʾ* (the Originator)—achieving in the physical world the status of Beginning itself, and in the realm of initiation the rank of Asher (the Tenth Intellect, the crowning perfection of the intellective order).

Thereupon, he called those twenty-seven to God through *Tawḥīd, Tanzīh, wa-Tajrīd* (Affirmation of Unity, Negation of Likeness, and Abstraction of Essence), ⟪ar:توحید، تنزیہ اور تجرید⟫. With alacrity they accepted his invitation and took in hand the *Mīthāq* (Covenant). He instructed them in the distinction between the *Ḥalāl* (Lawful) and *Ḥarām* (Forbidden), ordained for them the rules of worship, and commanded them to teach both the legal codes and the Sunnah of matrimony to those who would receive them.

From these twenty-eight, he selected sixteen to be witnesses (*Hujjāj*) in his spiritual regime. One was appointed his Gate (*Bāb*), one his Proof (*Hujjat*), one his Summoner of Proclamation (*Dāʿī al-Balāgh*), and one his Unrestricted Summoner (*Dāʿī al-Muṭlaq*). The remaining twelve he designated as *Ḥujjāj Laylīyyah* (Night-Watchers, ⟪ar:حجج لیلی⟫)—the silent keepers of his presence—and stationed them as his intimate circle, those who never diverge from him. The final eleven he scattered as *Ḥujjāj Nahāriyyah* (Day-Watchers, ⟪ar:حجج نہاری⟫) across the islands, commanding each to go forth and establish his *daʿwa* in their appointed lands.

### Hierarchy of the Twenty-Eight

The *Jaththa al-Abdāʾiyyah* (the Primordial Body, ⟪ar:جثہ ابدائیہ⟫) stands second only in rank to the First Intellect itself. After the Primordial Body, the *Ḥujjāj Laylīyyah* (Night-Watchers) constitute the next degree of excellence, for they are bound to the inward dimension alone, while the *Ḥujjāj Nahāriyyah* (Day-Watchers) sustain both inward and outward limits.[^cite-3] These twenty-eight are the *Aṣḥāb Ulū al-ʿIlm* (Possessors of Knowledge, ⟪ar:صاحبان اولو العلم⟫), to whom the Qur'ān bears witness:

> ⟪quran 3:18⟫
> ⟪ar:‏شَهِدَ ٱللَّهُ أَنَّهۥ لَآ إِلَهَ إِلَّا هُوَ وَٱلْمَلَـٰٓئِكَةُ وَأُو۟لُوا۟ ٱلْعِلْمِ قَـٰٓئِمًۢا بِٱلْقِسْطِ ۚ لَآ إِلَهَ إِلَّا هُوَ ٱلْعَزِيزُ ٱلْحَكِيمُ ‎⟫
> *Allah bears witness that there is no god save Him, and so do the angels and those possessed of knowledge standing firm in justice; there is no god save Him, the All-Mighty, the All-Wise.*

The *Aṣḥāb Ulū al-ʿIlm* are these twenty-eight, born of the *Jaththa al-Abdāʾiyyah* without corporeal genesis, whom God has made repositories of knowledge and whom He preserves in righteousness through that knowledge.

### The Two Communities

In the age of *Kashf* (Unveiling), the islands and the sacred presences (⟪ar:حضرات⟫) are not geographically distinct, just as in the age of *Satr* (Concealment) they become so. In terms of purity, subtlety, and knowledge during the *Kashf* period, the islands correspond exactly to the sacred presences. One of the twelve is the island where the Imam himself dwells (⟪ar:حضرات⟫); the remaining eleven, where the Imam is absent, are called islands (⟪ar:جزائر⟫). The distinction between presence and absence signifies that the Virtuous Person (*Shakhṣ Fāḍil*, ⟪ar:شخص فاضل⟫) retained the *Ḥujjāj Laylīyyah* (⟪ar:حجج لیلی⟫) in his immediate presence while dispatching the *Ḥujjāj Nahāriyyah* (⟪ar:حجج نہاری⟫) to distant regions of the world.

### The Divine Name in the Primordial Body

Every Imam, every *Qaʾim*, and every *Jaththa* (primordial body) encompasses twenty-eight souls, arrayed according to the letters of the divine invocation *Lā Ilāha Illā-Allāh* (⟪ar:کلمہ لا الہ الا اللہ⟫). The correspondence is as follows:

| Count | Composition |
| --- | --- |
| 2 | The Virtuous Person and his Gate |
| 3 | Those dwelling in the Presences and those dwelling on the Islands |
| 4 | Virtuous Person, Gate, Night-Watchers, Day-Watchers |
| 7 | Virtuous Person, Gate, Proof, Summoner of Proclamation, Unrestricted Summoner, Night-Watchers, Day-Watchers |
| 12 | The Twelve Proofs of the Imam |
| 28 | *Lā Ilāha Illā-Allāh* |

The twenty-eighth *Jaththa* of the second peacock was born in Lanka (⟪ar:جثہ ابدائیہ سراندیپ⟫), a sign of the global expansion of the spiritual hierarchy across epochs and lands.

## The Total Caliphate of the Virtuous Person

When the Virtuous Person of the *Jaththa al-Abdāʾiyyah* initiates his summons, his first intention is to prepare a successor—a true caliph who shall be his spiritual son. Just as the learned Virtuous Person occupies the station of the First Intellect in the physical world, his son shall occupy that same rank in the next cycle. The principle is absolute: every head of a spiritual circle stands in the place of *Ibdāʿ* (Origination), while his successor stands in the place of the Caliph-Prophet—the Imam in the place of the Originator, the Gate in the place of the Prophet-Legislator.

| Originator | Emanator |
| --- | --- |
| First Intellect | First Emanation |
| Virtuous Person of the Primordial Body | His Spiritual Son (Acting Caliph) |
| Imam | Gate |

The Virtuous Person ordains the Imamate upon his spiritual son and departs to the House of the Almighty, attaining the rank of Intellect and becoming the master of that realm—passing, that is, to *Ashirī* status (the Tenth Intellect). He shall hold this station until he produces a caliph equal to himself. The twenty-eight who have accrued to him include his Gate, which grew together with him from the beginning.

### The Partial and the Total

Although the Virtuous Person appoints his descendant as caliph in his stead, that caliph is not his equal. He is established only in part of the Virtuous Person's station. The caliph of the part is partial; the caliph of the whole is total. He shall govern until there is born a *Jaththa* like his own—but such a complete caliph can appear only at the end of the final peacock. Though many cycles and peacocks shall intervene, and a *Jaththa* shall arise with each cycle, all remain but partial caliphs of the first. The true total caliph of the Virtuous Person (*al-Abdāʾ*) can only be the *Qaʾim Kullī* (the Complete Resurrector, ⟪ar:قائم کلی⟫).

| The Virtuous Person (First Peacock) | The Complete Resurrector (Last Peacock) |
| --- | --- |
| *al-Abdāʾ* | *al-Qaʾim al-Kāmil* |

Although the First in rank takes precedence chronologically, that Caliphate attains its perfection only in the Last. The caliph is called the *Ibn al-Tamm* (the Perfect Son)—perfect in realization, if not in precedence.

### The Ascension Through the Intellects

When the first mortal sage (*al-Ashir*) prepares his caliph, he and all in his spiritual following ascend to the Ninth Intellect. From there, the Ninth Intellect and those in his circle ascend to the Eighth, which ascends to the Seventh; the Seventh to the Sixth; the Sixth to the Fifth; the Fifth to the Fourth; the Fourth to the Third; and the Third and all in its realm pass into the station of the Second Intellect, there to find eternal rest and praise. Paradise—*al-Mawā* (the Abode)—is the dwelling place of the sacred dead. Beyond this there is no higher station save the realm of the First Intellect, which is the station of Unity itself, and that is *Sidrat al-Muntahā* (the Lote-Tree of the Boundary).[^cite-4] All ten levels thus progress through the succession of cycles and epochs.

For this Paradise, the Prophet, upon him be peace, said: "Paradise is a place such that no eye has ever seen it, no ear has ever heard it, nor has it occurred to the heart of any man."[^cite-5] So transcendent is this realm that to define it would diminish its majesty; it can only be approached through humble description.

### The Perfection of the First Through the Last

The full development of the First (*al-Ashir*) shall come only when he brings forth his Total Caliph, the *Qaʾim al-Kāmil* (the Complete Resurrector) of the last peacock. All the stations between the First and the Last stand as partial in relation to both, while the First and Last are each total in their respective epochs.[^cite-6] The First Peacock—the Virtuous Person—carries within him ten stations, all of which shall be unveiled until the end of the world. Each shall manifest one after another in this realm, ascend to the First and take seat with him. When all these stations have ascended, the *Qaʾim al-Kāmil* shall appear, he who will raise the entire world into the spiritual realm. Thus the Qur'ān declares:

> ⟪ar:إِنَّ ٱلَّذِينَ كَذَّبُوا۟ بِـَٔايَـٰتِنَا وَٱسْتَكْبَرُوا۟ عَنْهَا لَا تُفَتَّحُ لَهُمْ أَبْوَٰبُ ٱلسَّمَآءِ وَلَا يَدْخُلُونَ ٱلْجَنَّةَ حَتَّىٰ يَلِجَ ٱلْجَمَلُ فِى سَمِّ ٱلْخِيَاطِ ۚ وَكَذَٰلِكَ نَجْزِى ٱلْمُجْرِمِينَ⟫
> *Verily those who deny Our signs and turn away from them in arrogance—the gates of heaven shall not be opened for them, nor shall they enter the Garden until the camel passes through the needle's eye. Thus do We requite the guilty.*
> — **Quran 7:40**

## The Arrangement of the Primordial Bodies

Imām Jaʿfar al-Ṣādiq told Umm al-Nadā': "A great peacock has already passed. Since then, cycles numbering ninety-nine have continued. Of the first run of the four-hundred cycles, we have dissolved seven sevens. We are on the verge of completing the seventh seventy. When the Resurrection dawns, it shall gather the entire span of four hundred thousand years."

Thus the three hundred ninety-ninth cycle has passed, and we dwell in the four-hundredth. The *Jaththa* (primordial body) that shall appear afterward—the four-hundred-first—will be the last to possess a comprehensive array. Its magnitude shall be such that it appears even at the *Kaʿbah*; before this, only the first body of the first peacock manifested at the House of God.

Within these four hundred cycles, there emerge forty distinguished bodies, one appearing at every ten cycles—at the 40th, 230th, 310th, and 400th markers. The grandeur of these ten exceeds all others, yet none surpasses the first body of the second peacock, and even that is exceeded only by the very first body of the first peacock, whose equal shall be found only in his caliph at the end of the final peacock.[^cite-7]

All such distinguished bodies are sacred sites. At the conclusion of the second peacock, the Sultan who comes shall gather the second peacock and seat himself at the horizon of the first peacock. Upon reaching that threshold, he receives a second seat. When the third peacock commences, the Virtuous Person of the First (*al-Ashir*) shall bequeath the final two bodies to the third peacock, becoming itself the caliph of the second part. This pattern continues until the last peacock is revealed—that shall be the total caliph of the first.

The second peacock is thus preparing to bring forth a third peacock in its own likeness—a rhythm of origination that pulses through all ages until the Finale.

## The Tenth Horizon

When the spiritual son of the Virtuous Person (*Shakhṣ al-Fāḍil*) was confirmed in his office by explicit designation, he summoned all to God according to his foreordained plan, calling the people to the Unity of God in order to liberate and rescue those souls trapped in the river of *Hūlī* (the abyss of heedlessness) and held in the captivity of ignorance. His second strategy was to separate from his own summons a successor-caliph, as his father had done for him. When he had prepared his son and completed his service, he took his entire congregation to the *Afq* (Horizon) of the Tenth—that is, to *Ashir*, who is himself his father. There he passed away and returned to his father's *Barzakh* (the Partition between worlds), finding a seat prepared for him.[^cite-8] He shall not discover there the station of the Complete Caliph, for that place is reserved only for the founder of the last peacock. In the same manner, every successor who dies in your place shall find a seat established in the *Ashir Horizon*. The *Afq al-ʿĀshir* (the Tenth Horizon) is the *Barzakh*—the spiritual partition where all these departed souls shall rest with their temples until the Day of Resurrection, as the Qur'ān affirms:

> ⟪ar:لَعَلِّى أَعْمَلُ صَـٰلِحًۭا فِيمَا تَرَكْتُ ۚ كَلَّآ ۚ إِنَّهَا كَلِمَةٌ هُوَ قَآئِلُهَا ۖ وَمِن وَرَآئِهِم بَرْزَخٌ إِلَىٰ يَوْمِ يُبْعَثُونَ⟫
> *Perchance I may do good in that which I have left behind. By no means! It is but a word he speaks. And before them is a barrier until the Day of Resurrection.*
> — **Quran 23:100**

### The Horizon of Asher: Seats and Succession

The *Ashir Horizon* is the *Barzakh* (Partition) for all Imāms and Imāma. Each *Qaʾim* transmits the names of the Imāms who preceded him to the *Qaʾim* who comes after. Every *Sāhibu al-Asbūʿ* (the Master of the Week, or Master of the Seven) receives the form of the six Imāms before him through the *Barzakh* of the Tenth Horizon. In the same way, the *Qaʾim* of every thousand years (*Sāhibu al-Alf*) draws his thousand years from that same *Barzakh*. Every week holds six seats. When these six seats of a week are summed, and when the seventh week arrives, the seventh of the previous week becomes also the seventh of the following week.[^cite-9]

One person becomes a resident (*Muqīm*) of the week; the next becomes its resident—for the primary purpose is to prepare the next sovereign. Though six Imāms may stand between two such sovereigns, these six are the partial caliphs of the *Sāhibu al-Asbūʿ*; only the successor is the complete caliph of the first. This principle applies throughout all ranks.

> *The caliph of the partial is partial; the caliph of the whole is whole.*

### The Pattern of Imamage and Stations

When Maulānā Abū Ṭālib occupied the station of the Imamate, he appointed ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib as the Imam. When ʿAlī attained the rank of the Complete (*al-Kāmil*), he established a relationship with ʿAlī separate from that of the *Wilāyah* (Guardianship).

When the Imam decides to pass away, his temple ascends to the *Ashir Horizon*. There are many grades of connection between the Tenth and the Imam:

| Imam | Station |
| --- | --- |
| Master of the Week | First of the seven |
| Master of the Forty-Nine | Master of the age |
| Master of the Era | Master of the thousand years |
| Master of the Grand Cycle | Master of four hundred thousand years |
| Master of the Peacock | Caliph of the Tenth Horizon |
| The Tenth | Ultimate Sanctuary |

Each upper station is the horizon for the one below it. The Imam's horizon is the *Sāhibu al-Asbūʿ*; at the Imam's death, his temple—which he possessed and received from his father—ascends to the *Ashir Horizon*.

## Grandfather, Opening, and Imagination: The Three Ambassadors

God addresses a human being in three ways and conveys His commands:

| Divine Transmission | Mode | Intermediary |
| --- | --- | --- |
| By Revelation | Direct, without veil | The Grandfather (*al-Jadd*) |
| Behind the Veil | Hidden in words | The Opening (*al-Fatḥ*) |
| By Messenger | In perceived form | The Imagination (*al-Khayāl*) |

### The Grandfather: Revelation

The first mode is revelation, and this is the station of the *Jadd* (the Grandfather). This rank exists and holds sway at all times and in all places, standing highest among the three. Revelation is the word of God which issues from the Holy Spirit (*Rūḥ al-Quds*) and descends directly upon the individual commanded to receive it without any intervening medium. It is the duty of the Almighty to convey the word of the Imām to all ranks below him.

### The Opening: Communication Behind the Veil

The second mode is speech from behind the veil, and this is the station of the *Fatḥ* (the Opening or Disclosure). Here, the divine commandments remain concealed in the garb of words. Just as the teachings in the Qur'ān hide their inner meanings beneath the veil of the outward letter—for instance, when "knowledge" is explained through the metaphor of "water"—so the task of the *Fatḥ* is to clothe God's message in outward words while revealing its inner sense to the listener, who understands what is truly meant.[^cite-12]

### The Imagination: The Messenger Angel

The third mode pertains to the *Khayāl* (the Imagination or Angelic Form)—the station of an angel (*Malik al-Mursal*) sent as a messenger in a form visible to the hearer or through a voice perceptible to him.

### The Three as Intermediaries

The high representatives of each of these three levels—the *Jadd*, the *Fatḥ*, and the *Khayāl* of any given station—are called the ambassadors between that rank and God. These three beings dwell in the world of the *Muḥtajab* (the Veiled, or the Hidden). Through these three verses (*Āyāt*), the Imām of God is linked to the Imām of the time. At each level of the hierarchy, these three modalities function:

| Intermediary | Message Mode | Corresponding Angel | Rank |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Imagination | Messenger | Gabriel | Current Rank + 1 |
| Opening | Behind the Veil | Michael | Current Rank + 2 |
| Grandfather | Revelation | Israfil | Current Rank + 3 |

When the Messenger of God (*s.a.w.a.*) was in the station of Prophet-Legislator, his *Jadd*, *Fatḥ*, and *Khayāl* were distinct persons transmitting to him the matters of prophethood. When he assumed the rank of Messenger, his three ambassadors became distinct persons revealing to him the realities of the Message. When he attained the station of Guardian (*Nāṭiq*, the Speaker), his three ambassadors were transformed. When he became the Imām, his *Jadd*, *Fatḥ*, and *Khayāl* were once again distinct personalities revealing to him the matters of spiritual imamate. These three stations function up to the level of Prophethood; they do not extend to the *Auliyāʾ* (the Friends of God).

## Muhammad and ʿAlī: The Grandfather, Opening, and Imagination

When the Prophet (*s.a.w.a.*) rose through the ranks and attained the station of the *Ḥijāb* (the Veil), and when his resident *Maulānā Abū Ṭālib* passed away, the Prophet became the *Ḥijāb* (Veil) of the *ʿAql al-ʿĀshir* (the Tenth Intellect, the Supreme One). He became the vessel through which the rays of the Tenth descended by means of his *Jadd*, *Fatḥ*, and *Khayāl*. These are spiritual limits—*Ḥudūd Rūḥāniyyah*—and in contrast to them stand the physical limits (*Ḥudūd Jismāniyyah*): ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib, Abū Ṭālib, and ʿAbdullāh.

In the world of the *Muḥtajab* (the Veiled), *Mīm* (the letter M, representing Muhammad) became the veil of the Tenth (*ʿAyn*), and the Tenth's support came to him through his *Jadd*, *Fatḥ*, and *Khayāl*. In the world of the veil, Muhammad—who is himself the veil of *Mīm*—received support from three ambassadors: ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib, Abū Ṭālib, and ʿAbdullāh. The Messenger gradually advanced ʿAlī through the prophetic ranks, and on the Day of Ghadir sealed him as the Imām and brought him to the miracle of God, until Amīr al-Muʾminīn (*a.s.*) himself became a place for the descent of the Tenth's rays and became the veil of the Tenth. Through Muhammad, the Tenth began to shine; and through him came support and aid to ʿAlī. At that time, ʿAlī too acquired both *ʿIlm al-Makān* and *ʿIlm al-Yakūn*.

## The Qa'im of the Cycle of Revelation

The fundamental structure of the cosmos, according to Ismaili cosmology, unfolds in cycles presided over by appointed lieutenants of the transcendent realm. When the seven-thousand-year period of *kashf* (revelation) concludes, a gathering of 124,000 spiritual forms assembles on the horizon of *ʿāshir* (the Tenth Intellect). The inheritor of this station—the caliph appointed by the preceding council—receives from the realm this collected multitude and establishes a temple constituted from the very imamic forms of those seven millennia. Every seven thousand years thereafter, a new *Qa'im* (the Standing One, or Establisher) emerges, collecting and amplifying the prior cycle's treasury. When the *Qa'im* of the seventh millennium finally appears—after a span of 49,000 years—the magnificence of that moment becomes immeasurable.[^cite-1] This supreme figure does not merely gather the forms of the immediately preceding seven-thousand-year period; rather, he draws into unity the accumulated substance of six complete cycles (each of 7,000 years) from *Barzakh Āshir* (the Tenth Interregnum). The six prior periods repose within the palace of the corporeal substance, while the seventh marks the ultimate boundary of constituted creation.

[See image 014.png — *Qa'im of the Cycle of Revelation*: Six numbered thrones (1–6), each representing 7,000 years (totaling 42,000), with arrows converging upon a seventh throne (7,000 years) below. Mathematical formula: 50,000 Years = 7000 × 7 = 49,000 + 1,000.]

### The Commencement of the Cycle of Concealment

When fifty thousand years of *kashf* (revelation) have been fulfilled, the seventy-year epoch commences. As the revelation-cycle reaches its perfection and knowledge attains its pinnacle, decline inevitably shadows the world. The power of understanding grows yet also becomes infirm; defects arise in the collective soul. The Imams and the *Ilyas* (the highest luminaries) withdraw behind the veil of *taqiyya* (prudential dissimulation). In place of the direct knowledge of realities (*ḥaqāʾiq*), the understanding of conventional truth (*ṣifāt*) spreads through the people.[^cite-2] The Imams consolidate their spiritual dominion, and their mastery over the world intensifies.

### The Manifestation of Forms in the Final Millennium of Revelation

In the final three thousand years of the revelation-cycle, two distinct categories of spiritual forms appear. One category pertains to revelation; the other to the hidden dimension. These forms shall constitute the spiritual archetypes upon which the seventy-year era unfolds and which shall manifest therein. In this age of revelation, there is no room for the *Mustawadaʿūn* (the temporary keepers of knowledge); the station belongs alone to the *Mustaqirūn* (the permanent possessors of wisdom).

### Adam and the Establishment of Shariah

During the seventy-year period now beginning, Adam (peace be upon him) will be the first to manifest. The Imam of his epoch shall establish him in his station. Adam, by command of his Imam, will summon the people to the affirmation of divine unity (*tawḥīd*) and shall establish the divinely ordained law (*Sharīʿah*). This period will commence in a state of weakness—figuratively, the "old age" of the creation—and shall progressively ascend toward strength, the period of revelation proper. Adam dwells within the palace of *Salalah* (the stage of ensoulment in fetal development). This state persists until Adam appoints his successor—Abel and then Seth (*Shīth*)—establishing an apostolic succession. And so throughout this seventy-thousand-year span, there unfold seven prophetic stations, each corresponding to the seven stages of human gestation within the womb.[^cite-3] Each messenger-prophet fashioned his own revealed law and embedded within it symbols and mystical allusions by means of which the believers could attain to both exoteric knowledge and esoteric wisdom.

The Imams of the seventy-year era outnumber those of the revelation-cycle. Moreover, the Imams succeeding the Prophet and Ali far exceed those who came before them in their multitude and station. In the ancient cycles, the four degrees of prophethood (*nubuwwah*), prophecy (*risālah*), imamate (*imāmah*), and testamentary inheritance (*waṣāyah*) were distributed among different bearers: the prophets held the first two degrees, while the imams held the latter two. When seven thousand years have elapsed, those who emerge into the seventy-year era gather into themselves the forms of those seven thousand years of the revelation-epoch. We ourselves stand in the seventh seventy of the first epoch; six great cycles have accordingly been completed and are known as *al-Sattah Ṣuwartī* (the Six Forms).

### The Reckoning Belongs Only to the Seventy-Year Era

There shall be no reckoning for those of the distant revelation-cycle. Though Allah forgives the sins of His servants through divine grace, only the Imams themselves possess the authority to forgive the transgressions of their followers. For this reason, the jurists are expelled from the inner circle of authority.

#### *Faqāq*: The Meaning of Liberation

*Faqāq* signifies liberation and the severing of chains. The burden of retaliation rests upon the self-consciousness of the one who erred—upon their guilt and their awareness of wrongdoing. Yet rather than demanding that a servant pay the *Qiṣāṣ* (the debt of recompense) by their own labor, the Imam—the vessel of divine mercy—renders the obligation null by a miraculous substitution (*al-Fiqāq*, the redemptive exchange): the account is settled, and the reckoning is complete.

## The Mill of Revelation and Concealment

Every cycle begins with a center, and every center originates from the distant past, and every epoch begins perpetually with the distant revelation. After *Sāḥib Alif* (the Master of the Thousand), the revelation-cycle (*kashf*) emerges as a potentiality. When *Qa'im ʿAlī* arrives at the remembrance of peace, he announces the beginning of the manifest revelation. *Sāḥib Alif* is *Mawlānā Aḥmad*—the thirty-fifth Imam—during whose tenure *Syedna Sulaiman Ṣāḥib* came to authority. He is given an example through the letter Alif, for Alif signifies the heart. His name appears too in the letter *Sīn* (the eschatological sign), corresponding to the very pulse of the cosmos.

| **Alif** | **Sulaiman** |
|---|---|
| **S (Seen)** | **S (Seen)** |
| *Heart of the Qur'an* | *Heart of the Prayers of the Custodians* |

When the *Qa'im*'s epoch concludes, he appoints his son and successor as Imam before his death; this son shall be the first Imam of the revelation-cycle. After the *Qa'im*'s passing, he ascends with his congregation to the rank of *ʿaql ʿāshir* (the Tenth Intellect), becoming the master of the physical realm in place of that intellect's former dominion. The sons of the *Qa'im*, in their capacity as Imams, rise from the summons (*daʿwah*) and pass from life in their appointed seasons, thereafter entering the horizon of their father *ʿāshir* with the congregation of their era. No place can be developed unless it first establishes a rank equivalent to its own essence. There is no caliph but the *Qa'im*. When the seventh Imam establishes his successor (the Caliph of the Whole) in his place, that successor is promoted to a new elevation.[^cite-14]

This process continues in perpetuity until the first seven thousand years of the revelation-cycle are exhausted, at which moment there emerges an establishment that gathers all the Imams of those millennia from the horizon of *ʿāshir*. Seven thousand more years then pass; at their end arises another standing-establishment, gathering the second seven-thousand-year cohort. In this manner, throughout the fifty thousand years of the revelation-cycle, seven such standing-forms manifest.

After that, the wise Imams dispatch the bodies and virtues of the custodian seventy-year era into the subsequent seven thousand years.

### The Emergence of the Virtues of Revelation Within the Revelation-Epoch

First, the holy ones of the Prophet's time (peace be upon him) who have passed from life and whose virtues and souls have matured shall be revealed in mystical form. After them, the souls and bodies of those who have suffered in the lowest punishment (the torment and severity of the veils) shall be sent forth in resurrection. This process continues for seven thousand years. Then comes the appearance of a gathered assembly of another seven thousand. Thereafter, the first bodies and virtues of Jesus shall be revealed across seven thousand years, followed by his resurrection-assembly. Then Moses, then Abraham, then Noah, and finally Adam shall each appear and resurrect their congregations across successive seven-thousand-year spans.[^cite-15]

### The Succession of Epochs: The Commencement of Each Revelation-Cycle

*Beginning with the Commencement of the Revelation-Epoch:*

- **7,000 years** — First 7,000 of the Revelation-Cycle
- **7,000 years** — Emergence of the Virtues of the Muhammadan Era
- **7,000 years** — Appearance of the Virtues of the Age of Jesus
- **7,000 years** — Appearance of the Virtues of the Age of Moses
- **7,000 years** — Appearance of the Virtues of the Age of Abraham
- **7,000 years** — Appearance of the Virtues of the Age of Noah
- **7,000 years** — Appearance of the Virtues of the Age of Adam
- *The Emergence of the Established Revelation-Epoch*
- *The Commencement of the Seventy-Year Era*

Thus, when fifty thousand years have been completed (seven thousand times seven plus one thousand), the revelation-epoch comes to an end, and with it emerges a *Qa'im* who assembles the six partial *Qa'im*s of prior millennia along with his own seven-thousand-year gathering. Within this seventy-year era, the final three thousand years constitute a period of five hundred years[^cite-16]; thereafter begins the seventy-year cycle proper. ## The Division of the Four Ranks

Adam and Noah were prophets of their respective ages. When *Ḥazrat Ibrāhīm* (peace be upon him) appeared, he gathered the records of both their epochs, and the four degrees of prophethood (*nubuwwah*), prophecy (*risālah*), imamate (*imāmah*), and testamentary succession (*waṣāyah*) all converged within him.

#### Abraham: Prime Minister of the Qa'im's Foundation

*Ibrāhīm* holds a singular place among the vicegerents of the *Qa'im*; he is the *Wazīr* (prime minister) of the Foundation itself.

*Ibrāhīm* divided the four ranks among his sons. To *Ḥazrat Isḥāq* he granted prophethood and prophecy; to *Mawlānā Ismāʿīl* he entrusted imamate and testamentary will. The rank of prophethood and prophecy descended through the heirs of Isaac, while imamate and perpetual guardianship (*istiqrār*) passed to the descendants of Ishmael. The descendants of Isaac became the *Ḥijāb* (the Veil, the intermediaries and protectors), maintaining the Prophet's knowledge in secrecy. All the Imams of Ishmael's line remained veiled from public view, while their *Ḥijāb* (the Isaacic descendants) stood exposed to opposition and endured tribulation.[^cite-26] This pattern persisted until all four ranks converged upon *Mawlānā Hāshim* (may peace be upon him), who thereafter bestowed all four assembled compilations upon his son *Mawlānā ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib*.[^cite-27]

### The Division of the Two Doors in ʿAbd al-Muttalib

*Mawlānā ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib* appointed two *Bāb* (doors or representatives) for himself. Both doors gathered the deceased and developing forms within the summons (*daʿwah*) of ʿAbd al-Muttalib. One of these doors compiled the chapters of the believers, the fully mature, and the ranks of the scholars—this was the supreme door. The second door gathered the questions of those fully obedient (*al-Mustajībīyīn*) who had taken the oath and departed from life in wilāyah (fealty to the Imam). The upper door was connected to *Mawlānā Abū Ṭālib* (peace be upon him), and the lower door to *ʿAbdullāh* (peace be upon him); for this reason both were exceedingly noble in the sight of Allah.

Therefore, in the realm of intention (Nīya), ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib perceived Ashrāf, and Muḥammad bin ʿAlī became Muḥammad. But when he entered the manifest world, Muḥammad bin ʿAlī ascended into Ashrāf. Muḥammad ibn ʿAlī is the fourth-greatest principle of the world, and his perception comes after the Ashrāf of ʿAyn al-ʿAzīma.

In the Qur'an are the degrees of the people of the Holy Qur'an. The letter *hā* (which counts as eight) refers to the perception of Muḥammad ibn ʿAlī in the realm of intentions.

The Messenger of Allah (may God's prayers and peace be upon him) was saying: "Kafalāt (Guardianship) is when someone lifts something great and small." As long as the Messenger of Allah was in charge of Kafalāt al-Kubrā (the Greater Guardianship), his rank exceeded that of Maulānā ʿAlī. But the moment he transferred the luminous forms (Anwār) to Maulānā ʿAlī, ʿAlī's status became superior to his. Yet even after this event, ʿAlī continued to pray behind the Messenger of Allah—something impossible if one superior in rank were to pray behind one inferior. The truth of this matter is that after the occasion of Ghadir Khumm, the veils (ḥijāb) of both changed. ʿAyn made Muḥammad a veil, and Mīm made ʿAlī a veil. After this transformation, ʿAlī became Muḥammad, and Muḥammad became ʿAlī—yet outwardly, ʿAlī prayed behind the Messenger of Allah.[^cite-2]

### Prayer at the Sixth Hour

The prayer of the Day of Ghadir is offered at the seventh hour. This is because there are six times signifying the Seven Horns. In each case, there gathers fifty-seven thousand years—that is, one Kashf gathering and one Seventy gathering. When the seventy have come to this age, the seventh hour shall be fulfilled.

### "Man Mullā Fahza ʿAlī Mullā"

Although the Prophet gave the highest of the two summonses to ʿAlī in the half of Shaʿbān, his greatness remained. Because on the night of the 27th of Rajab, he received a special blessing, and then another special blessing. Because of this, his supremacy was established over all, and all the prophets who were hitherto in their respective temples were placed under him. Throughout his life, ʿAlī did not receive this special Nasūt-kill. The Prophet (peace and blessings of God be upon him) said, "Man Mullā, Fahza ʿAlī Mullā." The Nasūt composed of the boundaries of the special gentlemen is so elevated that it resembles Lāhūt—this is Nasūt Khāṣ (the particular human form). This Nasūt was given to the Prophet for life, and ʿAlī received it on the Day of Ghadir. Therefore, the Prophet's status was superior to that of ʿAlī throughout his life.[^cite-3]

### The Change of Veil

As soon as the Prophet (peace and blessings of God be upon him) recited the text to Maulānā ʿAlī, the veil of both changed—that is, ʿAlī took the veil of the Prophet, and the Prophet took the veil of ʿAlī. The Nāṭiq (Speaker) and Awṣiyyā (Executors of the Will) die through their veils; that is, ʿAlī died during the Prophet's lifetime. The veil of the Prophet's death is actually the veil of ʿAyn. That is, ʿAyn died before Mīm.

In truth, this was the reason why ʿAlī became outwardly weak after the death of the Prophet (peace be upon him) and had to remain silent for thirty years. This was because the veil of mīm was working there, which was weaker than the veil of ʿAyn. Apart from this, another subtle matter: all the extraordinary feats of Maulānā ʿAlī—the uprooting of Khaybar, the incident of the Gate of Knowledge, and others—are all found in the life of the Holy Prophet. After his death, no extraordinary event is attributed to ʿAlī. This is why Maulānā ʿAlī threatened to divorce Āʾisha in the Battle of Jamal, for the veil of the Prophet was working there; otherwise there would be no point in ʿAlī divorcing the Prophet's wife.[^cite-4]

The Nāṭiq dies through his veils. That is, they use veils for their death and burial. Since the veil was changed, the Prophet died through the veil. ## The Ranks of the Five (Al-Panjatan)

Muḥammad Muṣṭafā (may God's prayers and peace be upon him) was the one who was martyred by bearing witness—two martyr-witnessings: "There is no god but God" (Ashhhadu an lā ilāha illā Allāh) and "I witness that Muḥammad is the Messenger of God" (Ashhadu anna Muḥammadan Rasūl Allāh). In this way, the Holy Prophet (peace be upon him) remained only with the outward summons. The people accepted Islam through him and joined him in struggle, fulfilled the prescribed prayers and the sunna he had established, and adhered to it. They all embraced ʿAlī's apparent wilāya (guardianship), and harbored no enmity toward him. Because of these actions, the souls of these people became jewels, their faces brightened, and their spirits glowed. When they died, they all moved into the temple of Maulānā Fāṭima. In this way, the congregation of outward worshippers and those professing wilāya joined with Maulānā Fāṭima—and this is why she is called Khalāṣat al-Jawāhir al-Islāmī (the Essence of Islamic Jewels), because the gathering of all Islamic forms assembled with her.[^cite-13]

Maulānā ʿAlī possessed a gathering of believers. When the temple of Maulānā Fāṭima was completed—she had been congregated in her temple for thirteen years—her father married her to Maulānā ʿAlī so that these Islamic and faithful forms could be reconciled. At this point, the summons was issued, and the summons to the inner world continued. In the same way, the affairs of these two (ʿAlī and Fāṭima) continued until al-Ḥasan (peace be upon him) was born from the outward summons and al-Ḥusayn (peace be upon him) came forth from the inward summons. The outward summons was the episode of the apparent, and the inward summons was the episode of the inner will.

The last and highest of all the great stations that the Prophet (peace be upon him) possessed is the veil. Among these ranks are Prophethood and the prescribed prayer (Salāt), which he owned and entrusted to al-Ḥasan (peace be upon him) through ʿAlī. He entrusted Prophethood and the prophetic office to Maulānā ʿAlī as trustee for al-Ḥasan, so that when he came of age and completed his education, ʿAlī would entrust these things to him.

After the death of the Prophet (peace be upon him), ʿAlī (peace be upon him) remained in all four ranks. That is, the Prophet (peace be upon him) did not give all things to al-Ḥasan (peace be upon him) at once. When he said, "Al-Ḥasan and al-Ḥusayn are the Imāms of truth; their grandfather and their father are better than them," he entrusted certain things to al-Ḥasan and al-Ḥusayn. For the remainder, he entrusted to ʿAlī the duty of handing these two sons their portion when they came of age.

### Facts

There are ten ranks of Imāmate. The first is the rank of Istijāba (Responsiveness, Response); the second is the rank of Īmān (Faith). In the same way, the ranks within Imāmate also increase progressively.

ʿAlī (peace be upon him) then entrusted Prophethood and the prophetic office to al-Ḥusayn (peace be upon him) before he became al-Ḥusayn, but he entrusted some ranks of Imāmate and prophecy to al-Ḥusayn, which made him Imām before al-Ḥusayn became so in fact. It has been reported that when the summons of Asher was given, al-Ḥasan (peace be upon him) came forward before al-Ḥusayn (peace be upon him), yet al-Ḥusayn possessed a more exalted perception than al-Ḥasan. For this reason, the condition of permanence was not with al-Ḥasan but with al-Ḥusayn. In this way, some of the ranks of al-Ḥusayn were established with al-Ḥasan. These ranks that have accumulated with you will no longer depart from you, and no one shall attain them except the Qāʾima. At the end, all these ranks will be gathered in the temple of Qāʾim al-Qiyāma ʿAlī Dhikrā al-Assalām, and you will become the soul of this temple. The rank of ʿAlī will be that of the heart, and the Messenger of Allah (peace be upon him) will take the place of the head. All others (the Triple Ambassadors, Fāṭima, and the Imāms) will become organs. The three ambassadors (ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib, Abū Ṭālib, and ʿAbd Allāh) will replace the powers of the intellect.[^cite-14]

### Adam of the Fathers (Ādam al-Ābāʾ)

When Imām Ibrāhīm (peace be upon him) was given the four ranks—Prophethood, the prophetic office, Imāmate, and the executor's will—he became a veil of Asher, which was called Khalīl Allāh (the Friend of God). He divided these four ranks: he gave the highest ranks to Maulānā Ismāʿīl and the lowest to Ḥaḍrat Isḥāq. Thereafter, these ranks passed through Maulānā Hāshim and gathered with Maulānā ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib. He also divided these ranks. From then on, the ranks were divided, passing onward until they came to Maulānā Imām ʿAlī Zayn al-ʿĀbidīn and were assembled. Upon reaching him, the condition of these gatherings underwent a transformation: from that time until the Day of Judgment, there is no division of these ranks. Imām ʿAlī Zayn al-ʿĀbidīn was the first to achieve this new state. This is why he is called Ādam al-Ābāʾ (the Adam of Fathers, the primordial ancestor who establishes a new dispensation). From the time of the Imāms onward until the Day of Judgment, the condition of the Imāms changed, and because of the consolidation of the four ranks, their stations became higher than those of the previous Imāms. For this reason, it is said in prayer: "Muḥammad and ʿAlī are the best of humanity, and their progeny is the best of progeny."[^cite-15]

On the basis of this principle, the Month of Rajab (Rajab al-Ḥarām) and the Month of God (Shahru Allāh al-Ḥarām) differ as day and night.

Although the proofs of the Imāms rank below in hierarchy, the magnificence of these scholars is very great. The splendor of the Mansūr (the Aided One) itself is enhanced. Just as the Prophet (peace be upon him) increased the splendor of the universe, so too the main purpose of the *Nās* (the people, or the visible guide) is to persuade the Mansūs (the hidden one). In this sense alone, the Ḥujjat (the proof; the Imām's son) is superior to the Imām.[^cite-16]

## The Resurrection in Totality (Baʿath Kullī)

We believe in the resurrection of the dead, and when we recite the prayer of witness (Taḥīb), we say: "O God, I am a messenger of God." That is, God will send forth those who are in the graves. The meaning of being raised from the graves means that just as God created us in this sphere through our parents, He will bring us into the next sphere through our parents as well. The mother's womb is like a grave in which the father buries his seed. The nobility of man is that he is born through both mother and father and comes to a state of faith. However, the Messenger of Allah (may God's prayers and peace be upon him) said that we should leave the line of parents and be born of God—that is, to join the body (of the Imam). This is called Baʿath Kullī (the Resurrection in Totality), which is the honor of the Prophet. God says in Sūrah Yāsīn:

> ⟪ar:يسٓ ۝ وَٱلْقُرْءَانِ ٱلْحَكِيمِ ۝ إِنَّكَ لَمِنَ ٱلْمُرْسَلِينَ ۝ عَلَىٰ صِرَٰطٍۢ مُّسْتَقِيمٍۢ⟫
> — **Quran 36:1**

That is, God addressed the Prophet (peace and blessings of God be upon him), praised his greatness, and then said: "You are on the straight path, which you do not discern." This is because God first explained the great qualities of his prophethood, and then spoke of a humble matter—that he was on the straight path. The "straight path" in this verse refers to the path of the Jāthā Ibdāʾiyya (the total emanation). That is, your caliphate is established in the Jāthā Ibdāʾiyya; as you advance on the day of the cycle, you will join the Jāthā. The Prophet said in the Qur'an:[^cite-17]

> ⟪ar:إِنِّى تَوَكَّلْتُ عَلَى ٱللَّهِ رَبِّى وَرَبِّكُم ۚ مَّا مِن دَآبَّةٍ إِلَّا هُوَ ءَاخِذٌۢ بِنَاصِيَتِهَآ ۚ إِنَّ رَبِّى عَلَىٰ صِرَٰطٍۢ مُّسْتَقِيمٍۢ⟫
> — **Quran 11:56**

His Lord is Maulānā Abū Ṭālib (peace be upon him)—that is, he praised his mentor and guide, saying that Maulānā Abū Ṭālib is also standing on the same path on which he will enter the body of the cycle.

### The Straight Path

When we recite in Sūrah al-Fātiḥa "Guide us to the straight path," the "straight path" means that our form should not only be on the right path but should also follow it. That is, to proceed from one limit to another and reach its extreme point, which is the Temple of the Imām. Do not remain stuck at any limit, but continue to grow and advance.[^cite-18]

## Maulānā Muḥammad b. Ismāʿīl

The chain of truth continued after Imām al-Ḥusayn (peace be upon him) through the week of Atmaʾ until the command reached Maulānā Muḥammad b. Ismāʿīl (peace be upon him), who was the founder and establisher of this period. He is the Creator (al-Mubdiʿ); you are the Original Kashf (the First Unveiler), because you revealed the inner knowledge. Those who come to witness are the Creators of the Kashf al-Kubrā (the Greater Unveiling).[^cite-21]

Maulānā Muḥammad bin Ismāʿīl possesses a very exalted rank. When he dies, he becomes the caliph of Asher. He is the Prophet because he gathered all six speakers before him. He has transcended the rank of Nāṭiq-i Sābʿa (the Seventh Speaker) and reached the supreme rank of Waḥdat Ilāhī (Divine Unity, the Qa'im). He assembled a very magnificent gathering—the gathering of the first week. Each of these Imāms (from al-Ḥasan to Ismāʿīl) is a Qāʾim. Therefore, when Muḥammad bin Ismāʿīl gathered them all, he gathered a group of Qāʾims. The virtue of Maulānā Muḥammad bin Ismāʿīl arises from several respects:[^cite-22]

### The Seventh Speech: Maulānā Muḥammad Bin Ismāʿīl

If the seventh speaker gathers the previous six points before him, does this mean that Maulānā Muḥammad bin Ismāʿīl is superior in rank to Muḥammad Muṣṭafā (peace be upon him)?

Muḥammad Muṣṭafā (peace be upon him) holds the rank of Nāṭiq Sādis (the Sixth Speaker), which is lower than Nāṭiq Sābʿa. But the rank of Mīm (the Second Principle) reached the veil of ʿAyn al-ʿAzīma, by virtue of which he became the veil of ʿAyn ʿAzīma and rose above the rank of al-Nāṭiq al-Sābʿa. He excelled over all the Imāms and Executors until the Day of Judgment and became the leader of all. This is why his rank is higher than that of Maulānā Muḥammad bin Ismāʿīl.[^cite-23]

In the same way, the Qa'im (peace be upon him) ascended from the point of Nāṭiq Sābʿa, and he made ʿAyn ʿAzīma his vizier, becoming himself the Qa'im of the entire period. This is how the ascendancy of those who remain within the horizon of the intellect rests upon the point. True monotheism (tawhīd) is the knowledge of which point it begins and at what point it ascends.

### Facts

In this period of Seventy, in a certain sense, Nāṭiq Sābʿa is Maulānā Muḥammad ibn Ismāʿīl (peace be upon him), because he is the seventh Imām of the week of Atmaʾ who gathered the six before him (excepting Imām al-Ḥusayn). The true Nāṭiq al-Sābʿa is actually the rank of the Qa'im (peace be upon him), because he is the gatherer of the six speakers. But since the rank of Qa'im far transcends that of Nāṭiq al-Sābʿa, he bestowed the rank of Nāṭiq al-Sābʿa upon Maulānā Muḥammad bin Ismāʿīl (peace be upon him).

### Imāmic and Qāʾimic Structure

Maulānā Muḥammad bin Ismāʿīl (from his father Ismāʿīl) possessed the temple of Imāmate. Apart from this, he assembled the structure of the Qa'im as the author of the Prophet. He gave the temple of Imāmate to his son ʿAbd Allāh and the temple of Imāmate to his eldest son Yaʿalī, who became the trustee of ʿAbd Allāh. Yaʿalī's veil became ʿAbd Allāh, but he also became the Imām of the time. Then ʿAbd Allāh's veil was established.

The genealogical succession from Muḥammad bin Ismāʿīl proceeds through two parallel lines: the visible line of Imāmate (Ḥijāb) and the concealed line of Qa'imiyat (Muḥtajab). The visible line runs through ʿAbd Allāh, Aḥmad, and Ḥusayn to ʿAbd Allāh al-Mahdī bi-Llāh. The concealed line runs through Yaʿalī, Abū Ṭālib, ʿAlī, and ʿAbd Allāh to the Qa'im al-Ḥākim bi-Amr Allāh. In this way, the virtues of the six Companions of the Cave were repeated in the virtues of these six people (Yaʿalī, Abū Ṭālib, ʿAlī, ʿAbd Allāh, Aḥmad, and Ḥusayn). The period of these six Imāms is also known as the period of Seventy and the period of Kashf. The time of the three Imāms is the period of Fatr, and the time of the manifest Imāms is the period of revelation. Imām al-Ṭayyib (peace be upon him) belongs to the Seventy period.[^cite-24]

## Glorification Before the Throne Verse

The Prophet, peace and blessings be upon him, was asked: "When believers depart from this life and enter the sanctuary of the Imam, peace be upon him, what do they occupy themselves with?" He replied: "They glorify Allah and praise Him continuously, never ceasing their sanctification, not even for the duration of an eye's blink."

Tasbīḥ (glorification) elevates the soul toward the realm of ʿiṣma (spiritual immunity from error). Through the perpetual glorification and sanctification engaged in by the angels, an effect became established within their nature such that they became incapable of transgression—that is, they attained spiritual immunity. The Qur'an addresses them thus:

> ⟪ar:يَـٰٓأَيُّهَا ٱلَّذِينَ ءَامَنُوا۟ قُوٓا۟ أَنفُسَكُمْ وَأَهْلِيكُمْ نَارًۭا وَقُودُهَا ٱلنَّاسُ وَٱلْحِجَارَةُ عَلَيْهَا مَلَـٰٓئِكَةٌ غِلَاظٌۭ شِدَادٌۭ لَّا يَعْصُونَ ٱللَّهَ مَآ أَمَرَهُمْ وَيَفْعَلُونَ مَا يُؤْمَرُونَ⟫
> O you who believe! Save yourselves and your families from a Fire whose fuel is men and stones, over which are angels stern and severe, who do not disobey God in what He commands them and do as they are commanded.
> — **Qur'an 66:6**

Tasbīḥ and taqddīs (sanctification) signify the attainment of knowledge of the Imam. After a believer's death, the account of action ceases, for action pertains to the body. Yet the longing for knowledge persists; therefore, knowledge is bestowed upon such souls within the sanctuary, regarding which the Qur'an states:

> ⟪ar:أُو۟لَـٰٓئِكَ هُمُ ٱلْمُؤْمِنُونَ حَقًّۭا ۚ لَّهُمْ دَرَجَـٰتٌ عِندَ رَبِّهِمْ وَمَغْفِرَةٌۭ وَرِزْقٌۭ كَرِيمٌۭ⟫
> They are the true believers. For them are degrees with their Lord, and forgiveness, and a generous sustenance.
> — **Qur'an 8:4**

Through this spiritual sustenance, believers engage in the perpetual acquisition of knowledge of the Imam.[^cite-1]

The believer recites the Throne Verse after every obligatory prayer. The essential core of this verse extends from "Allah—there is no god but He" to "the Most High, the Most Great." Yet the Prophet, peace be upon him, prescribed preparatory formulas before it, each carrying profound spiritual meanings. The worshipper recites:

**Allāhu Akbar, Allāhu Akbar, Allāhu Akbar, Allāhu Akbar**

Each utterance acknowledges that every Imam possessed at least three successors through whom the doctrine was transmitted. To demonstrate this knowledge, the worshipper pronounces the takbīr (magnification) three times:

**Subḥān Allāh (Glory be to Allah)**

Subḥān is the declaration of tanzīh (transcendence), which affirms Allah's subsistence while purifying Him of all created attributes. The term subḥān derives from the notion of buoyancy: as water bears the swimmer upward and prevents descent, so too does the Majesty of Allah—manifest in the Imam—elevate and sustain, never permitting diminishment. The Imam constitutes the Lāhūt (the divine attribute), a gathering of the doctrines transmitted from his predecessor's summons. Each Imam, moreover, prepares such a gathering for his successor. This divine assembly is indeed magnificent and exalted! In praise of the Almighty's governance and sanctity, the worshipper declares: "Glory be to Allah—what is the glory of Allah?"

**Al-Ḥamdu Lillāh (Praise be to Allah)**

The Imam's son—he who is destined to become Imam—is the nūr (light), and he mirrors the believers' praise. Thus: praise be to Allah; Allah is the present Imam, and ḥamd is his successor-son.[^cite-2] When this successor, the Nāsūt al-Ẓāhir (manifest form), receives the Lāhūt (esoteric doctrine) from his predecessor, he himself becomes the Imam—becomes "Allah," in the terminology of the summons. The worshipper affirms, moreover, that the union of Lāhūt with Nāsūt has been accomplished. Without the Imam preparing his successor and instructing him in the principles of spiritual authority, gratitude to Allah remains incomplete.

The term ḥawl signifies inner capacity, and quwwa denotes manifest strength. All existence in the universe operates within these two dimensions; nothing stands outside them. In the terminology of the summons, "hawl" is the inner station which assembles the ranks of the spiritual elite, while "strength" is the manifest station comprising the stations of the earthly propagators. The recitation of these words is necessary because neither the inner nor the outer dimension possesses sufficient capacity to form the sanctuary save through the Imam, who is the khalīfa (successor) and hijāb (veil) of ʿAyn al-ʿAẓīma (the Supreme Essence). Without Allah—without the Imam—there exists no capacity nor strength. Similarly, the existence of Ḥasan and Ḥusayn is impossible without ʿAlī al-ʿAẓīm; neither could ʿAlī's successors rival his station.[^cite-4]

Within the hierarchy of spiritual authority, "hawl" designates Lāhūt and "quwwa" designates Nāsūt. Thus, the one who invokes this phrase confesses that neither Lāhūt nor Nāsūt could exist independent of their possessor, the Imam.

The rank of hawl surpasses that of quwwa. As time advances, strength draws near to hawl, an approximation designated as "the Unique Verse."

## The Throne Verse

The verse is termed the "Throne Verse" (Āyat al-Kursī) because it employs the word ⟪ar:کرسی⟫ (kursī, throne), which provides the verse its distinctive nomenclature. This term appears uniquely in the Qur'an, referring to a singular entity that is matchless in itself. Numerous traditions attest diverse modes of reciting this verse. One such tradition holds that should a person recite it according to the numerical value of the word kursī (290) or according to the numeric value of their own name, extraordinary assistance descends from Allah. For instance, should one's name be ʿAlī, one should recite the verse 110 times.

⟪ar:أمیر المومنین⟫ The Commander of the Faithful declared: "None has encompassed this verse except the Messenger of Allah and the believer whom Allah has tested with faith." The Prophet, peace be upon him, proclaimed: ⟪ar-quote:فِی کُلِّ حَرفٍ اَلفَ رَحمَۃ وَاَلفَ بَرَکَۃ⟫ "In every letter of this verse reside a thousand mercies and a thousand blessings."

The exegete observes that the Throne Verse in fact comprises only ⟪ar:'اﷲُ وھو العلی العظیم'⟫ "Allah—He is the Most High, the Most Great." All that follows constitutes ⟪ar:جملۃُ المُعتَرِضَۃ⟫ (an interpolated phrase) describing Allah's attributes. After enumerating these attributes entirely, it concludes by revealing that the One ⟪ar:'اللہ'⟫ who possesses them all is ⟪ar:علی عظیم⟫ the Most High, the Most Great. In other words, the entire verse elaborates upon the attributes of ⟪ar:'عین عظیمۃ'⟫ the Supreme Essence.

> ⟪quran 2:255⟫
> ⟪ar:‏ٱللَّهُ لَآ إِلَهَ إِلَّا هُوَ ٱلْحَىُّ ٱلْقَيُّومُ ۚ لَا تَأْخُذُهُۥ سِنَةٌۭ وَلَا نَوْمٌۭ ۚ لَّهُۥ مَا فِى ٱلسَّمَوَتِ وَمَا فِى ٱلْأَرْضِ ۗ مَن ذَا ٱلَّذِى يَشْفَعُ عِندَهُۥٓ إِلَّا بِإِذْنِهِۦ ۚ يَعْلَمُ مَا بَيْنَ أَيْدِيهِمْ وَمَا خَلْفَهُمْ ۖ وَلَا يُحِيطُونَ بِشَىْءٍۢ مِّنْ عِلْمِهِۦٓ إِلَّا بِمَا شَآءَ ۚ وَسِعَ كُرْسِيُّهُ ٱلسَّمَوَتِ وَٱلْأَرْضَ ۖ وَلَا يَـُٔودُهُۥ حِفْظُهُمَا ۚ وَهُوَ ٱلْعَلِىُّ ٱلْعَظِيمُ ‎⟫
> Allah! There is no god but He, the Ever-Living, the Sustainer of existence. Neither slumber overtakes Him nor sleep. To Him belongs whatever is in the heavens and whatever is on the earth. Who is he that can intercede with Him except by His permission? He knows what lies before them and what shall come after them, yet they encompass not anything of His knowledge except that which He wills. His Throne encompasses the heavens and the earth, and the preservation of them wearies Him not. And He is the Most High, the Most Great.
> — **Qur'an 2:255**

### The Balance of the Throne Verse

Allah has established perfect equilibrium within this verse. Nine constituent parts form its structure, and the number nine denotes the culmination of unity, its perfection and essence—just as Ramaḍān, the ninth month of the Islamic calendar, is the summation of all other months.

| (1) Allah—there is no god but He, the Living, the Sustainer | (2) Neither slumber seizes Him, nor sleep | (3) To Him belongs all that is in the heavens and the earth |
| --- | --- | --- |
| (4) Who can intercede with Him except by His permission? | **(5) He knows what is before them and what comes after** | (6) Yet they encompass not His knowledge except as He wills |
| (7) His Throne encompasses the heavens and the earth | (8) And the preservation of them wearies Him not | (9) And He is the Most High, the Most Great |

When the reciter reaches the midpoint of the verse, contemplating both what precedes and what follows, recognition dawns that the two halves mirror one another. The central phrase declares: ⟪ar:يَعْلَمُ مَا بَيْنَ أَيْدِيهِمْ وَمَا خَلْفَهُمْ⟫ "He knows what is before them and what comes after." This phrase itself expounds the front and rear portions, articulating that the past is perpetually disclosed ahead. This exemplifies the form of the circle, which implies that what appears at the circle's beginning shall reappear at its completion. When we examine the correspondence of the first and last parts through the central phrase, these verses align thus:

**(1)** There is no god but Allah. He is the Living, the Sustainer of all. ↔ **(9)** And He is the Most High, the Most Great.

**(2)** Neither slumber nor sleep overtake Him. ↔ **(8)** And the preservation of them wearies Him not.

### Neither Slumber Nor Sleep

"He is not touched by slumber, nor does sleep overtake Him."

Heedlessness in our spiritual world manifests as sleep in this material realm. For this reason, sleep is dear to humankind, yet waking from it to pray at night, though arduous, yields immense reward. This signifies that one must break free from negligence and arise at the call of Imam al-Zamān, abandoning ease to undertake rigorous spiritual effort.

Negligence precedes sleep in the spiritual hierarchy; sleep is its materialization in the physical world. Slumber and negligence befall all human beings, but Allah—the Essence of ʿAyn al-ʿAẓīma—stands exalted above such deficiency. That is, the Essence has never been heedless in any moment of the past, nor shall It ever be negligent in any future time.[^cite-7]

### To Him Belongs All That Is in the Heavens and the Earth

"To Him belongs whatever is in the heavens and whatever is on the earth."

The heavens signify the attributes of the Imams, while the earth represents the proof-bearers (ḥujaj) of the Imams who summon to the Imam. Thus, the convergence of all Imams, their arguments, both esoteric and manifest, together with all their congregations, culminates within the Great Sanctuary of ʿAyn al-ʿAẓīma, who is the true proprietor of all forms of transmission.

### Who Can Intercede Without His Permission?

"Who is he that can intercede with Him except by His permission?"

Intercession signifies seeking deliverance from the seventy spiritual maladies of the inner world. In every era, the guardianship of ʿAyn al-ʿAẓīma has been the locus of salvation and intercession. It is impossible to escape the captivity of this chaotic realm without their guardianship and love.

### He Knows What Is Before and What Comes After

"He knows what is before them and what shall come after them. Yet they encompass not anything of His knowledge except that which He wills."

The Imam is cognizant of the affairs of each of his successors and all the believers within his circle, and aware of the matters of their former epochs. The Imams have ascended to their stations through the perfection they attained, and they possess knowledge of their affairs in future developments. All the Imams derive their knowledge from the grace of ʿAyn al-ʿAẓīma.

### His Throne Encompasses the Heavens and the Earth

"His Throne encompasses the heavens and the earth, and the preservation of them wearies Him not."

In this verse, the pronoun with "throne" appears as "His Throne," spread across sky and earth. The throne signifies the essence of the Great One who dominates all the Imams and their arguments. When one sits upon a throne, one bears the full weight and burden. Thus, the entire burden of the age was placed upon the shoulders of ʿAyn al-ʿAẓīma through the Qa'im of the Age. Yet just as the throne never tires from bearing weight, so too does the Essence never weary from the tremendous responsibility of the entire age, experiencing no difficulty whatsoever.[^cite-8]

## Islam: The Surrender of Forms

In Sūrat al-Baqara, ⟪ar:حضرت ابراہیم علیہ السلام⟫ Prophet Abraham, peace be upon him, enjoins upon his children:

> ⟪quran 2:132⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَوَصَّىٰ بِهَآ إِبْرَهِیمُ بَنِيهِ وَيَعْقُوبُ يَبَنِىَّ إِنَّ ٱللَّهَ ٱصْطَفَىٰ لَكُمُ ٱلدِّينَ فَلَا تَمُوتُنَّ إِلَّا وَأَنتُم مُّسْلِمُونَ ‎⟫
> And Abraham enjoined upon his children this religion, and so did Jacob, [saying]: "O my children, Allah has chosen the faith for you; therefore die not except as Muslims."
> — **Qur'an 2:132**

It may seem peculiar that ⟪ar:ابراھیم علیہ السلام⟫ Prophet Abraham addresses his children, who ⟪ar:نبوت (اسحاق)⟫ possessed prophethood and ⟪ar:امامت (اسماعیل)⟫ spiritual authority themselves, commanding that they die as Muslims. This apparent paradox resolves when we recognize that Islam comprises two distinct dimensions. The first is subordinate to faith, the level entered through the declaration ⟪ar:لا الہ الا اللہ⟫ "There is no god but Allah." The second constitutes a rank above faith, constituting what is properly termed ⟪ar:تسلیم الصور⟫ "the surrender of forms." That is, it entails entrusting the assemblies gathered near the stations of prophethood and spiritual authority to the Imams themselves. In this verse, ⟪ar:ابراھیم علیہ السلام⟫ Abraham instructs his sons ⟪ar:اسحاق و اسماعیل⟫ Isaac and Ishmael: "Do not pass away until you have transmitted the assemblies of those ⟪ar:نبوت و رسالت اور امامت و وصایت⟫ bearing prophethood, messengership, spiritual authority, and succession to those who shall inherit these stations." Likewise, the Messenger of Allah summoned all humanity to Islam—to enter the ⟪ar:اسلامی صورات⟫ "Islamic forms," the sacred assembly of the sanctuary.

The greater orbit of Islam encompasses within it a smaller orbit—that of faith (īmān). At the center of this concentric circle lies ⟪ar:' الاسلام'⟫ Islam itself, which in fact represents what ⟪ar:ابراھیم خلیل اللہ⟫ the Friend of Allah, Abraham, imparted to his progeny: the surrender of one's affairs to the true possessor. This constitutes post-faith Islam, ranking higher than faith itself. ⟪ar:الأسلام⟫ The Prophets, as ⟪ar:(مستودعین)⟫ trustees, practice an Islam wherein they entrust their sacred ranks to their Imam. ⟪ar:الأسلام⟫ Entry into this supreme Islam occurs solely through prayer. In other words, Islam develops through stages of faith. Within Islam itself there are no degrees, yet faith contains multiple ranks. This mirrors the relationship between ⟪ar:کمال اول⟫ primary perfection—which knows no hierarchy—and ⟪ar:کمال ثانی⟫ secondary perfection, wherein ranks and order emerge immediately. Faith stands intermediary between these two Islams. The first Islam, conditioned merely upon utterance of the word, encompasses all who profess and all who follow. But the second Islam requires the conditions of sincerity and devotion, for which reason ⟪ar:حضرت ابراھیم علیہ السلام⟫ Abraham commanded his children not to depart until ⟪ar:نبوت، رسالت، امامت، وصایت⟫ they had entrusted prophethood, messengership, spiritual authority, and succession to their superiors.

### Why Do We Purify Ourselves?

We seek to purify the self because we must present it to our higher self so that it may accept it. This principle governs all creation. The Islam of the mineral is its surrender to the roots of vegetation. The Islam of vegetation is its surrender to the animal. The duty of the animal ⟪ar:الأسلام⟫ —and of all creatures lawful—is to surrender its body to the human being, to bow its neck before the blade, so that man might perfect it as an organ of his own being. And it is humanity's ⟪ar:الاسلام⟫ duty to surrender itself ⟪ar:صورت شریف⟫ to its higher rank; ⟪ar:(تسلیم الصور)⟫ our higher rank is the master of our good.[^cite-9] This is why the Qur'an declares: "Long for death, for it is the gateway to freedom and the fulfillment of this aspect of Islam." Through death does the soul present and perfect its form. Death, in truth, is the believer's ⟪ar:الأسلام (تسلیم الصور)⟫ surrender of forms. The Qur'an proclaims:

> ⟪quran 2:94⟫
> ⟪ar:‏قُلْ إِن كَانَتْ لَكُمُ ٱلدَّارُ ٱلْآخِرَةُ عِندَ ٱللَّهِ خَالِصَةًۭ مِّن دُونِ ٱلنَّاسِ فَتَمَنَّوُا۟ ٱلْمَوْتَ إِن كُنتُمْ صَدِقِينَ ‎⟫
> Say: "If the abode of the Hereafter with Allah is for you alone, to the exclusion of all mankind, then long for death, if you are truthful."
> — **Qur'an 2:94**

The Hereafter is indeed the supreme realm where the form reaches its rest and perfection. In this verse, Allah states: "Seek to surrender to the Throne so that you may attain the abode of the Hereafter, which has been prepared for you." That is, your higher rank is your alone—not that of any other believer.[^cite-10]

## The Diverse Greetings of Peace

### The Greeting of Believers to One Another

The word "Islam" derives from "Salām," which signifies peace. The Prophet, peace and blessings be upon him, stated: ⟪ar-quote:السلام قبل الکلام⟫ "Extend the greeting before speech." When one believer salutes another, he affirms: we are both present together upon the foundation of Islam. You shall not be harmed by me in my presence, nor shall I slander you in your absence.

### The Rider's Greeting to the Pedestrian

The Prophet declared: "It is incumbent upon the rider to greet the pedestrian." The rider represents the dāʿī (the Summoner), and the pedestrian signifies the believer. The dāʿī is thus called by the term ⟪ar:حمار⟫ "a beast of burden," in that the preacher bears the full weight of nurturing all believers for the sake of the Imam, without complaint. The dāʿī gathers all into the congregation, regardless of their deeds.

### The Greeting of the Prophets

The transmission of the assemblies and doctrines of the Imams to others—the handing down of prophethood and messengership, spiritual authority and succession—is itself called salām. And when the Prophets confer exalted ranks ⟪ar:(امامت و وصایت)⟫ upon their successors, this too constitutes a greeting.

### The Transmission of Hierarchy

In truth, salām signifies "transmission" or "dissemination of doctrines." Thus does Allah say, in the description of ⟪ar:سورۃ القدر⟫ the Chapter of Might:

> ⟪quran 97:5⟫
> ⟪ar:‏سَلَمٌ هِىَ حَتَّىٰ مَطْلَعِ ٱلْفَجْرِ ‎⟫
> Peace! It is until the rising of the dawn.
> — **Qur'an 97:5**

⟪ar:لیلۃ القدر⟫ The Night of Might is the night of transmissions. Wherever spiritual centers are established and wherever powers emanate, a succession of transmissions is issued throughout this night.[^cite-11]
