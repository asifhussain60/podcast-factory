## Ali and False Allegations Concerning the Qur'an

A certain group among the Shi'a adherents of Amīr al-Mu'minīn ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib maintains the erroneous belief that the Qur'ān was revealed to ʿAlī rather than to Muḥammad ibn ʿAbdullāh (peace be upon him), and that the names of ʿAlī and his descendants appeared explicitly throughout the original text, only to be deliberately excised in later transmission. According to their doctrine, the Qur'ān underwent the same species of alteration (taḥrīf, textual corruption) that befell the Torah and Gospel at the hands of Jews and Christians. This belief contradicts the absolute truth, for Allāh Himself has pledged to preserve the Qur'ān:[^cite-1]

> ⟪quran 15:9⟫
> ⟪ar:‏إِنَّا نَحْنُ نَزَّلْنَا ٱلذِّكْرَ وَإِنَّا لَهُۥ لَحَفِظُونَ ‎⟫
> *Verily We: It is We Who have sent down the Dhikr (i.e. the Quran) and surely, We will guard it (from corruption).*

Before addressing the refutation of this false doctrine, it is expedient to clarify the distinction between the ranks of Muḥammad and ʿAlī—that is, between the Prophet and the Prophet's successor—both according to revealed law and through rational demonstration. This clarification will illuminate the true and correct belief of the people of truth.

**The Messenger of Allāh and Adam, Peace Be Upon Them Both**

According to divine law and the heavenly scriptures, Adam is the father of mankind, and Eve was created from one of his ribs. Allāh declares in the Qur'ān:

> ⟪quran 4:1⟫
> ⟪ar:‏يَٓأَيُّهَا ٱلنَّاسُ ٱتَّقُوا۟ رَبَّكُمُ ٱلَّذِى خَلَقَكُم مِّن نَّفْسٍۢ وَحِدَةٍۢ وَخَلَقَ مِنْهَا زَوْجَهَا وَبَثَّ مِنْهُمَا رِجَالًۭا كَثِيرًۭا وَنِسَآءًۭا ۚ وَٱتَّقُوا۟ ٱللَّهَ ٱلَّذِى تَسَآءَلُونَ بِهِۦ وَٱلْأَرْحَامَ ۚ إِنَّ ٱللَّهَ كَانَ عَلَيْكُمْ رَقِيبًۭا ‎⟫
> *O mankind! Be dutiful to your Lord, Who created you from a single person (Adam), and from him (Adam) He created his wife [Hawwa (Eve)], and from them both He created many men and women and fear Allah through Whom you demand your mutual (rights), and (do not cut the relations of) the wombs (kinship). Surely, Allah is Ever an All-Watcher over you.*

The exegetes among the people of *taʾwīl* (esoteric exegesis) understand that every prophet is the man of his age, inasmuch as he is the founder of his temporal epoch, and the entire era is attributed to him. Just as Adam (peace be upon him) was the first human to appear in human form—the first to manifest all names and to bring forth knowledge of material creation—so too in the final age, the Seal of the Prophets and Master of Messengers, Muḥammad (peace be upon him), emerged as the first to transcend the *ḥanīfiyyah* (monotheistic disposition) of his time. His was not merely the first of his corporeal self, but the first subtle essence (rūḥ) to attain knowledge of the sciences of the Hereafter.[^cite-2]

**ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib, Likened to Eve**

Just as Allāh created Adam—whom He called the "single soul" (*nafs wāḥidah*)—from dust, and fashioned from him his wife Eve, so did He create from the Prophet (peace be upon him) his spiritual and esoteric successor, ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib. Mawlānā ʿAlī bears the burden of the Messenger's trust and stands as custodian of mysteries, revelation, and the sciences of the Qur'ān—even as a woman safeguards her husband's seed and fashions it into beauteous form within her womb. Because of this profound spiritual kinship, the Prophet (peace be upon him) declared: "O ʿAlī, you and I are the parents of all the believers."[^cite-3] Were this utterance to refer merely to outward, physical parenthood, it would be manifest absurdity, for two persons cannot be the father and mother of all humanity. Rather, the Prophet's fatherhood over the believers is established through the words of Allāh Himself:

> ⟪quran 33:6⟫
> ⟪ar:‏ٱلنَّبِىُّ أَوْلَىٰ بِٱلْمُؤْمِنِينَ مِنْ أَنفُسِهِمْ ۖ وَأَزْوَجُهُۥٓ أُمَّهَتُهُمْ ۗ وَأُو۟لُوا۟ ٱلْأَرْحَامِ بَعْضُهُمْ أَوْلَىٰ بِبَعْضٍۢ فِى كِتَبِ ٱللَّهِ مِنَ ٱلْمُؤْمِنِينَ وَٱلْمُهَجِرِينَ إِلَّآ أَن تَفْعَلُوٓا۟ إِلَىٰٓ أَوْلِيَآئِكُم مَّعْرُوفًۭا ۚ كَانَ ذَلِكَ فِى ٱلْكِتَبِ مَسْطُورًۭا ‎⟫
> *The Prophet is closer to the believers than their ownselves, and his wives are their (believers') mothers (as regards respect and marriage).*

When the Prophet's wives are the mothers of the believers, the Prophet must be their father—a status that is established through religious law, not through physical generation. From Adam and Eve, countless men and women multiplied; likewise, from Muḥammad and ʿAlī there flourished a succession of Imāms—a spiritual and psychic lineage. Thus it becomes evident that the difference between Muḥammad and ʿAlī mirrors the difference between heaven and earth, between man and woman, between Adam and Eve. How, then, could the Qur'ān have been revealed to ʿAlī, born as he was through the Prophet's spiritual emanation?

**What Is Revelation?**

Let us now examine the nature of revelation (*waḥy*) and what is meant by revelation to the prophets. The fundamental difference between ourselves and the prophets lies in this: we are servants of nature, our souls enslaved to appetite and occupied solely with bodily pleasure and the care of this world. The prophets, by contrast, stand in an inverted relationship to nature—nature serves them. Their preoccupation is the perfection of the Hereafter, achieved through the abandonment of lust and bodily gratification.

For this reason, we depend entirely upon the five bodily senses to acquire all our knowledge and information. The prophets, by virtue of their intellectual clarity and luminosity of soul, acquire and comprehend the sciences of religion and the Hereafter without recourse to any outward sensory apparatus. They then convey these sciences to us through words, which we receive through our ears and from which we benefit. Because of their asceticism, devotion, and purity of soul, the prophets draw near to the angels and thus readily receive these sciences from them. These sciences are then clothed in meaningful words formed of alphabetic letters, and transmitted to our faculty of hearing. For this reason, Allāh says:

> ⟪quran 26:193-195⟫
> ⟪ar:‏نَزَلَ بِهِ ٱلرُّوحُ ٱلْأَمِينُ عَلَىٰ قَلْبِكَ لِتَكُونَ مِنَ ٱلْمُنذِرِينَ بِلِسَانٍ عَرَبِىٍّۢ مُّبِينٍۢ ‎⟫
> *Which the trustworthy Ruh [Jibrael (Gabriel)] has brought down upon your heart (O Muhammad PBUH) that you may be (one) of the warners, in the plain Arabic language.*

Observe: Allāh says the Qur'ān was revealed upon the Prophet's heart, not upon his ears. Whatever descends upon the heart requires no words. Then He states: "So that you may make clear this Qur'ān in Arabic, and warn the people"—meaning that the matter of the Qur'ān, which was revealed to your heart as an unworded revelation, has been explained and conveyed to these worldly people in the form of words. It is well known that when revelation descended upon the Prophet (peace be upon him), he felt a great burden upon himself. He would lose consciousness, become drenched in perspiration, and enter a state of oblivion such that even if flesh were cut from his blessed body, he would perceive nothing. This is why the disbelievers and enemies called him mad—a charge which Allāh refuted:

> ⟪quran 16:60⟫
> ⟪ar:‏لِلَّذِينَ لَا يُؤْمِنُونَ بِٱلْآخِرَةِ مَثَلُ ٱلسَّوْءِ ۖ وَلِلَّهِ ٱلْمَثَلُ ٱلْأَعْلَىٰ ۚ وَهُوَ ٱلْعَزِيزُ ٱلْحَكِيمُ ‎⟫
> *For those who believe not in the Hereafter is an evil description, and for Allah is the highest description. And He is the All-Mighty, the All-Wise.*

**A Rational Argument for the Greatness of the Prophet**

Let us now turn to rational examination. In every genus, there exists one species, and within that species one thing or person of supreme nobility that encompasses the perfection of all the rest. Consider the ruby among stones: it is stone, yet distinguished by special qualities that render it the finest and noblest of all stones. Allāh has marked it with a unique color and luster through the influence of the sun. We raise the Prophets upon this luminous rank among humanity, declaring that though the Prophets are human like us, they resemble red rubies among stones—incomparable in color and form, beyond price. Allāh granted them the power of prophethood and enabled them to bear the burden of His trust. Similarly, there exists a supreme quality within each genus: the date palm among plants, the human being among animals—superior because of intellect, thought, and reason. Thus the Prophets are the noblest of mankind because of their purity of soul and spiritual nobility, and after them, their successors stand in the next rank.

**On the Alleged Alteration of the Qur'ān**

Secondly, the false doctrines and deceptions claim that the present Qur'ān is flawed, its wording altered, containing names of ʿAlī and the Imāms of Ahl al-Bayt that have been expunged—as though, like the Jews and Christians who corrupted their heavenly books, the Qur'ān too has been distorted. This belief is utterly false. The truth is that the words of the Qur'ān cannot be altered. The Qur'ān before us today is precisely the one honored by the Messenger of Allāh. However, the disbelievers in the *walāyah* (guardianship) of ʿAlī and the enemies of Ahl al-Bayt have arbitrarily twisted the meanings and exegeses of its verses, interpreting them as they please. The verses that honor the infallible Imāms ought to be made manifest to the Muslims. For example, Allāh states:

> ⟪quran 2:143⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَكَذَلِكَ جَعَلْنَكُمْ أُمَّةًۭ وَسَطًۭا لِّتَكُونُوا۟ شُهَدَآءَ عَلَى ٱلنَّاسِ وَيَكُونَ ٱلرَّسُولُ عَلَيْكُمْ شَهِيدًۭا ۚ وَمَا جَعَلْنَا ٱلْقِبْلَةَ ٱلَّتِى كُنتَ عَلَيْهَآ إِلَّا لِنَعْلَمَ مَن يَتَّبِعُ ٱلرَّسُولَ مِمَّن يَنقَلِبُ عَلَىٰ عَقِبَيْهِ ۚ وَإِن كَانَتْ لَكَبِيرَةًۭ إِلَّا عَلَى ٱلَّذِينَ هَدَى ٱللَّهُ ۗ وَمَا كَانَ ٱللَّهُ لِيُضِيعَ إِيمَنَكُمْ ۚ إِنَّ ٱللَّهَ بِٱلنَّاسِ لَرَءُوفٌۭ رَّحِيمٌۭ ‎⟫
> *Thus We have made you [true Muslims - real believers of Islamic Monotheism, true followers of Prophet Muhammad PBUH and his Sunnah (legal ways)], a Wasat (just) (and the best) nation, that you be witnesses over mankind and the Messenger (Muhammad PBUH) be a witness over you.*

In this verse, the "central community" (*ummah wasaṭ*) refers to the Imāms of Ahl al-Bayt, who are witnesses to the people. Each Imam is a witness to the people of his time, and the Prophet is a witness over all of them. Yet the enemies of Imāmate and *walāyah* have reinterpreted the verse to mean that every Muslim constitutes the central community and all Muslims are witnesses to one another. This interpretation is absurd and hollow. If every Muslim is a witness, then who are the "witnessed ones"? Should one say the witnessed are Jews, Christians, and disbelievers, the falsehood multiplies; for Islamic law binds only Muslims, not non-Muslims. The duty of witnesses is to testify whether the followers of the Messenger have kept to his path and obeyed his commands. What have they to do with Jews, Christians, and polytheists? What does it mean to testify against them?

In other words, the exegetes of the common people have altered the meaning in the very way that Allāh describes:

> ⟪quran 5:13⟫
> ⟪ar:‏يُحَرِّفُونَ ٱلْكَلِمَ عَن مَّوَاضِعِهِۦ ‎⟫
> *They change the words from their (right) places.*

Words are distorted from their proper places and displaced—*the words of the Imāms* (peace be upon them).

Similarly, the verse of *Mubāhalah* (mutual invocation of curse), which was revealed exclusively to honor the Panj Tan al-Āl (the Five of the Household) and to establish their greatness and reverence—whose witnesses were Muḥammad, ʿAlī, Fāṭimah, al-Ḥasan, and al-Ḥusayn—these exegetes have stripped of its specificity and included all people within its scope.

To summarize: while the written letters of the Qur'ān cannot be altered, profound changes have occurred in the meanings conveyed. The Qur'ān itself mentions a parallel occurrence in Satan's claim, which he confirmed:

> ⟪quran 4:119⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَلَأُضِلَّنَّهُمْ وَلَأُمَنِّيَنَّهُمْ وَلَآمُرَنَّهُمْ فَلَيُبَتِّكُنَّ آذَانَ ٱلْأَنْعَامِ وَلَآمُرَنَّهُمْ فَلَيُغَيِّرُنَّ خَلْقَ ٱللَّهِ ۚ وَمَن يَتَّخِذِ ٱلشَّيْطَنَ وَلِيًّۭا مِّن دُونِ ٱللَّهِ فَقَدْ خَسِرَ خُسْرَانًۭا مُّبِينًۭا ‎⟫
> *Verily, I will mislead them, and surely, I will arouse in them false desires; and certainly, I will order them to slit the ears of cattle, and indeed I will order them to change the nature created by Allah.*

Satan never accomplished an alteration of human bodily form, nor is he capable of doing so. Were he able, there would be an evident difference between the forms of disbelievers and believers; the disbelievers' forms would be distorted into shapes unlike those created by Allāh. Yet this has not occurred. However, Satan *has* corrupted the psychic and spiritual features of his followers, distorting their beliefs and destroying their spiritual form. Indeed, in this manner the outward words of the Qur'ān are preserved, while the meanings have been transformed.

## Was ʿAlī Unable to Attain the Caliphate?

It is our firm conviction that ʿAlī (peace be upon him) refrained from claiming the caliphate neither out of humility nor weakness, but rather out of concern that the Muslims might turn toward idolatry and Islam might advance toward ruin. The Messenger of Allāh (peace be upon him) had forewarned him of events to come, saying: "O ʿAlī! Should the people make you rulers after me, some will accept what is good; but if they disagree and follow others, leave them to their fate. Allāh will find a way out for you." Thus ʿAlī remained silent during the caliphate of the three predecessors. Yet when the people came to him in despair, unable to turn elsewhere, they humbly pressed him to accept the throne of the caliphate. At that moment, he did not hesitate or make concessions. He confronted every rebel and every transgressor. In the encounters of Jamal, Ṣiffīn, and Nahrawān, the Sword of ʿAlī (*Dhū al-Fiqār*) shone brilliantly, subduing all opposition—thus confirming the Prophet's prophecy: "O ʿAlī, you shall wage jihād against those who break their oaths, those who act unjustly, and those who deny the truth."

On one occasion, Amīr al-Mu'minīn delivered a sermon in Kūfah, calling the people to battle against Mu'āwiyah and the people of Syria. Ash'ath ibn Qays al-Kindī rose and objected: "O ʿAlī, I see you constantly rehearsing the narrative of your oppression, repeatedly mentioning how you were wronged after the death of the Messenger of Allāh. What prevented you from drawing your sword and seizing your rights? Why did you bear such injustice?"

ʿAlī replied: "O Ash'ath, I was like one upon whom a covenant was laid—my brother Aaron. When Moses appointed him caliph and departed for the Mount, the Israelites turned to idol worship, fashioning an image from the Samaritans. Aaron explained the truth to them, yet they would not listen. He remained silent. When Moses returned and witnessed this, he asked: 'What is this? Why did you not prevent them?' Aaron answered: 'I feared you would say that I had sown discord among the Children of Israel and that they had not obeyed me.' The counsel Moses gave was: 'Go and reform matters; do not follow the path of the corrupters.'

"In the same way, my brother the Messenger of Allāh made me like Aaron and said: 'If the people make you rulers without strife or discord, that is well. But if they disagree and disobey you, then remain silent, preserve bloodshed, and remain in your house. Do not cause division in my community.' I too feared that my brother the Messenger of Allāh would say to me: 'You have sown discord in the community and have not heeded my word.' That is why I held back and said nothing."

Similarly, on another occasion, when hypocrites insulted him, saying "ʿAlī refrained from demanding his right to the caliphate out of humility and weakness," he addressed them: "I have heard that some people imagine I surrendered my right to the caliphate out of humility and helplessness. Hear me! This notion is absurd and wrong. I have merely followed the will of my brother, the Holy Prophet (peace be upon him). I swear by Allāh, had I desired it, I would have obtained it. My hands are neither weak nor feeble. We are the honored servants of Allāh, and we do not disobey His commands. And even if it were granted that I lost my right through weakness, what shame or harm would there be in me imitating six great prophets?

"Noah cried out: 'O Lord, help me, for I am overcome!' Abraham said: 'I have turned away from you and your gods.' Moses said to the people: 'I fled from you because I feared you.' Aaron said: 'The people thought me weak and sought to kill me.' Lot said: 'Would that I had power over you!' And my brother, the Messenger of Allāh (peace be upon him), was himself forced to migrate from Mecca. I have merely followed their example. Then, when the Muslims themselves came to me in desperate need, seeing no alternative but me, and humbly entreated me to mount the throne of the caliphate, I accepted the caliphate as necessity demanded. Thereafter, I did not relent or yield. The people broke their covenant, committed atrocities, turned from religion, and animosity spread in their hearts. I fought all of them without retreating. Therefore, judge me not hastily. We act in accordance with the demands of the time."

## Ma'mūn al-Rashīd's Disputation with Forty Scholars

What follows is a translation of the celebrated disputation that took place between Ma'mūn al-Rashīd and Isḥāq ibn Ibrāhīm, along with forty other prominent Sunni scholars, a record preserved in the book *al-ʿAqd al-Farīd*. Ma'mūn al-Rashīd asserted that after the Messenger of Allāh, ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib (peace be upon him) was the noblest of all the Companions and the most deserving of the caliphate. Qāḍī Isḥāq and the forty jurists opposed this claim, while Ma'mūn stood alone against them all. During the debate, the veil of domination was lifted and all parties enjoyed complete freedom of speech. From morning until nearly noon, the disputation continued; yet justice demands that Ma'mūn's mastery of the field remained unshaken.

**The Opening of the Debate**

**Ma'mūn al-Rashīd:**
My conviction is that after the Holy Prophet (peace be upon him), ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib (peace be upon him) is the best of all the caliphs and the most deserving of the caliphate.

**Isḥāq:**
I do not see the ground for what you say about Ḥaḍrat ʿAlī. Since you have been invited to debate, please furnish proof of your claim.

**Ma'mūn al-Rashīd:**
Very well, Isḥāq! First, tell me: by what cause do men increase in virtue over one another?

**Isḥāq:**
By righteous deeds.

**Ma'mūn al-Rashīd:**
Precisely so. Now tell me: if a person was the best among his peers during the lifetime of the Prophet, but after the Prophet's death, other people performed more righteous deeds—such as greater jihād, Hajj, fasting, prayer, and alms—would those people thereby surpass the one who was best during the Prophet's lifetime?

Upon hearing this question, Isḥāq fell into perplexity.

**Ma'mūn al-Rashīd:**
You cannot answer "yes," Isḥāq! For then I would ask: have not those who came after had opportunity for such righteous deeds as jihād, Hajj, fasting, prayer, and charity, to a greater degree than those who came before? If so, then all of them should exceed the predecessors!

**Isḥāq:**
Indeed, no one can ever rival the one who held the highest rank during the Prophet's lifetime.

**Ma'mūn al-Rashīd:**
Now listen carefully. Consider how many *āthār* (reports) and *ḥadīth* (narrations) have come down from your Companions, the Tabi'īn (Successors), the *muhaddithūn* (Hadith scholars), and those scholars whom we regard as the leaders and guides of our faith, all testifying to the virtues of ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib. Compare these narrations with those you have concerning the virtues of Abū Bakr. If the narrations of ʿAlī's virtues equal those of Abū Bakr, then you may claim that Abū Bakr surpasses ʿAlī. But no, by Allāh! Rather, combine the virtues of Abū Bakr and ʿUmar, and then set them alongside the virtues of ʿAlī. Even if their combined virtues only equal those of ʿAlī alone, then you might claim they surpass him. But no, by Allāh! Rather, gather the virtues of Abū Bakr, ʿUmar, and ʿUthmān—combine them all—and put them against the virtues of ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib alone. Even were they equal, no more could you claim superiority. But no, by Allāh! Rather, take the virtues of the ten promised Paradise—those to whom the Prophet (peace be upon him) himself bore witness of their Paradise-worthiness—combine all their virtues together and set them beside those of ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib. They may not equal his. If they do not, abandon this false notion.[^cite-4]

Very well. Let us move past all of this. Tell me, Isḥāq: what was the noblest deed at the time the Prophet was sent forth?

**Isḥāq:**
To declare the testimony of faith with sincere heart.

**Ma'mūn al-Rashīd:**
Was it not nobler still to be foremost in embracing Islam?

**Isḥāq:**
Of course it was!

**Ma'mūn al-Rashīd:**
Allāh says in the Qur'ān:

> ⟪quran 4:1⟫ [referenced in context]

Those who were first to embrace Islam are indeed the foremost. Did you know of anyone who became Muslim before ʿAlī?

**Isḥāq:**
ʿAlī embraced Islam at a tender age, while the Messenger of Allāh could not trouble him for anything. Abū Bakr came to maturity before embracing Islam, when he was in a state of hardship.

**Ma'mūn al-Rashīd:**
First, tell me who was the first to become Muslim. Then I shall debate with you regarding the question of maturity.

**Isḥāq:**
Without doubt, ʿAlī became Muslim before Abū Bakr, though he was a child at the time.

**Ma'mūn al-Rashīd:**
It is true that ʿAlī embraced Islam while young. Now tell me: did ʿAlī become Muslim through the Prophet's invitation, or through divine inspiration?

Isḥāq grew troubled at this question and began to reflect.

**Ma'mūn al-Rashīd:**
You cannot say through inspiration, for that would make ʿAlī superior to the Prophet—for even the Messenger of Allāh had not yet been inspired. Rather, Gabriel came to him and thereby the Prophet came to know Islam.[^cite-5]

**Isḥāq:**
Certainly, ʿAlī was not inspired. It was the Prophet who invited him to Islam.

**Ma'mūn al-Rashīd:**
This conclusion cannot stand without two conditions: either the Prophet invited ʿAlī to Islam by command of Allāh, or from his own desire?

Isḥāq was bewildered.

**Ma'mūn al-Rashīd:**
You cannot accuse the Messenger of Allāh (peace be upon him) of speaking from his own caprice, for Allāh Himself declares of the Prophet that he does not speak from desire, but only as revelation comes to him:

> ⟪quran 53:4-5⟫ [paraphrased in source]

**Isḥāq:**
Indeed, the Prophet could not speak from desire. Rather, he invited ʿAlī by command of Allāh.

**Ma'mūn al-Rashīd:**
Is it permissible for Allāh to command His Messengers to invite someone to something from which a child cannot bear hardship?

**Isḥāq:**
God forbid! It is not permissible.

**Ma'mūn al-Rashīd:**
But you yourself said that ʿAlī embraced Islam as a child, when it was not permissible for him to bear hardship. Does this not mean the Prophet caused hardship to children by inviting them to Islam in something beyond their capacity to bear? If those children later apostatized after accepting the Prophet's invitation and becoming Muslim, would they not deserve punishment for apostasy? And was it permissible for the Messenger of Allāh to harm them when they were young? Can you say such a thing of the Messenger of Allāh?

**Isḥāq:**
God forbid! That is not permissible.

**Ma'mūn al-Rashīd:**
Then it is proven that the Messenger of Allāh invited ʿAlī to Islam by divine command and granted him superiority over all creatures because of this virtue, singling him out so that the people might recognize his distinction. Otherwise, had Allāh commanded the Messenger to invite children in general, the Prophet would have invited other children from his family and relatives alongside ʿAlī!

**Isḥāq:**
Of course!

**Ma'mūn al-Rashīd:**
Do you know of any other child from his family and relatives whom the Messenger of Allāh invited to Islam? I ask this so you will not suppose that the Prophet invited ʿAlī merely because he was his cousin, or that ʿAlī possessed no personal virtue deserving this distinction.

**Isḥāq:**
I do not know, so I cannot say whether the Messenger of Allāh invited another child or not!

**Ma'mūn al-Rashīd:**
Isḥāq! Do you think you will be questioned on the Day of Resurrection regarding matters you did not know and did not understand?

**Isḥāq:**
No.

**Ma'mūn al-Rashīd:**
Why, then, do you mention and dispute about matters that Allāh has kept hidden from both you and me and for which there is no cause? Very well, now tell me: what has been the noblest deed since the beginning of Islam?

**Isḥāq:**
Striving in the way of Allāh (jihād).

**Ma'mūn al-Rashīd:**
Do you find the services ʿAlī rendered in jihād among any of the Prophet's Companions?

**Isḥāq:**
In which battle?

**Ma'mūn al-Rashīd:**
In any battle whatsoever!

**Isḥāq:**
Well then, take the Battle of Badr.

**Ma'mūn al-Rashīd:**
The Battle of Badr! Are not the services of others far less than those of ʿAlī in that battle? Tell me, how many polytheists were slain in it?

**Isḥāq:**
Approximately sixty.

**Ma'mūn al-Rashīd:**
And of these, how many fell at the hand of ʿAlī alone?

**Isḥāq:**
I do not know.

**Ma'mūn al-Rashīd:**
Hear this: twenty-three fell to ʿAlī, while the rest of the Islamic army killed forty in total.[^cite-6]

**Isḥāq:**
The Holy Prophet (peace be upon him) also saw that Abū Bakr was present in this battle alongside the Messenger of Allāh in the command tent (*ʿarsḥiyyah*).

**Ma'mūn al-Rashīd:**
Yes. But what were they doing there?

**Isḥāq:**
They were devising strategy.

**Ma'mūn al-Rashīd:**
Did the Messenger of Allāh devise strategy separately from them? Or was the strategy formulated with them? If the Messenger of Allāh was present, did the Prophet take them as partners in his deliberation? Or did he act independent of their counsel? Which of these three cases applies?

**Isḥāq:**
The Messenger of Allāh did not think apart from the Ḥaḍrat, nor did he make them partners, nor was the Messenger of Allāh in need of their opinion.

**Ma'mūn al-Rashīd:**
Then, what virtue accrues from sitting in the command tent? Is not the one who fights with the sword in defense of the Messenger of Allāh better than one who sits comfortably beside him?

**Isḥāq:**
The entire army was engaged in jihād, so what distinguishes ʿAlī?

**Ma'mūn al-Rashīd:**
It is true that everyone fought. But the one who fights with the sword, protecting the Messenger of Allāh, and those who sit at ease beside him—is not the one who fights certainly superior to the one who sits? Have you not read in the Holy Qur'ān:

> ⟪quran 4:95⟫ [referenced in context]

Those who sit at home and those who strive with their lives and wealth cannot be equal. Allāh has given superiority to those who strive with their lives and wealth over those who sit at home. Though Allāh promises good to all believers, He has given the strivers a great reward over those who remained at home.[^cite-7]

**Isḥāq:**
But Abū Bakr and ʿUmar were also mujāhidūn (those engaged in jihād)!

**Ma'mūn al-Rashīd:**
Did Abū Bakr and ʿUmar have superiority over those who did not participate in that battle?

**Isḥāq:**
Of course they did!

**Ma'mūn al-Rashīd:**
In the same way, is not the one who sacrifices his life and fights superior to Abū Bakr and ʿUmar, who sat comfortably in the command tent?

**Isḥāq:**
Yes, that is also true.

**Ma'mūn al-Rashīd:**
Isḥāq! You know the Qur'ān. Recite to me from Sūrah al-Insān.

Isḥāq began to recite the verse. When he reached the relevant passage, Ma'mūn said:

**Ma'mūn al-Rashīd:**
In honor of whom was this verse revealed?

**Isḥāq:**
In honor of ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib.

**Ma'mūn al-Rashīd:**
Do you know that when ʿAlī fed the needy, the orphan, and the captive, did he say: "We feed you only for the sake of Allāh"? Have you heard that Allāh praised ʿAlī as He has praised anyone else?

**Isḥāq:**
ʿAlī did not say "We feed you for the sake of Allāh," nor has any such praise been revealed in the Qur'ān concerning anyone!

**Ma'mūn al-Rashīd:**
Indeed! And the reason is that Allāh Himself knew the character of ʿAlī. Well, now tell me: do you testify that ten Companions are promised Paradise?

**Isḥāq:**
I do testify.

**Ma'mūn al-Rashīd:**
If someone says, "I do not know whether this *ḥadīth* is authentic or whether the Messenger of Allāh said it," does that make him a disbeliever?

**Isḥāq:**
God forbid, it does not.

**Ma'mūn al-Rashīd:**
But if that same person says, "I do not know whether Sūrah al-Insān is the word of Allāh," does he become a disbeliever?

**Isḥāq:**
Yes, he becomes a disbeliever.

**Ma'mūn al-Rashīd:**
Explain the difference between these two statements! And now, Isḥāq, do you also transmit *ḥadīth*?

**Isḥāq:**
Yes.

**Ma'mūn al-Rashīd:**
If you know it, tell me the ḥadīth of the bird (*tayr*).

**Isḥāq:**
I narrate the ḥadīth of the bird: A roasted bird was presented to the Messenger of Allāh (peace be upon him). At that moment, he prayed: "O Allāh, send to me the one whom You love most, so that he may join me in eating this bird." Then ʿAlī came, and the two of them ate the bird together.

**Ma'mūn al-Rashīd:**
Isḥāq, until now I debated with you thinking you are not an enemy of truth, but now your enmity with truth is clear! Are you certain this *ḥadīth* is authentic?

**Isḥāq:**
Yes, this *ḥadīth* is *ṣaḥīḥ* (authentic) because it is narrated by trustworthy scholars whose reports cannot be rejected.[^cite-8]

**Ma'mūn al-Rashīd:**
Do you understand that if someone believes this *ḥadīth* is authentic and still maintains that there was a person in the community better than Imam ʿAlī (peace be upon him), he must hold one of three beliefs: Either Allāh did not answer the prayer of the Messenger of Allāh (meaning He did not send the one He loved most), or that even though ʿAlī was the best, Allāh loved him most (but that would contradict your position), or that Allāh did not distinguish between the best and the superior. Since the Messenger's prayer was answered and Allāh sent ʿAlī, which of these three positions do you maintain?

Isḥāq was deeply disturbed and could not respond.

**Ma'mūn al-Rashīd:**
Surely you cannot maintain any of these three! Otherwise, I would refute you. If there is a fourth interpretation, please state it.

**Isḥāq:**
I am humbled in my answer, but Abū Bakr also possesses virtue.

**Ma'mūn al-Rashīd:**
Indeed, if Abū Bakr had no virtue, it would be pointless to say ʿAlī is superior to him, for superiority necessarily implies greater virtue. But what virtue did you recall at that time?

**Isḥāq:**
The verse of the cave, wherein it is said:

> ⟪quran 9:40⟫ [referenced in context]

Allāh calls Abū Bakr a companion of the Messenger of Allāh!

**Ma'mūn al-Rashīd:**
Allāh has indeed called Abū Bakr a companion of the Ḥaḍrat, but Allāh has also called a disbeliever a companion of one with whom Allāh was pleased and who was pleased with Allāh. Allāh states:

> ⟪quran 18:37⟫ [referenced in context]

His companion, who was conversing with him, said: "Do you disbelieve in your Lord who created you from dust and then from a sperm-drop?"

**Isḥāq:**
But that companion was a disbeliever, while Abū Bakr was a believer!

**Ma'mūn al-Rashīd:**
Exactly my point! When Allāh calls a disbeliever the companion of one whom He pleased and who pleased Him, it is certainly possible that He would call a believer a companion of His Prophet. But does companionship alone make one the best of believers?

**Isḥāq:**
Indeed! This verse is very significant for Abū Bakr's case, for Allāh says: "the second of the two." When Abū Bakr and the Messenger of Allāh were in the cave, the Prophet said to him: "Do not grieve; indeed, Allāh is with us."

**Ma'mūn al-Rashīd:**
Isḥāq, tell me: was the cause of Abū Bakr's grief joy or sorrow?

**Isḥāq:**
It was for the sake of the Messenger of Allāh (peace be upon him). Abū Bakr feared that some calamity might befall the Prophet.

**Ma'mūn al-Rashīd:**
That does not answer my question. What was the source of that grief—joy or anger?

**Isḥāq:**
It was the pleasure of Allāh!

**Ma'mūn al-Rashīd:**
Then it has become clear that Allāh sent us a Messenger who forbade people from the pleasure of Allāh!

**Isḥāq:**
God forbid! That is impossible!

**Ma'mūn al-Rashīd:**
Did you not just say that Abū Bakr's grief was the pleasure of Allāh?

**Isḥāq:**
Of course I said so.

**Ma'mūn al-Rashīd:**
Does the Qur'ān not contain the forbiddance of the Messenger to grieve, when he said: "Do not grieve"? In this way, did the Messenger forbid the pleasure of Allāh or not?

**Isḥāq:**
What an error I have made!

**Ma'mūn al-Rashīd:**
Now tell me what Allāh the Almighty said:

> ⟪quran 9:40⟫ [referenced in context, alternate phrasing]

When it says "Allāh helped them," does this refer to the Messenger of Allāh or to Abū Bakr?

**Isḥāq:**
No, it must refer to the Messenger of Allāh.

**Ma'mūn al-Rashīd:**
Now consider this verse:

> ⟪quran 9:25-26⟫ [referenced in context, paraphrased]

"Allāh helped you at Ḥunayn, when your numbers made you proud, yet the earth became narrow for you despite its vastness. Then you turned back in flight. But Allāh sent down His peace upon His Messenger and upon the believers."

In this verse, whom does "the believers" refer to?

**Isḥāq:**
I do not know.

**Ma'mūn al-Rashīd:**
At the Battle of Ḥunayn, all the Muslims fled. There remained with the Messenger of Allāh (peace be upon him) only seven men from Banū Hāshim. Among them, ʿAlī was fighting with his sword in the Prophet's defense. ʿAbbās held the reins of the Prophet's mule, and the other five surrounded him to prevent the polytheists from harming him. This continued until Allāh granted victory to His Messenger (peace be upon him). Thus, "the believers" in this verse specifically refer to ʿAlī and those of Banū Hāshim who stood with him at that critical hour. Now tell me: were those who stood with the Messenger in that moment of trial superior, or those who fled in panic—including Abū Bakr and ʿUmar—seeking to save their own lives? Allāh found no comfort was necessary for those who fled.

**Isḥāq:**
No, the superior one is the one upon whom Allāh sent down His peace and consolation.

**Ma'mūn al-Rashīd:**
Which is superior: the one who was in the cave with the Messenger of Allāh, or the one who slept upon the bed of the Messenger and, by risking his life unto death, saved the life of the Prophet so that the Hijrah could be completed? For Allāh commanded the Messenger of Allāh to ask ʿAlī to sleep upon his bed. Thus the Messenger said to ʿAlī [and he wept]. The Prophet asked: "O ʿAlī, do you weep out of fear of death?" ʿAlī replied: "By Allāh, I do not weep for that, but because I think you may not be in safety. If I sleep on your bed, will your life be saved?" The Messenger answered: "Yes, you will be safe." Then ʿAlī was gladdened and said: "O Messenger of Allāh, what matter is your safety to me? I shall sleep in your place with great joy and shall gladly give my life to save you!" That night, he wrapped the Prophet's cloak around himself and lay upon his bed. Soon the polytheists of Quraysh came and surrounded him. Thinking he was the Messenger, they plotted that each tribe of Quraysh would strike a single blow with the sword, so that Banū Hāshim would not be able to seek vengeance for a single killer. Yet ʿAlī, listening to all the advice of these polytheists, seeing them all prepared to take his life, felt no fear whatsoever. He slept peacefully, prepared to die. By contrast, Abū Bakr was terrified in the cave and wept.

When ʿAlī awoke in the morning, the Qurayshī polytheists looked at him and asked: "Where is Muḥammad?" ʿAlī replied: "What do I know?" They said: "ʿAlī, it seems you have been an enemy to yourself this night!" Thus, in every event until the death of the Messenger of Allāh (peace be upon him), ʿAlī proved superior to the people, and there was never an occasion when he was inferior to anyone in virtue. So tell me, Isḥāq, the *ḥadīth* of Ghadir!

**Isḥāq:**
Isḥāq narrated the *ḥadīth* of Ghadir...

**Ma'mūn al-Rashīd:**
Isḥāq! Observe that this *ḥadīth* obliged Abū Bakr and ʿUmar in a way it did not obligate ʿAlī—that is, obedience to ʿAlī was made obligatory for Abū Bakr and ʿUmar, but it was not obligatory for ʿAlī to obey both of them.[^cite-9]

**Isḥāq:**
People say that the *ḥadīth* of Ghadir was about Zayd ibn al-Ḥārith, because there were differences between him and ʿAlī, and he denied the *walāyah* (guardianship) of ʿAlī. The Messenger of Allāh said...

**Ma'mūn al-Rashīd:**
At what point was this *ḥadīth* spoken? Was it not at Ghadir Khumm on the return journey from the Farewell Pilgrimage?

**Isḥāq:**
Yes, it was spoken on that occasion.

**Ma'mūn al-Rashīd:**
But Zayd ibn al-Ḥārith was martyred before the Battle of Muʾtah, which preceded the Farewell Pilgrimage and Ghadir Khumm by years. How can you attribute the *ḥadīth* to him? Very well, now listen: if one of your sons, a mere five-year-old, were to say, "Whoever is my master is also the master of my son," would this not strike you as absurd—something your son should not be saying, something all the people already know and do not contest?

**Isḥāq:**
Yes, it would seem improper.

**Ma'mūn al-Rashīd:**
Then how can you accept for the Messenger of Allāh what you would reject for your own son? Woe to you! Have you not heard this *ḥadīth* from the Messenger of Allāh (peace be upon him): "O ʿAlī, you are to me as Aaron was to Moses"?

**Isḥāq:**
Yes, I have heard this *ḥadīth*.

**Ma'mūn al-Rashīd:**
Is it possible that the Messenger of Allāh spoke this *ḥadīth* in jest?

**Isḥāq:**
God forbid, not at all!

**Ma'mūn al-Rashīd:**
Did you say something that lacks sense, or something that no one understands?

**Isḥāq:**
God forbid, neither of those!

**Ma'mūn al-Rashīd:**
Do you not know that Aaron was the true brother of Moses?

**Isḥāq:**
Yes, I know that.

**Ma'mūn al-Rashīd:**
And Aaron was a prophet, while ʿAlī was not a prophet?

**Isḥāq:**
Yes, that is correct.

**Ma'mūn al-Rashīd:**
Then ʿAlī was neither the true brother nor a prophet of the Messenger, whereas Aaron possessed both these attributes. What, then, is the meaning of this statement of the Prophet?

**Isḥāq:**
The Prophet intended to please ʿAlī because the hypocrites had said that the Prophet left ʿAlī in Medina at the time of the Battle of Tabūk because he harbored resentment toward him.

**Ma'mūn al-Rashīd:**
So you are saying the Messenger of Allāh sought to please ʿAlī through a falsehood?

Isḥāq was greatly troubled at this question and could not reply.

**Ma'mūn al-Rashīd:**
Isḥāq, the meaning of this analogy is manifest in the Holy Qur'ān itself.

**Isḥāq:**
Master, what is that?

**Ma'mūn al-Rashīd:**
In the words of Prophet Moses, Allāh states:

> ⟪quran 20:29-32⟫ [referenced in context]

"And Moses said to his brother Aaron: 'You are my heirs among my people. Reform them and do not follow the path of those who make mischief.'"

**Isḥāq:**
Indeed! Moses made Aaron the caliph of his people when he departed for Mount Ṭūr by command of Allāh. In the same way, the Prophet fought the Battle of Tabūk during his lifetime.

**Ma'mūn al-Rashīd:**
Tell me: when Moses appointed Aaron as caliph, did any of his companions or any of the Israelites go with him?

**Isḥāq:**
No, no one went with him. He went to the mountain alone.

**Ma'mūn al-Rashīd:**
And was Aaron made caliph over a few individuals or over the entire nation?

**Isḥāq:**
Over the entire nation.

**Ma'mūn al-Rashīd:**
But when the Messenger of Allāh went to the Battle of Tabūk, he did not leave in Medina anyone except the elderly, the sick, women, and children. He took all the able-bodied with him. How then is this like what Moses did? The truth is that Allāh said to Moses:

> ⟪quran 20:32⟫ [referenced in context]

"O Allāh, make my brother Aaron my vizier from my household. Strengthen my back through him and make him a partner in my work, so that together we may glorify You greatly."

Similarly, the Messenger of Allāh said: "ʿAlī, you are as Aaron was to Moses." In other words: "You are my vizier, my brother, he through whom Allāh has strengthened my back, whom He has made my partner in my work, so that we together may glorify Him greatly." Now, is it possible for anyone to interpret this *ḥadīth* in any other way without invalidating the Prophet's word or rendering it false or absurd?

---

The author of *al-ʿAqd al-Farīd* records that the debate continued in this manner until it became very lengthy, and when noon approached, Isḥāq declared: "You have presented arguments so clear and established your claim through proofs that no one can refute them." Hearing this, Ma'mūn al-Rashīd prayed: "Lord, I have offered them good counsel and fulfilled the obligation of commanding the good. O Lord, I seek nearness to You through love for ʿAlī and devotion to his *walāyah* (guardianship), and I am a follower of this faith."

## Maulana Muhammad Baqir and the Haruri Debate

This celebrated disputation dates to the time of Maulana Imam Muhammad Baqir (peace be upon him). A prominent interlocutor named Haruri approached him with a direct challenge: "I have come to argue with you concerning whether Hazrat Abu Bakr truly deserved the Caliphate." The Imam responded with characteristic openness: "Present your case freely; I am prepared to address every question you raise." Haruri then enumerated four distinguishing merits he attributed to Abu Bakr, each of which he claimed, alone among the Companions, elevated Abu Bakr above all others and thus justified his succession to the Prophet.

| Quality | Claim |
| --- | --- |
| 1 | He was first to embrace faith |
| 2 | He accompanied the Messenger of Allah in the cave |
| 3 | He led the prayer during the Prophet's lifetime |
| 4 | He was buried adjacent to the Prophet's tomb |

Upon hearing these assertions, Imam Muhammad Baqir posed a penetrating question: "O Haruri, are you recounting their virtues or their defects?" Haruri, startled by the inversion, protested: "O Imam, surely you do not maintain that all four constitute faults?" The Imam replied with conviction: "Indeed I do. I shall demonstrate that each represents a grave defect in your argument."

### Abu Bakr's Precedence in Belief

The Imam began his rebuttal: "Did not ʿAmir al-Muʾminīn (the Commander of the Faithful) embrace Islam before Abu Bakr?" Haruri conceded: "Certainly, O Imam. Yet ʿAlī was but a youth, scarcely beyond childhood." The Imam's response struck at the heart of the matter: "Even granting that ʿAlī was a minor—was not the Messenger of Allah himself fully mature? How, then, did you accept the Islam of ʿAlī while simultaneously crediting Abu Bakr with precedence in belief? By what logic does the faith of a mature prophet carry less weight than that of a companion?" Haruri fell silent, his argument dissolving under this logical force.[^cite-10]

### The Cave and the Descent of Tranquility

The Imam next addressed the claim of Abu Bakr's companionship in the cave. "You assert," he said, "that Abu Bakr entered the cave as a willing companion to the Messenger of Allah. Yet do you not know that when the Prophet was commanded to emigrate, Abu Bakr compelled him to purchase a swift camel at ten times its market price? Abu Bakr was not summoned; he attached himself from self-interest. That night, when they sheltered in the cave, Abu Bakr wept in terror of the disbelievers. Allah Himself addressed this moment in revelation:

> ⟪quran 9:40⟫

Observe carefully: though two were present in the cave, when Allah describes the revelation of the Scrolls, He mentions only one—*We sent down the Scrolls upon him.* Scripture descended upon the Prophet alone, not upon Abu Bakr. This silence is eloquent."[^cite-11]

The Imam then posed another question: "Do you comprehend what *tranquility* (al-sakīna, divine peace) signifies?" He continued:

> ⟪quran 9:26⟫

"Allah declares that He sends down His tranquility only upon His Messenger or upon the believers. In that very verse of the cave, where stood only the Messenger and Abu Bakr, Allah bestowed tranquility upon the Messenger—but not upon Abu Bakr. By divine justice, were Abu Bakr a believer, would not tranquility have descended upon him equally? Yet Allah excluded him from this circle of faith. How then could He have chosen one excluded from faith to be His Successor?"[^cite-12]

### The Prayer Led by Abu Bakr

When Haruri cited the occasion on which Abu Bakr led the prayer, explaining that the Prophet had become ill and Aʿisha requested her father to conduct the Maghrib prayer, the Imam asked: "Tell me—when the Prophet arose with difficulty and removed Abu Bakr from the prayer leader's position, assuming it himself, did this removal constitute a defect in the prayer itself?" Haruri replied that it did not. The Imam pressed further: "Then this removal reveals something else. When the Prophet displaced Abu Bakr, was Abu Bakr not leading the prayer to the general congregation, and the Prophet to Abu Bakr alone?" 

"Perhaps," Haruri ventured.

"Have you ever witnessed," the Imam asked, "a prayer in which a single congregation has two prayer leaders—one leading the other, and that one leading the rest? Such a thing is impossible. Your foremost Caliph was neither truly a leader nor truly a follower; he was an anomaly displaced by the very Prophet who, in your account, chose him."[^cite-13]

### Burial in the Prophet's House

Finally, the Imam addressed the burial claim. "O Haruri, is it not the command of Allah:

> ⟪quran 24:27⟫

Does not Allah forbid entry into any house without permission? Yet Abu Bakr was placed in the Prophet's house. From whom was permission sought? From the Prophet himself, who lay dead? Haruri suggested that Aʿisha's ownership of the chamber granted sufficient permission. The Imam inquired: "How many wives had the Messenger of Allah?" "Nine," replied Haruri. "And what share does each wife inherit?" "One-eighth," Haruri answered. The Imam concluded: "If you apportion one-eighth of Aʿisha's inheritance—one-eighth of one-ninth of the house—there remains no space whatsoever for even a simple grave. How, then, could your first Caliph occupy two yards of burial space in a chamber where neither inherited title nor explicit permission existed? He was placed there by usurpation of the Prophet's sanctuary itself."[^cite-14]

When the Imam finished, all present declared: "O Imam, it is impossible to prevail against you."
