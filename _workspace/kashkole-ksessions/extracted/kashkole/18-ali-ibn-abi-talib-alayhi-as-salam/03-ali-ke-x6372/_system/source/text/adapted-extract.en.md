## Letter to Mālik al-Ashtar

⟪ar:مولانا علی ابن ابی طالب علیہ السلام⟫ sent this letter to ⟪ar:مالک اشتر⟫ when he appointed him governor of Egypt and dispatched him to Kūfah.

O Lord! The governor of Egypt must subdue his own appetites and master them through the sword of gratitude; for the soul (nafs) commands toward evil until the merciful servant of God draws near to the weak.[^cite-1] Master! I send you to a land where former rulers have wielded authority before you. They shall scrutinize your conduct as you yourself weigh the deeds of your predecessors. Let righteous action be your most treasured legacy. Restrain your desires. Take care lest you expend your soul in that which is unlawful—such restraint is justice toward yourself. Make benevolence and affection toward your subjects the mark upon your heart. Do not be to them as a wild beast, knowing only how to rend and tear.

There are two categories of people: either they are your brothers in religion, or they are God's creatures even as you are. They shall stumble into error. Forgive them as you would wish your Lord to forgive your transgressions.[^cite-2] Indeed, when he conceals the faults of his subjects, his own Lord shall conceal his faults more fully. Therefore, hide their misdeeds insofar as you are able, that your own sins may be veiled.

## Letter Concerning the Parable of the World

The world's likeness is a serpent—smooth to the touch, yet brimming with poison that brings death.[^cite-3] Turn away from the things of this world that you love; for scarcely anything in this world shall accompany you. Cleanse your heart of worldly desires even as you renounce the world itself. Beware the harm of infatuation with this fleeting existence. Whoever becomes absorbed in the desire of the world—the world casts him into hardship, transforms his longings into dread and terror.

Do you not see how the conditions of those bound to the world shift between dawn and dusk? One dies and people weep over him. Another lives yet sickens—no respite for him. One seeks the world while death pursues him. The ignorant man dwells in heedlessness, unaware that those who reckon with him do not themselves slumber. And those who came before follow the footsteps of the ancients.

Everyone in this world is consumed by hope for the coming day, desiring that tomorrow arrive swiftly so that his circumstances improve. He gives no thought to time's passage, to the shortening of his lifespan. Not a single leaf, not even a thorn in the garden of the world, exists without purpose. Even the basest of people has been created for some end—though you may not discern that mystery.

*(Compiled by: Late Haj Mullāh Ahmad Khurshīd Ḥasan)*

*(Your gifts sent to the believers on the day of ʿĪd)*

## Letter to Muʿāwiya: The Epistle Without Diacritical Points

Amīr al-Muʾminīn ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib composed the grammar of the Arabic language. In former times, Arabic letters bore no dots. He bestowed points upon all letters and established their rules.[^cite-4]

On one occasion, the Amīr al-Muʾminīn wrote an epistle to Muʿāwiya, yet he removed all dots from the letters and stripped away every diacritical mark. The marvel of this message lay in this: without dots, all its letters became indistinguishable. He thus wrote to Muʿāwiya:
