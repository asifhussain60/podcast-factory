## Letter to Malik al-Ashtar

Amīr al-Mu'minīn ⟪ar:مولانا علی ابن ابی طالب علیہ السلام⟫ addressed this letter to ⟪ar:مالک اشتر⟫ when he appointed him governor of Egypt and dispatched him to Kufa. The letter stands as a foundational text on just governance, wherein the Commander of the Faithful instructs his viceroy in the disciplines of rule tempered by mercy and restraint.

O Governor! A ruler of Egypt must first conquer his own appetites, subduing them through gratitude as one wields a sword in battle. The soul (nafs) commands toward evil until the merciful servant of God has joined the weak in humility. Beware: I am sending you to a province where many rulers before you have wielded authority. They will scrutinize your conduct as you yourself examine the deeds of your predecessors. Let righteous action be your most cherished treasury; guard against the spending of your soul on that which lies outside the bounds of the permissible. Such restraint is itself justice. Let love and affection for your subjects become the ornament of your heart, that you may not appear to them as a ravening beast, knowing only to tear them asunder.[^cite-1]

Know that there exist two categories of people: either they are your brothers in faith, or they are God's creatures even as you are. All are subject to error and transgression. Forgive them as you would wish the Lord to forgive your own sins. When the ruler conceals the faults of his subjects, his own guardian suffers punishment less severe than it otherwise would be. Therefore, conceal their sins to the utmost of your ability, that your own transgressions may likewise be veiled.[^cite-2]

## Letter to Salman al-Farisi

The parable of the world (dunyā) resembles a serpent: smooth and pleasant to the touch, yet filled with a venom that brings death to those who are deceived by its surface. Turn away from those things of this world to which you are inclined, knowing that few worldly possessions shall depart with you when death arrives. Keep at distance the desires of the world, yet do so while acknowledging that you have truly abandoned it. Fear the harm that springs from love of the world, for whoever becomes absorbed in worldly desire finds that the world casts him into hardship, transforming his longings into fear and dread.[^cite-3]

Do you not perceive how the conditions of worldly people shift from dawn to dusk? One dies, and people mourn. Another lives, yet sickness afflicts him without respite. Someone pursues the world while death pursues him. The heedless person dwells in negligence, unaware that those who take account of him remain ever vigilant. The ancients have passed away, and those who come after them follow in their footsteps.

All who inhabit the world are absorbed in hope for the coming day, wishing tomorrow to arrive so that their fortune might improve. Yet they care not for the passage of time or the shortening of their span of life. Not even a leaf or thorn in the garden of the world serves without purpose. Even the most contemptible human has been fashioned for some end, though you may not comprehend this secret. Everyone dreams of tomorrow, yet none can guarantee it shall come. The fool desires what lies ahead; the wise remembers those who have already passed.[^cite-4]

## Letter to Muawiyah

Amīr al-Mu'minīn ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib composed the rules of Arabic grammar. In former times, the Arabic alphabet possessed no dots upon its letters. It was he who added points to each letter and established their grammatical principles, creating a system of orthography that brought order to the language.

Upon one occasion, the Amīr al-Mu'minīn composed a message to Muʿāwiyah and inscribed it entirely without dots, removing all diacritical marks. The extraordinary power of this letter lay in the fact that without points, every letter appeared identical to every other. Thus he wrote to Muʿāwiyah:
