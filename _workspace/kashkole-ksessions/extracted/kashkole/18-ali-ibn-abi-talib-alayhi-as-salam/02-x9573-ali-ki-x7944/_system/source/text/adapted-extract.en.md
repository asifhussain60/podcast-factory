## Ḍirār ibn Ḍamra's Recollection of ʿAlī

Abū Ṣāliḥ narrates that the Amīr Muʿāwiya once turned to Ḍirār ibn Ḍamra and asked him to describe Ḥaḍrat ʿAlī (peace be upon him). Ḍirār begged to be excused, pleading that the task lay beyond him. Muʿāwiya pressed his request a second time, and a third, until at last Ḍirār relented and said, "Then listen."

"His gaze was always far-reaching, his frame uncommonly strong. His speech was lucid and unhedged, and his judgements were rendered with perfect justice and fairness. Springs of wisdom welled up from his very being. He kept himself estranged from the world and all its entanglements, and he preferred the company of nights and their darkness. By God as my witness, in those vigils he could not contain his tears; he would sink into long contemplation, turning his hands over in thought, speaking softly to himself. He wore coarse and heavy garments and ate plain, unflavoured food. By God, with his companions he was easy and free: when questioned, he answered; when he came among them, he opened the conversation; when invited, he kept his word and came. Yet for all our nearness and ease with him, we could not summon the courage to begin a topic in his presence. When he smiled, his teeth shone like a string of pearls. He revered the pious, loved the needy, and no powerful man could expect his backing in a wrongful cause, nor did any weak soul ever turn from his justice empty-handed.

"And I bear God to witness that I have seen him on many nights, when the darkness had drawn its veils across the sky and the stars had vanished from the horizon — I saw ʿAlī (peace be upon him) clutching his beard beneath the miḥrāb of the mosque, weeping like one bereft, bent over as though a venomous serpent or scorpion had struck him. I can still hear him crying out, 'O world, do you tempt me, or do you hope for something from me? Look elsewhere for your deceit; I have divorced you thrice, and there is no returning. Your span is short, your rewards are mean, your perils are sharp and severe. How meagre are the provisions, how long the road, how desolate the way!'"[^cite-1]

The narrator says that Muʿāwiya wept until the tears flowed down into his beard and his voice failed him. At length he said, "May God have mercy upon Abū al-Ḥasan — he was indeed as you describe. And tell me, Ḍirār, how do you bear his loss?" Ḍirār replied, "My mourning is that of a mother whose child was slaughtered in her very arms: her grief is neither restrained nor lessened by time."

## Faḍāʾil ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib (the Virtues of ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib)

The Prophet (peace and blessings be upon him) said of his brother and successor ⟪ar:امیر المؤمنین علی ابن ابی طالب علیہ السلام⟫: ⟪ar-quote:فَضَاءِلٌ لاَ تَحْصٰی کَثْرَۃ⟫ — "ʿAlī's virtues are too many to be counted." He further said:

⟪ar-quote:فَمَنْ ذَکَرَ فَضِیْلَۃَ مِنْ فَضَاءِلِہِ مُقِرَّاً بِھَا غَفَرَ اللّٰہُ لَہُ مَا تَقَدَّمَ مِنْ ذَنْبِہِ وَماَ تَاخَّرَ⟫
"Whoever acknowledges one of his virtues, God will forgive him his prior and his later sins."

⟪ar-quote:وَمَنْ کَتَبَ فَضِیْلَۃِ مِنْ فَضَاءِلِہِ لَمْ تَزَلْ المَلاَءِکَۃُ تَسْتَغفِرُ لَہُ مَا بَقَی لِذَالِکَ الْکِتَابَۃَ رَسَمٌ⟫
"And whoever inscribes any of his virtues, the angels will not cease to seek forgiveness for him so long as any trace of that writing endures."

⟪ar-quote:وَمَنْ اَسْتَمَعَ اِلیٰ فَضِیلَۃِ مِن فَضَاءِلِہِ غَفَرَتْ لَہُ ذُنُوبُہُ الَّتِی اِکْتَسَبَھَا بِالسَّمَاعِ⟫
"And whoever listens to one of his virtues, God will forgive him all the sins his hearing has earned."

⟪ar-quote:وَمَنْ نَظَرَ الیٰ فَضِیْلَۃِ مِنْ فَضَاءِلِہِ غَفَرَتْ لَہُ ذُنُوبُہُ اَلَّتِی اکْتَسَبَھَا بِالنَّظَرِ⟫
"And whoever beholds one of his virtues, God will forgive him all the sins his sight has earned."

⟪ar-quote:لَوْکَانَ الْغَیَاضَ اَقلامٌ وَالبَحْرُ مِدَادٌ وَالْجِنُّ حِسَابٌ وَلاِنْسُ کِتَابٌ مَا اَحْصُوْا فَضَاءِلُ اَمِیْرِ المُؤمِنِیْنَ عَلِی اِبْنِ اَبِی طَالِب⟫
"Even were the forests turned into pens, the sea into ink, the jinn into reckoners, and mankind into scribes, they could not exhaust the virtues of the Amīr al-Muʾminīn (the Commander of the Faithful), ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib."[^cite-2]

### ʿAlī's Face

⟪ar:اللہ عزوجل⟫ — God, mighty and majestic — blessed the noblest of His servants, ⟪ar:محمد مصطفی صلی اللہ علیہ وسلم⟫, the chosen of ⟪ar:رب العٰلمین⟫ (the Lord of the Worlds), with the lofty titles of ⟪ar:رحمت للعالمین و سید المرسلین⟫ (mercy unto the worlds and master of the messengers). The Messenger of God (peace be upon him and his family) declared, "ʿAlī's relation to me is as the head to the body": were the head severed from the body, the human being would perish, for it is the head by which a person is known, the very gateway of recognition. Alluding to this, he said: ⟪ar-quote:اَنَا مَدِینَۃُ اَلعِلمِ وَعَلِیٌّ بَابُھاَ⟫ — "I am the city of knowledge, and ʿAlī is its gate." Whoever seeks knowledge, let him knock at ʿAlī's door.

The face is the most noble and beautiful of the bodily organs, and where the being is exalted, the perfections gathered in the face are correspondingly exalted. The loftiest of prophets and messengers is the Holy Prophet (peace be upon him); how lofty, then, must be the one whom such a man calls his very face! The Messenger of God said:

⟪ar-quote:مَنْ اَرَادَ اَن یَّنظُرُ اِلَی آدَمَ فِی عِلْمِہِ وَ اِلَی نُوْحِ فِی التَّقوٰی وَ اِلَی اِبْرَاھِیْمَ فِی خُلَّتِۃِ وَاِلَی مُوْسٰی فِی ھَیْبَۃِ وَ اِلَی عِیسٰی فِی زُھدِہِ

وَاِلَی سُلَیماَنَ فِی حِکمَتِہِ وَالأَیُّوبِ فِی الصَّبرِہِ فَالیَنْظُر اِلَی وَجْہِ عَلِی اِبْنِ اَبِی طَالِبْ⟫
"Whoever wishes to behold Adam in his knowledge, Noah in his godliness, Abraham in his intimacy with God, Moses in his awe-inspiring presence, Jesus in his asceticism, Solomon in his wisdom, and Job in his patient endurance — let him gaze upon the face of ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib."[^cite-3]

This saying of the Prophet declares plainly that the qualities scattered through the one hundred and twenty-four thousand prophets are gathered together in a single human countenance. It is for this reason that the Prophet directed the faithful to look upon ʿAlī's face, for the face is the most manifest part of a person. He also said: "Looking upon ʿAlī's face is an act of worship." ʿAlī's love consumes sins as fire purges the dross from iron.

It is related on the authority of Jābir ibn ʿAbd Allāh al-Anṣārī (may God be pleased with him) that a dispute once arose among the people as to which of the Companions stood nearest to the Messenger of God. One party held that ⟪ar:’ابو بکر‘⟫ was nearest, on account of the great favours he had shown the Prophet and his companionship in the cave. Another insisted upon ⟪ar:’عمر ابن خطاب‘⟫, citing his place as the Prophet's father-in-law and the awe in which he was held. A third championed ⟪ar:’عثمان بن افعان‘⟫, the Prophet's son-in-law and a man of great wealth. A fourth group declined to name any of them, saying instead, "It is ⟪ar:’علی ابن ابی طالب‘⟫." When the Messenger of God learned of the dispute, he summoned the four parties and questioned them. Each confessed to its position. ⟪ar:اسامہ بن زید⟫ and others then rose and said, "O Messenger of God, settle this matter today with your own blessed tongue, that the argument may be ended and the truth made plain."

The Prophet said, "Gather yourselves tomorrow. I shall give you a parable that will scour the rust from your hearts, and you will yourselves understand the right of the matter, with no need for me to declare it." The people dispersed, and on the morrow a great throng assembled. The Messenger came forth and said, "Tell me what you wish," and they renewed their request of the day before.

He then said: "Let those of you who are nearest to me in lineage and kinship seat themselves on my right." At that, the Imām ʿAlī (peace be upon him) took his place immediately beside the Prophet, while ⟪ar:ابوبکر و عمر⟫ sat in their order — the former from the tribe of ⟪ar:بنی عُدَی⟫, the latter from ⟪ar:تمیم⟫. ⟪ar:عثمان⟫ sat closer to the Messenger than they did, since he was of ⟪ar:عبد مناف⟫. The Prophet said, "Do you now understand? Behold, this is your standing with me in kinship." Then he said, "Stand again, and seat yourselves in the order of ⟪ar:’سباقیت فی الایمان‘⟫ — precedence in faith." Ḥaḍrat ʿAlī again took his place beside the Prophet, while ⟪ar:عثمان⟫'s rank fell last. He said next, "Now seat yourselves by the order of who is the most learned among you." Once more ʿAlī sat at the Prophet's side, while the others were rearranged. He said again, "Now seat yourselves in the order of who is the bravest — the ⟪ar:شجیع⟫ — among you." Again ʿAlī took his place beside him, and the others shifted their order.

Then, turning to ʿAlī, he said, "O ʿAlī, only the people of knowledge will grasp this parable; the wrongdoers will never accept it." Turning then to the people, he said: ⟪ar-quote:یا أیھا الناس!  أول من آمن، علی ابن ابی طالب⟫ — "O people! The first to believe is ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib."

A telling observation: the phrases ⟪ar:أول من آمن⟫ and ⟪ar:علی ابن ابی طالب⟫ each carry an abjad value of 218 — a numerical correspondence noted three times for emphasis.

The Prophet then continued: "ʿAlī is the nearest to me among my kin, the most learned in religion, the one who strikes the enemies of God with his sword. He is my brother, my ⟪ar:ابن عم⟫ (paternal cousin), and after me my successor. If he strikes, the hand is mine; if he fights, the sword is mine. Only the believer will love him, and only the disbeliever will hate him. His closeness to me in this world will be his closeness to me in the hereafter. The ⟪ar:لِواءُ الحمد⟫ (the Banner of Praise) will be in ʿAlī's hand, and he will be the ⟪ar:قسیم النار والجنۃ⟫ (the divider of the Fire and the Garden)." ⟪ar:علی حبہ جنۃ قسیم النار والجنۃ وصی مصطفی حق امام الانس والجنۃ⟫[^cite-4]

## The Glad Tidings of ʿAlī in Sūrat al-Kawthar

It is related that when God informed the Messenger of God (peace be upon him and his family) of the oppression that would befall his progeny at the hands of the Umayyads, and that unworthy men would seat themselves upon his pulpit, he was overcome with sorrow and grief. At that moment the Most High revealed to him ⟪ar:سورۃ الکوثر⟫, with the glad tidings that We have granted you ⟪ar:’کوثر‘⟫ — abundance. We say: since the affliction concerned his progeny, the consolation must likewise concern his progeny. And since his descendants would continue until the Day of Judgement through his appointed successor, the glad tidings of ⟪ar:’علی‘⟫ must necessarily refer to that successor among the Companions, and not to the others. How may it be demonstrated that this annunciation is for ʿAlī alone — that ⟪ar:کوثر⟫ refers to the one whom you yourself bestowed upon the Prophet? The proof lies in rearranging the forty-three letters of this very Sūra, without addition or subtraction, and in identifying who the Prophet's true adversary was:

⟪ar-quote:الا ان الکوثر الطاھر وصیک علی ان تنحو فان شانئک بو بکر⟫

## Virtues of ʿAlī from the Verses of the Qurʾān

The verses that follow disclose, in turn, the name, the meaning, the kunya, the numerical value, the caliphate, the veracity, and the surpassing rank of Imām ʿAlī. Read together, they form a sustained Qurʾānic argument for the Imām's place beside the Prophet—an argument the early daʿwa (the summons) gathered into a deliberate sequence so that the reader who has been drawn to the call may pass from outward sense to taʾwīl (esoteric exegesis).

> ⟪quran 15:41⟫
> ⟪ar:‏قَالَ هَذَا صِرَطٌ عَلَىَّ مُسْتَقِيمٌ ‎⟫
> *(Allah) said: "This is the Way which will lead straight to Me." *
> said, This is the straight path for Me.
> — **Quran 15:41 (inline widget)**

The standard reading of this verse leaves the exegetes oddly tentative. They take ⟪ar:’علی‘⟫ as though it were ⟪ar:’الی‘⟫—"the path is straight unto Me"—which strains the eloquence of divine speech. Sensing the difficulty, some commentators offer the gloss:

| {{IMG_URL:.. /images/quote_open.gif}}He said this is a path on a straight 41 {{IMG_URL:.. /images/quote_closed.gif}} |
| --- |

—that is, "this is a path running level and straight." Yet a path's excellence lies in being straight, not in being elevated, so the reading falters under its own weight. The people of truth read the verse otherwise:

| {{IMG_URL:.. /images/quote_open.gif}}He said this is a path on a straight 41 {{IMG_URL:.. /images/quote_closed.gif}} |
| --- |

—"This is the path of ʿAlī, a straight one." Recited thus, the line is free of contortion and its sense is luminous: the Imām himself is the path along which the seeker travels to the divine.[^cite-7]

Ḥaḍrat Shākir Ṣāḥib has noted that the numerical value of ⟪ar:’یمین‘⟫ equals that of ⟪ar:’علی‘⟫—both yielding 110—so that the companions of the right hand, ⟪ar:’اصحاب الیمین‘⟫, are in truth ⟪ar:’اصحاب علی‘⟫, the companions of ʿAlī. The renowned Sufi Ḥasan al-Baṣrī used to recite the verse in this same way, declaring it to be the path of ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib and the path of religion: "Hold fast to it and walk upon it, for in it there is no defect." Another has observed that at the crossroads of the four companions, it is this verse that singles out the straight road.

## The Shared Excellence in the Names of Muḥammad and ʿAlī

Both names belong to the ⟪ar:أسمآء الحسنی⟫. Neither was current in the age of ignorance: the name Muḥammad emerged from the root ⟪ar:’حمد‘⟫, giving us ⟪ar:محمد و أحمد⟫, while the name ʿAlī emerged from ⟪ar:’اعلی‘⟫, yielding ⟪ar:علی اعظم⟫. The names are themselves coined from divine attributes, and arrive in history at the very moment the divine summons is unsealed.

---

If you join the names ⟪ar:محمد و علی⟫ together by the particle ⟪ar:’مع‘⟫ ("⟪ar:’ساتھ‘⟫"), you find the letters ⟪ar:میم اور عین⟫—mīm and ʿayn—the two ciphers that mark the eternal bond between the Prophet and the Imām. A tradition of the Prophet (ṣ) confirms the same: Allah placed our light in the loincloth of Adam, and from there we moved on, from one ⟪ar:ارحام زکیۃ⟫ to one ⟪ar:اصلاب طاھرۃ⟫, until at last the light reached ⟪ar:مولانا عبد المطلب⟫. From him I passed into the light of ⟪ar:عبد اللہ⟫ and ʿAlī into the light of ⟪ar:ابو طالب علیھم السلام⟫. From ⟪ar:عبد اللہ⟫ I was born, and from ⟪ar:ابو طالب⟫ ʿAlī was born.

---

The ⟪ar:میم⟫ of ⟪ar:محمد⟫ has the value 40, and the ⟪ar:عین⟫ of ⟪ar:علی⟫ has the value 70; together they yield 110, the very number of ⟪ar:’علی‘⟫. If you take the remaining letters and sum them, they amount to 92—the number of ⟪ar:’محمد‘⟫. No comparable property appears when the experiment is tried with any other name.

## Who Is the Testator of the Messenger of Allah?

⟪ar:حضرت سلمان فارسی⟫ (Salmān al-Fārsī) relates in ⟪ar:کفایۃ الطالب⟫ (Kifāyat al-Ṭālib): "One day I said to the Messenger of Allah, 'O Messenger of Allah, every prophet has a guardian (waṣī, testator). Who is your waṣī?' He fell silent and walked on. Then, when he had moved a short distance ahead, he called out to me. I answered ⟪ar:لبیک⟫ (labbayk — 'at your service') and hastened toward him. He asked, 'O Salmān, do you know who was the waṣī of Mūsā?' I replied, 'Yes — ⟪ar:یوشع بن نون⟫ (Yūshaʿ ibn Nūn).' He asked, 'And why was he made his waṣī?' I said, 'Because he was the most learned man in his ummah in that age.' At this the Messenger of Allah said: 'Listen — my waṣī, my confidant, and the best of my ummah, the one who will fulfil my promises and pay my debts, is my brother ⟪ar:'علی ابن ابی طالب'⟫ (ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib), in this world and in the Hereafter.'"

When the Prophet (ṣ) named Imam ʿAlī (ʿa) his brother, he was drawing attention to a still more pointed event. In the very first year of the Hijra at Medina, when he established brotherhood between the Muhājirūn and the Anṣār by pairing them one with another, he left ʿAlī aside. ʿAlī said, "O Messenger of Allah, you have made brotherhood among all the Muslims and have given me no brother." The Prophet replied: ⟪ar-quote:یا علی أنت أخی فی الدنیا و الآخرۃ⟫ — "O ʿAlī, you are my brother in this world and in the Hereafter. You hold with me the same rank and station that Hārūn held with Mūsā, save that there shall be no prophet after me. You shall dwell with me in my palace in Paradise, and with you shall be my daughter Fāṭima, and you shall be my companion. O ʿAlī, if anyone disputes with you and asks, 'Who are you?', say: 'I am a servant of God whom His Messenger has made his brother.' After me no one shall be entitled to make this claim except you; whoever else does so is a liar and an impostor."[^cite-11]

## I Bear Witness That Our Master ʿAlī Is the Walī of God

Sayyidunā Idrīs ibn al-Ḥasan records in his ʿUyūn al-Akhbār that, in the call to prayer (adhān), the muʾadhdhin should pronounce aloud the formulae "Our master ʿAlī is the *walī* (friend, guardian, and vicegerent) of God," "Muḥammad and ʿAlī are the best of mankind," and "their progeny is the most excellent of progenies." Under the imamate of our master al-Ḥākim bi-Amr Allāh, peace be upon him, the testimony "I bear witness that our master ʿAlī is the walī of God" was inserted into the adhān immediately after the testimony to the messengership of Muḥammad, the Messenger of God. In commanding that public proclamation, the Imam was making audible — five times a day, from every minaret of his realm — what the Prophet had already announced at Ghadīr Khumm: "Of whomsoever I am the master and guardian (*mawlā*), ʿAlī is his master and guardian."[^cite-15] To say "I bear witness that our master ʿAlī is the walī of God" is therefore not the importation of a novelty into the rite; it is the liturgical ratification of a Prophetic declaration, binding the worshipper, at the threshold of every prayer, to the Messenger and to the one whom the Messenger himself designated.

The muʾadhdhin would then continue: "Muḥammad and ʿAlī are the best of mankind, and their progeny is the most excellent of progenies." With this clause he gave voice to the unique excellence of the Prophet and of those nearest to him in flesh and in spirit. The nearest of them — his brother by oath of fraternity, his cousin by blood, and his designated successor (*waṣī*) — was the Commander of the Faithful, ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib. His daughter was Fāṭima al-Zahrāʾ; his grandsons were al-Ḥasan and al-Ḥusayn, peace be upon them all. From the seed of al-Ḥusayn descends the line of Imams whom God chose from the offspring of His Messenger and whom He appointed as the guides of all creation, summoning humankind, through them, to His path.[^cite-16]

When ʿAbd Allāh ibn ʿAbbās was asked who the kinsfolk of the Prophet are whom God has named in His verse — ⟪quran 42:23⟫ — he answered without hesitation: "They are ʿAlī, Fāṭima, al-Ḥasan, and al-Ḥusayn."

The majority who follow another path are fond of relating that Abū Bakr ibn Abī Quḥāfa once said, "We are the children of the Messenger of God." Yet, by God, Abū Bakr stood in no kinship to the soul of the Prophet. The most that could be claimed was a remote Qurashī tribal tie, and even that, if granted, was a distant association at best. ʿAlī, Fāṭima, al-Ḥasan, and al-Ḥusayn, by contrast, stood in the closest possible relation to him: closer than any other Companion, by blood and by bond. The poet al-Kumayt put the matter plainly when he sang that the House of Hāshim is the household of the Prophet — for whom and on whose account the Messenger was many times made glad and many times made angry. The conclusion is inescapable. They are the foremost of all people, and no others; their guardianship is the one to be acknowledged, and no other's.

A common objection is then raised. The opposing majority concede that Muḥammad, peace be upon him, is the best of mankind, since he is the master of the prophets and messengers; but they refuse to grant the same of ʿAlī, peace be upon him. Their reasoning is that prophets, too, are counted among mankind, and it cannot be permitted that ʿAlī should be superior to a prophet. Everyone agrees, however, that the community (*umma*) of the Seal of the Prophets is the best of communities, as God has said: "You are the best community brought forth for humankind." All also acknowledge that among later generations there have been heirs of the prophets and saintly men of the highest virtue. To this concession the Messenger of God himself adds his weight in a tradition narrated, among others, by Ḥudhayfa ibn al-Yamān: "I am leaving among you two weighty things. So long as you hold fast to them, you shall never go astray. One is the Book of God; the other is my ʿitra, my household. They are as the Ark of Nūḥ: whoever boards it is saved, and whoever lags behind is drowned."[^cite-17]

The Prophet also said of ʿAlī, peace be upon him: "ʿAlī is the best of mankind; whoever refuses this has disbelieved."

### An Instructive Anecdote

The incident now to be related took place during the reign of our master the Imam al-Ḥākim bi-Amr Allāh, peace be upon him. ʿAlī ibn al-Ḥusayn al-Qārī narrates: "One day I was standing with the Imam al-Ḥākim, peace be upon him, in the quarter known as al-Faraffa, near the mosque of al-Qaḍāʾī, when of a sudden we heard a commotion. The Imam dispatched a man to discover its cause. The attendant returned with the report: 'O our master, there is a quarrel between two parties from the people of the Maghribī district.' He ordered them brought before him. The attendant seized them and led them in. I saw that an Abyssinian had a man by the throat so fiercely that the latter could scarcely draw breath. The Imam commanded the Abyssinian to release his grip and asked, 'What is the matter? Why this violent struggle? And who are you?'

"The Abyssinian replied, 'I am the muʾadhdhin of the quarter, and this man is the preacher.' The Imam, peace be upon him, asked again, 'What is the matter?' The Abyssinian said, 'O our master, this man has said something today in our city the like of which we have never heard.' The Imam asked, 'And what is that?' The Abyssinian answered, 'In the call to prayer he pronounced: *Muḥammad wa ʿAlī khayr al-bashar wa ʿitratuhumā khayr al-ʿitar* — Muḥammad and ʿAlī are the best of mankind, and their progeny is the most excellent of progenies. We have never heard the adhān recited so. It is altogether singular and strange to us. To call Muḥammad, peace be upon him, the best of mankind is permissible; but to call ʿAlī, peace be upon him, the best of mankind is not permissible — for there are Ādam, Nūḥ, Ibrāhīm, Mūsā, and ʿĪsā, and it is not licit to say that ʿAlī is superior to these prophets and messengers.'

"The Imam al-Ḥākim said to him, 'O man, be at ease. We shall prove to you in a few sentences that ʿAlī is the best of mankind. Tell me first: have you studied the traditions (*aḥādīth*)?' The Abyssinian answered, 'Yes, I am acquainted with many of them.' The Imam said, 'I shall relate to you a tradition. If it appears authentic to you, confess it; if not, reject it, and I shall present another.'

"He then narrated, with its chain reaching back through Ibn Khālid from Ibn Suwayr, from the Caliph al-Maʾmūn al-ʿAbbāsī, from his father al-Rashīd, from his father al-Mahdī, from his father al-Manṣūr, on the authority of ʿAbd Allāh ibn ʿAbbās, the following:

"'One day we were in the presence of the Messenger of God. He was instructing us in the science of religion (*ʿilm al-dīn*) when his daughter Fāṭima, peace be upon her, entered weeping. The Messenger of God, peace be upon him, rose at once in alarm and asked her why she wept. She said, "My two sons, al-Ḥasan and al-Ḥusayn, have gone missing. My heart is in torment for them." The Prophet, peace be upon him, raised his eyes to heaven and prayed, trembling with fear. Gabriel descended at once and said, "O Muḥammad, do not be afraid. God Most High sends you His peace and says: Your two grandsons, al-Ḥasan and al-Ḥusayn, went to the orchard of the Banū al-Najjār and fell asleep there at play. I have sent to them a merciful angel as their companion, who has made a bed for them with one wing and a covering with the other. Go to them and bring back your grandchildren."

"'On hearing this, the Prophet went forth to the Banū al-Najjār with his companions and saw that his two grandsons were sleeping, with an angel watching over them. The Messenger of God lifted them up and seated them upon his shoulders. Abū Bakr said, "O Messenger of God, give me one to carry." The Messenger refused, saying, "I shall carry them myself, for just as their mount is the most excellent, so are these two riders most excellent, and their father is more excellent still than both of them." He then returned to the house to bring the good news to Fāṭima, peace be upon her. A great crowd had gathered in alarm during his absence, and addressing them he said: "O people, shall I not tell you of a man whose father and mother are the best of mankind? These are al-Ḥasan and al-Ḥusayn, whose father is ʿAlī — my successor (*waṣī*) and the most excellent of successors — and whose mother is Fāṭima, mistress of the women of the worlds. These are al-Ḥasan and al-Ḥusayn, whose grandfather is the Messenger of God and whose grandmother is Khadīja al-Kubrā — the first to believe in God and His Messenger.

"'"O people, shall I not tell you of those whose uncle and aunt are the best of mankind? These are al-Ḥasan and al-Ḥusayn, whose paternal uncle is Jaʿfar ibn Abī Ṭālib and whose paternal aunt is Umm Hāniʾ bint Abī Ṭālib, who was never a polytheist. Shall I not tell you of those whose maternal uncle and aunt are the best of mankind? These are al-Ḥasan and al-Ḥusayn, whose maternal uncle is al-Qāsim, son of the Messenger of God, and whose maternal aunt is Zaynab bint Rasūl Allāh." Then, after this proclamation, he said: "O God, You know that these two, and their father, and their mother, and their grandfather, and their grandmother, and their paternal uncle and aunt, and their maternal uncle and aunt, and their Shīʿa and their lovers, are all of the people of Paradise."'

"Having narrated this tradition, the Imam al-Ḥākim, peace be upon him, asked the Abyssinian, 'Is this tradition authentic?' The Abyssinian said, 'Yes, O Commander of the Faithful, this tradition is sound.' Then the Imam, peace be upon him, narrated further traditions and said: 'It is also reported that the Messenger of God, peace be upon him, while addressing the congregation upon that occasion, said: *al-Ḥasan wa-l-Ḥusayn imāmā ḥaqq qāmā aw qaʿadā wa abūhumā khayrun minhumā* — al-Ḥasan and al-Ḥusayn are both true Imams, whether they rise to claim their right or remain seated in quiet, and their father is better than both of them.' He then said: 'These two sons of mine are the masters of the youth of Paradise.'[^cite-18]

"'Are these two traditions sound or not?' the Imam asked. The Abyssinian said, 'Yes, O Commander of the Faithful, you have spoken truly.' Then the Imam, peace be upon him, asked, 'And have you heard the tradition that all those who enter Paradise enter as youths, never as elders?' The Abyssinian replied, 'Yes, O Commander of the Faithful, I have heard this tradition too, and it is correct.'

"Then the Imam, peace be upon him, said: 'O man, attend now. Will there be in Paradise Ādam, Nūḥ, Ibrāhīm, Mūsā, and ʿĪsā, or will there not? And if there will be, will they be there as youths or not?' The reply was, 'Yes, O Commander of the Faithful, they must be there, and they must be there as youths.' The Imam said: 'Listen, then. If al-Ḥasan and al-Ḥusayn are the masters of the youth of Paradise — and the prophets in Paradise are themselves youths — and if their father is better than both of them, it is established beyond any doubt that ʿAlī is the best of mankind, just as Muḥammad, peace be upon him, is the best of mankind.'

"The Abyssinian accepted defeat at this clear and unstrained argument and said, 'I have believed and I bear witness (*āmantu wa ṣaddaqtu*).' Then our master al-Ḥākim turned to the preacher and said, 'Now you may pronounce the call to prayer as you wish, and no one shall be permitted to object.'"

### The Truth of "ʿAlī Walī Allāh"

## Qurʾānic Proofs that ʿAlī is the Best of Mankind

The arguments gathered here are offered in answer to the objection raised against the Shīʿa for adjoining to their call to prayer the formula declaring that Muḥammad and ʿAlī are *khayr al-bashar* (the best of mankind) and that their progeny are *khayr al-ʿitra* (the best of the family). The objectors concede, without hesitation, that the Messenger of God is the best of mankind; what they refuse is the extension of that rank to ʿAlī and his descendants. The verses that follow show, by the plain witness of the Book, that this refusal cannot stand.

**The first proof.** God says in His Book:

> ⟪ar:‏وَقَرْنَ فِى بُيُوتِكُنَّ وَلَا تَبَرَّجْنَ تَبَرُّجَ ٱلْجَهِلِيَّةِ ٱلْأُولَىٰۖ وَأَقِمْنَ ٱلصَّلَوٰةَ وَآتِينَ ٱلزَّكَوٰةَ وَأَطِعْنَ ٱللَّهَ وَرَسُولَهُۚۥٓ إِنَّمَا يُرِيدُ ٱللَّهُ لِيُذْهِبَ عَنكُمُ ٱلرِّجْسَ أَهْلَ ٱلْبَيْتِ وَيُطَهِّرَكُمْ تَطْهِيرًۭا ‎⟫ (⟪quran 33:33⟫)

God wills to remove every impurity from the *Ahl al-Bayt* (the People of the House) and to purify them with a thorough purification. The verse declares, in effect, that God intends for the *Ahl al-Bayt* a purity matched only to that of the Messenger himself. Among them, the one nearest to the Prophet—his successor (*waṣī*), guardian (*walī*), and caliph (*khalīfa*)—is Mawlānā ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib. ʿAlī therefore shares with the Prophet in this declared purity, and through that sharing he shares also in the rank of *khayriyya* (preeminent goodness). It follows that ʿAlī is the best of mankind after the Messenger; and his descendants, being likewise of the People of the House and partaking in the same purity, are the best of mankind after him.[^cite-19]

**The second proof.** God says:

> ⟪ar:‏سَلَمٌ عَلَىٰٓ أٓلِ يَاسِينَ ‎⟫ (⟪quran 37:130⟫)
>
> ⟪ar:‏إِنَّا كَذَلِكَ نَجْزِى ٱلْمُحْسِنِينَ ‎⟫ (⟪quran 37:131⟫)

"Peace be upon the family of Yāsīn; thus do We reward the doers of good." The family of Yāsīn, by the consensus of the *taʾwīl* (esoteric exegesis) of our tradition, are the descendants of the Prophet who hold the rank of the Imāms. Those whom God has placed alongside the Messenger in this greeting are the offspring of the Messenger; and the best of them, nearest to the Prophet, is without question ʿAlī, whom the Prophet had taken as his brother and made his successor. The verse therefore certifies that the best of the family of Yāsīn—those whom God Himself greets and whom He names *muḥsinūn* (the doers of good)—stands at the summit of mankind; and that is ʿAlī. His descendants after him hold the same rank, for they too are, in truth, the family of the Messenger of God.[^cite-20]

**The third proof.** God says:

> ⟪ar:‏كُنتُمْ خَيْرَ أُمَّةٍ أُخْرِجَتْ لِلنَّاسِ تَأْمُرُونَ بِٱلْمَعْرُوفِ وَتَنْهَوْنَ عَنِ ٱلْمُنكَرِ وَتُؤْمِنُونَ بِٱللَّهِ ۗ وَلَوْ آمَنَ أَهْلُ ٱلْكِتَبِ لَكَانَ خَيْرًۭا لَّهُم ۚ مِّنْهُمُ ٱلْمُؤْمِنُونَ وَأَكْثَرُهُمُ ٱلْفَسِقُونَ ‎⟫ (⟪quran 3:110⟫)

"You are the best community brought forth for mankind: you enjoin the right (*al-maʿrūf*) and forbid the wrong (*al-munkar*), and you believe in God." The true addressees of this verse—those who, in the fullest sense, command the right and forbid the wrong—are the *Ahl al-Bayt*. None has discharged this office as they have, and none has been entrusted with it as they have. If the People of the House of the Prophet are the best of the community, and if the best of them is the Commander of the Faithful (*Amīr al-Muʾminīn*), then ʿAlī is the best of mankind after the Messenger. The matter admits of no doubt.

**The fourth proof.** God says, quoting Mūsā (Moses):

> ⟪ar:‏وَٱجْعَل لِّى وَزِيرًۭا مِّنْ أَهْلِى ‎⟫ (⟪quran 20:29⟫)

And He answers:

> ⟪ar:‏قَالَ قَدْ أُوتِيتَ سُؤْلَكَ يَمُوسَىٰ ‎⟫ (⟪quran 20:36⟫)

The verse makes plain that Moses asked his Lord to appoint his brother Hārūn (Aaron) as his minister (*wazīr*) and successor, and to make him a partner in the burden of prophecy, so that his hands might be strengthened and the remembrance of God multiplied. Elsewhere the same petition is recast:

> ⟪ar:‏وَأَخِى هَرُونُ هُوَ أَفْصَحُ مِنِّى لِسَانًۭا فَأَرْسِلْهُ مَعِىَ رِدْءًۭا يُصَدِّقُنِىٓ ۖ إِنِّىٓ أَخَافُ أَن يُكَذِّبُونِ ‎⟫ (⟪quran 28:34⟫)

Again God accepts:

> ⟪ar:‏قَالَ سَنَشُدُّ عَضُدَكَ بِأَخِيكَ وَنَجْعَلُ لَكُمَا سُلْطَنًۭا فَلَا يَصِلُونَ إِلَيْكُمَا ۚ بِـَٔايَتِنَآ أَنتُمَا وَمَنِ ٱتَّبَعَكُمَا ٱلْغَلِبُونَ ‎⟫ (⟪quran 28:35⟫)

These verses establish that God Himself joined to Moses a brother as his support, his confirming voice, and a sharer in his sovereignty. He then commands the Messenger Muḥammad—addressing him in the verses that follow—to walk the same road His earlier messengers walked:

> ⟪ar:‏أُو۟لَٓئِكَ ٱلَّذِينَ هَدَى ٱللَّهُ ۖ فَبِهُدَىٰهُمُ ٱقْتَدِهْ ۗ قُل لَّآ أَسْـَٔلُكُمْ عَلَيْهِ أَجْرًا ۖ إِنْ هُوَ إِلَّا ذِكْرَىٰ لِلْعَلَمِينَ ‎⟫ (⟪quran 6:90⟫)
>
> ⟪ar:‏مَّا يُقَالُ لَكَ إِلَّا مَا قَدْ قِيلَ لِلرُّسُلِ مِن قَبْلِكَ ۚ إِنَّ رَبَّكَ لَذُو مَغْفِرَةٍۢ وَذُو عِقَابٍ أَلِيمٍۢ ‎⟫ (⟪quran 41:43⟫)
>
> ⟪ar:‏ شَرَعَ لَكُم مِّنَ ٱلدِّينِ مَا وَصَّىٰ بِهِۦ نُوحًۭا وَٱلَّذِىٓ أَوْحَيْنَآ إِلَيْكَ وَمَا وَصَّيْنَا بِهِۦٓ إِبْرَهِيمَ وَمُوسَىٰ وَعِيسَىٰٓ ۖ أَنْ أَقِيمُوا۟ ٱلدِّينَ وَلَا تَتَفَرَّقُوا۟ فِيهِ ۚ كَبُرَ عَلَى ٱلْمُشْرِكِينَ مَا تَدْعُوهُمْ إِلَيْهِ ۚ ٱللَّهُ يَجْتَبِىٓ إِلَيْهِ مَن يَشَآءُ وَيَهْدِىٓ إِلَيْهِ مَن يُنِيبُ ‎⟫ (⟪quran 42:13⟫)

The cumulative force of these verses is unmistakable. The Messenger is bound to follow in the footsteps of those who came before him: to take a brother from his family as his minister, to make him a partner in the office, and to inherit, with him, the dominion that God grants. The Messenger did exactly this, naming ʿAlī as his brother and his successor; and God bestowed upon them the very *sulṭān* He had once bestowed upon Mūsā and Hārūn. For this is the *sunna* (settled way) of God, of which He Himself says:

> ⟪ar:‏سُنَّةَ مَن قَدْ أَرْسَلْنَا قَبْلَكَ مِن رُّسُلِنَا ۖ وَلَا تَجِدُ لِسُنَّتِنَا تَحْوِيلًا ‎⟫ (⟪quran 17:77⟫)
>
> ⟪ar:‏سُنَّةَ ٱللَّهِ فِى ٱلَّذِينَ خَلَوْا۟ مِن قَبْلُۖ وَلَن تَجِدَ لِسُنَّةِ ٱللَّهِ تَبْدِيلًۭا ‎⟫ (⟪quran 33:62⟫)

"You will find no change in Our way; you will find no substitution in the way of God." The conclusion, then, is that ʿAlī is to the Messenger what Hārūn was to Mūsā: his brother, his successor, and his partner in the dominion God conferred. This Qurʾānic argument is confirmed by the Prophet's own well-known declaration: *"Anta minnī bi-manzilati Hārūn min Mūsā illā annahu lā nabiyya baʿdī"*—"Your station with me is the station of Hārūn with Mūsā, except that there is no prophet after me."[^cite-21] Verse and *ḥadīth* together establish that since ʿAlī is the companion of the Messenger of God in his office and a sharer in the rank that flows from it, he is also the best of mankind after him.[^cite-22]

**The fifth proof.** God says:

> ⟪ar:‏إِنَّ ٱلَّذِينَ آمَنُوا۟ وَعَمِلُوا۟ ٱلصَّلِحَتِ أُو۟لَٓئِكَ هُمْ خَيْرُ ٱلْبَرِيَّةِ ‎⟫ (⟪quran 98:7⟫)

"Those who believe and do good deeds are the best of creatures." It has already been shown that the *Ahl al-Bayt* of the Prophet stand foremost among those who believe in God and act righteously. Their being thus foremost is at the same time a luminous demonstration that they are the best of mankind.

**The sixth proof.** God says:

> ⟪ar:‏ إِنَّ ٱللَّهَ ٱصْطَفَىٰٓ آدَمَ وَنُوحًۭا وَآلَ إِبْرَهِيمَ وَآلَ عِمْرَنَ عَلَى ٱلْعَلَمِينَ ‎⟫ (⟪quran 3:33⟫)

"God chose Ādam, Nūḥ, the family of Ibrāhīm, and the family of ʿImrān above all the worlds." From this it is clear that God elected the families of Ibrāhīm and ʿImrān for prophethood and chosen-ness (*iṣṭifāʾ*) just as He elected Ādam and Nūḥ as singular persons. And it is plainer than the sun at noon that the Messenger of God is greater than Ādam and Nūḥ. The family of the Messenger, then, must stand above the family of Ibrāhīm and the family of ʿImrān; and since ʿAlī is the best of the family of the Messenger, he is, of necessity, the best of mankind.[^cite-23]

**The seventh proof.** God says:

> ⟪ar:‏وَلَقَدْ نَجَّيْنَا بَنِىٓ إِسْرَٓءِيلَ مِنَ ٱلْعَذَابِ ٱلْمُهِينِ ‎⟫ (⟪quran 44:30⟫)

"And We delivered the Children of Israel from the humiliating torment"—that is, from Pharaoh, who was arrogant and a transgressor—and God then placed them, knowingly, above the peoples of their age. If God so honoured the children of a prophet, raising them above all mankind of their time, how much more must this hold for the family of the Lord of Messengers (*Sayyid al-Mursalīn*)? The family of the Prophet is superior to every prophet and to every prophetic house. And once this is established, it is established also that the phrase we adjoin in our *adhān* (call to prayer)—that Muḥammad and ʿAlī are *khayr al-bashar* and that their progeny are *khayr al-ʿitra*—is true, sound, and rightly upheld. For ʿAlī is the best and noblest among the family of the Prophet.

Praise be to God, Lord of the worlds, and blessings upon our master Muḥammad, lord of the messengers, and upon ʿAlī and the pure ones (*al-ṭāhirīn*) until the Day of Judgement, with peace.

## The Two Witnesses to the Messenger's Mission

Testimony, in its essence, is of two kinds: true or false.

**True testimony** takes one of two forms in the words of the witness: affirming a right or attribute that genuinely belongs to a person, or denying a right or attribute that does not in fact belong to them. If a man owns one cow and two goats, the true testimonies concerning him are that he owns one cow, that he does not own two cows, that he owns two goats, and that he does not own three goats. Each of these statements corresponds to the actual state of affairs.

**False testimony** likewise takes one of two forms: affirming a right or attribute that does not belong to the person in question, or denying one that does. With the same man — one cow, two goats — the false testimonies are that he owns two cows, that he owns only one goat, that he owns no cow at all, or that he owns no goats. Each statement misrepresents what is.

Witnesses themselves are also of two kinds: those who testify by sight, and those who testify by hearing.

**The eyewitness** is one who has seen an event unfold before his own eyes and knows directly whether it happened or not.

**The hearing witness** is one who has not seen the event itself but has been informed of it by a just and trustworthy source, and on the strength of that source's word offers his own testimony.

### Testimony to the Oneness of Allah

Everything in the cosmos is, in itself, a proof of the creation, oneness, lordship, and unicity of its Creator. Yet Mubdiʿ Taʿālā (the Originator, Most High) specifically appointed Muḥammad Muṣṭafā as His witness, declaring: "O Messenger of Allah, We have sent you as a witness for Us." You, O Messenger, are an eyewitness and a speaking witness on Our behalf to the whole cosmos. The first ray of being is your light, and with that light We brought the cosmos into being. You are Our first creation and Our final proof. The Messenger himself said:

> The first thing Allah created was my light, and from it He created the light of all creatures.

> Allah first created my light, and from my light He fashioned the whole cosmos.

The attributes of Allah cannot be perceived by sense or grasped by ordinary cognition; yet the Messenger beheld his Lord with the eye of the intellect and bore witness to His oneness. He said: "I did not know the Truth except by what I did, and what I did was done by me and by ʿAlī." And: "None has known me except Allah and ʿAlī, and none has known ʿAlī except Allah and me." This blessed report tells us that if anyone possessed the correct knowledge and monotheistic understanding of Allah it was the Messenger; and since the Messenger was the one who furnished ʿAlī's complete instruction, the source of that first knowledge can only have been the Messenger himself.[^cite-24]

### Two Witnesses to the Messenger's Prophethood

Allah says: O beloved one, just as you have testified to Our creation, oneness, and lordship, We too have appointed two witnesses to your prophethood — one is Our Book, the Qurʾān; the other is Our walī, ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib.

The witness who came after the Messenger in this verse is ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib; the witness who came before him is the Qurʾān. One might suppose that any reciter of the shahāda could serve as witness to the Messenger's mission, but the testimony of every casual professor of faith is not, in Allah's reckoning, reliable. Four kinds of people bear witness to the Prophet's prophethood: two according to the human estimate, and two according to Allah. The two we ourselves count are the believers and the hypocrites. The two Allah counts are the Qurʾān, and ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib.

### Witnesses by Our Reckoning

The testimony of the hypocrites carries no weight, for Allah Himself says:

> When the hypocrites come to you, they say, "We bear witness that you are indeed the Messenger of Allah." And Allah knows that you are His Messenger, yet Allah bears witness that the hypocrites are liars.

The testimony of the believers, by contrast, is reliable but incomplete, for they do not possess the inner knowledge of the Prophet. None has known the Messenger except Allah and ʿAlī.

### Witnesses by Allah's Reckoning

In Allah's reckoning the Messenger has two witnesses: one descending from Allah — the Qurʾān — and one issuing from the Messenger himself — ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib. The witness sent down from Allah appeared in this world from the household of the Messenger; the witness from the Messenger was born within the House of Allah itself. This is the proof of mutual indwelling: Our thing is your thing, your thing is Ours. The Qurʾān is the silent ṣāmit; ʿAlī is the speaking nāṭiq. Allah brought forth the more excellent of the two from the house of the Messenger, and the lesser (the Qurʾān) from His own House. And the Messenger drew Qurʾān and ʿAlī together when he said, "ʿAlī is with the Qurʾān and the Qurʾān is with ʿAlī."[^cite-25]

It is by virtue of this witnessing that the Messenger said to him, "O ʿAlī, you are to me as Aaron was to Moses." Hearing this, ʿAlī would reply: O Messenger of Allah! The work of summons is sometimes upon your shoulders and sometimes upon mine. You declare the message and I confirm it; you call the people openly and I remain hidden in the breast; you withdraw to the cave and I lie down upon your bed beneath the green cloak; you recite the words of the Qurʾān and I unfold their taʾwīl (esoteric exegesis); you become the commander of Islam and I its gatekeeper of knowledge; you are the image of mercy and I the executor of wrath; you wage jihād with the tongue and I wage it with the sword; you order the idols broken and I break them; you are the lord of Kawthar and Paradise, cupbearer and arbiter of heaven and hell. You are the city of knowledge and I its gate. O Prophet, I am the witness of your prophethood, of your mission, of your imamate, and of your testamentary appointment.

### The Relationship between ʿAlī and the Qurʾān

What then is the eminence of the Messenger, when one of his witnesses is the Qurʾān — the Book of Allah — and the other is the very soul of Allah, the cause of Allah, the tongue of Allah, the proof of Allah, on whose account some have been deceived into divinising him? Allah has appointed as witness to His Messenger's prophethood one who, when he sits in the assembly of the Sacred, is called the cause of Allah; when he comes to the assembly of the angels, becomes the teacher of Jibrāʾīl; when he sits in the assembly of the Prophet, is called the Soul of the Messenger; when he stands in the rank of the believers, is called the perfect in faith; when he supports the Prophet, is called the helper of the believers. For His beloved, Allah appointed two witnesses: the Qurʾān and ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib.

Everyone knows that two things are paired only where there is correspondence, likeness, kinship between them. How foolish to lock a nightingale and a crow in one cage. The proof here is that just as the Qurʾān admits no match in eloquence and clarity, neither do ʿAlī's knowledge, his deeds, and the perfections of his soul and intellect admit any rival. On one side, Allah says of His Book:

> There is no green nor withered thing but it is in a clear Book.

On the other side, He says of ʿAlī:

> We have encompassed every thing in a clear Imām.

The true witness to the Messenger's prophethood can only be one who possesses the knowledge of the Qurʾān, for the prophethood is the Qurʾān and the Qurʾān is the Messenger. After the Hijra, when the Messenger made the Muhājirūn and the Anṣār brothers to one another, he made ʿAlī his own brother, saying:

> O ʿAlī, you are my brother in this world and in the Hereafter.

Allah did not merely make His Messenger a witness for ʿAlī; He also made ʿAlī a witness for Himself. In the Qurʾān it is said:

> Allah bears witness that there is no god but He, and the angels and those endowed with knowledge — upholding justice. There is no god but He, the Mighty, the Wise.

Allah has thus enlisted those endowed with knowledge as witnesses alongside Himself and His angels. This witness possesses extraordinary stations: at times he stands with Allah in testifying to the messengership of the Messenger; at times he stands with the angels in testifying to Allah's justice; at times he stands with the Qurʾān in testifying to the Messenger. In the assembly of the Messenger he is the Soul of the Prophet; in the pulpit he is the orator "Ask me"; in the gathering of the believers he is the perfect in faith; in the assembly of the polytheists he is the arbiter against them; in the gathering of the hypocrites he is the resolute and unyielding; among the learned he is the most learned; under the cloak of the Prophet he is among the purified; in the court of governance he is the leader; before the disbelievers he is the most severe. He is the mirror of the prophets' attributes, the unraveller of difficulties, the breaker of idols, the hand of Allah, the soul of the Prophet, the helper of religion, possessor of the Aaronic station, likeness of Noah's Ark, the First Soul, master of the believers, prince of the bees, chief of the faith, al-Anzaʿ al-Baṭīn — for the soul, there is no field of spiritual perfection and no rung of spiritual capacity where this witness is not present. The vanquishing one is ʿAlī, none other than ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib.

Among the kinds of witnesses, ʿAlī's testimony to the Messenger is an eyewitness testimony, just as the Messenger's testimony to the oneness of Allah is an eyewitness testimony. The Messenger said:

> I and ʿAlī are from a single light — O ʿAlī, you and I are from one light.

This ḥadīth attests that since ʿAlī shared in the light of prophethood, when the crown of prophethood was placed upon the Messenger's head it was ʿAlī who could be eyewitness to that scene. And the divine wisdom in dividing the light of prophethood into two portions is itself a sign of unicity: Allah is one, and everything else stands in twos or more. It is established, then, that Allah sent both ʿAlī and the Qurʾān as witnesses to the Messenger.

If the rank of these two witnesses were unequal — one high, the other low — their testimony would diverge. Had an ignorant person been paired with the Qurʾān as witness, it would have been an insult to the Qurʾān. He therefore sent as witness one who is superior to the Qurʾān itself, for the Qurʾān is mute and silent, while ʿAlī is articulate and speaking. The Qurʾān and the Messenger are inseparable, and ʿAlī is the speaking voice of both.

Secondly, much of the Qurʾān is composed in reference to ʿAlī's deeds. ʿAlī was therefore the witness who attested to the Messenger's prophethood so that all people might know him as a spiritual scholar. The world bears witness that the world's learned complete the Qurʾān at most in two or four days; but those who have learned from the House of Allah, when they set one foot in the stirrup begin the Qurʾān, and by the time the other foot reaches the stirrup they have completed it.

The life of the Prophet has two aspects: one is the knowledge of the Messenger, the other is the action of the Messenger. For these two aspects Allah has chosen two witnesses: the Qurʾān, which bears witness to the deeds of the Messenger, and ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib, who bears witness to the knowledge of the Messenger. It is not proper to dispense with either. The Messenger said:

> Knowledge without works and works without knowledge are both misguidance.

> Knowledge without action is a calamity; action without knowledge is misguidance.[^cite-26]

*Majlis of Ḥaḍrat Naṣrullāh Ṣāḥib, may Allah sanctify his soul.*

## Dhū al-Qarnayn (The Two-Horned One)

The name Dhū al-Qarnayn means one possessed of two horns. It is said that Dhū al-Qarnayn ruled the whole world — one of his dominions in the east, the other in the west. The Messenger said to Imām ʿAlī: "O ʿAlī, you are the leader of this community and its Dhū al-Qarnayn." Though you did not, on the face of things, rule the world, you are master of two epochs in a scholarly and spiritual sense. There is the epoch of the Messenger, in which your station is that of the Gate of Knowledge and the Qurʾān itself; and there is the epoch of the Qāʾim, lord of the Resurrection and possessor of the Day of Judgement, from whose lineage will come the seal of prophethood, of imamate, and of testamentary office, joined to him as people are joined to the firmest handhold of Allah — ʿAlī. No one else will find refuge in that recess. In this manner he became the lord of two ages.

**A Statement**

Mawlānā ʿAlī was struck in the face only twice. Once in the Battle of the Trench, when the accursed ʿAmr ibn ʿAbd Wadd struck him on the head; and a second time when Ibn Muljam the accursed martyred him. From these two blows two horn-like marks remained upon his head — another reason he is called Dhū al-Qarnayn.

### The Story of Dhū al-Qarnayn and the Jewels

It is related that when Dhū al-Qarnayn entered a dark cave with his companions in search of the water of life, they noticed certain shining pebbles beneath their feet. The companions asked, "Are we passing over mere pebbles?" Dhū al-Qarnayn replied, "This is a matter of which both the one who lifts and the one who does not lift will come to regret." His companions, perplexed by the saying, split into two parties: one took some of the stones, the other left them. When they emerged from the darkness and saw the stones in the light, they discovered them to be precious gems. The taker regretted that he had carried so little — if only he had taken more; and the one who had taken nothing regretted being deprived of so great a treasure.

The resemblance of this story to the Master of the Believers is plain. The Commander of the Faithful, who is the Dhū al-Qarnayn of this Muslim community, leads his followers from darkness into the world of purity and light. The shining stones that the people sense beneath their feet are the jewels of his wise sayings, which they hear but do not understand, for those sayings are concealed in the darkness of words — words which increase the value of the human soul beyond price. Yet his companions did not weigh the worth of such words and listened to them inattentively.

Dhū al-Qarnayn's saying — that whoever lifts these stones will regret it and whoever does not lift them will also regret it — is utterly true; for he knew how much each one was able to carry. The taker, whatever he can fold into his arms and lift, will take. When he reaches the light he will lament that he settled for so little; why did he not pick up more of those rare gems? And the one who took nothing will mourn his misfortune. So too those who benefit from the words of the Commander of the Faithful: most lean toward this world and away from religion, listening to his sayings in leisure but not pondering them deeply. When they pass from this world of injustice and behold the abundant results of their few and indifferent good deeds, they will regret not having invested in the next life the time they spent earning the world. As for those who paid no attention at all — their regret in poverty and eternal misery is beyond description.

### The Story of Gog and Magog

A turn.

In his time Dhū al-Qarnayn built a strong wall, on the far side of which lived two peoples called Yājūj and Mājūj (Gog and Magog). These two peoples used to harm human beings. Yājūj and Mājūj are said to have been creatures of short stature with rough and sharp tongues, capable of slicing the hardest thing as thin as a wafer, who harmed people when they came near settled habitation. Dhū al-Qarnayn raised a wall between himself and them so as to protect humankind. From the moment that wall was raised, Yājūj and Mājūj have been licking and scraping at it through the night in their attempt to break through; the wall comes close to falling, but at that very moment dawn breaks, the muezzin gives the call to the Fajr prayer, and the wall is restored to its former state. The people flee.

The taʾwīl of this outward story is that these two peoples, Yājūj and Mājūj, are in fact two factions of qāṣir (those who diminish the rank of the Commander of the Faithful) and ghālī (those who exalt him excessively), both being mere outward worshippers devoid of inner knowledge. They are acquainted only with the natural and worldly sciences and have received no light from the real sciences and the lights of the upper world. A wall has been raised before these materialists, without which they cannot reach the knowledge of the Truth — and that wall is the wall of covenants and pledges.

Yājūj and Mājūj are said to have been of short stature. Their gaze does not rise toward the world of the soul and the world of the intellect; they do not attain heights, and even their necks are short. By contrast, the Messenger said: "On the Day of Resurrection the necks of the muezzins will be longest," meaning they will receive a greater share of Allah's mercy and grace. The sharp tongues of Yājūj and Mājūj, with which they pierce the wall and bring it to the edge of collapse, are the speeches of those — the enemies of ʿAlī — whose eloquence and clever language seek to overpower the knowledge of the true imams. They taunt the righteous and try to drag them down to their own convictions. When the believers are nearly worn out by these attacks and on the verge of breaking the wall of pledge and covenant, the duʿāt (the Summoners, who are the muezzins of the call to truth) reinforce their belief. The religious and true sciences firm up the weak thoughts, and the believers grow strong upon hearing the true sciences as though no weakness had ever entered their thinking. This state will continue until the Day of Judgement.

This story also confirms that the Commander of the Faithful is the Dhū al-Qarnayn of the community, for the wall of covenant that protects people from Yājūj and Mājūj is the covenant of Mawlānā ʿAlī's wilāya (spiritual authority).[^cite-27]

## al-Anzaʿ al-Baṭīn (The Bald, Big-Bellied One)

al-Anzaʿ means bald — without hair upon the head; al-Baṭīn means one possessed of a great belly. The Master of the Believers bears this title because in the cycle of seventy ages no person was loftier than he: hence al-Anzaʿ, the unrivalled summit upon which no equal stands. And because he was the greatest scholar of the inner sciences — and the larger the belly, the more it can contain — he is therefore called al-Baṭīn. The whole Qurʾān has been revealed in honour of the Master of the Believers, though there are persons of higher rank than he in other cycles. But since this cycle is the cycle of seventy, the Qurʾān was revealed in it, just as in every cycle of seventy something analogous has been revealed and shall be revealed. In the cycle of unveiling — the era of kashf — things higher still will come. Yet in this present cycle of seventy this Book was sent down in honour of the loftiest personage of the age, and that is the person of the Commander of the Faithful, Ḥaḍrat ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib.[^cite-28]

## Mawlānā Qanbar, the Servant of ʿAlī

I am the servant of the lord of the chargers.

The challenger of the prophets.

Master of the two qiblas.

Possessor of the two pledges.

Builder of the wall between the two ravines.

## Ali's Generosity

Among the narratives that illustrate the boundless generosity of the Master of the Universe (sayyid al-ʿālamīn), Amir al-Muʾminin ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib, the following is preserved. He was once travelling for purposes of trade, his goods loaded upon a train of camels, accompanied by his faithful servant Qambar. The caravan moved in single file along the road.

A beggar approached and asked for a loaf of bread. The Imam turned to his servant and said, "O Qambar, give this man bread." Qambar replied, "Master, the bread is inside the provision-bag (tūshadān)." "Then give him alms from what we carry." "But, master, the provision-bag is within the saddle-pack." "Then give him the saddle-pack." "The saddle-pack is upon the camel." "Then give him the camel." "But master, the camel is tethered in the file of the caravan." "Then give him the file itself."

Hearing this final command, Qambar turned and fled. When ʿAlī later found him and asked the reason for his flight, Qambar answered, "O my master, the river of your generosity was in such full flood that I feared you might give me away in charity along with the rest."

Generosity (sakhāwa), in the teachings of the Imams, is not mere benefaction but the outward sign of an inward freedom from attachment to material things — a station belonging properly to the friends of God (awliyāʾ Allāh).[^cite-30]

## The Fourth Caliph: ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib

ʿAbd Allāh ibn ʿAbbās relates that he was once sitting in the company of Amir al-Muʾminin when there entered upon them a man of striking beauty, fragrant of body and beautifully clad, with a strange radiance shining from his face. The visitor offered the following salutation:

> Peace be upon you, O Amir al-Muʾminin! You are he concerning whom the people doubt, while you yourself doubt none. Peace be upon you. You are he who draws forth the lords of meaning from the deepest of secrets, whom neither phantasms nor the senses can master, whom the people of every age have known — in the past, in the present, and in time to come. Those who know the truth (ahl al-ḥaqq) know your station. The peace of God be upon you. Peace be upon you, O fourth caliph! O great remembrance among the prophets, peace be upon you.

Amir al-Muʾminin raised his head and replied, "And upon you be peace. By what means have you known that I am the fourth caliph?"

The visitor answered: "Because Ādam is the first caliph of God, Hārūn the second caliph, Dāwūd the third caliph of the Prophet of God, and you, ʿAlī, are the fourth caliph of God and the proof of God upon His creation (ḥujjat Allāh). Your name has been ʿAlī from the first day. Whoever transgresses against you has transgressed against God, and whoever opposes you has opposed God and His Messenger."

Then the man departed and was never seen again. The people asked Amir al-Muʾminin who he had been. ## Ali's Names in His Own Words

Ḥātim ibn Ibrāhīm al-Ḥāmidī records, in his *Rumūz al-Dhakhīra*, that Mawlānā ʿAlī said:

## "This Person I Do Not Know"

Salmān al-Fārisī was once leaving the Mosque of the Prophet when he encountered Abū Bakr and ʿUmar. They asked him whence he came. He answered, "From the mosque of the Prophet." They asked who was present within. He replied, "Within the mosque is the Messenger of God, and another who was at prayer — a man whom I do not know."

The two surmised that some Arab unknown to Salmān must have been at prayer beside the Prophet. But when they reached the mosque, they were astonished to find that the man at prayer was none other than Amir al-Muʾminin ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib. They returned to the Messenger of God and said, "O Messenger of God, you have called Salmān one of the people of the household (*Salmān minnā ahl al-bayt*) and bidden us all invoke blessings upon him — yet today this same Salmān has lied to us." The Prophet answered, "Salmān's heart is pure and sound, and beautiful creatures dwell within his heart. A falsehood cannot pass from his tongue. Yet within every word of his there are a hundred hidden meanings."

They said, "O Messenger of God, we asked him who was within the mosque, and he replied that one was the Messenger of God and the other a man he did not know. Does Salmān not know ʿAlī — he who cleans his house each day with his own garments and walks in the marketplace with his two sons upon his shoulders? When men describe the virtues of ʿAlī, they enumerate no fewer than a thousand, and yet Salmān says, 'I do not know him.' If this be not a lie, what then is it?"

The Prophet said, "Go and call Salmān." When Salmān came into the Prophet's presence, the Messenger of God said, "O Salmān, these two are very angry with you." Salmān replied, "O my master, they are not in truth angry with me; rather, they are not pleased with you." The Prophet asked, "They claim that you have concealed the truth from them." Salmān said, "O Messenger of God, I have made the truth manifest." The Prophet asked, "In what manner?"

Salmān answered: "O Messenger of God — none knows God save myself and ʿAlī; none knows me save God and ʿAlī; and none knows ʿAlī save God and myself. Now, I am neither God nor the Messenger of God; therefore I said that I do not know him, for it is not within my capacity to comprehend the virtues of ʿAlī."[^cite-33]

This saying expresses, in the idiom of the early daʿwa (the summons), the doctrine that the inner reality of the Imam can only be known by one who shares his rank, and that the outward enumeration of virtues, however vast, does not amount to recognition (maʿrifa) of his true station.

## The Prostration of Salman al-Farsi before Ali

On another occasion, Salmān al-Fārisī and Abū Dharr al-Ghifārī entered the mosque where ʿAlī was present. Upon seeing him, Salmān prostrated himself. Abū Dharr said, "O Abā Muḥammad, do you prostrate before a human being? Do you bow down to one of your own kind?" When Amir al-Muʾminin came to learn of this, he said, "Were Abū Dharr to know what is in the heart of Salmān, he would slay Salmān."

By these words, he indicated the spiritual gnosis and elevated rank that lay hidden within the breast of Salmān — a knowledge so weighty that it could not safely be borne by another.

## The False Accusation of Stealing a Camel

It is reported that a certain devotee of ʿAlī (muḥibb), travelling from Kūfa, once passed into Syria, where Muʿāwiya held power. In the marketplace through which the Kūfan passed, there gathered a crowd of Syrians whose hearts were filled with rancour against the Commander of the Faithful. Recognizing the man as a partisan of ʿAlī, they devised a plot against him. Seeing that he had a camel with him, they agreed among themselves that one would step forward and claim to be the camel's owner, and that the rest would bear false witness on his behalf, so as to seize the animal and have the man punished for theft.

In accordance with the plan, one of them came forward and accused the Kūfan, saying, "This is my camel, which you have stolen." Though the victim tried to explain himself, the deceiver raised an uproar, and soon a great crowd had assembled. The accomplices then arrived and testified that the camel did indeed belong to their companion, and that the Kūfan had stolen it. The poor stranger was seized, and the case was brought before Muʿāwiya.

They repeated the accusation: the camel belonged to the Syrian, the man had stolen it, and they begged Muʿāwiya for justice and severe punishment. When Muʿāwiya asked the Kūfan to defend himself, he answered, "O Muʿāwiya, judge for yourself: how can this beast belong to this man, when what these men have all this while been claiming is not a camel at all, but a she-camel (nāqa)?"

Muʿāwiya, hearing the reply, perceived at once that the affair was a Syrian contrivance. He returned the camel to the Kūfan and said to him: "O man, return to Kūfa and say to your master, ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib: these ignorant Syrians are in such a state that they cannot distinguish between a male camel and a female; what difference, then, will you make between Muʿāwiya and ʿAlī?"

(*Majlis*, Mullā Ghulām Ḥusayn bin Ḥaḍrat Naṣr Allāh Muqaddas)

The narrative, beyond its surface humour, intimates the doctrine that recognition of the rightful Imam belongs to those endowed with discernment (tamyīz), and that those who cannot distinguish even between the things of sense will not distinguish between guidance and its counterfeit.[^cite-34]

## Sweetness or Bitterness

One day Amir al-Muʾminin ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib bade Bilāl bring a watermelon from the market. When Bilāl returned with the fruit, the Master of the Universe cut it open, tasted it, and said, "This is bitter. Take it back and bring another in its place."

When Bilāl had brought a second watermelon in exchange, Amir al-Muʾminin cut it open, tasted it, and said, "This watermelon is sweet." Then he turned to Bilāl and asked, "Bilāl, tell me why the one watermelon was bitter and the other sweet."

Bilāl answered, "O my master, you are the lord of the gnostics (sayyid al-ʿārifīn)." Mawlānā ʿAlī said, "The first watermelon, in the world of intentions (ʿālam al-niyyāt), hated me, and so it turned bitter; the second loved me in the world of intentions, and so it turned sweet."

(*Majlis* of Ḥaḍrat al-Manṣūr ʿAbd Allāh bin Hibat Allāh)

The teaching here is that the world of bodies is the outward shadow of an inward world of allegiances, and that even the fruits of the earth bear, in their taste, the trace of the souls' inclination toward — or away from — the friend of God.[^cite-35]
