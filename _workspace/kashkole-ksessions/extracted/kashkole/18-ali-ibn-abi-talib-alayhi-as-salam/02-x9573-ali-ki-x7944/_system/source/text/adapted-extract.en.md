# Maulana Ali's Virtues

## Zarrar bin Zamrah's Recollection of Ali

It is reported that when Amir Muawiyah questioned Zarrar ibn Zamrah about the character of Hazrat Ali (peace be upon him), Zarrar initially demurred, protesting the difficulty of the task. Only after Muawiyah insisted did he consent, beginning with the words: "Then listen!"

Zarrar's testimony unfolds as a portrait of profound virtue. Ali possessed farsighted vision and exceptional physical strength; his speech was lucid and unadorned, his judgments marked by absolute justice and impartiality. Fountains of wisdom emanated from his very being—yet he remained weary of the world and its entanglements, preferring the night and darkness to the distractions of daylight. By Allah as witness, during those nocturnal hours his tears flowed ceaselessly; he contemplated deeply and turned his hands in anxious prayer, speaking to himself. He wore rough garments and ate plain, unpalatable food. Yet for all this asceticism, he conducted himself with openness and ease among his companions: when questioned, he answered; when approaching them, he initiated discourse; when invited, he kept his promise and came.

We, despite our closeness to him and the simplicity of our station, lacked the courage to address him first; such awe did he command. When he smiled, his teeth appeared like a string of pearls. He revered the pious, loved the needy, and no power dared seek his support for wrongdoing—nor did any powerless being depart from him disappointed by his justice and mercy.[^cite-1]

Zarrar continued: I have witnessed countless nights when darkness spread its shroud and stars vanished from the horizon, yet Ali remained in the mosque's sanctuary, gripping his beard and wailing like one bereft of hope, hunched as if bitten by a poisonous serpent or scorpion. I heard him cry out: "O world, do you mock me or expect something from me? Expect nothing! Direct your deceit elsewhere. I have divorced you thrice—there is no hope of my returning to you. Your span is brief, your rewards paltry, your dangers terrible and extreme. How meager are the provisions, how long the journey, how desolate the path!"

Upon hearing this account, Muawiyah wept bitterly, tears flowing down his beard until he choked. At last he said: "May Allah have mercy upon Abul Hassan (peace be upon him)—he was precisely thus. Tell me, Zarrar, how do you endure his loss?" Zarrar replied: "My grief resembles that of a mother whose child has been slaughtered in her arms—a sorrow neither controlled nor diminished."

## The Virtues of Ali ibn Abi Talib

The Prophet (peace and blessings of Allah be upon him) said of his brother and successor, ⟪ar:امیر المؤمنین علی ابن ابی طالب علیہ السلام⟫ (Amir al-Mu'minin, the Commander of the Faithful):

> ⟪ar-quote:فَضَاءِلٌ لاَ تَحْصٰی کَثْرَۃ⟫
> 
> "Ali's virtues are those that cannot be counted in their abundance."

He further declared:

> ⟪ar-quote:فَمَنْ ذَکَرَ فَضِیْلَۃَ مِنْ فَضَاءِلِہِ مُقِرَّاً بِھَا غَفَرَ اللّٰہُ لَہُ مَا تَقَدَّمَ مِنْ ذَنْبِہِ وَماَ تَاخَّرَ⟫
> 
> "Whoever acknowledges one of Ali's virtues with sincere recognition, Allah shall forgive him his former and latter sins."

And further:

> ⟪ar-quote:وَمَنْ کَتَبَ فَضِیْلَۃِ مِنْ فَضَاءِلِہِ لَمْ تَزَلْ المَلاَءِکَۃُ تَسْتَغفِرُ لَہُ مَا بَقِیَ لِذَالِکَ الْکِتَابَۃَ رَسَمٌ⟫
> 
> "Whoever writes down any of Ali's virtues, the angels shall continually ask forgiveness for him for as long as that writing endures."

Likewise:

> ⟪ar-quote:وَمَنْ اَسْتَمَعَ اِلیٰ فَضِیلَۃِ مِن فَضَاءِلِہِ غَفَرَتْ لَہُ ذُنُوبُہُ الَّتِی اِکْتَسَبَھَا بِالسَّمَاعِ⟫
> 
> "Whoever listens to any of Ali's virtues, Allah shall forgive him all sins that his ears have committed through that listening."

And:

> ⟪ar-quote:وَمَنْ نَظَرَ الیٰ فَضِیْلَۃِ مِنْ فَضَاءِلِہِ غَفَرَتْ لَہُ ذُنُوبُہُ اَلَّتِی اکْتَسَبَھَا بِالنَّظَرِ⟫
> 
> "Whoever gazes upon any of Ali's virtues, Allah shall forgive him all sins that his eyes have committed through that gazing."

The Prophet (peace be upon him) summed this abundantly:

> ⟪ar-quote:لَوْکَانَ الْغَیَاضَ اَقلامٌ وَالبَحْرُ مِدَادٌ وَالْجِنُّ حِسَابٌ وَلاِنْسُ کِتَابٌ مَا اَحْصُوْا فَضَاءِلُ اَمِیْرِ المُؤمِنِیْنَ عَلِی اِبْنِ اَبِی طَالِب⟫
> 
> "Even if the forests were made into pens, the sea into ink, the jinn became accountants, and men became scribes, they could not enumerate the virtues of Amir al-Mu'minin, Ali ibn Abi Talib."

### Ali's Face

Blessed is the best and noblest servant of ⟪ar:اللہ عزوجل⟫ (Allah, the Mighty and Majestic), ⟪ar:محمد مصطفی صلی اللہ علیہ وسلم⟫ (Muhammad, the Chosen One, peace and blessings be upon him), ⟪ar:رب العٰلمین⟫ (the Lord of all Worlds), ⟪ar:رحمت للعالمین و سید المرسلین⟫ (a mercy to all peoples and the master of the Messengers). The Messenger of Allah (upon whom be peace and blessings) honored him with a singular title. He said: "Ali's station with me is as the head with the body."[^cite-2] Just as the head, when separated from the body, renders the human being lifeless, so too does the head identify and distinguish a person; it is, as it were, the gateway through which one recognizes another. Referring to this truth, the Prophet declared:

> ⟪ar-quote:اَنَا مَدِینَۃُ اَلعِلمِ وَعَلِیٌّ بَابُھاَ⟫
> 
> "I am the city of knowledge, and Ali is its gate."

Whoever desires to seek knowledge must approach it through Ali.

The human face is the most beautiful and noble organ of the entire body. If a being of lofty station possesses a face, then the perfections within that being are equally exalted. The highest among all Prophets and Messengers is the Holy Prophet (peace and blessings of Allah be upon him). When the Prophet himself refers to someone as his "face," how elevated and virtuous must that person be! The Messenger of Allah (upon whom be peace and blessings) declared:

> ⟪ar-quote:مَنْ اَرَادَ اَن یَّنظُرُ اِلَی آدَمَ فِی عِلْمِہِ وَ اِلَی نُوْحِ فِی التَّقوٰی وَ اِلَی اِبْرَاھِیْمِ فِی خُلَّتِۃِ وَاِلَی مُوْسٰی فِی ھَیْبَۃِ وَ اِلَی عِیسٰی فِی زُھدِہِ وَاِلَی سُلَیماَنَ فِی حِکمَتِہِ وَالأَیُّوبِ فِی الصَّبرِہِ فَالیَنْظُر اِلَی وَجْہِ عَلِی اِبْنِ اَبِی طَالِبْ⟫
> 
> "Whoever wishes to observe Adam in his knowledge, Noah in his piety, Abraham in his intimate friendship, Moses in his might, Jesus in his asceticism, Solomon in his wisdom, and Job in his patience—let him gaze upon the face of Ali ibn Abi Talib."

This saying of the Prophet makes abundantly clear that all the qualities dispersed throughout the one hundred twenty-four thousand prophets are concentrated and perfected in Ali. The Prophet thus instructs: "Look upon Ali's face, for the face is the most visible and apparent aspect of a person." Indeed, the Prophet further said: "To gaze upon Ali's face is an act of worship."[^cite-3] The love of Ali burns away sins as fire reduces iron to ash.

The learned scholar ⟪ar:راس الحدود مولوی عباس ندوی صاحب⟫ (Maulvi Abbas Nadwi) composed a teaching verse. He says: If you invoke the name of Muhammad, ⟪ar:'تعلیم'⟫ (instruction) is complete; the solution to the name of Ali, ⟪ar:'تعلیم'⟫ (instruction), is difficult.

How beautifully a Persian poet has associated the Master of the Universe with *Surah al-Ikhlas* (the Chapter of Purity):

> ⟪ar:یا علی ذاتک ثبوت قل ھو اللہ احد
> نام تو نقش نگیں امر اللہ الصمد

The great jurist Imam al-Shafi'i (may Allah be pleased with him) affirmed:

> ⟪ar-quote:کَفَا فِی فَضلِ مَولاَناَ عَلِیٍّ وُقُوعِ الشَّکِّ فِیہِ اِنَّ ھو اللہ⟫
> 
> "It suffices as proof of Maulana Ali's virtue that doubt itself arises as to whether he is Allah."

### The Messenger of Allah Establishes Ali's Station

It is narrated in *Kifayat al-Talib* that a dispute arose among the people concerning who held the closest station to the Messenger of Allah among his Companions. One said it was Abu Bakr, for he had rendered the Prophet great favors and had been his companion in the cave. Another maintained it was ⟪ar:'عمر ابن خطاب'⟫ (Umar ibn al-Khattab), for he was also the Prophet's father-in-law and possessed great reverence. A third claimed ⟪ar:'عثمان بن افعان'⟫ (Uthman ibn Affan) held this rank because he too was the Prophet's son-in-law and was immensely wealthy. Yet another group insisted: "All of you are wrong. It is ⟪ar:'علی ابن ابی طالب'⟫ (Ali ibn Abi Talib)."[^cite-4]

When the Messenger of Allah learned of this dispute, he gathered the four factions and inquired of them. All confessed to their beliefs. Then ⟪ar:اسامہ بن زید⟫ (Usamah ibn Zayd) rose and said: "O Messenger of Allah, settle this matter with your blessed tongue this day so that the argument may be concluded and the truth become evident." The Messenger of Allah replied: "Gather yourselves tomorrow. I shall give you a parable that will remove the rust from your hearts, and you yourselves will understand what is right—there will be no need for me to speak further." The people departed, and the next morning they reassembled in great numbers.

The Messenger of Allah then said: "Tell me what you wish." They reiterated their request. He said: "Those of you who are closest to me in genealogy and kinship—let them sit on my right side." At that moment, Imam Ali (peace be upon him) sat immediately beside the Prophet, while ⟪ar:ابوبکر و عمر⟫ (Abu Bakr and Umar) sat among the tribes of ⟪ar:بنی عُدَی⟫ (Banu Adi) and ⟪ar:تمیم⟫ (Tamim), the Prophet's kinship tribes. ⟪ar:عثمان⟫ (Uthman) sat closer to the Messenger than they did because he belonged to ⟪ar:عبد مناف⟫ (Abdu Manaf). The Messenger of Allah asked: "Do you understand now? This is your order with me in terms of kinship." He then commanded: "Now stand and sit near me in the order of who first believed in Islam—⟪ar:'سباقیت فی الایمان'⟫ (precedence in faith)." Ali again sat at his side, while ⟪ar:عثمان⟫ (Uthman) came last in the new arrangement. Then he said: "Now sit near me in the order of who among you is most learned." Even then, Ali sat beside him while the others rearranged themselves. Finally, he commanded: "Now sit near me in the order of who among you is the most courageous and ⟪ar:شجیع⟫ (valiant) in battle." Again, Ali took his place at the Prophet's side, and the others shifted once more.

Then the Prophet addressed Ali directly, saying: "O Ali! Only the truly learned understand this parable, and the oppressors shall never accept it." Turning to the assembly, he proclaimed:

> ⟪ar-quote:یا أیھا الناس! أول من آمن، علی ابن ابی طالب⟫
> 
> "O people! Ali ibn Abi Talib is the first of the believers."[^cite-5]

The Prophet then declared: "Ali is the closest to me among the relatives, the most knowledgeable in matters of religion, and he who fights the enemies in Allah's way with his sword. He is my brother, my cousin, and after me he is my caliph. When he commands, he commands in my stead; when he fights, he fights with my sword. Only the believer loves him, and only the disbeliever hates him. His nearness to me in this world shall be with me in the Hereafter. The banner of praise—⟪ar:لِواءُ الحمد⟫ (the Standard of Praise)—shall be in Ali's hands. He is ⟪ar:قسیم النار والجنۃ⟫ (the Divider of Fire and Paradise)—through his love comes Paradise, through his enmity comes the Fire. ⟪ar:علی حبہ جنۃ قسیم النار والجنۃ وصی مصطفی حق امام الانس والجنۃ⟫"

## The Sermon at the Mosque of Kufa

Upon his return to Kufa from the Battle of Nahrawan, ⟪ar:أمیر المومنین علی ابن ابی طالب علیہ السلام⟫ (Amir al-Mu'minin, Ali ibn Abi Talib, peace be upon him) delivered a discourse in the mosque of Kufa in which he introduced himself and his rank:

After praising Allah and invoking blessings upon Muhammad (peace be upon him) and his progeny, he said: I am the first of the believers. I am the first Muslim. I am the first of the worshippers. I am the first to perform the fast. I am the first of those who strive in Allah's way. I am the strong rope of Allah. I am the unsheathed sword of the Messenger of the Lord of all Worlds. I am the Greater Truthful One (al-Siddiq al-Akbar). I am the Greatest Distinguisher between truth and falsehood (al-Faruq al-Azam). I am the Gate of the City of Knowledge, and I am the highest refuge of forbearance. I am the guide to the right path. I am the one who gives verdicts with justice. I am the lamp of religion. I am the Commander of the Faithful. I am the Imam of the Godfearing. I am the master of the righteous. I am the leader of the witnesses. I am the piercing meteor. I am the punishment of Allah. I am the ocean of knowledge that never dries up. I am a noble one whose nobility cannot be eclipsed. I am he who surrounds the murderers of polytheists and destroys the disbelievers. I am the helper of the believers and the guide of the righteous. I am he who breaks the teeth of Hell and turns its wheels. I am he who herds the people of Hell toward it and torments them. My name is ⟪ar:'ایلیا'⟫ (Ilya) in the Torah of the Prophets of old, ⟪ar:'اوریا'⟫ (Auriya) in ⟪ar:'علی'⟫ (Ali) the Torah of the latter generation, and in the Qur'an—a knowledge possessed only by those who truly know. I am the righteous one whom Allah has commanded you to obey. I am ⟪ar:صالح المومنین⟫ (the Righteous Among the Believers). I am the one who gives the call to prayer in this world and in the Hereafter. I am the giver of zakat in the state of bowing. I am Fati, son of Fati, and brother of Fati. I am he whom Allah praised in ⟪ar:سورۃ 'ھل اتی'⟫ (Surah *Hal Atahu*—the Chapter of the Gifts). I am the face of Allah and the side of Allah. I am the embodiment of Allah's knowledge. I am he who possesses all knowledge of the past and future until the Day of Judgment. No one else can claim this, nor can anyone remove me from this rank. Allah has illumined my heart and loves my deeds. My Lord has granted me wisdom and nourished me with it. Since the moment I drew breath, I have never committed association of partners with Allah, not even for the twinkling of an eye. I have slain the chiefs of Arabia and their knights, and destroyed their rebels and their champions.

O people! Ask me about the knowledge of Allah and the wisdom stored within me![^cite-6]

## Tidings from *Surah al-Kawthar*

It is narrated that when Allah informed the Messenger of Allah (peace and blessings be upon him) of the oppression that the Umayyads would inflict upon his children and of unworthy persons who would ascend his pulpit, he became deeply troubled and grieved. At that moment, the Almighty revealed ⟪ar:سورۃ الکوثر⟫ (*Surah al-Kawthar*, the Chapter of Abundance) with glad tidings:

> "We have bestowed upon you ⟪ar:'کوثر'⟫ (abundance)."

We understand this thus: when the distress concerned his children, the glad tidings must equally concern his children. Since his descendants shall continue through his successor until the Day of Judgment, the glad tidings of ⟪ar:'علی'⟫ (Ali) must belong to his Companions and not to the other companions of the Prophet.

How may we establish and argue that this glad tidings is solely for Ali and pertains to ⟪ar:کوثر⟫ the gift that Allah bestowed upon the Prophet? The answer lies in combining the forty-three letters of this very Surah in diverse arrangements without any diminution or exaggeration, and in identifying who the Prophet's true adversary was:

> ⟪ar-quote:الا ان الکوثر الطاھر وصیک علی ان تنحو فان شانئک بو بکر⟫
> 
> "Behold! The pure abundance (Kawthar) is your successor, Ali—so turn toward him. For your enemy is Abu Bakr."[^cite-7]

## The Virtues of Ali in Qur'anic Verses

These verses of the Qur'an reveal through word, meaning, appellation, numerical value, and clear declaration the caliphate, truthfulness, and superiority of Imam Ali:

> ⟪quran 15:41⟫
> ⟪ar:‏قَالَ هَذَا صِرَطٌ عَلَىَّ مُسْتَقِيمٌ ‎⟫
> 
> *(The Lord) said: "This is the Way which will lead straight to Me."*

The interpretations of this verse by the exegetes have generally been vague. Some have rendered ⟪ar:'علی'⟫ (ali) as equivalent to ⟪ar:'الی'⟫ (ila), meaning "toward," so that the sense is "a path straight toward me." This is incompatible with the eloquence of Allah's word. Recognizing this error, certain commentators have interpreted the verse thus: "This is a path upon me, a straight (one)." Yet even this explanation falters, for the virtue of a path lies in its straightness, not in elevation. According to the people of truth, however, the verse reads: "This is a path—ali (Ali) is the straight (one)," signifying that Ali's path is the straight path. When the verse is recited in this manner, there is no discord and the meaning becomes perfectly clear.[^cite-8]

The noble scholar Shakir has noted that the numerical value of ⟪ar:'یمین'⟫ (the right hand) equals one hundred ten, as does ⟪ar:'علی'⟫ (Ali), and that ⟪ar:'اصحاب الیمین'⟫ (the Companions of the Right) in reality refers to ⟪ar:'اصحاب علی'⟫ (the Companions of Ali). The renowned Sufi al-Hasan al-Basri used to recite this verse in the same manner and would say: "This is the path of Ali ibn Abi Talib and the path of religion. Hold fast to it and walk upon it, for there is no error in it." Some have noted that at the confluence of the four Companions, this Qur'anic verse illuminates the straight path.

## Shared Excellences in the Names Muhammad and Ali

Both ⟪ar:أسمآء الحسنی⟫ (the Most Beautiful Names) are divine appellations that emerged in neither the Age of Ignorance nor through chance. ⟪ar:'حمد'⟫ (Hamad—praise) issued forth in ⟪ar:محمد و أحمد⟫ (Muhammad and Ahmad), while ⟪ar:'اعلی'⟫ (the Lofty) flowed into ⟪ar:علی اعظم⟫ (Ali the Greatest).

### The Union of Letters

When one combines the letters of ⟪ar:محمد و علی⟫ (Muhammad and Ali)—⟪ar:'مع'⟫ together the letter ⟪ar:میم⟫ (meem) and the letter ⟪ar:عین⟫ (ayn)—the meaning of these letters reveals the eternal and inseparable bond between the two. The Prophet (peace be upon him) himself affirmed a hadith stating: "Allah placed our light in Adam's handkerchief. Then we moved through the loins of the righteous and the wombs of the pure until it came to Maulana Abd al-Muttalib. From there I moved to the light of ⟪ar:عبد اللہ⟫ (Abdullah) and Ali to the light of ⟪ar:ابو طالب⟫ (Abu Talib, peace be upon them both), and ⟪ar:عبد اللہ⟫ (Abdullah) gave me birth and ⟪ar:ابو طالب⟫ (Abu Talib) gave Ali birth."[^cite-13]

### Numerical Harmony

Consider the numerical values: ⟪ar:محمد⟫ (Muhammad), with ⟪ar:میم⟫ (meem) equaling forty and ⟪ar:علی⟫ (Ali), with ⟪ar:عین⟫ (ayn) equaling seventy, sum to one hundred ten—the very value of ⟪ar:'علی'⟫ (Ali) alone. If one adds the remaining letters of Muhammad, they total ninety-two, which equals the numerical value of ⟪ar:'محمد'⟫ (Muhammad). This symmetry appears in no other comparison:

| Muhammad | Ali | Total |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 92 | 110 | 202 |

The sum of two hundred two equals the numerical value of ⟪ar:'رب'⟫ (Rabb—Lord), indicating that ⟪ar:محمد الرسول اللہ⟫ (Muhammad, the Messenger of Allah) and ⟪ar:علی ولی اللہ⟫ (Ali, the Guardian of Allah) together constitute the meaning of ⟪ar:رب⟫ (Lord):

| Muhammad | Ali | Lord |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 92 | 110 | 202 |

### The Two Frameworks

⟪ar:رسول اللہ صلی اللہ علیہ و آلہ وسلم⟫ (the Messenger of Allah) originated from ⟪ar:'اسلام'⟫ (Islam—surrender), while ⟪ar:امیر المومنین علی ابن ابی طالب علیہ السلام⟫ (the Commander of the Faithful) originated from ⟪ar:'ایمان'⟫ (iman—faith). If one tallies the letters of Muhammad's name, the sum equals the numerical value of ⟪ar:'اسلام'⟫ (Islam); likewise, if one sums the letters of Ali's name, the total equals the numerical value of ⟪ar:'ایمان'⟫ (iman). This teaches us that Muhammad established the external structure (⟪ar:اسلامی ھیکل⟫) of religion, while Ali perfected its spiritual edifice (⟪ar:ایمانی ھیکل⟫).

### The Silent and Speaking Qur'an

The letter ⟪ar:'م'⟫ (meem) of Muhammad's name resembles a closed mouth, while the letter ⟪ar:'ع'⟫ (ayn) of Ali's name opens like an utterance. This form conveys a profound truth: Muhammad (peace be upon him) is the ⟪ar:'صامت قرآن'⟫ (Silent Qur'an)—the one who conveyed the manifest words of Scripture—while Ali is the ⟪ar:'ناطق القرآن'⟫ (Speaking Qur'an)—the one who illumined its esoteric meanings and depths.

### Lips and Distinction

In the pronunciation of Muhammad's name, the lips meet twice, signifying his mission to unite all humanity without distinction of wealth, color, or descent. As the poet Allama Iqbal declared:

> Mahmud and Ayaz stood in the same row,

Conversely, in pronouncing Ali's name, the lips separate, symbolizing his role as the one who distinguishes between truth and falsehood, and in another sense, the one who overwhelms and destroys the disbelievers. As one poet has said:

> I shall tell you why Ali's mouth opens wide—  
> The disbeliever expects to be swallowed whole.

### Blessedness of Both Names

> Take the name of Muhammad and gladden your heart;

### Birth and Death

Both ⟪ar:محمد و علی⟫ (Muhammad and Ali) were born in ⟪ar:مکۃ المعظمۃ⟫ (the Honored City of Mecca). The Prophet (peace be upon him) was commissioned on the twenty-seventh of Rajab, and Ali (peace be upon him) was born on the twenty-seventh of Rajab.[^cite-14]

Both reached the age of sixty-three years before their death. The Prophet's martyrdom resulted from poison administered by a Jewish woman, while Maulana Ali was slain by a poisoned sword—yet he received what the Prophet called ⟪ar:'شھادت سری'⟫ (secret martyrdom), while Ali received ⟪ar:'شھادت جہری'⟫ (manifest martyrdom).

## Who Is the Successor of the Messenger of Allah?

It is reported in *Kifayat al-Talib* (The Sufficiency of the Seeker) that ⟪ar:حضرت سلمان فارسی⟫ (Salman al-Farisi, may Allah be pleased with him) said: "One day I asked the Messenger of Allah: 'O Messenger of Allah, every prophet has a successor and trustee. Who is yours?' He remained silent and continued walking. When I pressed forward and called out again, he turned to me. When I cried ⟪ar:لبیک⟫ (Here I am!), I hastened to him. He said: 'O Salman, do you know who were the successors of Moses?' I replied: 'Yes, ⟪ar:یوشع بن نون⟫ (Joshua son of Nun).' He asked: 'And why was he appointed as successor?' I said: 'Because he was the most learned person of his people at that time.' The Messenger of Allah then declared: 'Hear then! My successor, my confidant, and the best of my Community is he who will fulfill my covenant, pay my debts, and honor my covenants. He is my brother in this world and the next, and that is ⟪ar:'علی ابن ابی طالب'⟫ (Ali ibn Abi Talib).'"[^cite-15]

When the Prophet (peace be upon him) designated Imam Ali as his brother, he drew attention to a particular significance. In the very first year of the Hijrah in Medina, the Prophet had established fraternal bonds among the Muhajirin and the Ansar, creating pairs of brothers from among all the Companions. Yet he did not assign Hazrat Ali to any of them. Ali then said: "O Messenger of Allah, you have established brotherhood among all the Muslims and left me without a brother." The Messenger of Allah replied: ⟪ar-quote:یا علی أنت أخی فی الدنیا و الآخرۃ⟫ (O Ali, you are my brother in this world and in the Hereafter).[^cite-16]

The Prophet continued: "You possess with me the same rank and dignity that Aaron possessed with Moses—with the sole difference that there shall be no prophet after me. You will be with me in Paradise in my household, and with you will be my daughter Fatima, the chief of the women of the worlds. You will be my companion and my friend. O Ali, if anyone questions you, saying 'Who are you?', then respond: 'I am the servant of Allah whom His Messenger has made his brother.' No one after me shall be able to make such a claim except you; whosoever claims it is a liar and a fabricator."[^cite-17]

## The Importance of Wilayat

The Almighty has created all of His creation in pairs—the tangible things visible on the outward appearance, and the intelligible things imperceptible within. As He Himself declares through revelation, every outward form has been joined with its inner reality and made its companion: the world and the Hereafter, the body and the soul. The advantage of this union lies in the complementarity of opposites: one aspect is dynamic (the soul), while the other is static (the body).

| Dynamic | Static |
| --- | --- |
| Speaker | Guardian |

God bestowed upon humankind superiority over all creatures by virtue of the faculty of speech and the capacity to utter meaningful utterances. This rational faculty comprises at least two elements: one that is dynamic (the verb) and one that is static (the noun). This demonstrates that Allah has created all existence in moderation and differentiation. These paired principles appear everywhere and in all things—this equilibrium manifests itself in every religious and worldly matter, in the external and internal, in the sensible and the intelligible.[^cite-15]

The Qur'an repeatedly affirms this principle of paired creation. Everything possesses a counterpart or equal.

The Prophet (upon him be peace) established his religion upon this very divine nature, adapting the method of worship and guidance according to this same divine pattern, which constitutes the supreme method. When a rational person reflects deeply and contemplates this reality, he comes to understand that the religion of Islam and the sharīʿah (sacred law) of the Prophet rest upon the same principles as the creation of the heavens, the earth, and the entire cosmos—namely, that all principles have been created in pairs. Once he has contemplated this thoroughly, his faith becomes firm, and he becomes a true believer—a confirmer of the Messenger. Wherever he directs his gaze and whatever commandment he considers, he finds it in accord with the order of the universe. At that moment, he achieves complete conviction regarding this great miracle of the Messenger (the Qur'ān).[^cite-16] The Holy Prophet himself declared in his tradition that Allah has placed the foundation of His religion in accordance with His creation, so that creation itself may testify to the religion and prove its unity and concordance with the divinely ordained order.

### The Six Days of Allah

Allah says in His Book: "He created the heavens and the earth in six days, and then ascended the Throne in order to preserve and administer the strong order of the universe during those six days, so that all creation would not be disturbed, scattered, and destroyed." Exalted is Allah above the notion that His words are vain or erroneous, and exalted is He beyond the claim that He was unable to create the heavens and earth in an instant or in a shorter time. He Himself states in the Holy Qur'ān: ⟪quran 2:29⟫, affirming that within two days He created the earth, and within four days He established its sustenance upon it equally for all who seek it.

These declarations of Allah rest upon eloquent wisdom. When Allah possesses the power to create something when He intends it, the question naturally arises: why did He require six days to create the universe? This reveals that these verses contain inner meanings of profound expediency and wisdom concealed within them.[^cite-17]

### Argument Regarding Wilāyat al-Wāṣī from the *Daʿāʾim al-Islām*

In the same manner, if we examine the pillars of Islamic worship, we perceive that the Messenger of Allah (peace and blessings be upon him) established the foundation of his religion upon six pillars:

| 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 | 5 | 6 |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Purity | Prayer | Almsgiving | Fasting | Pilgrimage | Struggle in the Path of God |

Just as Allah preserved these six days' labors from the seventh day (when He ascended the Throne, *Istwā ʿalā al-ʿArsh*), exempting the seventh from ordinary toil, so too did the Prophet (peace and blessings be upon him) preserve these six ordinances of the firm religion through the guardianship of his successor:

**7. Guardianship (Wilāyat)**

This is the executor and testator whom he made his brother on the day of Ghadīr Khumm, whom he appointed as the custodian of his treasury of knowledge and the gate of his wisdom. He said in his honor: "I am the city of knowledge and ʿAlī is its gate."[^cite-18] This gate is the one through which Allah commands us to enter the house; otherwise, what meaning would Allah's command and warning possess when He declares: ⟪quran 5:55⟫ (Verily, your Walī is Allah, His Messenger, and the believers who establish prayer and pay the alms-tax while bowing in humility)?[^cite-19]

Every person of common sense understands that the gate of a house is built for a specific purpose. If this were unrelated to interpretation and wisdom, it would be entirely meaningless to preach such obvious common sense. Indeed, when the Ummah disobeyed its Prophet and turned away from obedience to the Walī (Guardian), it went astray—wandering from the path of guidance. Everyone began to follow their own opinions and interpret the Qur'ān according to their own conjectures. As a result, the entire Ummah, save those upon whom Allah had mercy, fell into discord similar to the Children of Israel. They too were commanded to enter the gate in prostration and to say with their tongues "Hittat" (we have heard and we obey), yet they altered what the Messenger had said and made mockery of it.

Here too, the Ummah abandoned the chapter of knowledge and disobeyed the Prophet. The wilāyat al-wāṣī (guardianship of the executor) is the seventh duty and is the most noble, the highest in rank; the six duties precede it in establishing and sustaining its foundation.

### Argument Regarding the Guardianship of the Prophet

The proof of this lies in the saying of the wise and the people of insight: that humankind is the noblest of all creatures in this world and represents the quintessence of all cosmic powers. Though appearing last in the order of creation, humanity is comprehensive of all things, and the perfection of all is embedded within it. Humanity stands as the seventh in the order of existence:

| 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 | 5 | 6 |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Minerals | Plants | Insects of the Earth | Cattle | Beasts of Prey | Birds |

**7. Humanity**

Thus, just as Allah created humankind in the seventh rank and bestowed upon it the station of grace and perfection, so did the Prophet (peace and blessings be upon him) make the will of the seventh member and grant it superiority over all preceding six ordinances. Among creatures below the level of humanity, they are reckoned among cattle, beasts, and the like—inferior to perfection. In the same way, a person who follows the six ordinances yet does not commit himself to the will and guardianship of Amīr al-Muʾminīn (the Commander of the Faithful) will not attain the station of perfection.[^cite-20]

### Argument Regarding Wilāyat from the Stages of Fetal Development

## I Testify That Maulana ʿAlī Is the Guardian of Allah

Sayyidunā Idrīs ibn al-Ḥasan reports in his work *ʿUyūn al-Akhbār* that in the call to prayer (adhān), the Muezzin should recite aloud the testimony regarding Maulana ʿAlī as Walī Allāh (Guardian of Allah) and Muhammad, affirming that "Muhammad and ʿAlī are the best of humankind and the noblest of all beings." In the reign of Maulana al-Ḥākim bi-Amr Allāh, after the muezzin had declared the testimony of Muhammad as Messenger of Allah, he was also called to testify to Maulana ʿAlī as Walī Allāh. In making this proclamation, he was reinforcing the declaration of the Prophet (peace and blessings be upon him) which he had uttered on the day of Ghadīr Khumm: "Whomever I am the master and guardian of, ʿAlī is his master and guardian."[^cite-22] Therefore, to say "I testify that Maulana ʿAlī is Walī Allāh" is to bear witness to the guardianship of the Messenger of Allah and Amīr al-Muʾminīn and to follow the Prophet's guidance. After that, the muezzin would declare: "Muhammad and ʿAlī are the best of humankind and the noblest of all beings." With this phrase, he expressed the virtue of the Prophet and those closest to him. Obviously, his closest brother and successor was Imām ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib, his daughter was Fāṭimah al-Zahrāʾ, and his grandsons were al-Ḥasan and al-Ḥusayn. Then there are the Imāms descended from al-Ḥusayn, whom Allah chose from among the descendants of His Messenger and whom He made guides for all creation to invite them to His path.

When ʿAbd Allāh ibn ʿAbbās was asked, "Who are the relatives of the Prophet whom Allah has mentioned in this verse?" he replied: "These people are ʿAlī, Fāṭimah, al-Ḥasan, and al-Ḥusayn."

The majority of historians report that Abū Bakr ibn Abī Quḥāfah claimed, "We are the kinfolk of the Messenger of Allah." By Allah, Abū Bakr had no kinship with the essence of Allah's Messenger. What existed was merely a distant tribal relationship through Quraysh. By contrast, ʿAlī, Fāṭimah, al-Ḥasan, and al-Ḥusayn possessed a most intimate relationship with the Prophet and were the closest of all his companions.[^cite-23] The renowned poet al-Armīqī affirmed that the family of Hāshim is the family of the Prophet, for whom and because of whom the Prophet was pleased many times and angered many times. This proves that these people are foremost among all people, and no others hold such a station. Their guardianship is essential; no one else's is.

The majority of people acknowledge that we accept Muhammad (peace be upon him) as the best of all human beings because he is the Master of the Prophets and the Messengers. Yet they are not prepared to accept ʿAlī (peace be upon him) as the best of human beings, reasoning that the Prophets also walk among humanity, and it is necessary that ʿAlī cannot surpass the Prophets. All people are unanimous that the Ummah of the Seal of the Prophets is the best Ummah ever brought forth for humankind, as Allah has declared: "You are the best Ummah that has been brought forth for the people." ## Two Witnesses to the Prophet's Prophethood

Testimony, as a principle of knowledge and truth, divides into two kinds: that which affirms reality, and that which contradicts it. True testimony consists in the witness's affirmation of what actually exists—whether confirming a possession or negating what does not belong. Thus, should a person own one cow and two goats, true testimony declares both the presence of the cow and the absence of a third goat. False testimony, by contrast, inverts this correspondence with reality: it asserts the presence of what does not exist or denies what plainly does.

Witnesses themselves are likewise of two orders: those who testify by direct sight, and those who testify by report from trustworthy authority. The eyewitness (shāhid al-ʿiyān) perceives the matter with his own faculties; the hearing witness (shāhid al-samāʿ) knows through the testimony of a just and truthful intermediary, and thereafter bears witness to that verified report.[^cite-31]

Yet the testimony that encompasses all creation is singular and preeminent. Every atom of the universe bears witness to Allah's creation, His oneness (tawḥīd), and His lordship. Allah the Exalted appointed the Prophet Muḥammad Mustafā (upon whom be peace) as His manifest witness to the worlds. Thus the Qur'ān declares: "O Messenger, We have sent you as a witness unto Us."[^cite-32] The Prophet stands as both eyewitness and speaking witness—the first ray of existence through which all creation was brought forth. He himself testified: "Allah created my light first, and created the entire universe by means of my light."[^cite-33]

The divine attributes transcend human comprehension and perception; yet the Messenger of Allah perceived His oneness through the eye of intellect (ʿayn al-ʿaql). He proclaimed: "I knew not the truth save what I accomplished, and Ali accomplished it with me. None has known me save Allah and Ali, and none has known Ali save Allah and me."[^cite-34] This blessed tradition reveals that true knowledge of Allah's unity originates with the Messenger and flows through him whose heart he illuminated—ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib.

To this perfect testimony of the Prophet regarding Allah's oneness, Allah appointed two further witnesses concerning His Messenger's prophethood (nubuwwa). The first is the Qur'ān, the Book of Allah; the second is ʿAlī, the *Walī* (Guardian) of the Messenger. One proceeds from Allah Himself, appearing in the household of the Prophet; the other was born in the House of Allah, a sign of divine love and perfect correspondence—"Our matter is your matter, and your matter is Our matter."[^cite-35]

The Qur'ān stands mute and preserved; ʿAlī speaks with wisdom and knowledge. The Qur'ān is the testimony from Allah regarding the Messenger's prophethood; ʿAlī is the testimony from the Messenger himself, appointed by divine wisdom. The Qur'ān bears witness to the Prophet's deeds and states (ḥāl); ʿAlī bears witness to the Messenger's knowledge and inward secrets. The Prophet declared: "O ʿAlī, you hold toward me the station that Hārūn held toward Mūsā."[^cite-36]

This pairing—of the Qur'ān and ʿAlī as two witnesses—appears nowhere by accident. When two things are brought together in testimony, there must exist between them a fundamental similarity and correspondence (shabah, mushābaha). To cage a nightingale with a crow would be a mark of profound foolishness. Just as the Qur'ān possesses unparalleled eloquence (bayān, faṣāḥa)—concerning which Allah states: "There is no dry nor wet thing but it is in the Clear Book" (⟪ar: ما مِن دابّة على الأرض إلّا على الله رزقها⟫)—so too does ʿAlī embody unparalleled knowledge, perfection of soul, and spiritual accomplishment (kamāl). Allah says of the Imām al-Mubīn (the Clear Imam): "We have inscribed all things in the Clear Imam" (⟪ar: وكل شيء أحصيناه في إمام مبين⟫).[^cite-37]

The light of prophethood (nūr al-nubuwwa) was divided by divine wisdom into two emanations: the written testimony of the Qur'ān and the living testimony of ʿAlī. The Prophet said: "ʿAlī and I are created from a single light, and you, O ʿAlī, are from that same light."[^cite-38] This division of light signifies Allah's absolute unity—that He alone is truly One, while all else is multiplicity. ʿAlī thus stands as the eyewitness to the Prophet's state and knowledge, even as the Qur'ān stands as the eyewitness to his deeds and utterances.

When the Prophet made brotherhood (muʾākhāh) between the Emigrants (Muhājirūn) and the Helpers (Anṣār), he took ʿAlī as his brother, saying: "O ʿAlī, you are my brother in this world and in the Hereafter." The divine wisdom in this appointment manifests through the testimonies themselves: Allah bears witness through His Qur'ān, the angels bear witness through the preserved Tablet, and those possessed of knowledge (ahl al-ʿilm) bear witness through their recognition. Thus does Allah declare in the Qur'ān: "Allah bears witness that there is no god but He, and the angels and those possessed of knowledge" (⟪quran 3:18⟫).[^cite-39]

The witness to the Prophet's prophethood who bears the attributes of the people of knowledge (ahl al-ʿilm) demonstrates a remarkable multiplicity of manifestations. In the gathering of the Prophet, he appears as the soul of the Messenger; among believers, as the embodiment of perfect faith (kamāl al-īmān); among the scholars, as the master of knowledge; among the righteous, as their purifier; in the station of authority, as the commander; before the disbelievers, as the sharpest sword (ashadd); before the polytheists, as the judge of their falsehood; before the hypocrites, as their exposer. He is the mirror of the Prophet's attributes, the breaker of idols, the hand of Allah, the tongue of Allah, the supporter of Allah's religion. There is no station of spiritual perfection, no degree of inward realization, wherein this witness is absent. Truly, ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib alone is the supreme witness.

The testimony of ʿAlī differs from the testimony of the Prophet regarding divine oneness: the latter is an eyewitness testimony (shāhid ʿiyān), whereas the former is also an eyewitness testimony, yet to the Prophet's prophethood and knowledge, not to the divine essence itself. This distinction reveals divine wisdom: that the Qur'ān, being silent and unchanging, required a living witness—one who speaks, who teaches, who illuminates the meanings hidden within the divine words. The Qur'ān and ʿAlī are inseparable, as the Prophet affirmed: "ʿAlī is with the Qur'ān, and the Qur'ān is with ʿAlī; they shall not be parted until they meet me at the Pool of Kawthar."[^cite-40]

A profound matter is thus established: Allah sent two witnesses to the prophethood of His Messenger—one the written, eternal word; the other, the living, perfected human. One is speech preserved; the other is knowledge embodied. One declares the Messenger's utterances; the other declares his inner realities. And should these two witnesses possess unequal stations—one high, one low—their testimony would be rendered suspect, for an unlearned witness standing beside the Qur'ān would constitute an affront to Scripture itself. Therefore, Allah appointed as witness one whose knowledge exceeds even the eloquence of the Qur'ān—for the Qur'ān is mute, while ʿAlī is the *Nāṭiq*, the Speaker, the living interpreter of its mysteries.

The life of the Prophet comprises two aspects: the knowledge of the Messenger (ʿilm al-Rasūl) and the actions of the Messenger (ʿamal al-Rasūl). For these two realms, Allah appointed two witnesses: the Qur'ān testifies to the Messenger's deeds, while ʿAlī testifies to his knowledge and spiritual states. Neither may be omitted, for the Prophet taught: "Knowledge without action is calamity; action without knowledge is misguidance."[^cite-41]

## The Dhū al-Qarnayn of the Ummah

The epithet Dhū al-Qarnayn (he of the two horns) denotes a ruler whose dominion spans east and west—a sovereignty both temporal and spiritual. The Prophet declared to ʿAlī: "O ʿAlī, you are the leader of this Ummah and its Dhū al-Qarnayn." Though you did not rule the world in name, you are master of both ages in knowledge (ʿilm) and spiritual station (maqām).[^cite-42] The first age encompasses the era of the Prophet Muḥammad, wherein your station is the Gate of Knowledge (Bāb al-ʿIlm), and its testimony is the Qur'ān itself. The second age belongs to the Qāʾim (the Messiah who shall arise), Lord of the Resurrection and master of the Day of Judgment, to whom the descendants of the Prophet shall belong, united through the covenant (ʿahd) of ʿAlī's guardianship (wilāya). In this manner, ʿAlī became the master of two centuries (qarayn).

| **The First Century** | **The Second Century** |
|---|---|
| The Knowledge of Muḥammad (upon whom be peace) | The established will from your generation |

Yet there is another meaning. ʿAlī was struck in the face but twice: once during the Battle of the Trench, when the accursed ʿAmr ibn ʿAbd Waddād struck him upon the head; and again when Ibn Muljam the Cursed dealt him his final blow.[^cite-43] These two blows left upon his head two horn-like marks, wherefore too he is called Dhū al-Qarnayn.

The story of Dhū al-Qarnayn and the precious stones (al-jawāhir) teaches a profound lesson. It is narrated that when Dhū al-Qarnayn entered a dark cavern in search of the Water of Life, he and his companions beheld shining pebbles beneath their feet. His companions asked in wonder: "Are we walking upon stones?" Dhū al-Qarnayn replied: "These are such that whoever gathers them shall regret it, and whoever leaves them shall also regret it." His companions marveled at this paradox and divided into two groups—one gathering the stones, the other leaving them untouched. When they emerged into the light, those who had gathered beheld jewels of priceless worth, yet regretted their limitation: "Why did we take so little? Had we but gathered more!" Those who had gathered nothing lamented their deprivation: "Why did we forfeit such treasure?"[^cite-44]

The likeness of this story to ʿAlī, Dhū al-Qarnayn of the Muslim Ummah, is manifest. He leads his followers from the darkness of ignorance toward the light of purity and knowledge. The shining stones beneath their feet are the jewels of his wisdom (al-ḥikma), words of guidance that people hear but do not fully comprehend, for they remain veiled in the darkness of outward expression. Yet these words increase the worth of the human soul immeasurably, rendering it priceless.

His companions do not value these precious utterances as they ought; they listen with inattention and carelessness. The saying of Dhū al-Qarnayn—that both the taker and the non-taker shall regret—holds absolute truth. Those who benefit from the words of ʿAlī, yet incline their hearts more toward the world than toward religion, shall in the next life perceive their loss: they shall say, "If we had spent the time we devoted to worldly gain in heeding his wisdom, how great would be our felicity now!" And those who paid him no heed whatsoever shall endure a regret beyond measure—eternal poverty and deprivation.

The story of Gog and Magog (Yāʾjūj wa-Māʾjūj) reveals further depths. In his age, Dhū al-Qarnayn built a great wall against two nations, Gog and Magog, whose depredations harassed mankind. These peoples are described as stunted in stature, with rough and penetrating tongues that could pierce even hardness and render it thin as a wafer. Against them Dhū al-Qarnayn erected an impregnable wall. Each night, these nations would scratch and lick the wall, nearly breaching it; but at dawn, when the muezzin called the *Fajr* (pre-dawn prayer), the wall would return to its original wholeness, and the peoples would flee.[^cite-45]

The inner meaning (bāṭin) of this apparent narrative concerns two groups: the *Ghāli* (those who exaggerate ʿAlī's virtues beyond truth) and the *Nāṣibī* (those who diminish his station through hatred). Both are superficial worshippers, devoid of true inner knowledge (ʿilm bāṭin). They possess only outward sciences and worldly knowledge, deprived of the light of true gnosis. A wall has been erected before these materialists—the wall of covenant (ʿahd) and divine compact (mīthāq)—without which they cannot attain the knowledge of truth.

| **Gog** | **Magog** |
|---|---|
| The *Ghāli* (those who exaggerate) | The *Nāṣibī* (those who diminish) |

Their low stature signifies that their gaze does not rise toward the realm of spirit and intellect; they attain no heights. The sharp tongue with which they pierce the wall represents the eloquent speeches of these adversaries, whose clever rhetoric attempts to overcome the knowledge of the true Imāms and mock the righteous, seeking to draw them toward falsehood. When believers are brought to the verge of losing their covenant through such assaults—when the wall seems ready to crumble—the muezzins of truth (those who call to the remembrance of Allah) strengthen their convictions. Their religious knowledge and true sciences make firm the wavering thoughts of believers, who emerge renewed, as though no weakness had ever touched them. This state shall endure until the Day of Judgment.[^cite-46]

This narrative further confirms that ʿAlī is the Dhū al-Qarnayn of the Ummah, for the wall of covenant that protects people from Gog and Magog is the covenant of ʿAlī's guardianship (wilāyat Mawlānā ʿAlī).

## Al-Anẓaʿ al-Baṭīn: The Bald One, the Great-Bellied

*Al-Anẓaʿ* means bald—one upon whose head no hair grows—and *al-Baṭīn* signifies great-bellied, one whose belly is vast and capacious. The Master of the Universe is called Bald because in seventy ages, no person has attained a rank higher than his. And he is called Great-Bellied because he is the greatest scholar of inner knowledge (ʿilm bāṭin), and as the belly contains much nourishment, so his breast contains unbounded knowledge.[^cite-47]

The entire Qur'ān was revealed in honor of the highest personality of that age—though there shall be those of greater station in ages to come. Yet in this epoch of seventy years, when the Book was sent down, it was revealed in honor of the supreme person of that time, and that supreme person is ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib, the Commander of the Faithful. Each age has its culmination in seventy, and each shall continue thus until the final manifestation (Kashf).

## The Generosity of ʿAlī

It is related that once ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib set forth on a journey of commerce, riding with laden camels in a great caravan, attended by his loyal slave Qambar. The camels walked in orderly file, one behind another.

A poor beggar approached and asked ʿAlī for bread. The Commander of the Faithful said to Qambar: "O Qambar, give this beggar bread." Qambar replied: "The bread is in the saddlebag, Master." ʿAlī said: "Then give him a gift from the saddlebag." Qambar protested: "But Master, the saddlebag is upon the saddle." ʿAlī replied: "Give him the saddle then." Qambar said: "But the saddle is on the camel, Master." Without hesitation, ʿAlī declared: "Give him the camel."

When Qambar attempted to explain that the camel stood in line with the others, ʿAlī simply said: "Give him the entire caravan." At this, Qambar fled from his master's presence.

Later, when ʿAlī met his servant and asked why he had run away, Qambar replied with great apprehension: "O Master! Your river of generosity was in full flood. I feared you might give charity to me as well!"[^cite-49]

## "I Know Not This Person"

Once Hazrat Salmān al-Fārsī was departing the Mosque of the Prophet when he encountered Hazrat Abū Bakr and Hazrat ʿUmar. They asked him: "Whence do you come?"

He replied: "From the Mosque of the Prophet."

They inquired: "Who was present in the mosque?"

Salmān answered: "The Messenger of Allah was there, and another person worshipping whom I do not know."

The two companions thought surely some Arab unknown to Salmān must have been praying alongside the Prophet. Upon reaching the mosque, they were astonished to find that the other person was none other than ʿAmīr al-Muʾminīn ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib. They approached the Messenger of Allah and said: "O Messenger of Allah! Salmān claims he does not recognize one of those in the mosque. Yet Salmān is of the Ahl al-Bayt (the Family of the Prophet). Upon him be peace. Did he lie to us?"

The Prophet replied: "Salmān's heart is pure and crystalline; his tongue cannot speak falsehood. Indeed, there are a hundred secrets hidden in every word he utters."

They pressed further: "O Messenger, we asked him who were in the mosque."

The Prophet said: "And I say: the Messenger of Allah was in the mosque, and the other is one whom Salmān knows not. Does Salmān not recognize ʿAlī? Why, he sweeps the mosque with his garment and carries his two sons upon his shoulders through the market! When people recount ʿAlī's virtues, they do not enumerate fewer than a thousand. And yet Salmān says: 'I know him not.' If this is not truth, what else could it be?"[^cite-52]

The Prophet commanded: "Go, call Salmān to me."

When Salmān al-Fārsī came into the presence of the Messenger, the Prophet said: "O Salmān, these two are greatly displeased with you."

Salmān replied: "My Lord, they are not truly angry with me; they are discontented with you."

The Prophet asked: "How is this?"

Salmān said: "They claim you have hidden the truth from them, yet I have made the truth manifest."

The Prophet inquired: "How have you made it manifest?"

Salmān answered: "O Messenger of Allah, no one knows Allah except me and ʿAlī, and no one knows me except Allah and ʿAlī, and no one knows ʿAlī except Allah and me. I am neither the Messenger of Allah nor Allah Himself; therefore I said that I know him not, for it is impossible for me to know the virtues of ʿAlī."[^cite-53]

## The False Accusation of Theft

It is related that once a Lover of ʿAlī (Muḥib ʿAlī) journeyed from Kūfa to Syria, where Muʿāwiya held dominion. In the market of Kūfa through which he passed, there gathered a crowd of Syrians whose hearts burned with hatred against ʿAlī. Recognizing this devotee of ʿAlī, they hatched a wicked scheme. Observing that the man possessed a camel, they conspired together: one would claim ownership of the beast, while the others would testify in his favor, thereby seizing the camel and securing the Kūfī's punishment for theft.

According to their design, one stepped forward and cried out in the marketplace: "This camel is mine, which you have stolen!" The victim protested earnestly, yet the false accuser raised such a clamor that a great crowd assembled. Soon the confederates appeared and testified that the camel indeed belonged to their companion and that the stranger had indeed stolen it. The authorities arrested the poor traveler, and the matter was brought before Muʿāwiya's court.

The accusers repeated their charge: that the camel belonged to the Syrian and that the Kūfī had stolen it. They demanded that Muʿāwiya render justice and punish the thief severely. When Muʿāwiya questioned the Kūfī for his defense, the man replied: "O Muʿāwiya! Judge rightly: how can this camel belong to this man? For the creature they have been claiming as their own all this while is not a camel at all—it is a she-camel!"[^cite-55]

Muʿāwiya heard this reply and immediately grasped the Syrians' deception. He restored the camel to the Kūfī and said to him: "O man, return to Kūfa and tell your master, ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib, that these ignorant Syrians are in such a state that they cannot distinguish between a camel and a she-camel. What then can such folk understand of the difference between Muʿāwiya and ʿAlī?"[^cite-56]

## Sweetness or Bitterness

One day ʿAmīr al-Muʾminīn ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib asked Hazrat Bilāl to bring a watermelon from the market. When Bilāl returned with it, the Master of the Universe cut the fruit and tasted it. "It is bitter!" he declared. "Return it and bring me another."

When Bilāl brought a second melon, ʿAlī cut it and tasted it. "This one is sweet!" he proclaimed. Then he asked Bilāl: "Tell me, O Bilāl, why is one watermelon bitter and the other sweet?"

Bilāl said: "O Master, you are *Sayyid al-ʿĀrifīn* (the Master of Those Who Know Divine Mysteries)."

Mawlānā ʿAlī replied: "This watermelon harbored hatred toward me in the realm of intentions (ʿālam al-niyyat), and therefore it became bitter. The other watermelon loved me in the realm of intentions, and therefore it became sweet."[^cite-57]
