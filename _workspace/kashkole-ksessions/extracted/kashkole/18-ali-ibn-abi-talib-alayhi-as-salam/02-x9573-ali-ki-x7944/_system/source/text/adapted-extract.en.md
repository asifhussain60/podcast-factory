## Zarrar ibn Zamra's Recollection of ⟪ar:امیر المؤمنین علی ابن ابی طالب علیہ السلام⟫

It is narrated that Amir Muawiyah once inquired of Zarrar ibn Zamra concerning ⟪ar:امیر المؤمنین علی ابن ابی طالب علیہ السلام⟫. When Zarrar initially demurred, preferring to be excused from so weighty a task, Muawiyah insisted. Zarrar then complied, offering this portrait:

"His vision was farsighted and penetrating. His body possessed extraordinary strength; his speech was clear and unadorned, delivered with complete justice and equity. From his very being flowed fountains of wisdom. He turned away from worldly attachments and their entanglements, preferring the night and darkness to day. By Allah as my witness, throughout the night his tears would flow without cease; he remained absorbed in contemplation and deep thought, often turning his hands while conversing with himself. He wore rough, thick garments and consumed only stale, flavorless food. Yet he conducted himself with openness and freedom among his companions. When questioned, he would answer; when he approached others, he would initiate conversation; when invited, he kept his promise. We, despite our nearness and simplicity, could not gather courage to speak before him or begin a topic. When he smiled, his teeth appeared as a string of pearls. He revered the pious, loved the needy; no power dared seek his support for wrong causes, and no powerless being turned away disappointed from his justice and mercy.

"By Allah, I have witnessed many nights when darkness spread its cover and stars vanished from the horizon—nights when ⟪ar:امیر المؤمنین علی ابن ابی طالب علیہ السلام⟫ stood weeping in the mosque's prayer niche, holding his beard like one in distress, his body bent as if bitten by a poisonous serpent or scorpion. I heard him crying out: 'O world, are you taunting me or do you expect something from me? Expect nothing from me; turn your deception toward another. I have divorced you thrice—there remains no hope of reconciliation. Your life is short, your rewards are meager, your perils extreme. How scanty are the provisions, how long the journey, how desolate the path!'"

The narrator relates that Muawiyah wept upon hearing this, tears flowing down his beard until he choked. He finally said, "May Allah have mercy upon Abul Hassan. He was indeed as you describe. Tell me, Zarrar—how do you bear his loss?" Zarrar replied: "My grief is like that of a mother whose child was slaughtered in her arms—neither controlled nor diminished."

## The Virtues of ⟪ar:امیر المؤمنین علی ابن ابی طالب علیہ السلام⟫

⟪ar:محمد مصطفی صلی اللہ علیہ وسلم⟫ proclaimed concerning his brother and successor: "The virtues of Ali are countless in their abundance." He further declared:[^cite-1]

*Whoever acknowledges one of Ali's virtues, Allah will forgive him his previous and subsequent sins.* [^cite-2]

*Whoever writes down any of Ali's virtues—the angels shall ever beseech forgiveness for him so long as that writing endures.* [^cite-3]

*Whoever listens to an account of Ali's virtue, Allah shall forgive him all the sins his ears have incurred.* [^cite-4]

*Whoever looks upon any of Ali's virtues, Allah shall forgive him all the sins his eyes have committed.* [^cite-5]

*Even if the forests became pens, the sea became ink, the jinn became accountants, and mankind became scribes, they could not enumerate the virtues of ⟪ar:أمیر المؤمنین علی ابن ابی طالب⟫.* [^cite-6]

### Ali's Face

⟪ar:اللہ عزوجل⟫ blessed His noblest servant, ⟪ar:محمد مصطفی صلی اللہ علیہ وسلم⟫, with countless honors, ⟪ar:رب العٰلمین⟫ and ⟪ar:رحمت للعالمین و سید المرسلین⟫. The Messenger of Allah declared: "Ali's station with me is as the head is to the body." When the head separates from the body, the person perishes. The head is the means by which one recognizes a person—it is the gateway to knowledge of identity. Thus the Prophet stated:[^cite-7]

"I am the City of Knowledge, and Ali is its Gate."

Whoever seeks knowledge must approach it through Ali. The human face is the noblest and most beautiful organ in the entire body. If the face reflects higher realities, then the perfections manifested therein must be equally exalted. The highest of all prophets and messengers is ⟪ar:محمد مصطفی صلی اللہ علیہ وسلم⟫. How then can we comprehend the loftiness of one to whom the Prophet himself alludes as his own face? The Messenger of Allah proclaimed:[^cite-8]

*Whoever wishes to see Adam in his knowledge, Noah in his piety, Abraham in his intimacy with Allah, Moses in his majesty, Jesus in his asceticism, Solomon in his wisdom, and Job in his patience—let him look upon the face of Ali ibn Abi Talib.*

This utterance of the Prophet clarifies that all the virtues scattered among the one hundred twenty-four thousand prophets converge in the countenance of Ali. The Prophet thus bade us: "Gaze upon Ali's face, for the face is the most visible and manifest part of a person." He further said: "To look upon Ali's face is worship." Love of Ali burns away sins as fire consumes the coating of iron.

A poet versed in ⟪ar:' تعلیم'⟫ composed, addressing the theme of ⟪ar:'علی'⟫:[^cite-9]

*When you comprehend Ali, the teaching is complete.*

How beautifully a Persian poet has woven the Master of the Universe with Surah al-Ikhlas (Chapter 112):

⟪ar:یا علی ذاتک ثبوت قل ھو اللہ احد

نام تو نقش نگیں امر اللہ الصمد

**لم یلد**مثلت، ما درگیتی **ولم یولد**چو تو

**ولم یکن لہ**بعد از نبی مثلت **کفوا احد**⟫

Imam al-Shafi'i (may Allah be pleased with him) declared:[^cite-10]

*It suffices as proof of Maulana Ali's virtue that doubt itself concerning him is unbelief.*

## ⟪ar:رسول اللہ کا علی کے مرتبہ کو ثابت کرنا:

### ⟪ar:جابر بن عبد اللہ الانصاری رضی اللہ تعالی عنہ⟫

It is narrated that a dispute arose among the people concerning who held the closest station to the Messenger of Allah among his Companions. One party held that ⟪ar:'ابو بکر'⟫ was nearest, for he had rendered great service and shared the cave with the Prophet during the Hijrah. Another asserted that ⟪ar:'عمر ابن خطاب'⟫ was closest, being also the Prophet's father-in-law and possessing great reverence. A third claimed that ⟪ar:'عثمان بن افعان'⟫ was nearest, likewise being the Prophet's son-in-law and of considerable wealth. Yet another group insisted: "Nay, but ⟪ar:'علی ابن ابی طالب'⟫."

When the Messenger of Allah learned of this disagreement, he summoned all four parties and questioned them. They affirmed their respective positions. Then one among them—⟪ar:اسامہ بن زید⟫—rose and said: "O Messenger of Allah, settle this matter with your blessed tongue that the argument may end and truth become manifest. No need remain for further discourse." The Messenger replied: "Gather tomorrow. I shall give you an example that will remove the rust from your hearts, and you yourselves shall understand the right course. I need not speak further." The people departed, and the next day they assembled in great numbers.

The Prophet came and said: "What do you wish?" They reiterated their request. He then commanded: "Let those of you nearest to me in kinship and lineage sit on my right side." ⟪ar:أمیر المؤمنین علی ابن ابی طالب علیہ السلام⟫ sat immediately beside the Prophet, while ⟪ar:ابوبکر و عمر⟫ took their places farther away. ⟪ar:عثمان⟫ sat closer to the Prophet than they, for he belonged to the tribe of ⟪ar:عبد مناف⟫. The Prophet asked: "Do you understand now? This is your rank with me in terms of kinship." He continued: "Now sit according to ⟪ar:'سباقیت فی الایمان'⟫—those first in faith—near me." Again, Ali sat beside him while ⟪ar:عثمان⟫ came last. Then he said: "Now sit in the order of your learning." Once more, Ali took the seat nearest to the Prophet, the others rearranging themselves. Finally he commanded: "Sit according to ⟪ar:شجیع⟫—your bravery and courage." Again, Ali occupied the position closest to him, the others changing their order once more.

Then the Prophet addressed Ali directly, saying: "O Ali! Only the learned shall grasp this parable, and the oppressors shall never accept it." Turning to the assembly, he proclaimed:[^cite-11]

*"O people! ⟪ar:أول من آمن⟫ is ⟪ar:علی ابن ابی طالب⟫."*

Then he declared: "Ali is nearest to me among kinfolk, most learned in faith, and the one who fights the enemies of Allah with the sword. He is my brother, my ⟪ar:ابن عم⟫, and after me, my caliph. If he commands, it shall be as my command; if he fights, he fights with my sword. The believer loves him; the disbeliever hates him. His proximity to me in this world shall be with me in the Hereafter. The ⟪ar:لِواءُ الحمد⟫ shall be in his hand. He is the ⟪ar:قسیم النار والجنۃ⟫."[^cite-12]

*⟪ar:علی حبہ جنۃ قسیم النار والجنۃ وصی مصطفی حق امام الانس والجنۃ⟫*

## The Glad Tidings from ⟪ar:سورۃ الکوثر⟫

When ⟪ar:رسول اللہ صلی اللہ علیہ وسلم⟫ learned from the Almighty of the grievances his descendants would suffer at the hands of the Umayyads—that unworthy men would mount his pulpit—he fell into profound sorrow. At that moment, ⟪ar:اللہ عزوجل⟫ revealed the good news: "Verily, We have granted you ⟪ar:کوثر⟫."[^cite-13] 

The interpretation is thus: since the trouble concerns your descendants, the tidings must also concern them. Your successors shall continue until the Day of Judgment through your appointed heir. Therefore, the glad tidings must pertain specifically to ⟪ar:'علی'⟫, not to other companions. How do we establish that these tidings are solely for Ali? The answer lies in combining the forty-three letters of the surah in various sequences without addition or subtraction, and by identifying your true enemy:[^cite-14]

*"Verily, al-Kawthar al-tahir is your successor Ali. Indeed, your enemy is Abu Bakr."*

## The Virtues of Ali from the Verses of the Qur'an

The following Qur'anic verses demonstrate—through word, meaning, epithet, numerical value, caliphate, truthfulness, and pre-eminence—the station of Imam Ali:

> ⟪quran 15:41⟫
> ⟪ar:‏قَالَ هَذَا صِرَطٌ عَلَىَّ مُسْتَقِيمٌ ‎⟫
> *(Allah) said: "This is the Way which leads straight to Me."*

The conventional exegetical interpretation of this verse has been vague. Exegetes have read ⟪ar:'علی'⟫ as ⟪ar:'الی'⟫, rendering "the path is straight to me." Yet this contradicts the eloquence of the Divine Word. In light of this error, an alternative reading emerges:[^cite-15]

*"He said: This path is Ali—straight and direct."*

This interpretation preserves both clarity and Qur'anic eloquence, for the virtue of a path lies not merely in its being elevated, but in its being straight. According to the people of truth, the verse signifies: *"This is Ali's path; it is the straight path."* When recited thus, no obscurity remains.

It has been observed that the numerical value of ⟪ar:'یمین'⟫ (the right) equals 110, the same as that of ⟪ar:'علی'⟫, and that ⟪ar:'اصحاب الیمین'⟫ (the Companions of the Right) actually refers to ⟪ar:'اصحاب علی'⟫ (the Companions of Ali). The eminent Sufi al-Hasan al-Basri would recite this verse in this manner, declaring: "This is the path of Ali ibn Abi Talib and the path of religion. Hold fast to it and walk upon it, for there is no fault therein."

## The Shared Virtues in the Names Muhammad and Ali

Both names are among the ⟪ar:أسمآء الحسنی⟫ (Divine Beautiful Names). These were not names borne in the Age of Ignorance. ⟪ar:'حمد'⟫ derives from ⟪ar:محمد و أحمد⟫, and ⟪ar:'اعلی'⟫ derives from ⟪ar:علی اعظم⟫.[^cite-32]

Should one combine the letters ⟪ar:محمد و علی⟫—⟪ar:'مع'⟫ (together)—namely ⟪ar:میم اور عین⟫ (mim and 'ain), one discerns the eternal union between the two. There is a prophetic tradition that Allah placed our light in Adam's handkerchief (al-amanah), then transferred it through ⟪ar:ارحام زکیۃ⟫ (chaste wombs) and ⟪ar:اصلاب طاھرۃ⟫ (purified loins) until it reached the loins of ⟪ar:مولانا عبد المطلب⟫. From there, I transferred to ⟪ar:عبد اللہ⟫, and Ali transferred to ⟪ar:ابو طالب علیھم السلام⟫. Thus was ⟪ar:محمد⟫ born of ⟪ar:عبد اللہ⟫ and ⟪ar:ابو طالب⟫ gave birth to Ali.

The letter ⟪ar:میم⟫ in Muhammad equals 40, and ⟪ar:عین⟫ in ⟪ar:علی⟫ equals 70, totaling 110—the numerical value of ⟪ar:'علی'⟫. The remaining letters of Muhammad sum to 52, and those of Ali to 40, which together equal 92—the numerical value of ⟪ar:'محمد'⟫.[^cite-33]

It is narrated in ⟪ar:کفایۃ الطالب⟫ that ⟪ar:حضرت سلمان فارسی⟫ said: "One day I asked the Messenger of Allah, 'O Messenger of Allah, every prophet has a successor. Who is yours?' When the Prophet referred to Imam Ali as "his brother," he drew attention to a particular moment. ## The Importance of Wilayat

Allah the Almighty has created all of His creation in pairs—both the tangible things visible on the outward plane and the intelligible realities imperceptible to the senses. As He Himself declares in His Book, the pairing of creation reflects a fundamental cosmic principle: every external manifestation is united with its internal counterpart, just as the material world is paired with the world to come, or the body with the soul.[^cite-38] The advantage of this union of opposites lies in their complementary functions: one principle is active (as the soul animates), while the other is static (as the body receives animation).

| Active | Static |
| --- | --- |
| Speaker | Guardian |

Allah granted humanity superiority over all creatures because of the faculty of speech and the ability to utter meaningful utterances. This faculty of reason itself comprises at minimum two letters—one dynamic (a verb) and one static (a noun)—thereby demonstrating that Allah has created all things in measured balance and differentiation. These archetypal pairs are discovered everywhere and in all phenomena; this equilibrium manifests in every matter of religion and worldly life, in the external and the internal, in the sensible and the intelligible. Allah says:

> ⟪ar:‏شَرَعَ لَكُم مِّنَ ٱلدِّينِ مَا وَصَّىٰ بِهِۦ نُوحًۭا وَٱلَّذِىٓ أَوْحَيْنَآ إِلَيْكَ وَمَا وَصَّيْنَا بِهِۦٓ إِبْرَهِيمَ وَمُوسَىٰ وَعِيسَىٰٓ ۖ أَنْ أَقِيمُوا۟ ٱلدِّينَ وَلَا تَتَفَرَّقُوا۟ فِيهِ ۚ كَبُرَ عَلَى ٱلْمُشْرِكِينَ مَا تَدْعُوهُمْ إِلَيْهِ ۚ ٱللَّهُ يَجْتَبِىٓ إِلَيْهِ مَن يَشَآءُ وَيَهْدِىٓ إِلَيْهِ مَن يُنِيبُ ‎⟫

Indeed, the Messenger (upon whom be blessings) based his religion upon this same divine pattern, adapting the method of worship and guidance according to this immutable divine wisdom—which is the supreme manner. When a rational person reflects carefully upon these truths, he will come to recognize that the religion of Islam and the Sharīʿa of the Prophet embody the same principles as govern the creation of the heavens, the earth, and the entire universe: all principles are established in pairs. After deep contemplation, his faith becomes unshakeable, and he becomes a true believer—that is, one who confirms the Messenger. Wherever he turns his gaze, whatever command he considers, he discovers it reflected in the structure of creation. At that moment, he attains complete conviction in this great miracle of the Messenger—the Qur'ān.[^cite-39] The Holy Prophet himself declared in his sacred tradition that Allah has laid the foundation of His religion in accordance with His creation, so that creation itself bears witness to religion and proves its oneness and unity with the divine order.

### The Six Days of Allah

Allah says in His Book: "He created the heavens and the earth in six days, and on the seventh day He established Himself upon the Throne in order to preserve and administer the strong order of the universe."[^cite-40] The Almighty is exalted and glorified above the supposition that His words could be vain or erroneous, and He is infinitely above being unable to create the heavens and earth in an instant, or in even briefer span. Yet He Himself declares in the Holy Qur'ān:

> ⟪quran 17:77⟫
> ⟪ar:‏سُنَّةَ مَن قَدْ أَرْسَلْنَا قَبْلَكَ مِن رُّسُلِنَا ۖ وَلَا تَجِدُ لِسُنَّتِنَا تَحْوِيلًا ‎⟫

These utterances of Allah are grounded in eloquent wisdom. When Allah possesses the power to create anything at the moment of His will, one must ask: why did the creation of the universe require six days? This question indicates that this verse contains inner meanings—*taʾwīl* (esoteric exegesis)—of profound import and hidden expediency.

## I Bear Witness: Maulana Ali, the Guardian of Allah

Sayyiduna Idrīs ibn Ḥasan records in his work *ʿUyūn al-Akhbār* that in the sacred call to prayer (*adhān*), after the testimony to Muhammad as the Messenger of Allah, there should be recited a testimony to Maulana Ali as the Guardian of Allah and to Muhammad and Ali as the best of humankind and the most fragrant of the elect. During the reign of Maulana al-Ḥākim al-Mustanṣir bi-llāh (may his soul be sanctified), this proclamation of "I bear witness that Maulana Ali is the Guardian of Allah" (*Ashhadu anna Maulanā ʿAlī Walī Allāh*) was indeed recited immediately after the muezzin's witness to Muhammad. In making this declaration, the muezzin was reinforcing the pronouncement of the Prophet himself, which he had made on the day of Ghadīr Khumm: "Whosoever's master I am, Ali is his master."[^cite-42] To proclaim "I bear witness that Maulana Ali is the Guardian of Allah" is to testify to the guardianship of the successor of the Messenger and to affirm one's following of the Prophet. The muezzin would then declare: "Muhammad and Ali are the best of humankind and the most fragrant of the elect." With these words, he expressed the excellence of the Prophet and those nearest to him. Manifestly, his nearest brother and successor was the Prince of the Believers, Imam ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib; his daughter was Fāṭima al-Zahrāʾ; his grandsons were al-Ḥasan and al-Ḥusayn. And after them came the Imams descended from al-Ḥusayn, whom Allah chose from among the descendants of His Messenger and appointed as guides for all creation, summoning them to His path.

When ʿAbd Allāh ibn ʿAbbās was asked, "Who are the relatives of the Prophet whom Allah mentions in the verse," he replied: "These are ʿAlī, Fāṭima, al-Ḥasan, and al-Ḥusayn."[^cite-43] The majority narrate that when Abū Bakr ibn Abī Quḥāfa claimed, "We are the family of the Messenger," this assertion possessed no truth whatsoever. Though his connection to the Messenger derived from Qurayshin kinship, even granting this a relationship, it was exceedingly remote. By contrast, ʿAlī, Fāṭima, al-Ḥasan, and al-Ḥusayn possessed the closest and most direct relationship to the Prophet—they were the noblest among all the Companions. The celebrated poet Armakīt proclaimed that the family of Hāshim is the family of the Prophet, for whom and because of whom the Prophet felt joy countless times and anger countless times. This reality proves that these people are the foremost of all people, and none other. Their guardianship is established by necessity, not that of anyone else.

The majority of people concede that we acknowledge Muḥammad (upon whom be peace) as the best of human beings because he is the Master of the Prophets and Messengers. Yet they hesitate to acknowledge ʿAlī (upon whom be peace) as best among humankind, reasoning that prophets too are numbered among humanity, and therefore it seems impossible that ʿAlī could surpass the Prophets. However, all are unanimous that the Community of the Seal of Prophecy is the best Community ever brought forth, as Allah says: "You are the best community brought forth for mankind."[^cite-44] They also acknowledge that within subsequent generations are found the spiritual heirs of the prophets and the saints of immense virtue. The Messenger of Allah transmitted this tradition through Ḥudhayfa ibn al-Yamān and others:

> I leave among you two weighty things. So long as you hold fast to them, you shall never go astray: the Book of Allah and my Family. They are as the Ark of Noah—whosoever embarks upon it is saved, and whosoever is left behind is drowned.

The Prophet said to ʿAlī (upon whom be peace): "ʿAlī is the best of all human beings. Whoever denies this has committed disbelief."[^cite-45]

### An Illuminating Narrative

This account took place during the time of Maulana Imam al-Ḥākim (may his soul be sanctified). ʿAlī ibn al-Ḥusayn al-Qāriʾ relates: One day I stood with Imam al-Ḥākim near al-Farāfra, close by the mosque of al-Qāḍī, when suddenly a commotion arose. He dispatched someone to inquire into its cause. The messenger returned with news: "O Maulana! A dispute has broken out between two people of al-Aftāḥ." He commanded them to be brought before him. When they arrived, I observed an Abyssinian holding a man by the collar with such force that the man could scarcely breathe. The Imam ordered the Abyssinian to release him and asked, "What is the matter? Why do you struggle so fiercely, and who are you?"

The Abyssinian replied, "I am the muezzin of al-Aftāḥ, and this man is a preacher." The Imam asked, "What is the cause of your dispute?" The Abyssinian answered: "O Maulana, this man spoke words in our city today that we had never heard before." "What were they?" inquired the Imam. The Abyssinian said: "In the call to prayer, he proclaimed: *Muḥammad wa ʿAlī Khayru al-Bashar wa Atrathumā Khayru al-ʿAṭṭār*—Muhammad and ʿAlī are the best of humankind and the most fragrant of the elect. We have never heard the call to prayer recited in this manner. It is absolutely unprecedented and strange to us. It may be permissible to call Muḥammad the best, but it is impermissible to call ʿAlī the best, for there are Adam, Noah, Abraham, Moses, and Jesus—it is not lawful to say that ʿAlī surpasses these Prophets and Messengers."

Maulana al-Ḥākim said to the Abyssinian: "O man, be assured! I shall prove to you in a few brief statements that ʿAlī is indeed the best of humankind. First, tell me: are you familiar with the prophetic traditions?" The Abyssinian replied, "Yes, I know many ḥadīths." The Imam said, "I shall narrate a ḥadīth to you. If you find it authentic, acknowledge it; if not, I shall present another."

Here the Imam narrated through a reliable chain: ʿAbd Allāh ibn al-ʿAbbās reported that one day we were in the presence of the Messenger of Allah. He was instructing us in the knowledge of religion when his daughter Fāṭima (upon whom be peace) suddenly arrived in tears. The Messenger of Allah rose in alarm and asked why she was weeping. She said, "My two sons, al-Ḥasan and al-Ḥusayn, have gone missing. My heart is troubled for them." The Prophet looked toward the heavens and prayed with great anxiety. Gabriel descended immediately and said, "O Muḥammad, do not fear. Peace be upon you, and peace upon the righteous. Your two sons, al-Ḥasan and al-Ḥusayn, have gone to the garden of Banī Najjār and fallen asleep at play. I have sent them a merciful and faithful angel, who has made a bed for them on one arm and a covering on the other. Go and bring your grandsons."

When the Prophet heard this news, he went to Banī Najjār with his companions and found his two grandsons sleeping, with an angel guarding over them. The Messenger lifted them up and sat them upon his shoulders. Abū Bakr said, "O Messenger of Allah, give them to me and I shall carry them." The Messenger of Allah refused his request, saying, "I shall carry them, for just as their rider is noble, so too are these two riders, and their father is better than both of them."[^cite-46] He then came to the house to bring Fāṭima the good news. A great gathering had assembled there in anxiety during this time. Addressing the assembly, he said:

"O people! Shall I not inform you of a man whose father and mother are the best of all men? These are al-Ḥasan and al-Ḥusayn, whose father is ʿAlī, my successor and the best of the Successors, and whose mother is Fāṭima, the best of all women. These are al-Ḥasan and al-Ḥusayn, whose grandfather is the Messenger of Allah and whose grandmother is Khadīja al-Kubrā, the first to believe in Allah and His Messenger.

O people! Shall I not tell you of those whose uncles and aunts are the best? These are al-Ḥasan and al-Ḥusayn, whose uncle is Jaʿfar ibn Abī Ṭālib and whose paternal aunt is Umm Hānī bint Abī Ṭālib, who never associated partners with Allah. O people! Shall I not inform you of those whose maternal uncles and aunts are the best? These are al-Ḥasan and al-Ḥusayn, whose maternal uncle is Qāsim, son of the Messenger of Allah, and whose aunt is Zaynab, daughter of the Messenger.

After this introduction, he declared: 'Allah knows that these two, their father, their mother, their grandfather, their grandmother, their uncles, their paternal uncles, their aunts, their Shīʿa and their lovers—all are among the people of Paradise.'"[^cite-47]

After presenting this ḥadīth, Imam al-Ḥākim asked the Abyssinian, "Is this ḥadīth authentic?" The Abyssinian replied, "Yes, O Prince of the Believers, this ḥadīth is sound." Then the Imam narrated additional ḥadīths and said:

"It is also transmitted in a ḥadīth that the Messenger of Allah, while addressing the congregation, declared: 'Al-Ḥasan and al-Ḥusayn are the two leaders of truth among the youth of Paradise, whether they rise to claim their rights or sit quietly, and their father is better than both of them.'"[^cite-48]

Then he said, "My two sons are the leaders of the youth of Paradise."

"Are these two ḥadīths authentic or not?" asked the Imam. The Abyssinian said, "Yes, O Prince of the Believers, you are right." The Imam then asked, "Have you heard the ḥadīth that all those who enter Paradise are young and shall not grow old?" The Abyssinian replied, "Yes, O Prince of the Believers, I have heard this ḥadīth and it is authentic."

The Imam then said, "O man, listen carefully! Will Adam, Noah, Abraham, Moses, and Jesus enter Paradise or not? And if so, will they be young?" The answer was: "Yes, O Prince of the Believers, it must be so." The Imam said, "Consider! When al-Ḥasan and al-Ḥusayn are the leaders of the youth of Paradise and their father is better than both of them, then it is undoubtedly proven that ʿAlī is the best of all human beings, just as Muḥammad (upon whom be peace) is the best of all human beings."

The Abyssinian accepted defeat at this reasoning and declared, "I believe and I confirm!" (*Āmant wa Ṣaddaqt*). Maulana al-Ḥākim then turned to the preacher and said, "Now you may call the adhān as you wish. No one shall be permitted to object."[^cite-49]

### The Reality of Ali, Guardian of Allah

This proclamation was included in the call to prayer by Maulana the Prince of the Believers, ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib (upon whom be peace and blessings), when he was given authority by the command of the Prophet (upon whom be peace). Just as Allah testifies to His Oneness by saying: "Allah testifies that there is no deity except Him,"[^cite-50] and the Messenger of Allah testified to his prophethood by saying: "I bear witness that Muḥammad is the Messenger of Allah," so ʿAlī proclaimed in honor of his guardianship:

> ⟪quran 5:55⟫
> ⟪ar:‏إِنَّمَا وَلِيُّكُمُ ٱللَّهُ وَرَسُولُهُۥ وَٱلَّذِينَ آمَنُوا۟ ٱلَّذِينَ يُقِيمُونَ ٱلصَّلَوٰةَ وَيُؤْتُونَ ٱلزَّكَوٰةَ وَهُمْ رَاكِعُونَ ‎⟫

After this verse was revealed, he added testimony to his martyrdom together with his succession in principle—this being his rightful due.

"I bear witness that ʿAlī is the Guardian of Allah."

## Qur'ānic Arguments for ʿAlī as the Best of Humankind

These Qur'ānic proofs were adduced to respond to the objection of certain Muslims that the Shīʿa have committed grave error by adding to the call to prayer the phrase "Muḥammad and ʿAlī are the best of humankind and the most fragrant of the elect." They concede that Muḥammad (upon whom be peace) is certainly the best of humankind, yet they maintain it is erroneous to accord this designation to ʿAlī and his descendants.

### The First Argument

Allah declares in His Book:

> ⟪quran 33:33⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَقَرْنَ فِى بُيُوتِكُنَّ وَلَا تَبَرَّجْنَ تَبَرُّجَ ٱلْجَهِلِيَّةِ ٱلْأُولَىٰۖ وَأَقِمْنَ ٱلصَّلَوٰةَ وَآتِينَ ٱلزَّكَوٰةَ وَأَطِعْنَ ٱللَّهُ وَرَسُولَهُۚۥٓ إِنَّمَا يُرِيدُ ٱللَّهُ لِيُذْهِبَ عَنكُمُ ٱلرِّجْسَ أَهْلَ ٱلْبَيْتِ وَيُطَهِّرَكُمْ تَطْهِيرًۭا ‎⟫

Allah wishes to remove all uncleanness from the Ahl al-Bayt (the People of the House) and to purify them with a perfect purification.[^cite-51] That is, Allah has rendered the Ahl al-Bayt equal to the Messenger of Allah (upon whom be peace) in purity and sanctity. Among the Ahl al-Bayt, none stands closer to the Prophet than Maulana ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib, his successor, guardian, and caliph. ʿAlī was thus made similar to the Prophet in ritual and spiritual purity, and by virtue of this similarity he partook of the *akhirat* (goodness). Therefore, it stands proven that ʿAlī is the best of humankind after the Prophet, and his descendants likewise became the best of humankind because they too are of the Ahl al-Bayt of the Prophet and possess, like Muḥammad and ʿAlī, ritual and spiritual purity. This is a Qur'ānic proof that no Muslim can refute.

### The Second Argument

Allah declares:

> ⟪quran 37:130⟫
> ⟪ar:‏سَلَمٌ عَلَىٰٓ آلِ يَاسِينَ ‎⟫
>
> ⟪quran 37:131⟫
> ⟪ar:‏إِنَّا كَذَلِكَ نَجْزِى ٱلْمُحْسِنِينَ ‎⟫

"Peace be upon the family of Yāsīn; thus do We reward the virtuous."[^cite-52] Al-Yāsīn designates the Imams from the descendants of the Prophet, who are indeed his family. Therefore, those whom Allah associates with the Messenger in salutation are the family of the Messenger. The most excellent among them and the nearest to the Prophet is undoubtedly ʿAlī, whom the Prophet appointed as his successor and brother. Thus it is proven that the best of the family of Yāsīn, those upon whom Allah sends peace and whom He calls the virtuous, is he who is the best of all humankind—and that is ʿAlī (upon whom be peace). And his descendants after him are the best of humankind, for they are truly the spiritual heirs of the Messenger of Allah.

### The Third Argument

Allah declares:

> ⟪quran 3:110⟫
> ⟪ar:‏كُنتُمْ خَيْرَ أُمَّةٍ أُخْرِجَتْ لِلنَّاسِ تَأْمُرُونَ بِٱلْمَعْرُوفِ وَتَنْهَوْنَ عَنِ ٱلْمُنكَرِ وَتُؤْمِنُونَ بِٱللَّهِ ۗ وَلَوْ آمَنَ أَهْلُ ٱلْكِتَبِ لَكَانَ خَيْرًۭا لَّهُم ۚ مِّنْهُمُ ٱلْمُؤْمِنُونَ وَأَكْثَرُهُمُ ٱلْفَسِقُونَ ‎⟫

"You are the best community brought forth for mankind. You enjoin what is right and forbid what is wrong, and you believe in Allah."[^cite-53] The Ahl al-Bayt to whom this verse is addressed, and who constitute the best of the Community, are certainly the Ahl al-Bayt, for they are the ones who most fervently command what is right and forbid what is wrong. Since the Ahl al-Bayt of the Prophet are the best of the Community and the best among them is the Prince of the Believers, it stands proven that ʿAlī is the best of humankind after the Prophet. This is a manifest matter admitting of no doubt.

"Allah chose Adam, Noah, the family of Abraham, and the family of ʿImrān above all the worlds."[^cite-65] From this verse it is transparently clear that Allah selected the family of Abraham and the family of ʿImrān in the very manner He had chosen Adam and Noah for prophethood. Therefore, it stands proven that the family of the Prophet is superior to the family of Abraham and the family of ʿImrān. For ʿAlī is the most excellent and most noble of the Prophet's family.

## Two Witnesses to the Messenger's Prophethood

Testimony exists in two fundamental forms: the true and the false. True testimony affirms what genuinely belongs to a subject or negates what does not. If a person possesses one cow and two goats, truthful witness declares both that "he owns one cow" and "he does not own two cows"; similarly, "he owns two goats" and "he does not own three goats." By contrast, false testimony either confirms what is not actually his or denies what truly is—claiming he owns two cows when he owns one, or asserting he owns no goats when he possesses two.

Witnesses themselves fall into two categories: the eyewitness and the auditory witness. An eyewitness testifies to what he has directly observed, whether the event occurs before him or has already transpired. The auditory witness, by contrast, reports what he has learned from another credible and just person; having not himself witnessed the event, he bears testimony founded upon trustworthy transmission.

### Testimony to the Oneness of Allah

All things in creation stand as proof of Allah's creative act, divine unity (*tawḥīd*), sovereignty, and dominion. Yet the Almighty appointed Muḥammad Muṣṭafā (peace be upon him) as His particular witness. The Qur'ān declares: "O Messenger, We have sent you as a witness upon Us."[^cite-67] You are an eyewitness and a speaking witness on Our behalf to all creation. The first ray of existence emanates from your light; by that light We created the entire universe. You are Our first creation and Our final proof. The Prophet (peace and blessings be upon him) proclaimed:

> Allah created my light before all things, and created the entire universe from my light.

Thus, although no attribute of Allah may be perceived or comprehended by mortal faculty—for the Divine transcends sensory knowledge—the Messenger of Allah beheld the Almighty with the eye of intellect and witnessed His unity. The Prophet (peace and blessings be upon him) declared: "I did not know the truth save through that which Allah taught me, and whatever I accomplished, Allah and ʿAlī accomplished it with me."[^cite-68] None has known me save Allah and ʿAlī, and none has known ʿAlī save Allah and me. This noble *ḥadīth* teaches that whoever possesses true knowledge and monotheistic conviction of Allah is indeed following the Messenger of Allah. Since the Messenger (peace and blessings be upon him) was the one who bestowed complete instruction upon ʿAlī, all primary knowledge must have flowed from the Messenger himself.

### Two Witnesses to Prophethood

Allah says: "O Beloved, as you have testified to Our creation, unity, and lordship, so too have We sent two witnesses to your prophethood."[^cite-69] One is Our Qur'ān, and the other is Our *Walī* (Guardian), ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib.

The witness who appears after the Messenger in this verse is ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib, and the witness who appears before him is the Qur'ān itself. If one were to consider that any who merely recites the Messenger's words might testify to his prophethood, such testimony would prove unreliable in Allah's sight. Four categories of people bear witness to the Messenger's prophethood: two according to human opinion and discourse, and two according to Allah's determination. Among those who testify according to our reckoning are believers and hypocrites; according to Allah's knowledge, the first witness is the Qur'ān, and the second is ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib.

### Witnesses According to Human Opinion

The testimony of hypocrites carries no weight, for Allah Himself declares: "When the hypocrites come to you, they say: 'We bear witness that you are the Messenger of Allah,' and Allah knows you are His Messenger—yet Allah bears witness that the hypocrites are liars."[^cite-70] The testimony of believers, though reliable, remains incomplete; they lack the true knowledge of the Messenger, for none knew him save Allah and ʿAlī.

### Witnesses According to Allah's Knowledge

According to Allah's determination, two witnesses attest to the Messenger of Allah: one proceeds from Allah—that is, the Qur'ān—and the other proceeds from the Messenger himself—ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib. The witness from Allah appeared in this world from the household of the Messenger; the witness from the Messenger was born in the house of Allah. This is the argument of love and the proof that "My affair is your affair, and your affair is My affair." The Qur'ān is sound and unchanging; ʿAlī al-Nāṭiq (the Speaking One) is alive and articulate. Therefore, Allah did not send one of low station alongside the Qur'ān—for that would be an affront to the Book. Rather, He sent one whose excellence surpasses the Qur'ān itself: the Qur'ān is mute and silent; ʿAlī is conscious and speaking. The Messenger of Allah (peace and blessings be upon him) affirmed: "ʿAlī is with the Qur'ān, and the Qur'ān is with ʿAlī."[^cite-71]

Because of this witness, the Messenger of Allah said: "O ʿAlī, your relation to me is as the relation of Hārūn to Mūsā!" Upon hearing these words, ʿAlī replied with profound eloquence:

> O Messenger of Allah! Sometimes you preach upon your shoulders, sometimes upon mine! You claim messengership—let me affirm it! You summon people to the right—I shall guard the gate! You entered the cave—I took your place upon your bed with a green sheet! You recite the Qur'ān—I shall expound its meaning! You command Islam—I stand as its doorkeeper of knowledge! A mercy to you amid wrath! I wage jihād with the sword while you wage jihād with the tongue! You order the breaking of idols—I become the idol-breaker! You are master of Kawthar and Paradise, the cupbearer and sovereign of heaven and hell! In the city of knowledge, in the chamber of wisdom! O Prophet, I am the interpreter! Your prophethood and mission, my Imāmate and Will!

### The Relationship between ʿAlī and the Qur'ān

What, then, is the greatness of the Messenger of Allah (peace and blessings be upon him) when one witness is the Qur'ān, the Book of Allah, and the other is the soul of Allah (*nafs Allāh*), the cause of Allah (*sabab Allāh*), the tongue of Allah (*lisān Allāh*), the rope of Allah (*ḥabl Allāh*)? People are astounded, imagining him to be divine. Yet Allah appointed one to bear witness to His Messenger's prophethood such that in the assembly of the Holy (*Bazm-i Quds*), he is called the cause of Allah; in the gathering of sovereignty, he becomes the teacher of Gabriel; in the Prophet's assembly, he is named the soul of the Messenger; among believers, he is called the perfect in faith (*kāmil al-īmān*); and when he aids the Prophet, Allah grants him the title "Helper of the Believers" (*Nāṣir al-Muʾminīn*). Thus Allah appointed two witnesses for His Beloved: the Qur'ān and ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib.

Two things are united only when similarity, affinity, and congruence exist between them. How foolish would be one who confined a nightingale and a crow in a single cage! The proof lies in this: just as the Qur'ān cannot be matched in eloquence and rhetorical beauty, so too is ʿAlī unparalleled in knowledge and deed, in perfections of soul and intellect. On the one hand, Allah says of His Book: "There is no dry or moist thing but is recorded in the Clear Record (*al-Kitāb al-Mubīn*)."[^cite-72] On the other hand, Allah says of ʿAlī: "We have encompassed all things within the Manifest Imām (*al-Imām al-Mubīn*)."[^cite-73]

The true witness to the Messenger's prophethood can only be one who possesses the knowledge of the Qur'ān, for prophethood is the Qur'ān, and the Qur'ān is the Messenger of Allah. After the Hijra, when the Messenger of Allah (peace and blessings be upon him) made brothers of the *Muhājirūn* (emigrants) and the *Anṣār* (helpers) with one another, he made ʿAlī his brother, saying: "O ʿAlī, you are my brother in this world and in the Hereafter."[^cite-74]

Allah did not only make His Messenger witness to ʿAlī; He also made His own testimony derive from ʿAlī. The Qur'ān declares: "Allah bears witness that there is no god but He, and so do the angels and the possessors of knowledge. Maintaining justice, there is no god but He, the Mighty, the Wise."[^cite-75] Allah has thus appointed the possessors of knowledge (*ahl al-ʿilm*) as witnesses. This witness possesses extraordinary qualities: sometimes he comes forth with Allah in witnessing to the Messenger; sometimes he comes forth with the angels to testify to Allah's judgment; sometimes he witnesses to the Messenger alongside the Qur'ān. In the assembly of the Prophet, he is the soul of the Prophet; in the pulpit of preaching, the eloquent speaker; in the gathering of believers, perfect faith; among polytheists, a judge against them; among hypocrites, a constant warner; in the assembly of the learned, a scholar; in the Prophet's household, one who purifies; in the court of governance, a leader; before the disbelievers, a mighty warrior. In one place, a mirror of the prophets' attributes; in another, a judge of the lawful and unlawful; in yet another, an idol-breaker; somewhere the hand of Allah (*yad Allāh*), somewhere the soul of the Prophet (*nafs al-Rasūl*), somewhere the supporter of religion, somewhere the possessor of Hārūnian station (*maqām Hārūnī*), somewhere the example of Noah's Ark (*Safīnat Nūḥ*), somewhere the first soul, somewhere the master of believers (*amīr al-muʾminīn*). Sometimes he is called "Prince of the Believers," sometimes "Helper of Religion," sometimes "Guardian of the Hidden," and again "Seal of Truthfulness." There is no domain of spiritual perfection, no stage of spiritual power, where this witness is not manifest. Verily, ʿAlī alone is ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib!

Among the kinds of witnesses, ʿAlī's testimony is that of an eyewitness, while the Messenger's testimony to Allah's unity is the testimony of one who has directly beheld. The Messenger of Allah (peace and blessings be upon him) said: "I and ʿAlī are from one light, and you, O ʿAlī, are from one light."[^cite-76] This *ḥadīth* bears witness to the fact that since ʿAlī partook of the light of prophethood, when the crown of prophethood was placed upon his head, it was ʿAlī who witnessed the scene. This divine wisdom in dividing the light of prophethood into two manifestations points to His unity (*tawḥīd*)—that Allah is One and all else is multiplicity. Thus it is proven that Allah sent ʿAlī and the Qur'ān as witnesses to the Messenger of Allah.

If the stations of these two witnesses were not equal—if one were exalted and the other diminished—then their testimony would carry different weight. Had an ignorant person been presented as a witness alongside the Qur'ān, it would have been a dishonor to the Book. Therefore, He sent one whose excellence transcends the Qur'ān itself: the Qur'ān is mute and voiceless; ʿAlī is possessed of intellect and speech.

Moreover, much of the Qur'ān is constituted by the deeds of ʿAlī. Thus was ʿAlī the witness who testified to the prophethood of the Prophet so that all humanity would recognize him as a spiritual scholar. The world attests that worldly scholars complete the Qur'ān in two or four days at most; but those who have learned in the house of Allah (*bayt Allāh*)—when they place one foot in the stirrup and the other is raised, they have begun and completed the Qur'ān between the mounting of the horse!

The Prophet's life comprises two aspects: the knowledge of the Messenger and the deeds of the Messenger. For these two aspects, Allah appointed two witnesses: the Qur'ān witnesses to the deeds of the Messenger, and ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib witnesses to the Messenger's knowledge. It is not fitting to omit either. The Prophet (peace and blessings be upon him) declared: "Knowledge without deed and deed without knowledge is misguidance."[^cite-77]

## Dhū al-Qarnayn

The meaning of Dhū al-Qarnayn (he of the two horns) is one who ruled the entire world—one authority in the East and another in the West. The Prophet (peace be upon him) said to Imam ʿAlī: "O ʿAlī, you are the leader of this *Umma* and its Dhū al-Qarnayn." Though you did not rule the world outwardly, you are the master of both ages in the domain of knowledge. The first age is that of the Prophet (peace be upon him), in which your station is the Gate of Knowledge, and it is the Qur'ān. The second is that of the *Qāʾim* (the Upholder, the Mahdī), who is the Lord of the Resurrection and master of the Day of Judgment; to his descendants belong the end of prophethood, of prophethood's seal, of Imāmate, and of the Will—all connected to him through the mighty rope of ʿAlī (*ḥabl Allāh al-Mateen*). None other shall find shelter in his sanctuary. In this manner, he became master of two centuries.

### Statement

Maulana ʿAlī received blows to his face only twice. Once in the Battle of the Trench, when the accursed ʿAmr ibn ʿAbd Wudd struck him upon the head; and again when Ibn Muljam al-Laʿīn (the accursed) martyred him. From these two blows, two horn-like marks appeared upon his head. For this reason, he is also called Dhū al-Qarnayn.

### The Story of Dhū al-Qarnayn and the Jewels

It is narrated that when Dhū al-Qarnayn entered a dark cave with his companions in search of the water of life, they perceived shining pebbles beneath their feet. His companions asked, "Are we walking upon pebbles?" Dhū al-Qarnayn replied, "This is something such that whoever takes it will regret it, and whoever leaves it will also regret it." His companions marveled at this answer and divided into two groups: one gathered some stones, the other left them. When they emerged from darkness into light, they discovered the stones were precious gems. Those who took them regretted having taken so little—why had they not gathered more? Those who took nothing regretted their deprivation of such treasure.

The parallel to the Master of the Universe is this: Amīr al-Muʾminīn (the Commander of the Faithful), who is the Dhū al-Qarnayn of this Muslim *Umma*, leads his followers and devotees from darkness into the world of purity and light. The gleaming stones that people feel beneath their feet are the jewels of your wise utterances—words they hear yet do not understand, hidden as they are in the darkness of language, words that elevate the human soul to pricelessness. Yet your companions do not prize these precious words; they listen carelessly and with inattention.

Your saying—that one who takes these stones will regret it and one who takes nothing will also regret it—rings absolutely true, for you knew the measure of regret for each. The one who takes them can gather only what his arms can embrace and what his strength can lift. When he reaches the light, he shall regret having contented himself with so little—why did he not gather more rare and precious gems? And he who bears nothing shall mourn his misfortune and eternal deprivation. Similarly, those who benefit from the words of Amīr al-Muʾminīn are those inclined more toward the worldly life and less toward religion. They listen to his utterances in their leisure, giving them scant thought. When these people depart from the world of affliction and perceive the fruits of their meager, halfhearted deeds, they shall regret, saying: "Had we devoted to this what we spent earning the world, how much greater would our fortune be today!" The regret of those who pay no heed—their poverty, wretchedness, and eternal misery—is beyond description.

### The Story of Gog and Magog

In his time, Dhū al-Qarnayn erected a mighty wall, behind which dwelt two nations called Gog and Magog, whose mischief troubled humanity. Gog and Magog are said to have been creatures of low stature with rough and piercing tongues, able to penetrate hard things and render them thin as a wafer, causing harm to humans when they approached human settlements. To protect his people, Dhū al-Qarnayn built a wall between himself and them. From the time of its construction, Gog and Magog have licked and scratched it throughout the night, striving to breach it, until the wall nears collapse. Then, as dawn breaks and the muezzin calls the *Fajr* prayer, the wall returns to its original state, and the people are relieved.

The inner meaning of this apparent narrative is that Gog and Magog represent two groups of exaggerators and diminishers (*ghāʾīs wa-nāqisīs*)—those who are only outward worshippers, devoid of inner gnosis. They are those who know only the sciences of the physical world and worldly knowledge, having received no light of true sciences or illumination of the divine. A wall stands before these materialists, through which they cannot reach true knowledge: this is the wall of covenants and bonds.

This state shall endure until the Day of Judgment.

This narrative also proves that Amīr al-Muʾminīn is the Dhū al-Qarnayn of the *Umma*, for the wall of covenant that protects people from Gog and Magog is the covenant of Maulana ʿAlī's *wilāya* (guardianship).

## Al-Anẓaʿ al-Baṭīn

"Al-Adhhā" (the bald one) refers to one without hair upon the head; "al-Baṭīn" (the one of large belly) signifies one who contains much knowledge within. The Master of the Universe is called Bald because there is no one of higher station than you in all the seventy ages! And you are called One-of-the-Large-Belly because you possess the greatest knowledge of the inner (*bāṭin*) dimensions—the larger the belly, the more it contains. The entire Qur'ān was revealed in honor of the Creator of the Universe; though there are those of higher rank than you, this seventy-year cycle has occurred with the Qur'ān's revelation, as each era unfolds in seventy and shall continue thus in every seventy. Even higher mysteries may appear in the era of unveiling (*Kashf*). But within this seventy-year cycle, this Book was revealed in honor of the highest personality of that time—and that is the station of Amīr al-Muʾminīn Ḥazrat ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib.

I am a devotee of the one on whom the light of two eyes shone,
And the challenge of the prophets, the two qiblas,
And the possessor of two glorious allegiances,

## The Generosity of ʿAlī

In narration of the Master of the Universe's generosity, it is related that once Amīr al-Muʾminīn ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib was traveling for trade, accompanied by goods upon several camels in caravan formation, with his loyal servant Mr. Qambar at his side. All the camels proceeded in a line.

A beggar approached Ḥazrat ʿAlī and asked for a loaf of bread. Ḥazrat ʿAlī (peace be upon him) said to Qambar: "O Qambar, give bread to this beggar." Mr. Qambar replied, "The bread is in the bread-bag (*toshdān*), Master." He said, "Then give him a gift." Qambar said, "But Master, the bread-bag is in the saddle." He said, "Then give it to him." Qambar said, "The bundle is upon a camel," and said: "Then give him a camel." Qambar said, "But Master, the camel is in the caravan line—give him the entire caravan!"

Hearing this, Ḥazrat Qambar fled from that place. Later, when Ḥazrat ʿAlī found him, he asked: "O Qambar, why did you run away?" Mr. Qambar replied: "O Master! Your river of generosity was flowing in full torrent—I feared you might give me away in charity as well!"

## I Do Not Know This Person

Once Ḥazrat Salmān al-Fārsī was leaving the Prophet's mosque when he encountered Ḥazrat Abū Bakr and Ḥazrat ʿUmar. They asked him: "Where are you coming from?" He replied: "I am coming from the mosque of the Prophet." They asked who was present in the mosque. He answered: "There is the Messenger of Allah in the mosque, and the other is a person worshipping whom I do not know."

The two companions thought that there must be an Arab unknown to Salmān the Persian who was praying with the Messenger of Allah. When the two reached the mosque, they were astonished to find that one in the mosque was the Messenger and the other was Amīr al-Muʾminīn ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib. They came to the Messenger of Allah (peace and blessings be upon him) and said: "O Messenger of Allah! Salmān says he does not recognize the one worshipping here. Let us send our peace upon him." But Salmān has deceived us today! The Prophet replied: "Salmān's heart is pure and immaculate, and only beautiful things dwell in his heart. A lie cannot emerge from his tongue! Yes, within every word of his lie a hundred secrets!"

They said: "O Messenger of Allah, we asked him who were in the mosque." He answered: "There is the Messenger of Allah in the mosque and the other is one whom I do not recognize. How can Salmān al-Fārsī not recognize ʿAlī? He washes his house daily with his robe and walks through the market with his two sons upon his shoulders. When people recount the virtues of ʿAlī, they mention no fewer than a thousand virtues, and then they say: 'I do not recognize him.' Is this not a lie?"

The Prophet said: "Go, call Salmān!" When Salmān al-Fārsī came into the Messenger's presence, the Messenger of Allah (peace and blessings be upon him) said: "O Salmān! Both of them are very displeased with you." Salmān replied: "My Lord, they are not displeased with me, but rather they are displeased with you." The Prophet asked: "They claim you have concealed the truth from them." Salmān said: "O Messenger of Allah, I have revealed the truth! The Prophet asked: "How?"

Salmān said: "O Messenger of Allah, no one knows Allah except you and ʿAlī, and no one knows you except Allah and ʿAlī, and no one knows ʿAlī except Allah and you. Therefore, O Messenger of Allah, I am neither the Messenger nor Allah, so I said I do not know him, for it is impossible for me to know the virtues of ʿAlī."

## The Accusation of Camel Theft

It is mentioned that once a devotee of ʿAlī passed from Kufa to Syria, where Muʿāwiya held sway. In the marketplace through which he was passing in Kufa, there gathered a crowd of Syrians whose hearts harbored hatred against ʿAlī (peace be upon him). They recognized this devotee of ʿAlī and contrived a plot to persecute him. Seeing that the man possessed a camel, they resolved among themselves that one would claim to be its owner and the rest would testify in his favor, thus wresting the camel from the man and also securing his punishment for theft.

According to plan, one of them came forward and went to the Kufan and said: "This is my camel, which you have stolen." The victim endeavored to explain, but the deceitful man raised a clamor, and within a short time a great crowd assembled. Meanwhile, the deceiver's associates also arrived and testified that the camel indeed belonged to their friend and that the Kufan had truly stolen it. The people seized this poor stranger, and the whole matter was brought before Muʿāwiya's court.

They repeated their claim to Muʿāwiya: that the camel belonged to the Syrian and that the Kufan had stolen it. They pleaded with Muʿāwiya for justice and demanded severe punishment for the thief. When Muʿāwiya asked the Kufan for clarification, the poor man replied: "O Muʿāwiya! Consider—how can this camel be this man's? The creature they have been claiming to own for so long is not a camel (*jamal*) at all, but a she-camel (*nāqa*)!"

Muʿāwiya heard the Kufan's reply and at once grasped that it was a Syrian stratagem. He handed the camel over to the Kufan and said to him: "O man, return to Kufa and tell your master, ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib, that these ignorant Syrians are in such a state that they cannot distinguish between a camel and a she-camel. How, then, could they distinguish between Muʿāwiya and ʿAlī?"

## Sweetness or Bitterness

One day, Amīr al-Muʾminīn ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib (peace be upon him) asked Ḥazrat Bilāl to bring watermelon from the market. When Ḥazrat Bilāl brought the watermelon, the Master of the Universe cut it and tasted it and said: "It is bitter! Go and return it and bring another watermelon in its place!"

When Ḥazrat Bilāl brought another watermelon in exchange, Amīr al-Muʾminīn cut it and tasted it and said: "This watermelon is sweet!" Then he asked Ḥazrat Bilāl: "Bilāl! Tell me—why was that watermelon bitter and this one sweet?"

Ḥazrat Bilāl said: "O Master, you are the Master of Knowers of Reality (*Sayyid al-ʿĀrifīn*)!" Maulana ʿAlī replied: "This watermelon harbored hatred for me in the realm of intention (*ʿālam al-niyyah*), and so it became bitter; this watermelon loved me in the realm of intention, and so it became sweet."
