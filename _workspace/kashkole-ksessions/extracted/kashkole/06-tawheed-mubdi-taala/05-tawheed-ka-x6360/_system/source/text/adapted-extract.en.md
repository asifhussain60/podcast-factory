## Conditions Before Islam

When the seventh century began, merciful concepts gradually yielded to satanic ones, and the understanding of divine unity deteriorated into polytheism. Since pre-Islamic Arabs believed that God was the source of all profit and loss, they began to worship whatever benefited them or whatever they feared might harm them. In this manner, what they called the 'Hindu' religion took root among the Arabs of Mecca, and they fashioned countless idols—some traditions record 360,000. As time passed, they recognized the impracticality of worshipping such multitudes, and so they reduced their idols to 365, one for each day of the year, installing them in the Kaaba and venerating one daily.[^cite-1]

Of these idols, three held special veneration: ⟪ar:لات⟫, ⟪ar:منات⟫, and ⟪ar:عزۃ⟫ (the goddesses). Other accounts report that a temple was constructed in Taif where ⟪ar:عزۃ⟫ was worshipped, while idols of ⟪ar:ھُبُل⟫ were positioned upon the roof of the Kaaba itself. These three deities corresponded, in the understanding of later Hindu tradition, to ⟪ar:برھما⟫ (the Creator), ⟪ar:وشنو⟫ (the Sustainer), and ⟪ar:شوا⟫ (the Destroyer).

To remedy this spiritual disease, the Almighty dispatched messengers in every age. Adam, Noah, Abraham, Moses, and Jesus were all sent to establish the concept of divine unity (tawhīd). When Jesus presented the doctrine of monotheism to his community, he expounded it through three modes of understanding. Yet his disciples went astray, reverting to their former patterns and dividing the Godhead into three persons—the Father, the Son, and ⟪ar:روح القدس⟫ (the Holy Spirit). In truth, Jesus addressed his Imam as 'Father'—for the teacher dwells in the father's station and the message-bearer in that of the son. His disciples, however, misinterpreted this metaphor: reasoning that if they called him 'father,' they themselves must be 'sons.' And when he declared, "A revelation comes to me," they mistook the angel of revelation for divinity itself. Thus was ⟪ar:تثلیث⟫ (the doctrine of three gods) established among them.[^cite-2]

After Jesus, a subsequent age reasoned differently. Observing that all things in creation possessed either a good or evil nature, some concluded that two divine principles must exist: ⟪ar:اھرمن⟫ (the god of evil) and ⟪ar:یزداں⟫ (the god of all good).[^cite-3] This dualism became entrenched in their theology.

⟪ar:پیغمبر علیہ السلام⟫ Before the advent of the Arab Prophet, the age had descended into extreme depravity. Every conceivable transgression flourished among the Arabian peoples. Their ignorance was so profound that in years when the date palm yielded abundantly, they would fashion an idol from the surplus dates and worship it, prostrating before it throughout the year. When the harvest failed, they would demolish the same idol and distribute it among themselves for consumption. The Arab nation had sunk into barbarism of the deepest kind. They consumed the flesh of crocodiles and elephants, roasted goat-skin together with goat meat, burned their enemies alive, flayed the corpses of their foes, shattered their skulls, and drank wine from the broken bone vessels. Women stained their garments with the blood of the slain. An enemy's camel would be tethered at his grave and left to perish from starvation. They murdered pregnant women and buried newborn girls alive, and seized the children of their enemies for use as living targets. The Arabs had become so hardened that their own prayers reflected their brutality:

⟪ar-quote:یا ربِّ فلا  تجعل حیاتی مَذِلَّۃ ولا موتَتِی بَینَ النِّسآء نوائِحِ⟫

"O Lord, do not let my life be one of shame, and do not allow my death to come while women lament around me."

⟪ar-quote:ولاکن قَتیلاً  تَمزقُ الطیر حولَہُ و تَشرُبُ غِربانُ الدَّم من جوارح⟫

"Rather, grant me a death in battle, where birds tear at my corpse and ravens drink blood from my limbs."

Women occupied no place of honor in society, treated as chattels no better than livestock. Slavery flourished, with masters possessing absolute dominion over the bodies, lives, and deaths of their enslaved. Adultery, gambling, and debauchery reached their zenith. Brother married sister; son took mother as wife. Tales of obscenity and moral depravity were recited openly in verse.

In matters of religion, the Arabs were given wholly to idolatry. Every city, village, and tribe worshipped goddesses and deities specific to their locality. The Kaaba itself was adorned with 360 idols, each representing the god of a particular tribe. ⟪ar:لات⟫ was venerated by the Thaqif tribes of Taif; the goddess ⟪ar:عزۃ⟫ was worshipped by Quraysh and the tribes of Canaan; and ⟪ar:منات⟫ was the deity of the Uth and Khazraj peoples. All these idols possessed a cold stone hub (al-hub) that was placed atop the Kaaba's roof. Within the Kaaba's walls hung images of Abraham and Ishmael, Mary and Jesus. The people of Himyar (Yemen) venerated the sun; the peoples of Canaan worshipped the moon. The fate of humankind was believed to be determined by the movements and influences of the stars.

## The Three Ways of Knowledge

The wise have established that the intellect possesses only three methods by which to apprehend any object:

**The Five Senses**

The first way ⟪ar:حواس خمسۃ⟫ is through the sensory faculties—sight, hearing, smell, taste, and touch. All phenomena possessing material form are apprehended ⟪ar:حواس خمسۃ⟫ through these five senses.

**Intellection**

The second is the method ⟪ar:عقل⟫ that operates through and beyond the sensory faculties ⟪ar:حواس خمسۃ⟫. By means of the intellect, we proceed from ⟪ar:محسوسات⟫ (sensible things) to ⟪ar:معقولات⟫ (intelligible things), or we infer the absence of a phenomenon through the attributes of what is present. For example, upon observing smoke, the intellect concludes that fire must exist somewhere.[^cite-4]

**Logical Demonstration and Dialectical Argument**

The third way is the apprehension of an object through ⟪ar:عقل⟫ rational demonstration and dialectical argument, in which both the five senses and intellection are employed. Through this method, we seek to comprehend things that transcend the realm of concrete phenomena and their sensible attributes by employing reason and logical philosophy—the method of the wise.

**The Proof of an Axiomatic Truth Through the Five Senses and Inference**

⟪ar:مبدع تعالی⟫ The Originator, Most High, belongs to a category that cannot be apprehended by ⟪ar:حواس خمسۃ⟫ the five senses. Since no attribute inheres in His essence, it is impossible to say that He resembles anything, and therefore ⟪ar:تشبیح⟫ anthropomorphization is forbidden. For this reason, comprehension of Him through the intellect alone is equally impossible. His existence can be proved only through logical demonstration and rational argument, in which both ⟪ar:عقل⟫ intellection and ⟪ar:حواس خمسۃ⟫ the five senses are engaged. In this manner, we may acknowledge the existence of the Originator through philosophical discourse and rational proof; yet we cannot know Him.

## What Is Knowledge of the Originator, Most High?

Knowledge of ⟪ar:مبدع تعالی⟫ the Originator is His oneness (tawhīd), and His oneness is the name given to the act of distinguishing Him from His creation. To know truly is to know that comprehension of Him is impossible. Whatever enters imagination contradicts this truth; there is no equivalent to His essence. Tongues fail in articulating His attributes, and intellects cannot grasp Him. He who speaks of Him commits ignorance; he who remains silent commits folly. Only the Almighty exists without denial, and knowledge of Him remains impossible. Whoever claims to have grasped Him speaks falsely. Whatever we conceive and whatever image we form, no matter how compelling it seems to our minds, will be reversed by the reality of His transcendence; in our imagination there will appear a creature fashioned in our own likeness.[^cite-5]

The correct principle of knowing and grasping any object is that there must exist some correlation between that object and the one who perceives it. Perception cannot occur without relationship. For instance, there exists a correlation between the physical body and the visible object; between sound and the ear ⟪ar:قوت ریحیہ⟫; between odor and the sense of smell ⟪ar:قوت بخاریۃ⟫; between flavor and the faculty of taste ⟪ar:قوت ذائقہ⟫; and between tangible things and the sense of touch ⟪ar:قوت حسیہ⟫.

If we were to assume that the Almighty could be sensed and imagined by us, and that we could thereby know Him, we would thereby establish a relationship between the Divine and the human intellect through which knowledge might be obtained. To accept this belief would compel us to affirm that there flows a divine influence from the Almighty into every human being—and thus we would fall into grave error (nāʿūdhu bi-llāh), affirming impermissible partnership with the Divine.

⟪ar:غیب الغیوب⟫ He is hidden beyond all hiddenness. Our hearts dare not venture to comprehend Him, and our rational faculty ⟪ar:ناطقۃ⟫ is bereft of understanding Him. Therefore ⟪ar:مولانا علی⟫ Imam Ali declares:

| ⟪ar-quote:اَلعِجزُ عَن دَرَکِ اِلأدرَاکِہِ اِدراکٌ⟫ | ⟪ar-quote:وَالبَحثُ عَن سِرِّ ذاَتِ السِّرِّ اِشرَاکٌ⟫ |
| --- | --- |
| To fall short in grasping Him is itself to grasp Him truly. | And to inquire into the secret of His very essence is to commit association with Him. |

All the jinn and men, having humbled themselves in their knowledge of Him, occupy themselves with knowledge of His creation. The guardian of the Almighty is he who possesses the courage to understand the creation of the heavens and earth, and whom the Almighty has guided to this attainment. Neither our thoughts can encompass Him, nor can any name or attribute be predicated of Him. We have no recourse except to acknowledge the existence of the Originator of all these worlds and to confess that all intellects are insufficient to comprehend His creative power. That is, we can neither comprehend ⟪ar:مبدع تعالی⟫ the Originator, nor can we comprehend how He exists above and beyond us.

The Messenger of the Almighty (peace and blessings be upon him) declared: ⟪ar-quote:وَاِذاَ بَلَّغتَ اِلیَ اللّٰہِ تَعاَلیٰ فَسکُتُو⟫ "When you attain understanding of the Almighty, be silent"; for to the degree that you have approached comprehension of the Divine, your silence itself becomes your confession of His unity. ⟪ar:سیدنا مؤید الشیرازی⟫ Sayyidina al-Muʾayyad al-Shirazi has taught: "Monotheism is not directed toward Allah, but toward the recognition of His limits."[^cite-6] The Almighty says in the Holy Qur'an:

> ⟪quran 3:18⟫
> ⟪ar:‏شَهِدَ ٱللَّهُ أَنَّهُۥ لَآ إِلَهَ إِلَّا هُوَ وَٱلْمَلَٓئِكَةُ وَأُو۟لُوا۟ ٱلْعِلْمِ قَآئِمًۢا بِٱلْقِسْطِ ۚ لَآ إِلَهَ إِلَّا هُوَ ٱلْعَزِيزُ ٱلْحَكِيمُ ‎⟫
> *Allah bears witness that La ilaha illa Huwa (none has the right to be worshipped but He), and the angels, and those having knowledge (also give this witness); (He is always) maintaining His creation in Justice. La ilah illa Huwa (none has the right to be worshipped but He), the All-Mighty, the All-Wise.*

This verse demonstrates that ⟪ar:اللہ⟫ Allah and ⟪ar:ھو⟫ the pronoun "He" refer to two distinct categories. The word ⟪ar:ھو⟫ "He" is a physical designation and a creature of our speech; therefore, to apply it to ⟪ar:مبدع تعالی⟫ the Originator is sinful. Yet, constrained by our humility and lack of essential truth, we are compelled to use it ⟪ar:ھو⟫. ⟪ar:سیدنا مؤید (ھبۃ اللہ بن موسیٰ الشیرازی⟫ Sayyidina al-Muʾayyad (Hibat Allah ibn Musa al-Shirazi) and ⟪ar:سیدنا حمید الدین کرمانی⟫ Sayyidina Hamid al-Din al-Kirmani have said: "We are caught in the snare of ⟪ar:عبودیت⟫ servitude and confined within the straits of created nature. When we seek to praise You with words befitting Your glory, we are compelled by necessity to point toward You through speech, which constitutes a sin—yet this sin of ours is joined to forgiveness."[^cite-7] Hence ⟪ar:سیدنا مؤید⟫ Sayyidina al-Muʾayyad seeks pardon from the Almighty for this transgression, stating in what is recorded in ⟪ar:کتاب کنز الولد⟫ the *Kitab Kanz al-Walad* (The Book of the Treasure of the Child):

"O Allah, I seek Your forgiveness for that which is bound in the chains of servitude and confined to narrow limits. When I made mention of You with words unworthy of Your station, it was because I was compelled by necessity to name You—a sin that is indivisibly linked to Your forgiveness."

## Knowledge of the Originator Through Logical Demonstration

### The Verb Is Absolute

Everything in this universe is divided into the agent (fāʿil, the doer) and the patient (mafʿūl, that upon which action is performed). The world is governed throughout by the principle of cause and effect. Rain is the effect of cloud-thunder; clouds arise as the effect of vapor; vapor rises as the effect of rivers. In short, every object is subjected to action. And he who is the agent in one action becomes the patient in another. Proceeding backward through this chain of causation, we arrive at the first agent—that which has never been the object of anyone else's action, which is the ultimate cause, the Originator ⟪ar:مبدع تعالی⟫. The masses understand this first agent as ⟪ar:اللہ⟫ Allah.

For anything to possess or receive an effect, three conditions must obtain:

| **Agent** ⟪ar:فاعل⟫ | **Action** ⟪ar:فعل⟫ | **Effect** ⟪ar:مفعول⟫ |
| --- | --- | --- |
| That which causes an effect and becomes a cause | The action itself | That which receives an effect and becomes a cause |

Accordingly, if we regard ⟪ar:مبدع تعالی⟫ the Creator as merely the Knower, He will possess three states: the Knower, who possesses knowledge; the knowledge by which He knows; and the Known, which He comprehends. If Allah is the Knower of the universe, the universe must first exist, and He must possess the capacity to know it. This reasoning seems to make Him dependent upon the universe in order for Him to be its Knower—as if His becoming Knower of the universe is conditional upon the universe's pre-existence. These three states apply equally to all His other attributes:

| **Agent** | **Action** | **Effect** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| *ʿAlim* (Knower) | *ʿilm* (Knowledge) | *Maʿlūm* (Known) |
| *Qādir* (Powerful) | *Qudra* (Power) | *Maqdūr* (Empowered thing) |
| *Rahīm* (Merciful) | *Rahma* (Mercy) | *Marhūm* (One shown mercy) |

There is a critical distinction between this understanding and that of ⟪ar:عالم مطلق⟫ the absolutely unrestricted Knower. To understand ⟪ar:عالم⟫ the Knower in isolation is to commit shirk (association), for if He is a knower, then we too are knowers, though our knowledge is inferior to His. By contrast, ⟪ar:عالم مطلق⟫ the absolutely unrestricted Knower is one who is not dependent upon cause and effect for His knowledge.

### He Has No Partner

⟪ar:مبدع تعالی⟫ The Originator has no partner. If we hypothetically assume a partner exists alongside Allah, we may examine this through the method of *proof by contradiction*. Four conditions are logically possible regarding the power of such a putative partner:

**Both Are Defective in Essence**

If both beings possessed defective natures, there would be no grounds for either to exist at all. This condition is therefore impossible.

**Both Are Perfect in Essence**

If both were perfect and equal in their natures with no difference or contradiction between them, the existence of one would be superfluous and redundant. Since all power flows completely from a single God, such duplication is logically impossible.

**One Is Perfect, the Other Defective**

If one is defective and the other is fully perfect in power, then the defective being is unnecessary and redundant. This condition cannot obtain.

**Perfection Is Divided Between Them**

If either of the two is defective in its own essence, both must be proved defective. For if both attain perfection only through reliance upon each other, then individually each is impaired. It is impossible for the Divine to be imperfect.

All these demonstrations prove that ⟪ar:مبدع تعالی⟫ Allah is One, perfect in His power, and utterly without partner.

### He Is One, But Not in Number

⟪ar:مبدع تعالی⟫ The Originator is one, but not in terms of numerical count. That is, if we examine Him from the perspective of enumeration, then the counter and the counted (ʿād and maʿdūd) are two categories of created things. If we take ⟪ar:مبدع تعالی⟫ the Originator to be "one" in the numerical sense, He would assume the state of ⟪ar:مفعول⟫ the counted, and the Divine cannot be a counted thing. This is why the Qur'an, in ⟪ar:سورۃ اخلاص⟫ the Chapter of Sincerity, uses the word ⟪ar:أحد⟫ (unique) rather than ⟪ar:واحد⟫ (one):

> ⟪quran 112:1⟫
> ⟪ar:‏قُلْ هُوَ ٱللَّهُ أَحَدٌ ‎⟫
> *Say (O Muhammad): "He is Allah, (the) One (Unique).*

Furthermore, He cannot be counted, for counting requires confinement to a boundary. To call ⟪ar:مبدع تعالی⟫ the Originator "one" in the numerical sense would be to confine Him within a limit—yet He is the very Creator of limits themselves, and cannot be imprisoned by them. Moreover, the number "one" itself implies relation to a multitude; to say "a book is one" means it is one among many books. Similarly, to say a god is one would suggest he is one among many gods. Thus the Qur'an employs ⟪ar:أحد⟫ to convey utter uniqueness.

### He Is Incorporeal and Unbounded

Everything that possesses body is encompassed within six dimensions—upward, downward, forward, backward, right, and left—confined within what is called its "space." What exists in one place does not exist in another. Everything in creation, even the heavens themselves, is bound to its own space and cannot transgress it. Every body depends upon its Creator for sustenance, which itself testifies to ⟪ar:صمدیت⟫ self-sufficiency and the transcendence of ⟪ar:مبدع تعالی⟫ the Originator. The essence of Allah is unlimited; it cannot be said that He exists here and not there, in the east but not the west, in the north but not the south. All things in creation are encompassed within their spaces—a characteristic of the physical world. But Allah is the Creator of both time and space.[^cite-8]

Everything is governed by the boundaries of its space. Were ⟪ar:مبدع تعالی⟫ the Originator confined to a space, His dominion and power would likewise be limited, as though His kingdom extended no beyond a particular boundary.

### He Is Timeless

Time is the measure of celestial motion by which we discern whether an object came into existence first and then continued to exist. Scholars teach that time is the effect (maʿlūl) and the motion of the heavens is its cause (ʿilla). The motion of the heavens brought forth time. Similarly, every movement has its counterpart in stillness; the interval between movement and stillness is called time. Both motion and stillness apply exclusively to physical bodies. In ⟪ar:مبدع تعالی⟫ the Originator, there is no movement between existence and what precedes it, nor is there stillness—for these belong to time. Words are powerless to describe Him. One must merely acknowledge that He has always been.

⟪ar:امام جعفر الصادق⟫ Imam Jaʿfar al-Sadiq once declared in an assembly: "Allah is greater!" He then asked the listeners what this phrase meant. One replied: "I understand it to mean ⟪ar:اللہ اکبر مِن کُلِّ اَکبَر⟫ 'Allah is greater than all that is great.'" The Imam responded: ⟪ar:اِذاً حَدَّتَّہُ⟫ "Then you have restricted Him." For if we say the Small is the Almighty and the Almighty, then the Small Almighty still bears some relation to the Great Almighty—just as a drop relates to the ocean. He then explained: "The true meaning of ⟪ar:اللہ اکبر⟫ is ⟪ar-quote:اَللّٰہُ اَکبَر مِن اَن یُّحَدَّ وَ یُحسَر⟫ 'Allah is too great to be confined to any limit or measure.'"[^cite-9]

### He Is Eternal

Our created realm has been brought from non-existence (ʿadam) into existence (wujūd). It possesses many parts and components interdependent for their subsistence, all relying upon a Creator for their being. ⟪ar:مبدع تعالی⟫ Allah is ancient and eternal—not in the sense of temporal antiquity, but in the sense that the origin of His existence is beyond knowledge. There was no prior nothingness for Him. Time itself was created by Him, and therefore He cannot exist under the dominion of time.[^cite-10]

### He Created Without Matter or Deficiency

He created all beings from absolute nothingness, without any pre-existing matter. Had He created from something, His action would be flawed, implying He could not act without that substance. Had He created in a place, He would depend upon that place to sustain these beings, rendering Him defective. Yet the Divine cannot be defective; therefore it is proven that He created all beings from pure nothingness, without matter, without substance, and without deficiency.[^cite-11]

### He Is Neither Form Nor Substance

⟪ar:مبدع تعالی⟫ Allah is not a form, for every form depends upon its Creator, and all that depends upon another for existence is a creature, not a Creator. Every creature bears witness: "My Creator is not like me." Similarly, every substance depends upon its Creator and ⟪ar:مبدع تعالی⟫ cannot depend upon Him. In other words, Allah is neither form nor substance, but the Creator of both. To conceive otherwise would divide Him into two parts, each requiring a third party superior and stronger than both for its existence.[^cite-12]

### He Has No Name

A name is a symbol by which creatures of different forms are distinguished from one another, enabling recognition of each type and form without tedious elaboration. Since ⟪ar:مبدع تعالی⟫ Allah possesses no form—neither intellectual nor physical nor arbitrary—His essence transcends nomination. The names of creatures cannot be affixed to Him. Yet when He created the world from nothingness, He gave it names so that distinction might arise among different forms. This is why the Almighty says:

> ⟪quran 2:31⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَعَلَّمَ آدَمَ ٱلْأَسْمَآءَ كُلَّهَا ثُمَّ عَرَضَهُمْ عَلَى ٱلْمَلَٓئِكَةِ فَقَالَ أَنۢبِـُٔونِى بِأَسْمَآءِ هَٓؤُلَآءِ إِن كُنتُمْ صَدِقِينَ ‎⟫
> *And He taught Adam all the names (of everything), then He showed them to the angels and said, "Tell Me the names of these if you are truthful."*

Yet He Himself is exalted beyond all names and appellations:

> ⟪quran 37:159⟫
> ⟪ar:‏سُبْحَنَ ٱللَّهُ عَمَّا يَصِفُونَ ‎⟫
> *Glorified is Allah! (He is Free) from what they attribute unto Him!*

Compelled by necessity, prayer has beckoned to Him by the pronoun ⟪ar:ھو⟫ "He." To call Allah by the name "Almighty" or even to designate Him as ⟪ar:مُبدِع⟫ Creator or refer to Him as ⟪ar:ھو⟫ "He" is fundamentally erroneous. He possesses neither noun (ism) nor adjective (sifah). We dare not interpret the Divine in the words of our tongues. Letters are themselves mute ⟪ar:معجم⟫, and we are their speakers; letters cannot be uttered unless their speaker is present. Thus we attempt to interpret our Creator through our creation (letters)—an impossibility![^cite-13]

**Clarification**

⟪ar:اللہ⟫ Allah is not merely a name, but rather a designation. This designation refers not to the essence of ⟪ar:مبدع تعالی⟫ the Originator, but to the manifestation of ⟪ar:عقل اول⟫ the First Intellect (al-ʿAql al-Awwal). The First Intellect itself possesses ninety-nine attributes, each described by a divine name. These ninety-nine attributes together with the essence constitute one hundred.[^cite-14]

### He Has No Attributes

All attributes inhere in objects, whether physical or spiritual. Physical bodies are characterized by sensible properties—height, color, and the like. The soul is characterized by its properties—knowledge, ignorance, virtue, vice—by which it is defined. Nouns (asmaʾ) and adjectives (sifāt) are inseparable from each other; they occupy the same plane of reality. For example, the noun ⟪ar:اسم (رحیم)⟫ (the Merciful) corresponds to the adjective ⟪ar:صفت (رحم)⟫ (mercy). We have perceived the merciful and possess a concept, measure, and exemplar of mercy, so we may observe a merciful person and say he is ⟪ar:رحیم⟫ merciful.

⟪ar:مبدع تعالی⟫ The Originator is neither body nor soul. It is impossible for ⟪ar:اسم⟫ a noun to exist when ⟪ar:صفت⟫ an adjective does not. He is neither object nor creature, but the Creator of all; therefore, the adjectives that adhere to creation cannot adhere to the Creator. It is incumbent upon us to negate and deny every attribute of His.[^cite-15]

### He Has No Rival

There is no rival to Allah. Let us not imagine Satan standing in opposition to the Divine. No one can compete with God. If we examine the story of Adam, we find that Satan himself believed in Allah. He denied Adam and swore by Allah: "I will surely lead Your servants astray," and he requested respite from the Almighty until the Day of Resurrection. Satan is not ignorant; he knows all things, yet he refused obedience. ⟪ar:معلم الملکوت⟫ He was the teacher of the angels; he possessed experience. Yet his knowledge and service availed him nothing, for he disobeyed God; and therefore was he called ⟪ar:رجیم⟫ the Accursed, and Hell became his abode. Whoever follows him shall enter Hell. Yet Allah says in the Qur'an: ⟪ar-quote:لا اکراہ فی الدین⟫ "There is no compulsion in religion." Whether a person turns toward the Merciful or toward Satan, it is his choice. This is why we pray in every prayer: ⟪ar-quote:اھدنا الصراط المستقیم⟫ "Guide us to the right path"—because both paths remain open to us.[^cite-16]

### Can the Almighty Commit Injustice?

⟪ar:مبدع تعالی⟫ The Almighty cannot revoke existence once granted. A debate arose between the believers and certain heretical sects regarding the question: "Is Allah able to accomplish all things?" The believers responded: "Indeed, Allah can accomplish all things, yet He cannot commit injustice." The heretics objected: "Why do you limit the power of Allah by asserting that He cannot commit injustice?" The answer came by way of an analogy: "Can you eat all things?" "Certainly, I can eat all things!" "Then see this dirt before you—eat it." Thus the conclusion is clear: Allah possesses absolute power, but He does not commit injustice. He does not oppress despite possessing power, not because injustice is beyond His capability, but solely because it stands in contradiction to His majesty and glory.[^cite-17]

## Monotheism and Sincerity

Monotheism (tawhīd) is achieved only through sincerity (ikhlāṣ). Regarding sincerity, ⟪ar:مولانا علی⟫ Imam Ali teaches:

⟪ar-quote:اَلأِخْلاَصْ یَکُونُ بِہِ اَلْخَلاَصْ وَ اَلأِخْلاَصْ ثَمَرَۃُ الیَقِین وَاَلأِخْلاَصْ ثَمَرَۃُ العِباَدَۃْ واَلأِخْلاَصْ ھِیَ خَیرُ العَمَل وَلاَ یَکُونُ خَیرُ العَمَل اِلاَّ بِالعِلم وَاَجَلُّ اَلْعِلْم، اَلعِلْمُ التَّوحِید⟫

"Sincerity is that through which salvation is attained. Sincerity is the fruit of faith, and sincerity is the fruit of worship (meaning: as our faith and worship increase, so too does our sincerity increase). Sincerity is the best of all deeds, and no deed can be best without knowledge, and the highest knowledge is the knowledge of monotheism."[^cite-18]

The Originator is One (Aḥad), Self-Sufficient (Samad), Eternal and Everlasting. He is the First, before whom nothing existed, and the Last, whom all else shall leave behind, yet He shall remain. He is not confined to any place, nor are His days and years bound by time. He brought all things into existence from non-being, without exertion, pain, movement, or rest—not after the likeness of any other thing, not from any substance, out of no need, for no purpose, and not from fear of solitude.

He does not fear losing what is in His dominion and possession, and therefore He does not hasten. He does not act out of need for anything. He is the All-Knowing, one whose knowledge admits no doubt. He is the Ruler who commits no oppression. He is the Forbearing One who does not rush in His judgments. He neither dozes nor sleeps. He is free from all error, and He is one possessed of opinion that admits no blame. He is exalted above all things. He is the All-Powerful, the Protector, and there is nothing like unto Him. He hears and sees. He could perceive even when there was nothing to perceive.[^cite-19]

The foundation of religion is the knowledge of His existence (i.e., the acknowledgment that He is the Creator), and the reality of knowing Him is His oneness (tawhīd), and the structure of tawhīd rests upon sound intellect bearing witness that He is free from all attributes—for every attribute and the one described by it are created. Every created thing bears witness to its Creator. Attributes and the attributed are intertwined, and their connection testifies that they are created beings (brought into existence after non-existence). Every creature itself testifies: "I am not eternal; therefore I am not the Creator."

He initiated all creation, which proves He has no beginning—for the initiator of every beginning is someone else. He endowed mankind with limbs (eyes, ears, hands, feet, etc.), which bear witness that He is without limbs, for those possessing limbs are dependent upon the Creator of their limbs. His names are but expressions, not realities. Whoever describes His attributes is ignorant. Whoever named Him has sinned. Whoever described His essence has likened Him to something. Whoever mentioned places has confined Him, and whatever is confined is numbered. He is One (Aḥad), yet not counted. He is Self-Sufficient (Samad), yet not dependent on body. He is the Hidden, yet His existence is manifest. He is the Manifest, yet not before the eyes. He shines forth, yet cannot be seen by sight. He is near, yet not in proximity. He is far, yet not in distance. He is the All-Seeing (Baṣīr), yet without eyes. He is not bound by time, nor is He confined to place. He is the Agent, yet He does not act through compulsion. He is the Self-Sufficient, yet not through benefit from what is or what is not.

Our consciousness and senses have taught us that He has no boundary. The essence of essences revealed that He has no maker of His essence. The creation of creatures told us that He has no Creator. The opposition of things informed us that He has no opposite. The pairing of things revealed that He has no partner. Time and space, being limited, revealed that He is not subject to time and space. He was the Creator even before He created creation, and He was the Originator even before He brought forth originated beings.

He transformed darkness into light, gloom into radiance, softness into hardness, heat into cold, and created a connection between opposing things. Unity despite multiplicity, and multiplicity despite unity, indicate His Lordship (rubūbiyya). All these varying states and conditions bear witness to His power and are evidence of His hiddenness.

This can be confirmed only through intellect, and intellect affirms that there is no faith without piety, no piety without knowledge, no knowledge without affirmation, no affirmation without tawhīd, no tawhīd without sincerity, and no sincerity coupled with anthropomorphism (tashbīh)—for anthropomorphism implies attributing qualities to Him.[^cite-20]

Tawhīd cannot exist without the complete negation of attributes. When the essence is described through an attribute, limitation is evident. What is understood through examples is perceived by intellect. What enters imagination takes on form. What is surrounded by directions is encompassed by causes. What exists in one space is absent from another. What has a body has weight. What is stationary undergoes motion. What is attached to something is dependent on its connection. What the intellect remembers through recollection is also subject to forgetting. He who speaks with the aid of letters is constrained. He who deliberates through thought is occupied. He who creates through consultation is deficient in essence. What comes to imagination resembles something else, and what is encompassed by imagination is not transcendent. ⟪ar:مبدع تعالی⟫ The Originator is exalted above all these constraints and limitations!

His praise is the garment of others. That is, words of praise arising from creation belong fitly only to creation, for what is owned by someone revolves within his possession. The absence of attributes is His attribute. There is no limit to His glory. There is no reason for His actions. There is no duration to His existence. None of His creation can attain Him, and none can tear away the veils of His hiddenness.

Only Allah is exalted above all the attributes of His creation. Attributes reveal their own essence and describe something akin to themselves. No created being brought into being the essence of the Originator, and no kingdom made Him its master—rather, He was Master from the beginning. Attributes did not bring Him into existence; rather, He is the Originator of every existent being and the Creator of every attribute and the attributed, the Knower and the Known. He is the Agent of every action, and His action is without direct contact (mubāshara). His guidance is without indication. His speech is without utterance. His face is wherever you direct your gaze. His path is the uprightness of the faith (istiqāmat al-dīn). He explains us to ourselves and teaches us through ourselves.[^cite-21]

## He Who Knows His Own Soul Has Known His Lord

This hadith belongs to that category of prophetic utterances for which the Messenger declared: ⟪ar-quote:بَعَثتُ بِجَوَاِمعُ الکَلاَم⟫ "I have been sent with comprehensive words," distilling wisdom into lapidary speech.[^cite-22] When someone asked him to teach them the knowledge of Allah, ⟪ar:رسول اللہ صلی اللہ علیہ و آلہ و سلم⟫ the Prophet (upon him be peace and blessings) replied: ⟪ar-quote:مَن عَرَفَ نَفسُہُ فَقَد عَرَفَ رَبُّہُ⟫ "He who knows his own soul has done so through knowledge of his Lord." On another occasion, he elaborated: ⟪ar-quote:اَعرَفکُم بِنَفسِہِ اَعرَفکُم بِرَبِّہِ⟫ "The deeper your self-knowledge, the deeper your knowledge of your Lord."[^cite-23] In these formulations, ⟪ar:رسول اللہ صلی اللہ علیہ و آلہ و سلم⟫ the Prophet established a profound principle: knowledge of the Divine cannot proceed from a direct assault upon the Essence. Rather, the path to gnosis of the Lord (⟪ar:مبدع تعالی⟫, the Originator, Most High) runs through the careful investigation of one's own being. How can the limited intellect comprehend the Maker of that very intellect? If man lacks the capacity to fathom his own soul in its entirety, by what faculty could he possibly apprehend the Creator, who transcends all created attributes?[^cite-24] Hence it must be acknowledged: the Divine Essence cannot be known. One can only confess the absolute Reality of His Being.

Knowledge and recognition always belong to that which occupies the higher rank. The hadith itself presents three interrelated stations:

| Al-ʿAql (Intellect) | Al-Nafs (Soul) | Al-Rabb (The Lord) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| The Discerning Faculty | The Self | The Lord |

The particle ⟪ar:مَن⟫ in this hadith refers to the intellect—that faculty which endeavors to recognize the inner capacities of the soul and, through those capacities, to advance toward the knowledge and recognition of the Lord.

For the exotericists (ahl al-zahir), the meaning is straightforward: when a person contemplates his own genesis, observing how Allah fashioned him from ⟪ar:سلالۃ⟫ sperm-drop and ⟪ar:نطفۃ⟫ clay—gradually transforming it into ⟪ar:عَلَقَۃ⟫ clot, then ⟪ar:مُضغَۃ⟫ morsel, then ⟪ar:عظام⟫ bone, clothing it with ⟪ar:لحم⟫ flesh, and breathing into it the Spirit—he witnesses that he did not bring himself into being. This contemplation awakens the certainty that some other Agent drew him from nothingness into existence. Further, when he reflects upon his own progression—from natal weakness to youthful vigor to the decline of old age—he recognizes the pattern Allah describes:

> ⟪quran 30:54⟫
> ⟪ar:‏ ٱللَّهُ ٱلَّذِى خَلَقَكُم مِّن ضَعْفٍۢ ثُمَّ جَعَلَ مِنۢ بَعْدِ ضَعْفٍۢ قُوَّةًۭ ثُمَّ جَعَلَ مِنۢ بَعْدِ قُوَّةٍۢ ضَعْفًۭا وَشَيْبَةًۭ ۚ يَخْلُقُ مَا يَشَآءُ ۖ وَهُوَ ٱلْعَلِيمُ ٱلْقَدِيرُ ‎⟫
> *Allah is He Who created you in (a state of) weakness, then gave you strength after weakness, then after strength gave (you) weakness and grey hair. He creates what He wills. And it is He Who is the All-Knowing, the All-Powerful.*

And in another passage:

> ⟪quran 18:37⟫
> ⟪ar:‏قَالَ لَهُۥ صَاحِبُهُۥ وَهُوَ يُحَاوِرُهُۥٓ أَكَفَرْتَ بِٱلَّذِى خَلَقَكَ مِن تُرَابٍۢ ثُمَّ مِن نُّطْفَةٍۢ ثُمَّ سَوَّىٰكَ رَجُلًۭا ‎⟫
> *His companion said to him, during their discourse: "Do you disbelieve in Him Who created you out of dust, then out of a sperm-drop, then fashioned you into a man?"*

### Direct and Indirect Creation

The people of esoteric wisdom, however, perceive a subtler truth in this Prophetic utterance. Man is not the direct creation of Allah, for the All-Mighty is bound neither by time nor place in His acts. He requires neither the ⟪ar:عناصر اربع⟫ four elements (fire, air, water, earth) to accomplish His will. It is only man—limited, dependent, and bound by material necessity—who must marshal tools and means to fashion anything. The Creator alone is free from all such constraints, transcending utterly the conditions of contingent existence.

To attribute material instrumentality to Allah in the creation of man would be to ascribe to Him the weakness and limitation proper to creatures. Does the Divine Pen require paper, ink, and hand? Does the Divine Potter require clay, wheel, and time to fashion form? Such conceptions contradict the true meaning of monotheism (tawhīd). Rather, man emerges gradually from the elements of this world and is therefore an *indirect* creation—a *mubdaʿ* dependent upon medial causes. The physical world, by contrast, requires neither instrument nor duration for its origination; it is the *direct* creation of Allah. As He declares:

> ⟪quran 16:40⟫
> ⟪ar:‏إِنَّمَا قَوْلُنَا لِشَىْءٍ إِذَآ أَرَدْنَهُ أَن نَّقُولَ لَهُۥ كُن فَيَكُونُ ‎⟫
> *Verily! Our Word unto a thing when We intend it, is only that We say unto it: "Be!" and it is.*

And concerning the human birth and the cosmos:

> ⟪quran 10:3⟫
> ⟪ar:‏إِنَّ رَبَّكُمُ ٱللَّهُ ٱلَّذِى خَلَقَ ٱلسَّمَوَتِ وَٱلْأَرْضَ فِى سِتَّةِ أَيَّامٍۢ ثُمَّ ٱسْتَوىٰ عَلَى ٱلْعَرْشِ ۖ يُدَبِّرُ ٱلْأَمْرَ ۖ مَا مِن شَفِيعٍ إِلَّا مِنۢ بَعْدِ إِذْنِهِۦ ۚ ذَلِكُمُ ٱللَّهُ رَبُّكُمْ فَٱعْبُدُوهُ ۚ أَفَلَا تَذَكَّرُونَ ‎⟫
> *Surely, your Lord is Allah Who created the heavens and the earth in six Days and then Istawa (rose over) the Throne, disposing the affair of all things. No intercessor (can plead with Him) except after His Leave. That is Allah, your Lord; so worship Him (Alone). Then, will you not remember?*

### Component or Effect Versus Whole

⟪ar:سیدنا مؤید اعلی اﷲ قدسہ⟫ In his exegesis of the hadith "He who knows his own soul knows his Lord," Our Master al-Muʾayyad (may Allah sanctify his secret) teaches that man is compounded of two distinct principles. The first is the body, constituted from the ⟪ar:عناصر اربع⟫ four elements (fire, air, water, earth). From the celestial sphere (⟪ar:کرۃ الأرض⟫) man draws his earthy component; through the nostrils comes his aerial portion; from bodily heat, the fiery element; from water, the aqueous. Yet within this dense corporeal frame there dwells a subtle reality—a power by which man encompasses in his imaginative faculty the heavens and the earth without physical burden or movement. This force is called the ⟪ar:نفس ناطقۃ⟫ rational soul, and by virtue of it man is reckoned among the ⟪ar:اشرف المخلوقات⟫ most noble of created beings, distinguished from the ⟪ar:نفس حیوانیۃ⟫ animal soul.

Yet there is a faculty still more subtle than the soul itself: the intellect (ʿaql), by which man discerns the good from the evil. Justice demands that as the body possesses a governing center, so too must soul and intellect possess theirs. This universal center we call the ⟪ar:نفس کلیۃ⟫ Universal Soul and ⟪ar:عقل کلیۃ⟫ Universal Intellect, since all the particular souls and intellects of the physical cosmos derive their powers from this transcendent source.

By this analysis, man stands revealed as a compound being: in bodily constitution, he is a child of the natural world; in soul and intellect, a child of another realm. Thus man is the child of two worlds:

| Body | Soul | Intellect |
| --- | --- | --- |
| The Four Elements | Nafs Kuliyya (Universal Soul) | ʿAql Kuliyya (Universal Intellect) |

When we examine the corporeal component man receives from earth—such a negligible fraction that no true proportion can be established between it and the terrestrial sphere; when we consider the air man draws (⟪ar:کرۃ ہوائیۃ⟫) compared to the celestial sphere itself, or the fiery element (⟪ar:کرۃ النار⟫) in relation to the cosmic fire, or the water (⟪ar:کرۃ آبی⟫) within him weighed against the waters of creation—in every case the disproportion is absolute. **The four elements that compose the human body are not parts of those cosmic elements; they are *effects*, not *components*.** 

When we turn to the Universal Soul and Universal Intellect, we perceive that the human soul and intellect are themselves merely particular—effects alone, never parts. Hence we arrive at a profound conclusion: despite all our striving, we cannot establish any true relation of human soul and intellect to that Universal Soul and Intellect which is the ⟪ar:عالم کل⟫ totality of being.

If man, born from realms to which he is undeniably related, cannot attain knowledge of his own relation to them, how could he possibly grasp the nature of the ⟪ar:مبدِع تعالی⟫ Originator, Most High—with whom no relation whatsoever obtains? Man must confess that what he took for monotheism is but mirage. Then he understands the truth in the words of ⟪ar:مولانا علی⟫ our Master ʿAlī: ⟪ar-quote:اَلعِجزُ عَن دَرَکِ اِلأدرَاکِہِ اِدراک⟫ *"The inability to attain perception is itself a true perception."*[^cite-25] Then the Prophetic utterance becomes manifest: "If he cannot know his own soul, how shall he know his Lord?"

## The Journey from Knowledge of Self to Knowledge of the Lord

⟪ar:مولانا امیر المؤمنین سید العارفین، علی ابن ابی طالب علیہ وعلیٰ آلہٖ افضل السلام⟫ When asked about the saying of the Prophet, "He who knows his own soul knows his Lord," the Commander of the Faithful, the Master of the Knowers, ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib (upon him and his family be the most excellent peace) expounded the matter thus:

"It is essential for one to understand his self before attaining knowledge of the Divine. Without this foundation, authentic knowledge of God cannot be achieved. Before recognizing one's self, consider that it is composed of four contradictory ⟪ar:عناصر⟫ natures—fire, water, earth, and air—also called the ⟪ar:اُمہات⟫ mothers or roots of matter. These four opposing elements are unified by nature into a dense body, perceptible through the five senses and bounded by three ⟪ar:ثلاثہ ابعاد⟫ dimensions (length, width, depth). Yet this composite form tends toward dissolution, for it must inevitably return to its constituent elements, becoming dust and ash.

"Next, the seeker should recognize that this dense body, like all material forms, cannot move itself. There must exist something subtle, something immaterial, that animates the body—this is the soul. From the soul flows life; in its departure lies death and chaos. As one perceives the superiority of the subtle soul over the gross body, one realizes that the soul, being infinite in its nature, does not require the necessities, instruments, and senses proper to physicality. It transcends the three ⟪ar:ثلاثہ ابعاد⟫ dimensions. Thus one understands that the characteristics of soul are of an entirely different order—higher and superior to the body, and perceptible only to the inner eye, for they are spiritual in essence.

"In contemplating the soul, one must imagine also a spiritual world. Through continued reflection, one arrives at a certainty: that by walking the path of truth, by joining knowledge and practice in acts of worship, and by cultivating piety, the soul will at last depart the body and return to its origin. As it is written: ⟪ar-quote:کُلَّ شَئی یَرجِعُ اِلاَ اَصلِہِ⟫ *'Everything returns to its source.'*[^cite-26]

"When one contemplates this spiritual realm, one must confess that it stands free from all the attributes and conditions of the physical world. There is nothing in the material universe that resembles it. Eating, drinking, rising, sitting, sleeping, waking—all these belong to the body and have no place in the spiritual realm. Pursuing this inquiry further, one comprehends that the ⟪ar:مبدع تعالی⟫ Creator of these two worlds must be utterly liberated from the attributes of both. He is exalted, sanctified, glorified, and elevated above the names and praises we ascribe to Him. We can only invoke Him by ⟪ar:ھو⟫ 'He' (the bare pronoun), yet even in using this, we confess that His ⟪ar:ھویت⟫ very essence transcends our words. ⟪ar:اللہ اکبر⟫ Allah is beyond all comparison! Our speech, our sentences, our compositions cannot reach that height—much less can our thoughts, imaginings, and suppositions attain it.

"In sum, the one who enters the field of true epistemology will eventually confess: ⟪ar-quote:اَلعِجزُ عَن اِدرَاکُہُ اِدرَاک⟫ *'The inability to comprehend is itself a comprehension.'*[^cite-27] Such a seeker admits his own inadequacy, and this confession is the beginning of true knowledge."

### Knowledge of Allah After the Manner of Abraham

On the other hand, stands the Abrahamic way—the path of gradual ascent through the hierarchies, the cultivation of discipleship, the discernment of rank and station. ⟪ar:حضرت ابراھیم علیہ السلام⟫ Abraham (upon him be peace) ascended degree by degree, maintaining the proper distinction between each rank, until he acknowledged the truth: ⟪ar:مبدع المبدعات⟫ There is none who brings creation into being save the Creator of all creators; ⟪ar:لاَ اِلٰہَ اِلاَّ ھُو⟫ there is no god save He alone. The Qur'an narrates this ascent:

> ⟪quran 6:76-79⟫
> ⟪ar:‏فَلَمَّا جَنَّ عَلَيْهِ ٱلَّيْلُ رَآ كَوْكَبًۭا ۖ قَالَ هَذَا رَبِّى ۖ فَلَمَّآ أَفَلَ قَالَ لَآ أُحِبُّ ٱلْآفِلِينَ ‎ ‏فَلَمَّا رَآ ٱلْقَمَرَ بَازِغًۭا قَالَ هَذَا رَبِّى ۖ فَلَمَّآ أَفَلَ قَالَ لَىِٕن لَّمْ يَهْدِنِى رَبِّى لَأَكُونَنَّ مِنَ ٱلْقَوْمِ ٱلضَّآلِّينَ ‎ ‏فَلَمَّا رَآ ٱلشَّمْسَ بَازِغَةًۭ قَالَ هَذَا رَبِّى هَذآ أَكْبَرُ ۖ فَلَمَّآ أَفَلَتْ قَالَ يَقَوْمِ إِنِّى بَرِىٓءٌۭ مِّممَّا تُشْرِكُونَ ‎ ‏إِنِّى وَجَّهْتُ وَجْهِىَ لِلَّذِى فَطَرَ ٱلسَّمَوَتِ وَٱلْأَرْضَ حَنِيفًۭا ۖ وَمَآ أَنَا۠ مِنَ ٱلْمُشْرِكِينَ ‎⟫
> *When the night covered him over with darkness he saw a star. He said: "This is my lord." But when it set, he said: "I like not those that set." When he saw the moon rising up, he said: "This is my lord." But when it set, he said: "Unless my Lord guides me, I shall surely be among the erring people." When he saw the sun rising up, he said: "This is my lord. This is greater." But when it set, he said: "O my people! I am indeed free from all that you join as partners in worship with Allah. Verily, I have turned my face towards Him Who has created the heavens and the earth Hanifa (Islamic Monotheism, i.e. worshipping none but Allah Alone) and I am not of Al-Mushrikun."*

In this mystery lies a profound teaching: each ⟪ar:مؤمن مستفید⟫ believing soul in fact possesses its own ⟪ar:مفید⟫ master (its immediate superior), for the spiritual life of the believer derives from him, and above both stands the highest ⟪ar:مُوجد⟫ source of their spiritual being—the Intellect itself and the one who guides them in the faith. When a believer recognizes the excellence and perfection proper to his own station, he will surely acknowledge the greatness of his ⟪ar:مَن فَوق⟫ superior limit and confess: "Without him, my own higher rank would not exist; just as my existence would not be possible without my own station." Thus ⟪ar:مولانا امام الزمان علیہ السلام⟫ the Master of the Age (upon him be peace) is the Lord of all limits, and the ultimate limit of all limits is the Highest.

When Allah grants deeper understanding and the seeker comes to know his superior's rank more fully, the eye of insight opens. One recognizes then that each limit depends for its subsistence upon the limit above it, from whom it receives grace and derivation. This rank is called ⟪ar:خیال⟫ imagination or vision, residing in the palace of the Imam's soul. Above this is the rank of ⟪ar:فتح⟫ opening or divine disclosure, from which the ⟪ar:خیال⟫ vision receives its seal. Above that stands the rank of ⟪ar:جد⟫ might or majesty, which bestows support upon the ⟪ar:فتح⟫ opening. Thus, through progressive knowledge, one ascends to the ⟪ar:تالی⟫ Second Intellect (also called the ⟪ar:عقل ثانی⟫ Second Intellect), which is the wellspring of all spiritual life for the ranks beneath it. Then, through knowledge of the ⟪ar:سابق⟫ First Intellect (also called ⟪ar:عقل اول⟫ the First Intellect—⟪ar:مبد ع المبدعات⟫ the Originator of all originators), one confesses that comprehension is impossible, that grasp is forever denied. None has fathomed how the ⟪ar:ابداع⟫ emanation occurred, and so none can fathom the ⟪ar:مبدع تعالیٰ⟫ Source of all emanation. The human intellect lays down all its weapons and yields in confession: ⟪ar-quote:اَلعِجزُ عَن اِدرَاکُہُ اِدرَاک⟫ *"The inability to perceive is itself a true perception."*[^cite-28] This is authentic monotheism and the true knowledge of the Lord.

The Master of divine mysteries, ⟪ar:حضرت مولانا المنصوب محمد شاکر صاحب ضیا⟫ in his verse couplets has organized this teaching thus:

| These are the special servants of God | It is not possible to imagine God from us |
| --- | --- |
| Which is higher than imagination | How did they come into the human mind |

And the great philosopher Iqbāl has expressed the same mystery in memorable verse:

## The Definition of Monotheism

Allah cannot be truly believed in until one first believes in oneself as ⟪ar:'یکتا'⟫ unique and singular. Every human being is one in their own station. Since Allah Himself is one (⟪ar:'یکتا'⟫), He has created each human as a unity—a fact modern science has confirmed. Despite physical similarities among humans—all possess two eyes, two legs, two hands, one head—each person carries a unique DNA that matches no other, and no two fingerprints are identical. Similarly, each person's temperament, thoughts, and convictions differ. Thus, though all Muslims accept the five daily prayers, some emphasize prayer, others fasting, yet others pilgrimage. Authentic monotheism begins when a person first recognizes himself as singular, without peer, and offers this singularity to Allah, affirming: "My Creator, too, is singular and without partner." This is the concept of tawhīd that the Qur'an teaches: the Creator has fashioned all beings as singular and unique, each one a ⟪ar:'حد'⟫ (boundary or limit) within its own sphere.[^cite-30]

Monotheism is the knowledge of the limits—both spiritual and physical—together with the knowledge of which is supreme, and the confession that each limit is singular in its own rank, incomparable and irreplaceable.

The order and hierarchy of these limits becomes apparent through knowledge of the superior limits and understanding of the lower. Should one know the ten ranks above oneself and comprehend their hierarchy—which is highest, which lowest—then one will understand the order of all limits and their proper stations. ⟪ar:توحید، حدود روحانی و جسمانی و نفسانی⟫ Authentic tawhīd consists in knowing the spiritual, sensual, and physical boundaries and believing that each limit is unique in its place, neither elevated beyond its rank (⟪ar:غلو⟫) nor diminished below it (⟪ar:قلو⟫).

## The Upper Limit and the Lower Limit

⟪ar:صاحب رسائل اخوان الصفاء⟫ The authors of the *Rasāʾil Ikhwān al-Safāʾ* (Epistles of the Brethren of Purity) explain that everything in the universe—the heavens, the celestial bodies, all created things—stands connected in a single chain, like links bound to one another. Each link is joined above to the link above it and below to the link below it. Everything in the cosmos possesses its proper place within this continuous series, determined by its proximity to or distance from matter. This chain of limits begins with the Intellect and ascends through the heavens (which are angelic in nature) and descends through the four elements to reach humanity, who constitute the crown of creation. Humans occupy the middle point of this chain: connected above to the angels and below to the beasts.[^cite-31]

In this progression, each class is bound to both its superior and its inferior class. On the ladder of development, earth and water relate to minerals; minerals to plants; plants and animals intermingle; animals relate to humans. The lowest degree of minerals is copper (or base metal), while the highest is the crimson ruby. Among plants, the lowest is the moss that freezes upon water, and the highest is the palm tree. The highest of the mineral kingdom (the red ruby) verges upon the lowest of the plant kingdom (algae); the highest genus of plants borders upon the lowest genus of animals; the highest of animals touches the lowest of humankind.

By this hierarchy, minerals become nourishment for plants, plants for animals, and animals for humans. And in this same manner, the highest genus of humanity becomes sustenance for the angels. Each finite being is nourished and develops through that which stands at the extremity of its own boundary. In the language of the daʿwa (the divine summons), this is expressed thus: the greater fish swallows the lesser fish.

## What Is a Limit (Ḥadd)?

In the terminology of the daʿwa (the divine summons), a ḥadd (limit or boundary) is called such because each rank possesses, according to its own degree of subtlety, a distinct jurisdiction within which it is confined. Should a being desire to increase its powers, it must first elevate its rank. No two limits can coexist in the same station simultaneously. Unless a lower limit has prepared a successor, no other can assume its position; and no limit can vacate its rank unless it has readied its subordinate for elevation. This is why ⟪ar:سیدنا مؤید⟫ Our Master al-Muʾayyad taught: ⟪ar-quote:اَلمُؤمِنُ لاَ یَمُوتُ اِلاَّ یَخلُقُ المُؤمِنُ مِثلُہُ⟫ *"A believer does not depart this world until he has created (trained and prepared) another believer like himself."*[^cite-32] And he said: ⟪ar-quote:اَلتَّوحِیدُ لاَ فِی اللّٰہ بَل فِی الحُدوُد⟫ *"Monotheism (in practice) is not with respect to Allah, but with respect to His limits."*[^cite-33] The Qur'an declares:

> ⟪quran 51:56⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَمَا خَلَقْتُ ٱلْجِنَّ وَٱلْإِنسَ إِلَّا لِيَعْبُدُونِ ‎⟫
> *And I (Allah) created not the jinns and humans except they should worship Me (Alone).*

That is to say, every exalted being desires from its limits one thing alone: that they know it, emulate it, offer it their humility, and acknowledge its greatness. This is because every master is himself humble before the one above him, and he instructs his subordinates to render him the same obedience and deference he renders his superior. This mutual humility and respect has been made the ladder of spiritual ascent through the ranks. The true purpose of the Most High is to elevate His limits and establish them in their proper stations.

| Exoteric Knowledge | Esoteric Knowledge |

⟪ar:مبدع تعالی⟫ The Most High Creator fashioned all beings in the same fundamental condition and granted them all the capacity to exercise their powers. Each being acts through its own agency, establishing thereby the order of limits. To know this sequence of limits—which is ascending in rank and which descending—constitutes the true ⟪ar:معرفت و توحید⟫ knowledge and monotheism of those limits. A body is the name given to a collection of organs. All these organs together constitute ⟪ar:معرفت کلی⟫ complete knowledge of the body. Such too is the ⟪ar:مبدع تعالی⟫ knowledge by which one knows all the limits created by the Most High Creator.

To know the beginning of existence is to know the boundaries of Divine existence and presence. To comprehend the origin and culmination of a limit's existence—in which direction it tends and where it terminates—pertains to esoteric knowing. ⟪ar:عالم ابداء⟫ Within the world of emanation, various feasts have been prepared, and invitations extended to each ⟪ar:کرۃ⟫ sphere. ⟪ar:معرفت باطنۃ⟫ Esoteric knowledge concerns the extent to which the invitation was accepted and the point at which the ⟪ar:خمیرۃ ابدائیۃ⟫ fundamental nature was first kindled. Every limit possesses self-knowledge and is made known to others. What it accomplished in previous ⟪ar:توحید⟫ monotheistic epochs (⟪ar:کرّات⟫), it is guided by, and then it is positioned at the point from which it will advance through its own effort and practice.

## The Definition of Abstraction (Tajrīd)

The abstraction (tajrīd) of the Messenger of Allah consists in his separation from all innovators and creatures, and from all attributes of innovators. ⟪ar:الہیت، أبداع الذوات⟫ Abstraction is the removal of the attribute of ⟪ar:مُبدَعات⟫ createdness (the bringing of entities into existence from non-existence) from all ⟪ar:مخلوقات⟫ created and spiritual beings, together with adherence to the ⟪ar:مبدع تعالی⟫ Originator, Most High.

The very word tajrīd evokes the image of wringing a wet cloth to remove water completely (⟪ar:جَرَدَ⟫), or stripping the skin from a goat's body—a separation and extraction. Thus, abstraction means to conceive that there is no limit among all the limits of the spiritual and physical realms that possesses the power of ⟪ar:الہیت⟫ divinity—that is, the power to bring existence from non-existence. Though there are limits supremely high and lofty, endowed with great powers—some called ⟪ar:'قادر'⟫ the Mighty, some ⟪ar:'رازق'⟫ the Provider, some ⟪ar:'قھار'⟫ the Overpowering, some ⟪ar:'جبار'⟫ the Irresistible—yet none of them possesses this power. None is ⟪ar:'الہ'⟫ a god in the truest sense. That is, ⟪ar:'نیست'⟫ there is no being from ⟪ar:'ہست'⟫ among the existent except the ⟪ar:مبدع تعالی⟫ Creator, Most High, who holds the power to create from absolute nothingness.[^cite-34]

## The Definition of Transcendence (Tanziih)

⟪ar-quote:وَالتَّنزِیہ نَفِی اَلصِّفاَتُ عَن غَیبُ الغُیُوب وَالأِعتَراَف عَن جَمِیعِ الصِّفاَت الشَّرَفِ وَالجَلاَلَۃِ وَماَ یَعبَرُ بِہِ جَمِیعَ الُّغاَت وَالأِشارَۃ بِنَعُوتِ الأِلہِیَۃ وَأِنَّھاَ وَاقِعَۃٌ اِلیٰ العَقلِ اَوَّلِ⟫

*Transcendence is the negation of all attributes from the Hidden of the Hidden, together with the confession that all attributes of glory and majesty—whether expressed in diverse languages or by gesture—pointing to Divine Lordship, all rest ultimately with the First Intellect alone.*

⟪ar:تنزیہ⟫ Tanziih derives from the root ⟪ar:نزح⟫ (cleanliness or separation). It denotes the negation of all attributes from the ⟪ar:مبدع تعالی⟫ Originator, Most High, and it is expressed thus: the ⟪ar:مُنَزَّح عَنِ الصِّفاَت⟫ One Stripped of Attributes. The highest of all limits—the ⟪ar:عقل اول⟫ First Intellect—is that toward which all lesser intellects tend. To negate all His great attributes through which He is praised—whether they be expressed in any tongue or gesture—and to ascribe them only to Him, is truly tanziih.[^cite-35]

⟪ar:صفات⟫ Attributes (sifāt) are qualities that make a noun known. Allah ⟪ar:أسم ذات⟫ possesses the attribute of Essence and ninety-nine Divine Names. Should these attributes be fully comprehended, knowledge of the Essence would be attained. Since the ⟪ar:مبدع تعالی⟫ Originator possesses His ⟪ar:ذات⟫ Essence, He must possess His ⟪ar:صفات⟫ Attributes. Prior to all ⟪ar:مُبدَعات⟫ creation, the highest Creator exists, and that is the ⟪ar:عقل اول⟫ First Intellect. For the comprehension of this Being, ninety-nine attributes appeared. To attribute all these qualities is called tanziih, the ⟪ar:عقل اول⟫ First Intellect being the ⟪ar:مبدع تعالی⟫ point to which they all refer.

The ninety-nine Divine Names are, in reality, the ⟪ar:عالم روحانی، نفسانی اور جسمانی⟫ limits—spiritual, sensual, and physical—divided into groups of thirty-three. When we express a quality, we cannot declare its full measure. For instance, we do not know the full extent of ⟪ar:(سَتَّار)⟫ the Veiler's attribute within the ⟪ar:عالم روحانی، نفسانی و جسمانی⟫ three worlds. Therefore, we refer all attributes to their master—the ⟪ar:عقل اول⟫ First Intellect—who is the Imam of this entire tasbīḥ (glorification). For what is in the parts is certainly present in the whole.[^cite-36]

*Statement:* Tajrīd means to strip from oneself and all below one the attributes of those above; tanziih means to purify oneself and all below one from the attributes of ⟪ar:من فوق⟫ those above. That is to say, one should not claim kinship with the higher stations.

Therefore, Allah declares in the Qur'an:

> ⟪quran 37:159⟫
> ⟪ar:‏سُبْحَنَ ٱللَّهِ عَمَّا يَصِفُونَ ‎⟫
> *Glorified is Allah! (He is Free) from what they attribute unto Him!*

## The Distinction Between Ilāh and Allāh

The meaning of 'elevation' derives from ⟪ar:عُلُوٌّ⟫ the root ⟪ar:تعالی⟫, from which the words ⟪ar:متعال⟫ 'exalted' are formed. There are two kinds of ⟪ar:الٰھیت⟫ divinity, both related to wonder. The first is wonder at ⟪ar:أبداعُ الذَّوَات⟫ creation—how being emerges from non-being through the ⟪ar:عقل اول⟫ First Intellect. This wonder is called ⟪ar:ھُوِیَتِ مُتَعاَلِیَۃ⟫ wondrous transcendence; in other words, wonder concerning the ⟪ar:ھو⟫ nature itself of that ⟪ar:الہیت⟫ attribute.

The second kind of wonder concerns each lower limit marveling at the precedence of its superior limit in monotheism—how my superior advanced in monotheism when we both began alike in ⟪ar:کمال اول⟫ primary perfection (knowledge, power, life). This state of affairs is expressed as ⟪ar:أَلِھَا یَعلُہُ⟫ 'being amazed by it,' such that all ⟪ar:مُبدَعَات⟫ created beings marvel at themselves, and at the ⟪ar:عقل اول⟫ First Intellect they marvel, just as the ⟪ar:عقل اول⟫ First Intellect marvels at the ⟪ar:مبدع تعالی⟫ Originator, Most High.

| Ilāh (The Marvel of Origin) | Allāh (The Marvel of Transcendence and Primacy) |
| --- | --- |
| ⟪ar:الٰھیت⟫ Divinity as Originator | ⟪ar:الٰھیت⟫ Divinity as Absolute Transcendence |

The majesty of which our intellect stands astonished, and whose greatness our intellect cannot compass, is called ⟪ar:الہ⟫ *ilāh*. The entire kalimah (the declaration of monotheism) ⟪ar:لا الہ الا اللہ⟫ *"There is no god save Allah"* is composed of the three letters alif, lām, and hāʾ. The word is formed from the negation ⟪ar:(الہ)⟫ 'no god' and the affirmation ⟪ar:معبود⟫ 'the Worshipped,' and this is the reason why the true worth of the word—the valuation it deserves—cannot be attained from us: ⟪ar:وَماَ قَدرُ اﷲِ حَقَّ قَدرِہِ⟫ *"And they have not esteemed Allah with His right estimation."*[^cite-37]

In Arabic grammar, the definite article ⟪ar:علامت معرفہ (ال)⟫ serves to render a noun specific and particular. When we say "book," we mean any book; but "the book" indicates a specific book. The English equivalent is "the." Allah—in fact—is ⟪ar:ال + الہ⟫ (the + ilāh), and when the definite article is attached to ⟪ar:الہ⟫ ilāh, it becomes ⟪ar:(اللہ)⟫ Allāh. In Arabic, ⟪ar:الہ⟫ ilāh means ⟪ar:مالک⟫ "Lord," and the true owner of a thing is one who created it. In this sense, ⟪ar:الہیت⟫ Lordship attaches to our Creator. Since our ⟪ar:مبدع تعالی⟫ Almighty is the Creator of our form and our ultimate destiny, He is for us the ⟪ar:الہ⟫ ilāh. When our limit stands in the position of the highest ⟪ar:الہ⟫ ilāh, all the limits above it stand also in that station for us.[^cite-38]

We advance in the field of monotheism by knowing the limits, crossing them one after another, until we arrive at a point where our thoughts cannot compass them because of their magnitude. In the knowledge of it, our intellect becomes humble, bewildered, and disturbed. At that moment, that limit becomes reserved for us, and we apply the definite article ⟪ar:علامت معرفہ (ال)⟫ to it, making ⟪ar:ال + الہ = اللہ⟫ of it the Allāh for us. According to this analogy, each person's Allāh is distinct; thus all the Imāms, the Naqības, and their followers become Allāh for us, because we cannot comprehend the elevation and majesty of their stations, and for them our rank is ⟪ar:وَلِھَا یَعلُہ⟫ cause for wonder.

*Statement:* We regard the Imam (upon him be peace) as a god (ilāh) not because we merely wonder at his exalted rank, but because we confess that we shall never attain his great station. They possess the power to create forms; we cannot. They produce theologians; we cannot.

## The Monotheism, Abstraction, and Transcendence of the Originator, Most High

⟪ar:مومن مستجیب⟫ The moment a ⟪ar:مکاسر⟫ Breaker speaks, he is astonished at his rank, and to the wonderment of the believer, he becomes his master (⟪ar:الہ⟫). Then the believer perceives that the Breaker himself marvels at the ⟪ar:ماذون⟫ Licenser. Seeing this, he realizes that the Breaker is not his ultimate master, but that it is the ⟪ar:الہ⟫ Licenser who is so. Then he observes that the Licenser too marvels at the one above him. He traces this wonder through all the circles until he brings his amazement to the ⟪ar:امام علیہ السلام⟫ Imam (upon him be peace), master of the physical limits. Upon reaching there, he sees that the Imam himself is perplexed by the spiritual limits above him.

At this juncture, the believer begins to explore the spiritual limits and arrives at the seat of his own wonder—the ⟪ar:عقل اول⟫ First Intellect, chief of the spiritual limits. Upon reaching there and perceiving that even the ⟪ar:عقل اول⟫ First Intellect marvels at the ⟪ar:مبدع تعالی⟫ Originator, Most High, and itself has an ⟪ar:الہ⟫ ilāh, he recognizes that access to what lies beyond is impossible. He comes to realize that none of these ⟪ar:الہ⟫ spiritual and physical limits possess the power to bring creation from nothingness. Having attained monotheism in this way, through knowledge of all the limits, he is addressed by the All-Knowing:

After this command, the believer begins to negate the ⟪ar:عقل اول⟫ First Intellect, declaring that it is not his true ⟪ar:الہ⟫ master because it too is perplexed by something above it. He brings negation to bear through all the spiritual and physical limits, confessing that none of these limits is his true ⟪ar:الہ⟫ (Creator), and he acknowledges that all the attributes existing in all these limits are not intrinsic to them but are ascribed to the ⟪ar:عقل اول⟫ First Intellect. In sum, the believer, accepting each limit (because of the magnitude of its rank), takes his wonder upward through the realms until he reaches the ⟪ar:عقل اول⟫ First Intellect. Upon arrival, he begins the work of negation, bringing that negation downward through each circle until he returns to himself, the ⟪ar:مکاسر⟫ Breaker. Upon reaching that point, he becomes convinced that none of the attributes of creation can adhere to the ⟪ar:مبدع تعالی⟫ Originator, Most High, and he purges himself of all ⟪ar:مبدع تعالی⟫ names and attributes other than the Originator's.

## The Monotheism, Abstraction, and Transcendence of the Highest Limit

### The Unification of the Highest Limit

Unification (tawhīd) with respect to the higher limit means that we recognize the greatness of his station and acknowledge that my superior is higher in rank than I, and I lower than him. We confess that he is singular in his rank and possesses no partner in that degree.

### The Abstraction of the Highest Limit

Abstraction (tajrīd) with respect to the highest means to admit that I do not possess the attribute of divinity—that I lack the power to create my own form and to elevate myself to the station of my superior, just as the ⟪ar:غیب الغیوب⟫ Hidden of the Hidden created us both in one place of ⟪ar:(کمال اول)⟫ primary perfection. Only my superior can elevate me to my rank. I cannot do so myself. The Almighty fashions the form of his superior; I cannot fashion the form of my superior. To strip from myself the ⟪ar:صفت الہیت⟫ attribute of divinity and from all the lower limits as well is truly abstraction.

### The Transcendence of the Highest Limit

Transcendence (tanziih) means to acknowledge that all the qualities I possess—wisdom, beauty, benevolence, generosity, miracles, and the like—are inferior to his. If I am ignorant, my superior is wise; if I am a worshipper, my superior is superior to me in worship. If I am a scholar, my superior possesses knowledge beyond mine, while I am ignorant by comparison. Whatever excellences I possess, these qualities are not truly mine; they belong to my superior, for he is greater than I.

And to establish the principle that my superior grants me knowledge, not I him—that he provides for me, not I for him; that he sustains me, and I cannot sustain him—is to truly effect the removal of all the attributes of one's superior from one's own attributes. This is his transcendence (tanziih).[^cite-40]
