## Imam Jaʿfar's Discourse with the Doubter

On one occasion, a man came to see ⟪ar:امام جعفر الصادق علیہ السلام⟫ (Imam Jaʿfar al-Ṣādiq, peace be upon him) who harbored doubt concerning the existence of Allah. The Imam inquired as to his name, and the man replied, "My name is ⟪ar:عبد اﷲ⟫ (ʿAbd Allāh, Slave of Allah)." The Imam then posed a penetrating question: "You bear the name of one enslaved to Allah—but who is your master? Who is the sovereign of the earth and of the heavens?" The man fell silent, unable to answer.

The Imam continued with a further question: "Are you aware that this earth possesses an upper region and a lower region?" The man admitted his ignorance. "Likewise," the Imam explained, "the heavens too possess upper and lower regions. Do you know of them?" When the man again confessed ignorance, adding that he did not believe anything inhabited those celestial realms, the Imam responded with gentle rebuke: "Glory be to Allah (subḥān Allāh)! ⟪ar:سبحان اﷲ!⟫ You have neither traversed the heavens nor the earth, you have not acquainted yourself with the east or the west, yet you presume to deny what dwells within them. Is it fitting for a prudent person to deny that of which he possesses no knowledge?"

When the Imam asked what the man thought of God's existence, the doubter replied hesitantly, "Perhaps God exists, perhaps He does not." The Imam observed: "A person ignorant of a matter possesses no refutation against one who knows it." He then drew the man's attention to the celestial bodies: "Do you not observe that the sun and moon appear in succession, never erring in their movements, never deviating from their ordered courses? This constancy cannot be accidental. They move as if constrained—as if imprisoned within spheres from which they cannot escape. If they possessed sovereignty and the power to break free from their orbits, why do they not do so? There is but one explanation: they have been compelled by One who is mightier than they, who rules over them."[^cite-1]

The Imam continued his argument: "If you suppose that time itself has prescribed their movements, then why does time not alter their order? Why does it not displace them from their appointed stations? When they arrive at their destined times, why does time not recall them before their cycle completes? Why does the earth eternally remain below? Why does the sky eternally remain above? Why does not the sky descend beneath its horizons when all things incline toward the earth? Why do the heavens and earth not cleave together? Why does not all terrestrial matter cohere into one mass?"

When the man asked why all creatures worship Allah when they cannot perceive Him, the Imam replied: "Because their hearts have witnessed Him through the radiance of faith (nūr al-īmān). They obtain proof of His existence through their inner vision, just as ordinary human beings establish the existence of someone through sensory sight. The eyes recognize His reality through the order and stability of His creation. Mankind have believed in Him through His Messengers, His Books, and His manifest verses. And the scholars perceive Him without seeing—they discern Him through the signs of His greatness and majesty."[^cite-2]

When pressed on how to verify the truthfulness of the prophets, the Imam explained: "Just as we have demonstrated that there exists a Creator of this universe who transcends the comprehension of created sight, and who cannot be in direct relation with His creatures nor they with Him—save through intermediaries—it becomes evident that the Creator could not Himself guide the servants toward good and forbid them from evil. Therefore, He has selected from among His servants certain individuals of distinction who speak on His behalf and convey His commands to the people. These are His Messengers (al-rusul), sent with proofs and miracles. They are wise in themselves and teach others the knowledge of reason. Although they resemble humanity in bodily form, they are distinct and surpass others in their spiritual stations. Unto them have been granted miracles: they revive the dead, restore sight to the blind, and cure the leper of his affliction."

When the man asked from what substance the Creator fashioned all creatures, the Imam responded: "From nothingness (min al-ʿadam)." The man then inquired, "How can anything come into being from nothingness?" The Imam replied: "Every thing must originate either from another thing or from non-being. If it originates from another thing, then that second thing must be eternal and unchanging in its nature—possessing always the same color, the same original essence. But if this world originated from such a thing, how did diversity of colors and forms emerge from it? If that eternal thing were alive, whence came death? And if it were dead, whence came life?"[^cite-3]

When the man persisted, asking why the Creator does not reveal Himself so that all doubt might be dispelled and all people could worship Him with complete certainty, the Imam replied with intensity: "Woe unto you! He has manifested His might plainly and openly within you and through you, yet you claim not to have seen Him! When you did not exist, He brought you into being. In your infancy, He caused you to grow. In your weakness, He granted you strength. Who heals you of sickness? Who brings you joy after sorrow and sorrow after joy? Who grants you rest after fatigue and fatigue after rest? Who kindles love after hatred and hatred after love?" The Imam paused, then said: "The Imam showed me so many signs of Allah that I began to imagine if the Imam had not ceased his discourse, Allah would have appeared to me in sensible form."

## The Rational Proofs for the Existence of the Creator

### The First Argument: The World is Contingent, Not Eternal

The world is ḥādith (حادث, contingent)—that is, it has not existed eternally, but came into being from non-existence at some point in time. Everything within this world depends upon something other than itself, a dependence that testifies to its needfulness. In other words, the world operates according to the principle of causality: every cause is itself dependent upon its own prior cause. **The very fact that everything in this world is contingent upon something else proves that the world itself must be contingent upon an agent other than itself.** No entity within creation can establish its own existence. Compounded things become dispersed; disparate elements cohere. Nothing persists eternally in a single state. Existence alternates with ceasing; ceasing alternates with existence again. These perpetual transformations in circumstances and conditions constitute clear evidence of its ḥādith character. Once it becomes manifest that the world is contingent, it likewise becomes evident that there must exist One who brought this world into being.

### The Second Argument: Motion and the Mover

A thing and its cause are two distinct entities. We observe that a mill moves; it possesses motion. Although the mill possesses the capacity for motion, this motion does not originate from its own essence. If movement were intrinsic to the mill and constitutive of its nature, it would perpetually be in motion. Yet we observe that the mill is sometimes in motion and sometimes at rest. These two states—motion and rest—cannot arise from the mill's own essence. When they do not emanate from the essence, we are compelled to conclude that the motion of the mill cannot exist unless there is an agent who moves it. It has been demonstrated that nothing brings itself into existence; rather, all things come into being through an external force or agency. Once this principle is accepted, we must equally accept that the creation and motion of this universe cannot occur through itself. Therefore, there must necessarily exist a ṣāniʿ (صانع, Creator) to account for its being.

### The Third Argument: The Harmony of Disparate Parts

The entire universe resembles a single body composed of different and distinct parts, limited in number and varied in form. We observe, for instance, that the heavens and planets differ essentially from fire and water in their nature. Similarly, fire and water differ from air and earth. The three kingdoms—mineral (maʿdan), vegetable (nabāt), and animal (ḥaywān)—differ from air and soil in their character. ⟪ar:موالید ثلاثۃ (معدن، نبات اور حیوان)⟫ Yet despite these profound differences in appearance, all are bound to one another in a unified system and work in concert toward common purposes.

Consider the example of a wooden door: it comprises different components—planks, hinges, screws, posts—all connected together. Although each part differs from the others in form and substance, they are nonetheless harmoniously arranged and coordinated to fulfill their collective function. This harmony would be impossible were they not properly fitted together. Yet we observe that these parts, despite their harmony, lack the capacity within themselves to arrange themselves in this precise order.

Similarly, the universe contains many parts that differ from one another—such as the spheres (aflāk) ⟪ar:افلاک⟫ and animals (ḥaywān) ⟪ar:حیوان⟫—and some that resemble one another, such as the sphere of Saturn (falak al-Zuḥal) ⟪ar:فلک زحل⟫ and the sphere of Mars (falak al-Mirrīkh) ⟪ar:فلک مریخ⟫ (both being celestial spheres), or apples and grapes (both being fruits). All are interwoven in the same purposive order and harmony. This coordination cannot result from their own nature; it must proceed from an external agent. This demonstrates that there exists One capable of bringing all things into existence and binding them together with that particular order and harmony that is the ṣāniʿ.

### The Fourth Argument: Letters and Their Arrangement

We observe that different languages exist in the world. Each language possesses distinct characters that are individually without meaning. Yet when these characters are combined in a particular order, they produce various words through which the states of the soul are expressed. These characters do not arrange themselves; rather, an intelligent agent joins them in a specific sequence. Consider the word ⟪ar:مبدع⟫ (mubdiʿ, Creator): the letter ⟪ar:میم⟫ (mīm) comes first; then the letter ⟪ar:ب⟫ (bāʾ) is attached; then ⟪ar:دال⟫ (dāl) is added; and finally ⟪ar:عین⟫ (ʿayn) at the end. In their original, undifferentiated state, these letters possess a different natural order: ⟪ar:ب⟫ would follow ⟪ar:دال⟫, ⟪ar:عین⟫ would follow in turn, and ⟪ar:میم⟫ would be at the end—a sequence very different from the word ⟪ar:مبدع⟫. This proves that the letters do not cohere of themselves; rather, they are joined together by an intelligent agent to create a specific meaning and intention, thereby indicating that someone else is coordinating them.

In the same manner, this universe resembles letters that, although they possess an order in their primordial state, are connected to one another in an altogether different arrangement. This reordering cannot come about through their own essence. It follows, therefore, that there exists One who arranges all things in various orders for specific purposes, and this One is the ṣāniʿ.

### The Fifth Argument: Cause and Effect

Everything that is receptive to effect and undergoes change of state by receiving that effect is itself an effect. The transformation in a thing's state cannot occur through itself unless some cause acts upon it. Conversely, whatever is unreceptive to effect and remains immutable in its original state cannot be invalid; it must be necessary and eternal. We observe that there are different realms in the universe—the corporeal (jirmānī), the celestial spheres (aflāk), the elements (ummahāt), plants (nabāt), minerals (maʿdan), and animals (ḥaywān) ⟪ar:جرمانی، افلاک، امہات، نبات، معدن، حیوان⟫. Not all remain in a single state. The planets constantly move from one position to another. Day perpetually transforms into night, and night into day. The seasons continually change. The occurrence of change in all these conditions indicates that all these realms are receptive to effect and are thus causal. Where there are those that receive effect, there must necessarily be an agent—a ṣāniʿ—who produces that effect.

### The Sixth Argument: The Senses and the Sensible World

This world is the world of sensible things (muḥassasāt), meaning everything ⟪ar:محسوسات⟫ within it can be perceived by the five senses (al-ḥawāss al-khamsah) ⟪ar:حواس خمسۃ⟫. Everything in this world can be seen, heard, smelled, tasted, or felt. Here we have two orders: the power of sense (quwwat al-ḥiss) ⟪ar:قوت حس⟫ and the world that can be perceived through this sense. If we regard this world as eternal, we would necessarily have to regard the senses as even more ancient and prior, for the perceiving faculty stands at a higher rank than the perceived object—just as a vessel is superior to the contents it holds, since without the vessel the contents would be lost. We have already established that this world did not come into being through itself. If the lowest order cannot bring itself into existence, how much less can the power of perception itself (al-ḥawāss al-khamsah), which is far more subtle and refined, bring itself into being? There must necessarily exist a ṣāniʿ to account for its existence.

### The Seventh Argument: Senses and Sensations in Relation

The senses can perceive only those things whose elements are proportionate to their powers and inferior in rank to them. The power of sight can perceive only what is visible to the eye; the power of smell operates only upon things that possess odor. When an object is more subtle and refined than the five senses (al-ḥawāss al-khamsah) ⟪ar:حواس خمسۃ⟫ and thus superior in rank to them, those senses cannot perceive it. This demonstrates that this sensible world is inferior to the senses. Visibility in an object results from the power of sight acting upon the visible thing. That is, we perceive things through the effects these senses produce. The sensible objects are without effect without the sensing power, and the sensing power is without effect without the sensible objects ⟪ar:حواس خمسۃ⟫. The senses exert an influence; the objects receive that influence, and this receptivity causes the object to become sensible. When all these things exist together, and their very existence is impossible through themselves, there must necessarily be a ṣāniʿ who brings them into being.

### The Eighth Argument: The Attributes of Different Parts and Their Distinction

The universe comprises different parts, each possessing attributes unique to itself—such as lightness, darkness, subtlety, and heaviness. If this world were eternal, we would not observe the hierarchy and differentiation among its parts that we in fact perceive. For example, luminosity is the special attribute of the sun; moisture and fluidity belong exclusively to water. Similarly, heat is the distinctive property of fire, lightness belongs to air, and heaviness characterizes the earth. When these specific attributes are present in these particular parts, it becomes evident that there must exist a cause that produces these attributes within them—a cause that creates these distinguishing characteristics by which the parts are classified and differentiated from one another. When such a cause exists, there must equally exist an agent who produces that cause. If there were no prior cause to bring about this cause (which endowed all these parts with their distinctive properties), then all parts would be identical. All would be bright, or all dark. All would be heavy, or all light. This demonstrates that there must necessarily exist a ṣāniʿ in this universe.
