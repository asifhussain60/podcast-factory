## The Call to Prayer

The muezzin (mu'adhdhin) functions simultaneously as preacher and as the voice of the summons (daʿwa, the invitation to knowledge). The call to prayer (adhān), proclaimed aloud from the place of prayer, represents an outward proclamation of the preacher's message—or of the Imam's teaching—to all people, in both private and public gatherings. The structure of the adhān itself embodies cosmic significance: the minarets from which it is called exemplify the Prophet's station as the highest and noblest of the physical limits (ḥadd dānī, the lower limit).[^cite-1] When the muezzin recites the adhān, all present should respond with its words, for the Messenger of Allah declared: "Whoever hears the call to prayer at the mosque and then departs is a hypocrite"—one whose tongue and heart speak in contradiction.

The adhān functions on multiple levels of meaning. At one level, it invites participation in Islamic tradition; at another, it acknowledges our binding within a chain of transmission (silsila) that begins and ends with ʿAlī. The adhān comprises twenty-two words, corresponding to the twenty-two *words* of the Qur'an—understood in Ismaili exegesis as the succession of Imams from ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib to Imam ʿAlī al-Ṭayyib.[^cite-2]

The total utterances of the adhān number ten, which correspond to the ten limits of religion (hudūd al-dīn): five spiritual limits—the First Intellect (al-ʿAql al-Awwal), the Universal Soul (al-nafs al-kulliyya), and three others—and five physical limits, namely the Imam, the Gate (bāb), the Proof (ḥujja), the Summoner (dāʿī), and the Preparatory One (muʿaddun). The repetition of these phrases twice indicates the conjunction of intellect and soul, the rational and the foundational. Each successive recitation shows that the rank of the upper limit corresponds to that of its lower counterpart, yet exceeds it in dignity.

**"Allahu Akbar, Allahu Akbar"**

These four magnifications are grounded in the four supreme limits of religion—the First Intellect, the Second Intellect, Intellect proper, and Foundation—and signify the elevation of their ranks.[^cite-3]

**"Ashhad an la ilaha illa Allah" (twice)**

This attestation declares the unique and transcendent status of the First Intellect (al-ʿAql al-Awwal) as the true Lord of both spiritual and physical worlds. All other limits remain bewildered in contemplation of its knowledge. The first utterance illuminates the spiritual dimension; the second, the physical.

**"Ashhad anna Muhammad al-Rasul Allah" (twice)**

This bears witness to the Prophet's exalted station and to a relationship with the First Intellect unprecedented in creation—a proximity so intimate that no separation existed between them. The Prophet received knowledge directly from the First Intellect and became its servant and messenger. The first attestation honors Muhammad Muṣṭafā; the second honors Maulana Muhammad ibn Ismāʿīl, the Seventh Imam, through whom the concealed Imam era commenced.[^cite-4]

**"Ashhad anna Maulana ʿAlī Walī Allah" (twice)**

The first testimony is for Ḥaḍrat ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib; the second, for the Qāʾim al-Qiyāma (the Resurrector). In this phrase alone is ʿAlī called *Mawlā* (Master), whereas the Prophet is not so named in the preceding clause—a deliberate distinction. It commemorates the Day of Ghadīr, when the Messenger of Allah proclaimed: "Whomsoever I am mawlā (master), ʿAlī is also mawlā." Thus the *walāya* (spiritual guardianship) transferred from the Prophet to ʿAlī.

**"Hayya ʿala al-ṣalāt" (twice)**

This summons joy to the believers and the spiritually mature who adhere to the limits of the Qur'an and defend the truths of religion. These form the blessed community of the Yemenite companions.

**"Hayya ʿala al-falāḥ" (twice)**

Good news is proclaimed to those newly entered into the era of the Imam of the Age (Imām al-Zamān)—exhortation to exert themselves and join the companions of Yemen in spiritual flourishing.

**"Hayya ʿala khayri al-ʿamal" (twice)**

This refers to the Imam's fulfilment of the Prophet's supplication and acknowledges him as the liberator of drowning souls from the sea of confusion—the supreme deed.

**"Muhammad wa ʿAlī khayru al-Bashar wa ʿAtratuhum Khayru al-ʿAttar" (twice)**

This establishes that genealogy and succession transcend mere lineage. Muhammad ibn ʿAbd Allah and ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib and their progeny surpass all Imams of previous epochs. These two recitations (called *duffatayn* in the adhān) indicate that the Imams of the Mahdī era and the Qāʾim and their successors shall exceed the Imams of the Muhammadan dispensation in rank. At a higher mystical level, it alludes to a moment when the veils between Muhammad and ʿAlī were momentarily equalized in the Triple Crown (tāj-i thulāthī).[^cite-5]

**"Allahu Akbar, Allahu Akbar"**

These concluding magnifications affirm that the First Intellect alone holds supreme rank among all spiritual and physical limits.

**"La ilaha illa Allah" (twice)**

This final testimony points to the singularity of the First Intellect—that station of divine unity (tawḥīd) and the realm of unification—surpassing all other intellects. No other intellect may attain this station. Though all intellects stand opposite to *Allah*, none possesses the capacity to become the First Intellect itself. In the same manner, each Speaking Prophet (Nāṭiq) serves as the locus of revelation in his age, and the Imam in his era.

## The Standing

The Iqāma comprises nineteen words, each corresponding to one of the Imams—from al-Ḥusayn ibn ʿAlī through Imam ʿAlī al-Ṭayyib—excluding the two concealed Imams (Imam al-Ḥasan and the Eleventh Imam, Maulana ʿAbdullah al-Mahdī).[^cite-6] 

The Iqāma represents an inward summons to the inner self, constituting an aspect of the narrator's (rāwī) spiritual life alone. The lowering of the voice signals that the preacher must veil the esoteric knowledge (ʿilm al-bāṭin) and conceal it from the undeserving. Where the Speaking Prophet (Nāṭiq) exemplifies the adhān, the Foundation (Asās) exemplifies the Iqāma. The Foundation's essential function is to extend the inward invitation; thus the adhān must precede the Iqāma—for without the outward proclamation, the inner summons cannot manifest.

**"Qad qamat al-ṣalāt" (twice)**

This phrase indicates that the Summoner (dāʿī) maintains fidelity to both the outward and the inward invitations, bound by obligation to each.

**"La ilaha illa Allah" (once)**

This single utterance reaffirms that the last limit—the Qāʾim al-Qiyāma—stands alone in the rank of monotheistic uniqueness, their station so exalted that none shall ever equal them in the physical realm.[^cite-7]

## The Distinction Between Adhān and Iqāma

The adhān summons to spiritual communion (uns-i rūḥānī); when proclaimed, revelations descend from the realm of divine life. The radiance (ishrāq) attending the adhān differs from that of the Iqāma. The adhān's radiance is called the Islamic form; that of the Iqāma, the form of faith. The adhān propagates prophethood (nubuwwa) and divine sending; the Iqāma establishes the standing of the Qāʾim and the seat of infallibility (ʿiṣma).

It is commanded that the adhān be recited with melodious tone, gathering the sounds once apparent in this world and now assembling them in spiritual capacity for prayer. The Iqāma, conversely, is performed rapidly and without ornamentation, for those illuminated by its forms—the people of the inner—require no outward melody; they answer the muezzin's inner call to prayer.

When the muezzin proclaims "Hayya ʿala al-ṣalāt" in the Iqāma, the Islamic form is illuminated—wherefore he follows with "La ḥawl wa lā quwwa illā bi-llāh al-ʿAlī al-ʿAẓīm" (There is no strength save in Allah, the Most High, the Mighty). When he proclaims "Hayya ʿala al-falāḥ," the forms of faith radiate forth—the supreme radiance—and thus he declares "Mashā Allah" (What Allah wills).[^cite-8]

The adhān solicits intercession (tawassul) through the Imam; the Iqāma solicits intercession through his divine lordship (ʿilāhiyyat). Through the adhān alone do we enter the world of Islam; through the Iqāma do we enter the world of faith.

## "I Testify That Muhammad Is His Servant and His Messenger"

It is well established that the Prophet sent his greetings to Imam Muḥammad al-Bāqir through Jābir ibn ʿAbd Allah al-Anṣārī. Maulana Muḥammad al-Bāqir is the first Imam after the Prophet. Indeed, the Prophet conveyed to al-Bāqir, through Jābir, instructions for his training within the Assembly of the Small (Majmaʿ al-Ṣaghīr), that hidden convocation where esoteric knowledge is transmitted.[^cite-9]

When the believer first attests in the adhān, "I testify that Muḥammad is the servant of Allah and His Messenger," the Messenger himself does not utter this testimony—his station transcends such declaration. Rather, the people of the Assembly of the Small testify: our visitors and teachers are not Muhammad alone, but also others to come. Maulana Muḥammad al-Bāqir will arrive after you, they declare, to guide us further.

Second, this attestation prefigures Maulana Muḥammad ibn Ismāʿīl—meaning that Muḥammad al-Bāqir transferred the Assembly to Muḥammad ibn Ismāʿīl, from whom it would pass to Maulana ʿAbdullah al-Mahdī, and thence to the Qāʾim, where all shall be unified and perfected.

Similarly, among the Imams bearing the name ʿAlī, they received the Assembly through Amīr al-Muʾminīn ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib—first among them being Maulana ʿAlī Zayn al-ʿĀbidīn, whose spiritual authority and luminous knowledge established the esoteric foundations for the Imams to follow.
