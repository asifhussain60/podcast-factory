## A Comparison of Mawlānā ʿAlī and Prophet ʿĪsā (Jesus)

Considerable parallels emerge between Jesus—known in Islamic tradition as the First Messiah—and Mawlānā ʿAlī, regarded by the faithful as the Second Messiah. The attributes ascribed by the followers of Jesus are found, in their entirety, reflected in ʿAlī. Moreover, the disposition of the people of Jesus toward their Prophet paralleled, almost identically, the attitude of the Muslim community toward the Prophet Muḥammad. For this reason the Prophet himself drew the comparison explicitly, declaring that ʿAlī's example (*mathal*) is that of Jesus son of Mary.[^cite-1]

In elucidating why Jesus received the appellation "the Messiah" (al-Masīḥ), scholars have explained that he was called thus because he was anointed—whether with the waters of the Jordan or with oil, authorities differ. In a parallel manner, ʿAlī was anointed in purity such that no trace of pre-Islamic ignorance (*jāhiliyyah*) adhered to him. Just as Jesus received prophethood while still a child, so too did ʿAlī accept faith in the Prophet when he was young and remained in solitary prayer with him for seven years. And as the Messiah abandoned the world by pronouncing a triple repudiation—⟪ar-quote:طلقتک ثلاثا⟫—the Messenger of Allāh addressed the material world in similar terms: ⟪ar-quote:طلقتک ثلاثا⟫ "O gold and silver, deceive whosoever you will—all save me."[^cite-2]

When describing the virtues of ʿAlī, the Prophet declared: "Were I not apprehensive that people would say of you what the Christians said of Jesus, I would speak such words concerning your excellence that whoever heard them would place the dust from beneath your feet upon his eyes and drink the water of your ritual ablution."[^cite-3] This restraint itself testifies to the profound spiritual rank ʿAlī occupied.

Allāh—mighty and exalted—drew the comparison between ʿAlī and the Messiah in the Qurʾān:

> ⟪quran 43:57-58⟫
> ⟪ar:‏ وَلَمَّا ضُرِبَ ٱبْنُ مَرْيَمَ مَثَلًا إِذَا قَوْمُكَ مِنْهُ يَصِدُّونَ ‎ ‏وَقَالُوٓا۟ ءَأَلِهَتُنَا خَيْرٌ أَمْ هُوَ ۚ مَا ضَرَبُوهُ لَكَ إِلَّا جَدَلًۢا ۚ بَلْ هُمْ قَوْمٌ خَصِمُونَ ‎⟫
> *And when the son of Maryam (Mary) is quoted as an example, behold! Your people cry aloud (laugh out at the example). And say: "Are our aliha (gods) better or is he [ʿIsa (Jesus)]?" They quoted not the above example except for argument. Nay! But they are a quarrelsome people.*
> — **Quran 43:57–58**

In this verse, the reference to the Messiah is understood by the people of interpretation (*ahl al-taʾwīl*) and the people of intellect to pertain to Ḥazrat ʿAlī. Consider: what enmity could the Prophet's idolatrous companions have harbored toward Jesus, a figure separated from them by six centuries and belonging to a foreign faith? One loves or hates only those one knows. The Quraysh polytheists had no acquaintance with Christianity and no reason to oppose a distant historical figure. Yet they grew enraged at the mere mention of this name, contending with the Messenger himself. Our reasoning thus demonstrates that the "Messiah" referenced here is one contemporary with the Prophet's people, one whose presence provoked their immediate hostility. When they heard the Prophet's name invoked, they quarreled and fought against the Messenger. In the second clause of the verse, Allāh states: "Are our gods greater or is he?" The terms ⟪ar:'اللہ'⟫ (Allāh), ⟪ar:'الہ'⟫ (deity), or ⟪ar:'معبود'⟫ (the worshipped one) here refer to those persons whom the Prophet's people regarded as their leaders and elders, whom they followed unthinkingly, without discerning their worthiness.

---

On one occasion, a Christian visitor approached the Messenger of Allāh and, referencing this verse, objected: "O Messenger of Allāh! We have never worshipped our scholars. We did not bow to them, nor did we call them our lords. Why then do you level such an accusation against us?"

> ⟪quran 9:31⟫
> ⟪ar:‏ٱتَّخَذُوٓا۟ أَحْبَارَهُمْ وَرُهْبَنَهُمْ أَرْبَابًۭا مِّن دُونِ ٱللَّهِ وَٱلْمَسِيحَ ٱبْنَ مَرْيَمَ وَمَآ أُمِرُوٓا۟ إِلَّا لِيَعْبُدُوٓا۟ إِلَهًۭا وَحِدًۭا ۖ لَّآ إِلَهَ إِلَّا هُوَ ۚ سُبْحَنَهُۥ عَمَّا يُشْرِكُونَ ‎⟫
> *They (Jews and Christians) took their rabbis and their monks to be their lords besides Allah (by obeying them in things which they made lawful or unlawful according to their own desires without being ordered by Allah), and (they also took as their Lord) Messiah, son of Maryam (Mary), while they (Jews and Christians) were commanded [in the Taurat (Torah) and the Injeel (Gospel)) to worship none but One Ilah (God - Allah) La ilaha illa Huwa (none has the right to be worshipped but He). Praise and glory be to Him, (far above is He) from having the partners they associate (with Him).*
> — **Quran 9:31**

The Prophet replied: "Did you not obey their unlawful commands—those ordinances they declared permissible or forbidden without warrant from the Qurʾān and Sunnah—and accept them as binding?" The Christian acknowledged, "Yes, we obeyed their every command." The Prophet then said: "This is worship. This is precisely what you do when you acknowledge these scholars as lords before Allāh."[^cite-4]

---

On this principle, the followers of the Prophet disputed with him, saying: ⟪ar-quote:وَقَالُوٓا۟ ءَأَلِھَتُنَا خَيْرٌ أَمْ ھو ؟⟫ "Are our caliphs and the Imāms whom we have chosen superior, or is ʿAlī?"

> ⟪quran 43:59⟫
> ⟪ar:‏إِنْ هُوَ إِلَّا عَبْدٌ أَنْعَمْنَا عَلَيْهِ وَجَعَلْنَاهُ مَثَلًۭا لِّبَنِىٓ إِسْرَٓءِيلَ ‎⟫
> *He [ʿIsa (Jesus)] was not more than a slave. We granted Our Favour to him, and We made him an example to the Children of Israel.*
> — **Quran 43:59**

The nature of blessing (*niʿma*) is clarified further in the verse:

> ⟪quran 5:3⟫
> ⟪ar:‏حُرِّمَتْ عَلَيْكُمُ ٱلْمَيْتَةُ وَٱلدَّمُ وَلَحْمُ ٱلْخِنزِيرِ وَمَآ أُهِلَّ لِغَيْرِ ٱللَّهِ بِهِۦ وَٱلْمُنْخَنِقَةُ وَٱلْمَوْقُوذَةُ وَٱلْمُتَرَدِّيَةُ وَٱلنَّطِيحَةُ وَمَآ أَكَلَ ٱلسَّبُعُ إِلَّا مَا ذَكَّيْتُمْ وَمَا ذُبِحَ عَلَى ٱلنُّصُبِ وَأَن تَسْتَقْسِمُوا۟ بِٱلْأَزْلَمِ ۚ ذَلِكُمْ فِسْقٌ ۗ ٱلْيَوْمَ يَئِسَ ٱلَّذِينَ كَفَرُوا۟ مِن دِينِكُمْ فَلَا تَخْشَوْهُمْ وَٱخْشَوْنِ ۚ ٱلْيَوْمَ أَكْمَلْتُ لَكُمْ دِينَكُمْ وَأَتْمَمْتُ عَلَيْكُمْ نِعْمَتِى وَرَضِيتُ لَكُمُ ٱلْإِسْلَمَ دِينًۭا ۚ فَمَنِ ٱضْطُرَّ فِى مَخْمَصَةٍ غَيْرَ مُتَجَانِفٍۢ لِّإِثْمٍۢ ۙ فَإِنَّ ٱللَّهَ غَفُورٌۭ رَّحِيمٌۭ ‎⟫
> *Forbidden to you (for food) are: Al-Maytatah (the dead animals—cattle-beast not slaughtered), blood, the flesh of swine, and the meat of that which has been slaughtered as a sacrifice for others than Allah, or has been slaughtered for idols, etc., or on which Allah's Name has not been mentioned while slaughtering, and that which has been killed by strangling, or by a violent blow, or by a headlong fall, or by the goring of horns—and that which has been (partly) eaten by a wild animal—unless you are able to slaughter it (before its death); and that which is sacrificed (slaughtered) on An-Nusub (stone altars). (Forbidden) also is to use arrows seeking luck or decision, (all) that is Fisqun (disobedience of Allah and sin). This day, those who disbelieved have given up all hope of your religion, so fear them not, but fear Me. This day, I have perfected your religion for you, completed My Favour upon you, and have chosen for you Islam as your religion. But as for him who is forced by severe hunger, with no inclination to sin (such can eat these above-mentioned meats), then surely, Allah is Oft-Forgiving, Most Merciful.*
> — **Quran 5:3**

The first Messiah was merely a servant (*ʿabd*) of Allāh to whom Allāh bestowed blessing, yet the Second Messiah is himself a blessing (*niʿma*) incarnate—his entire being constitutes a grace for the people of religion. The entire import of the verse declares ⟪ar-quote:وجعلنا مثلا لبنی اسرائیل⟫: "We have made him an example for the Children of Israel." The ancient Israelites were descendants of the prophets of Israel; that is, all were believers and exemplars of Israelite virtue. The descendants of the First Messiah—the Christians—did not honor the Messiah, and the Muslims, followers of the Second Messiah, honored him as the very foundation of faith. When the parallel holds true, just as Christians erroneously elevated their Messiah to divinity, so too some have called ʿAlī divine—a grievous error born of the very same misunderstanding.[^cite-5]

A further subtle correspondence exists between the First and Last Messiahs. Regarding the First, three positions emerged: the Christians called him the Son of God and God himself; the Jews branded him the son of adultery—a bastard, they claimed, and a deceiver. The Muslims, guided by the teaching of their Prophet, placed him in his rightful station, acknowledging him as a messenger of Allāh. Likewise, three positions have arisen concerning the Second Messiah. Some have called him God, earning themselves the label "Nāṣirī."[^cite-6] Others have deprived him of the rightful inheritance of the Prophet's caliphate (*imāma*), and these are called the "Marājī" (those who defer). And the third are the people of truth, who have accepted you—ʿAlī—as the rightful guardian (*walī*) appointed by the Prophet.
