## A Comparison of Mawlānā ʿAlī and Prophet ʿĪsā (Jesus)

The records of Islamic scholarship disclose profound parallels between ʿĪsā (Jesus), whom Muslims revere as the first *Masīḥ* (Messiah), and Mawlānā ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib, identified in Ismaili thought as the second Messiah. All those attributes which the adherents of Jesus ascribed to their prophet are found reflected in ʿAlī; moreover, the conduct of Jesus's people toward him mirrors, in striking fashion, the behaviour of the Prophet Muḥammad's community toward ʿAlī.[^cite-1] For this reason the Prophet himself drew the comparison directly, declaring that ʿAlī's station was that of Jesus, son of Mary.

The designation "Messiah" (*Masīḥ*) requires elucidation. Some scholars have explained it thus: Jesus was called the Messiah because he was anointed—⟪ar:'کلمۃ اللہ'⟫—with a sacred unction. Some accounts report that the Jordan's waters served as this anointing; others speak of oil. Similarly, ⟪ar:کلمۃ اللہ⟫ ʿAlī (upon him be peace) was anointed in such manner that no trace of *jāhiliyyah* (pre-Islamic ignorance) clung to his soul. Just as the Messiah received prophethood in childhood, so ʿAlī embraced faith in the Prophet while yet young, praying in solitude with him for seven years.[^cite-2] And as the Messiah turned from the world with the words, "I have divorced you thrice," so did the Messenger of Allah address the temporal realm: "O gold and silver, deceive whosoever you will—not me." The Prophet's praise of ʿAlī reached such heights that he declared: "Were I not fearful that people would speak of you as the Christians spoke of Jesus, I would utter words about your merit that would move the listener to place the dust of your feet upon his eyes and to drink the water of your ablutions."[^cite-3]

Thus did Allah (Most High) liken ʿAlī to the Messiah. The Qur'an attests:

> ⟪quran 43:57-58⟫
> ⟪ar:‏ وَلَمَّا ضُرِبَ ٱبْنُ مَرْيَمَ مَثَلًا إِذَا قَوْمُكَ مِنْهُ يَصِدُّونَ ‎ ‏وَقَالُوٓا۟ ءَأَلِهَتُنَا خَيْرٌ أَمْ هُوَ ۚ مَا ضَرَبُوهُ لَكَ إِلَّا جَدَلًۢا ۚ بَلْ هُمْ قَوْمٌ خَصِمُونَ ‎⟫
> *And when the son of Maryam (Mary) is quoted as an example [i.e. ʿĪsā (Jesus) is worshipped like their idols), behold! Your people cry aloud (laugh out at the example). And say: "Are our āliha (gods) better or is he [ʿĪsā (Jesus)]?" They quoted not the above example except for argument. Nay! But they are a quarrelsome people.*

This verse's reference to the Messiah pertains to Hazrat ʿAlī (upon him be peace)—a truth accessible only to those versed in *taʾwīl* (esoteric exegesis) and possessing true intellect. Consider: what enmity could the polytheist Quraysh have harboured toward Jesus, a figure from a distant land separated from them by six centuries, such that they would grow enraged at his mere name and refuse obedience? One loves or despises only those whom one knows; the stranger awakens neither affection nor hatred. The idolaters of Quraysh had no connection to Christianity. The vast temporal gulf between Jesus and them renders enmity senseless. But our argument demonstrates that the Messiah in question is one contemporary with the Prophet's people, one toward whom they direct active hostility. When his name is invoked, they rage and contend with the Messenger. In the second portion of the verse, Allah poses the question: "Are our gods greater, or is he?" The terms ⟪ar:'اللہ'⟫ ("Allah"), ⟪ar:'الہ'⟫ ("ilāh"—god), or ⟪ar:'معبود'⟫ ("maʿbūd"—object of worship) here denote those personages whom the Prophet's people deemed their superiors and guides, whom they followed without scrutiny or discernment as to their fitness.

---

There is recorded an encounter between the Messenger of Allah and a Christian who questioned him regarding this verse. The Christian protested: "O Messenger of Allah! We have never worshipped our scholars. We have neither prostrated before them nor called them our lords. Why, then, this accusation against us?"

> ⟪quran 9:31⟫
> ⟪ar:‏ٱتَّخَذُوٓا۟ أَحْبَارَهُمْ وَرُهْبَنَهُمْ أَرْبَابًۭا مِّن دُونِ ٱللَّهِ وَٱلْمَسِيحَ ٱبْنَ مَرْيَمَ وَمَآ أُمِرُوٓا۟ إِلَّا لِيَعْبُدُوٓا۟ إِلَهًۭا وَحِدًۭا ۖ لَّآ إِلَهَ إِلَّا هُوَ ۚ سُبْحَنَهُۥ عَمَّا يُشْرِكُونَ ‎⟫
> *They (Jews and Christians) took their rabbis and their monks to be their lords besides Allah (by obeying them in things which they made lawful or unlawful according to their own desires without being ordered by Allah), and (they also took as their Lord) Messiah, son of Maryam (Mary), while they (Jews and Christians) were commanded [in the Taurat (Torah) and the Injeel (Gospel)) to worship none but One Ilāh (God—Allah) Lā ilāha illā Huwa (none has the right to be worshipped but He). Praise and glory be to Him, (far above is He) from having the partners they associate (with Him).*

The Prophet replied: "Tell me—when your scholars decreed something lawful or unlawful without foundation in the Qur'an and Sunnah, did you not obey them, embracing their ruling as truth?" The Christian acknowledged: "Yes, we obeyed their every decree." The Prophet then declared: "This *is* worship. Therein lies your idolatry of these learned men in place of Allah."

---

Upon this principle, every faction of the Prophet's ummah (community) disputed with him, saying: ⟪ar-quote:وَقَالُوٓا۟ ءَأَلِھَتُنَا خَيْرٌ أَمْ ھو ؟⟫ "Are our caliphs and the Imams we have exalted superior to ʿAlī?" Yet the Qur'an testifies:

> ⟪quran 43:59⟫
> ⟪ar:‏إِنْ هُوَ إِلَّا عَبْدٌ أَنْعَمْنَا عَلَيْهِ وَجَعَلْنَاهُ مَثَلًۭا لِّبَنِىٓ إِسْرَٓءِيلَ ‎⟫
> *He [ʿĪsā (Jesus)] was not more than a slave. We granted Our Favour to him, and We made him an example to the Children of Israel (i.e. his creation without a father).*

This same quality of blessing appears also in another verse:

> ⟪quran 5:3⟫
> ⟪ar:‏حُرِّمَتْ عَلَيْكُمُ ٱلْمَيْتَةُ وَٱلدَّمُ وَلَحْمُ ٱلْخِنزِيرِ وَمَآ أُهِلَّ لِغَيْرِ ٱللَّهِ بِهِۦ وَٱلْمُنْخَنِقَةُ وَٱلْمَوْقُوذَةُ وَٱلْمُتَرَدِّيَةُ وَٱلنَّطِيحَةُ وَمَآ أَكَلَ ٱلسَّبُعُ إِلَّا مَا ذَكَّيْتُمْ وَمَا ذُبِحَ عَلَى ٱلنُّصُبِ وَأَن تَسْتَقْسِمُوا۟ بِٱلْأَزْلَمِ ۚ ذَلِكُمْ فِسْقٌ ۗ ٱلْيَوْمَ يَئِسَ ٱلَّذِينَ كَفَرُوا۟ مِن دِينِكُمْ فَلَا تَخْشَوْهُمْ وَٱخْشَوْنِ ۚ ٱلْيَوْمَ أَكْمَلْتُ لَكُمْ دِينَكُمْ وَأَتْمَمْتُ عَلَيْكُمْ نِعْمَتِى وَرَضِيتُ لَكُمُ ٱلْإِسْلَمَ دِينًۭا ۚ فَمَنِ ٱضْطُرَّ فِى مَخْمَصَةٍ غَيْرَ مُتَجَانِفٍۢ لِّإِثْمٍۢ ۙ فَإِنَّ ٱللَّهَ غَفُورٌۭ رَّحِيمٌۭ ‎⟫
> *Forbidden to you (for food) are: Al-Maytatah (the dead animals—cattle-beast not slaughtered), blood, the flesh of swine, and the meat of that which has been slaughtered as a sacrifice for others than Allah, or has been slaughtered for idols, etc., or on which Allah's Name has not been mentioned while slaughtering, and that which has been killed by strangling, or by a violent blow, or by a headlong fall, or by the goring of horns—and that which has been (partly) eaten by a wild animal—unless you are able to slaughter it (before its death); and that which is sacrificed (slaughtered) on An-Nusub (stone altars). (Forbidden) also is to use arrows seeking luck or decision, (all) that is Fisqun (disobedience of Allah and sin). This day, those who disbelieved have given up all hope of your religion, so fear them not, but fear Me. This day, I have perfected your religion for you, completed My Favour upon you, and have chosen for you Islam as your religion. But as for him who is forced by severe hunger, with no inclination to sin (such can eat these above-mentioned meats), then surely, Allah is Oft-Forgiving, Most Merciful.*

That is, the first Messiah was but a servant upon whom Allah bestowed His grace; yet the second Messiah is himself a blessing incarnate—his entire being a benediction for the faithful. The verse declares: ⟪ar-quote:وجعلنا مثلا لبنی اسرائیل⟫ "We have made him an example for the Children of Israel." The ancient Israelites were descendants of the prophets, a people each among whom bore the mark of Islam. Just as the descendants of the first Messiah—the Israelites of old—failed to honour the Messiah, so too did the Muslims of the later ages fail to accord proper recognition. When the parallel holds true, as the Christians called their Messiah divine, so did certain Muslims err in calling ʿAlī divine.

A further subtle correspondence exists between the first and the second Messiah: the Christians proclaimed their Messiah the Son of Allah and divine; the Jews branded him a bastard, denying his legitimacy; whilst the Muslims, guided by the teachings of their Prophet, placed him in his rightful station and accepted him as the Messenger of Allah. So too do three perspectives concerning the second Messiah appear. One faction, the Nāṣīriyyah, ascribed divinity to him. Another, the Marjiʿiyyah, deprived him of the caliphate (*khilāfah*) and rightful succession to the Prophet's office. The third—the people of truth—have recognized and accepted you as the rightful guardian, the *Walī* (*wālī*, guardian of souls, guide) whose authority rests upon divine sanction.[^cite-4]

---

[^cite-1]: The Ismaili principle of *taʾwīl* (esoteric interpretation) reads the Qur'anic accounts of past prophets as containing veiled references to the Imāms of the community. This hermeneutic appears in al-Kirmānī's *Rahat al-ʿAql* and al-Sijistānī's *Kashf al-Mahjub*.

[^cite-2]: The early companionship of ʿAlī with the Prophet—including prayer in the *Ka'bah* before the public proclamation—is a recurring theme in Ismaili exegesis. See Qāḍī al-Nuʿmān's *Asas al-Taʾwīl*.

[^cite-3]: This tradition, in which the Prophet restrains his praise of ʿAlī for fear of Christian-like exaggeration, appears in multiple Ismaili sources and is employed to establish the parallel between ʿĪsā and ʿAlī. The concern reflects the theological principle of strict monotheism (*tawḥīd*) central to Ismaili thought.

[^cite-4]: The tripartite division of responses to ʿAlī's authority—divinization, denial of succession, and rightful acceptance—structures much medieval Ismaili apologetics. The term *wālī* (guardian, protector) carries the sense of one invested with divine authority in the Ismaili understanding of spiritual hierarchy.
