## A Comparison of Mawlānā ʿAlī and Prophet ʿĪsā (Jesus)

Profound similarities unite Prophet ʿĪsā (Jesus Christ) and Mawlānā ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib, whom the Ismaili tradition recognizes as the Second Messiah. All the attributes which the followers of ʿĪsā ascribed to their Prophet appear equally in ʿAlī; and the attitude of those who believed in ʿĪsā toward their Prophet parallels almost precisely the stance that the *umma* (community) took toward the Messenger of Allah. For this reason, the Prophet himself established an explicit analogy: ʿAlī's example is that of ʿĪsā, son of Maryam.[^cite-1]

The appellation "Messiah" (*al-Masīḥ*) derives from the practice of anointing. Some scholars hold that ʿĪsā was anointed with water from the Jordan; others maintain he was anointed with oil. In parallel fashion, ʿAlī (peace be upon him) was anointed—touched by divine grace—in such manner that no stain of the pre-Islamic age (*Jāhiliyya*) adhered to him. Just as ʿĪsā attained prophethood while yet a child, so too did ʿAlī embrace the Prophet's teaching in childhood, standing alone with him in prayer for seven years before any other believer. And as the First Messiah renounced the world with the words ⟪ar-quote:طلقتک ثلاثا⟫ ("I have divorced you thrice"), so the Messenger of Allah addressed the realm of material possession, declaring: ⟪ar-quote:طلقتک ثلاثا⟫ ("O gold and silver, I divorce you thrice—deceive none but those other than me.")

The Prophet's eulogy of ʿAlī approaches the threshold of what Christians attributed to ʿĪsā. He declared: "Were I not fearful lest men speak of you as Christians speak of ʿĪsā, I would utter words concerning your merit such that whosoever heard them would take the dust beneath your feet and place it upon his eyes, and would drink the water of your ablution."[^cite-2] In this restraint lies a profound mercy, for the *umma* requires protection against the excess—the *ghuluww* (exaggeration)—into which devotion can descend.

### The Qurʾānic Sign

Allah, Most High, likened ʿAlī to the Messiah through verse and sign. Consider the words:

> ⟪quran 43:57-58⟫
> ⟪ar:‏ وَلَمَّا ضُرِبَ ٱبْنُ مَرْيَمَ مَثَلًا إِذَا قَوْمُكَ مِنْهُ يَصِدُّونَ ‎ ‏وَقَالُوٓا۟ ءَأَلِهَتُنَا خَيْرٌ أَمْ هُوَ ۚ مَا ضَرَبُوهُ لَكَ إِلَّا جَدَلًۢا ۚ بَلْ هُمْ قَوْمٌ خَصِمُونَ ‎⟫
> *And when the son of Maryam (Mary) is quoted as an example [i.e. ʿIesa (Jesus) is worshipped like their idols], behold! Your people cry aloud (laugh out at the example). And say: "Are our āliha (gods) better or is he [ʿIesa (Jesus)]?" They quoted not the above example except for argument. Nay! But they are a quarrelsome people.*

That this verse refers to Hazrat ʿAlī (peace be upon him) becomes clear to the people of *taʾwīl* (esoteric exegesis) and those who possess true understanding (*ʿaql*, the intellect). If the reference were to the historical ʿĪsā, how could one explain the enmity of the Quraysh toward him? Those pagan Arabs had no connection to Christianity; six centuries separated them from ʿĪsā's earthly ministry. One cannot love or hate whom one does not know; enmity requires familiarity. The polytheists of Quraysh had encountered neither ʿĪsā nor his teaching.

The argument becomes luminous only when we recognize that "the Messiah" (*al-Masīḥ*) in this context signifies ʿAlī—one who dwelled among them, whom they encountered daily, and against whom they harbored active hostility. Their fury ignited at the very mention of the Prophet's name; they fought against the Messenger and his closest companion. Here lies the logical coherence: the verse speaks of one contemporary with its addressees, one whom they actively opposed.[^cite-3]

The second portion of the verse—"Are our gods greater, or is he?"—requires careful *taʾwīl*. The term ⟪ar:'الہ'⟫ (*āliha*, gods) and ⟪ar:'معبود'⟫ (*maʿbūd*, objects of worship) refer not to statues of stone but to those whom the Prophet's people revered as leaders and authorities without scrutiny, following them blindly in matters of law and doctrine.

### The Inquiry of a Christian Scholar

A Christian once approached the Messenger of Allah with this very objection:

"O Messenger of Allah! We have never worshipped our *ʿulamāʾ* (scholars). We did not bow before them, nor did we call them our Lords. Then why do you accuse us and our ancestors in this manner?"

The Prophet responded with a question that pierced to the heart: "Did you not obey their commands when they declared something permissible or forbidden—commands backed by neither Qurʾān nor *Sunna*—and you accepted them without hesitation?"

The Christian acknowledged: "Yes, we obeyed their every decree."

The Prophet replied: "That obedience—that acceptance of their authority in matters of *dīn* (religion)—is itself *ʿibāda* (worship). This is what you do when you grant these learned men dominion over matters that belong to Allah alone."[^cite-4]

> ⟪quran 9:31⟫
> ⟪ar:‏ٱتَّخَذُوٓا۟ أَحْبَارَهُمْ وَرُهْبَنَهُمْ أَرْبَابًۭا مِّن دُونِ ٱللَّهِ وَٱلْمَسِيحَ ٱبْنَ مَرْيَمَ وَمَآ أُمِرُوٓا۟ إِلَّا لِيَعْبُدُوٓا۟ إِلَهًۭا وَحِدًۭا ۖ لَّآ إِلَهَ إِلَّا هُوَ ۚ سُبْحَنَهُۥ عَمَّا يُشْرِكُونَ ‎⟫
> *They (Jews and Christians) took their rabbis and their monks to be their lords besides Allah (by obeying them in things which they made lawful or unlawful according to their own desires without being ordered by Allah), and (they also took as their Lord) Messiah, son of Maryam (Mary), while they (Jews and Christians) were commanded to worship none but One Ilāh (God—Allah), Lā ilāha illā Huwa (none has the right to be worshipped but He). Praise and glory be to Him, (far above is He) from having the partners they associate (with Him).*

Upon this principle rests the whole argument. Every *umma* (community) of the Prophet opposed him, saying: ⟪ar-quote:وَقَالُوٓا۟ ءَأَلِھَتُنَا خَيْرٌ أَمْ ھو ؟⟫ ("Are our leaders and imams, who have guided us, superior to ʿAlī?")

> ⟪quran 43:59⟫
> ⟪ar:‏إِنْ هُوَ إِلَّا عَبْدٌ أَنْعَمْنَا عَلَيْهِ وَجَعَلْنَاهُ مَثَلًۭا لِّبَنِىٓ إِسْرَٓءِيلَ ‎⟫
> *He [ʿIesa (Jesus)] was not more than a slave. We granted Our Favour to him, and We made him an example to the Children of Israel (i.e. his creation without a father).*

### The Grace of the Two Messiahs

A further subtlety illuminates the nature of the blessing (*niʿma*) conferred upon both Messiahs:

> ⟪quran 5:3⟫
> ⟪ar:‏ٱلْيَوْمَ أَكْمَلْتُ لَكُمْ دِينَكُمْ وَأَتْمَمْتُ عَلَيْكُمْ نِعْمَتِى وَرَضِيتُ لَكُمُ ٱلْإِسْلَمَ دِينًۭا ‎⟫
> *This day, I have perfected your religion for you, completed My Favour upon you, and have chosen for you Islam as your religion.*

The First Messiah was merely a servant (*ʿabd*) upon whom Allah bestowed His blessing; but the Second Messiah *is himself* the blessing—his entire being constitutes a *niʿma* for the *umma*, a grace through which the people of faith are elevated. The verse continues: ⟪ar-quote:وجعلنا مثلا لبنی اسرائیل⟫ ("We made him an example for the Children of Israel"). The ancient Israelites were the offspring of the prophets of Israel—every word, every deed among them carried prophetic weight. Yet they did not honor the First Messiah; the Muslims of the Second Messiah, receiving him from their own Prophet's teaching, recognized him aright. When the parallelism is properly drawn, the comparison becomes exact: just as some Christians deified the First Messiah, calling him divine, so too did certain groups among later Muslims attempt to elevate ʿAlī beyond his station.[^cite-5]

### The Three Positions

Another elegant symmetry appears in the threefold responses to each Messiah. The Christians declared ʿĪsā the Son of Allah and divine. The Jews maligned him as *walad al-zinā* (the son of adultery)—a charge of bastardy and deception. Yet the Muslims, guided by the clear teaching of their Prophet, placed ʿĪsā in his rightful station: *Rasūl Allāh* (the Messenger of Allah), honored and without peer among men, yet entirely human, entirely a *ʿabd* (servant) of the Most High.

The Second Messiah—Mawlānā ʿAlī—has likewise encountered three positions among those who came after the Prophet. One group, the *Nāṣiriyya* (the Nasiri faction), ascribed divinity to him, erring into *ghuluww*. Another, whom we call the *Marjiyyūn* (the Postponers), denied him the rightful inheritance of the *khilāfa* (Caliphate) and the *imāma* (spiritual leadership) that the Prophet explicitly confirmed. The third—the people of truth and wisdom—accept ʿAlī in his true station: as the rightful *Walī* (Guardian) and *Imām* (Leader) of the *umma*, chosen by Allah and designated by His Messenger, a human being perfected by divine grace, not deified, but revered as the one through whom the guidance of Allah continues to flow.
