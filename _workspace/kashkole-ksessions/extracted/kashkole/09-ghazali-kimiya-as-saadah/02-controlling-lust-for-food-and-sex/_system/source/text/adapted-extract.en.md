# Controlling Lust For Food and Sex

## The Virtue and Spiritual Reward of Hunger (Faḍīlat al-Jūʿ)

Know that the belly is the tank of the body, and the vessels proceeding from it to the seven members are like channels of irrigation. The source of all appetites is the belly. This appetite stands as the most common affliction befalling the descendants of Adam—for Adam himself was cast out of heaven because of appetite. This appetite is the origin of all other appetites: a full belly awakens the appetite for sexual intercourse. The **appetites of the belly** and the genitals cannot be addressed without **wealth**; hence arises the greed for riches. Wealth cannot be obtained except through **rank and retinue** (a body of retainers in attendance upon an important personage), so the greed for rank emerges. And wealth and rank cannot be preserved except by antagonizing people. From these conditions spring forth **envy, bigotry, enmity, pride, dissimulation, hatred**, and **dispute**—a cascading chain of vices rooted in the first appetite.

The Messenger (may God bless him and grant him peace) said: "Strive against your selves (⟪ar:نفوس⟫) with hunger and thirst, for its spiritual reward is similar to that of striving in battle against the unbelievers. There is no behavior more pleasing to Allah Most High than hunger and thirst."[^cite-1]

And he said: "Whoever fills his stomach will not be admitted into the heavenly kingdom."[^cite-2]

When the Messenger was asked, "Who is the most superior of all people?" he replied: "He who eats little, laughs little, and is content with breeches."[^cite-3]

And he said: "The lord and the leader of all acts is hunger."[^cite-4]

And he said: "Wear clothing. Eat and drink until the belly is half-full, for that is a portion of prophethood."[^cite-5]

And he said: "Reflection is one half of worship; eating sparingly is the other half of it."

And he said: "The most virtuous of you to Allah Most High is he whose meditation and hunger are lengthy. The most unfavorable of you to Allah Most High is he who eats much, drinks much, and sleeps much."

And he said: "Allah Most High boasts to the angels of the person who eats little, saying: 'Look! I have afflicted him with the appetite for food and he withholds from it for My sake. Bear witness, O angels! For every bite that he has passed up, I shall give him a rank in heaven.'"[^cite-6]

And he said: "Do not kill your souls with large quantities of food and drink, for the soul is like a sowing ground; if it is over-watered, the plants will die."[^cite-7]

And he said: "A man fills no container worse than his stomach. A few bites will suffice for a person to keep his back straight. If there is no remedy, then one third of the stomach is for food, one third for drink, and one third for breath."[^cite-8] According to another version: "One third for remembrance."

Jesus (upon him be peace) said: "Keep yourselves hungry and naked so that it enables your souls to see Allah."[^cite-9]

Our Messenger said: "Satan flows inside the body of a person like blood in the veins. Make his passage difficult with hunger."[^cite-10]

And he said: "The believer eats with one intestine; the hypocrite with seven." This means that the carnal appetite and eating of the hypocrite is seven times that of a believer.[^cite-11]

ʿAʾisha said that the Messenger said: "Continually knock on the door of heaven until your door is opened." She asked: "O Messenger of God! With what shall I knock?" He said: "Hunger and thirst."[^cite-12]

Abu Juhayfah belched in the presence of the Messenger. He said: "Keep your belch at a distance, for whoever is more satisfied in this world will be more hungry in the next."[^cite-13]

ʿAʾisha said: "The Messenger never sated himself with food. I used to feel compassion for him because of his hunger. I would lay my hand on his stomach and say: 'May my body be sacrificed for you! What will happen if you eat enough of this world to not be hungry?' He said: 'O ʿAʾisha! The prophets of highest resolve, my brethren who have preceded me, have obtained grace from Allah Most High. I fear that if I enjoy comfort and ease, my rank will be less than theirs. I prefer being patient with little for a few days, instead of having my delight lessen in the Hereafter. Nothing is dearer to me than reaching my brethren.'" ʿAʾisha says: "By God and His Messenger! He did not live more than a week after saying that."[^cite-14]

Fāṭima (upon her be peace), holding a piece of bread in her hand, went to the Messenger. He asked: "What is that?" She replied: "A loaf of bread that I baked, which I could not eat without you." He said: "This is the first food that will enter your father's mouth in three days." Abu Hurairah (may God be pleased with him) says: "The Household (Ahl al-Bayt) never ate bread made of wheat for three days in a row during the time of the Messenger."[^cite-15]

Abu Sulaymān al-Dāranī (may God be pleased with him) says: "I prefer to eat one morsel less of the evening meal than to perform formal prayer throughout the night until morning."[^cite-16]

Fudayl (may God be pleased with him) used to say to himself: "What are you afraid of? Are you afraid that you will remain hungry? What an idea—that Allah Most High would give hunger to Muḥammad and his Companions, and withhold it from you and the likes of you!"[^cite-17]

Kahmās (may God be pleased with him) prayed: "O Lord! You keep me hungry! You keep me in Your company in long nights of seclusion. How have I achieved this station with You? For You did not do this with anyone but Your friends."[^cite-18]

Mālik al-Dīnār said: "Blessed is the person who possesses enough grain to suffice his needs and be independent of people."[^cite-19]

Muḥammad al-Wāsī (may God be pleased with him) said: "Blessed is the person who is hungry in the morning and hungry in the evening and with whom Allah Most High is pleased."[^cite-20]

Sahl al-Tustarī said: "The saints and the wise have searched and found nothing more beneficial in religion and the world than hunger, and have found nothing more harmful to the Hereafter than satiety."[^cite-21]

ʿAbd al-Wāḥid Zayd said: "Allah Most High has not befriended any person except through hunger. No one has walked upon water except through hunger. No one has rolled up the earth so as to travel a hundred *parasangs* (an ancient Persian unit of distance, equal to approximately 3.5 miles) in a night except through hunger."[^cite-22]

In the traditions, it is related that Moses (upon him be peace), during those forty days when Allah Most High wished to talk with him, ate nothing.[^cite-23]

It is the custom of those who have trod the path of the Hereafter to refrain from food. They have opposed the self (*nafs*, the ego) in its every craving, for when the self satisfies its appetite, pride, heedlessness, and darkness appear. Being in the world becomes preferred, and death becomes an enemy. One must restrict oneself in the world to the point that the world becomes its prison and death its liberation. No one for whom all the means of comfort have been provided and whose every whim has been facilitated has been highly regarded.

The Messenger said: "The worst of my people are those whose bodies have been planted upon the comforts of this world. All their aspirations are for different kinds of food and different kinds of clothing. Then they declaim long and lavish speeches."[^cite-24]

A revelation came to Moses: "O Moses, know that your residence is the grave. You must restrain your body from too many carnal appetites."[^cite-25]

When Ibn ʿUmar (may God be pleased with him) was ill, he expressed a desire for grilled fish. His slave Nāfiʿ said: "I could not find a single fish in all of Madīna. After a great deal of effort, I finally found and bought one for one and a half *dirhams*. I grilled it and brought it to him. A poor man came to the door of the residence, and Ibn ʿUmar said: 'Take this and give it to him.' I said: 'But this is what you desired, and I have obtained it with great effort. Let me give him its price instead.' He said: 'No, give the fish to him.' I gave it to the poor man, then followed him and bought it back from him. I returned with it to Ibn ʿUmar again and said: 'I gave the poor man the price for the fish.' He said: 'Go back and give him the fish and let him keep the money you gave him as well. I have heard the Messenger say: "Whoever desires something, buys it, and then abstains from it for the sake of Allah Most High will be forgiven."'"[^cite-26]

ʿUtba al-Ghulām (may God be pleased with him) would place dough in the sun until it was dry before he ate it. He would not bake it in order to abstain from its good taste. He would then take water out from the sun and drink it warm.[^cite-27]

Mālik al-Dīnār (may God be pleased with him) desired milk for forty years but never drank it. Someone brought fresh dates to him. He turned them over in his hands for a long time and then said to his companions: "You eat them, for I have not eaten any for forty years."[^cite-28]

Aḥmad ibn Abī al-Hawārī (may God be pleased with him) was the disciple of Abū Sulaymān al-Dāranī. Aḥmad said: "My master used to wish for hot bread so he could eat it with salt. I brought it. He took one bite, then put it aside and began to weep. He said: 'O Lord Allah, You have placed my desire before me. Will You punish me? I have repented; forgive me.'"[^cite-29]

Mālik ibn Daiġham (may God be pleased with him) said: "I was walking through the market of Basra. I saw very fine leeks. The appetite for them leaped inside me. I swore an oath not to eat them until that time when I reach Allah."[^cite-30]

Mālik al-Dīnār said: "It has been fifty years since I have divorced myself from the world in the desire for a single draught of milk. I have not drunk it and I shall not drink it until that time when I reach God."[^cite-31]

Hāmmād (may God be pleased with him) says: "I arrived at the door of Dāwud al-Ṭāʾī and heard his voice. He was saying: 'Once you desired a carrot and I gave it to you. Now you want dates? You will never get them and never eat them!' When I entered I did not see anyone with him, and I realized that he had been talking to his own body."[^cite-32]

## The Benefits of Hunger and the Afflictions of Satiety

Know that the merit of hunger does not lie in anguish, just as the merit of medicine does not lie in its bitterness. However, in hunger there are ten benefits.

The first benefit is that hunger purifies and illuminates the soul, while satiety blinds it and makes it dull. A vapor rises from the gut to the brain, which stupefies people and confuses their thoughts. Concerning this, the Messenger said: "Enliven your souls by laughing little; cleanse them with hunger until they become finely pure."[^cite-33] And he said: "Whoever stays hungry becomes astute and his thoughts become great."[^cite-34] Shibli (may God be pleased with him) said: "There was never a day when I sat hungry for the sake of Allah that I did not receive a new wisdom from Him in my soul."[^cite-35] The Messenger said: "Do not fill yourselves with food, for it will extinguish the light of spiritual knowledge (ʿilm, knowledge of divine matters) in your souls."[^cite-36] Spiritual knowledge is the way to Paradise, and hunger is the threshold of that spiritual knowledge. Therefore remaining hungry is knocking on the door of heaven, as he said: "Continue to knock at the door of Paradise with hunger until it opens."[^cite-37]

The second benefit is that the soul becomes refined and finds pleasure in the remembrance and conversations with Allah. When harshness and hardness of the soul arise from satiety, every remembrance made is done only with the tongue and does not enter the soul. Junayd (may God be pleased with him) says: "Each one of you has placed a food bag crammed with provisions between yourself and Allah Most High. After that you desire to find the pleasure of conversation with Allah? This will never happen!"[^cite-38]

The third benefit is that while arrogance and negligence are the gates of hell, humility, helplessness, and weakness are the thresholds of heaven. Satiety brings arrogance and heedlessness, while hunger brings weakness and humility. Until the servant chooses to look at himself with the eye of weakness—by missing one bite—the world becomes straitened and dark for him. He will not see the majesty and power of Allah. It was for this reason that when the keys to the treasures of this world were offered to the Messenger and he said: "I do not want it! I will replace it with a day of satiety and a day of hunger (fast). When I become hungry, I will be patient and when I am sated, I will give thanks."[^cite-39] (A reference to the Fasting of David, *Saum-i Dāwud*)

The fourth benefit is that when one is sated, one forgets the hungry, feels no compassion for Allah's creation, and forgets the punishment of the Hereafter. But when one is hungry, one recalls the people of hell. When one is thirsty, one recalls the thirst of the people on the day of Resurrection. Fear of the Hereafter and compassion for mankind are among the thresholds of heaven. It was for this reason that it was said to Joseph (upon him be peace): "You possess the treasures of this world. Then why do you remain hungry?" He said: "I fear that if I eat my fill, I shall forget the hunger of the poor."[^cite-40]

The fifth benefit—which stands at the head of all benefits and spiritual happinesses—is that hunger helps keep one's self (⟪ar:نفس⟫) under one's own control. Wretched is he who is under the control of his self. Just as a stubborn animal cannot be tamed and softened except through hunger, the same applies to the human self. This is not merely a single benefit but an alchemy of benefits. For **all sins arise from the carnal appetites and all carnal appetites arise from satiety**.[^cite-41] Dhū al-Nūn (may God be pleased with him) says: "I never ate my fill. Because of that, I have not sinned nor have I intended to sin."[^cite-42] ʿAʾisha says: "The first innovation (⟪ar:بدعۃ⟫) that appeared after the Messenger was sating oneself. Because the people eat their fill, their souls became disobedient."[^cite-43] If there were no benefits of hunger except that the appetite of the genitals is weakened and the appetite for idle talk departs, that in itself would be sufficient! For everyone who eats to satiety becomes engaged in gossip and slander and is overpowered by the cravings of the genitals. When one controls the genitalia, one controls the eye; when one controls the eye, one controls the speculations of the soul. Hunger suffices for all of these. It is for this reason that it was said: "Hunger is a jewel in the treasures of Allah Most High. He gives it to those who love Him, but He does not give it to everyone."[^cite-44] One of the learned has said: "Allah Most High removes the thought of women from the soul of every disciple who eats dry bread for a year and eats half of that amount to which he is accustomed."[^cite-45]

The sixth benefit is sleeping less—a foundation of all acts of worship, conversations with Allah, remembrance, and reflections upon Him, especially during the night. Anyone who eats to satiety is overcome by sleep and falls like a corpse; his life is wasted. Every night a spiritual guide used to announce at the dining table: "O disciples! Do not eat much of your bread, for then you will drink too much water, and then you will sleep too much; then you will experience much grief at the Resurrection."[^cite-46] Seventy truly righteous people are in agreement that over-sleeping comes from drinking too much water. Since the capital of a human being is his life, and every breath is a substance by which he may meet the happiness of the Hereafter, excessive sleep is injurious to life and wastes it. What could be more precious than the fending off of sleep? Whoever spends the night in worship and prayer while sated does not experience the pleasure of conversation with Allah—sleep overcomes him and pollutes his night worship. So many troubles are born from overeating. Abū Sulaymān al-Dāranī says: "Nocturnal pollution is in itself a punishment caused by satiety."[^cite-47]

The seventh benefit is that time becomes ample for the hungry, and he may attend to work and knowledge. When one eats a lot, he will spend most of his time cooking, buying, preparing, waiting for the means, and then finally sleeping. He will also spend time going to the restrooms for his bowel movements. **Every breath is a jewel and it is the capital of the human being. Wasting it unnecessarily is foolishness.**[^cite-48] Sarī al-Saqaṭī said: "I saw ʿAlī al-Gurgānī throwing barley meal into his mouth. I asked: 'Do you not eat bread?' He said: 'Between this and my eating bread there is a difference of seventy glorifications of Allah, and I do not waste time. For this reason, I have not eaten bread for forty years, so that I would not have to chew it and not lose this benefit.'"[^cite-49] There is no doubt that fasting is easier for whoever is accustomed to hunger. He can stay in retreat in the mosque and always remain in a state of purification. Such benefits are not dismissed lightly by those engaged in the business of the Hereafter.

Abū Sulaymān al-Dāranī says: "Six things occur to whoever eats his fill:

1. He does not obtain the sweetness of worship

2. His desire and memory for learning wisdom weakens

3. He is deprived of compassion for others, for he assumes that everyone else is as sated as he is

4. Worship becomes troublesome for him

5. His carnal appetites are increased

6. While believers are going around the mosques, he is going around privies and dung heaps."[^cite-50]

The eighth benefit is that whoever eats sparingly is always healthy and is delivered from the torment of illness, the provision of medicine, the need of doctors, and the taste of bitter medicine. The learned and the physicians are in agreement that **there is nothing in this world that contains only benefits and not a single harm except in eating sparingly**.[^cite-51] One of the wise has said: "The best thing which a person eats is the pomegranate, and the worse is jerked meat; but eating a little jerky is better than eating too many pomegranates." In the Traditions, it is related: "Fast, so that you remain healthy."[^cite-52]

The ninth benefit is that for whoever eats sparingly, there is little monetary expense, and he does not need much wealth. All calamities, sins, and distractions of the soul arise from the need for wealth. If one must eat a great quantity of something delicious on a daily basis, one will go through too much trouble to obtain it every day, leading one to fall into the doubtful and cravings for the forbidden. A sage says: "I arrange for most of my needs by abandoning them. That is easier for me." Another says: "When I needed something, I would borrow from my stomach and abandon the desire for that thing." **Ibrāhīm al-Adham was once asked the price of something in a brother's possession. He said: 'It is expensive!' To which Ibrāhīm replied: 'Make it cheap by abandoning it.'"**[^cite-53]

The tenth benefit is that by mastering one's appetite for food, one masters the art of voluntary charity, giving in abundance, and generosity. For **the destination of whatever enters the stomach is foul, while the destination of whatever is given in charity is the pure treasures of Allah Most High**.[^cite-54] The Messenger looked at someone who had a fat belly and, pointing to it, said: "If this was not where you have put it, and had you put it some place else, it would have been better for you"—that is, in voluntary charity for the sake of Allah Most High.[^cite-55]
