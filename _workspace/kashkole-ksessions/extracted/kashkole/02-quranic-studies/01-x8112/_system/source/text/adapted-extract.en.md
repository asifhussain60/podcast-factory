## Definitions

⟪ar-quote:الہ⟫

The word *ilāh* is a generic term closest to the English "God," yet far richer in connotation. It denotes:

- An entity worthy of worship and devotion
- A deity; the object of veneration
- That to which the heart turns in desperate times
- One towards whom passion is directed with such intensity that thought cannot be diverted
- Literally, something upon which one leans—when exhausted, a person leans upon an object, and that object itself is termed an instrument of support

The word *love* (maḥabba) is already embedded within *ilāh*, inseparable from its meaning. To name God is to acknowledge the love that binds the servant to the Divine.

O God! You are worthiest of remembrance and of worship. You are the mightiest helper of those who seek You, most merciful of rulers, most generous of those from whom gifts are requested, and most abundant of those who give. You are the Sovereign without partner, the Unique without equal. All things perish save Your Face. None can be obeyed except by Your permission; none can disobey save by Your knowledge. When obeyed, You give thanks; when disobeyed, You forgive. You are the nearest witness and the closest guardian. You have stood between souls and their desires; You have seized the forelock of all that exists; You have inscribed the traces and annulled the ages. Humble his station before Your throne. O God! You are the Pardoner, the Noble. You love pardon; so pardon Your servant.

## Description of Jahannam in the Quran

The Quran abounds in descriptions of those who are punished, of Jahannam itself, and of the corrupt whose deeds are sealed and rendered void on the Day of Judgment. Witness those who stand in bewilderment when the decree is opened before them. The Quran speaks of Muslims, as recounted in Sūrat al-Ḥadīd, who stand in shock—for they walked in the company of believers, journeying together toward the Garden, when suddenly a wall falls between them and Paradise.

⟪ar-quote:يُنَادُونَهُمْ أَلَمْ نَكُن مَّعَكُمْ⟫

"Did we not walk with you?" they call out. "Were we not together?"

⟪ar-quote:قَالُوا۟ بَلَىٰ وَلَكِنَّكُمْ فَتَنتُمْ أَنفُسَكُمْ وَتَرَبَّصْتُمْ وَٱرْتَبْتُمْ وَغَرَّتْكُمُ ٱلْأَمَانِىُّ حَتَّىٰ جَآءَ أَمْرُ ٱللَّهِ وَغَرَّكُم بِٱللَّهِ ٱلْغَرُورُ⟫

"Indeed you were with us," come the words from the other side, "but you led your souls into tribulation. You hesitated and procrastinated. You doubted. Vain hopes deluded you until the command of God arrived, and the Great Deceiver deceived you." This is an ayah of piercing terror. Those on the far side of the wall, having walked toward the Garden, speak plainly to those who remain behind it: you engaged yourselves in fitna (trial and discord); you did not hold fast to religion with seriousness. You said, "I will change. I will cease this forbidden thing. I will pray with regularity—but after summer ends. After my children are grown and married. After I leave the alcohol trade, whatever my particular sin." Always, always postponement. Always, tomorrow.

Those standing in clarity say to you: we prayed together; we lived in the same neighborhood; we attended the same congregation; we greeted one another with *as-salāmu ʿalaykum*. But you threw yourselves into fitna—into doubt and trial.

⟪ar-quote:وَٱرْتَبْتُمْ⟫

You harbored doubt in My signs. Perhaps you made jokes about religion, spoke irreverently, treated the Divine threats as nothing more than words. A wall? You believed you could climb it. This casual dismissal, this refusal to take seriously the warnings of God—this too is part of your downfall.

⟪ar-quote:وَغَرَّتْكُمُ ٱلْأَمَانِىُّ⟫

Your false hopes, your self-deceptions, deluded you. You believed that despite everything—despite your delays, your compromises, your half-measures—a few tears shed on the prayer mat, a few supplications whispered, would be sufficient. That you would somehow simply enter Paradise. As God asks:

⟪ar-quote:أَمْ حَسِبْتُمْ أَن تَدْخُلُوا۟ ٱلْجَنَّةَ⟫

"Did you imagine you would simply enter the Garden?" You were made drunk with delusion.

⟪ar-quote:حَتَّىٰ جَآءَ أَمْرُ ٱللَّهِ⟫

Until, at last, the decree of God came.

⟪ar-quote:وَغَرَّكُم بِٱللَّهِ ٱلْغَرُورُ⟫

And he who is most skilled at deception—the Master of Delusion—worked his will upon you completely. You were fully confident that Paradise was your birthright. These verses exist in the Quran, and they are profoundly sobering.

Yet herein lies a great danger: if one reads and focuses solely upon such passages, despair becomes all-consuming. One begins to think, "Nothing I do is sufficient. I do not know if I stand on the saved side of the wall or the damned. I do not know if my forgiveness is real or an illusion." How, then, is one to know? The answer lies in walking the path between fear and hope, understanding that the warning speaks to the heart that *can* change, that *can* choose sincerity now, before the wall descends.

## Verses to Add to Sūrat al-Fātiḥa

The Prophet was with ʿAlī when he said:

⟪ar-quote:ستكون فتن كقطع الليل المظلم⟫

"There shall come tribulations, like fragments of the darkest night."

⟪ar-quote:تُرَطِّقُ بعضُہُ بَعضَا⟫

"One shall make the other seem light by comparison." ʿAlī then asked:

⟪ar-quote:وما الخلاصُ یوم اذن یا رسول اللہ و ما مَخرجُ یوم اذن یا رسول اللہ⟫

"What is the way of escape on that day, O Messenger of God? What is the path of deliverance?" The Prophet replied, "The Book of God":

⟪ar-quote:فیہ نبأُ ما قبلکم و خبر ما بعدکم و الحکم ما بینکم⟫

"In it is the account of those who came before you, and knowledge of those who come after, and the judgment that shall fall between you."[^cite-9]

The Quran contains within itself the very patterns by which God orders creation. The *sunan Allāh* (customs of God) do not change; what transpired in the past recurs in the present under different names and faces. Every story in the Quran serves not merely as historical record but as ʿibra—a warning and a teaching. The Quran is not a history book, despite the histories it contains. Its purpose is not to inform us of what *happened*, but to teach us the unchanging conditions (aḥwāl) and circumstances under which human beings live. Situations alter only in their labels, places, and persons; their underlying character remains eternal. Oppression (ẓulm) is oppression in whatever language it is spoken, whatever land it occurs in. Pharaoh is both a historical figure *and* an archetypal pattern of tyrannical behavior. Thus the Quran lays bare the patterns that govern creation.

God has given us this Book, and within this Book He has given us another Book—Sūrat al-Fātiḥa itself, which is ⟪ar:سبع المثانی⟫ (the Seven Oft-Repeated Verses). This Sūra is a summation of the entire Quran compressed into seven verses. It stands as one of the mightiest testimonies to the Qur'anic miracle (*muʿjiza*). The Quran is a Book that rendered all creation incapable of producing its like. The scholars of rhetoric and eloquence (balāgha) are unanimous: the incapacitating element of the Quran—its overwhelming inimitability—lies in the fact that no human being possesses the capacity to bring forth anything resembling it, neither in its linguistic form nor in its meanings alone, and far less in the two together. Sūrat al-Fātiḥa embodies this miracle in distilled form.[^cite-10]

I am the master of him who struck with two swords and thrust with two spears; who prayed toward both *qiblas* and took the oath of allegiance twice; who made two emigrations and whose faith never faltered, not even for the blink of an eye.

I am the master of the righteous among the believers, the heir of the Prophets, the finest of the executors, the greatest of the Muslims, the standard-bearer of the faithful, the light of the striving, the foremost of those who weep, the ornament of the worshippers, the lamp of the past, the illumination of the standing, the most excellent of the devout, the tongue of the Messenger of the Lord of the Worlds, the first of the executors from the House of Yasin, reinforced by Gabriel the Trusted, supported by Michael the Strong, praised by all the People of Heaven, the defender of the sanctuary of the Muslims, the warrior against his enemies the rebellious, the extinguisher of the fires of those who kindle strife, the most excellent among all of Quraysh, the foremost in struggle and conquest—*Amīr al-Muʾminīn*, the Successor of his Prophet among the worlds, the Trust upon creation, the Khalīfa of Him who was sent to all peoples, the Chief of the Muslims and the Forerunners, the slayer of the oath-breakers and the unjust, the destroyer of the polytheists, the arrow aimed by God at the hypocrites.

When Qunbar, the servant of ʿAlī, was asked, "Whose master are you?" he replied: "I am the servant of him who struck with two swords and thrust with two spears; who prayed toward both *qiblas* and took the oath of allegiance twice; who made two emigrations and whose faith never faltered, not even for the blink of an eye. I am the servant of the righteous among the believers, the heir of the Prophets, the finest of the executors, the greatest of the Muslims, the standard-bearer of the faithful, the light of the striving, the foremost of those who weep, the ornament of the worshippers, the lamp of the past, the illumination of the standing, the most excellent of the devout, the tongue of the Messenger of the Lord of the Worlds, the first of the executors from the House of Yasin, reinforced by Gabriel the Trusted, supported by Michael the Strong, praised by all the People of Heaven, the defender of the sanctuary of the Muslims, the warrior against his enemies the rebellious, the extinguisher of the fires of those who kindle strife, the most excellent among all of Quraysh, the foremost in struggle and conquest—the Commander of the Faithful, the Successor of his Prophet among the worlds, the Trust upon all that He created, the Khalīfa of Him whose message reached all peoples, the Chief of the Muslims and the Forerunners, the slayer of the oath-breakers and the unjust, the destroyer of the polytheists, the arrow aimed by God at the hypocrites, the tongue of the Word of God and its helper upon His earth, the repository of His knowledge and the refuge of His religion. Leader of the righteous, approved by the Most High, the Mighty—clement, generous, noble, forbearing, wise, pure, of noble descent, eminent among warriors, patient, one who fasts, rightly guided, resolute in action, the severer of backs, the disperser of bands, the exalted in rank. The firmest in binding the reins, the most steadfast in character, the most rigid in strength; fierce, mighty, the lion of lions, the determined, the resolute, the shrewd, the eloquent, the arguable, noble in origin, distinguished in conclusion, the best of his tribe, pure of his lineage, verified in his sanctity, the fulfiller of the trust from the House of Hashim and the cousin of the Prophet, the Guided Imam, free from corruption, the lion of lions, the hero of heroes..."[^cite-12]

These titles and descriptions, preserved in the tradition and upon the lips of those closest to him, speak to the station (*maqām*) of ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib—a figure of supreme spiritual and moral standing whose devotion, courage, and unwavering adherence to the Faith exemplified the highest form of human servitude to God.
