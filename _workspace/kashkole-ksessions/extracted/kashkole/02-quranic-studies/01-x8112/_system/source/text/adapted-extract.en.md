## Definitions

⟪ar-quote:الہ⟫

The term *ilāh* (that which is worshipped) is extraordinarily rich, laden with depths of meaning. It signifies:

An entity worthy of worship; a deity; an object of devotion and love; one to whom we turn in moments of desperation; one in whose remembrance we lose ourselves through passionate attachment; literally, something upon which we lean when exhausted—the very instrument of our support.

Love itself is embedded within the word *ilāh*. To speak of God as *Ilāh* is already to speak of the relationship of love that draws the soul toward its Sustainer. The word does not merely denote a distant object of belief but rather the living reality of dependence, longing, and intimate orientation toward the Divine.

This is not pessimism but realism—a corrective to the delusion that acquisition and construction can purchase permanence.

## The Description of Hell in the Qur'ān

The Qur'ān overflows with descriptions of those who are punished, of Jahannam (hell) itself, and of those whose corruption renders their deeds void—people whose works are erased on the Day of Judgment, who shall stand shocked and bewildered. Among them are Muslims mentioned in Sūrat al-Ḥadīd who, having walked alongside believers toward Paradise, suddenly find a wall erected before them.

⟪ar-quote:يُنَادُونَهُمْ أَلَمْ نَكُن مَّعَكُمْ⟫

They cry out: "Were we not with you?" We walked the same path. We were supposed to be together.

⟪ar-quote:قَالُوا۟ بَلَىٰ وَلَكِنَّكُمْ فَتَنتُمْ أَنفُسَكُمْ وَتَرَبَّصْتُمْ وَٱرْتَبْتُمْ وَغَرَّتْكُمُ ٱلْأَمَانِىُّ حَتَّىٰ جَآءَ أَمْرُ ٱللَّهِ وَغَرَّكُم بِٱللَّهِ ٱلْغَرُورُ⟫

Those on the blessed side respond: "Yes, you were. But you led yourselves into fitna (trial and corruption). You procrastinated. You doubted the promise of God. False hopes beguiled you, until the command of God arrived. And the Great Deceiver—pride itself—deceived you exceedingly well."[^cite-7]

This is indeed a terrifying āyah. Consider those who delayed repentance: "I will change after summer ends. I will stop the forbidden business once my children marry. I will pray regularly once I have more time. I will cease the haram practice after circumstances improve." Year upon year, they postponed the turning. They said they would stop, yet procrastination became their default. They made light of these divine warnings, even joking about religion, unbent by fear of consequences.

⟪ar-quote:وَٱرْتَبْتُمْ⟫

And you harbored doubt—not whether God exists, but whether His word applies to you. Surely such a wall will not fall before me. If it does, I can climb it. This attitude of dismissiveness, this refusal to take the threats seriously—it is the fatal sin.

⟪ar-quote:وَغَرَّتْكُمُ ٱلْأَمَانِىُّ⟫

False hopes (al-amanī) deluded you. What does this mean? That after all your neglect and violation, you imagined you could simply cry on the prayer mat, make a few supplications, and enter Paradise. You reckoned entrance to the Garden would be granted freely, as though you had done nothing wrong.

⟪ar-quote:أَمْ حَسِبْتُمْ أَن تَدْخُلُوا۟ ٱلْجَنَّةَ⟫

Did you reckon you would enter Paradise merely by assumption?

⟪ar-quote:حَتَّىٰ جَآءَ أَمْرُ ٱللَّهِ⟫

Until the final decision of God arrived.

⟪ar-quote:وَغَرَّكُم بِٱللَّهِ ٱلْغَرُورُ⟫

And the Master Deceiver—the nafs (self) in its deceptive aspect—utterly beguiled you. You had grown completely convinced that Paradise was made for you, despite everything. The Qur'ān contains these āyāt to pierce complacency; they are profoundly disquieting.

Yet there is wisdom in this fear. If one merely reads and focuses on such passages without balance, despair settles in: *Nothing I do is ever good enough. I do not know which side of the wall I shall find myself on. I cannot distinguish genuine forgiveness from self-deception.* How then does one proceed?

The answer lies in understanding that fear and hope work together in the believer's heart. The āyāt of punishment exist not to crush the soul into paralysis but to awaken it from negligence. They demand seriousness; they demand that we examine ourselves before God examines us on that terrible Day.

## Passages to Add to the Fātiḥah

The Prophet, peace be upon him, was with ʿAlī and said to him:

⟪ar-quote:ستكون فتن كقطع الليل المظلم⟫

There shall come tribulations like segments of a dark night—each one blending into the next, each one rendering the previous tribulation to seem mild.

⟪ar-quote:تُرَطِّقُ بعضُہُ بَعضَا⟫

One tribulation shall make the other seem light by comparison. They shall pile upon one another until the heart grows numb.

ʿAlī then asked:

⟪ar-quote:وما الخلاصُ یوم اذن یا رسول اللہ و ما مَخرجُ یوم اذن یا رسول اللہ⟫

What then is deliverance in that hour, O Messenger of God? What is the way of escape?

The Prophet replied: *The Book of God.*

⟪ar-quote:فیہ نبأُ ما قبلکم و خبر ما بعدکم و الحکم ما بینکم⟫

Within it is the news of those who came before you, and tidings of those who shall come after you, and the judgment between you.[^cite-8]

In other words, the Qur'ān contains not merely historical accounts but the sunnah (patterns, customs) of God—the unalterable ways by which He operates. These patterns do not change from age to age. Every story narrated in the Qur'ān serves not merely as historical record but as ʿibrah (lesson, exemplar) for those who have eyes to understand. The Qur'ān is not a history book, though it contains history. Its function is not to catalogue the past but to illuminate the patterns whereby God operates, so that we may apply those patterns to every circumstance we encounter.

Situations change only in their names, places, and individuals. The conditions (aḥwāl) underlying them—the states of the human soul—remain eternally the same. Oppression (ẓulm) is oppression, whether in ancient Egypt or the modern age. Fir'awn (Pharaoh) is a historical figure, but he is also the archetypal embodiment of tyranny—a behavioral pattern that transcends time. Thus the Qur'ān lays before us not dead histories but living exemplars. God gives us this Book; and within it, God grants us a book within the book: Sūrat al-Fātiḥah, ⟪ar:سبع المثانی⟫ (the Seven Oft-Repeated Verses).

Sūrat al-Fātiḥah is a summation of the entire Qur'ān compressed into seven verses. It ranks among the distinguishing characteristics—the muʿjizāt (miraculous proofs)—of the Qur'ān itself. The Qur'ān is a book that entirely incapacitated creation from producing its like. The majority of the scholars of balaghah (eloquence) maintain that the i'jāz (inimitability) of the Qur'ān lies precisely in this: no human capacity, linguistic or semantic, can match it. Neither the language alone, nor the meaning alone, nor the two in concert can be replicated by human effort. The Fātiḥah distills this inimitability into its most concentrated form.

I am the mawlā (master, patron) of him who struck with two swords, who thrust with two spears, who prayed toward both qiblah-directions, who gave allegiance in both pledges, who made both hijrahs (emigrations), and who never turned away from God for the blink of an eye. I am the mawlā of the righteous believers, the heir of the prophets, the best of the executors of will (waṣiyyīn), the greatest of Muslims, the overseer of the believers (yaʿsūb al-muʾminīn), the light of the mujāhidīn (those who struggle in God's path), the chief of those who weep, the adornment of the worshippers, the lamp of those who have passed away, the radiance of those who stand, the best of the obedient ones, the tongue of the Messenger of the Lord of the worlds, the first of the waṣiyyīn from the Family of Yā Sīn, the one confirmed by Jibrīl the Trustworthy, the one supported by Mikāʾīl the Mighty, the one praised by all the inhabitants of heaven, the defender of the sanctity of Muslims, the fighter against his enemies, the quenchers of the fires kindled by the rejecters, the noblest of those who walked from Quraysh in their entirety, the first to engage in struggle and draw forth victory. I am the mawlā of the Commander of the Faithful, the executor of will of His Prophet among all the worlds, His trustee over creation, His vicegerent for all whom He has sent. I am the lord of Muslims and the forerunners, the slayer of the oath-breakers and the unjust, the destroyer of the polytheists, and the arrow of God directed at the hypocrites.[^cite-9]

[The speaker continues:] The tongue of God's Word, His helper in His earth, the repository of His knowledge, the sanctuary of His faith, the Imam of the righteous, well-pleasing to the Most High, the Mighty. These passages from the tradition of the Imam ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib are not mere panegyric but theological proclamations of his station (maqām) as the executor of will (waṣī), the appointed guardian, and the light through which divine guidance flows to the community. They affirm that the choice of the Imam is not a human matter but ordained by God through His Prophet.
