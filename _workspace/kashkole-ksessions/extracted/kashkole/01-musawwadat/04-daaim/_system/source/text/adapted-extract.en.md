# دعائم

## The Five Foundations of Islam

From His infinite knowledge, God has provided humanity with a complete way of living in this world, which is called Islam. ⟪ar-quote:ان الدین عند اللہ الاسلام⟫ There exists but one path capable of leading to true success. Of all others, Allah declares: ⟪ar-quote:وَمَن يَبْتَغِ غَيْرَ ٱلْإِسْلَمِ دِينًۭا فَلَن يُقْبَلَ مِنْهُ وَهُوَ فِى ٱلْآخِرَةِ مِنَ ٱلْخَسِرِينَ⟫ Whosoever abandons Islam for another path shall find neither acceptance nor success in the life to come.

Islam comprises two essential dimensions: ʿibādāt (ritual devotion) and akhlāqiyyāt (moral conduct).[^cite-1] The former reconnects us with our Creator; the latter binds us to His creation. Weakness in worship severs our bond with Allah; weakness in conduct fractures our relation with humanity. Both are indispensable to authentic religious life.

Human existence unfolds between two inescapable predestined moments: birth and death. Neither enters the world through personal choice, nor departs by personal will. Yet between these twin boundaries, we have been granted free will—the capacity to act as we choose. Allah says: ⟪ar-quote:وَهَدَيْنَهُ ٱلنَّجْدَيْنِ⟫[^cite-2] and ⟪ar-quote:إِنَّا هَدَيْنَاهُ ٱلسَّبِيلَ إِمَّا شَاكِرًۭا وَإِمَّا كَفُورًا⟫ We have guided you toward both paths: choose gratitude, or choose denial—both lie open before you. In this intermediate state, you shall not be reckoned with, for this world is not the seat of judgment. ⟪ar-quote:وَقُلِ ٱلْحَقُّ مِن رَّبِّكُمْ ۖ فَمَن شَآءَ فَلْيُؤْمِن وَمَن شَآءَ فَلْيَكْفُرْ⟫ Rather, this realm is a place of trial.

This explains why righteousness and wickedness coexist in the world. The pious and the impious both may prosper; the just and unjust both may suffer. Allah has not yet assumed the role of Malik Yaum al-Din (Master of the Day of Judgment). He observes, but does not yet judge. Yet that Day approaches: ⟪ar-quote:وَلَا تَحْسَبَنَّ ٱللَّهَ غَفِلًا عَمَّا يَعْمَلُ ٱلظَّلِمُونَ⟫ and ⟪ar-quote:إِنَّمَا يُؤَخِّرُهُمْ لِيَوْمٍۢ تَشْخَصُ فِيهِ ٱلْأَبْصَرُ⟫ —the Day of al-Taghabun (Mutual Loss), when the reckoning shall come suddenly, without warning. ⟪ar-quote:لَا تَأْتِيكُمْ إِلَّا بَغْتَةًۭ⟫

How, then, shall one navigate this delicate balance? How does one discern the choices that yield both worldly flourishing and ultimate success? Only the One who created us and placed us in this intermediate state can illumine that path. This is the gift of Islam. The Prophet (peace and blessings be upon him and his family) declared:

⟪ar-quote:بُنِیَ الاسلامُ عَلَی خَمسٍ..... شَھَادَۃِ أَن لا الہ الا اللہ، و أَنَّ محمداً رَسُولُ اللہ..... وَأِقَامِ الصَّلاۃ، وَ أِیتَاءِ الزَّکاۃ، وَ صَومِ رَمَضان، وَ حَجِّ البَیت مَن اِستِطَاع اِلَیہِ سَبِیلا⟫[^cite-3]

The foundation of Islam rests upon five pillars:

1. Testimony that there is no god but Allah, and that Muhammad (peace and blessings be upon him and his family) is His Messenger
2. Establishment of prayer
3. Giving of zakāt (alms)
4. Fasting during Ramadan
5. Pilgrimage to the House of God for those who possess the means

According to the Sulaymānī tradition, Islam is understood to rest on seven pillars: wilāya (spiritual affinity and guardianship), ṭahāra (purity), salāt (prayer), zakāt (alms), ṣawm (fasting), ḥajj (pilgrimage), and jihād (spiritual struggle).[^cite-4] Yet the hadith of the Prophet does not explicitly enumerate wilāya, ṭahāra, and jihād as separate foundations. This apparent discord resolves upon deeper reflection: these three are implicit within the five mentioned. Wilāya is contained within the shahāda (testimony); ṭahāra and jihād are woven through all the remaining pillars. Before elaborating upon the dynamics of these latter five, I wish first to unfold the deeper meaning of these explicit commands—truths that Allah Most High has ordained for all who wish to submit, for all who seek to give thanks.

### Islam as the Journey and Development of Love

There exists one interpretation of exquisite beauty, one that has pierced the depths of the heart most profoundly.

Islam begins with the shahāda, which comprises two distinct elements, for to inhabit this world means to engage with two realities: the Creator and the creation. ⟪ar:لا الہ الا اللہ⟫ pertains to ʿibādāt (worship)—our transaction with the Divine—while ⟪ar:محمدا رسول اللہ⟫ pertains to muʿāmalāt (dealings)—our conduct toward creation. As one progresses in the study of the Sharīʿa, one discovers that the demands of worship are circumscribed, yet the demands of moral conduct—of akhlāq—are boundless.

Why does Islam commence with the shahāda? Because it concerns wilāya (spiritual affinity and closeness). We must first understand this principle. Wilāya derives from the root *wa-li-ya* (⟪ar:وَلِیَ⟫), signifying a joining so complete that separation becomes impossible—"two bodies, one soul," as the Urdu phrase expresses it. In its broadest sense, wilāya denotes spiritual intimacy, an affinity so profound it transcends ordinary friendship. The derivative *mawlā* (master, guardian) refers to the one who possesses authority, who guides and controls.

This is why wilāya stands at the very heart of the shahāda, which begins with the negation: *lā ilāha illā'llāh*—there is no god but Allah. The shahāda opens with negation—the annihilation of all creation so that the One Creator alone remains. This negation is love's very architecture. Love itself is precisely this: the negation of all possibilities except the Beloved. It is the isolation of the One from the infinite many. From all conceivable choices, "you" select that singular person to love. When you have thus negated all others to affirm the One Beloved alone, no intermediary stands between you and your Beloved. This is wilāya itself—the first sacred stage.

⟪ar:محمدا رسول اللہ⟫ represents the final stage of the shahāda. The Prophet (peace and blessings be upon him and his family) stands as the pinnacle—the perfect, tangible embodiment of the shahāda's truth. Thus *lā ilāha illā'llāh* is the seed, and *Muhammad rasūl Allah* is the tree that springs from it. If you truly believe that there exists no god but Allah, then reshape yourself according to the example of the Prophet (peace and blessings be upon him and his family). This is why he is called *mawlā*—the one in command, the one in control. By testifying to *Muhammad rasūl Allah*, we transfer the governance of our souls and the mastery of our actions to him. We make him our *mawlā*.

According to our tradition, the verse of perfection was revealed on the day of Ghadīr, immediately after the Prophet (peace and blessings be upon him and his family) appointed Mawlānā ʿAlī (peace be upon him) as his successor. This verse unfolds in three dimensions:

*Al-yawm akmaltu lakum dīnakum*—Today I have perfected your dīn (way of life) for you. The word *al-yawm* (today) signals that this perfection is occurring in this present moment, unprecedented in its completeness. *Lakum* (for you) indicates that this perfection is being granted to your community alone, never before granted to the nation of any other prophet.

*Wa atmamtu ʿalaykum niʿmatī*—And I have completed My blessings upon you. The final clause declares: *wa raḍītu lakum al-islām dīnan*—And I am content with Islam as your dīn. Allah thus speaks of three interconnected realities: the perfection of the dīn, the completion of divine blessings, and Allah's own contentment with Islam as the path of the community. On that day, the dīn reached its fullness; on that day, Allah's grace became complete; and on that day, Allah accepted the covenant of submission.

The verse holds three poles: Muhammad, ʿAlī, and Allah. It is through their interconnection that the religion achieves its perfection.[^cite-5]

The spiritual path unveiled by the Prophet (peace and blessings be upon him and his family) rests upon two pillars: the Qur'ān (revelation) and the Sunna (the lived practice). Yet Sunna does not signify mere imitation of outward acts—a reduction to which many have reduced it. Rather, by Sunna we mean the very substance of prophetic perfection to which the Qur'ān itself testifies: ⟪ar-quote:وَإِنَّكَ لَعَلَىٰ خُلُقٍ عَظِيمٍۢ⟫ Indeed, you embody a character of transcendent greatness.

The purpose of Sunna for every Muslim is not the mechanical repetition of words and gestures, but rather the interior assimilation of its essence. There exists a natural affinity between the believing soul and the prophetic nature of the Messenger. The soul yearns by its very constitution (fiṭra—primordial nature) for all that the Prophet's life embodied. Thus the Qur'ān declares: ⟪ar-quote:ٱلنَّبِىُّ أَوْلَىٰ بِٱلْمُؤْمِنِينَ مِنْ أَنفُسِهِمْ⟫ The Prophet stands closer to the believers than their own selves. Here again, the root *walā* appears—denoting that closeness which transcends ordinary kinship. The Prophet outwardly manifests the perfection that the believing soul instinctively craves. And among all who lived after the Prophet, only one person truly and completely internalized the prophetic substance: Amīr al-Mu'minīn ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib. This is affirmed by the Prophet's declaration: ⟪ar-quote:العَلِیُّ کَنَفسِی⟫ Ali is as my own soul! And again: ⟪ar-quote:أَنتَ مِنِّی وَ أنَا مِنکَ⟫ You are of me, and I am of you. Thus the Prophet's profound bond with ʿAlī becomes intelligible through the lens of Sunna.

⟪ar-quote:انا مدینۃ العلم و علی بابھا، فمن اراد المدینۃ فلیات الباب⟫ I am the city of knowledge, and ʿAlī is its gate. Whoever wishes to enter the city must pass through the gate. The gate is the city's guardian and protector. ʿAlī is the gateway to prophetic wisdom.

And the second foundation of the Prophet's existence was the Qur'ān itself. The Prophet proclaimed: ⟪ar-quote:علي مع القرآن ، والقرآن مع علي ، لن يفترقا حتى يردا على الحوض⟫ ʿAlī is with the Qur'ān, and the Qur'ān is with ʿAlī. They shall never be separated until they reach me at the Pool. Thus the essence of both Qur'ān and Sunna were perfected within Mawlānā ʿAlī.

According to our Sulaymānī tradition, on the day of Ghadīr—Yaum-i Ghadīr—the Prophet (peace and blessings be upon him and his family) transferred his sovereignty to Mawlānā ʿAlī. He announced:

⟪ar-quote:مَنْ کُنتُ مَولاہُ فَھٰذا عَلِیٌّ مَولاہُ⟫[^cite-6]

Whosoever has considered me his *mawlā*, from this hour forth, ʿAlī is his *mawlā*. 

⟪ar-quote:مَنْ کُنتُ وَلِیُّہُ فَعَلِیٌّ وَلِیُّہُ⟫

Whosoever has held me as *wali* (guardian, intimate friend, one in authority)—from this moment, ʿAlī is your *wali*.

⟪ar-quote:مَنْ کُنتُ نَبِیُّہُ فَعَلِیٌّ اَمِیرُہُ⟫

Whosoever has accepted me as prophet, from this day, ʿAlī is his leader and commander. Thus on Ghadīr, there was an explicit transfer of *mawlāyat* (mastery and guardianship). This is why, in our tradition, *wilāya* is intimately bound to Mawlānā ʿAlī (peace be upon him). When we recite the call to prayer (adhān), we proclaim:

⟪ar-quote:اشھد ان محمدا رسول اللہ..... اشھد ان مولانا علی ولی اللہ⟫

Why do we not say instead: *Ashhad anna Muhammadan rasūl Allah*? Why the distinction? Because sovereignty has been transferred to ʿAlī. This does not diminish the Prophet's *mawlāyat*—he remains forever *mawlā*. Rather, the Prophet has joined his intimacy and closeness, his affinity and friendship, with that of Mawlānā ʿAlī. The path to Allah's closeness runs through the *wilāya* of the Prophet (peace and blessings be upon him and his family). And the path to the Prophet's closeness runs through the *wilāya* of Mawlānā ʿAlī. This is why, after declaring ⟪ar:من کنت مولاہ فھذا علی مولاہ⟫, the Prophet immediately invoked:

⟪ar-quote:اللّٰھُمَّ فَوَالِ مَن وَالاہُ، وَعَادِ مَن عَادَاہُ، وَانْصُر مَن نَصَرَہُ، وَاخْذُل مَن خَذَلَہُ، وَأَدِرِ الحَقَّ مَعَہُ حَیثُ دَارَ⟫[^cite-7]

O Allah! Befriend the one who befriends him; be the enemy of the one who opposes him; support the one who supports him; humiliate the one who abandons him; and let truth revolve wherever he revolves. Our scholars have explained that *al-Haqq* (Truth) here refers to the Imāms who shall descend from the progeny of Mawlānā ʿAlī and Mawlāt Fāṭima. We believers are the followers of *Haqq*—of the Imāms—and *Haqq* itself follows Mawlānā ʿAlī.

Thus the very seed of Islam is *wilāya*: the negation of all creation for the affirmation of the One True Beloved, Allah Most High. Wilāya establishes direction; it orients the qibla of the heart. It sets before us a destination—the love of Allah.

⟪ar-quote:يا ابن آدم .. أنا لَكَ مُحِبُّ..... فَبِحَقِّي عَليكَ كُنْ لِي مُحِبَّا⟫[^cite-8]

O children of Adam! I love you. And by My right over you—by My very nature as your Creator—love Me in return.

Through the shahāda, we commit ourselves to an inward transformation, to reshape ourselves according to the template of the one the Prophet loved. The testimony that Muhammad (peace and blessings be upon him and his family) is the true Messenger means precisely this: he is the authentic embodiment of the virtues contained in *lā ilāha illā'llāh*. This includes the testimony that Mawlānā ʿAlī and the Imāms are the true practical manifestations of the *Kalima* (testimony) in the ages that follow. If you love Him, become like these people. The poet Abū Nuwās captured this yearning:

⟪ar-quote:أشْبَهت أعدائي فَصِرتُ أُحِبُّهمْ… إذ صَارَ حُظّي مِنكَ حُظّي مِنهُمُ…
وأَهَنْتني فأَهَنْتُ نفسي صاغرًا … ما مَن يهونُ عليك مِمّن أُكرِمُ…⟫

You appeared before me in the guise of my enemy, and thus I came to love my enemy—for whatever your reflection falls upon becomes beautiful in my sight. You have humiliated me, and I have humiliated myself; surely none whom you diminish is truly honored.

This is the true essence of the shahāda: it penetrates so deeply into the inner self that the outer life is transformed into a living mirror of the Prophet and Mawlānā ʿAlī.

### Salāt: The Connection

Once the direction is set, once the Beloved stands recognized as the qibla of the heart, the next imperative is to *connect* with the Beloved. This is salāt. The word *salāt* derives from *sila*, which means "to join with," "to link together." It also carries the sense of "to burn," for when one is joined to Allah, one burns in His love. Sin too is consumed in His presence. Now that we have surrendered to love, the next natural step is to maintain constant connection. Thus: *Iqāmat al-salāt*—Establish the prayer, maintain the connection. For love is nourished through connection; it deepens with each bond renewed.

In previous ages, lovers maintained their bond through letters; now they write emails; now they send messages through the instant platforms of the digital age. Yet all are forms of connection with the beloved. This is why salāt is such a precious gift for those who truly love. They cannot bear to wait until the next prayer. When the time arrives, they rush toward it. And once in prayer, they prolong the moment as long as possible, because to be in the presence of the Beloved is to be alive.

### Zakāt: Spending for the Beloved

From love and constant connection springs the next natural consequence: love for those whom the Beloved loves. When you love someone and maintain constant communion with that person, you inevitably develop affection for the people they cherish. Should you learn that your beloved's child will visit your town, you will ensure their comfort and care—not for their sake alone, but because they are precious to your beloved. This is the interior principle of zakāt. Indeed, throughout the Qur'ān, these two practices appear inseparably linked:

⟪ar-quote:وَأَقِيمُوا۟ ٱلصَّلَوٰةَ وَآتُوا۟ ٱلزَّكَوٰةَ⟫

Establish prayer and give zakāt. Why this pairing? Because if you love Me, you will care for those I love. The hadith too mirrors this connection: immediately following the command to establish prayer comes the command to give zakāt:

⟪ar-quote:وَأِقَامِ الصَّلاۃ، وَ أِیتَاءِ الزَّکاۃ⟫

### Ṣawm: The Passion of Love

Until this juncture, the scholars say, the believer dwells in a state of *hubb*—love, affection, personal fondness. Yet through the sustained reinforcement of prayer and almsgiving, this love undergoes a transformation. The believer ascends to *ʿishq*—passionate love, an obsession that borders on the overwhelming. At this stage, the lover becomes so consumed by the Beloved that the ordinary sustenance of food and drink loses its appeal. This is the domain of ṣawm (fasting). Here the lover begins to sacrifice his own desires—indeed, his own body—for the sake of the Beloved.

One meaning of the word *ʿishq* is "to seek," "to strive for," "to pursue." Another meaning is "fool"—for the *ʿāshiq* (passionate lover) appears foolish to the world, having surrendered intellect to the heart. *ʿIshq* is also likened to a fatal disease, to a parasitic vine that slowly strangles the life from the plant, drawing all its nourishment to itself until the plant withers and perishes in the act of sustaining the vine.

Such is ṣawm: the passionate love of Allah Most Mighty. The Prophet (peace and blessings be upon him and his family) declared in a profound hadith:

⟪ar-quote:قال اللہ تعالی، کُلَّ عَمَلَ ابْنَ آدَمَ لَہُ اِلَّا الصِّیَام..... فَأِنَّہُ لی وَ أنَا أُجزِیَ بِہِ⟫

Allah Most High says: All deeds of the children of Adam belong to them—except fasting. Fasting is for Me alone, and I Myself shall reward it.

Why does Allah single out fasting when surely all acts of worship are devoted to Him, and He rewards them all? The scholars explain that it is because of the exalted station fasting holds in His sight, and because it demonstrates singular sincerity toward Him. Fasting is a secret pact between a believer and his Lord—unseen by any eye save Allah's. It is also the most demanding of devotions, for the believer must surrender even the basic necessity of nourishment out of love for Allah.

Furthermore, other deeds are enumerated with specific multipliers—a good deed is rewarded tenfold. But by declaring "Fasting is for Me and I shall reward it," Allah indicates: I shall multiply its reward according to My discretion, as I choose. For fasting is an act of *sabr* (patience, endurance), and the Qur'ān testifies:

⟪ar-quote:إِنَّمَا يُوَفَّى ٱلصَّبِرُونَ أَجْرَهُم بِغَيْرِ حِسَابٍۢ⟫

Indeed, the patient shall receive their reward without measure.

Etymologically, the word *ṣawm* means "to abandon," "to sacrifice for the beloved." It elevates love (*hubb*) to the station of passionate obsession (*ʿishq*). And one always finds the *ʿāshiq* in a state of destitution, for he has become so absorbed in his beloved that every other desire of the world has lost its hold upon him. He abandons all else. The material world can no longer entrance him. This is the state of *ʿishq*.

### Ḥajj: Meeting the Beloved

Yet then comes the remedy for love: *Dawā' al-ʿishq al-ittiṣāl*—The cure for passionate love is meeting the Beloved. Now the lover reaches a point of such desperation to encounter the Beloved that he will traverse the ends of the earth. Consider Salmān al-Fārisī! Behold his love, his yearning! He journeyed across the world seeking the Prophet (peace and blessings be upon him and his family). Whenever he heard of a righteous person, he would travel to that place hoping this might be the prophet. His odyssey to find the Messenger is a testament to love's power. What hardships he endured, what distances he covered, all for the sake of meeting his Beloved! And his love extended not only to the Prophet but to the entire family—to Mawlānā ʿAlī, to al-Ḥasan and al-Ḥusayn. Such was his intimacy that he would cleanse the floors of Mawlāt Fāṭima's house with his own cloak. He drew so close to the Prophet that the Messenger said: ⟪ar:سلمان منا اھل البیت⟫ Salmān is of our household. Behold what love achieves! Consider Owayss al-Qaranī, who loved the Prophet so intensely that when he heard a tooth of the Prophet had been broken in battle, he began extracting his own teeth one by one, saying to himself: perhaps it was this one; no, perhaps this one—until he had removed them all. This is true love! My Beloved suffered pain; therefore I shall suffer that pain. This is *ʿishq*!

Look at Mālik al-Ashtar, at Maymūna, one of the Prophet's wives. For thirty years she lived as his companion. When death drew near, do you know where she wished to be? In that very place where she had spent her first night with the Prophet (peace and blessings be upon him and his family)—and there she chose to be buried. Behold that love! Such was the *ʿishq* that these noble souls possessed. Today, it is a tragedy that we have extracted love from Islam and transformed it into something grotesque. Yet this religion was always founded upon love; it was built upon love's very pillars.

And so the Beloved now extends an invitation to the lover: Come, meet with Me. This is *ḥajj*—a summons from the Beloved to the lover to come into His presence. We hold as sacred principle that when we undertake ʿumra or ḥajj, we do so in response to Allah's invitation to His House. For it would be improper to appear at another's door uninvited. Thus *ḥajj* is *al-ittiṣāl*—the final union with the Beloved. And notice how the prescribed rituals of ʿumra and ḥajj are designed to reflect the appearance of one crazed with love: we do not adorn ourselves; we abstain from perfume; we do not comb the hair. Instead, we wrap ourselves in two simple cloths and set forth toward the Beloved. This is the culmination of *ʿishq*—the lover is so frenzied with desire to meet the Beloved that upon receiving the invitation, he rushes out without delay.

The word *ʿumra* derives from the root *ʿ-m-r* (⟪ar:عَمَرَ⟫), meaning "to inhabit," "to dwell," "to repair," "to cultivate." The root of *ḥajj* is *ḥ-j-j* (⟪ar:حَجَّ⟫), meaning "to aim at something once," for *ḥajj* is performed only once in each year and is obligatory only once in a lifetime. *ʿUmra is the preparatory stage of *ḥajj*, and one of its meanings is to visit an inhabited dwelling. Yet Mecca—the House of the King of Kings—has always been a barren, desolate land, surrounded by harsh heat and sand. By all logic, such a place should have been beautified beyond measure: surrounded by verdant mountains, cascading waterfalls, blessed with the most temperate of climates, made into the most magnificent place on earth. Yet instead, it remains historically arid and desolate, offering its visitors nothing but extreme hardship.

Why is this? It is a test of the lover's sincerity.

*Ān hī paththaron par chel kar agr āskō tō āō*
*Mere ghar ke rāste mein koī kahakashan nahīn hai*

(If you can walk upon these very stones, then come. There is no starlit path to my house.)

When the lover is truly mad with passion, hardship holds no terror. All he craves is one glimpse of the Beloved. Thus *ḥajj* becomes the moment when the lover, insane with longing, rushes toward the Beloved with cries and shouts. When Allah called humanity to prayer, He commanded: ⟪ar-quote:ٱدْعُوا۟ رَبَّكُمْ تَضَرُّعًۭا وَخُفْيَةً..... إِنَّهُۥ لَا يُحِبُّ ٱلْمُعْتَدِينَ⟫ Call upon your Lord in humility and in private; He does not love the transgressive. Yet when Allah called toward His House, He said: ⟪ar-quote:فَلعَجَّ وَ ثَج⟫ Come to Me shouting and crying, for that is the mark of the passionate lover. Thus the lover rushes toward the Beloved crying out:

⟪ar-quote:لب بیک اللھم لبیک⟫
⟪ar-quote:لبیک لا شریک لک لبیک⟫
⟪ar-quote:ان الحمد و النعمت لک.....  والملک لا شریک لہ⟫

Here I am, O my Lord, here I am! There is no partner with You; here I am! Truly all praise and blessings belong to You, and dominion as well—and You have no partner.

Once, Junaid al-Baghdādī was performing *tawāf* (circumambulation) around the Kaʿba when he observed a young woman also circumambulating, reciting verses:

⟪ar-quote:أَبىَ الحُبُّ أن يَّخْفىٰ وَكَمْ قَد كَتَمْتَهُ ..... فأَصْبَحْ عِندِى قَد أَنَاخَ وَطَنَّبَا⟫

Love refused to remain hidden; though I concealed it long, it has now pitched its tent and settled within me.

⟪ar-quote:إِذَا اشْتَدَّ شَوقِى هَامَ قَلبِى بِذِكرِهِ ..... فَإِنْ رُمتُ قُرباً مِن حَبِيبِى تَقَرَّبَا⟫

When longing grows fierce, my heart trembles in remembrance of Him. And as soon as I desire nearness to my Beloved, He rushes to meet me.

⟪ar-quote:يَبدُو فَأَفنٰى ثُمَّ أَحْيٰى لَهُ بِهِ ..... وَيُسْعِدُنِى حَتّٰى ألَذَّ وأطْرَبَا⟫

He appears and I am annihilated; then through Him I am revived. His love grants me such happiness that I am both delighted and enraptured.

Junaid heard these verses and flew into a rage. How dare you speak such things in this place! Do you not understand where you stand? The young woman replied: Do not mistake the meaning of my words. These verses concern the same One for whom you circumambulate:

⟪ar-quote:لَولا التُّقىٰ لَم تَرِنى أَهجِر طِيبِ الوَسَنِ⟫
⟪ar-quote:إِنَّ التُّقىٰ شَرَّدَنى كمَا تَرى عَن وَطَنى⟫

Had it not been for my reverence, you would find me sleeping in comfort within my chamber. It is the love of Allah that has driven me from my home and brought me before Him.

Then she asked Junaid: Around what do you circumambulate? And he answered: Around the Kaʿba. Upon hearing this, she laughed and responded:

⟪ar-quote:يَطُوفُونَ بالأحجار يَبغُونَ قُربَةُ..... إِلَيكَ وَهمٌ أَقسٰى قُلُوباً مِنَ الصَّخَرِ⟫

⟪ar-quote:فَلَو أخْلَصُوا فِى الوُدَّ غَابَت صِفَاتِهِم..... وَقَامَتُ صِفَاتُ الوَدَّ لِلحَقِّ بِالذِّكَرِ⟫

What strange folk these are, circling stones in search of nearness to You, when their hearts are harder than stone! Were they sincere in their love, they would lose all sense of self; and in its place would arise only the attributes of love directed toward the Truth through remembrance.

When Junaid heard these verses, he lost consciousness. When he regained awareness, the woman had vanished.

Thus unfolds the progression of love through the prescribed forms of worship. The shahāda is the opening gesture—the identification of the Beloved through the negation of all others, and the understanding of love's final manifestation in the Prophet (peace and blessings be upon him and his family). The shahāda is *taʿarruf*—recognition and introduction. Then come salāt and zakāt, which constitute *taʿallaq*—attachment and binding. They join the lover to the Beloved and kindle love. Then comes ṣawm, which ignites the fires of *ʿishq*—passionate obsession—elevating love to a state where the lover willingly abandons all else for the Beloved. In this condition, the beauties and pleasures of the world lose all power to attract. And this very *ʿishq* compels the lover toward the Beloved's door, the place where the Beloved extends the invitation to *ḥajj*. Thus *ḥajj* is *wiṣāl, liqāʾ al-Maḥbūb*—union, the meeting with the Beloved.

*Tīre koche is bahāne mujhe din se rāt karnā*
*Kabhī is se bāt karnā, kabhī us se bāt karnā*

(For the sake of reaching your street, I care not if day turns to night. Sometimes I speak with this companion, sometimes with that—)

The essence and spirit of *ḥajj* is to journey in search of Allah. It is the final *hijra* (migration, departure). The word *hijra* means to move from one location to another permanently, severing all ties to the past. There is the *ẓāhirī hijra* (outward migration) which the Prophet (peace and blessings be upon him and his family) undertook when he moved from Mecca to Medina. Yet this physical migration is a sign and reminder for every believer that we too must undertake a spiritual *hijra*—a movement away from sin, from transgression, from disobedience toward submission; from evil toward good; from spiritual tribulation toward peace and safety (*salāma*), toward *islām* itself. Someone once asked the Prophet: ⟪ar-quote:یا رسول اللہ! من الاسلام؟⟫ O Prophet of Allah, what is Islam? He replied: ⟪ar-quote:أَن تُسَلَّمَ قَلبَکَ لِی اللہ⟫ To surrender your heart completely to Allah.

This is the true essence of *ḥajj*: a determination to transform the heart before returning home. It is a shift in consciousness—recognizing that until now I have lived according to my own will and desires, but henceforth I shall live according to Allah's will and commandments, which is the *sīra* (way, conduct) of the Prophet (peace and blessings be upon him and his family)—to do what he commands and abstain from what he forbids. This is the inner meaning of the cry *labbayk Allahumma labbayk*.

*Ḥajj* is the most complete and comprehensive form of worship, for it encompasses all the others. In *ḥajj*, you journey toward the qibla, which is the essence of the shahāda; you perform prayer; you expend wealth; you fast; and every act demands exertion and struggle, which is *jihād*. *Jihād* derives from the root *j-h-d*, meaning "to strive," "to struggle." Every form of worship involves effort and striving, the surmounting of resistance both external and internal.
