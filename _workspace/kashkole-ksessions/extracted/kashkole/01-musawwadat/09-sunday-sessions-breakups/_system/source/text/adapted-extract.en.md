## The Inquiry Into Fatima's Illness

When the Messenger of Allah (upon him be peace and blessings) learned of Mawlānā Fāṭima's affliction, he journeyed to her dwelling to inquire of her condition. He was accompanied by one of his most trusted companions, ʿImrān ibn Ḥuṣayn. Upon arrival, he called at the door: "Fāṭima, may I enter? ʿImrān is with me." She replied, "O Messenger of Allah, I lack even a single sheet with which to cover myself." Thereupon, he removed the shawl from his own shoulders, saying, "My daughter, wrap yourself in this." When he entered and asked after her state, she began to weep—not from pain of body, but from the profound nearness of her father. Through her tears, she spoke: "O Messenger of Allah, once there was hunger and no food to sustain us. Now there is illness, and no remedy to comfort us."[^cite-1]

The Messenger embraced her with tenderness, and his own tears began to flow. Those very stones of Ṭāʾif that had rained blows upon him could not draw forth his weeping; yet the love he bore his daughter moved him to tears. As his eyes wept, he said to her:

"I swear by Him who sent your father as a prophet in truth: for three days now I have not tasted even a morsel of bread. Had I willed it, these mountains would have been transformed into gold and silver for me—yet I chose otherwise."[^cite-2]

Then, with the tenderness of a father and the wisdom of a prophet, he continued: "Fāṭima, let it suffice you for your honor that you are *Sayyidat nisāʾ al-janna*—the leader of the women of Paradise. This is not how the lords of Paradise are found."[^cite-3]

## Abraham and Ishmael's Sacrifice

A man stands at ninety-two years of age, a knife trembling in his hand. The child beside him has reached eighty-four. When he could no longer slaughter even a fowl, yet Allah placed upon him this supreme trial. Both stand now in the valley of Mīnā, and Abraham speaks to his son with a father's anguish and a prophet's resignation:

"My son, I have seen in a dream that I am slaughtering you. ⟪ar-quote:فَلَمَّا بَلَغَ مَعَهُ ٱلسَّعْىَ قَالَ يَبُنَىَّ إِنِّىٓ أَرَىٰ فِى ٱلْمَنَامِ أَنِّىٓ أَذْبَحُكَ فَٱنظُرْ مَاذَا تَرَىٰ⟫"[^cite-4]

This is no mere fancy, for the dreams of the prophets do not deceive. Yet how does a father's heart contend with such a vision? Abraham would not have acted until the command came manifest. But the dream itself—and the dreams of the prophets are true—compelled him to consult with his son. ⟪ar-quote:فَٱنظُرْ مَاذَا تَرَىٰ⟫—"Consider what you see fit."

Tell me: why would Abraham seek counsel from a child? In our time, we account it shameful to ask our children for counsel. What could they know, being mere children? And yet Abraham does not ask the boy's opinion on the color of garments or the hue of a wall. He seeks his judgment on his own immolation. Thus the Qur'ān teaches us the manner in which parents must cultivate the hearts and minds of their children. Observe his son's response:

⟪ar-quote:قَالَ يَٓأَبَتِ ٱفْعَلْ مَا تُؤْمَرُ⟫—"Father, do that which you have been commanded."[^cite-5] And he adds: ⟪ar-quote:سَتَجِدُنِىٓ إِن شَآءَ ٱللَّهُ مِنَ ٱلصَّبِرِينَ⟫—"God willing, you will find me among those who are patient."[^cite-6] In these verses, the Qur'ān reveals both the duty of the son and the nobility that must dwell within him.

Consider what passed in Ishmael's heart. He said: "Father, I understand what you say. You will slaughter me, and I shall be slaughtered. Yet in exchange for you, I shall attain to God—and God is better than you. The Paradise that awaits is better than the life of this world. But I confess to you: I think upon my mother, how greatly she will grieve to hear of my slaughter. Therefore, take this shirt of mine and give it to her as a sign. When she sees it, she will remember me, and through remembrance, I shall live on in her heart. And please, wrap me in your cloak when you bury me there."

With a voice thick with sorrow, he continued: "Bind my hands and feet with rope, so that if I should struggle in pain, my suffering may not afflict your eyes."[^cite-7]

Imagine this: for sixty years this man had prayed—*dua* (supplication)—that God would grant him a child. After six decades, God answered. Now, as a father, he binds those limbs as a shepherd binds the feet of a goat before slaughter. He laid his son upon his face, lest the father see the eyes he loved. Then he knelt upon the boy's back, and lifted his head, and placed the knife beneath his throat.

At that moment, Abraham looked toward the heavens and spoke—not to his son, but to his Lord: "O my Lord! O my Friend! Whose love is greater in my heart—the love of Ishmael, or the love of You? Surely, not the former! You alone dwell supreme within me. I have placed this knife at Ishmael's throat as a sacrifice to You."[^cite-8]

At these words, a cry arose among the angels of the seven heavens.

Abraham closed his eyes and whispered: *Bismillāh Allāhu Akbar*—"In the name of God, God is Most Great." In that instant, the Lord placed beneath the knife a great ram from Paradise in place of Ishmael, so that Abraham did not even perceive the change. He drew the blade as though he had slaughtered his son. Then a voice thundered from the heavens:

"It is enough, O Abraham! You have fulfilled the trust! We shall reward the doers of good in the same manner."

And the Qur'ān records this moment:

⟪ar-quote:إِنَّ هَذَا لَهُوَ ٱلْبَلَٓؤُا۟ ٱلْمُبِينُ۔ وَفَدَيْنَاهُ بِذِبْحٍ عَظِيمٍۢ۔ وَتَرَكْنَا عَلَيْهِ فِى ٱلْآخِرِينَ ۔ سَلَمٌ عَلَىٰٓ إِبْرَهِيمَ ۔ كَذَلِكَ نَجْزِى ٱلْمُحْسِنِينَ ۔ إِنَّهُۥ مِنْ عِبَادِنَا ٱلْمُؤْمِنِينَ⟫[^cite-9]

"This was indeed the mighty trial with which We tested Abraham. We redeemed him with a great sacrifice. We left his name exalted among the generations. Peace be upon Abraham. Thus do We reward those who do good. Surely, he was among Our believing servants."

## The Messenger's Trial in Ṭāʾif

The Messenger of Allah fled for his life through Ṭāʾif, and the people of that city pursued him, raining stones upon his back. He turned from house to house, knocking at each closed door, seeking shelter—yet none would grant him sanctuary. Stones struck his body until both his shoes filled with blood. He fell, then rose; rose again, then fell.

For whom did he endure this? What depths of suffering must have rent the heart of that greatest of beings in the universe—he whose face the Lord Himself has sworn by, the one whose very countenance is honored above all creation. Yet when his patience could bear no more, he poured out his heart to Allah in supplication:

> ⟪ar:اللَّهُمَّ إِنِّي أَشْكُو إِلَيْكَ ضَعْفَ قُوَّتِي وَقِلَّةَ حِيلَتِي وَهَوَانِي عَلَى النَّاسِ يَا أَرْحَمَ الرَّاحِمِينَ، أَنْتَ رَبُّ الْمُسْتَضْعَفِينَ وَأَنْتَ رَبِّي إِلَى مَنْ تَكِلُنِي إِلَى بَعِيدٍ يَتَجَهَّمُنِي أَمْ إِلَى عَدُوٍّ مَلَّكْتَهُ أَمْرِي إِنْ لَمْ يَكُنْ بِكَ عَلَيَّ غَضَبٌ فَلَا أُبَالِي وَلَكِنَّ عَافِيَتَكَ هِيَ أَوْسَعُ لِي أَعُوذُ بِنُورِ وَجْهِكَ الَّذِي أَشْرَقَتْ لَهُ الظُّلُمَاتُ وَصَلَحَ عَلَيْهِ أَمْرُ الدُّنْيَا وَالْآخِرَةِ مِنْ أَنْ تُنْزِلَ بِي غَضَبَكَ أَوْ يَحِلَّ عَلَيَّ سَخَطُكَ لَكَ الْعُتْبَى حَتَّى تَرْضَى وَلَا حَوْلَ وَلَا قُوَّةَ إِلَّا بِكَ⟫

"O Allah, I complain unto You of my weakness and my lack of means and my insignificance before the people. O Most Merciful of the merciful! You are the Lord of the weak. You are my Lord. To whom do You entrust me? To one far off who will treat me with enmity? Or to an enemy whom You have granted dominion over my affair? If Your wrath is not upon me, then I care not. Yet Your well-being is broader for me. I seek refuge in the light of Your Face, by which the darknesses are illumined, and upon which depend all matters of this world and the next—that You do not bring down Your wrath upon me, nor let Your displeasure settle upon me. For You alone are my appeal until You are pleased. There is no power and no strength save in You."

This supplication—the *duʿāʾ al-Ṭāʾif*—echoes through the ages as the prayer of one who had given all, yet received rejection from those he sought to guide.

This is he by whose back the Lord makes mention—
This is he by whose heart the Lord has spoken—
This is he by whose cloak the Lord has spoken: "O you wrapped in garment" (*Yā Ayyuhā al-Muzammil*)
This is he by whose covering the Lord has mentioned: "O you concealed in blanket" (*Yā Ayyuhā al-Muddaththir*)—
This is he whose sustenance the Lord has mentioned—
This is he whose mighty deeds the Lord has mentioned—

This is he whose day the Lord has mentioned—

The Qur'ān alone suffices as guidance for those lost in error. And the Lord declares: "We sent you not save as a bringer of good tidings and a warner to all."[^cite-10]

This is he to whom the Lord promised the Station of Praise (*Maqām al-Maḥmūd*), when Jesus himself shall bear witness.

This is he whose night journey the Lord has recorded: ⟪ar-quote:سُبْحَانَ الَّذِي أَسْرَىٰ بِعَبْدِهِ لَيْلًا مِّنَ الْمَسْجِدِ الْحَرَامِ إِلَىٰ الْمَسْجِدِ الْأَقْصَى الَّذِي بَارَكْنَا حَوْلَهُ⟫—"Glory be to Him who carried His servant by night from the Sacred Mosque to the Furthest Mosque, whose precincts We have blessed."[^cite-11]

Throughout the entire Qur'ān, the Lord describes His love for His Prophet. How much pain, then, should wound our hearts when we see how His *Walī* (his sacred legacy) is treated among us? What do we make of this great trust, this precious inheritance?

## The Passing of Āmina

He was six years old when Āmina, his mother, passed from this world. For a child of six, the entire cosmos is contained within the mother—friend, nurse, protector, universe entire. Years passed in sorrow's silence. When he reached manhood and undertook his final pilgrimage, fifty-seven years had elapsed since her death. He was now sixty-three years old, yet the wound remained fresh.

As his caravan approached Abwāʾ, he passed by his mother's grave. The month was scorching—the desert heat of Arabia blazing without mercy. He descended from his camel and sat at the head of her tomb. Drawing both hands around his knees, he lowered his head between them and wept—wept as a small child weeps, with the full depth of grief that knows no restraint. He remained there for hours, his tears flowing, heedless of the burning sun, seeing nothing but the memory of her whom he had lost so young.[^cite-12]

Consider what we perceive in this moment: the one for whom the universe itself was created was subjected to such a trial. His mother, Āmina, was but twenty-five years old when the Lord took her. Had she lived four days longer, she would have reached Mecca and found the security of her son's protection, the care of grandfathers and uncles. Yet death claimed her in the wilderness—on a lonely journey. Her grave rests upon a small hill, unmarked and unadorned.

The Prophet was but six years old, accompanied only by his servant Umm Aymān (the mother of Ayman), for his father had not lived to see him. Who was she to the orphaned child? All things at once—mother and nurse, sister and friend, guardian and comfort. Imagine a child of six: his entire world is his mother. When he thirsted, the Messenger ran like an arrow to bring her water. As his mother's strength failed him, what agony filled the Prophet's heart to witness her decline!

But we have strayed far from such knowledge. Then, as her spirit departed from her blessed body, she spoke words that would echo through eternity:

*Every living being must taste death, every youth must grow old, everything new must grow ancient. I too am departing, yet my name shall live forever in this world, for I am leaving behind a perfect goodness that shall purify all creation.*[^cite-13]

Life surrenders to death's dominion.

Umm Aymān turned to depart, then glanced back—and saw the Prophet clinging to his mother's grave, weeping without measure. She heard him cry out: "O Mother! Where have you left me? You knew I had no one but you. Where shall I go now? To whom shall I turn?" And again: "Mother, let me stay here with you!" But Umm Aymān drew him away and seated him upon the camel. Three nights they traveled to reach Mecca, and throughout that journey, none could wipe away his tears.

When Umm Aymān came to the burial mound, she saw the earthly remains of the Mother of the Messenger of Allah.

## "Walk Through Their Dwellings"

⟪ar-quote:أَحیَ قَلبَکَ بِالمَوعِضَۃ، وَ نَوِّرہُ بِالحِکمَۃ، وَقوِّھِی بِالزُّھَد، وَذَلِّلہُ بِالمَوت، وَقَرِّرہُ بِالفَناَء،وحَزِّنہُ وَسُولَتَ الدَّھَر،وَتَقَلُّبَ الَّیالِی وَالأَیَّام⟫[^cite-14]

"Quicken your heart with admonition and illuminate it with wisdom. Strengthen it through asceticism (*zuuhd*—renunciation of worldly attachment), humble it by remembrance of death, and make it grasp its own evanescence. Let it grieve at the turning of ages, the succession of nights and days."

⟪ar-quote:أَعرِض علیهِ أخبارالماضین وذکرهُ بِما أصابَ مَن کانَ قَبلُکَ مِن الاوَّلِین ۔⟫

"Present to him the accounts of those who have gone before, and remind him of what befell the nations of old."[^cite-15]

Go sometimes to the cemetery. You will find that graves are piled upon graves—there is no space left to dig a new tomb. These were once men who broke the sacred and the forbidden to erect mansions of twenty and thirty *kanals* (large plots). They lied, they deceived, they transgressed every boundary of human decency. Yet today they sleep in four feet of earth. This is the end of the world; this is the destiny all must face.

Mawlānā ʿAlī has said:

⟪ar-quote:و سِر فِی دِیارِهِم فانْظُر فی آثارِهِم ۔⟫[^cite-16]

"Walk through their dwellings and observe the traces they have left behind."

⟪ar-quote:أَنتَقِلُو مِن دارِ الغُرور وَنَزلُو⟫—"They have departed from the house of deception and descended into the house of ruin and perdition, and there is no return from the abode of the hereafter."

Wander through the ruins—those shattered dwellings—and observe their desolation. See what they accomplished! How long did they remain in this world? And now observe: their caravans have moved from the house of delusion to the house of poverty, ruin, and destruction. Do you find any trace of them today?

My children: one day you too shall perish. One day you shall sleep beneath the dust forever. What, then, shall you do?

⟪ar-quote:لا تبع آخرتک بِالدنیا⟫—"Do not sell your hereafter for the sake of this world!"[^cite-17]

My children, there will come a day when the world stands on one side and the hereafter on the other. On one side, truth; on the other, falsehood. On one side, Allah; on the other, the world. In that hour, when you must choose, if you have any wisdom, save the hereafter—let the world go.

⟪ar-quote:لا تبع آخرتک بِالدنیا⟫—"Do not sell your hereafter for the sake of this world!"

If you must abandon the world, abandon it—for death will release you from it. But do not abandon the hereafter! Hold fast to the hereafter! The will of the father is being watered by the poison of transgression and bowed beneath the weight of spears.

## Junaid Baghdadi and the Girl's Circumambulation

One day Junaid al-Baghdādī thought to himself: the sanctuary grows crowded during the day; why should I not perform the *ṭawāf* (circumambulation) at night, when few are present? Upon arriving at the House of Allah, he observed a young woman preceding him in circumambulation, reciting verses of such passion and longing that his heart was moved:

| ⟪ar-quote:أبىَ الحُبُّ أنْ يَخفىٰ وكَمَ قَدْ كَتَمتُهُ⟫ | ⟪ar-quote:فأصْبَح عِندى قَدْ أناخَ وَطَنَّباَ⟫ |
| --- | --- |
| Love refused to be hidden, though I concealed it much; now it has encamped within me and pitched its tent. |
| ⟪ar-quote:إذَا اشْتَدَّ شَوقِى هاَمَ قَلبى بِذِكرِهِ⟫ | ⟪ar-quote:فَإن رُمتُ قُرباً مِنْ حَبِيبِى تَقَرَّباَ⟫ |
| When my passion grows intense, my heart yearns at the memory of him; when I seek nearness to my beloved, he draws near to me. |
| ⟪ar-quote:يَبدُو فأفْنىٰ ثُمَّ أحيىٰ لَهُ بِه⟫ | ⟪ar-quote:وَيُسعِدُنِى حتَّى ألَذَّ وأطْرَبا⟫ |
| At his appearing, I dissolve; then he revives me through himself. He delights me until I find joy and rapture. |

She wept as she recited: "I struggled to hide this love, yet I could not. Love has encamped within my heart and taken it captive. When my longing grows, my heart yearns for him. When I seek him, he draws near. At the sight of him, I perish; then through him, I am restored to life. He cherishes me, and my desire for love increases."[^cite-18]

Even a scholar of Junaid al-Baghdādī's stature failed to comprehend her meaning. In anger, he demanded: "O girl, what nonsense are you speaking here in the House of Allah?" The young woman turned to him with reproach and said:

⟪ar-quote:لَولا التُّقىٰ لَم تَرِنى⟫ ⟪ar-quote:أَهجر طِيبِ الوَسَنِ⟫
⟪ar-quote:إنَّ التُّقىٰ شَرَّدَنى⟫⟪ar-quote:كما تَرى عَن وَطَنى⟫
⟪ar-quote:أفرَّ مِن وَجدِى بِهِ⟫ ⟪ar-quote:فَحُبُّہ هَيِّمُنِى⟫[^cite-19]

"It is fear of God that brings me here—else you would not see me. It is piety that has driven me from the comfort of my home, just as you see. In His love, I flee from my own suffering; yet His love holds me in thrall."

Then she continued: "Do not misunderstand my verses, Shaykh. It is this very love that compels me to be here. Otherwise, I would rest peacefully at home. The love of the One I seek has torn me from my homeland. His love has robbed me of sleep; in His love alone do I find my sorrow and my joy."

Junaid al-Baghdādī was stunned. She was speaking of one reality, yet he had perceived another entirely. Then the girl asked him: "Whom do you circumambulate?"

He answered: "The House of Allah."

At this, she laughed aloud and spoke:

⟪ar-quote:يَطُوفُونَ بِالأَحجاَرُ يَبغُونَ قُربَةٌ إليَكَ ؛⟫⟪ar-quote:وَهُم أَقسىٰ قُلُوباً مِنَ الصَّخَرِ⟫
⟪ar-quote:فَلَو أَخلَصُوا فى الوَدُّ غاَبَت صِفاَتُهُم⟫ ; ⟪ar-quote:وَقاَمَت صِفَاتُ الوَدَّ للَحقِّ بِالذِّكرِ⟫[^cite-20]

"They circumambulate stones, seeking nearness to You; yet their hearts are harder than the very rocks. If they were sincere in their love, their attributes would fade away, and the attributes of love alone would remain, devoted to Truth through remembrance."[^cite-21]

Junaid al-Baghdādī then understood: this girl surpassed him in the knowledge of Allah (maʿrifat Allāh). He fell to the ground in prostration of his misunderstanding. When he raised his head, the girl had vanished.

## Bohaira the Monk's Witnessing

Allah has addressed all the worlds, pebbles, stones, creatures great and small, birds, leaves, and trees, proclaiming: "These are My beloved, My cherished ones."

When the Messenger was but twelve years old, he traveled to Syria on a business journey with his uncle, Abū Ṭālib. As they journeyed through Zarqāʾ, there dwelt a monk named Bahīrā (Bohaira) in his monastery. The monk observed something extraordinary: a boy walking amid the caravan with a cloud overshadowing his head—a cloud that moved with him through the heat and dust of the Arabian desert.

Everyone gathered in the shade of a tree to rest from the burning sun. Bahīrā asked Abū Ṭālib: "Are all these people present?" Abū Ṭālib replied, "Yes, except for one child who tends the camels." Bahīrā said, "Bring the child to me!" When the boy approached and sat down, something miraculous occurred: the very branches of the tree shifted forward and cast their shadow upon his head alone, though all others sat in blazing sun.

The greatness of this youth was perceived by clouds and by trees—yet we fail to comprehend it. The trees of Syria recognized him as *al-nabī al-khātim*, the seal of the prophets, the final messenger of God. They knew him as the crown of humanity, the Beloved of Allah, the bearer of Paradise—Muzammil, Muddaththir, Ṭā-Hā, Yāsīn—all his sacred names.[^cite-22]

## The Messenger and the Doe

The Prophet heard a voice calling: "O Messenger of Allah!" He turned, but saw no one. Again the voice came: "O Messenger of Allah!" And this time, as he looked about, he beheld a doe tied to a tree with a rope around her neck. The creature was calling to him, crying out: "O Messenger of Allah, my companion has captured me. I am resigned to Allah's decree—I know what fate awaits me. But I have small children at home, and they are hungry. If you would permit me, I could go and nurse them. I swear I shall return to you."[^cite-23]

The Prophet asked: "And if you do not return, what then?"

The doe replied: "O Messenger of Allah, if I do not return, God will cast me into the fire of Hell. How could I break an oath made before the Messenger of Allah?"

At these words, the Prophet untied the rope and sat in the place where the deer had been bound. For it was a covenant, a sacred trust—and nothing of such weight could be surrendered without the owner's permission. He remained, watching and waiting.

After a time, he saw her returning swiftly from the mountains, her hooves striking the earth with urgency. She came and stood before him and said, "O Messenger of Allah, I have returned!" The Prophet then bound her again gently and waited for her owner to appear.

When the man arrived and found the Messenger sitting in his deer's place, he was astonished. "O Messenger of Allah, what brings you here?" the man asked. The Prophet replied, "I have undertaken a trust for you. This creature made a covenant with me that she would return, and she has kept her word. Can you release her to me?"

The man responded with joy: "O Messenger of Allah, everything belongs to you! I would be honored beyond measure to gift her to you."

The Prophet then untied the rope from about the doe's neck. He gazed upon her with tenderness and spoke:

⟪ar-quote:اِذْهَبِى بِأَهْلِكِ⟫—"Go to your family."[^cite-24]

The doe bowed her head in gratitude, then moved forward slowly. She paused, turned, bowed her head again, stepped onward, turned once more, bowed a final time, and disappeared into the mountains—her heart full of thanksgiving to the Messenger of Allah. And the Messenger spoke the eternal truth:

⟪ar-quote:الصِّدْقُ يَنْجِي وَالْكَذِبُ يُهْلِكُ⟫—"Truth saves; lies destroy."[^cite-25]
