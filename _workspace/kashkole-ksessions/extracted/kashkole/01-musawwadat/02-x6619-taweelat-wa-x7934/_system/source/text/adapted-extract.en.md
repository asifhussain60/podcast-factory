## Ominous Days and Dates of the Year

### Seven Inauspicious Dates Within Each Month

⟪ar:امیر المومنین⟫ The traditions of the Commander of the Faithful further specify seven unlucky dates that recur throughout every month, universally regarded as days of caution:

**Inauspicious Dates of Every Month**  
3 · 5 · 13 · 16 · 21 · 24 · 25

The wisdom underlying this counsel is practical: by exercising vigilance during these intervals, one may ward off impediment and secure the blessing of divine aid in one's endeavours. Prudent Muslims are therefore advised to abstain from the following activities on these dates:

| 1 | Commencing new work or enterprise |
| --- | --- |
| 2 | Undertaking travel or journeying |
| 3 | Donning new garments |
| 4 | Contracting marriage |
| 5 | Digging a well |
| 6 | Excavating or building foundations |
| 7 | Purchasing property or dwelling |
| 8 | Attending the court of a ruler |

Wednesday, too, is traditionally held to be inauspicious. Observance of precaution on this day yields similar benefit.

## The Year of Kabithah—The Dual State of Dhu al-Hijjah

The Divine Word declares:

> ⟪quran 55:5⟫
> ⟪ar:‏ٱلشَّمْسُ وَٱلْقَمَرُ بِحُسْبَانٍۢ ‎⟫
> *The sun and the moon run on their fixed courses (exactly) calculated with measured out stages for each.*
> — **Qur'an 55:5**

> ⟪quran 10:5⟫
> ⟪ar:‏هُوَ ٱلَّذِى جَعَلَ ٱلشَّمْسَ ضِيَآءًۭ وَٱلْقَمَرَ نُورًۭا وَقَدَّرَهُۥ مَنَازِلَ لِتَعْلَمُوا۟ عَدَدَ ٱلسِّنِينَ وَٱلْحِسَابَ ۚ مَا خَلَقَ ٱللَّهُ ذَلِكَ إِلَّا بِٱلْحَقِّ ۚ يُفَصِّلُ ٱلْآيَتِ لِقَوْمٍۢ يَعْلَمُونَ ‎⟫
> *It is He Who made the sun a shining thing and the moon as a light and measured out its (their) stages, that you might know the number of years and the reckoning. God has created all this with deliberation.*
> — **Qur'an 10:5**

Both the sun and the moon move in their appointed orbits. God has ordained the lunar circle around the solar path—a relationship neither diminishes nor transgresses. This celestial arrangement allows humanity to distinguish the passage of years and the reckoning of seasons.[^cite-1]

Although the Almighty has calculated the trajectories of both luminaries with perfect precision, many remain perplexed by the lunar cycle's inner and outer manifestations. The moon traverses twenty-eight distinct stations (*manzil*, pl. *manazil*) in its monthly course. One might therefore expect twelve months of twenty-eight days each throughout the year; yet the moon's actual period—some twenty-nine and a half days—introduces a fractional remainder.

The lunar year thus contains 354 days (29.5 × 12), a sum that cannot be divided evenly into integral months. God has therefore ordained that some months possess thirty days while others contain twenty-nine, creating a rhythmic alternation through the twelve months:

| 30 days (complete) | 29 days (incomplete) |
| --- | --- |
| Muharram al-Haram | Safar |
| Rabi' al-Awwal | Rabi' al-Akhir |
| Jumada al-Ula | Jumada al-Akhira |
| Rajab | Sha'ban |
| Ramadan | Shawwal |
| Dhu al-Qa'dah | Dhu al-Hijjah |

The fractional surplus—those accumulated half-days—possesses singular significance. Across the twelve months, these 1.5-day fractions accumulate to eighteen days (1.5 × 12 = 18), a measure equivalent to the holiest days of the year.[^cite-2]

Minute variations in the lunar day's duration—fragments of hours and minutes—gradually accumulate until, over two to three years, they form a complete day. When this occurs, Dhu al-Hijjah, normally a defective month of twenty-nine days, becomes perfected with thirty days. The year thereby extends from 354 to 355 days, and such a year is called a *Kabithah* (abundant year).

**The Duality of Dhu al-Hijjah**  
29 · 30  
Defective · Perfect (Year of Kabithah)

Thus does the least increment—the minute and the hour—through patient accumulation achieve perfection.

### Hazrat Hurr: The Martyr of Karbala

Hurr ibn Yazid al-Riyahi occupied a station of paradox in the tragedy of Karbala. Initially, under the command of Ibn Sa'd, he was appointed to obstruct Imam al-Husayn (peace be upon him) upon the plains of Karbala—a role of constraint and opposition. Yet in that very moment, he prayed the noon prayer (*salat al-Zuhr*) behind the Imam, receiving from him a blessing that would transmute his entire being. When the moment of battle arrived, Hurr became foremost among the martyrs of the Imam, the first to shed his blood in that sacred struggle.

Such was his transformation: from one who detained the Imam to one who preceded all others in sacrifice. In him resided the duality of the *Kabithah* year—the fractional increment that ripens into wholeness.

#### The Headband of Hurr

When Hurr fell wounded upon the field and cried out in anguish, Imam al-Husayn hastened to him. The Imam beheld blood flowing freely from Hurr's brow. From his own garment, the Imam drew forth a cloth and bound it upon that noble forehead, stanching the flow. In that moment, the Imam spoke words of ineffable tenderness: "Your mother erred not in naming you Hurr—the Free One. You are free in this world, and free in the hereafter!" [^cite-3]

The people of Banu Asad later retrieved his body and conveyed it to a place near Karbala, where they interred him. His shrine endures there to this day.

Three centuries passed. A king of Persia, Abbas al-Saqafi, journeyed to visit the tomb of Imam al-Husayn and, from thence, proceeded to the sepulcher of the noble Hurr. A thought seized him: perhaps, he reasoned, had Hurr not detained the Imam, the tragedy of Karbala might have been averted. Impelled by this reflection, he resolved to examine the state of Hurr's mortal remains. He commanded his servants to excavate the grave.

To his astonishment, Hurr's body lay incorrupt within a coffin of marble—as though preserved, like the prophets of old. When Abbas opened the sepulcher, he perceived that the blessed form remained unchanged, and the headband, that sacred cloth bestowed by the Imam's own hand, remained bound upon the brow.

Abbas advanced to remove the cloth, intending to cleanse the brow beneath. But as his fingers touched the binding, a voice—neither of flesh nor wholly of the unseen—issued forth: "Abbas! I received this cloth as a gift from Imam al-Husayn, sealed with the blood of my devotion. Would you strip from my head that precious treasure? This bandage is my greatest wealth!"

At those words, Abbas withdrew his hand, seized with reverence and awe. He caused the grave to be sealed anew and erected above it a magnificent dome, inscribing upon it the Imam's own declaration: *"Ma akhta'at umuka idh sammaitka Hurran—anta Hurrun fi 'l-dunya wa 'l-akhirah!"* ("Your mother did not err in naming you Free—you are free in this world and in the Hereafter!")

**The Essence Manifested**: Just as the fractional hours and minutes, through their patient gathering, ascend into wholeness and perfection, so too did Hurr, who began as one constrained by duty to oppose, transform through the grace of the Imam into the first and foremost of martyrs. His progression moved from the imperfect to the perfect, from restraint to liberation eternal.

### John: The Ethiopian Martyr of Karbala

The martyrs of Karbala came forth one by one, receiving the Imam's permission to advance into the field of sacrifice. Among them was one whom tradition calls John (*Juhn*), an Abyssinian enslaved to the Imam's household. His appearance was dark, and his perspiration bore an acrid odor that offended the senses. With deep reverence, he approached and kissed the feet of Imam al-Husayn, beseeching leave to enter the fray.

The Imam, perceiving his intent, said: "You are not of Arabia but of Abyssinia. You have already served your master abundantly—such service suffices. Why would you imperil your life for us?"

At these words, John's countenance transformed. His voice trembled with devotion: "O Imam! Do you not wish a black skin to mingle with a white? Do you fear that my foul sweat would sully the fragrance of yours?"

Moved by such humility and yearning, the Imam granted him permission. John advanced into battle and fell a martyr. When those gathering the fallen came upon his body, witnesses beheld a luminous radiance emanating from his face—his visage no longer dark but radiant. The odor that once was foul had become perfumed, as though the very earth received the sweetness of his sacrifice.[^cite-4]

**The Essence Manifested**: He too bore the duality of the *Kabithah* year. From degradation and darkness—the enslaved, the scorned for his skin and his sweat—he ascended through martyrdom into luminescence and fragrance. The fractional became whole; the imperfect, perfected.

### Maulana Salman al-Farsi

From the distant lands of Persia came Salman, bearing no wealth but an insatiable hunger for truth. He journeyed to the Prophet Muhammad (may peace and blessings be upon him), and through assiduous study and devotion, he advanced so rapidly in the sciences of faith that the Prophet himself declared: "Salman is of the *Ahl al-Bayt* (the Household)—Salman has become one of us."[^cite-5]

Here too manifested the duality: a stranger from abroad who became counted among the very members of the Prophetic House. The fractional hour accumulated into the whole day of belonging.

His spiritual rank among the *Bab* (doors, i.e., the hierarchical gates of the daʿwa, the divine summons) became so lofty that none of his peers in any subsequent epoch could attain his station. Even Sayyidna al-Muʾayyad, that supreme preacher and bearer of the Imam's wisdom, confessed: "I stand near to Salman al-Farsi in the ranks of the gates—yet I cannot myself be Salman!" Such was the incomparable grace bestowed upon him. When the time of his death arrived, the *Hijab* (the Veil—that is, the representative of the Imam himself) came to perform the rites, washing his blessed body, shrouding him, and committing him to the grave with his own hands.

### Sayyidna Muhammad ibn Fahd

When Sayyidna Jaʿfar ibn Sulayman departed from India for Yemen, bearing with him a text of initiation for Muhammad ibn Fahd, the ordinary course might have been this: the recipient would remain in a state of incompleteness until such a time as the living master stood before him in presence. Yet here the miracle unfolded otherwise. With the completion of that written text, Sayyidna Jaʿfar became exalted as the supreme preacher (*daʿi* al-daʿwat), and Sayyidna Muhammad ibn Fahd, even in his earthly lifetime, achieved the rank of preacher in his own right. The fractional increment—the letter, the text transmitted across distance—became the day of perfection. Duality resolved into wholeness.

### Imam ʿAli Zayn al-ʿAbidin (peace be upon him)

On the Day of Resurrection, when the ranks of the righteous shall be arrayed, the Prophet Muhammad will gaze upon a figure of such luminous beauty and ask in wonder: "Who is this Zayn al-ʿAbidin?" At that moment, Zayn al-ʿAbidin shall rise and pass through the very ranks of the Imams themselves, ascending beyond that assembly. The Prophet will declare: "*Jhumta huwa alladhī tafakhkhara bi-fawzihi*—This one has surpassed all in his attainment!"

He shall thus ascend from the rank of Imam to that transcendent station called the *Asbaʿiyyat* (the sevenfold splendor). Where once he was counted as but one among the Imams, he shall become glorified above all Imams. The fractional hour has become the perfect day. His duality mirrors that of the *Kabithah* year.

### The Year Within the Standing Form (Qaʾim Haykal)

The entire mystical year unfolds within the Standing Form of the Imam of the Cycle (*Qaʾim-i ʿAli*, in His presence be peace). This Imam-of-the-Age shall be surrounded by twelve supreme spiritual presences, corresponding to and surpassing the twelve months of the year—twelve gates transcendent above all other hierarchies within the communion of believers. Beneath each of these twelve supreme Imams, there shall be (28 or 29.5) lesser spiritual forms (*suwar*, sing. *sura*), each attaining the perfection of Imamate. These subordinate forms correspond to the days of the month.

Yet beyond these ordained Imams and their accompanying forms, there shall emerge in the Temple of the Qaʾim successive spiritual embodiments—forms that maintain their stations and advance perpetually in the Standing Form, ascending toward perfect unity. They shall reach the pinnacle of perfection in the ultimate Day and shall manifest as Imams in human form, precisely as the minutes and hours, through patient gathering, constitute the perfect day. ⟪ar:قائمی ھیکل · 29.5 X 12 = 354 · صورات · امام · +1 · 355⟫

## The Four Orders of Numerical Division

In Arabic mathematical and mystical science, every number is divisible into four fundamental orders (*rutab*, sing. *rutba*), each reflecting distinct perspectives upon the fabric of creation:

| Unit (1) | Decade (10) | Hundred (100) | Thousand (1000) |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Unit | Decade | Hundred | Thousand |

The enumeration commences with unity and ascends through the unit order until reaching nine—the culmination and summary of the unit realm. The number nine marks the completion of a cycle. When one exceeds nine, one enters the order of tens: the decade (*ʿAshr*). The decimal boundary marks a qualitative leap; a new hierarchy of relation commences. Thus, in the spiritual cosmos, nine intellects (*ʿuqul*, sing. *ʿaql*) were established, their stations ordained solely upon the foundation of monotheistic submission. But the tenth rank, entering the decade order, introduces an additional spiritual function: the station of repentance (*tawba*) joins with unification.

The progression unfolds thus. In the palace of unity dwell the ⟪ar:'جثہ ابدائیۃ'⟫ (the primordial essences); in the palace of the decade are stationed the ⟪ar:'حوارین'⟫ (the apostles); in the palace of the hundred, the ⟪ar:'ملائکہ'⟫ (the angels); and in the palace of the thousand, the ⟪ar:'قوائم'⟫ (the pillars) and the ⟪ar:'صاحبان اسبوعین'⟫ (the holders of the weeks).

| 1 | 10 | 100 | 1000 |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Jatha Ibtidaʾiyya (Primordial Essences) | Huwariyyun (Apostles) | Malaʾika (Angels) | Quwaʾim and Sahib al-Asbʿiyn (Pillars and Holders of the Weeks) |

Each order ascends in complexity and scope, reflecting the graduated unfoldment of the Divine order throughout the celestial and terrestrial realms.
