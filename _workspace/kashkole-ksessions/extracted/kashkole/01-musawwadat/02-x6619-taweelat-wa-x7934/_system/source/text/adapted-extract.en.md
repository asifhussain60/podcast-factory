## Inauspicious Days and Dates

### The Twelve Inauspicious Dates of the Year

Tradition records that ⟪ar:مولانا أمیر المؤمنین علی علیہ السلام⟫ — Mawlānā Amīr al-Muʾminīn ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib — enumerated twelve days of the year that the believer should treat with caution, one in each lunar month. They are gathered in the table below.

| Month | Inauspicious Date |
| --- | --- |
| Muḥarram al-Ḥarām | 12 |
| Ṣafar | 20 |
| Rabīʿ al-Awwal | 4 |
| Rabīʿ al-Ākhir | 28 |
| Jumādā al-Ūlā | 28 |
| Jumādā al-Ākhira | 12 |
| Rajab al-Murajjab | 12 |
| Shaʿbān al-Karīm | 26 |
| Ramaḍān al-Ḥarām | 24 |
| Shawwāl | 8 |
| Dhū al-Qaʿda al-Ḥarām | 28 |
| Dhū al-Ḥijja al-Ḥarām | 8 |

### The Seven Inauspicious Dates of Every Month

In a further passage attributed to ⟪ar:امیر المومنین⟫, seven recurring dates of every month are named, on which the same caution is enjoined:

> 3 · 5 · 13 · 16 · 21 · 24 · 25

After listing them, he closes the passage with a counsel: take precaution on these days, and your affairs will prosper. The following undertakings are accordingly best avoided on such dates:

| 1 | Commencing a new venture |
| --- | --- |
| 2 | Setting out on a journey |
| 3 | Donning new garments |
| 4 | Contracting a marriage |
| 5 | [item unclear in source] |
| 6 | Digging a well |
| 7 | Purchasing a new dwelling |
| 8 | Appearing at the court of a ruler |

Wednesday too is generally counted among the inauspicious days; here as well, prudence and care are said to bring the desired success.

## The Leap Year (Sana Kabīsa) and the Dual Phase of Dhū al-Ḥijja

The two governing luminaries of the visible heavens are themselves witnesses to the order that the verses announce:

> ⟪quran 55:5⟫
> ⟪ar:‏ٱلشَّمْسُ وَٱلْقَمَرُ بِحُسْبَانٍۢ ‎⟫
> *The sun and the moon run on their fixed courses (exactly) calculated with measured out stages for each (for reckoning, etc.). *
> the sun and the moon run in a reckoning.
> — **Quran 55:5 (inline widget)**

> ⟪quran 10:5⟫
> ⟪ar:‏هُوَ ٱلَّذِى جَعَلَ ٱلشَّمْسَ ضِيَآءًۭ وَٱلْقَمَرَ نُورًۭا وَقَدَّرَهُۥ مَنَازِلَ لِتَعْلَمُوا۟ عَدَدَ ٱلسِّنِينَ وَٱلْحِسَابَ ۚ مَا خَلَقَ ٱللَّهُ ذَلِكَ إِلَّا بِٱلْحَقِّ ۚ يُفَصِّلُ ٱلْآيَتِ لِقَوْمٍۢ يَعْلَمُونَ ‎⟫
> *It is He Who made the sun a shining thing and the moon as a light and measured out its (their) stages, that you might know the number of years and the reckoning. Allah did not create this but in truth. He explains the Ayat (proofs, evidences, verses, lessons, signs, revelations, etc.) in detail for people who have knowledge.*
> It is He who made the sun a light, and the moon a light, and determined it in phases, that you may know the number of years and the reckoning. God has created all this with deliberation. He distinguishes His verses for a people who understand.
> — **Quran 10:5 (inline widget)**

Sun and moon move each in its appointed orbit. God has set the moon to circle the sun in a course that does not deviate, and by means of that motion He has given humankind the means to distinguish years and months. Yet though the courses of both luminaries are reckoned with exactness, the apparent and inward movements of the moon remain a matter on which observers stumble.

The moon traverses its twenty-eight stations as it circles the sun. On that basis there are twelve months in the year and twenty-eight days in each month. But because the lunar gait carries a small surplus, the moon completes its circuit in roughly twenty-nine and a half days (29½), so the full year amounts to 354 days divided among twelve months. Since no month can in itself comprise a fractional day, the months are paired: one of thirty (30) days followed by one of twenty-nine (29). God has thus joined two months into a single couple, the thirty-day month belonging to the "house of the male" and the twenty-nine-day month to the "house of the female."

Each month, in effect, holds 29.5 days — that is, 28 + 1.5. Multiplied across the twelve months (29.5 × 12) these yield 354 days. The half-day surplus carries a particular dignity: spread across the twelve months it accumulates to 18 days (1.5 × 12 = 18), a figure equal to the count of the year's holiest days.

| 30 (Complete) | 29 (Defective) |
| --- | --- |
| Muḥarram al-Ḥarām | Ṣafar |
| Rabīʿ al-Awwal | Rabīʿ al-Ākhir |
| Jumādā al-Ūlā | Jumādā al-Ākhira |
| Rajab | Shaʿbān |
| Ramaḍān | Shawwāl |
| Dhū al-Qaʿda | Dhū al-Ḥijja |

Even 29½ days does not exhaust the lunar reckoning, for there remain minutes and hours of further difference. These small fractions accumulate, and over the course of two or three years they combine into a full additional day. In such a year, Dhū al-Ḥijja is lengthened from twenty-nine (29) days to thirty (30), and the whole year becomes 355 days instead of 354.

That is to say: Dhū al-Ḥijja is originally a defective (29-day) month, but the gradual accretion of minutes and hours adds a complete day at last, raising the month from imperfection (*nāqiṣ*) to perfection (*kāmil*). Such a year is called the *kabīsa* (the intercalated, or leap, year). Dhū al-Ḥijja thus possesses two distinct phases — defective in most years, perfect in the year of *kabīsa*.

> **The duality of Dhū al-Ḥijja**
> 29 · 30
> Defective · Perfect (year of *kabīsa*)

This same movement from defect to completion — small increments accumulating until what was imperfect becomes whole — is, in the Fatimid spiritual sciences, the very structure of the soul's ascent. The believer who joins himself to the daʿwa (the summons / the invitation) advances from his *kamāl awwal* (primary perfection) to a *kamāl thānī* (secondary perfection) which his nature alone could not attain.[^cite-1] The figures who follow are exemplars of precisely this passage.

### Ḥurr — Martyr of Karbalāʾ

Ḥurr ibn Yazīd al-Riyāḥī (upon him peace) was the first commander, dispatched at Ibn Saʿd's order, to halt Imām al-Ḥusayn (upon him peace) on the plain of Karbalāʾ. At the time of the noon prayer he prayed behind the Imām; and from that prayer he received such a measure of grace that, when the hour of battle came, he was the first to be martyred fighting on the Imām's side. His state was therefore doubled: in one aspect he was numbered among those who had blocked the Imām's path, and in another he was numbered among his martyrs. He stands, accordingly, as the very emblem of the year of *kabīsa*.

**The bandage on Ḥurr's brow.** When Ḥurr was wounded on the field and called out to the Imām, al-Ḥusayn came to him, saw the blood streaming from his forehead, drew a kerchief from his own garment and bound it about Ḥurr's brow, and the bleeding stopped. At that moment the Imām said: *"Your mother did not err when she named you Ḥurr — you are free (ḥurr) in this world and the hereafter."* The men of Banū Asad afterwards came, carried him a short way from Karbalāʾ, and buried him where his shrine stands today.

Three centuries later, a Persian king named ʿAbbās al-Thaqafī came to visit Imām al-Ḥusayn (upon him peace) and went also to the noble grave of Ḥurr. He reasoned that the Imām had been martyred on Ḥurr's account, and that had Ḥurr not first halted him there would have been no Karbalāʾ. "Let me see," he said, "what state Ḥurr's body is now in," and so he opened the grave. Like the coffin of Sayyidnā Sulaymān, Ḥurr's body was found preserved in a noble bier; when the king lifted its cover he saw the blessed body still in its original form, the bandage still bound about the brow. He intended to loosen it and cleanse the forehead; but as he began to untie it, a voice from the unseen called out: "ʿAbbās! This bandage I received as a gift from Imām al-Ḥusayn after I had shed my blood — would you remove it from my head? It is the greatest of my treasures." ʿAbbās stayed his hand at once. He restored the grave, raised a splendid dome above it, and inscribed upon it the very words the Imām had spoken: *Mā akhṭaʾat ummuka lammā sammatka Ḥurran fa-anta ḥurr fī al-dunyā wa-l-ākhira.*

**The lesson.** Just as minutes and hours accumulate until they make a full day, carrying what was defective into perfection, so Ḥurr — having stood at first with the army of the unbelievers — was joined, through a sequence of shocks and astonishments, to the company of the martyrs, and passed from imperfection to completion.

### Jawn — Martyr of Karbalāʾ

The companions of Imām al-Ḥusayn (upon him peace) were going out one by one to the field, with his leave, to give battle in his defence and to receive martyrdom. Among them came Jawn ibn Ḥuwayy, the freed Abyssinian slave of Mawlānā Abū Dharr al-Ghifārī — that Abū Dharr of whom the Messenger (peace and blessings upon him and his family) had said, *"The earth did not bear nor the sky overshadow anyone more truthful than Abū Dharr."* Jawn kissed the Imām's feet and asked permission to fight. He was of a dark complexion, and the odour of his perspiration was unpleasant.

The Imām said: "You are not of the Arabs, you are of Abyssinia. You have already served your master long, and that is enough. Why should you imperil your life for our sake?" At these words Jawn's countenance suddenly altered, and he said: "O Imām, would you have it that a black man not sleep beside a white, that my unsweet sweat not mingle with your fragrant sweat?" Hearing this, the Imām granted him permission. He fought, and was martyred. It is reported that when the companions came to bury the martyrs they saw a strange light streaming from Jawn's face and a sweet fragrance rising from his perspiration.

Here too the doubled state is manifest: a slave from Abyssinia, dark of face, whose sweat was foul — who came forward, gained martyrdom for the Imām, and emerged with a face illumined and a perfume on his brow. The minutes and hours, joined together, had at last become the day.

### Mawlānā Salmān al-Fārsī

Salmān came to the Prophet (upon him and his family be peace) from Persia, and made such progress in the religious sciences that the Prophet said of him: *"Salmān is of the People of the House; Salmān has become one of our ahl al-bayt."* In him too was a doubled state: he was from the depository of one nation but was joined to the household of another. The minutes and hours, joined together, became the day.

So lofty was the rank of the bāb al-abwāb (the gate of gates, chief of the daʿī / duʿāt — the Summoner / the Summoners) that he attained, that none could equal Salmān in station and majesty throughout the long ranks of the *abwāb*, nor will any do so until the Day of Judgement. Even Sayyidnā al-Muʾayyad says: "Among the ranks of the bāb al-abwāb I stand close to Salmān, but I cannot be Salmān." The Ḥujja of Amīr al-Muʾminīn came to him in miracle, washed and shrouded him and laid him in the grave.

### Sayyidnā Muḥammad ibn Fahd

When Sayyidnā Jaʿfar ibn Sayyidnā Sulaymān left India for Yemen, he conferred the *naṣṣ* (designation) upon Muḥammad ibn Fahd. Ordinarily, when the dāʿī writes such an appointment upon another, the recipient remains as one in attendance until the bearer of the office has passed away. Here it was otherwise. With the completion of the *naṣṣ*, Sayyidnā Jaʿfar became the unique dāʿī of his moment, and Sayyidnā Muḥammad ibn Fahd became the unique dāʿī during his own lifetime as well. In this manner the doubled state was for him too: the minutes and hours were joined, and became the day.

### Imām ʿAlī Zayn al-ʿĀbidīn (upon him peace)

On the Day of Judgement, when the ranks of the saved are drawn up, the Prophet (upon him and his family peace) will call out: "O Zayn al-ʿĀbidīn! Is this Zayn al-ʿĀbidīn?" At that summons Zayn al-ʿĀbidīn will break through the ranks of the Imāms and come forward in joyful swinging step to receive his recompense from God. The Prophet will say: "Such striding belongs to him who is exalted in his attainment." That is to say: Zayn al-ʿĀbidīn will be raised above the bare rank of Imāmate to the highest station of the *sābiqūn* (the foremost). Where formerly he was numbered among the Imāms, he will now be set above them. The day of minutes and hours has, in him also, accomplished its work — and in this respect too he is reckoned among the exemplars of the year of *kabīsa*.

### The Year in the Qāʾimī Haykal

The whole life of the Qāʾim (upon him peace) corresponds to a single year. He will have twelve ḥadd (the upper limit) / ḥudūd of supreme rank — superior to all the other ḥudūd of his daʿwa, and equal in number to the twelve months. Beneath each of these twelve there will be further ḥudūd (in the number 28 or 29½) which will attain the loftier ranks of Imāmate; and these correspond to the days of the month (28 or 29½).

Beyond these Imāms, however, certain other forms will subsist within the haykal (cosmological body) of the Qāʾim (upon him peace). They will remain in their stations and continue gradually to advance within the standing body until they reach perfection on the day of the month — appearing then as an Imām in human stature, exactly as the minutes and hours together complete a full day. ## The Four Orders of Number

There are, in the Arabic reckoning, just four orders into which all number is divided. These four divisions are themselves *ḥaqāʾiq* — fundamental realities — and they refer to the several aspects of order established in the world of being.

| Āḥād (1) | ʿAshrāt (10) | Miʾāt (100) | Ulūf (1000) |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Units | Tens | Hundreds | Thousands |

Counting begins from one and progresses within the order of units until it reaches nine. Nine is the last and the summation of the unit-order. Then comes ten, which marks the inception of the order of tens: at this point the count leaves the unit-order behind (the units, as it were, depart on their journey) and enters the order of tens. A new sequence begins from the decade. Thus, in the spiritual world, ten intellects were established. Within the nine, ranks were ordered solely upon the basis of *tawḥīd* (unification) — the very principle of the *āḥād*. But the tenth received, alongside *tawḥīd*, also the ranks of the order of tens — that is, the principle of the *ʿashrāt* was joined to the principle of the *āḥād* within it.[^cite-2]

If, then, the ⟪ar:’جثہ ابدائیۃ‘⟫ (al-jaththa al-ibdāʿiyya, the originating body of the *ibdāʿ*) belong to the palace of the unit, those who inhabit the palace of the tens are the ⟪ar:’حوارین‘⟫ (the *ḥawāriyyūn*, apostles); those of the hundreds are the ⟪ar:’ملائکہ‘⟫ (*malāʾika*, angels) and the ⟪ar:’قوائم‘⟫ (*qawāʾim*, the standing/instituting figures); and those of the thousands are the ⟪ar:’صاحبان اسبوعین‘⟫ (*ṣāḥibān asbūʿayn*, the lords of the two weeks).

| 1 | 10 | 100 | 1000 |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| *al-jaththa al-ibdāʿiyya* | *al-ḥawāriyyūn* (apostles) | *al-malāʾika* / *al-qawāʾim* (angels / standing ones) | *ṣāḥibān asbūʿayn* (lords of the two weeks) |

[^cite-1]: See Ḥamīd al-Dīn al-Kirmānī, *Rāḥat al-ʿAql*, on the doctrine that the soul's *kamāl awwal* is brought to its *kamāl thānī* only by joining itself to the *ḥudūd* of the daʿwa — the entire structure of ascent from the lower limit (*ḥadd dānī*) to the upper (*ḥadd ʿālī*).
[^cite-2]: Cf. Abū Yaʿqūb al-Sijistānī, *Kitāb al-Yanābīʿ*, on the orders of being as they emanate in tiered ranks from the Mubdiʿ Taʿālā (the Originator, Most High) — and on the tenth principle as the point at which the unitary order yields to a new manifold.
