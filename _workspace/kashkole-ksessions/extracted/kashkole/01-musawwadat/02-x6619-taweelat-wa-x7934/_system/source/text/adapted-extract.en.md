## Ominous Days and Dates of the Year

⟪ar:مولانا أمیر المؤمنین علی علیہ السلام⟫ The tradition records twelve ominous days distributed throughout the Islamic calendar. These dates, according to the teachings preserved in our sources, demand heightened spiritual vigilance:

| Month | The Inauspicious Date |
| --- | --- |
| Muharram al-Haram | 12 |
| Safar | 20 |
| Rabi' al-Awwal | 4 |
| Rabi' al-Akhir | 28 |
| Jumada I | 28 |
| Jumada al-Akhir | 12 |
| Rajab al-Murajjab | 12 |
| Sha'ban al-Karīm | 26 |
| Ramadan al-Haram | 24 |
| Shawwal | 8 |
| Dhū al-Qa'da al-Haram | 28 |
| Dhū al-Hijja al-Haram | 8 |

Beyond these monthly markers, ⟪ar:امیر المومنین⟫ the Commander of the Faithful also identified seven universally inauspicious dates recurring within every lunar month: the 3rd, 5th, 13th, 16th, 21st, 24th, and 25th. The wisdom behind observing such caution lies not in fatalism, but in disciplined preparation. One should refrain from initiating significant undertakings on these dates—the commencement of new enterprises, journeys, the donning of fresh garments, marriage contracts, the sinking of wells, the acquisition of property, or attendance at the courts of rulers.

Wednesday, too, customarily carries inauspicious influence. With vigilant precaution during these periods, one achieves success in one's endeavors.

## The Eight Gates of Paradise

The tradition preserves eight distinct designations for the gateways through which the righteous enter the Divine abode:

1. Jannat al-Mawa (Paradise of Refuge)
2. Dar al-Maqam (the Abode of Standing)
3. Dar al-Salaam (the House of Peace)
4. Dar al-Karama (the House of Honour)
5. Jannat ʿAdin (the Garden of Eternity)
6. Jannat al-Naʿīm (the Garden of Bliss)
7. Jannat al-Kashf (the Garden of Unveiling)
8. Jannat al-Khulud (the Garden of Eternity)

## The Year of Abundance: The Dual Nature of Dhū al-Hijja

The Qur'an affirms:

> ⟪quran 55:5⟫
> ⟪ar:‏ٱلشَّمْسُ وَٱلْقَمَرُ بِحُسْبَانٍۢ ‎⟫
> *The sun and the moon run on their courses by precise calculation.*

> ⟪quran 10:5⟫
> ⟪ar:‏هُوَ ٱلَّذِى جَعَلَ ٱلشَّمْسَ ضِيَآءًۭ وَٱلْقَمَرَ نُورًۭا وَقَدَّرَهُۥ مَنَازِلَ لِتَعْلَمُوا۟ عَدَدَ ٱلسِّنِينَ وَٱلْحِسَابَ ۚ مَا خَلَقَ ٱللَّهُ ذَلِكَ إِلَّا بِٱلْحَقِّ ۚ يُفَصِّلُ ٱلْآيَتِ لِقَوْمٍۢ يَعْلَمُونَ ‎⟫
> *It is He who made the sun a light and the moon a light, and determined for it stages, that you might know the number of years and the count. God created all this only in truth. He makes clear His signs to those who know.*

The celestial bodies move within their appointed orbits, the moon circling in relation to the sun in a harmony never subject to variation. The Originator, Most High (Mubdiʿ Taʿālā), ordained this arrangement so that humankind might distinguish between years and their divisions. Yet despite this divine calculation, uncertainty persists among many regarding the moon's exoteric and esoteric movements.[^cite-1]

The lunar cycle encompasses twenty-eight mansions (manāzil), which correspond to the twenty-eight or twenty-nine and one-half days of each month. Because the lunar month contains twenty-nine and one-half days precisely, the Islamic calendar accommodates this fractional increment through alternation: one month receives thirty days, the next twenty-nine. Thus, by divine arrangement, the year divides into twelve months—six of complete (thirty-day) duration and six of deficient (twenty-nine-day) duration. Together these constitute the lunar year of three hundred and fifty-four days.[^cite-2]

The extraordinary feature lies in those fractional half-days. Multiplied across twelve months, these fractions—(1.5 × 12)—accumulate to eighteen additional days. This very number corresponds to the holiest observances within the sacred calendar. The wisdom here manifests in dual form: each lunar month embodies perfection and imperfection simultaneously. The pattern emerges thus:

| Thirty Days (Complete) | Twenty-nine Days (Deficient) |
| --- | --- |
| Muharram al-Haram | Safar |
| Rabi' al-Awwal | Rabi' al-Akhir |
| Jumada I | Jumada al-Akhir |
| Rajab | Sha'ban |
| Ramadan | Shawwal |
| Dhū al-Qa'da | Dhū al-Hijja |

Yet the lunar reckoning contains yet another subtlety. Minutes and hours accumulate across the months; these fractional components eventually coalesce, within the span of two or three years, into a complete day. When such accumulation occurs, the month of Dhū al-Hijja—ordinarily deficient at twenty-nine days—transitions to thirty days, and the year expands from three hundred and fifty-four to three hundred and fifty-five days. Such a year the scholars term *kabīsa* (the year of abundance or intercalation).

Thus Dhū al-Hijja embodies a remarkable duality:

| Twenty-nine Days (Deficient) | Thirty Days (Complete) |
| --- | --- |
| Ordinary State | Year of *Kabīsa* |

This cosmic principle of fractional increments completing themselves into wholeness finds its spiritual parallel in human transformation.

### Hurr al-Riyāḥī: The First Martyr of Karbalā

Hurr (upon whom be peace) was the first to halt Imam al-Ḥusayn (upon whom be peace) upon the plains of Karbalā, acting under the command of Ibn Saʿd. Yet at the noon prayer (Ẓuhr), Hurr prayed behind the Imam himself. In that moment, such grace descended upon him that when battle was joined, he became the Imam's first martyr—and indeed, his most devoted defender. His condition embodied duality: he was simultaneously among those who obstructed the Imam and among those who sacrificed themselves for him.[^cite-3] Thus he exemplified the principle of the year of *kabīsa*, wherein deficiency becomes perfection.

When Hurr fell wounded upon the battlefield and cried out to the Imam, the Imam approached and beheld blood flowing from Hurr's brow. He drew forth a kerchief from his own garment and bound it upon the wound, stanching the flow. In that sacred moment, Imam al-Ḥusayn declared: "Your mother erred not in naming you Hurr (the Free). You are free in this world, and free likewise in the world to come." The Banū Asad later retrieved his body and interred it near Karbalā, where his shrine stands to this day.[^cite-4]

Three centuries later, a Persian king named Abbās al-Saqafī journeyed to visit the shrine of Imam al-Ḥusayn and proceeded thence to the tomb of the venerable Hurr. A doubt arose within him: had not Hurr's initial obstruction precipitated the tragedy of Karbalā? Seized by curiosity, he ordered the grave opened. To his wonder, the blessed body remained incorrupt, and upon the brow lay the very kerchief bound by the Imam's own hand. When Abbās approached to unwrap it, a voice emerged from the unseen realm: "O Abbās! This bandage I received from Imam al-Ḥusayn as a treasure, purchased by my blood. Would you strip it from me? This is my greatest wealth!" Abbās ceased at once, reburied the sacred remains, and erected over them a magnificent dome, inscribing the Imam's words upon it: *"Your mother erred not, O Hurr—you are free in this world and free in the Hereafter."*

**The Spiritual Principle:** Just as minutes and hours, when gathered, transform deficiency into completeness—from imperfect to perfect day—so too did Hurr ascend from among the army of obstruction to stand as the most exalted martyr, his status elevated from deficiency to perfection.[^cite-5]

### John (Jūhn): The Abyssinian Freedman

While the other companions of Imam al-Ḥusayn advanced toward the field of combat, bearing the Imam's permission to fight and embracing martyrdom, there came an enslaved man from Abyssinia named John, whom Mawlānā Abū Dharr al-Ghiffārī—he whose truthfulness the Prophet (upon him be peace and blessings) praised, saying, "The earth has not borne, nor the heavens overshadowed, a tongue more truthful than that of Abū Dharr"—brought forward. John approached and kissed the Imam's feet, seeking leave to fight. His countenance was exceedingly dark; the odor of his perspiration was foul.

The Imam observed: "You are not of Arabia, but of Abyssinia. You have served your master long and well—this is sufficient for you. Why would you cast away your life for us?" At these words, John's face transformed. With passion he replied: "O Imam, do you wish that a black man should not mingle with the white? Do you forbid my foul sweat to intermingle with your fragrant sweat?" The Imam, moved by this sincerity, granted him permission. John fought valiantly and fell as a martyr. When the burial parties came to lay the bodies to rest, they beheld upon John's countenance a radiance descending from above, and from his flesh issued forth a perfume most precious.[^cite-6]

**The Spiritual Principle:** John's transformation enacted the law of *kabīsa*. He began as a slave of foul appearance and odor—deficient and despised—yet through his devotion to the Imam, he emerged purified, radiant, fragrant, exalted from imperfection to the highest station. Minutes and hours had become the perfect day.

### Mawlānā Salmān al-Fārisī

From Persia came Salmān to the Prophet (upon him be peace), and such was his dedication to sacred knowledge that the Prophet declared: "Salmān is of the *Ahl al-Bayt* (the Household)—Salmān has become one of our Household." Thus he embodied duality: born a foreigner to Arabia, he became of the intimate family of the Prophet. Minutes and hours had merged into the perfect day.[^cite-7]

His station among the *bābs* (the Summoners, duʿāt) reached such heights that none of his fellow *bābs* could attain his rank or glory until the Day of Judgment itself. Even Sayyidnā al-Muʾayyad declared: "I draw near to Salmān al-Fārisī in the hierarchy of the *bābs*, yet I cannot be Salmān." The Commander of the Faithful (Amīr al-Muʾminīn) himself came to him as a miracle, performing the ritual bathing, shrouding, and interment of this most exalted servant.

### Sayyidnā Muḥammad ibn Fahd

When Sayyidnā Jaʿfar ibn Sayyidnā Sulaymān departed India for Yemen, he transmitted to Muḥammad ibn Fahd a text (nass) of designation. Ordinarily, when a dāʿī (summoner) commits such a transmission to writing, the designated successor remains in obscurity until the predecessor's death. Yet here, the principle of *kabīsa* manifested uniquely: with the completion of the text itself, Sayyidnā Jaʿfar achieved the station of supreme dāʿī whilst Sayyidnā Muḥammad ibn Fahd simultaneously ascended to the dāʿī's rank—both perfected in a single moment during their joint lifetime. Thus their dual transformation became the perfect day.[^cite-8]

### Imām ʿAlī Zayn al-ʿĀbidīn (upon whom be peace)

Upon the Day of Rising, when the ranks of the saved are arrayed, the Prophet (upon him be peace) will cry out: "Is this truly Zayn al-ʿĀbidīn? Is this Zayn al-ʿĀbidīn?" At that moment, the Imām will surge forth through the ranks of all the Imāms and will be exalted above them, receiving the divine reward. The Prophet will proclaim: "He who is proud of his success shall it be!" Thus shall Zayn al-ʿĀbidīn surpass the station of Imāmate itself and attain the highest rank of the *aṣbāʿiyyīn* (the Finger-like Ones, a rank of cosmic significance). Whereas once he stood merely as one among the Imāms, he shall henceforth be counted above them all. The fractional parts have become the complete day. Thus he, too, embodies the duality of *kabīsa*, his deficiency transformed into ultimate perfection.

### The Year Within the Standing Form

An entire cosmic year unfolds within the epoch of the Qāʾim al-ʿAlī Dhikr al-Salām (the Standing Imam, bearer of the Divine Remembrance). This year shall possess twelve supreme *ḥudūd* (limits, or divine officers) that transcend all others within the congregation, corresponding exactly to the twelve months of the lunar year. Beneath each of these supreme limits dwell twenty-eight or twenty-nine and one-half subordinate forms (*ṣuwwar*), which shall attain the highest stations of Imāmate. These forms denote the days within each month.

Yet beyond these Imāms exists another order of spiritual forms dwelling within the Standing Form (*al-haykal al-qāʾimī*) of the Qāʾim. These figures shall progress gradually in their appointed stations, ever advancing toward perfection within the *Standing Form*, and shall manifest the rank of Imāmate in human stature. Just as minutes and hours coalesce into the complete day, so shall these lesser forms ascend through accumulation until they achieve the fullness of Imāmatic station.

## The Four Divisions of Number

In the Arabic science of number, four fundamental categories alone subsist. These four divisions constitute the principal realities; they correspond to four distinct stations manifested within the domain of existence and becoming.

Enumeration commences from unity and proceeds through the single-digit realm until it reaches nine, the ultimate digit and summary of the unit order. Next arises ten, the threshold that separates the units from the tens. At this crossing, the order transforms entirely; a new sequence of reckoning begins. This principle governs the hierarchy of intelligences (*ʿuqūl*): nine intellects were established in the celestial domain, their orders determined solely by the principle of monotheism (tawḥīd), which is the unit. But the Tenth Intellect—the al-ʿAql al-Awwal (the First Intellect), the Emanator (al-Munbaʿith)—bears the stations of repentance, alongside monotheism itself.[^cite-9]

Just as the unit palace ('*jathā ibdāʾiyya*') gives way to the tens palace, the hierarchies transform accordingly:

| 1 (Unit) | 10 (Tens) | 100 (Hundreds) | 1000 (Thousands) |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| The Primordial Gatherings (*Jathā al-Ibdāʾiyya*) | The Apostles (*al-Ḥawāriyyūn*) | The Angels (*al-Malāʾika*) | The Cosmic Officers (*al-Qawāʾim* / *Aṣḥāb al-Asbūʿiyyīn*) |

Within this numerological system lies the cosmological architecture revealed to the faithful—a geometry of spiritual reality reflected in the order of creation itself.
