## God's Questions to Us

In the Qur'an, the Divine presents us with three fundamental questions that strike at the root of human existence and responsibility.

The first inquiry takes this form: ⟪ar-quote:أَم خُلق من غیر شئی⟫ (Are you born spontaneously, without a Creator?)—a question that confronts the ancient materialist claim that all things arise by chance. Have you become a mere coincidence? Consider the celestial order: Did the sun illuminate itself? Did the moon cool of its own accord? Did the winds grow gentle, the earth spread itself, the mountains establish their foundations, the rivers and oceans commence their flowing—all of this happening by accident? When we examine the intricate patterns of nature—flowers blooming in their season, plants growing from soil—the notion that such harmony emerges without design becomes untenable.[^cite-1] The very question contains within it its own refutation.

The medieval Persian philosopher ⟪ar:جلال‌الدین محمد بلخى⟫ (Jalāl al-Dīn Muḥammad Balkhī, known as Rūmī, died 1273 CE) once encountered the Sufi master Shams Tabrīz at his library. When Shams observed the shelves laden with books and asked dismissively, "What is this?", Rūmī replied with arrogance that these were beyond the visitor's understanding. Shams thereupon removed all the books, casting them into a pool of water. When Rūmī asked in confusion what had happened, Shams replied: "This is what you do not understand"—meaning that mere accumulation of external knowledge, without inner transformation, avails nothing. The lesson resonates: unless we grasp the purposefulness underlying creation, we remain drowning in the detritus of mere learning.

The second question follows: ⟪ar-quote:أَم ھُمُ الخالقون⟫ (Are you yourself the creator of yourself?) Did you paint your own eyes? Did you fashion your facial features, determine your height and complexion, choose your nation, family, region, and country? If you truly possessed such creative power over yourself, then you would be free—free to remake yourself at will. Yet this freedom is patent fiction.

The third and most consequential question is posed: ⟪ar-quote:أَم خُلِق السموات والارض⟫ (Did you create the heavens and the earth?) If indeed you had fashioned all of creation, then do as you wish: build a mosque or a temple; manufacture wine or idol-houses; the choice would be yours alone, and no demand could be made upon you. But if none of these three propositions is true—if you are neither self-originated, nor your own creator, nor the creator of the cosmos—then before you learn the sciences of this world (whether you become engineer, physician, or educator), the first knowledge you must acquire is this: Who is your Creator? Who brought the heavens and the earth into being, and why? Why did He create us? And what does He require of us?

Consider the profound difference between human life and that of animals. Both eat and drink; both build dwellings and raise offspring; both engage in recreation. Yet if our existence amounts merely to these functions, then what distinguishes us from the beasts? We sleep in beds while they rest in forests; we cook our food while they consume it raw; we pursue more elaborate entertainments—but these are differences of degree, not of kind. Animals too perform all such works. Therefore, if we have become merely more comfortable versions of the animals, what purpose has our existence served?

God has granted humanity one faculty that He has withheld from all other creatures: intellect (ʿaql). Why would the Divine bestow intellect if we are merely to employ it in the service of animal functions? If we use our reasoning only to eat and drink more cleverly, to build more elaborate houses, to pursue more sophisticated amusements, then what is the purpose of this intellect at all?

The Qur'an directs our attention to the signs inscribed within creation itself:

> ⟪quran 3:190⟫
> ⟪ar:‏إِنَّ فِى خَلْقِ ٱلسَّمَوَتِ وَٱلْأَرْضِ وَٱخْتِلَفِ ٱلَّيْلِ وَٱلنَّهَارِ لَآيَتٍۢ لِّأُو۟لِى ٱلْأَلْبَبِ ‎⟫
> *Verily! In the creation of the heavens and the earth, and in the alternation of night and day, there are indeed signs for men of understanding.*

Allah concludes this verse with the phrase ⟪ar:'لأولی الألباب'⟫—"for those possessed of intellect." The signs of creation are not addressed to those without understanding; they speak to the reasoning mind. Allah declares: "I have endowed you with wisdom precisely so that you may contemplate the creation of the heavens and the earth, and the changing of night and day." Yet consider: If all of this arose by itself, by blind chance, why does night not precede day? Why does water flow downward and not upward? Why does the earth maintain its velocity and not scatter? A simple automobile does not run by itself; it requires a driver. Shall we imagine then that this vast universe operates automatically, without governance, without a Sustainer?[^cite-2]

The answer is evident: There exists One who creates and maintains. There is a Creator whose will orders all existence.

### Why Did God Create Us and What Does He Want from Us?

Why is it that human desire is never truly fulfilled? People spend their days moving between social media, email, work, and entertainment, yet they experience a persistent loneliness despite—or perhaps because of—these distractions. Why does anxiety lodge itself within the human heart? If wealth were the source of happiness, would millionaires not be spared despair and suicide? Why do the wealthy pursue riches ceaselessly when they already possess abundance? Why do human beings enslave themselves to alcohol and drugs?

Consider the parable of two dogs. An urban dog, well-fed and healthy, encountered a wild dog in the forest. The wild dog, emaciated and hungry, asked: "What do you eat to grow so robust?" The city dog replied: "I eat meat and eggs, drink milk. Come with me, and you too shall grow fat." They walked together from forest to city. As they emerged into the light, the wild dog saw a leash around the urban dog's neck and was horrified. "What is this strap?" he asked. "It is the sign of servitude," replied the city dog. "My master feeds me meat and milk, but I am always bound. Sometimes they free me briefly, but mostly I remain shackled." The wild dog responded: "Keep your meat and milk! I cannot accept this bondage. My hunger in freedom is better than your plenty in chains."

Today, we have mistaken the chains of this world for freedom. We call automobiles and wealth freedom; we call high office and titles freedom; we celebrate fame, honor, praise, and applause as freedom. Yet all the while, invisible fetters bind us to temporary things. The contemporary artist Junaid Jamshed once reflected on his success: "At the peak of my artistic career, I possess everything a Pakistani youth dreams of—yet my heart remains empty. I am like a boat without a shore."[^cite-3] He had spent his youth in relentless pursuit, had moved to distant lands, had accumulated wealth and pleasure both lawful and unlawful, yet the heart found no peace. Every relationship brought disappointment; every friendship, betrayal; every happiness evaporated within moments, giving way to an eternal search for something else.

This craving—this perpetual seeking for "and then"—reveals a fundamental truth: The heart does not seek what the body desires. The heart is not material (it is ⟪ar:'روحانیات'⟫, spiritual in essence); it yearns for what transcends the physical. The heart seeks Allah (⟪ar:'اللہ'⟫) and cannot rest until it finds Him. As the Punjabi mystic poet ⟪ar:خواجہ غلام فرید الدین⟫ expressed it: Where shall a person who thirsts find peace in wine or syrup? Thirst is quenched only by water. Similarly, the soul separated from its Source cannot be satisfied by worldly pleasures.

The great theologian and jurist ⟪ar:علامہ غزالی⟫ (al-Ghazālī) experienced this truth profoundly. Though he held the highest positions—Chief Justice and eminent scholar—his heart remained restless. External sciences, he realized, do not reach the heart. After four years of internal struggle, he abandoned his worldly rank and wandered the deserts for a decade. When someone asked what he had gained from renouncing everything, he replied: "I left Laylā and Saʿdā (worldly pleasures) in search of my true Beloved. As I walked, my courage failed and my feet filled with tears. Then, when I was about to fall, I glimpsed the signs of the Beloved, the marks of my destination. And my heart whispered: 'Persist, persist—just ahead lies the Beloved.' Thus I abandoned the vice-chancellorship and found Allah in return."

These questions, then, form the foundation of our inquiry: Why have you come into existence? Where are you going? Who sent you? And what is the path by which these answers may be discovered?

## Who Is Allah?

⟪ar:ان الحکیم قال⟫:

| **Four Oceans of Human Experience** |
| --- |
| ⟪ar:Al-Dunya Bahr al-Dhanub, wa al-Nafs Bahr al-Shahwat, wa al-Mawt Bahr al-Amal, wa al-Qabr Bahr al-Nadamah⟫ |
| *The world is an ocean of sins; the self is an ocean of lusts; death is an ocean of deeds; the grave is an ocean of regret.* |

By "ocean" here is meant abundance and the characteristic quality of each state. The world, which is ⟪ar-quote:دار السقط اللہ⟫ (the abode of God's turning-away), is a place where one cannot escape the snares of transgression. At every turn, the self presents waves of desire; if a person fails to exercise intellect, the nafs (ego) leads toward destruction. After death, only one's deeds remain—hence one should perform as many righteous acts as possible in this life. In the grave, when divine punishment befalls the soul, regret becomes overwhelming: one wishes ardently that one had refrained from evil deeds, or that one had performed yet more good works.

### Knowledge of God: The Outward and the Inward

What does it mean to *know* God? Knowledge consists of acquiring understanding of a person's station, status, and nature. There are two kinds of knowledge: the outward and the inward. The outward knowledge is to recognize someone through form and appearance—to know one's mother by her face, her features, such that you distinguish her from all other women and call her "mother." This outward knowledge constitutes confession (ikrār).

But the inward knowledge encompasses understanding her station: her suffering in bearing and nurturing you, her sleepless nights devoted to your care, her rights upon you. When such inner knowledge develops, the child begins to love the mother. Love arises from knowledge; as knowledge deepens, love grows, and so too does reverence (taqwā).

Yet Allah, may He be exalted, is so great and elevated that neither outward nor inward knowledge of Him can be complete or exhaustive. The Imam Jaʿfar al-Ṣādiq (upon him be peace) proclaimed: ⟪ar:'اللہ اکبر'⟫ (Allah is greater than all comparison). The existence of God can only be affirmed—that He *is*—but His nature cannot be fully comprehended. So how does one begin the journey toward understanding the Divine? There is but one path: to move toward Allah through His creation. It is through creation that an argument (ḥujja) can be raised before the Creator. We examine first the system of creation in this microcosm (this world), and through it, we come to understand the macrocosm (the vast order that is Allah's creation).

### The Creator's Design: A Parallel with Human Products

When a manufacturing company produces a good, it asks immediately: What is its market value? For whom is it made? In other words, every created object exists for a purpose, and that purpose centers on its use. If a company manufactures something for which there are no buyers, the venture is a loss. When Apple created the iPhone, its goal was to make communication easier for humanity. All business, whether directly or indirectly, is ultimately in service to human benefit. Human beings have always been at the center of creation and commerce. No business is conceived for the convenience of a chair or table; the car was built for human ease. Every accessory of every product exists, directly or indirectly, for human convenience.

When a company produces a good, it places its logo upon it—a mark that identifies the maker. This seal or emblem serves as proof of the creator's authorship.

Furthermore, when an item is manufactured, a user manual is created. Why? So that even a less intelligent person can use the product properly. Apple provides instructions: how to make a call, how to send a message, what not to do (do not drop it; do not wash it in a washing machine). The manual's purpose is to ensure the product's preservation and correct use through both negation and affirmation, so that the user may derive full benefit.

Yet a manual alone is insufficient if people cannot understand it or follow its instructions. This is why Apple appoints representatives who demonstrate all the phone's functions in real conditions. These representatives show practically what is written in the manual, so that potential buyers are assured that Apple has not created something impossible to use. These representatives perform the same functions under varying circumstances: the settings for home, for office, for travel, for sleep when one desires not to be disturbed. By showing these practical examples, they enable humans to learn through observation.

As people master the outward use of the product, some wish to understand its inner workings: How did Apple's engineers construct this remarkable device? To grasp this, one must understand the motherboard, the circuits, the wiring, how microphones and speakers connect. For this deeper understanding, Apple produces a technical manual—but not for ordinary people. This manual is only for those with technical expertise in electronics and mechanics. If you give such a manual to a doctor, professor, or carpenter, it will confuse rather than enlighten them; they may inadvertently damage the device and harm themselves in the process.

In summary, then, every created product embodies five elements: (1) the benefit of the human being, (2) the seal of the creator, (3) the user manual, (4) the representative who demonstrates its use, and (5) the technical manual for those who wish to understand its deepest principles.

### Allah's Creation Follows This Same Pattern

Now consider the vast universe. Allah the Almighty created this great cosmos, and within it, this terrestrial world. For whom did He create it? Three types of creatures inhabit this world: minerals, plants, animals, and human beings. Minerals lie inert, deriving no benefit. Plants grow and nourish animals; absent animals to consume them, plants rot and perish. Did God create the world for animals? Yet animals lack intellect. They cannot organize themselves; they cannot protect one another from predators. A thousand gathered goats cannot produce from among themselves a shepherd to lead them to pasture or protect them from wolves. If the world were made for animals, only the strongest beasts would survive, having consumed the weak. It is inconceivable that Allah Almighty would have set the entire machinery of the universe in motion for an irrational creature like an animal.

There exists one creature to which Allah has granted the essence of intellect (ʿaql)—a faculty by which it can distinguish good from evil and establish dominion over all other creatures. Humanity alone possesses this gift. Through intellect, humans have made minerals and plants subservient to themselves, fashioning from them jewelry, weapons, clothing, and dwellings. They have ruled over animals, using some for food, some for transport, some for labor in farming. Humans are the only creatures that comprehend the Word and speak; no other worldly creature possesses speech—they produce only sounds to convey meaning.

Reason itself declares that Allah created this world for humanity, for only humans can derive comprehensive benefit from its treasures.

If Allah created the world, then this creation must bear His seal and emblem, proving His authorship. That emblem is the testimony ⟪ar:'لا الہ الا اللہ'⟫ (there is no god but Allah). Scholars have written extensively about how this proclamation is imprinted upon every aspect of creation.[^cite-4]

Allah also must have provided a user manual—the Holy Qur'ān—in which affirmation and negation speak to human benefit and harm: "Do this and you will prosper; do that and you will be ruined."

Yet the Qur'ān, like any written text, does not speak by itself. Thus there was need for a representative—one who could not merely recite the Qur'ān but could demonstrate its commands in practice, showing what true worship entails, what moral virtue looks like, what patience and sincerity and courage truly mean, how to act in poverty and in affluence.

But Allah also provided the technical manual: the Imāms of Amīr al-Muʾminīn ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib and his descendants, whose line continues until the Day of Resurrection. These Imāms explained the secrets (asrār) hidden within the outward commands of the Qur'ān and the inner realities (ḥaqāʾiq) concealed within them. They revealed where we came from, why we were sent, what awaits us after death, and where we shall go. They unveiled the connections between the world and the Hereafter, the true reality of prayer, fasting, pilgrimage, and alms—why the Maghrib prayer comprises three cycles while ʿIshāʾ comprises four, why Fajr has only two (if time constraints were the reason, would not the longer morning allow for more cycles?), and the spiritual significance of each prostration.[^cite-5]

Just as technical manuals are not distributed to everyone (the ordinary person cannot benefit from them), these sciences are not given to all. They are disclosed only to those whose minds have ripened and matured, and only to those who have entered the era of the Imām al-Zamān (the Imam of this age). If these sciences were taught indiscriminately, those without the capacity to comprehend would distort them, causing great harm. When people cannot understand something, they reshape it into forms their minds can grasp. History shows that the scriptures of earlier prophets were corrupted—things removed that displeased people, things added that pleased them. Yet the Qur'ān remains preserved after fourteen centuries, precisely because Muslims have not fully grasped its true meaning. Had they understood its realities and to whom it refers, they would have altered it according to their preferences.

### The Signs of God in Creation

Allah has created the universe. Its seal is ⟪ar:'لا الہ الا اللہ'⟫. Its user manual is the Qur'ān. Its representative is the Prophet Muhammad (upon him be blessings and peace). Its technical manual is ʿAlī and the Imāms. We perceive God's existence first through His manual, His representative, and His technical manual. Thereafter, every intellect strives to understand through reason whether God is indeed truly God.

For whom did God create all this? The Qur'ān answers:

⟪ar-quote:هُوَ ٱلَّذِى خَلَقَ لَكُم مَّا فِى ٱلْأَرْضِ جَمِيعًۭا⟫

"It is He who created for you all that is in the earth."

⟪ar-quote:ثُمَّ ٱسْتَوَىٰٓ إِلَى ٱلسَّمَآءِ فَسَوَّىٰهُنَّ سَبْعَ سَمَوَتٍۢ ۚ وَهُوَ بِكُلِّ شَىْءٍ عَلِيمٌۭ⟫

"Then He turned toward the heaven and fashioned them as seven heavens, and He has knowledge of all things."

Having created all this, Allah introduces Himself:

⟪ar-quote:إِنَّنِىٓ أَنَا ٱللَّهُ لَآ إِلَهَ إِلَّآ أَنَا۠ فَٱعْبُدْنِى وَأَقِمِ ٱلصَّلَوٰةَ لِذِكْرِىٓ⟫

"I am Allah; there is no god but I. So serve Me, and perform prayer for My remembrance."

And how does He describe Himself?

⟪ar-quote:ٱللَّهُ لَآ إِلَهَ إِلَّا هُوَ ٱلْحَىُّ ٱلْقَيُّومُ⟫

"Allah—there is no god but He—the Ever-Living, the Self-Sustaining."

⟪ar-quote:لَا تَأْخُذُهُۥ سِنَةٌۭ وَلَا نَوْمٌۭ⟫

"Neither slumber seizes Him nor sleep."

⟪ar-quote:لَّهُۥ مَا فِى ٱلسَّمَوَتِ وَمَا فِى ٱلْأَرْضِ⟫

"To Him belongs all that is in the heavens and all that is on the earth."

What are the limits of His knowledge?

⟪ar-quote:يَعْلَمُ مَا بَيْنَ أَيْدِيهِمْ وَمَا خَلْفَهُمْ ۖ وَلَا يُحِيطُونَ بِشَىْءٍۢ مِّنْ عِلْمِهِۦٓ إِلَّا بِمَا شَآءَ⟫

"He knows what is before them and what is after them, and they encompass not a thing of His knowledge save as He wills."

What is the extent of His dominion?

⟪ar-quote:وَسِعَ كُرْسِيُّهُ ٱلسَّمَوَتِ وَٱلْأَرْضَ ۖ وَلَا يَـُٔودُهُۥ حِفْظُهُمَا وَهُوَ ٱلْعَلِىُّ ٱلْعَظِيمُ⟫

"His Throne encompasses the heavens and the earth, and their preservation tires Him not. He is the Most High, the All-Great."

What is His relationship with us?

⟪ar-quote:هُوَ ٱلَّذِى خَلَقَكُم مِّن تُرَابٍۢ ثُمَّ مِن نُّطْفَةٍۢ ثُمَّ مِنْ عَلَقَةٍۢ⟫

"It is He who created you from dust, then from a sperm-drop, then from a clot of blood."

⟪ar-quote:ثُمَّ يُخْرِجُكُمْ طِفْلًۭا⟫

"Then He brings you forth as a child."

⟪ar-quote:ثُمَّ لِتَبْلُغُوٓا۟ أَشُدَّكُمْ⟫

"Then that you may reach your maturity."

⟪ar-quote:ثُمَّ لِتَكُونُوا۟ شُيُوخًۭاۚ⟫

"Then that you may become old."

⟪ar-quote:وَمِنكُم مَّن يُتَوَفَّىٰ مِن قَبْلُ⟫

"And some of you die before that."

⟪ar-quote:‏هُوَ ٱلَّذِى خَلَقَكُم مِّن طِينٍۢ ثُمَّ قَضَىٰٓ أَجَلًۭا⟫

"It is He who created you from clay, then appointed a term for you."

Allah declares: Strike as many hands and feet as you wish; try as hard as you strive; flatter the powerful of this world; lie and deceive; strive with all your might—you will gain only what I have decreed for you. "I have created the heavens and the earth and all between them, and I alone am Lord of all. You shall receive from My treasury only what I give. Honor comes from Me; humiliation comes from Me."

⟪ar-quote:قُلِ ٱللَّهُمَّ مَلِكَ ٱلْمُلْكِ۔ تُؤْتِى ٱلْمُلْكَ مَن تَشَآءُ وَتَنزِعُ ٱلْمُلْكَ مِمَّن تَشَآءُ ۔ وَتُعِزُّ مَن تَشَآءُ وَتُذِلُّ مَن تَشَآءُ ۖ بِيَدِكَ ٱلْخَيْرُ ۖ إِنَّكَ عَلَىٰ كُلِّ شَىْءٍۢ قَدِيرٌۭ⟫

"Say: 'O Allah, Master of all dominion! Thou givest dominion to whom Thou wilt, and Thou strippest dominion from whom Thou wilt. Thou exaltest whom Thou wilt, and Thou abasest whom Thou wilt. In Thy hand is all good; surely Thou art Powerful over all things.'"

Do you not see the signs of His existence scattered throughout the earth and the sky, and all that lies between them?

⟪ar-quote:وَٱلسَّمَآءَ بَنَيْنَهَا بِأَيْي۟دٍۢ وَإِنَّا لَمُوسِعُونَ⟫

"And the heaven—We have built it with our hands, and We are ever expanding it."

⟪ar-quote:رَفَعَ سَمْكَهَا فَسَوَّىٰهَا⟫

"He raised its vault and fashioned it level."

⟪ar-quote:‏وَأَغْطَشَ لَيْلَهَا وَأَخْرَجَ ضُحَىٰهَا⟫

"And He darkened its night and brought forth its morning light."

The meaning runs deeper than obedience; it speaks to the total orientation of the human being toward the Divine—not through compulsion, but through love and recognition of truth.

## Death

Bismillāh al-Raḥmān al-Raḥīm (In the name of Allah, the Merciful, the Compassionate)

⟪ar-quote:الموت لیس منہ الفوت۔ ان أَقَمتُم أَخَذَکُم، وَ اِن فَرَرتُم مِنہُ اَدرَکَکُم⟫

Death is not a condition one escapes. If you confront it, you will be seized; if you flee from it, it will overtake you.

⟪ar-quote:ھَیھاتَ أَن یَفُوتَ المَوتَ مَن طَلَبَ، أَو یَنجُو مِنہُ مَن ھَرَبَ⟫

It is impossible for one who seeks death to escape it, and impossible for one who flees death to be spared.

⟪ar-quote:أِنَّا قَولَناَ أِنَّا للہ أِقرارٌ علی أَنفُسِناَ بِالمِلْک، وقولنا أنا الیہ راجعون، أِقرارٌ علی أنفُسِنا بِالھُلْک⟫

When we declare "Innā li-llāh" (Verily we belong to Allah), we affirm our recognition of Allah's dominion over us. When we say "wa-innā ilayhi rājiʿūn" (and to Him we return), we affirm our acknowledgment of death and dissolution.

⟪ar-quote:أن الموت لزائزٌ غیر محبوب، وواترٌ غیر مطلوب، وقِرنٌ غیر مغلوب⟫

Death is a guest whom no one welcomes, a visitor who brings no joy, an adversary whom no one can vanquish.

⟪ar-quote:شَوِّقُوا أَنفُسِکُم الی نعیم الجَنَّۃ، تُحِبُّوا الموتَ وَتَمقُتُو الحیاۃَ⟫

Fill your souls with longing for the bliss of Paradise, and you will love death and despise this life.

⟪ar-quote:کُلَّ مُتَوَقِّعٌ آت۔ وَکُلَّ آتٍ قَرِیب۔ وَکُلَّ قَریبٌ دان۔ کُلَّ أَمرءِ لاقَ حِمَامُہُ۔⟫

⟪ar-quote:کیف یَسلمُ من الموت طالِبُہُ؟ کیف تَنسیٰ الموت و آثارُہُ تُذَکِّرُکَ⟫

How can one who seeks death be saved from it? How can you forget death when its signs constantly remind you?

⟪ar-quote:لِکُل نَاجَمَ أَفُولٌ وَ لِکُلِّ نَفسٍ حِمامٌ، وَلكل حَیٍّ مَوتٌ⟫

Every star that rises must set; every soul has an appointed hour; every living thing shall die.

## Creation Through Love

Love forms the foundation upon which both this world and the Divine religion rest—not enmity, not anger, but the principle of love. The entire universe is sustained by love. The moment love ceases, the world itself ceases. 

Hazrat Nasrullāh Sahib explained a profound truth: on the Day of Judgment, each soul will be absorbed in its own fate. The father will not ask about his son; the mother will not inquire after her daughter; the brother will show no concern for his brother. This demonstrates that love—as it exists in this world—will not endure in the Hereafter. Therefore, the absence of love in any moment signals the arrival of that final day. The day in which love has vanished is the day of Judgment itself.

### The Principle of Love in Divine Action

When Pharaoh was drowning, a court jester who had mimicked Moses stood watching the spectacle from the shore. Afterward, when Moses reported to Allah, he complained: "O Allah! You drowned the one who mocked and imitated me, yet You spared the jester who imitated him!" Allah replied: "O Moses! You do not understand the principle of love. I did not drown him because he was your image and form at that moment. To drown the image of the beloved contradicts the principle of love."[^cite-7] This teaching reveals the very heart of creation: Allah loves His creation, and from this love all sustenance and mercy flow.

Nutrition itself operates on this principle. For minerals to become part of a plant, the plant must accept them—must "acknowledge" them. The plant does not force minerals into itself; rather, through recognition and acceptance, the minerals transform into the plant's substance. Similarly, animals do not digest food through force; the food must submit, must be received. A goat cannot eat until it accepts what is placed before it, bowing to consume it. When a person slaughters the goat, the goat submits to this fate. Only through this submission, this acceptance—this acknowledgment—does the animal become part of the human body.

In the same way, for a person to become part of an angel's substance (to achieve the station of the angels), the person must first accept themselves—must declare ⟪ar:'أنا'⟫ ("I am," in the sense of acknowledging one's essence). But this acceptance is possible only through love, never through lustful desire. Lust seeks to take and consume; love seeks to give and transform. In love, there is a recognition of the beloved that permits union; in lust, there is only appetite.

### Allah's Love: The Depths Beyond Measure

A scholar recorded an account from the great theologian: One day, Hazrat ʿĪsā (Jesus, upon him be peace) passed by a mountain where a devotee sat alone in prayer. The worshiper's back had become bent from years of continuous supplication; his body, withered by asceticism, resembled thorns. Jesus asked: "O devoted one, how long have you been engaged in this worship?" The elder replied: "O Prophet of Allah, I have been in worship for seventy years, yet not a single prayer of mine has been answered." The elder then requested Jesus to intercede for him with Allah. Jesus asked: "What do you need?" The elder answered: "I desire only that Allah grant me a particle of His pure love."

Hazrat ʿĪsā raised his hands in prayer, and the supplication of the Spirit of Allah was accepted. When Jesus passed by that same place a few days later, he found the sanctuary in ruins—the ground split open, and the worshiper standing within the crevasse, mouth agape, eyes closed, his gaze fixed upward toward the heavens.

When Jesus inquired what had become of him, the reply came: "O Spirit of Allah! This worshiper asked for a particle of Our pure love. Knowing he could not bear it, We gave him only one part out of seventy thousand parts of Our love. Observe his condition! Tell Us, what would have happened to him had We granted him even slightly more?"

This account unveils a mystery: the heart of the human being, when touched by the Divine love, enters a state beyond the ordinary faculties. The eyes of such a one, closed to the material world, perceive the Divine presence. The mouth, opened in wonder, cannot speak—it can only bear witness to what the heart has discovered. The gaze turns inward, then upward, toward the Source of all being.

The true mark of love is that it is not one-sided but reciprocal—a love that flows in both directions. Allah says:

⟪ar-quote:يَٓأَيُّهَا ٱلَّذِينَ آمَنُوا۟ مَن يَرْتَدَّ مِنكُمْ عَن دِينِهِۦ فَسَوْفَ يَأْتِى ٱللَّهُ بِقَوْمٍۢ يُحِبُّهُمْ وَيُحِبُّونَهُ⟫

"O you who believe! If any of you turn back from your faith, Allah will bring a people whom He loves and who love Him."

The joy of love is realized only when the fire burns in both hearts. The love of Allah belongs alone to those who follow the Messenger (upon him be blessings and peace) in true sincerity. The Qur'ān declares:

> ⟪quran 3:31⟫
> ⟪ar:‏قُلْ إِن كُنتُمْ تُحِبُّونَ ٱللَّهَ فَٱتَّبِعُونِى يُحْبِبْكُمُ ٱللَّهُ وَيَغْفِرْ لَكُمْ ذُنُوبَكُمْ ۗ وَٱللَّهُ غَفُورٌۭ رَّحِيمٌۭ ‎⟫
> *Say: "If you love Allah, then follow me; Allah will love you, and forgive you your sins. Allah is Forgiving, Merciful."*

Love, then, is not a sentiment that stands apart from obedience and action. It is the consummation of faith. When love binds the human heart to Allah, obedience becomes not a burden but an expression of devotion. The lover does not ask "Why must I obey?" but rather "How may I please the Beloved?" In this transformation, the soul finds its true nature, its purpose, and at last, its peace.
