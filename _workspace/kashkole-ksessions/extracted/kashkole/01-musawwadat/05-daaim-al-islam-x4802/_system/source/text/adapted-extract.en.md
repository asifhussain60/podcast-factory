# Pillars of Islam - Fasting

## Fasting

Allah says in the Qur'an:

> ⟪quran 2:185⟫
> ⟪ar:‏شَهْرُ رَمَضَانَ ٱلَّذِىٓ أُنزِلَ فِيهِ ٱلْقُرْآنُ هُدًۭى لِّلنَّاسِ وَبَيِّنَتٍۢ مِّنَ ٱلْهُدَىٰ وَٱلْفُرْقَانُ ۚ فَمَن شَهِدَ مِنكُمُ ٱلشَّهْرَ فَلْيَصُمْهُ ۖ وَمَن كَانَ مَرِيضًا أَوْ عَلَىٰ سَفَرٍۢ فَعِدَّةٌۭ مِّنْ أَيَّامٍ أُخَرَ ۗ يُرِيدُ ٱللَّهُ بِكُمُ ٱلْيُسْرَ وَلَا يُرِيدُ بِكُمُ ٱلْعُسْرَ وَلِتُكْمِلُوا۟ ٱلْعِدَّةَ وَلِتُكَبِّرُوا۟ ٱللَّهَ عَلَىٰ مَا هَدَىٰكُمْ وَلَعَلَّكُمْ تَشْكُرُونَ ‎⟫
> *The month of Ramadan in which was revealed the Quran, a guidance for mankind and clear proofs for the guidance and the criterion (between right and wrong). So whoever of you sights (the crescent on the first night of) the month (of Ramadan i.e. is present at his home), he must observe Saum (fasts) that month, and whoever is ill or on a journey, the same number [of days which one did not observe Saum (fasts) must be made up] from other days. Allah intends for you ease, and He does not want to make things difficult for you. (He wants that you) must complete the same number (of days), and that you must magnify Allah [i.e. to say Takbîr (Allahu-Akbar; Allah is the Most Great) on seeing the crescent of the months of Ramadan and Shawwal] for having guided you so that you may be grateful to Him.*

That is, recite the takbīr (glorification) ⟪ar:عاشر علیہ السلام⟫ for ⟪ar:شَیبَۃُ الحَمْد (مولانا عبد المطلب)⟫ the sake of the Imam ⟪ar:مولانا ابو طالب⟫ of the One who has guided you, so that you may be grateful—that is, to establish those who may serve as your spiritual guides in your assemblies and in your hearts. To establish one's Imāmate (spiritual leadership) in reality is to fulfill the shukr (gratitude) ⟪ar:شکر⟫ of Allah.

In a fundamental sense, a Muslim is one who submits and obeys what is commanded to him. In the esoteric circles, "Muslim" refers to the Imāms who entrust their congregations to their successors in the daʿwa (the summons). ⟪ar:سارم الدین سیدنا ابراھیم بن محمد بن فھد اعلی اللہ قدسہ⟫ speaks in his supplications of those who are ⟪ar:مقیمین نطقا⟫ "established in eloquent witness"—referring to the Imāms (upon whom be peace) who have transmitted the Sharīʿa (divine law) and guidance to their followers.[^cite-1] These are ⟪ar:مولانا ھنید، مولانا ھود، مولانا صالح، مولانا اُد بن اُدد، مولانا خزیمۃ، اور مولانا ابو طالب علیھم السلام⟫—they who showed the path of obedience.

The fast of thirty days in the month of Ramadan cleanses the body of all accumulated waste and corruption that has entered it from the previous lunar period. Once purified, the believer becomes capable of spiritual ascent when the time comes for elevation. Through this cleansing, the faithful are gathered together ⟪ar:تجتمع⟫ and linked ⟪ar:تلحقو⟫ with realms of existence higher than their own. For this reason, in the supplication of visitation, it is said: "O Allah, join our subtlety with their subtlety, and unite our assembly with their assembly." The believers thus orient themselves through prayer toward their celestial counterparts.

When the month of Ramadan commenced, Imām Jaʿfar al-Ṣādiq (upon whom be peace) would counsel his sons to exert themselves in spiritual striving during this month, for in it sustenance is distributed among the creatures, the decree of death for those destined to meet Allah is inscribed, and there occurs a night in which righteous action surpasses the deeds of a thousand months.[^cite-2] Because of the nobility and virtue peculiar to this month, the spiritual essences of believers ascend and descend through the levels of their inner natures—through the khimāʾir (the leavening principles) ⟪ar:خمائر⟫ and khulāṣa (the refined extracts) ⟪ar:خلاصۃ⟫. The luminous forms ⟪ar:صورات نورانیہ⟫ of the angels are made manifest to them, and they experience the ascending and descending ⟪ar:سعود و ھبوط⟫ of divine nearness. There stands in every age an Imām, the Kātib al-Waqt (the Scribe of the Time) ⟪ar:کاتب الوقت⟫, who through occultation ⟪ar:احتجاب⟫ and the Great Eye ⟪ar:عین عظیمہ⟫ determines the appointed term of each soul. Believers should intensify their prayers, supplications, and petitions for forgiveness during these blessed nights, that their Lord might show mercy unto them, pardon their transgressions, augment their sustenance, and extend their lifespan. Compared to all other months, the divine presence of the Imām (upon whom be peace) ⟪ar:امام علیہ السلام⟫ becomes through occultation ⟪ar:احتجاب⟫ more frequent in its manifestations within the Temple ⟪ar:ھیکل⟫ of the hearts, shining forth with greater illumination ⟪ar:اشراق⟫ and luminous forms ⟪ar:صورات نیرۃ⟫ upon the believers—most especially on the Night of Ascendancy, the Night of Power (Laylat al-Qadr) ⟪ar:لیلۃ المعروفۃ (القدر)⟫.

When sins veil a person's heart, the Imām (upon whom be peace) ⟪ar:امام علیہ السلام⟫ withholds his sustenance or abbreviates his appointed span. Yet when he supplicates and seeks forgiveness on that blessed night, Allah pardons him, restores his provision, and extends his fixed term. The decree that the Wise Governor ⟪ar:مدبر حکیم⟫ establishes in this month becomes binding upon the servant to fulfill. Communion with the believers and expenditure of one's wealth in righteousness increases one's sustenance; heedlessness and cruelty diminish it. Kinship and mercy toward one's kinfolk lengthen the lifespan. Know well that the Imam ⟪ar:حجاب امامی⟫ and the Concealed Divine ⟪ar:محتجب الہی⟫ possess absolute authority over all affairs—creation, institution, transformation, alteration, increase, and diminution ⟪ar:أمور، تکوین، أنشا، تبدیل، تحویل، زیادتی و نقصان⟫. As Imām Jaʿfar al-Ṣādiq (upon whom be peace) ⟪ar:امام جعفر الصادق علیہ السلام⟫ declared: "Verily, to Allah belongs origination and will in all things, save only in the Imāmate."[^cite-3]

*The lifespan of each human being is established at fifty-one years and twenty-seven days; yet through virtue and spiritual excellence, this term may be contracted or prolonged.*

In this month, a believer's sustenance increases through prayer, almsgiving, and compassion toward the community of the faithful. The Wise Disposer has the right to bestow upon him either in his lifetime or after his departure, or upon the emergence of the Imāms in the age to come. Because of the distinctive forms of radiance that manifest in this month, believers accumulate within their spiritual essence and faculties—their khimāʾir and jawānib (reservoirs and dimensions)—certain qualities that do not accumulate in other months. Once this month concludes, no such accumulation recurs until the next Ramadan returns.

**Reward of Deeds in the Month of Allah**

Whoever undertakes a single good quality in this month, his reward equals the fulfillment of an obligation. Whoever performs one obligatory act has accomplished seventy obligations. And whoever provides sustenance to a fasting person so that he may break his fast, Allah shall forgive his sins and liberate him from the Fire of Hell.[^cite-4]

The month of Allah is the ninth month of the lunar year, and it corresponds to the ninth cycle—the period of Prophethood (Dūr al-Nubuwwa) ⟪ar:دور نبوی علیہ السلام⟫—which spans three thousand years from the time of Adam until the Messenger of Allah (upon whom be blessings). The reward of a good deed in this month equals that of an obligation, signifying that the recompense for establishing a righteous act in this prophetic era is equivalent to establishing a limit (ḥadd) in all previous eons. The limit exists in counterpoint to the obligation; the obligatory is proof of the act of virtue and submission. Thus, whoever in the time of the Prophet established even one bound whose observance became obligatory ⟪ar:مفترز الطاعہ حد⟫ for all believers, it was as though he had established seventy such limits in the ages preceding him. This understanding is true in its outward expression, inward reality, and transcendent essence.

Whoever absolves a sin (through fasting) in the first period frees his neck from the fire of trial and perdition in that age. Whoever accomplishes this in the month of Ramadan—in the time of the Messenger of Allah ⟪ar:رسول اللہ (ﷺ)⟫—receives a manifold reward.

The meaning of "Iftār" (breaking the fast) is that whoever liberates another from the servitude of sin in a prior age shall himself be freed from the fire of examination in his own era. The khimāʾir—the seminal essence or seed—contains the waste of those destined to appear in their proper time, so that they might manifest themselves when their hour arrives. This is called the khulāṣa, the extracted essence. The particular virtues are drawn forth from the waste, isolated, and given opportunity to develop in their appointed season.

The Messenger of Allah (upon whom be blessings and peace) ⟪ar:لیلۃ القدر⟫ said, "Seek it in the final ten nights." He did not specify the precise date lest the polytheists obtain the blessings and grace of that Night of Power. Thus, through the concealment of the Imam, the rank and station of all the Guides—those from whom the subsequent Imāms emerge—remained veiled. The sanctified Lady, Our Mistress Fātima ⟪ar:مولاتنا فاطمۃ⟫, bears witness that these mysteries are preserved within the hearts of those loyal to the successors of Muhammad.

Within one thousand months there are thirty thousand days with seven hundred twenty thousand hours. Of these, we set aside at minimum ten hours from sunset (Maghrib) until the breaking of dawn (Fajr). One single hour of the Night of Power equals seventy-two thousand ordinary hours; one minute of it equals one thousand two hundred hours. Every instant of this blessed night is of immeasurable worth.

## The Fast Removes Maladies

If the one who fasts does not guard his tongue, his ears, his eyes, and his faculties—does not protect them from that which Allah has forbidden—then his fast shall benefit him not at all. Neither shall his body be purified of filth, nor his soul cleansed of spiritual corruption. The forbidden things in the context of fasting are those who perpetuate false narratives and divisive invocations: those who dwell in heedlessness at all times. In other words, if the fasting person does not restrain his tongue from praising such falsehoods, from hearkening to them, from gazing upon them with satisfaction, and from participating in their assemblies, then he has not truly discharged the right incumbent upon the fast. To look outwardly and inwardly upon forbidden things, to incline one's ear toward them, to praise them, and to act upon them—these generate afflictions and maladies that accumulate upon the soul as spiritual pollution.

Consider: when we trim our nails, they separate from us, yet in the natural cycle they regrow and reintegrate into our body. Although nails are of our substance, they cannot remain with us once they become diseased or deformed; if they persisted, they would harm the entire body. Likewise, the waste matter in our house does not originate from strangers but from the very items of our own household. Yet we must expel it. Those substances came to us through the relationships we cultivated; we became magnets for them through our associations. When the connection and its karmic consequences are exhausted, separation occurs naturally. Similarly, when the Imām removes those among his followers whose spiritual perfection has expired—whose time within his Temple has concluded—he extracts them and entrusts them to those of lower rank for further cultivation, that they might nurture themselves and thereby become qualified to rejoin the sacred precinct.[^cite-5]

## Four Persons Whose Fast Is Not Accepted

Imām Jaʿfar al-Ṣādiq (upon whom be peace) ⟪ar:امام جعفر الصادق علیہ السلام⟫ taught that the fast of four categories of people is not acceptable:

| Category | Description |
| --- | --- |
| 1 | ⟪ar-quote:لا صیام لمن عصی الامام⟫ Whoever disobeys the Imām (upon whom be peace) |
| 2 | ⟪ar-quote:و لا صیام لعبد الناصل حتی یرجع⟫ A slave who flees from his master until he returns |
| 3 | ⟪ar-quote:و لا صیام لامراۃ ناشزۃ حتی تتوب⟫ A woman who does not fulfill her husband's rights until she repents |
| 4 | ⟪ar-quote:و لا صیام لولد عاق حتی یبر⟫ A child who is ungrateful to his parents until he makes amends |

**⟪ar:لا صیام لمن عصی الامام⟫ — Whoever Disobeys the Imām**

Whoever disobeys the Imām or violates his covenant gains no benefit from concealing his substance through abstinence. Neither are the impurities of his body removed, nor are the corruptions of his soul cleansed and purified. In the era of the Messenger of Allah ⟪ar:رسول اللہ (ﷺ)⟫, there were those ⟪ar:عبد الاَّت بن عثمان (ابو بکر)⟫ who were disobedient—they had studied the final teachings of Jesus and grasped the inner meanings of fasting and kept them concealed. Yet their practices benefited them not, for they had spurned the Messenger of Allah.

**⟪ar:ولا صیام لعبد ناصل⟫ — The Fugitive Slave**

A slave is a person who has been placed under spiritual discipline by his guardian because of transgression and the corruption of his deeds, and from whom progress has been withheld. Should such a person break free from his master's authority and separate himself, his fasting shall not benefit him whatsoever, for his confinement and his yearning for reunion are themselves his true fast. Among the fugitive slaves ⟪ar:عمرو⟫ were those who had been prevented from advancing through the ranks of the Prophet's religion. Yet they displayed themselves as persons of rank.

**⟪ar:ولا صیام لأمراۃ الناشبۃ⟫ — The Disobedient Wife**

It is not acceptable for a woman who disobeys her husband until she repents. A woman is one of the virtues of the people of rank, an innocent guide who prevents herself from communion with her husband. The only remedy for this person is to present her repentance to her master and to persuade him, so that she may progress by reestablishing her bond with him.

The disobedient woman ⟪ar:عائشہ⟫ who is counted among the wives of the Prophet (upon whom be blessings) and ⟪ar:عبد اللہ ابن رواحا⟫ who is married to the Muhammadan nature ⟪ar:ذات محمدیۃ⟫ does not truly stand in this bond.

**⟪ar:ولا صیام لولد عاق⟫ — The Ungrateful Child**

The child of ingratitude is one who grows up within the daʿwa (the summons) of his guardian, but whom the guardian then expels because of his disobedience. The fast of such a person benefits him not at all. Among such ungrateful children ⟪ar:زبیر بن العوام⟫ were those who grew up within the summons of the chosen one, yet were thereafter excluded because of their rejection of the covenant of ʿAlī.

These are the people who have opposed the Imāms and shall be held accountable before the Master of the Age ⟪ar:قائم علی ذکرہ السلام⟫. Their fasting shall not avail them, and they shall be put to death along with their followers, hung upon palm fronds. Just as the verses of the Imāms reach the believers with illuminating grace, so does the number of the Guides of each Imām cast a light upon these four and upon all their adherents, drawing them into their circle through divine invitation. In like manner, all the people of rebellion, the people of concealment and apostasy, are recompensed in this world for their outward and manifest deeds, yet have no share in the world to come.

## The Seven Obligatory Fasts and the Twelve Recommended Fasts

The Majālis al-Mustanṣiriyya ⟪ar:مجالس مستنصریہ⟫ in the fourteenth session presents the categories of fasting thus:

### The Seven Obligatory Fasts (Sawm al-Farāʾiḍ)

| Number | Description |
| --- | --- |
| 1 | The thirty days of Ramadan in the full month of Ramadan every year |
| 2 | The fast of one who did not fast in Ramadan without excuse and does not possess a slave to emancipate as expiation |
| 3 | The fast of one who kills a believer without justification or intent, if he does not possess a slave to set free |
| 4 | Two consecutive months of fasting as expiation for *ẓihār* (the pronouncement by which one forbids to oneself one's wife by calling her resemblant to one's mother or sister), for one who does not possess a slave to free |
| 5 | Three days of fasting as expiation for breaking an oath, if one cannot afford to feed a poor person or lack the means to clothe and liberate a slave |
| 6 | Ten days of fasting if one does not have access to a sacrifice offering or cannot afford to purchase one, during the rite combining ʿUmra with Hajj |
| 7 | The fast of a vow made to Allah |

### The Twelve Recommended Fasts (Sawm al-Sunan)

| Number | Description |
| --- | --- |
| 1 | *Iʿtikāf* (spiritual seclusion in the mosque for three consecutive days) |
| 2 | Fasting throughout the month of Shaʿbān |
| 3 | Fasting on the first Thursday of every month |
| 4 | Fasting on the middle Wednesday of every month |
| 5 | Fasting on the last Thursday of every month |
| 6 | The Days of the Bright Ones (the 13th, 14th, and 15th of each lunar month) |
| 7 | The Fast of David (upon whom be peace), in which one fasts on alternate days |
| 8 | Fasting on Friday, provided it is combined with Thursday or Saturday |
| 9 | Fasting on the Day of Arafāt, for one not engaged in the Hajj pilgrimage |
| 10 | Fasting for a child when he reaches seven years of age |
| 11 | If one hunts while in the state of *iḥrām*, then one must fast in expiation or perform the prescribed act, according to the command of the Imām (upon whom be peace)—these fasts vary in frequency and obligation; there is no fixed enumeration of them |
| 12 | Fasting as expiation for shaving one's head due to some affliction or pain |

## The Glorifications of the Night of Eid al-Fitr

Maulānā ʿAlī teaches: "You suppose that there exist three divisions of time—past, present, and future. Yet it is not so! Time consists only of the present and the future, for every present moment transforms into the past with each instant that passes.

All the blessed nights possess great dignity, yet none surpasses the principle that the night precedes the day in every cycle. The glory of the day always rests upon the night; the night is blessed because of its day. The Imām is the exemplar of the day, and the night is his proof—his ḥujja (the argument). Their proofs are so elevated and blessed that their favors upon the believers commence at night. For this reason, the blessed nights begin immediately after sunset. Through these sacred supplications, proofs, and Qur'ānic chapters, our Imām (upon whom be peace) reaches us just as the transition from night to day is established. Thus it has been said: the night precedes the day in rank, yet that precedence does not diminish the rank of the day.[^cite-6]

The takbīr (glorification) becomes obligatory from the time of sunset. The takbīr is recited during the obligatory prayers. The takbīra is the mysterious wisdom of Allah repeated five times: "Allāhu Akbar" (Allah is Most Great). In the testimony of faith—"There is no god save Allah"—the letters alif and lām appear five times together. This is the meaning of "Five matching five." Five multiplied by five yields twenty-five.[^cite-7]

There are ten *tasbīḥ*s (glorifications) of Allah, which together represent five times two—each evoking the sanctification of the divine throne and the perfection manifest in creation.
