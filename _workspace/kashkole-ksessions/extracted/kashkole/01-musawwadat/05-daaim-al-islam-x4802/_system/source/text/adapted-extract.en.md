# Pillars of Islam - Fasting

## Fasting

Allah says in the Qur'an:

> ⟪quran 2:185⟫
> ⟪ar:‏شَهْرُ رَمَضَانَ ٱلَّذِىٓ أُنزِلَ فِيهِ ٱلْقُرْآنُ هُدًۭى لِّلنَّاسِ وَبَيِّنَتٍۢ مِّنَ ٱلْهُدَىٰ وَٱلْفُرْقَانِ ۚ فَمَن شَهِدَ مِنكُمُ ٱلشَّهْرَ فَلْيَصُمْهُ ۖ وَمَن كَانَ مَرِيضًا أَوْ عَلَىٰ سَفَرٍۢ فَعِدَّةٌۭ مِّنْ أَيَّامٍ أُخَرَ ۗ يُرِيدُ ٱللَّهُ بِكُمُ ٱلْيُسْرَ وَلَا يُرِيدُ بِكُمُ ٱلْعُسْرَ وَلِتُكْمِلُوا۟ ٱلْعِدَّةَ وَلِتُكَبِّرُوا۟ ٱللَّهَ عَلَىٰ مَا هَدَىٰكُمْ وَلَعَلَّكُمْ تَشْكُرُونَ ‎⟫
> *The month of Ramadan is that in which was revealed the Qur'an, a guidance for mankind and clear proofs for the guidance and the criterion (between right and wrong). So whoever of you sights the month, let him fast it; and whoever is ill or upon a journey, then an equal number of other days. Allah intends for you ease, and He intends not for you hardship, and that you complete the number, and that you magnify Allah for that He has guided you, and that you may give thanks.*

The divine imperative to "magnify Allah" (*takbīr*, تكبير) for His guidance is not mere verbal utterance, but rather an acknowledgment of sovereignty through obedience. This command concerns the establishment of the Imam's authority—to recognize and uphold that sacred trust which Allah has vested in those guides whom He has chosen. To fulfill the obligation of gratitude (*shukr*, شكر) is to honor the Imams in one's gatherings and households, thereby actualizing their spiritual authority in the realm of practice.

A Muslim (*muslimūn*, مسلمون), in its deepest signification, is one who yields his will entirely to the divine command and recognizes the binding authority of the Imams, who distribute the Sacred Law (*sharīʿa*, شريعة) and its esoteric meaning (*taʾwīl*, تأويل) to their congregations.[^cite-1] In the words of Imam Jaʿfar al-Ṣādiq (upon him be peace), fasting during the month of Ramadan possesses a sanctity transcending ordinary disciplines. This month witnesses a particular descent of divine mercy, wherein the sustenance of every believer is apportioned and the term of each soul is inscribed in the Divine Presence. Within these nights moves the Imam of the age (*imām al-ʿasr*, إمام العصر), the Timekeeper (*kātib al-waqt*, كاتب الوقت), who—through his concealment (*iḥtijāb*, احتجاب)—determines the span of human life by his very luminous form (*ṣūrat nūrāniyya*, صورة نورانية).[^cite-2]

Believers should therefore multiply their petitions, supplications, and prayers for forgiveness during these sacred nights, beseeching their Lord for mercy, remission of sins, and increase in sustenance and lifespan. The Imam's manifestation during Ramadan exceeds that of all other months; his presence becomes radiant and transparent to the faithful, most particularly upon the Night of Power (*laylat al-qadr*, ليلة القدر). Because of human transgression, the Imam may withhold sustenance or curtail the appointed term; yet when one turns in sincere repentance and supplication on that blessed night, Allah forgives and restores both provision and life-span. The Imam, as the wise disposer (*mudabbir ḥakīm*, مدبر حكيم) of the age, fixes the duration of each person's existence, and thereafter that fixed period becomes binding. It is through connection with the believers and expenditure of wealth in righteous deeds that sustenance increases; through negligence it diminishes. Kinship itself prolongs the span of years.

Know that the Imam holds complete authority (*sulṭān tāmm*, سلطان تام) in all realms—creation, generation, transformation, increase, and diminishment—save one alone. As Imam Jaʿfar al-Ṣādiq taught, "Verily, to Allah belong origination and will in all things, excepting the Imamate" (*aʿlā al-ʿaliyyin*, على العليين).[^cite-3]

The span appointed for human life is fifty-one years and twenty-seven days; through virtuous conduct and noble deeds this period may be increased or diminished. In this month especially, the believer's sustenance grows through prayer, charity, and compassion toward the faithful. The Wise Disposer grants this provision either in the present life or thereafter, in the realm beyond. By reason of the distinctive forms of luminescence (*ṣūrat nīriyya*, صورة نيرية) that descend in Ramadan, believers accumulate spiritual substances in their innermost hearts that do not gather in other months; once this month concludes, such accumulation ceases until the next Ramadan returns.

### Reward of Deeds in the City of Allah

Whosoever adopts a single virtuous quality in this month, its reward shall equal that of an obligation; and whosoever fulfills one obligation shall have fulfilled seventy. Whoever provides sustenance to a fasting person—enabling him to break his fast—Allah shall forgive his sins and liberate him from Hellfire.[^cite-4]

Ramadan is the ninth month of the year, and it corresponds to the ninth cosmic cycle (*dawr*, دور)—the prophetic era (*dawr al-nabawī*, دور النبوي). From Adam until the Messenger of Allah there elapsed three thousand years. In that remote age, a virtuous deed earned reward equal to an obligation; now, in this prophetic epoch, the reward for a single virtuous act equals an obligatory deed—meaning that the spiritual recompense for establishing a virtue in this era parallels the establishment of a divine boundary (*ḥadd*, حد) in former ages. The obligation stands in contrast to the voluntary act, while the obligatory act itself is evidence of obedience and virtue. In the prophetic era, whoever established a single soul bound by obligation—one for whom obedience became incumbent—achieved the spiritual station of one who had established seventy sacred boundaries in the epochs that preceded. This truth holds in its outward form, in its esoteric meaning, and in its ultimate reality.

Whoever remits a sin in the first cosmic cycle shall have his neck freed from the fire of trial in that epoch; and whoever does likewise in the city of Ramadan—in the prophetic age—shall receive manifold reward. The meaning of *iftār* (breaking the fast, افطار) extends thus: whoever emancipates a person from sin in a former era shall himself be freed from the fire of examination in that same time.

The waste (*khimīra*, خمیرہ) of those destined to reappear in future ages is now fermented, that they might manifest in their appointed time. This substance is called the abstract (*tajrīd*, تجريد). The special virtues are extracted from this waste, separated, and given opportunity to develop according to their season.

The Messenger of Allah (upon him be the finest peace and blessings) said, "Seek it in the last ten nights"—referring to the Night of Power. He did not specify its date lest the polytheists gain access to its blessings and illuminations, concealing thereby the station of all the Imams from whom every Imam has emerged. There are thirty thousand days in one thousand months, comprising seven hundred and twenty thousand hours. Within this period, we reckon at minimum ten hours from sunset (*maghrib*, مغرب) to the time of dawn breaking. Thus one hour of that night equals seventy-two thousand ordinary hours, and one minute of it equals twelve hundred hours. Every instant of that night is precious beyond measure.

## Fasting Removes Illnesses

If the person fasting does not guard his tongue, his ears, his eyes, and his appetite—does not preserve them from what Allah has forbidden—then his fast shall benefit him not at all. Neither shall his body be purified of impurities, nor shall his soul be cleansed of spiritual corruption. The things Allah forbids during fasting concern the multiplication of the summons (*daʿwa*, دعوة), those persons who remain stationed in their habitations at all times. In other words, if the faster (*sāʾim*, صائم) did not restrain his tongue from praising such people, from hearing them, from viewing them with satisfied eyes, and from participating in their gatherings, then he has not truly honored the covenant of the fast.

Looking outwardly and esoterically upon forbidden things, turning one's ears toward them, praising them, and acting upon them—these generate causes and diseases. They create evil waste (*khilāʿ*, خلاع) that attaches itself to the soul. When we trim our nails, they separate from us, yet they grow anew within the body's process. Although nails belong to us, they cannot remain with us at the moment of their separation; should they remain, they would harm the body. Just as the refuse within a household does not come from outside, but from the very items of that household, yet we expel it—so too those things reached us through the relationships we forged, making us magnets for them. When that connection is severed and the debt is paid, separation occurs. Similarly, when the Imam cuts away the expired virtues from his luminous form, he distributes them to those beneath him for refinement, so that they may nurture such virtues and enable their eventual reintegration into the sacred sanctuary.

## Four Whose Fasting Is Not Accepted

Imam Jaʿfar al-Ṣādiq (upon him be peace) said that the fasting of four persons is not acceptable:

| Category | Condition |
| --- | --- |
| 1 | ⟪ar-quote:لا صیام لمن عصی الامام⟫ Whoever disobeys the Imam (a.s.) |
| 2 | ⟪ar-quote:و لا صیام لعبد الناصل حتی یرجع⟫ A slave who flees from his master until he returns |
| 3 | ⟪ar-quote:و لا صیام لامراۃ ناشزۃ حتی تتوب⟫ A woman who neglects her husband's rights until she repents |
| 4 | ⟪ar-quote:و لا صیام لولد عاق حتی یبر⟫ A disobedient child toward his parents, until he shows filial piety |

**⟪ar:لا صیام لمن عصی الامام⟫ — Whoever Disobeys the Imam**

Whoever disobeys the Imam or severs his covenant receives no benefit from concealing his substance. Neither the impurities of his body are cleansed from him, nor are the impurities upon his soul purified. In the prophetic age, certain persons studied from the final days of Jesus' dispensation and grasped the inward meaning of the fast, yet kept this knowledge hidden. But their actions shall not avail them, for they disobeyed the Messenger of Allah.

**⟪ar:ولا صیام لعبد ناصل⟫ — The Fugitive Slave**

A slave is one whom his guardian—his master—has confined to a particular spiritual station because of certain obligations and deficiencies in deed, thereby arresting his progress. Should such a person breach the barrier set by his guardian and flee, his fasting shall not benefit him whatsoever, for his confinement and contrition are themselves his true fast. Those slaves called fugitives (*ʿamr*, عمرو) are so named because they were prevented from advancing through the ranks of the Prophet's religion; yet they presented themselves as persons of rank.

**⟪ar:ولا صیام لأمراۃ الناشبۃ⟫ — The Disobedient Wife**

It is not acceptable for a woman who disobeys her husband until she repents. Such a woman is one of the virtues belonging to people of rank, who are without blemish, yet she prevents herself from association with her husband. The sole path remaining to her is to present her remorse before her master and seek his forgiveness, so that through renewed connection she may progress spiritually.

The disobedient woman counted among the wives of the Prophet (peace be upon him)—she who was married to the Prophet yet not truly joined to his spiritual reality (*dhāt muḥammadiyya*, ذات محمدية)—stands in this station.

**⟪ar:ولا صیام لولد عاق⟫ — The Disobedient Child**

The child called ʿāq (*ʿāq*, عاق) is one who grew up in the summons of his guardian, yet was expelled from that summons because of his disobedience. The fasting of such a person shall benefit him not at all. Such were the children of renunciation who grew up in the summons yet were excluded because of their repudiation of the covenant with ʿAlī.

These are the ones who confronted the Imams; they shall be called to account before the Qāʾim (the Riser). Their fasting shall not benefit them, and they shall be slain alongside their followers and hung upon palm branches. Just as the verses of the Imams are issued to believers, so too the Imam's light casts upon these four and their followers, and they labor to include themselves in his circle. Similarly, all people of rebellion, of hidden renunciation and open opposition, receive recompense in this world for their outward deeds alone; they have no share in the Hereafter.

## The Seven Obligatory Fasts and Twelve Sunna Fasts

In the fourteenth gathering of the Majālis al-Mustanṣriyya, it is stated:

### Fasts of the Seven Obligations

| Number | Category |
| --- | --- |
| 1 | Thirty days of Ramadan in the city of Allah, every year |
| 2 | For one who neglected fasting in the month of Ramadan without excuse—if he cannot free a slave, he must fast |
| 3 | For one who kills a believer without intention—if he cannot free a slave, he must fast |
| 4 | Two consecutive months of expiation for *ẓihār* (declaring one's wife unlawful as one's mother or sister)—if one cannot free a slave |
| 5 | Three days of expiation for breaking an oath, if one cannot provide food to a poor person or lacks means to clothe and free a slave |
| 6 | Ten days, if one cannot obtain a sacrifice or afford to purchase it, during *ʿumra* combined with the Hajj |
| 7 | Fasting of vows |

### Fasts of the Twelve Sunnas

| Number | Category |
| --- | --- |
| 1 | *Iʿtikāf*—seclusion in the mosque for three consecutive days |
| 2 | Fasting throughout the month of Shaʿbān |
| 3 | Fasting on the first Thursday of every month |
| 4 | Fasting on the middle Wednesday of every month |
| 5 | Fasting on the last Thursday of every month |
| 6 | The *Days of Brightness* (the thirteenth, fourteenth, and fifteenth of each month) |
| 7 | The *Fast of David*, wherein one fasts every other day |
| 8 | Fasting on Friday—provided such a fast is combined with Thursday or Saturday |
| 9 | Fasting on the Day of ʿArafāt, for one not performing the Hajj |
| 10 | Fasting for a child when seven years of age |
| 11 | Fasting as expiation for hunting whilst in the sacred state (*iḥrām*, احرام), or according to the Imam's command; these fasts increase or diminish in frequency without fixed enumeration |
| 12 | Fasting as expiation for shaving one's head due to pain or ailment |

## The Takbīr of the Night of ʿĪd al-Fiṭr

Mawlānā ʿAlī said: "You suppose that time comprises three dimensions—past, present, and future. It is otherwise. Time is only the present and the future; the present, at every instant, transforms into the past.

All blessed nights possess great majesty, yet the Night stands in relation to the Day such that night is never antecedent to day. The glory of the day ever rests upon the night; the night is blessed because of its day. The Imam embodies the day, and his proof (*ḥujja*, حجة) is the night. Their arguments are so elevated and blessed that their favors toward believers commence at night. For this reason the blessed nights begin immediately after sunset, and through these luminous supplications, proofs, and chapters our Imam (upon him be peace) becomes accessible to us, just as he is reached through night's passage into day. That is why it has been said that night is never antecedent to day—meaning that although night comes before day in sequence, its rank never exceeds the day's.

*Takbīr* (the utterance of *Allāhu Akbar*, "God is Most Great") becomes obligatory from the setting of the sun. It shall be recited during the five obligatory prayers. The *takbīr* is the subtle wisdom of Allah; one repeats five times *Allāhu Akbar*. In the testimony *Lā ilāha illā Allāh* ("there is no god but Allah"), both the *alif* and the *lām* are mentioned five times. ⟪ar-quote:خَمسَۃٌ بِعِضَاءِ خَمسَۃ⟫ Five paired with five.[^cite-5] Five times five yields twenty-five.

There are ten praises of Allah—*tasbīḥ*, تسبيح—which are five doubled, or five times two.
