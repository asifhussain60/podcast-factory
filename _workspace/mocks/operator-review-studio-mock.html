And the soul, having returned from its descent into the realm of generation, recovers the recognition of its principles. it the recognitionit recovers the recognition of these principles is the work of the intellect, not of the imagination. Al-Kirmānī affirms this throughout the Rāḥat al-ʿAql.

The seven hierarchical positions of the daʿwa, when traced back to their epistemological foundations, mirror the seven heavenly spheres in form but not in substance. This is the central insight of the present treatise — a claim that the author defends through three converging lines of evidence drawn from earlier Ismāʿīlī sources.

Concerning the matter of the jazāʾir, the author writes:

"the seven jazāʾir, when properly understood, do not refer to geographical islands but to the seven preacher-positions that structured the daʿwa hierarchy in the Fatimid period. Each jazīra corresponded to a particular region under the spiritual jurisdiction of a single ḥujja."

Al-Sijistānī describes this arrangement in his Kitāb al-Maqālīd, noting that the hierarchy was originally instituted by the Imām and was preserved through successive cycles of concealment and manifestation.

The reader should note here a subtle distinction: the geographical jazāʾir of the early Arab geographers (such as al-Idrīsī) are not what the author means. angles fell at the wrong momentangels appeared at the appointed moment in the spiritual order — and so the daʿwa was reconstituted around these new positions.

The second author of the treatise, whose voice shifts noticeably at this juncture, introduces the doctrine of tawḥīd al-mubdiʿ — the unity of the Originator. This shift is grammatically marked by the change from third-person reportative ("they say...") to first-person assertive ("I affirm that...").

The doctrine itself, while consonant with the earlier sections, introduces a vocabulary not previously deployed: ibdāʿ rather than khalq, al-mubdaʿ al-awwal rather than al-ʿaql al-fāʿil. This vocabulary belongs to the Ismāʿīlī Neoplatonic tradition of al-Sijistānī and his successors.

Three implications follow. First, the author distinguishes between origination and creation. Second, origination has no temporal anteriority. Third, what is originated does not require a substrate.

Returning to the question of the spheres, the author asserts:

"the celestial spheres are intelligences, and their motions are acts of contemplation. To assign to them physical bodies, as the philosophers of Greece have done, is to miss the symbolic dimension entirely."

This reading, while idiosyncratic, is consistent with the broader Ismāʿīlī tradition that reads the corpus of Greek natural philosophy through an esoteric lens. The intellectus agens of Aristotle becomes, in this tradition, the First Originated.

A further passage, which the OCR has handled imperfectly, reads as follows:

"the human soul ascends through the same seven stages that mirror the descent of the spheres. these stages are not literal but symbolic of the soul's purification."

The marginal annotations in the manuscript indicate that this passage was contested even at the time of its composition.

The treatise turns next to ethical practice — the cultivation of the virtues that prepare the soul for its ascent. The author lists four cardinal virtues, but renames them: al-fiṭra, al-rawiyya, al-ʿiffa, and al-ʿadāla. Note the substitution of al-fiṭra (innate constitution) where the Greek tradition would have phronēsis (practical wisdom).

This substitution is doctrinally significant. The author signals that the prerequisite for virtue is not deliberation but the recovery of an original disposition that the soul has forgotten in its descent.
