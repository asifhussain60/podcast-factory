# Glossary for Listeners — *The Master and the Disciple*

**[NotebookLM Instruction]** When a term from this list appears for the first time in any episode, briefly define it using the "Plain-English meaning" column. Do NOT define it again in later episodes — the audience has heard it. Pronunciation comes from `01-pronunciation-guide.md`; this file is for meaning, not sound.

---

### Allaah
- **Plain-English meaning:** God. The single, transcendent Creator of Islamic theology.
- **Why it matters in this series:** The series is structured around Allaah's justice — the idea that God does not leave creation without guidance.

### Sharee-ah
- **Plain-English meaning:** The revealed path of religious practice. The visible order of obligations and prohibitions.
- **Why it matters:** Ch 6 distinguishes Sharee-ah (the body of practice) from religion itself (the spirit). Listeners should hear: Sharee-ah is the body, religion is the soul; both are needed.

### Zaahir / Baatin / Baatin al-Baatin
- **Plain-English meaning:** Outward / inner / innermost. Three levels of meaning the book teaches through the egg analogy (shell / white / yolk).
- **Why it matters:** The whole series turns on the question of how outward practice points to inner meaning, and how the innermost is reached only through disciplined interpretation under rightful guidance.

### Taa-weel
- **Plain-English meaning:** Disciplined interpretation. The return of a symbol to its rightful inner meaning.
- **Why it matters:** Ch 7's road-sign analogy applies here. Taa-weel is not free symbol-reading; it requires authority. Without that authority, interpretation becomes guessing.

### Wilaayah
- **Plain-English meaning:** Spiritual allegiance to the rightful guide; rightful devotion. Has three components: affirmation with the heart, declaration with the tongue, action with the limbs.
- **Why it matters:** Ch 2 establishes Wilaayah as the seventh principle — the "air" that encompasses the other six pillars of practice.

### Imaam
- **Plain-English meaning:** The rightful spiritual guide. In Ismaili and broader Shia tradition, the Imaam is the living inheritor of the prophetic light.
- **Why it matters:** The book argues that guidance is living, not archival. The Imaam is the figure who makes living guidance possible.

### Hujjah
- **Plain-English meaning:** Literally "proof"; the rank that supports the Imaam, conveys his teaching in detail, and bears authoritative witness.
- **Why it matters:** The hierarchy of Imaam → Hujjah → Daa-ee is the chain through which guidance reaches the seeker.

### Daa-ee (plural: Du-aat)
- **Plain-English meaning:** "One who calls" — a teacher who calls others toward guidance with the permission of the Imaam.
- **Why it matters:** The scholar in this book is a Daa-ee. The Du-aat are the working layer of the Ismaili tradition's pedagogical structure.

### Naa-tiq
- **Plain-English meaning:** A "speaker" prophet — one of the seven prophets who brings a new revealed path. (Adam, Noah, Abraham, Moses, Jesus, Muhammad, and one more in some Ismaili traditions.)
- **Why it matters:** The Naa-tiqs are the inner aspect of the seven heavens in Ch 2's cosmology.

### Wasi / Asaas
- **Plain-English meaning:** Wasi: trustee; the rightful successor of a Naa-tiq. Asaas: foundation; in Ismaili tradition, the inaugural Wasi (Ali is the Asaas of the Prophet Muhammad's cycle).
- **Why it matters:** Without the Wasi, the Naa-tiq's teaching has no living interpreter; without the Asaas, the cycle has no foundation.

### Naqeebs
- **Plain-English meaning:** Twelve chiefs who assist a Naa-tiq.
- **Why it matters:** The twelve mansions of the Zodiac are their outward symbol in Ch 2's cosmological mapping.

### Adab
- **Plain-English meaning:** Refined conduct. The discipline that lets the seeker bear knowledge without misusing it.
- **Why it matters:** Ch 4 turns on Adab. The young man's silence before the greater Shaykh is Adab in action.

### Hudood ad-deen
- **Plain-English meaning:** The ranks of religion. The recognized levels of authority within a religious community.
- **Why it matters:** The book treats hierarchy as discipline under rightful guidance, not personality worship — see Ch 4 listener objection.

### The Living Rope (Hablullaah)
- **Plain-English meaning:** The rope of Allaah. The continuous, unbroken connection from divine light to the seeker, transmitted through rightful authority.
- **Why it matters:** This is the master image of Ch 7. The rope descends from above the seeker but reaches him where he stands; he must hold it while still climbing himself.

### The Oath / Covenant
- **Plain-English meaning:** A formal binding into responsibility, loyalty, and protection. Not a sentence repeated by the tongue — entry into a structure.
- **Why it matters:** Ch 2 makes the oath the door through which deeper knowledge enters. The book's central claim is that knowledge cannot precede covenant.

### The Jewel and the Glass
- **Plain-English meaning:** The metaphor in Ch 6 distinguishing verified knowledge (the jewel, tested by jewelers) from imitation knowledge (the glass, accepted because it shines under poor light).
- **Why it matters:** This is the book's primary epistemological metaphor. The seeker is taught how to test claims, not just how to receive them.

### Just Witness (Shaheed Adl)
- **Plain-English meaning:** A divinely-appointed witness on earth. Prophets and their rightful inheritors.
- **Why it matters:** Ch 6 argues that Allaah's justice requires the continuous presence of just witnesses. To say "there is no prophet or guide in our time" is to accuse Allaah of withdrawing justice.

### The City of Knowledge / The Gate
- **Plain-English meaning:** The Prophetic teaching, traditionally attributed: "I am the city of knowledge, and Ali is its gate." Knowledge has a structured entrance; one does not enter from any direction.
- **Why it matters:** The image gathers the whole series — guidance is not free-form spiritual searching; it has a rightful entrance. **Source-integrity note: see `03-source-integrity-notes.md` for verification status.**

### Blind Imitation (Taqleed without inquiry)
- **Plain-English meaning:** Accepting a teaching without examination. The book treats this as injustice both to the seeker and to wisdom itself.
- **Why it matters:** Ch 6 names blind imitation as one of the failures of the religious communities Aboo Maa-lik describes.

### Ka'b Al-Ahbaar
- **Plain-English meaning:** A title indicating recognized stature among scholars. In this text, applied to Aboo Maa-lik to mark him as a respected outsider.
- **Why it matters:** Ch 5 introduces Aboo Maa-lik through this title to signal that he is not a casual figure — his serious examination of Saa-lih's teaching has weight.

### The Twelve Jazāʾir (al-jazāʾir al-ithnāʿashar)
- **Plain-English meaning:** The twelve "islands" or regions into which the classical Fatimid Ismaili da'wa divided the inhabited world. Each region was headed by a chief dāʿī bearing the title *ḥujja*. In the symbolic cosmology of Ch 2, the twelve regions on earth point to the twelve hujjas who assist each Imam.
- **Why it matters:** Ch 2 asserts "twelve regions" but does not enumerate them. The historically attested enumeration is: al-ʿArab, al-Rūm, al-Ṣaqāliba, al-Nūba, al-Khazar, al-Hind, al-Sind, al-Zanj, al-Ḥabash, al-Ṣīn, al-Daylam, al-Barbar. (Sources: Encyclopedia Iranica §"Dāʿī"; Ibn Ḥawqal; Nāṣir-i Khusraw on Khurāsān as a jazīra.)
- **Host enumeration policy:** deliver the list ONCE, in Episode 2. Do not repeat in later episodes.

### The Seven Seas (al-biḥār al-sabʿa) — Yaʿqūbī enumeration
- **Plain-English meaning:** The seven maritime regions known to 9th-century Arab geographers — period-contemporary with Ja'far ibn Mansūr al-Yaman.
- **Why it matters:** Ch 2 calls the seven seas "intermediaries between Allah and the Imams following the Natiq." The Yaʿqūbī list: Sea of Fars (Persian Gulf), Sea of Larwi (Arabian Sea), Sea of Harkand (Bay of Bengal), Sea of Kalāh-bār (Strait of Malacca), Sea of Ṣalāhiṭ (Singapore Strait), Sea of Kardanj (Gulf of Thailand / lower South China Sea), Sea of Ṣanjī (South China Sea).
- **Host framing:** "the seven seas of classical Arab geography known at the time the book was written." Do NOT substitute the Greek/Mediterranean list (Aegean, Adriatic, etc.) — that is not the Ismaili reference frame.
- **Host enumeration policy:** deliver the list ONCE, in Episode 2.

### The Seven Oft-Repeated (al-sabʿ al-mathānī)
- **Plain-English meaning:** Sūrat al-Fātiḥah — the seven-verse opening surah of the Qur'an, recited in every unit of prayer. Ch 2 assigns it as Monday's symbol.
- **Why it matters:** Ch 2 names "the seven doubled ones" as Monday's symbol without identifying it. Without this fill, listeners hear an opaque reference. The cross-tradition consensus (Sunni and Shia tafsīr) is al-Fātiḥah.

### The Asāas
- **Plain-English meaning:** "Foundation" — in Ismaili tradition, the figure who functions as the Naṭiq's living interpreter during his lifetime and becomes the Wasi (trustee) after his death.
- **Why it matters:** Ch 2 references the Asāas without naming whose Asāas. For Muhammad's cycle, the Asāas is Imam Ali, peace be upon him. Implicit in the chapter; explicit in foundational doctrine.

### Hisab al-Jummal (Abjad numerals)
- **Plain-English meaning:** The traditional Arabic-letter numerology system in which each letter has a numerical value. See `content/_shared/arabic/06-abjad-numerals.md` for the full table.
- **Why it matters:** Ch 2 contains explicit letter-count claims (the 7 letters of "kun fayakun"; the 12 letters of "iradah amr bi-qawl") and one cryptic abjad-encoded sphere sequence (B3 in source-integrity notes). The reader / host needs the Abjad table to verify the claims and to handle the cipher.

## Listener-friendly definition rhythm

**[Podcast Host Cue]** The first time you use any term from this list, deliver it in this pattern: pronounce → define → return to the conversation. Do NOT stack three new terms in one sentence. Example: *"The book uses the word Wilaayah — Wi-LAA-yah — which means spiritual allegiance to the rightful guide. Hold onto that as we read this next exchange."*

When a chapter uses several new terms (especially Ch 2), define them across the episode at the moments they first appear, not in a block at the top.
