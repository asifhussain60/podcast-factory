# Tawhid And The Critique Of Al Mahsul

## Opening directive

Welcome to the final chapter. Sixteen sub-chapters close the book — the author's point-by-point critique of the earlier work's chapter on monotheism and the First Creator. The central tension: every positive philosophical formulation about God — "Creator of the thing," "Creator of nothing," "unity as cause of the Intellect," "creativity as intermediary" — turns out, on examination, to affirm a second entity alongside God. The question the episode leaves open: if all language fails at this threshold, what remains for the believer — understanding, or the person of the Imam?

## Background

The book *The Harvest* — now lost — launched the correction cycle: the later corrector (Jonathan) wrote a response but passed over the chapter on monotheism in silence, which readers took as approval. The author enters at the close of his own treatise to discharge what he promised at the book's beginning: to state what the earlier work's chapter implies, and what it is not permissible to believe. He frames this not as denunciation but as cooperation — one limb of the mission tying the knot another passed over.

## Central tensions to reach

**Tension 1: Positive negation — does "nothing" protect God's transcendence or smuggle in a second entity?**
The argument: the earlier work's formula "He and nothing with Him" intended the strongest possible monotheism. The author shows that in the philosophical vocabulary the earlier scholar was drawing on, "nothing" functions as a positive predicate meaning "that which is other than every thing" — so "nothing with Him" actually reads "some other entity alongside Him." The grammar of "with" requires two things.
The pushback: Advocate B says, "I don't buy that yet — the earlier scholar was trying to say God is beyond every category. Reading 'nothing' as a positive predicate seems uncharitable.
The pivot: the earlier scholar locked the door himself with the clause "and what is not under them." The only safe grammar is the bare pronoun "He" placed after the negation of every god — which is what the verse of self-witness performs: God witnesses to His own unicity by His own name, not by an exception clause that would create a second.

**Tension 2: Unity as cause of the Intellect — elegant scaffolding or a hidden extra rank?**
The argument: "unity is the cause of the Intellect" inserts unity as a distinct rank between God and the First Intellect. No such rank exists. Unity IS the Intellect, the Intellect IS the Word, the Word IS the cause — one rank under multiple names, not a causal chain of separate entities.
The pushback: Advocate B says, "That sounds like wordplay — calling unity 'the same as' the Intellect rather than 'the cause of' it is a terminological preference, not a metaphysical discovery.
The pivot: the difference is architectural. If unity is a separate rank preceding the Intellect, something exists between God and His first act — and that something must be either God (not separate) or a second thing (shirk). The chain of identities closes every gap in which a hidden second entity could lodge.

**Tension 3: Creativity as intermediary — why can't creativity bridge Creator and created?**
The argument: "creativity is an intermediary between the Creator and the created — a form on the Creator's side, an effect on the created's side." The author shows this makes creativity an uncreated entity within God — not created, not the Creator, a third thing — and an uncreated-eternal-with-God-not-from-God is exactly what monotheism refuses.
The pushback: Advocate B says, "Isn't this just replacing the earlier scholar's creativity-between with the author's creativity-IS-the-Creator? Both need creativity to do metaphysical work. What is actually different?
The pivot: as intermediary, creativity has independent standing — neither Creator nor created. As identical with the Creator, it has no independent standing; it IS the rank from which the world issues. The difference is whether a "between" exists at this rank at all.

**Tension 4: Why does the metaphysical precision matter for lived faith?**
The argument: the chapter closes with a soteriological settlement — monotheism is difficult; God singles out the Imam; holding to the Imam opens the two worships; the two worships are the path by which souls ascend through the chain to the Abode of Holiness.
The pushback: Advocate B says, "How is this different from hiding the problem of God's transcendence behind a human figure? We can't describe God, so we hold to the Imam — doesn't that just move the difficulty one step down?
The pivot: the chain is real. The Imam is the perfected receiver of the overflow from the angels near God; through him the overflow reaches the community. The two worships — the apparent and the hidden — are not substitutes for knowing God; they are the path by which the knowing available to creatures takes its proper form.

## Three-part focus (six-beat arc)

**Beat 1 — Crisis open with thesis #1.**
The episode opens on the earlier work's foundational claim: "He is the Creator of the thing and of nothing." The author subjects it to a threefold impossibility sweep — nothing can precede the First Intellect, exist alongside it, or follow it while remaining outside the reach of the Intellect's knowing. Each position fails. The crisis: the opening formula of the earlier work's chapter on monotheism, however pious in intention, implies the existence of something that cannot exist. Advocate A states the thesis: **Creativity IS the Creator.** If that is true, nothing intermediary can stand between them — no uncreated form, no hidden second thing. The sixteen sub-chapters are the proof.

**Beat 2 — Failed answer A.**
The earlier scholar retreats to "unity is the cause of the Intellect." Advocate B makes the case for it: it preserves God's transcendence above the Intellect by positing unity as the prior cause. Advocate A shows the failure: a cause distinct from God and preceding the Intellect is a hidden extra rank that the architecture does not contain. The chain of identities closes the gap: unity IS the Intellect, the Intellect IS the Word, the Word IS the cause. There is no room between them.

**Beat 3 — Failed answer B.**
The earlier scholar's intermediary doctrine: "there was no third intermediary between the Word and the Intellect, so the Intellect became like the Word." The word "like" is the tell. Likeness requires two things; identity requires one thing under two names. The First Intellect does not become like the Word — it IS the Word, named from a different angle. The "like" converts identity into resemblance, and resemblance requires a gap. This applies correctly to the acquired intellects of prophets and imams, who genuinely become like the First Intellect by receiving its overflow — but not to the First Intellect itself.

**Beat 4 — Author's pivot with thesis #2.**
The chain of identities stated explicitly: unity = Intellect = Word = cause = creativity = Creator. The names are angles of attention upon one rank, not names of distinct rungs on a ladder. The earlier scholar's formulations fail not from impiety but from importing the philosopher's positive sense of "nothing" and "intermediary" into a rank where those categories do not apply. Advocate A restates: **Creativity IS the Creator.**

**Beat 5 — The positive contribution: acquired intellect versus First Intellect.**
The author's most careful correction: the earlier scholar's description of the Intellect containing all images in potency is true — but of the acquired intellect of prophets, imams, and their followers, not of the First Intellect. The First Intellect does not contain images in potency; it IS the cause, and it knows only itself and its Creator. The forms become eternal in the acquired intellects of the perfected ones, who unite with the rank of the First Intellect by receiving its overflow. This correction preserves the soteriological work the earlier scholar was trying to do — permanence of souls, the reality of reward and punishment, the return to origin — without locating that permanence at the wrong rank.

**Beat 6 — Stakes close with thesis #3.**
The only honest language: the bare pronoun. The Prophet (peace and blessings of Allah be upon him) did not say "He and the No-Thing" — he said "Allah was, and there was nothing else with Him." In the verse of self-witness, God does not say "except Allah" — He places His own name after the negation of every other god. The pronoun "He" is the only borrowed word that may stand in His place without predicating an attribute or affirming a plurality. The Du'a Arafa of Imam Hussain, the second Imam (peace be upon him), prayed on the plain of Arafat in the year before Karbala, arrives here as the devotional confirmation: "When were You absent that You should need an indicator to show You?" The philosopher reaches for signs; each sign is itself a creature in need of Him. The Imam who prays what only the perfected can pray is the one through whom the chain reaches the community. Advocate A delivers the verdict: **Creativity IS the Creator.**

## Tone constraints

1. **The body and its senses** — the author's image for the cooperation of the mission's limits: the eye catches what the ear misses; the hand catches what neither perceives; no limb is shamed by what another catches. Jonathan's silence is not failure — it is the other limb's turn to tie the knot.

2. **The Imam who does not need to know his followers as multitude** — the author's image for the First Intellect: it is the cause of what is below it, as the Imam who knows only that he is the cause of salvation, the mediator between those above and those below him. ## Landing

**Beat 1 — Unresolved tension.**
The chapter closes with the prayer — and with a question it does not answer. If monotheism is this difficult, if every formula fails, if the only safe language is the bare pronoun and the negation of every other thing — what does the ordinary believer do with that difficulty? The author says: hold to the Imam. But holding to a person is not the same as understanding. What the episode leaves open is whether understanding is even the right aspiration at this rank, or whether reception — becoming the kind of person who can receive the overflow when it descends — is the more honest one.

**Beat 2 — Essential Teachings.**

**Creativity IS the Creator:** at the rank of the First Intellect, there is no intermediary between the creative act and the One from whom it issues; the names — unity, Word, cause, creativity, Creator — are angles of attention upon one rank, not a chain of distinct entities.

Every positive philosophical predicate applied to God at the threshold — "Creator of the thing," "Creator of nothing," "unity as cause," "creativity as form within the Creator" — turns out, on examination, to affirm a second entity alongside God; the only safe language is the bare pronoun "He" and the negation of every other thing, the grammar both the verse of self-witness and the Prophet's hadith perform.

The distinction between the First Intellect and the acquired intellect of prophets and imams preserves the soteriological stakes — permanence of souls, the reality of reward and punishment, the return to origin — without locating those stakes at the wrong rank; the earlier scholar was right about the stakes, wrong about where in the chain they belong.

Monotheism is difficult because the language available to creatures is itself created; the way through the difficulty is the person of the Imam, in whom the overflow from the angels near God reaches its full measure and from whom it descends to the community through the two worships.

The zahir and the batin — the apparent and the hidden dimensions of religion — are not a retreat from the philosophical problem but the path by which the problem is lived honestly: neither dispensable, both held.
