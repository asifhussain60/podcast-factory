## Definition of the Shīʿa

⟪ar:امیر المؤمنین حضرت علی ابن ابی طالب⟫ said: "Indeed, our Shīʿa (the followers of the Imams) are those who believe in God and His Messenger." They are those who keep the commandments of God, turn away from sins, and act upon what We enjoin upon them. Our Shīʿa are those who consult the sun, the moon, and the stars to determine the times of prayer. Our Shīʿa are those whose lips are parched and whose stomachs are drawn inward from the discipline of fasting—those whose faces bear the imprint of asceticism. He is not of our Shīʿa who usurps the rights of others, who oppresses the people, or who lays hold of that to which he has no claim.[^cite-1]

⟪ar:امام جعفر الصادق علیہ السلام⟫, addressing his companions, said: "I counsel you to fear God, to obey His commands, to abstain from sins, and to render in full the trusts that God has placed in your keeping. I counsel you to deal kindly with those in whose company I have set you, and I commend to you our silent prayers.

"When you act upon the commandments of God which We have enjoined upon you, and when you forbid one another from what God has forbidden; when you deal with people in truth and justice, when you discharge your trusts equitably, when you enjoin the good and forbid the evil—then the people, becoming aware of your goodness, will say: 'These are followers of such-and-such an Imam! May God have mercy upon that Imam—how excellently has he trained his companions, and how fine are the manners he has taught them!' Thus shall they recognise Our grace, and incline toward Our excellence and perfection.

"Whoever falls short in the duty of God by even the slightest measure shall not be granted God's mercy, nor shall he be entitled to the intercession of Muḥammad ⟪ar:مصطفی صلعم⟫ on the Day of Resurrection. Therefore, listen to us and act upon what God has ordained for you. Do not be disobedient to God or to us, opposing what we say. By God, there is no deity but Him.

"Our Shīʿa are of us, and all others will be cast into the Fire. God is worshipped through us. Whoever disobeys us has disobeyed God, and whoever obeys us has obeyed God: our obedience is God's own command upon His creation. God accepts the deeds of a servant only through us, nor does He extend His mercy to anyone save through us. We are the Gate of God and His proof; we are the trustees over His creation, the custodians of His mysteries, and the guardians of His knowledge. He who does not render us our due out of his wealth shall have no share in Paradise."

⟪ar:امیر المؤمنین حضرت علی ابن ابی طالب⟫ said: "Whomsoever God tests the heart of for piety, He will surely cause him to love us with his whole heart; and the one with whom God is wroth and displeased will surely harbour in his breast enmity and hatred toward us. Therefore, whoever loves us must love us with a sincere love—like gold in which there is not the slightest adulteration. And he who hates us shall remain in that hatred. We are the noble ones, born of the noble; our children are the children of the prophets, and I am the leader of all the saints. I am of the army of God and His Messenger, while every other party is of the army of Satan. Whoever wavers concerning us, departs from us, and joins our enemies is not of us. If any of you would know who loves us and who hates us, let him probe the heart: if it harbours love for any of our enemies, let him know that God, His Messenger, and the angels Gabriel and Michael are his enemies—and God is the enemy of those who disbelieve."

⟪ar:امام باقر علیہ السلام⟫ is reported to have said to some of his Shīʿa: "O Shīʿa, hearken to our counsel and grasp our testaments; abide by the covenant we take from our devoted ones. Speak truth and fulfil your oaths. Aid one another with wealth, and love one another with the whole of your hearts. Distribute alms to those in want; hold fast to unity and concord. Treat no one with deceit, forgery, or treachery. Let no doubt enter your hearts after conviction; do not turn back in cowardice after a step taken. Let none of you turn his back upon his beloved ones, nor incline his desire toward another; let your dying be only for them, your works only for God, and your faith and purpose only in your Prophet.

"Among our Shīʿa, the saints and the lovers of God and His Messenger are those who speak truth, fulfil their promises, are faithful with what is entrusted to them, render dues when claimed, and act when commanded. Our Shīʿa are those from whose ears knowledge does not pass out unheeded. They are those who do not praise the ones who blame us or hate us; nor do they consort with those who diminish our virtues. When our Shīʿa meet a believer, they honour him; when they meet one who is ignorant, they turn away.

"Our Shīʿa are not such as bark like dogs in the cold, nor are they avaricious like crows. When they have need to ask of someone, they ask only of their own brethren—even should they perish of hunger. Our Shīʿa are those who speak openly of us, who forsake even their friends out of love for us, who draw near those estranged from us, and who keep at a distance those whose nearness is harmful."

A man asked him: "O son of the Messenger, where shall we find such Shīʿa?"

He replied: "We shall meet them around the earth. They will be exceedingly poor and lowly, yet the coolness of contentment will rest in their eyes. When they are seen, they are not recognised; when they vanish, they are not sought. When they are sick, they are not visited; when they desire to marry, none gives them in marriage; when they come upon a path, the path is closed against them. And when the ignorant address them with ignorance, they reply with ⟪ar:السلام⟫: 'Peace.'"

The man then asked: "O son of the Messenger, what will be the lot of those who profess to be Shīʿa with their tongues, while their hearts are against the Shīʿa?"

He answered: "An age shall come upon them that will destroy them, and slay them through hatred and dissension. Beware! By Him who aided us with His angels, God shall slay them by His own hand."

The believer who severs himself from the people of this world—from his parents and his children—and who loves, counsels, and cooperates with his brethren for the sake of God, were he even an Ethiopian or a Christian: such a one will not be raised from his grave on the Day of Resurrection except as one white as snow, bathed in the waters of Paradise. He shall receive an enduring blessing, and shall enjoy the company of the angels, the prophets, and the messengers. There is no honour greater in the sight of God than that of a servant who has been driven out for the sake of God, until he meets his Lord in exile. Our Shīʿa are the harbingers of the Day of Judgement upon the earth, a lamp for those who seek, a sign and a light, and a guide for those who obey God. Whoever comes to them finds peace; whoever loves them is met with gentleness and kindness. Our Shīʿa are generous, forbearing, and merciful.

The learned among our Shīʿa restrains his tongue, takes contentment in obedience to God and His Messenger, and conceals his vigilance against our enemies within his heart. When he goes forth in the morning, he is aware of all their faults, yet he does not disclose what he holds within. He sees their wickedness with his eyes, hears it with his ears, and curses it with his tongue. Those who hate them, he holds as friends; those who love them, he holds as enemies. To such a one the very firmament is commanded to give shade, and in this world he finds whatever he asks of God.

⟪ar:امام جعفر الصادق علیہ السلام⟫ is reported to have said to his Shīʿa: "Bind yourselves to asceticism and piety. Speak truth and render trusts. Abide steadfast upon the path on which you stand: for when the soul of one of you reaches his throat, he shall rejoice. If you live, you shall see that which cools the eyes; and if you die, by God, you shall be met with the best of words. By God, you are upon the religion of God and the religion of my fathers. By God, by 'my fathers' I do not mean ⟪ar:محمدؑ (الباقر) بن علی⟫ alone and ⟪ar:علیؑ (زین العابدین) بن الحسین⟫, but also those noble ones ⟪ar:حضرت ابراھیم و اسماعیل اور اسحاق و یعقوب علیہم السلام⟫: they all share one and the same religion. Fear God, and aid us through piety and devotion. By God, the prayer, the fast, the pilgrimage, and the alms shall be accepted only from you, and God shall forgive you. But remember: our Shīʿa are those who follow us, not those who oppose us. When we are in fear, they too are in fear; when we dwell in peace, they too dwell in peace and tranquillity. These are our Shīʿa. When Iblīs went among the rest of mankind, they obeyed him; but when he came to our Shīʿa, they refused him obedience. Therefore Iblīs incited the people against the Shīʿa, and for this reason our Shīʿa suffer at their hands."

*The Book of Supplications of Islam.*

### The Shīʿa Likened to the Sun

The sun has demonstrated, through millions of years of unbroken revolution, that there is no waver in its course. Its rising each day and its setting each evening are certain. Through its constancy and its action, it has proved that it never breaks its compact.

The true believer is likewise: through past life and unwavering consistency, he sets so firm an example that judgements may be made concerning him before he acts—so that, if such-and-such an occasion should arise, men will already know what such a believer will do.
*(From the Lessons of Mullā Ghulām Ḥusain ibn Ḥaẓrat Naṣrullāh, 2005.)*

## Attributes of the God-Fearing Believer

It is narrated that ⟪ar:مولانا علی علیہ السلام⟫ was once approached by ⟪ar:حمام ابن شریح⟫, a devout and pious companion, who said: "Describe to me, ⟪ar:أمیر المومنین!⟫, the characteristics of the God-fearing in such a manner that I may behold them with my own eyes." ⟪ar:أمیر المومنین⟫ replied: "O Hammām, fear God and act righteously, for ⟪ar-quote:إِنَّ ٱللَّهَ مَعَ ٱلَّذِينَ ٱتَّقَوا۟ وَّٱلَّذِينَ هُم مُّحْسِنُونَ⟫—God is with those who fear Him and those who do good." Hammām, however, was not content with this brief reply, and pressed for a fuller answer. Maulānā ʿAlī was thereupon moved to speak:[^cite-2]

All praise belongs to God, the Creator of all creation. Peace and blessings be upon the Messenger ⟪ar:محمد مصطفی صلی اللہ علیہ و آلہ و سلم⟫ of God, and upon the elect of His servants. Glory be to God, who fashioned all creatures in such wise that He stands in no need of their obedience, and is shielded from their disobedience. The sins of those who sin do Him no harm, and the obedience of those who obey brings Him no profit. He has apportioned each one's sustenance, and has assigned to each his station in this world.

Yet the God-fearing hold a special station in His creation. Their speech is concise, their attire moderate, their bearing humble. They avert their eyes from what God has forbidden, and incline their ears to beneficial knowledge. In days of trial they remain as composed as in days of ease. Were it not that God had appointed for each a definite term, their souls would not abide in their bodies even a moment, so great is their dread of His reward and His chastisement. The majesty of God is so deeply graven upon their hearts that all else in the world has become contemptible in their sight. Paradise is to them as though they beheld it with their eyes and were already tasting its delights; and Hell is to them as though they saw it before them and were already enduring the anguish of its torment.

Their hearts are sorrow-stricken; they are kept safe from every evil. Their bodies are slender, their wants are few, their souls are pure. They have purchased an enduring repose at the cost of brief tribulation—a profitable exchange which God has made easy for them. The world set itself against them, yet they did not set themselves against the world. The world made them captive, but they ransomed their souls and so won their freedom.

They pass the night reciting the Qurʾān while standing, calming their hearts with it and curing therewith the sicknesses of their souls. When a verse comes upon the blessings of Paradise, their souls lean toward it with longing, as though Paradise were set before their very eyes; and when a verse comes upon the torments of Hell, they incline their ears as though they heard the roaring of the Fire and its grievous cries. They bend their backs in bowing; they rest their cheeks in prostration; and they cast themselves before God upon their palms, their knees, and their feet, seeking deliverance. They spend their days in patience, in knowledge, in scrupulous restraint and piety. The fear of God has worn their bodies thin as arrows: one beholding them would suppose them sick, though they are not; and people, on seeing their condition, call them mad and possessed—when in truth it is the contemplation of the Hereafter that has rendered them so.

They take no satisfaction in their lesser deeds, and even their most brilliant works they hold in contempt. They blame themselves continually, and fear concerning what they do. When they are praised they say: "I know myself better than they do, and God knows me better still. O God, do not call me to account according to what they say of me. Make me better than they suppose, and forgive me those sins which only You know and which they do not perceive."

You will observe in them: that they are humble in their religion yet gentle in their natures; perfect in faith and eager in pursuit of knowledge; moderate in the gaining of wealth and ardent in worship; grateful in hunger and steadfast in suffering; desirous only of what is lawful; joyful in guidance; and abhorring greed. They are anxious concerning their good deeds. They are diligent in giving thanks to God by night, and in remembering Him by day. They pass the night in fear and meet the morning in joy—fear lest they should forget the remembrance of God in the night, and joy at being recipients of His favours. **When their souls recoil from a righteous deed, they do not yield to that recoil.** They do not occupy their thoughts with passing things; they take the coolness of their eyes from imperishable works. **They convert their knowledge into forbearance, and their words into deeds.**

You will see that their hopes are short and their failures few; their hearts in awe, their souls content; their food plain, their religion safeguarded, their desires put to death, their anger subdued. Only good is expected of them; from evil there is no fear. When they are among those who forget God, they are among those who remember Him. They forgive those who have wronged them; they give to those who would deprive them; they answer ill-treatment with kindness.

Coarseness of speech is far from them. Their words are gentle, their failings hidden, their virtues ever in evidence. Their good is before them, and evil has turned away from them. **They are dignified in adversity, patient in hardship, and grateful in days of ease.** They do not transgress against those they hate, nor sin for the sake of those they love. They acknowledge the truth before evidence is even brought against them. They do not betray a trust, nor neglect what they are charged to remember. They do not assail others with foul names, nor injure their neighbours. They do not rejoice in the misfortune of others. They neither stray into error nor wander out of truth.

If they keep silent, their silence does them no harm; if they laugh, their voices are not raised. Should any wrong them, they bear it patiently until God Himself takes vengeance on their behalf. **They burden their own souls so that others may find repose with them.** **They cause their own souls to suffer for the sake of the Hereafter, and they shield people from their own ill.** Their distance from people is for the sake of piety and purity, and their nearness to people is in compassion and gentleness. They are not estranged from people through ostentation or pride, nor near to them through cunning or deceit.

Upon hearing this sermon, ⟪ar:حمام⟫ was so overcome that his soul flew forth from his body. At that, ⟪ar:امیر المومنین⟫ said: "By God, this is what I feared. Thus do effective counsels work upon those minds that are prepared to receive them."

## Qualities of the Believer

Once, while ⟪ar:امیر المومنین مولانا علی ابن ابی طالب⟫ was delivering a sermon, a devout and earnest man rose and asked him to describe—⟪ar:یا امیر المومنین!⟫—a believer, as though they beheld him with their own eyes.

He replied: "O servant, the believer is a man of discernment and wisdom. His face is cheerful, his heart ⟪ar:حزین⟫ sorrowful, his breast spacious. He holds in contempt every transient thing; he remembers God always, and combines patience with gratitude. **He sorrows in the things of the Hereafter, and rejoices in his poverty.** His knowledge is vast, his forbearance great, his mercy abundant. He keeps himself far from hatred. He is not hasty in any matter. His heart is not perturbed by anything, nor does he stoop to anything unworthy. He neither oppresses others nor wrongs himself. **In the face of suffering his soul is harder than stone.** His striving for livelihood is as sweet as honey. He is not so greedy as to seize the rights of others. He is neither harsh nor impatient. He neither broods nor frets over worldly matters. When he is angered, he acts justly. When something is asked of him, he is courteous. He does not act in anger, does not violate ⟪ar:حتک⟫ the honour of any, oppresses no one, and is a true lover. He keeps his promises and fulfils his covenants. He is first to come to the aid of the people. He lives his life in obscurity. He speaks no idle word; he is pleased with God Most High. He opposes his own appetites, and shows tenderness to those younger than himself. He does not turn his mind to trifles. He maintains his ties with friends without burdening them. He spends without extravagance. He neither deceives nor betrays. He does not pursue what would expose another's fault. He oppresses no one, but is a guardian to the people and labours on their behalf. He is helper of the weak and succour of the afflicted. He neither conceals what should be made known nor reveals what should remain hidden. He bears much suffering, yet never complains with his tongue. If he sees a good in another, he speaks of it; if he sees an evil, he conceals it. He covers the faults of people, and turns his gaze from what he should not see. He accepts the excuses men offer and forgives their lapses. **When he learns of a good thing, he does not let it slip; nor does he depart without amending an evil.** He is trustworthy and pious. His inward self is pure, and people are at ease with him. He occupies himself with sorrow for the Hereafter, and places his trust in none save God. He lives in this world the life of a traveller. **He loves solitude and longs for salvation in the Hereafter. He loves a man for the sake of God, and exerts himself for the sake of God's good pleasure. He does not avenge himself, but leaves such matters to God. He does not befriend any enemy of God.** He seeks the company of the poor. He is protector of the people, helper of the people of truth, succour of kin, a father to orphans, a husband to widows, and a caretaker of the afflicted. In every adversity, men hope for his aid; in every difficulty, he places his hope in God. He is generous and of cheerful countenance. He is firm in purpose, restrained in anger, ⟪ar:مُتَبَسِّم⟫ ever-smiling, ⟪ar:دقیق النظر⟫ keen in his observation, and scrupulous in his religion. He has no liking for miserliness. If people withhold from him what is his due, he endures it patiently. He shuns all that is base. He is rich through contentment. His modesty prevails over his desire, and his love does not permit the spirit of jealousy to arise."[^cite-3]

## What Is the Reality of Love?

Allah has fashioned the human being such that he loves only himself; he loves no other in his own right. If he appears to love another, it is because that ⟪ar:’⟫person stands in some close relation to him⟪ar:‘⟫ — his ⟪ar:’⟫children⟪ar:‘⟫, his parents, ⟪ar:’⟫his friends, his family⟪ar:‘⟫, and the like. Yet even in his love for others, the pin of his "I" remains fixed at the centre. He is always the centre of his own circle; whoever enters that circle is loved, and the closer they stand to its centre, the greater his love for them.

For this reason, every human being is self-centred — and in this lies a great divine wisdom. He is sincere with none save himself. Each person is bound to the pole of his own self (nafs) and engaged in its service. The soul is the centre of his existence, and throughout his life he revolves around its temperaments and desires. The self commands him to pride (⟪ar:کبر⟫), envy (⟪ar:حسد⟫), and greed (⟪ar:حرس⟫); it bids him to do evil upon evil. The man does all that lies in his power, submits to its every command, and falls into the snare of false desires and worthless feelings. He remains imprisoned in this bondage until death knocks at his door. At that hour, the wages of vain praise and self-aggrandisement gathered in the service of his own soul avail him nothing, and he merits the Fire for the squandering of his life.

### Love for Others

When that same person falls in love with another, he makes his beloved the centre of his existence; he begins to leave the centre of his self and to revolve around the axis of his beloved. He learns to accept the beloved's will in every matter — small or great, just or unjust — and his sole aim becomes to gain the beloved's pleasure and acceptance.

Yet even in this state his self-interest persists, for through the acceptance of his beloved he is in fact seeking the satisfaction of his own soul. Only the centre of his orbit has shifted; his exertions are now directed away from his own self toward the self of the beloved.

In other words, Allah has placed in the human being both faculties — self-worship and the surrender of self. To revolve about one's own centre is selfishness; to overturn oneself in order to win another's pleasure is love.

### Love for Allah and the Messenger (peace be upon him)

Allah says in the Qurʾān:

> ⟪quran 51:56⟫
> ⟪ar:‏وَمَا خَلَقْتُ ٱلْجِنَّ وَٱلْإِنسَ إِلَّا لِيَعْبُدُونِ ‎⟫
> *And I (Allah) created not the jinns and humans except they should worship Me (Alone).*
> And I did not create the jinn and mankind except to worship Me.
> — **Quran 51:56 (inline widget)**

In this verse Allah uses the word ⟪ar:’عبد‘⟫ — meaning servant, slave. The human being is created for ⟪ar:’تسلیم‘⟫ (taslīm, the surrender of one's will). So long as he directs that surrender toward his own self or toward another mortal like himself, he can never progress. For this reason Allah declares that He has created jinn and mankind for His ⟪ar:عَبدِیَت⟫ (ʿabdiyya, the condition of being His servant). If a person would advance beyond this mortal world, he must abandon the bondage of his own soul and enter, with heartfelt joy, the bondage of Allah — the great daʿwa (the summons / the invitation). He must leave the narrow circle of his self and become a traveller in a vaster firmament; he must submit to Allah's commands and seek only His pleasure. This is ⟪ar:’اسلام‘⟫ (islām). Though the task is arduous, by it the loftiest ranks are attained.[^cite-4] Upon this very principle of acceptance Islam established the practice of placing one's brow at the feet of an elder, in which a person lifts the crown of arrogance — the crown of ⟪ar:’أنا‘⟫ (anā, the "I") — from his head and lays it before another.

For this reason, when ⟪ar:رسول اللہ (ﷺ)⟫ caused mankind to bear witness to his prophethood, he first declared his own ⟪ar:عبدیت⟫ in the formula of testimony, affirming ⟪ar:’عبدیت‘⟫ before ⟪ar:’رسالت‘⟫, saying: ⟪ar:اَشھَدُ اَنَّ مُحَمَّدً عبدُہُ وَ رَسُولہُ⟫. So pleased was Allah with this humility that, in mentioning the Prophet's Ascension, He chose the word ⟪ar:عبد⟫ in place of ⟪ar:رسول⟫ — for the rank of being Allah's servant (⟪ar:عبدیت⟫) is, in His sight, more honoured and more trustworthy than the rank of messengership (⟪ar:رسالت⟫).

> ⟪quran 17:1⟫
> ⟪ar:‏سُبْحَنَ ٱلَّذِىٓ أَسْرَىٰ بِعَبْدِهِۦ لَيْلًۭا مِّنَ ٱلْمَسْجِدِ ٱلْحَرَامِ إِلَى ٱلْمَسْجِدِ ٱلْأَقْصَا ٱلَّذِى بَرَكْنَا حَوْلَهُۥ لِنُرِيَهُۥ مِنْ آيَتِنَآ ۚ إِنَّهُۥ هُوَ ٱلسَّمِيعُ ٱلْبَصِيرُ ‎⟫
> *Who took His slave (Muhammad PBUH) for a journey by night from Al-Masjid-al-Haram (at Makkah) to the farthest mosque (in Jerusalem), the neighbourhood whereof We have blessed, in order that We might show him (Muhammad PBUH) of Our Ayat (proofs, evidences, lessons, signs, etc.). Verily, He is the All-Hearer, the All-Seer.*
> glory be to Him who took His servant by night from the Sacred Mosque to the Farthest Mosque, the precincts of which We have blessed, that We might show him some of Our signs. Indeed, He is the All-hearing, the All-seeing.
> — **Quran 17:1 (inline widget)**

The Prophet (peace and blessings be upon him) said: "Speak the truth to the people, even if they dislike it." But a man, on account of his self-love, does not wish to be ill thought of among his fellows. It has been the way of the prophets and messengers of truth to utter the truth among men: they have abased themselves before others in order to seek Allah's pleasure, and by this their rank and reward with Him have been confirmed.[^cite-5] Allah says:

> ⟪quran 3:31⟫
> ⟪ar:‏قُلْ إِن كُنتُمْ تُحِبُّونَ ٱللَّهَ فَٱتَّبِعُونِى يُحْبِبْكُمُ ٱللَّهُ وَيَغْفِرْ لَكُمْ ذُنُوبَكُمْ ۗ وَٱللَّهُ غَفُورٌۭ رَّحِيمٌۭ ‎⟫
> *Say (O Muhammad PBUH to mankind): "If you (really) love Allah then follow me (i.e. accept Islamic Monotheism, follow the Quran and the Sunnah), Allah will love you and forgive you of your sins. And Allah is Oft-Forgiving, Most Merciful." *
Say, > if you love God, then follow me, and God will love you and forgive you your sins. God is Forgiving and Merciful.
> — **Quran 3:31 (inline widget)**

In this eloquent verse Allah has set the standard of His love through the person of His Messenger. He addresses mankind through His Prophet and says: "Follow him, if you would seek the love of Allah." The meaning is plain: if you obey the Messenger by setting aside your own appetites and inclinations, Allah will love you and forgive you all your sins. Such is the magnet of the Messenger's love, and so abundant are its blessings, that for the sake of His own love for His Prophet, Allah forgives the sins of those who follow him.

## The Law of Alif — The Law of Affinity

Allah Most High has founded His religion upon love, which is known as ⟪ar:قانون الف⟫ (qānūn-i alif, the Law of Affinity). Love, in its essence, is the act of yielding one's own will, one's own desire, and one's own choice for the sake of another.

If we consider the structure of this universe, we see the same law operative at every level. Until the mineral is ground down, no plant can absorb it; to become vegetation, the mineral must submit. So too the plant cannot become animal flesh until it has bowed beneath the grazing beast. And the animal cannot rise into the upright human ⟪ar:قامت الفی⟫ (qāmat-i alifī, "alif-shaped stature") of the slaughterer's hand until it has offered its neck, undefended, beneath the knife. In just this way, until a person yields his will, his desires, his deeds, and his ambitions to the command of Allah, Allah does not accept him nor render him worthy of Paradise. To attain any progress, every living thing must pass through stages of suffering and patience. Yet sincerity is the very condition of love — and for sincerity to arise, the human acknowledgement must be free and uncoerced. For this reason, there is no compulsion in religion.[^cite-6]

This law is manifest in the very person of man, of whom ⟪ar:نبی کریم (ﷺ)⟫ said: ⟪ar-quote:حب الوطن من الأیمان⟫ ("Love of one's homeland is a part of faith"). Were every external element of a man to act of its own accord, pursuing its own desires, he could never assemble himself at all. ## Love and Friendship with God

In the verse cited at the close of this section, the Creator addresses the believers thus: O you who believe, whoever among you turns back from his religion, let him turn back — God will bring in his place a people whom He loves and who love Him, humble before the believers and mighty against the disbelievers, striving in the path of God and undismayed by the reproach of any reproacher. That is the bounty of God; He bestows it upon whom He wills, and He is all-embracing, all-knowing.

The verse condemns one company and praises another. Tradition relates that in the time of the Messenger of God there were those who, displeased with a particular ruling, threatened to renounce Islam and to join the Jews, the Christians, or the polytheists. To them God replies that their apostasy would impoverish only themselves: in their place He would raise up a sincere community whom He loves and who love Him in return.

What greater perfection can be ascribed to a community than this — that the world of creation should praise them, and the Creator Himself should praise them in such terms? The praise spoken by creatures is one thing; the eulogy uttered on the tongue of nature itself is another. And the first attribute by which God identifies this favoured community is reciprocal love: they love God, and God loves them. Many claim to love God; only those whose love is requited by Him are confirmed in their claim. One-sided love is defective; perfect love is mutual.

This is no minor consideration. Even the greatest of saints have stood astonished at the magnitude of divine love. Human capacity is too narrow to bear it. Anyone who has observed how even the smallest worldly passion unsettles the mind and crowds out every other thought will sense the danger: when creaturely love alone is enough to disturb the reason, what hope has a finite vessel of bearing the infinite love of God?

### Jesus and the Aged Worshipper

It is related in the older sources that one day Jesus, peace be upon him, passed by a hermitage on the slope of a mountain and found within it a worshipper bent and withered by long devotion. Asked how long he had served his Lord, the old man replied: "Seventy years, and only one petition of mine remains unanswered." Jesus enquired what it was. He said: "I have asked God for a single particle of His pure love. Intercede for me, O Spirit of God." Jesus raised his hands, and the prayer was granted.

Some days later Jesus passed by the same place and found the hermitage collapsed, the earth beneath it torn open, and the old worshipper standing in the cleft, mouth open, eyes fixed upon the heavens. Jesus greeted him; he gave no reply. Astonished, Jesus asked: "Lord, what has befallen this servant of Yours?" The reply came: "O Jesus, this worshipper asked of Me a particle of My love. I gave him one part out of seventy thousand, knowing he could not bear more. Behold the state of My prophet. Had I given him a little more, you may conjecture what would have come of him."

### The Love of Shuʿayb

In the same vein, the Prophet Shuʿayb — the father-in-law of Moses — was so absorbed in the remembrance of God that his sight failed. God restored it. He wept again, and was blinded a second time. God restored it once more. On the third occasion the divine address came: "O Shuʿayb! If you weep in longing for paradise, paradise is yours; if you weep in fear of hell, the Fire is forbidden to you." Shuʿayb replied: "Lord, I weep neither for paradise nor from fear of hell; I weep only out of love for You." The revelation came again: "What is your wish, O prophet?" He said: "Lord of the worlds, say but once: O Shuʿayb, you are My beloved." The voice answered: "Yes, O Shuʿayb, you are My beloved." At this Shuʿayb's soul departed from the cage of his body in the joy of that single word.

### The Three Stations of Worship

It is for this reason that the Master of the Universe, ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib, declares in his sermons: "O God, I do not worship You out of desire for Your paradise, nor out of fear of Your fire; I have found You worthy of worship, and so I worship You."[^cite-13] Such worship, the Imam teaches, is the worship of free men. Those who worship in longing for paradise practise the worship of merchants who seek a price for their devotion; those who weep in dread of hell display the disposition of slaves who act under compulsion. Only the free worship for love alone.

Walāya (the friendship and authoritative trust of God) is not a passive title bestowed at random. It denotes a reciprocal bond. The walī names God his beloved, and God in turn names him His beloved. The joy of love is realised only when the flame burns on both sides. And true love of God belongs only to those who are true followers of the Messenger. God Himself commands the Prophet to say:

### The Three Outward Signs of Love

The scholars enumerate three outward signs by which lovers are known: emaciation of the body, pallor of the complexion, and a marked change in temperament — all arising from the fear that the beloved may have grown displeased. Yet these signs belong only to those uncertain of their standing with the beloved. They do not appear in one fully assured of his rank.

A questioner once asked the Commander of the Faithful: "Lord, why is it that the faces of all worshippers and lovers turn pale and their bodies wither, yet your face remains radiant and your frame strong?" His reply has been recorded: such men do not know their standing. "As for me," he said, "the veils have been lifted from my eyes — *law kushifa al-ghiṭāʾ mā izdadtu yaqīnan* (even were every veil removed from before me, my certainty would not increase by a hair). When I look with the eyes of the heart and find my Beloved pleased with me, my dread departs at once. And when God Himself has given me the glad tidings of acceptance, what room remains for anxiety?"

Yet the Commander of the Faithful was always cheerful in the place of nearness and altered only in the place of submission. The blood would drain from his face when he stood before God to obey. Once, setting out for the Ḥajj, he meant to pronounce the talbiya. He began to tremble; his colour faded; he fell to the ground. To those who asked, he said: "I feared lest I say *labbayk* and God should answer back, *lā labbayka wa lā saʿdayka* — and what then would become of me?"

### The Three Degrees of Love

The scholars further distinguish three degrees of love. The first is the love of one who, the moment he meets pain at the hands of his beloved, opens a register of complaints. This is a defective and incomplete metaphorical love. The second is the love of one who bears the pain of his beloved in silence, never permitting a word of complaint to cross his tongue — a higher metaphorical love.

> Grief is not disturbed by the world.
>
> Noise does not disturb oysters in the river.
>

> I am a believer, not a spill.

The third degree is the love of one who, on encountering hardship in the path of the beloved's pleasure, finds in that hardship a savour and a delight; whose passion reaches such a pitch that the heart longs for the suffering to increase. This is the loftiest station of love. The great lovers cannot attain it; it is reserved for Muḥammad and the Family of Muḥammad. And history is its witness: the heavier the burden of suffering laid upon them, the brighter the light upon their faces. The greater the trials Ḥusayn met on the field of Karbalāʾ, the more upright his bearing grew.

### The Test of True Love

The truest measure of love is the field of war. In comfort and assembly, enemies pass as friends. In every age, sincere men have gathered around their prophets and imams, loving them with all their heart. But the companions of Ḥusayn were rare exemplars of true love and astonishing emblems of fidelity. They are reported as seventy-two — and by God, those who stood firm in their pledge, true to their intention, sound in faith, scrupulous in devotion, righteous and resolute, were the most extraordinary company. ## The Worship Reserved for Allāh Alone

Imam Jaʿfar al-Ṣādiq (a.s.) said: "A person's outward life is the mirror of his inner self, and his inner self is concealed within his outward life." Some try to dissemble, covering the duplicity of their hearts with a polished manner. Yet however hard they labour at this, such people are swiftly betrayed by their own words and deeds. A slip of the tongue reveals the secrets of the heart, and the garment of pretence is torn through.

The pious and the learned, by contrast, always seek to conceal the purity and devotion of their inner state, lest they be praised or made famous for it. And yet, despite their effort to remain hidden, their excellence becomes known among the people, and the purity of their soul begins to shine forth, on every side, like the sun.

Hypocrisy (riyāʾ) is the attempt to win a place in the hearts of others — whether by deeds, by appearance, by dress, or by any other means of conduct. Every such hypocrite, in effect, acknowledges more than one god. To them Allāh will say on the Day of Resurrection: "Go and seek your reward from those whom you used to delight in this world."

The true believer must therefore perform every deed for the sake of Allāh and for Allāh alone, associating no one with Him in service or worship.[^cite-16] The mark of the one who joins others with Allāh in worship is that he performs good deeds in order to be seen, and labours so that others may praise him.

Whoever performs a good deed in secret from this world — there is not a day in which Allāh does not reward him with a fair recompense; and whoever commits a sin in secret — there is not a day in which Allāh does not requite him with what is due.

Why is it that those of you who commit sins commit them in secret? The very fact of concealment is itself a witness against the deed, for the human being is indeed a witness against himself. The outward act of one whose inner self is pure is always plain and open, while the outward act of one whose inner self is corrupted is always concealed.

Whoever performs even a little deed sincerely for the sake of Allāh, Allāh will reward him many times over for that deed. And whoever labours much in order to be seen by people, wearing out his body with night-long vigils, Allāh will void all his deeds and abase him in the sight of the one for whose sake he performed them.

## Life between Fear and Hope

Allāh is the Possessor of every power and of every mercy, of wrath and of pleasure alike. His wrath is in proportion to His mercy, and His punishments are commensurate with the vastness of His blessings. Because His mercy is beyond measure, every sinner may yet hope to be forgiven; and because His justice is unyielding, every sinner must fear that he might justly merit a painful retribution. The believer, therefore, must always live between hope and fear (khawf wa rajāʾ) — fearing, because he does not know for which sin he might be called to account and inscribed in the register of the offenders, and hoping that his frail good deeds might yet be accepted and his name inscribed among those upon whom Allāh is about to pour out His blessings.[^cite-17] He must therefore exert himself in good deeds and guard himself from every evil.

Imam Jaʿfar al-Ṣādiq said: "Fear Allāh as though you see Him; and if you cannot see Him, at least convince yourself that He sees you." If you believe that He cannot see you, then you are an unbeliever. And if you believe that He sees you and yet you sin in His sight, then you have impugned His power over you.

Indeed, the true believer knows and is certain that Allāh sees him and hears his every word, the fair and the foul alike, and so he restrains his soul from every craving and his body from the pleasures of every evil act. Whoever knows Allāh fears Him; and whoever fears Him turns away from this world. Only the one who knows truly fears. The more a person's knowledge grows, the more his fear deepens.

The believer always lives between two kinds of fear: the first concerns the sins already written against him in the past, for he does not know what Allāh will do with those sins; and the second concerns the time of life that yet remains, for he does not know what further sins he may commit. The morning of such a believer dawns in fear, and it is this fear that keeps his soul upon the straight path.

Whoever fears Allāh, Allāh casts a fear of him into the heart of every creature in this world; and whoever does not fear Allāh, Allāh makes him afraid of every creature in this world. Nothing was dearer to the Messenger of Allāh than that a person should hunger and live his life in this world in fear of his Lord.

The believer must fear Allāh like one who is about to be cast into a blazing fire, and must hope from Allāh like one who has been given the glad tidings of the gardens of paradise. Allāh rewards or punishes a person in accord with his intention. Those who are suspended between fear and hope are as restless as the bird whose heart trembles in the claw of a hunter.

Allāh takes from you His favours and His loans, that He may test your patience and your gratitude. In that moment, hope for His mercy in such a way that hope does not slacken your obedience to Him, and fear Him in such a way that fear does not extinguish your hope of His mercy.

A person is not a true believer in Allāh until he lives between hope and fear; and he cannot live between hope and fear until he translates that fear and that hope into action. For a person pursues what he hopes for, and flees from what he fears.

My counsel to you is this: place no hope in anyone but Allāh, and fear nothing save your own sins. Remember Allāh before you undertake any deed: if it leads you to obedience to Him, do it; and if it leads you to disobedience, abandon it.

A little deed performed with sincerity is better than countless deeds done without it. He was asked: "What is the parable of one who performs deeds insincerely?" He replied: "It is the one who feeds people, looks after his neighbours, serves the guest — and then, when the door of the forbidden is opened before him, walks through it."

(*Wilāyat Ahl al-Bayt: Imam Jaʿfar al-Ṣādiq (a.s.)*)

## The Reality of This World

When a person turns away from this world, all that the world contains becomes worthless and cheap in his eyes. Then he never grieves over what eludes him, and never rejoices over what comes to him. By contrast, the one who is bound to the world grieves over every loss, every severed tie, and rejoices over every gain. A person's joys and his sorrows are therefore the mirror of his inward state.

The likeness of the world is the brine of the sea: the thirsty drink of it, and their thirst only grows, until the water itself destroys them. Or it is like the silkworm: the more it wraps itself in its own silk, the harder it becomes to escape, until it dies within the cocoon of its own making. Or it is like the serpent — soft to the touch, but full of a deadly venom.[^cite-18] By Allāh, this world is to me like a carcass: when hunger compels me, I take a little from it — that is, I take of it only under compulsion.

A man came to Imam Jaʿfar al-Ṣādiq (a.s.) from the prison of Kūfa and asked him to pray for his release. The Imam asked him: "Tell me, how did you find the prison of Kūfa?" He said: "May Allāh exalt you — it is a place of distress and terror, and its inmates are in the most wretched of conditions." The Imam replied: "You are still in a yet worse prison — and you wish to remain in it. Do you not know that this world is a prison for the believer?" Those whose hearts are bound to this world are in three states at once: ceaseless anxiety, unfulfilled hopes, and desires without end.

Do not imagine that the oppressor stands alone in his wrongdoing. Every oppressor has three companions: the one who commits the wrong, the one who assists him in it, and the one who delights in the sight of the oppression. All three will be fed of it together.

Two men came to Imam al-Ṣādiq (a.s.) with grievances against one another. Before he had heard their case, he said: "The one who triumphs in his injustice gains no good from it." The wronged person, by virtue of the wrong inflicted upon him, takes out a loan against the oppressor — for the oppressor will be called to answer his victim on the Day of Judgement. And the debt owed to the wronged is far greater than whatever worldly benefit the oppressor has snatched from him. The oppressor has no right to complain when he is wronged in turn tomorrow, for the children of Ādam reap in their lives the harvest of what they have themselves sown. No one gathers sweetness from bitter seed, nor bitterness from sweet. Hearing this, the two men quietly returned the way they had come.

## The Counsel of Imam Jaʿfar al-Ṣādiq

When a seeker came to Imam Jaʿfar al-Ṣādiq, peace be upon him, asking for counsel, the Imam answered with a sustained discourse whose force lies less in any single maxim than in the steady accumulation of its questions and commands. He began by holding up the contradictions of the heedless life to the light of certainty: *If Allah is the Provider, why are you anxious? If sustenance is already apportioned, why this haste to seize it? If reward is from Allah, why do you stint your own works? If Allah is the Giver of every blessing, why are you miserly? If the Fire is real, why do you disobey? If death is certain, why this laughter? If the standing before Allah is true, why do you deceive His servants? Since Satan is the open enemy, why this indifference towards him? If the Bridge must be crossed, why this dissembling? If everything has been ordained, why this grief? And since the life of this world is fleeting, why repose your trust in it?*[^cite-19]

The Imam then turned from interrogation to admonition. *You live, he said, only in numbered days, and death arrives upon you suddenly. He who sows good reaps joy; he who sows evil reaps regret upon regret. The sluggish do not outrun their decree, nor do the hasty seize anything before its time except what Allah has already ordained. Whoever has received good, it is Allah who has bestowed it; whoever has been spared evil, it is Allah who has spared him. Whoever fears Allah, Allah will guard him from every calamity. Whoever is grateful to Him, He will increase His bounty. Whoever lends to Him, He repays in full.*

When the world begins to ensnare you with its trap and to delight you with the play of its creatures, the Imam counselled, remember death. Remember the loneliness of the grave; the hour in which the eyes fall back upon the cheeks; the loosening of the limbs from one another within the earth; the pressure that bears down upon the flesh; the dissolution of the body and the departure from this world. *All of this*, he said, *will press you to action and unbind your heart from this world.*

The contented man, the Imam continued, is the wealthy man; the one whose eye is always upon the wealth of others dies in poverty. Whoever is not satisfied with what Allah has apportioned has doubted the power of Allah. Whoever holds his own fault small holds the fault of others great. Whoever uncovers the fault of another, Allah will uncover his own. Whoever draws the sword of disobedience to Allah will perish by it. Whoever digs a pit for his brother falls into it himself. The one who keeps the company of the ignorant is humbled; the one who befriends this world is shamed; the one who frequents an evil place earns the world's blame.

*Always speak the truth*, he said, *even if the truth tells against you. Beware the slanderer, for he sows hatred in the heart. When you go out in search of generosity, look for it in its proper places, for generosity has a soil, and that soil has a root, and that root puts forth branches, and good fruit is not gathered except from a sound branch.* Remember Allah often by day and night, for He loves such servants; no good is attained without obedience to Him. Beware the sins of the outward and the sins of the inward; follow not desire or conjecture, lest you stray; uphold the prayers, and especially the middle prayer.

Beware, too, of the pursuit of worldly grandeur, for true majesty and glory belong to Allah alone, and whoever seeks to share in that majesty will be broken by Allah and abased before all on the Day of Resurrection. Envy not one another, for the root of unbelief is envy. *Know that islām is the name of submission, and submission is islām: the one who surrenders his desires and his soul to the will of Allah is the true Muslim.* Beware, for Satan lays his trap subtly — forbidding you a kindness to your brother, urging you to sleep through the dawn prayer.

Let not the taunts of the ignorant grieve you, nor their praise gladden you, lest your deeds make you proud. Do not exhaust your own provision in trying to enrich the provision of others, for it is your own that will be your sole capital at the moment of death. Be content with what Allah has given. Be not ungrateful in days of ease nor impatient in days of want. Acquaint yourself with the beginning and end of any matter before you set out upon it. Keep silent — *silence is a sword against the world and a shield for the ignorant.*[^cite-20]

Guard your sight, for the eye sows the seed of desire in the heart; *blessed is the one whose vision lies not in his eyes but in his heart.* Meet the one who deserts you; give to the one who withholds from you; show mercy to the one who oppresses you; deal justly with the one who wrongs you; forgive the one who offends you. Do you not see that the sun rises evenly on every holy place, and the rain benefits the righteous and the wicked alike?

He who passes the night with a full belly while his neighbour sleeps in hunger has not believed in Allah and the Last Day. Whoever relieves a believer in distress, Allah will relieve him on the Day when there is no refuge but Him. Whoever meets one need of his brother, Allah meets many of his. Whoever clothes a believer, Allah clothes him in the raiment of Paradise on the Day of Resurrection. Whoever feeds a believer, Allah feeds him from Paradise; whoever gives a believing brother drink, Allah will give him drink from the Pool of Kawthar.

Whoever searches out the faults of his brother, Allah will set out his faults in his book of deeds on the Day of Resurrection and expose them before every onlooker. *The least thing that makes a believer an unbeliever is that he should remember some matter about his brother only that he may afterwards bring him low.* Such a one has no share in the Hereafter. Whoever gladdens the heart of his believing brother gladdens the heart of the Ahl al-Bayt (the People of the House); whoever gladdens the heart of the Ahl al-Bayt gladdens the Messenger of Allah, may Allah bless him and his family; and whoever gladdens the Messenger gladdens Allah Himself.

Beware of suspicion, for it brings your deeds to nothing. Shun disputation, for it disgraces you, and argument, for it turns you from Allah. *Those who went before you learned silence, while you are still learning speech.* Blessed is the one who keeps silent in place of cursing and who endures every evil for the sake of Allah; these are in truth the people of faith. By God, were one of you to give in alms gold equal to the weight of the earth, then envy his brother, he would be burned with it on the Day of Resurrection.

If you desire the unalloyed love of your brother, do not mock him, do not contend with him, do not display your greatness over him, and do not quarrel with him. Do not unburden your secret to your friend, lest tomorrow he become an enemy and wound you with it. *True eloquence does not lie in sharpening the tongue or multiplying speech, but in shaping meaning and presenting the proof of the Oneness of Allah.* The one who does not guard his tongue in anger and does not deal beautifully with his friend is not of us.

Know that no piety is greater than forgiving your believing brother and restraining yourself from his backbiting.

Establish love by the exchange of gifts. When one gives you a gift, know that Allah has placed that gift in your hand by means of him: he deserves your praise and thanks, but never forget that the Giver, in truth, is Allah. Do not befriend the sinners, lest they make you partner in their sins.

If Allah bestows a blessing upon you and you desire its permanence, then praise Him with much praise and glorification, *for Allah has said in His Book, "If you are grateful, I will surely give you more"*. If you find a constriction in your sustenance, then seek much forgiveness, *for Allah has said in His Book, "Ask forgiveness of Me, and I shall forgive you."*

Eat not what you dislike, for such food breeds folly. Do not eat until you are hungry; when you eat, eat only what is lawful, and say *Bismi'llāh al-Raḥmān al-Raḥīm* before eating. Remember that man fills no vessel more evil than his stomach; since he must fill it, let him fill a third with food, a third with water, and a third with air.

If one accuses you, say to him: *May Allah forgive me if what you say is true, and forgive you if you have lied.* Whoever threatens you with an evil tongue, return him good counsel and have a care for him.

Ask the scholar what you do not know, but not in order to confound or test him. Beware of grafting your opinion onto religion, and do not let your neck become a bridge for others to cross over. Every perfection lies in the understanding of religion and in patience under affliction.

It is a sign of patience that you should be content with the lowest seat in any gathering you enter; that you greet the one whom you meet; that you do not dispute about a matter when the truth is plain; and that you do not crave praise for your piety.

When your brother is absent, remember him with the best of words — with the words you would wish him to remember you. If you intend a good deed, hasten to it; if you intend an evil one, hold yourself back.

The one who worships without knowledge, the Imam concluded, *is like a man pursuing a mirage: the swifter he runs towards it, the farther his destination recedes from him.*

*(Source: Wilāyat Ahl al-Bayt — Imam Jaʿfar al-Ṣādiq, peace be upon him.)*

## The Primary and Secondary Causes of Every Obligation

For every religious obligation, Allah has appointed two causes whose conjunction alone makes the duty binding. Of these two, the first is prior and general; the second is consequent and particular. The prior cause is, as it were, the ground that makes the second cause possible, and the duty becomes incumbent only when this second, specifying cause is present.

Consider the noon prayer. Its general cause is the day itself, since the prayer cannot fall in the night; the day is therefore the first and prior cause. Yet the prayer is not offered at any moment of the day at random: it is bound to the sun's passing of the meridian. The declension of the sun is thus the second and particular cause — the cause reserved for the actual binding of the obligation. The same pattern obtains in every prayer.

So too with the alms tax. The possession of the nisāb (the legal threshold of wealth) is the first cause; but the obligation does not fall upon the owner the moment the threshold is reached. A full year must turn over upon that wealth; the lapse of the year is the second cause, and only then does the zakāt become due.

Likewise with the fast. The sighting that severs the new moon from the old, marking the first day of Ramaḍān, is the first cause; yet this does not by itself oblige one to fast. The actual binding cause is the breaking of the dawn — and this is the second cause of fasting.

So with the pilgrimage. The capacity to bear its expense and its travel is the prior and most general condition; but the obligation does not arise the moment one becomes capable. There is a designated day and a designated place — and this temporal and spatial fixing is the second cause that binds the duty.

So too with jihād. Its first cause is rebellion against the Imam of the age; but rebellion does not by itself make jihād incumbent. Only the issuance of the command of the Imam, peace be upon him, brings the duty into force.

In contrast to all of this, *wilāya* (the loving allegiance owed to the Imam) is an obligation with no second cause whatever. It is binding upon the human being in every state, from birth to death. For this reason, it is not assigned to any one of the bodily organs but to the heart, which is the inalienable possession of every human being, the seat by which life itself is sustained, and the one place in which a person cannot lie. Even when the unjust hand presses a sword to the throat and compels the tongue to falsehood for the sake of preserving life, *it is the tongue that speaks the lie, and not the heart.* No power but the self has any access to the heart. It is for this reason that every other act rests upon *wilāya* as its ground: because *wilāya* belongs to that one faculty over which every human being possesses complete and unbreakable command.[^cite-21][^cite-22]

*(Source: al-Majālis al-Muʾayyadiyya — the Select Councils.)*

[^cite-21]: Qāḍī al-Nuʿmān places *walāya* at the head of the seven pillars of Islam in *Daʿāʾim al-Islām*, vol. 1, opening *Kitāb al-Walāya*, where it is declared the ground upon which the validity of all other acts of worship depends — a doctrinal anchor for the present argument that the other obligations cannot stand without it.
[^cite-22]: For the esoteric reading of *walāya* as the inward locus of religion — the act of the heart that no outward coercion can dislodge — see Qāḍī al-Nuʿmān, *Taʾwīl al-Daʿāʾim*, in the opening sections on the interior meaning of the pillars; the doctrine that *walāya* belongs to the heart, and the heart alone, is integral to the Fatimid daʿwa's understanding of its uncoerced and continuous obligation.
