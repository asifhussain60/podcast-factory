## The Najāsa (Impurity) of the Disbelievers and Polytheists

In this world, one frequently encounters those who reject faith (kāfir) and polytheists (mushrik) who possess admirable outward character and speak in measured, pleasant tones. Their exterior appearance often impresses the unwary believer, who may regard them with undeserved respect and honor. Yet the Almighty makes plain their true condition, declaring their essential impurity—not merely a surface blemish but a corruption of the very soul.

This doctrine carries multiple layers of meaning. In one interpretation, the Qurʾānic verse concerning ⟪ar:مسجد الحرام⟫ (the Sacred Mosque) signifies the essence of the Imam (ʿalayhi al-salām), for neither the polytheist nor those who harbor affection or enmity toward him can penetrate the Imāmate's ⟪ar:ھیکل⟫ (sanctuary or structure). Rather, the verse's tenor indicates that they cannot even approach such a sanctuary—let alone enter it.[^cite-1] Just as polytheists are forbidden to draw near the Kaʿba, so too are they barred from the Imāmate, because both constitute sanctified loci of divine presence.

In another dimension, the Qurʾānic verse opposes the polytheist's self (nafs) to the believer's heart (qalb), for the believer's heart is fashioned like ⟪ar:بیت اللہ⟫ (the House of God)—a spiritual sanctuary. The believer must therefore guard against admitting the affection or influence of polytheists into his heart; to do so is to permit them to lodge within his spiritual structure, whereupon they obstruct spiritual advancement through ⟪ar:فضلات ھابطۃ⟫ (descending filth). Such inward contamination proves far more dangerous than bodily defilement.

## Urine, Excrement, and Sleep (as Sources of Impurity)

The Messenger of Allāh (ṣallā Allāhu ʿalayhi wa-ālihi), the Commander of the Faithful (Mawlānā ʿAlī), Imām al-Bāqir, and Imām Jaʿfar al-Ṣādiq (ʿalayhim al-salām) have transmitted that certain emissions invalidate ritual ablution (wuḍūʾ). These include discharge from the front or rear orifice—whether urine, feces, flatus, or semen.[^cite-2] Apart from these corporeal substances, there exists another cause of ritual impurity: sleep that entirely overcomes the mind. The Imāms (ʿalayihm al-salām) have established that one who falls into such profound slumber—remaining wholly unconscious of his state—must renew ablution upon waking. However, if sleep overtakes one only lightly, and he retains awareness of his movements, sensation, and hearing, his ablution remains valid.

**Urine and Feces**

The human body excretes diverse substances: tears from the eyes, nasal moisture, cerumen from the ears, saliva from the mouth, perspiration from the pores. Yet the material discharged from the front and rear openings exceeds all others in severity of impurity, which is why ablution becomes obligatory solely upon their expulsion. Urine carries greater impurity than feces.[^cite-3] In their spiritual significance, words and phrases uttered by theological opponents and innovations they have fabricated from their own desires exemplify such impurities: whether arising from incorrect external obedience, or from unrealistic taʾwīl (esoteric interpretation), these represent the contamination of doctrine.

**Sleep**

Profound sleep—the dimming of intellect and abandonment of the path of true religion—represents ignorance and heedlessness toward taʾwīl and recognition of the True Imām. The person rendered wholly unconscious, bereft of worldly awareness, mirrors one spiritually oblivious to the straight path of religion and the realities of the Hereafter. His negligence indicates that he has fallen under the sway of his opponents' heretical teachings, whether materially or spiritually. Therefore, such a person must purify himself by acquiring the knowledge of spiritual reality (maʿrifat). Conversely, one who dozes lightly while retaining full awareness of his movements resembles a believer in a state of partial heedlessness who has not yet succumbed to the innovations and corrupted doctrines of disbelievers and polytheists. For such a believer, renewal of ablution is not obligatory.

## The Root of Impurities — the Mothers of Sins (Ummahāt al-Maʿāṣī)

Prayer and ablution have been conjoined by divine law. Through their combined performance, the believer eliminates three cardinal spiritual impurities whose very existence generates all manner of defilement: arrogance (⟪ar:کبر⟫), greed (⟪ar:حرس⟫), and envy (⟪ar:حسد⟫). To approach the state of prayer after ablution signifies entering the protection of the Imam of the Age (al-Imām al-Zamān, ʿalayhi al-salām) and withdrawing from Satan's defilement. These mothers of sin hollow out the roots of our religion.

Our master ʿAlī ibn Sulaymān (may Allāh elevate his station) wrote in his work *Shuʿaʿ al-Anwār*: In the time of Ādam (ʿalayhi al-salām), there existed twelve angels:

| ⟪ar:جبرئیل⟫ | ⟪ar:میکائیل⟫ | ⟪ar:اسرافیل⟫ | ⟪ar:عزازیل⟫ | ⟪ar:دردیائیل⟫ | ⟪ar:سحطائیل⟫ |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| ⟪ar:امحائیل⟫ | ⟪ar:تحیائیل⟫ | ⟪ar:ھوبائیل⟫ | ⟪ar:ہرقیائیل⟫ | ⟪ar:ملکیائیل⟫ | ⟪ar:حرقیائیل⟫ |

One of these, ⟪ar:عزازیل⟫, became ⟪ar:ابلیس لعین⟫ (Iblīs, the accursed). Through ⟪ar:کبر⟫ (arrogance), his soul conquered his form; he declared, "I am created from fire," and refused to prostrate before Ādam because he was fashioned from clay.[^cite-4] The remaining eleven angels shield the faithful from Iblīs's temptations and preserve them from straying. Even when believers are momentarily deceived, these angels redirect them to ⟪ar:صراط مستقیم⟫ (the Straight Path).

From arrogance (al-kibr) arose Iblīs: cursed, he descended from divine mercy and remains accursed until the Day of Judgment. His pride—that he was created from fire while Ādam was created from clay—accumulated all forms of arrogance within him, bringing about his fall. He became the source of the serpent.

From greed (al-ḥirs) arose Ḥawwāʾ (Eve): she and Ādam were induced to eat the forbidden fruit, and this lust drove them both from Paradise.

From envy (al-ḥasad) arose Qābīl (Cain): when Allāh did not accept his offering but accepted that of Hābīl (Abel, ʿalayhi al-salām), envy compelled him to slay his brother. Through arrogance, a person incurs curse before Allāh; through greed, he is deprived of divine mercy; through envy, he is driven to murder—both outwardly and inwardly.

| Arrogance | Greed | Envy |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Iblīs | Eve | Cain |
| Cursed | Distance from Mercy | Murder |

These three vices are called ⟪ar:امھات معاصی⟫ (Mothers of Sins) because seventy evil offspring arise from each. The *Daʿāʾim al-Islām* functions as a cure for these seventy spiritual maladies. Ablution and purity constitute the means by which sins and polytheism are eradicated.

## Purification from Najāsa (Procedural Rules)

Impurity enters when we lend ear to Satan's summons (daʿwa) in the spiritual realm. No matter how many times a garment is laundered, cleanliness is achieved only in the place where impurity adhered; the already-clean portions remain unaffected. Similarly, when we heed the Iblīsī call and persist in it, various spiritual evils contaminate our souls. For this reason, the Prophet (ṣallā Allāhu ʿalayhi wa-ālihi) established seven prescribed supplications of the Sharīʿa.

**Bodily and Spiritual Impurities**

Corporeal impurities arise from exhalations of the esophagus, blood, pus, and abdominal discharge. Spiritual impurities, by contrast, consist of ignorance, disobedience, polytheism (shirk), ⟪ar:تعطیل⟫ (denying divine attributes), ⟪ar:تشبیح⟫ (anthropomorphism—attributing material form to the Divine), befriending the enemies of Allāh's saints, and harboring disgust toward the friends of Allāh.

**Three Modes of Purification**

Three methods exist for eliminating physical and spiritual impurities, rendering the person worthy to stand in prayer:

| Pure Soil | Pure Water | Purification by Fire |
| --- | --- | --- |
| The Knowledge of the Sharīʿa | The Science of Taʾwīl | The Realities of Faith (Ḥaqāʾiq) |

The first pertains to external purification of the body; the second addresses the heart and the commanding self (⟪ar:نفس أمارۃ بالسوء⟫, nafs al-ammāra bi-al-sūʾ)—the self that urges toward evil, whose seat is the liver. This self is the satanic impulse flowing through the veins with the blood, forever commanding transgression. Just as water removes bodily impurity, so the impurities of ⟪ar:قلب و نفس⟫ (heart and soul) are cleansed through diverse spiritual sciences.

**The Perpetual Need for Purification**

Physical and psychological purification (taṭhīr) constitutes an ongoing process ⟪ar:من الحد المہد⟫ throughout life. Despite untiring effort, one remains perpetually surrounded by sin; no person can claim that, having once acquired knowledge, he shall never again transgress. Just as one who leaves the lavatory cannot declare that he shall never again require its use, so the believer must continuously study and internalize the sciences to maintain spiritual purity. The need to return repeatedly arises from our inherent deficiencies: persistent corruptions enter our spiritual diet, which we must ceaselessly remove. Similarly, through their ⟪ar:استحسان و میلان⟫ (preference and inclination), the enemies of truth introduce defects into our souls, which must be eliminated through ⟪ar:عولم الہیۃ⟫ (divine sciences) and their means.

## The Lavatory (Bayt al-Khalāʾ)

Ḥamīd al-Dīn al-Kirmānī (may Allāh elevate his station), in his *Risālat al-Wadīʾa*, states: A person who dwells in human form yet fails to recognize the defects of his soul has become more contemptible than ⟪ar:اھبط من القرود والخنازیر⟫ (lower than the apes and swine).

The human body contains twelve apertures through which ⟪ar:حسیۃ اور نامیۃ⟫ (sensory and spiritual perceptions) are established. In correspondence with these, twelve etiquettes govern the lavatory:

| 2 Eyes | 2 Nostrils | 2 Ears | 1 Mouth | 2 Breasts | 1 Navel | 2 Lower Orifices |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |

These twelve lavatory etiquettes correspond to twelve realities of the spiritual realm and the ⟪ar:دعوت ھادیۃ⟫ (Guided Summons). The lavatory is the dwelling of impure souls—those of disbelievers and polytheists. Everything pertaining to these souls, even their very breath, carries impurity. All these satanic forces possess potential existence and shall journey toward the Seven Doors after death. For this reason, upon entering the lavatory, we recite the protective invocation ⟪ar-quote:أعوذ باﷲ من الرجس النجس الخبیث المخبث الشیطان العین الرجیم⟫ ("I seek refuge with Allāh from filth, impurity, wickedness, corruption, the accursed Satan and the evil eye"). Through this supplication, we invoke Allāh's—that is, the Imām's—protection from seven spiritual impurities:

| 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 | 5 | 6 | 7 |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Filth (al-Rijs) | Impurity (al-Waqs) | Malice (al-Aks) | Malignancy (al-Baʿr) | The Devil (al-Shaytān) | The Evil Eye (al-ʿAyn) | Destruction (al-Damar) |

Upon seeing the waste, one recites: ⟪ar-quote:اللّٰھُمَّ الرزُقنِیَ الحَلاَلَ وَ جَنِّبنِی الحرام⟫ ("O Allāh, grant me lawful sustenance and protect me from unlawful sustenance"). The Prophet (ṣallā Allāhu ʿalayhi wa-ālihi) has taught that when a person defecates, an angel compels him to gaze upon his excrement, saying: "O son of Ādam! Behold what thou hast consumed and into what it has transformed! This is the world for which thou strivest with such eagerness. Observe whence it came and whither it hath gone!" That is, although the world and its pleasures appear outwardly as fragrant and delicious as nourishment, when the veil of refinement is lifted upon death, their true nature is revealed—as repugnant and foul as waste itself. So too with the disbeliever and polytheist: when the curtain of outward propriety is withdrawn at death, his inner corruption becomes manifest, and the believer exclaims, "What deceived me about thee, and what thou hast become!"[^cite-5]

After completing one's need, we recite: ⟪ar-quote:الحمد اللہ الذی امات عنی الآذا⟫ ("Praise be to Allāh, who has removed my suffering"). This indicates that the substances we expel, though they issue from us, do not truly belong to our essential nature; their departure brings relief. The phrase ⟪ar-quote:وھنانی طعامی و شرابی⟫ ("and He has granted ease in my food and drink") appears in two supplications. The first alludes to pleasures arising from external sciences; the second, to pleasures derived from inner sciences. In both instances, we praise Allāh for removing ⟪ar:نفس نامیۃ اور نفس حسیۃ⟫ (the attributive self and the sensory self)—the evils that have accrued to us.

## The Twelve Etiquettes of the Lavatory

The Holy Prophet (ṣallā Allāhu ʿalayhi wa-ālihi) said to ʿAlī (ʿalayhi al-salām): "Whosoever does not fulfill the etiquette of the lavatory and does not know these observances has neither known thee nor me." That is, whoever does not comprehend the twelve ⟪ar:ھیاکل⟫ (sacred structures or limits) of Muḥammad and ʿAlī has not truly known ʿAlī or the Messenger. Only through knowledge of these twelve sacred limits can these two luminous beings be truly understood.

**(1) Do not enter the lavatory barefoot.**

The lavatory's ground does not belong to the realm of the Sharīʿa-based summons (daʿwa), for it represents the spiritual domain of disbelief and polytheism. Wearing slippers signifies maintaining distance and distinction between oneself and the beliefs of that realm. The slippers represent the ⟪ar:نفس حسیۃ⟫ (sensory self) of the believer, distinguishing it from ⟪ar:نفس نامیۃ⟫ (the attributive self). Should a believer enter the abode of those who follow external doctrine without safeguarding his spiritual nature, he would become like them. When a believer mingles with outward-oriented people, he is obligated to veil both his external and internal knowledge (both feet).

**(2) Cover the head in the lavatory.**

The head exemplifies the chief and leader of religion, upon whom attention is focused to understand inner sciences. Concealing the head signifies the necessity of veiling and protecting the guide of one's religion from the apparent worshippers.

**(3) Place the left foot in the lavatory first.**

The left foot represents the teacher and source of external sciences; the right foot, the teacher and source of inner sciences. Through these two guides, the believer's existence becomes active. The significance of this etiquette is that when a believer enters the realm of Islam (as understood externally), he should speak openly and mention the chiefs of Islam with reverence, as taqiyya requires, while acknowledging their miracles.

**(4) Do not sit facing the Qibla.**

The Qibla symbolizes the summoner (dāʿī) of the era or the Imam of the Age. One should not openly display his love for the Imām and those of the inner path in the presence of apparent worshippers.

**(5) Do not sit with your back to the Qibla.**

That is, one should not speak to outward-oriented people of severing ties with the True Summoner (dāʿī al-Ḥaqq) or the Imam of the Age.

**(6) Sit with pressure upon your left foot.**

Burdening the left foot signifies relying upon one's external knowledge in disputation with those of apparent Islam. Thus it is crucial to master external sciences before approaching inner realities. Should a believer lack knowledge of the sciences of the apparent, the external clergy will overwhelm him—as the fish swallowed the Prophet Yūnus (ʿalayhi al-salām). Another meaning is to address apparent worshippers with respect, acknowledging the scholars and leaders whom they revere.

**(7) Do not speak while fulfilling one's needs.**

Urine represents a compound of doubt, polytheism, and hypocrisy. Just as emitted air remains invisible yet its stench is perceived, so the hypocrisy of the hypocrite is not outwardly apparent; only his words and deeds betray his true state. The etiquette signifies that wisdom and taʾwīl (esoteric interpretation) must not be disclosed to apparent worshippers, for extremist believers are composed of the same elements as the bodies of disbelievers and polytheists. The urine and excrement of believers here represent evil concepts formed by doubt and polytheism.

**(8) Do not remain in the lavatory excessively.**

One should not prolong association with outward worshippers and disbelievers. Interact with them only to the extent necessary.

**(9) Do not cleanse the stool with stones, dung, or similar substances.**

That is, one should not remove shirk, disbelief, and doubt through the knowledge of apparent worshippers. Such spiritual impurities can only be purified through the knowledge of the Ahl al-Bayt (the People of the Household)—represented by water.

**(10) In the absence of water, cleanse with three loose clay or brick pieces.**

In the Imām's occultation (ghaybat al-imām), one should remove doubts through the knowledge of the Hujjat (Proof), the Dāʿī (Summoner), and the Maʿzūn (Appointed One).

**(11) Do not pour water upon the impurity; rather, remove it.**

One should not seek to reinterpret the words of external scholars if they have mingled their own conjectures therein, for authentic taʾwīl pertains only to the evident meanings that the Messenger of Allāh (ṣallā Allāhu ʿalayhi wa-ālihi) has explicitly taught. Water applied to the external scholars' doctrines will become spiritually impure if it touches impure matter; subsequently applied to impurity, it too becomes unclean.

**(12) When departing the lavatory, step out with the right foot first.**

Upon leaving the gathering of apparent worshippers, one should reaffirm his love for the Imam of the Age (Imām al-Zamān) and his Guardian (walī), and renew trust in their inner knowledge—exemplified by the right foot.

## The Magians of Muḥammad's Community (a Cautionary Discussion)

The Prophet (ṣallā Allāhu ʿalayhi wa-ālihi) declared: "The Qadariyya are the Magi (al-Majūs) of this Muslim Community." He thus characterized the Qadariyya sect—a Sunni theological school—by comparing them to the ancient Zoroastrians, who worshipped fire and upheld belief in two divine powers. Concerning the Magians, Allāh the Exalted says:

⟪quran 16:51⟫
⟪ar:‏ وَقَالَ ٱللَّهُ لَا تَتَّخِذُوٓا۟ إِلَهَيْنِ ٱثْنَيْنِ ۖ إِنَّمَا هُوَ إِلَهٌۭ وَحِدٌۭ ۖ فَإِيَّىَ فَٱرْهَبُونِ ‎⟫

"And Allāh said: 'Take not two gods in worship. Verily, He alone is the One God. Fear Me, then, alone.'" (Qurʾān 16:51)

In this verse, Allāh refutes the Magian belief in two divine principles. In their cosmology, these forces are termed Yazdan (the principle of light and good) and Ahriman (the principle of darkness and evil). The wise have observed that light possesses a source or origin from which it emanates, whereas darkness possesses none; rather, the absence or withdrawal of light is what we call darkness. Thus, the Magi of this Community are those who combine allegiance to the Commander of the Faithful (Amīr al-Muʾminīn, ʿAlī) with allegiance to his opponent—just as the Magians believe in two gods and conflate light with darkness.[^cite-6] The parallel rests upon this theological resemblance.

The wise have further expounded: Light possesses a source, whereas darkness does not. The "light" refers to the Successor of the Messenger of Allāh, and its "source" is the Messenger himself, whom Allāh has established as the sun illuminating the scholars of religion and the Sharīʿa. From this luminous sun flows all the life of the material world and protection from calamity. Similarly, the essence and wellspring of the life of religion and Sharīʿa is the light of the Messenger of Allāh (ṣallā Allāhu ʿalayhi wa-ālihi); the opposite of this walāya (guardianship) is oppression, which is immense and devoid of reality.
