## The Inquiry into the Illness of Fatima

When the Messenger of Allah (s.a.w.a.) learned that Mawlānā Fātima lay gravely ill, he journeyed to her dwelling to inquire into her condition, bringing with him one of his companions, ʿImrān ibn Ḥuṣayn. He knocked upon the door, announcing himself with customary deference: "Fātima, may I enter? ʿImrān is with me." She replied, "O Messenger of Allah! I possess not even a single sheet with which to cover myself." He removed his own shawl from his shoulders and draped it across her, saying, "My daughter, clothe yourself with this." [^cite-1]

When he entered and inquired after her welfare, she began to weep—not from pain alone, but from profound longing at her father's nearness. Through her tears she spoke: "O Messenger of Allah! Once we knew only hunger and deprivation, with no sustenance to ease our suffering. Now there is no remedy for this sickness, no medicine to restore me."

The Messenger embraced her tenderly, and his own eyes filled with tears. He who had remained unmoved when the stones of Ṭāʾif rained down upon him now wept at the sight of his daughter's suffering. His tears fell freely as he said:

> By Him who has sent your father as a bearer of truth! For three days now I have not tasted even a morsel of bread. Yet had I willed it, these mountains would have been rendered into gold and silver for me. I chose otherwise.

He drew her close and spoke words of comfort: "Fātima! Know that your honor is exalted—you are the foremost of the women of Paradise. Such is not the station of the chiefs of Paradise easily found among creation."[^cite-2]

## The Sacrifice of Abraham and Ishmael

An old man, ninety-two years of age, stands with a knife trembling in his hand. The boy beside him is thirteen. For sixty years Abraham had prayed that God would grant him offspring. At last, in his extremity, God answered. Now, at an age when a man can scarce slaughter a fowl, God commands him to this ultimate trial. Both father and son stand in the valley of Mīnā.

Abraham turns to his son and speaks with measured gravity: "My son, I have seen in a dream that I am slaughtering you. Consider—what is your counsel?" He does not command outright; he asks. The Qurʾān presents us with this moment deliberately, for it teaches us how a father must train his child. Abraham does not consult the boy about the color of his garments or the decoration of a chamber. He seeks the boy's judgment concerning his own sacrifice—his own slaughter. [^cite-3]

⟪ar-quote:فَلَمَّا بَلَغَ مَعَهُ ٱلسَّعْىَ قَالَ يَبُنَىَّ إِنِّىٓ أَرَىٰ فِى ٱلْمَنَامِ أَنِّىٓ أَذْبَحُكَ فَٱنظُرْ مَاذَا تَرَىٰ⟫

God has not yet commanded it; Abraham has only seen it in a dream. The dreams of prophets are true, yet when it concerns our own children, we compose a thousand interpretations, hesitating and equivocating. Abraham does not hesitate. He has seen it in the dream, and so he asks his son directly: ⟪ar-quote:فَٱنظُرْ مَاذَا تَرَىٰ⟫ "Tell me what you perceive."

And what does Ishmael answer? Not with fear, not with resistance, but with submission and clarity:

⟪ar-quote:قَالَ يَٓأَبَتِ ٱفْعَلْ مَا تُؤْمَرُ⟫

"Father, do that which you are commanded. Hasten to accomplish what God has ordained." The boy continues: ⟪ar-quote:سَتَجِدُنِىٓ إِن شَآءَ ٱللَّهُ مِنَ ٱلصَّبِرِينَ⟫ "God willing, you will find me among the patient ones."[^cite-4]

In these verses, the Qurʾān instructs us about the nature of true fatherhood and true sonship. Ishmael understands what his father is saying. He grasps it fully: "You will slaughter me, and I shall be slaughtered. Yet in exchange for you, I shall have gained God—and God is better than you. In exchange for the world, I shall have gained Paradise—and Paradise is better than the world."

Yet Ishmael harbors one tender concern. "Father," he says, "I think of my mother, how she shall be utterly shattered when she learns of my slaughter." And with this thought, he removes his shirt and gives it to Abraham. "Take this garment," he instructs, "and give it to my mother as a sign. When memory fails, this shirt will remind her of me. And cover me with your own mantle, and bury me here in this place."

Then, his voice steady, he adds: "Bind my hands and feet tightly with rope, so that if I writhe in agony, your heart will not be wounded by witnessing my suffering."

Consider this moment. Sixty years of prayer had preceded this child's birth. Sixty years of supplication. And now Abraham binds the hands and feet of his son as a butcher binds the limbs of a goat. He lays him upon his face so that the boy's countenance will not be visible. Then Abraham kneels upon the boy's back, lifts his head, and places the knife beneath his throat.

He lifts his eyes toward heaven and speaks words of burning intensity: "O my Lord! O my Friend! Consider what you have made of my heart. Is Ishmael more beloved to you than you are to me? Is the love of Ishmael in my breast greater than your love? No—a thousand times no! You witness it: I have placed the knife at his throat to honor your command."

At these words, a cry arose among the angels in all seven heavens.

Abraham closes his eyes. "Bismillāh Allāhu Akbar!" In that instant, God places a great ram—a creature from Paradise—beneath the knife in place of Ishmael, so swiftly that Abraham does not perceive the exchange. He draws the blade downward, believing he has sacrificed his son. Then a cry comes from the heavens: "Abraham! It is enough! You have fulfilled the vision!"

⟪ar-quote:إِنَّ هَذَا لَهُوَ ٱلْبَلَٓؤُا۟ ٱلْمُبِينُ۔ وَفَدَيْنَاهُ بِذِبْحٍ عَظِيمٍۢ۔ وَتَرَكْنَا عَلَيْهِ فِى ٱلْآخِرِينَ ۔سَلَمٌ عَلَىٰٓ إِبْرَهِيمَ ۔كَذَلِكَ نَجْزِى ٱلْمُحْسِنِينَ ۔ إِنَّهُۥ مِنْ عِبَادِنَا ٱلْمُؤْمِنِينَ⟫

"Verily, this was the manifest trial. We ransomed him with a mighty sacrifice. We left upon him a blessing among the generations to come. Peace be upon Abraham! Thus do We reward those who do good. He was indeed among Our believing servants."[^cite-5]

## The Messenger's Suffering in Taif

The Messenger of Allah (s.a.w.a.) flees for his very life in Ṭāʾif. The people of the city pursue him, raining stones upon his body. He knocks upon the closed doors of the great families, begging sanctuary: "Will you not shelter me? Will you not offer sanctuary to one who seeks refuge?" None opens their door. He is struck again and again. He rises, bloodied. He falls. He rises again. His body bleeds so profusely that both his shoes fill with blood.

For whom does he endure this? For whom is this anguish borne?

What must have become of the heart of my Messenger—the greatest being in the universe, the supreme entity by whom God Himself has sworn! Then, wounded and alone, he complained unto God:

> ⟪ar:اللَّهُمَّ إِنِّي أَشْكُو إِلَيْكَ ضَعْفَ قُوَّتِي وَقِلَّةَ حِيلَتِي وَهَوَانِي عَلَى النَّاسِ يَا أَرْحَمَ الرَّاحِمِينَ، أَنْتَ رَبُّ الْمُسْتَضْعَفِينَ وَأَنْتَ رَبِّي إِلَى مَنْ تَكِلُنِي إِلَى بَعِيدٍ يَتَجَهَّمُنِي أَمْ إِلَى عَدُوٍّ مَلَّكْتَهُ أَمْرِي إِنْ لَمْ يَكُنْ بِكَ عَلَيَّ غَضَبٌ فَلَا أُبَالِي وَلَكِنَّ عَافِيَتَكَ هِيَ أَوْسَعُ لِي أَعُوذُ بِنُورِ وَجْهِكَ الَّذِي أَشْرَقَتْ لَهُ الظُّلُمَاتُ وَصَلَحَ عَلَيْهِ أَمْرُ الدُّنْيَا وَالْآخِرَةِ مِنْ أَنْ تُنْزِلَ بِي غَضَبَكَ أَوْ يَحِلَّ عَلَيَّ سَخَطُكَ لَكَ الْعُتْبَى حَتَّى تَرْضَى وَلَا حَوْلَ وَلَا قُوَّةَ إِلَّا بِكَ⟫

"O Allah! I complain to You of my weakness, my lack of means, and my humiliation before the people. O Most Merciful of the merciful! You are the Lord of the weak and lowly, and You are my Lord. To whom do You entrust me? To a stranger who frowns upon me? Or to an enemy to whom You have granted authority over my affairs? If Your anger is not upon me, then I care not. Yet Your mercy encompasses me. I take refuge in the light of Your countenance by which the darkness is illumined, and by which are set right all affairs of this world and the next—that You bring not down upon me Your wrath, nor that Your displeasure settle upon me. To You belong all complaints, until You are pleased. There is no strength and no power save in You."[^cite-6]

## The Passing of Amina

He was six years of age when Ḥazrat Āminā passed away. For a six-year-old child, the entire cosmos is the mother—companion, nurse, guardian, and world entire. He set out upon his final pilgrimage fifty-seven years later. It had been fifty-seven years since his mother's death, when he was but a child of six, and now, at sixty-three years of age, he arrived at Abwāʾ and passed by her grave. The month was April—the Arabian sun blazes mercilessly.

He descended from his camel and sat at the head of his mother's grave. Drawing his knees upward, he rested his head upon them and wept. He wept as little children weep—with the abandonment of pure sorrow. Hour after hour he sat there, heedless of the burning sun, unmindful of the scorching heat, seeing nothing but the grave of his mother. [^cite-7]

For whom was the universe created, God subjected to such a test. His mother was but twenty-five years old when death claimed her. Had she lived but four more days, she would have reached Mecca and there received the care of all her kinfolk and guardians. Yet death found her in the wilderness, far from home. Her grave stands upon a small hill, and her son—then only six years old—was attended by none save Umm Ayman, his nurse, who served as both mother and guardian, sister and friend, companion and servant.

Imagine a child of six years, for whom the entire universe is embodied in his mother. When he asked for water, the Messenger of Allah ran like an arrow to bring it to him. How deeply worldly concerns have drawn us away from what truly matters. As his mother's breath grew faint, the Prophet watched—helpless, a child watching death approach.

At last she spoke her final words:

> Every living thing must taste death, every youth must pass through age. Every ancient name fades into obscurity. Today too I depart, but my memory shall endure throughout the world—for I leave behind a perfect good, which shall purify all creation. The spirit took flight from the blessed form.

Life must surrender its hold.

Who dug the grave? Who passed that way? Umm Ayman turned from the procession, looked back, and saw that the Prophet was missing. She turned the camel around and climbed the rise, wondering what she would find. There was the Prophet of Allah, clinging to his mother's grave, weeping. When Umm Ayman drew near, she heard him cry out: "O Mother! Where have you left me? You knew I had no one but you. Where shall I go now? To whom shall I turn?" He held fast to the grave, saying: "Mother, release me! I will remain with you here, at your grave." But Umm Ayman took him gently and made him mount the camel. [^cite-8]

It took three nights of travel to reach Mecca. All that time, no hand came to wipe away his tears.

## Walk Among Their Ruins

⟪ar-quote:أَحیَ قَلبَکَ بِالمَوعِضَۃ، وَ نَوِّرہُ بِالحِکمَۃ، وَقوِّھِی بِالزُّھَد، وَذَلِّلہُ بِالمَوت، وَقَرِّرہُ بِالفَناَء،وحَزِّنہُ وَسُولَتَ الدَّھَر،وَتَقَلُّبَ الَّیالِی وَالأَیَّام، و⟪ar-quote:أَعرِض علیهِ أخبارالماضین وذکرهُ بما أصابَ مَن کانَ قَبلُکَ مِن الاوَّلِین ۔⟫⟫

Let the heart be revived by admonition and illumined by wisdom. Strengthen it through asceticism; humble it with the remembrance of death; confirm it with the knowledge of annihilation. Sadden it through the changing signs of time, through the turning of nights and days, so that it may understand the swift passage of moments. Present to it the histories of those who have passed away; remind it of what befell those nations before you.[^cite-9]

Go, sometimes, to the cemetery. There is scarcely room to dig a grave—the graves are piled upon one another. These same people once broke the bounds of lawful and unlawful to raise mansions of twenty kanals upon the earth. They lied; they deceived; they transgressed all limits of human decency. Today they sleep in four feet of earth. This is the end of this world. This is what becomes of it.

Mawlānā ʿAlī has said:

⟪ar-quote:و سِر فِی دِیارِهِم فانْظُر فی ⟪ar-quote:آثارِهِم ۔⟫⟫

Walk among their dwellings and consider their ruins. ⟪ar-quote:⟪ar-quote:أَنتَقِلُو مِن دارِ الغُرور وَنَزلُو⟫ They departed from the house of deception and descended into nothingness⟫.[^cite-10]

Wander through the shattered vestiges of their lives, and observe what remains. What trace of them lingers in this world? And see how their caravan journeyed from the house of illusion to ruin and desolation and darkness. Do you discern any sign of them today?

My children, one day you too shall perish. One day you too shall pass away and sleep beneath the dust forever. So what then must you do?

⟪ar-quote:⟪ar-quote:لا تبع آخرتک بالدنیا⟫ Do not sell your Hereafter for this world!!⟫ [^cite-11]

My children! A day will come when before you stand two things: on one side the world, on the other the Hereafter. On one side truth, on the other falsehood. On one side God, on the other the world and its pleasures. If the world is saved, the Hereafter is lost. If the Hereafter is saved, the world is left behind.

So when that day arrives—if you must choose—abandon the world and preserve the Hereafter. Let the world go.

⟪ar-quote:⟪ar-quote:لا تبع آخرتک بالدنیا⟫ Do not sell your Hereafter for this world!!⟫

If you must part from the world, release it, for death shall separate you from it in any case. Do not part from the Hereafter! Do not relinquish it! Hold fast to it! This is the counsel of the father, watered with the blood of poison, impressed upon you by the bowing of the head upon spears.

## Junayd of Baghdad and the Girl's Circumambulation

One day Junayd Baghdādī thought to himself: "Why should I perform the circumambulation (ṭawāf) during the rush of day? Let me circle the House of God at night." When he arrived at the Kaʿba, he perceived a girl performing ṭawāf before him, and she recited these verses:

| ⟪ar-quote:أبىَ الحُبُّ أنْ يَخفىٰ وكَمَ قَدْ كَتَمتُهُ⟫ | ⟪ar-quote:فأصْبَح عِندى قَدْ أناخَ وَطَنَّباَ⟫ |
| --- | --- |
| Love refused to remain hidden, however much I concealed it. And now that love has encamped in my heart and raised its tent. |
| ⟪ar-quote:إذَا اشْتَدَّ شَوقِى هاَمَ قَلبى بِذِكرِهِ⟫ | ⟪ar-quote:فَإن رُمتُ قُرباً مِنْ حَبِيبِى تَقَرَّباَ⟫ |
| When my longing intensifies, my heart wanders in remembrance of Him. And when I seek to draw near to my Beloved, He draws near to me. |
| ⟪ar-quote:يَبدُو فأفْنىٰ ثُمَّ أحيىٰ لَهُ بِه⟫ | ⟪ar-quote:وَيُسعِدُنِى حتَّى ألَذَّ وأطْرَبا⟫ |
| When I perceive Him, I am annihilated, and then through Him I am brought back to life. He makes me blissful, until my soul finds delight and ecstasy. |

Her meaning was clear: she had struggled to conceal love, yet it had conquered her entirely, pitching its tent within her breast. When longing grew intense, her heart yearned at the memory of the Beloved. When she sought to draw near to Him, He would draw near to her. Absence brought her dying away; presence restored her to life. His love for her only increased her desire for union.[^cite-12]

Even a scholar as learned as Junayd Baghdādī failed to grasp her meaning. Thinking her words were mere frivolity, he said sharply: "O girl! What nonsense do you speak in the House of God?" 

The girl turned upon him with luminous severity and said:

⟪ar-quote:لَولا التُّقىٰ لَم تَرِنى⟫ ⟪ar-quote:أَهجر طِيبِ الوَسَنِ⟫
⟪ar-quote:إنَّ التُّقىٰ شَرَّدَنى⟫⟪ar-quote:كما تَرى عَن وَطَنى⟫

⟪ar-quote:أفرَّ مِن وَجدِى بِهِ⟫ ⟪ar-quote:فَحُبُّہ هَيِّمُنِى⟫

"It is piety (taqwā) alone that has drawn me here. Without it, you would not see me—I would be resting peacefully at home. Piety has scattered me from my homeland as you see. His love has driven me to flee the sweetness of sleep. I cannot flee from my anguish through His love; rather, His love has rendered me mad."[^cite-13]

Junayd Baghdādī was struck with sudden realization. She spoke of something entirely different from what he had understood. This girl surpassed him in the knowledge of God. Then the girl asked him: "And you—whose circumambulation are you performing?"

"The House of Allah," he replied.

Upon hearing this, the girl laughed aloud and said:

⟪ar-quote:يَطُوفُونَ بِالأَحجاَرُ يَبغُونَ قُربَةٌ⟫ ⟪ar-quote:إليَكَ ؛⟫⟪ar-quote:وَهُم أَقسىٰ قُلُوباً مِنَ الصَّخَرِ⟫
⟪ar-quote:فَلَو أَخلَصُوا فى الوَدُّ غَابَت صِفاَتُهُم⟫ ; ⟪ar-quote:وَقاَمَت صِفَاتُ الوَدَّ للَحقِّ بِالذِّكرِ⟫

"O Allah! How mad are Your servants, who circle stones in search of closeness to You, yet their hearts are harder than stone itself! If they were truthful in their love, they would have lost themselves entirely in that love. They would have forgotten all attributes of self, and in their place would have stood nothing but the attributes of love for the Truth, sustained through remembrance."[^cite-14]

Junayd fell to his knees in awe and confession. He realized that this girl had penetrated mysteries of divine love that he himself had not yet reached. When he lifted his head again to speak, the girl had vanished. She was gone.

## The Tale of Baḥīrā the Monk

God has declared to all the worlds—to pebbles and stones, to beasts and birds, to leaves and trees—that all creation says: "He is my Beloved, my Beloved." [^cite-15]

When the Messenger was but twelve years old, he journeyed to Syria on a trading expedition with his uncle. The caravan passed through Zarqāʾ. There dwelt in that place a Christian monk named Baḥīrā. As the caravan approached, Baḥīrā observed a boy walking among the travelers, and above the boy's head he saw a cloud suspended—a miraculous sign, in that desert of Arabia where no shade exists.

All the people of the caravan sat themselves beneath a great tree seeking relief from the heat. But Baḥīrā observed that when the boy sat, the branches of the tree advanced and bent themselves to cast their shade over his head.

The greatness of this boy was understood by clouds and by trees—understood by creation itself. Yet we, who walk upon the earth, do not comprehend his greatness. The trees of Syria recognized that this was the final prophet, the seal of the messengers, the crown of humanity, the Beloved of God, the bearer of Paradise. His names are Muḥammad, his titles are Muẓammil, Mudaththir, Ṭāhā, Yāsīn. [^cite-16]

Baḥīrā asked the boy's uncle, Abū Ṭālib: "Are all your people here?" He replied, "All are present." Baḥīrā said, "There is one missing. Bring me the child who tends the camels." When the boy arrived, Baḥīrā saw that all the people were sheltered beneath the tree's shade, yet the boy sat in the sun. As soon as the boy took his seat, the branches stepped forward and cast their shadow wholly upon him.

## The Messenger and the Deer

A voice called out: "O Messenger of Allah!" The Prophet turned, but saw no one. Again the voice came: "O Messenger of Allah!" Then he perceived a deer, a rope around its neck, bound to a tree. The creature was calling to him in human speech: "O Messenger of Allah! Your companion has caught me. I am resigned to God's decree—I know what awaits me. Yet I have small children, famished and hungry. If you permit me, might I nurse them? I shall return, I promise you."

The Messenger asked, "If you do not return, what then?"

The deer replied, "O Messenger of Allah! If I do not return, God will consign me to the fire of hell. I fear that more than death itself."

Hearing this, the Messenger untied the rope and set the deer free, saying, "Go to your young ones." And he sat down in that place where the deer had been bound. For he understood something of divine trust—that nothing may be given without permission, and nothing taken without right.[^cite-17]

After a time, the Messenger heard hoofbeats approaching rapidly. The deer came running back through the mountains, swift as the wind. She reached the spot and spoke: "O Messenger of Allah, I am here. I have returned as I promised."

The Messenger retied the rope gently around the deer's neck and waited. Presently the hunter arrived and beheld the Messenger sitting with his deer. Astonished, he asked, "O Messenger of Allah, what brings you here?"

The Prophet replied, "Your animal asked a kindness of me. I granted it."

The hunter, overwhelmed with reverence, said, "O Messenger of Allah, all that I possess belongs to you. Everything is yours. Take the deer. I am honored to give it freely."

The Messenger stood, removed the rope from the deer's neck, and smiled at the creature. He spoke these words:

⟪ar-quote:أِ⟫⟪ar-quote:ذہَبِی بأَھلِکی⟫

"Go to your family. Go in peace. Truth delivers; falsehood destroys." ⟪ar-quote:الصدق ینجی و الکذب یھلک⟫[^cite-18]

The deer bowed its neck before the Messenger. It stepped forward a few paces, then turned and bowed again. It stepped forward once more, turned, bowed its neck in gratitude, and then disappeared into the mountains, vanishing like a dream into the wilderness.
