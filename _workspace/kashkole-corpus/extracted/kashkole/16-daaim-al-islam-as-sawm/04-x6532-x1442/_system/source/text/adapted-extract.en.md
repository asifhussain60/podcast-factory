## Ṣalāt al-Wadāʿ — The Farewell Prayer (the Night of Names)

The Night of Farewell (⟪ar:لیلۃ الوداع⟫, *laylat al-wadāʿ*) and the Night of Fast-Breaking (⟪ar:لیلۃ الفتر⟫, *laylat al-fiṭr*) mark a profound threshold in sacred time. These nights serve as witnesses and guides, pointing towards their spiritual correlates in the cycle of revelation. The example of this correspondence is the Mahdi, who embodies the proof (*ḥujja*, attestation) of the Qa'im, whilst the Night of Farewell constitutes an argument (*ḥujja*) for the Mahdi's own argument—one that points to the conclusion of the Muhammadan era and announces the inauguration of a new age.

| Farewell Night | Night of Eid al-Fiṭr |
| --- | --- |
| The Mahdi's Argument | The Argument of the Qa'im (Mahdi of the End of Time) |

Upon this night, nine sacred words are recited, each repeated ten times. The number ten signifies perfection (*kamāl*, completeness); the ninefold utterance corresponds to nine levels of spiritual ascent, each traversed ten times. Thus ninety degrees of luminous stations unfold. The tenth repetition culminates in the remembrance of the Qa'im—the Establisher upon whose name (⟪ar:علی ذکر السلام⟫, *ʿAlī Dhikr al-Salām*)—blessings descend.[^cite-1]

### Subḥān Allāh

With this invocation of holiness and transcendence, we acknowledge Allah's absolute purity and our own profound ignorance of His true nature. This utterance exemplifies the divinity of the Imam, whose form illuminates the believer on these blessed nights. In the higher spiritual realms, this invocation corresponds to the station of Mary—the veiled one (*al-muḥajjab*) of Muhammad, the Chosen One—upon whom the Mahdi casts the form of the Veiled One of the End of Time. The arguments that precede the believer will unveil the glory of the Mahdi to his specially chosen servants. This unveiling itself is named Subḥān Allāh.[^cite-2]

### Al-Ḥamdu li-Llāh

We express gratitude for the powers and perfections that Allah the Most High has bestowed upon us through this word. It exemplifies the station of the Imam's son, who shall himself become Imam on the morrow. The entire congregation of the Imam assembles at his inner gate (*Bāb al-Bāṭin*, the Gate of the Inward). When the Imam recites the testament to his son, he passes from this world and transfers the temple of Imamate to his successor. This handing over of the sacred trust constitutes the establishment of the next *daʿwa* (the summons, the invitation to faith). Therefore we say: "Praise be to Allah, for the summons has proceeded and has not ceased." Allah is the source of all praise, and their gratitude is eternal. Until the successor is appointed, the gratitude due to Allah remains unfulfilled.

### Istighfār Allāh

By this word, we beseech Allah's pardon for our failings and shortcomings. These are virtues held within the temple of Imam al-Zamān (the Imam of the Age), yet they have been diminished through error. It is narrated of Ayyūb (upon him be peace) that when a worm fell from his body, he would return it gently to his flesh, recognizing those creatures as members of his community who had transgressed.[^cite-3] Similarly, Imam Zayn al-ʿĀbidīn would perform a thousand *rakʿāt* (prayer units) at once, bearing within his devotion a multitude whose sins weighed upon his supplications. In like manner, Imams al-Ḥasan and al-Ḥusayn and Commander of the Faithful ʿAlī laboured to cleanse the courts of their gatherings. These are souls whom the Imam has taken unto himself through love alone, not through their deeds. When we pronounce this word, we are in fact interceding on their behalf, expressing remorse for their transgressions.

In the time of the Mahdi, all polytheists shall gather and wage war. In the first battle, the disbelievers shall prevail—this occurs because of the sins of the believers, yet serves their purification. In the second campaign, the believers shall triumph. After this victory, the state of having been overcome becomes itself a supplication: Istighfār Allāh.

### Lā Ḥawla wa-Lā Quwwata Illā bi-Llāh al-ʿAlī al-ʿAẓīm

*Ḥawl* (change, transformation) refers to a shift in spiritual dimension. We affirm that none possesses the power to transform dimensions save Allah. In another sense, *ḥawl* denotes the outer gate and *quwwa* (power, force) the inner gate. Both forms are unified in the Imam al-Zamān, yet neither the outer nor the inner gate holds dominion over these forms—only Allah, manifested through the Imam, possesses true authority, for the Mighty Eye (⟪ar:العین العظیمۃ⟫, *al-ʿAyn al-ʿAẓīma*) remains veiled within him.

In the circles of those devoted to the Qa'im (*Qāʾim-iyyīn*), this invocation rests upon two gates—the first and the last—which are established for all beginning and ending. The gate of Muhammad (upon him be peace) shall be Khadīja the Greater; the gate of the Mahdi shall be his wife, who will be as Khadīja in the World to Come. By "the Mighty One" (*al-ʿAẓīm*), we mean that these two gates shall lack the strength to bear these tremendous events—their power arises only through His Greatness.

**Khadīja the First**

The Prophet possessed five teachers, among them Khadīja. When the testament was recited concerning ʿAlī, she too became his teacher. Yet ʿAlī was overwhelmed anew.

**Khadīja the Last**

She too shall be teacher to the Mahdi and the Qa'im, yet afterwards she shall transcend them in rank, even as she remains subordinate to them.

### Allāhu Ṣallallāhu ʿalayhī wa-Sallam ʿAlā Abī Ādam

We invoke blessings upon our father Ādam (upon him be peace) and acknowledge the blessing he brought to us—his appearance in this world and the grace that sanctified it. Our first divine principle in this world corresponds to Ādam. All things emerge from this divine origin. When the era is flawed and incomplete, imperfect things manifest; when the era achieves perfection, perfect things appear. When the first body of eternity emerged, within its core were the coordinates of all who would appear throughout it. Each soul's point corresponds to the state it intended in the spiritual realm. The entire cosmos of the peacock gathers in the peacock's beak.

The Prophet possessed all followers from Ādam. He had five great congregations (*masājid*), for which reason he could not recite the Friday sermon without at least five persons present. He shall hand over all these gatherings to our Lord the Mahdi of the Age. The Mahdi shall receive from him many things besides. The Mahdi shall stand in a rank higher than Muhammad. As soon as the Mahdi's veil lifts, Ādam's virtues shall begin to manifest, and all shall come to the Mahdi—that is, the appearance and summaries of the gatherings of the *Mustawdaʿīn* (those entrusted with knowledge).[^cite-4]

### Allāhu Ṣallam ʿAlā Ummunā Hawwāʾ

With this invocation, we send blessings upon our inner mother, Eve. At the beginning of the cosmic body, woman was born from the water left over from this divine origin. The manifestations of a divine principle are called brothers and sisters. Here, the divine principle from which man was born became the divine principle of his wife in marriage. The divine principle from which his wife emerged holds the status of Eve. The points of the Imams' arguments gathered near Eve. This refers to the gathering of the *Wasiyyīn* (the Executors of the Will) who joined the Prophet and whom he entrusted to Fāṭima. This gathering shall be adjacent to the Mahdi at the End of Time, and from it shall reach the Qa'im.

### Allāhu Ṣallam ʿAlā Jāmiʿ al-Anbiyāʾ wa-al-Mursalīn

We invoke blessings upon all Prophets and Messengers—all one hundred twenty-four thousand—for on this night and on the Night of Fast-Breaking, the rays of every Prophet and Messenger are released from the World of Life, affecting our form, soul, and body, and creating within us the power of spiritual development. Prophethood (*nubuwwa*) bears the command of announcement; Messengership (*risāla*, the Sending) bears the command to convey the message along with its proof. Thus the station of Messengership surpasses that of Prophethood.

The gathering of all Prophets and Messengers (*Jāmiʿ al-Anbiyāʾ wa-al-Mursalīn*) indicates that the entire congregation of the *Muṣṭafā* (the Chosen Ones) united with Muḥammad al-Muṣṭafā (upon him be peace), and he surpassed them all, becoming their leader. This gathering shall be connected to the Mahdi of the End of Time through Muḥammad al-Muṣṭafā, and shall be encompassed within his temple.

### Allāhu Ṣallam ʿAlā Muḥammad wa-ʾĀlihi

We then invoke blessings upon all the leaders of the Prophets and Messengers and all the Imams from their families, unto whom Allah says: "O Prophet, were it not for thee, I would not have created the heavens." This indicates that all the *Mustawdaʿīn* shall be connected to the divine principle, and the form of the Mahdi shall continue to illuminate them.

### Aʿūdhu bi-Llāh min al-Shaytān al-Rajīm

Finally, we seek refuge in Allah from Satan the Accursed, that he obstruct not our blessings and spiritual progress. This is the return of all forms of the Mahdi's temple to the protection of the Sustainer (*al-Qayyūm*) from all falsehood and corruption. It points to this truth: although all forms shall be gathered with the Mahdi, the era of the Sustainer shall be accounted separately.

### The Farewell Prayer Itself

The prayers offered on this night are the "soul" animating these ten words. In this prayer, one recites Sūrat al-Fātiḥa once, followed by Sūrat al-Ikhlās ten times, and Sūrat al-Falaq seven times. The single recitation of Sūrat al-Fātiḥa embodies two qualities—one exoteric, one esoteric. Sūrat al-Ikhlās refers to the form of the Qa'im and is recited ten times because he stands at the tenth station. Sūrat al-Falaq refers to your gate and is recited seven times because it corresponds to the seventh station.

Sūrat al-Fātiḥa is the summary of the Qur'an's summaries. In a certain sense, this truth applies equally to Fāṭima. Its seven verses concern the seven *nukabāʾ* (chiefs) of the Imams established by her and the descendants of ʿAlī; their names are hidden within these verses. It begins with the letter *alif*, unconnected to any other, for it is the essence of Fāṭima, distinct and unique. The *lām* that follows is for Allah—the gathering of al-Ḥasan with al-Ḥusayn. The *rāʾ* is for Imam Zayn al-ʿĀbidīn; the *bāʾ* for our Lord al-Bāqir; the *ʿayn* for Imam Jaʿfar al-Ṣādiq; the *lām-ʿalif* for Imam Ismāʿīl; and the *lām-mīm* for Imam Muḥammad ibn Ismāʿīl.

In a single *rakʿa* (prayer unit), we recite these sūras seventeen times in total, corresponding to the seventeen obligatory *rakʿāt* (prayer units of daily prayer), each of which is a form of illumination that shines during prayer. Sūrat al-Fātiḥa corresponds to the eighteenth form, which is perpetually illuminated by the Imamic temple. Thus it is indicated that for the higher circles, this is the form of eternity. When we recite the *istibrāq* (the seeking of radiance) after the Dawn Prayer, we ask for *ishrāq* (illumination) from the veils of these seventeen sūras; for this reason we place a cloth between our face and our hands.

Sūrat al-Nās and Sūrat al-Falaq are the two guardians of the Holy Qur'an—that is, they are the two great forms of the Imamic temple that protect the temple at all times.

## The Four Blessed Nights of the Year

Commander of the Faithful ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib (upon him be peace) said: "I love that a person devote himself to the remembrance and worship of Allah during these four nights in a year."

| 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| First of Rajab | Mid-Shaʿbān | Eid al-Fiṭr | Eid al-Aḍḥā |

The best season of worship extends from the Sacred Month of Rajab to the city of Ramaḍān. The revelation (*tanzīl*, the sending down) was handled by Muḥammad, and the esoteric exegesis (*taʾwīl*, the hermeneutical turning inward) was accomplished by our Lord ʿAlī. To this, there is clear testimony on the night of Mid-Shaʿbān.

These four nights open special festivals that do not reopen throughout the year. The elders among the interpreters perform the *ishrāq* (the sunrise prayer) only on these four nights. When the lights of the heart connect with the lights of the sun, these mighty forms are attracted to both heart and radiance, and their rays are released into this world. The images of the saints (*awliyāʾ*) are connected to the *nūrāniyyah* (luminous dimension). When the Imam's form becomes receptive, he gazes upon his scattered virtues in the world and yearns to gather them as a unified self.

During these nights, these forms labour in concern to gather their scattered remnants and various subtle essences. The process continues of replicating their bodies from above to below and from below to above. The summary of scattering means that certain subtle things separate from them, which require time to recover. When the illuminations of these saints descend to the earth, their greatness and powers likewise affect the surroundings. For this reason, humanity has been urged to pray on these nights, lest the virtues of these nights be lost. These luminous rays resurrect the trumpets of the believers—that is, they take their prepared form from them and elevate it.

On these four nights, the support (*saʿd*) of the Imams rests upon their followers, and the Imams receive their caliphates anew. This remains true in both the period of concealment and the period of manifestation.

## Yawm al-ʿĪdayn — The Day of the Two ʿĪds

### The Importance of Ritual Bathing, Dress, and Fragrance on ʿĪd

Beautiful clothing should be worn on ʿĪd, and one should bathe and apply fragrance, for fine garments are woven from the refined essence of waste, and perfume and bathwater exemplify ascending substances. When the Qa'im—the Establisher upon whose name blessings descend—manifests, the living shall be "gladdened," for they shall be held to account; the dead shall be "silent," for their enemies shall be avenged. The eras rejoice at the vengeance of the Qa'im.

It is commanded that whoever departs from his house for ʿĪd prayer should wear the finest clothes and apply the finest fragrance. Additionally, bathing for ʿĪd has been made obligatory. The water of the ʿĪd bath is composed of the finest substances, purified through periods and reaching the believer. Clothing is woven from the refined essence of waste; perfume too is made of pure and exalted essence. Water, cloth, and scent—all are ascending substances that shall become part of the believer's body in the future. They come on these nights to participate in this believer's body. Should the believer neglect this, the luminous rays are withdrawn from him, and his prayer is returned empty. This has been specifically ordained for ʿĪd and Friday because of the nobility of these nights' stations.[^cite-5]

### The Seventy Days Between the Two ʿĪds

There are seventy days between Eid al-Fiṭr and Eid al-Aḍḥā, represented by the seventy persons who shall appear between the Mahdi and the Qa'im. These people hold an exceedingly high rank, for they are the gates (*bāb*), arguments (*ḥujaj*), and prayers of a temple spanning four hundred thousand years. All of them shall serve as a means of recognizing the Mahdi of the End of Time to the Qa'im—upon whose name blessings descend—and their hearts shall become his temple, just as the seventy intervening days connect Eid al-Fiṭr to Eid al-Aḍḥā. These seventy persons shall depart and reach the Qa'im, after which the Qa'im shall manifest. Many shall come from Egypt, Syria, and Iraq; two from India; Salmān the Persian from Persia. All shall journey first to the Illuminated City and then onward. Referring to this, the Prophet said: "There are seventy scholars between me and my Lord."[^cite-6]

### The *Laṭāʾif* (Subtle Spiritual Essences)

In the ʿĪd prayers, the *kullī* (universal principle) of the sūra of *ishrāq* is performed. Special subtle essences (*laṭāʾif*) descend and ascend within it. In the four sacred nights of the year (*ayām Allāh*—the 17th, 19th, 21st, 23rd of Ramaḍān), the lights descend and ascend. But in Eid al-Aḍḥā, the *laṭāʾif* ascend and descend. Those awakening from the ferment of remorse are called *laṭāʾif*. These are the ignorant among the followers of Moses who do not come to this world, yet have become its masters. Their illuminations appear on these nights. The Imam and the Companions of the Believers are the Companions of the Believers.

Beyond this, the ferments of those Imams who waged *jihād* (spiritual struggle) and endured the Imams' sufferings ascend on these nights. The hidden Imams—from ʿAbdullāh al-Mahdī (the 11th Imam) to ʿĀmir (the 20th Imam)—come into the veil. In addition, the veiled *nuṭaqāʾ* (speakers, the prophets of each era) who waged jihād, and the ferments of their foundations, descend and ascend. And the ferments of the veil of al-Ḥusayn, and the ferments of those who waged jihād in various periods—their essences ascend and descend on ʿĪd.

## Why Did the Prophet Return from ʿĪd Prayer by a Different Route?

When the Messenger of Allah returned after the ʿĪd prayer, he did not depart by the path on which he had come from his house to the mosque. The path from house to mosque signifies the *daʿwa* (the summons, the invitation) and contains the virtues of those who were trusted in the hidden period. The path taken in return from mosque to house is indicative of stability and establishment.

The ʿĪd prayers signify the manifestation of the Mahdi and the Qa'im. Since the reappearance of both shall conclude and the era of the *Mustawdaʿīn* (the entrusted ones) shall begin, the return path is different. The period of the *Mustawdaʿīn* has no place in the era of manifestation (*kashf*), for their services have been completed in the hidden period. The one who turns back with his head bowed (in concealment) and the one who declares openly (in manifestation) both return to the Muslims.

The path which the believer departs from his house to reach the mosque—and whilst walking this path, the air, soil, light, and stones—all are decided and brought to completion. Therefore, it is commanded to take a different route when returning home from the mosque, for the divine right (*ḥaqq*) has been removed from the things that lay upon the previous path. If the believer returns by the same path again, he creates new divine rights.

According to the Sharīʿa (divine law), on Eid al-Aḍḥā, slaughter must be performed first, then ʿĪd prayer. Thus, on the path that the Qa'im took, he slew the tyrant, and then came to the mosque and offered the ʿĪd prayer. On the return, one cannot take the same route because blood stains the road. Pointing to this reality that shall occur in the future, the legislator (*Shāriʿ*) has made this practice a *Sunna* (established custom).

## Why ʿĪd Prayer Is Not Performed on a Rooftop or Inside a House

ʿĪd prayers should not be offered on the roof or within a house. Rather, these prayers must be offered openly in the mosque. This constitutes a clear declaration of the significance of these two ʿĪds. The parables of ʿĪd are exceedingly lofty and shall not appear in concealment, but shall manifest openly and visibly.

The slain shall be slaughtered openly in those days, and their bodies cast into the field. None shall be able to enter a house at that time, nor shall they find refuge upon a roof. They shall not be given any place to hide.

In another sense, houses and rooftops are the bounds of the Imamic dominion in which there is no *qiṣāṣ* (retribution) for them to bear the burden of *jihād* or to argue with others. That is, the people of the islands (*jazāʾir*) who are guilty of murder shall be brought forth for the slaughter of the tyrant. The open field belongs to those who have been entrusted with the responsibility of *jihād*.

## Ṣalāt ʿĪd al-Fiṭr — The Prayer of the Festival of Fast-Breaking

The night of Eid al-Fiṭr is the second pilgrimage of the Mahdi to the End of Time, which shall endure throughout the Mahdi's era and make special people aware of his station. The day of Eid al-Fiṭr refers to our Lord the Mahdi.

| Night of Eid al-Fiṭr | Day of Eid al-Fiṭr |
| --- | --- |
| The Second Argument of the Mahdi | The Mahdi (upon him be peace) |

The Mahdi shall possess both exoteric knowledge and esoteric knowledge. Those who have waged *jihād* shall ascend on the night of ʿĪd, and the eleventh Imam—the Mahdi—shall be referred to through our Lord ʿAbdullāh al-Mahdī, and they shall join him. Apart from them, the Prophet Muḥammad al-Muṣṭafā shall also be connected with you, for you are the Mīm of the World to Come.[^cite-7]

### Examples of the ʿĪd Rakʿāt

Two *rakʿāt* of Eid al-Fiṭr prayer are offered. The first *rakʿa* refers to the father of the Prophet, our Lord ʿAbdullāh ibn ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib (the First), and the second *rakʿa* refers to our Lord ʿAbdullāh the Last, who shall be the father of the Mahdi. ʿAbdullāh the Second shall repeat the virtues of ʿAbdullāh the First.

| First Rakʿa | Second Rakʿa |
| --- | --- |
| Our Lord ʿAbdullāh the First | Our Lord ʿAbdullāh the Last |
| The Mahdi of the End of Time | The station that shall emerge from ʿAbdullāh's essence |

### Examples of the Sūras

In the first *rakʿa* of Eid al-Fiṭr prayer, Sūrat al-Shams is recited, which signifies our Lord the Mahdi, for his station shall be as clear as the sun, and his rank is supreme among all the bounds (*ḥudūd*, the limits) of religion. In the second *rakʿa*, Sūrat al-Ghāshiya is recited, which signifies the terror and dread of the disbelievers and hypocrites, and the peace that the believers shall attain.

| Sūrat al-Shams | Sūrat al-Ghāshiya |
| --- | --- |
| Our Lord the Mahdi (upon him be peace) | Fear and dread upon the Mahdi's manifestation |

### Ṣalāt al-ʿAwad al-Khutba (The Prayer of Return—The Sermon Prayer)

This is followed by the recitation of two *rakʿāt* of Ṣalāt al-ʿAwad al-Khutba (the Prayer of Return and the Sermon). The first constitutes the first *rakʿa* and the second the final *rakʿa*. Fāṭima the First shall repeat the virtues of Fāṭima the Last.

| First Rakʿa | Second Rakʿa |
| --- | --- |
| Fāṭima the First | Fāṭima the Last |

Two *rakʿāt* of the sermon (*khutba*, the address recited in place of the formal sermon) are not recited together with the Imamate. This indicates that the Imam has withdrawn into concealment because of the sins of the believers. When the Mahdi of the End of Time and the Qa'im—upon whose name blessings descend—shall manifest, this prayer shall be lifted. This prayer also signifies that the followers should have the opportunity to face trials, that retribution shall be fulfilled, and that forbidden things shall be removed from the pores of the believers.

## Ṣalāt ʿĪd al-Aḍḥā — The Prayer of the Festival of Sacrifice

The day of Eid al-Aḍḥā is celebrated as the manifestation of our Lord the Qa'im—the Establisher upon whose name blessings descend. We call the Qa'im "*Qa'im al-Qiyāma*" (the Establisher of the Standing, the Rising) when they merge with the First Eye and the Eye of the World to Come. The night of Eid al-Aḍḥā exemplifies our Lord the Mahdi of the End of Time, who is the Great Argument of the Qa'im.

| Night of Eid al-Aḍḥā | Day of Eid al-Aḍḥā |
| --- | --- |
| The Mahdi (upon him be peace) | The Qa'im—upon whose name blessings descend |

The virtues of the Commander of the Faithful ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib shall be revealed in our Lord the Qa'im—upon whose name blessings descend. At the end of each era and each *nuṭq* (prophetic cycle), there shall appear two great figures who shall compete in the stations of Eid al-Fiṭr and Eid al-Aḍḥā, and from their virtues things shall develop and mature.

### Examples of the ʿĪd Rakʿāt

Two *rakʿāt* of Eid al-Aḍḥā prayer are offered. The first *rakʿa* refers to our Lord Abū Ṭālib ibn ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib (the First), and the second *rakʿa* refers to our Lord Abū Ṭālib the Last, who shall be the father of the Qa'im—upon whose name blessings descend. Our Lord Abū Ṭālib the Second shall repeat the virtues of our Lord Abū Ṭālib the First.

| First Rakʿa | Second Rakʿa |
| --- | --- |
| Our Lord Abū Ṭālib the First | Our Lord Abū Ṭālib the Last |
| The Station of Becoming | The Station of al-Ḥusayn (upon him be peace) |

### Examples of the Sūras

In the first *rakʿa* of Eid al-Aḍḥā prayer, Sūrat al-Shams is recited, signifying that our Lord the Qa'im shall be as clear as the sun, and all the followers of the religion shall receive the light of guidance from him. His rank is supreme throughout the entire age (spanning four hundred thousand years). In the second *rakʿa*, Sūrat al-Ghāshiya is recited, signifying the terror that shall befall the disbelievers and hypocrites when they are slain, and indicating the peace that believers shall attain when their retribution and retaliation are fulfilled.

| Sūrat al-Shams | Sūrat al-Ghāshiya |
| --- | --- |
| Our Lord the Qa'im—upon whose name blessings descend | Fear and terror upon the Qa'im's emergence |

### Ṣalāt al-ʿAwad al-Khutba (The Prayer of Return—The Sermon Prayer)

This is followed by the recitation of two *rakʿāt* of Ṣalāt al-ʿAwad al-Khutba. The first *rakʿa* is al-Ḥusayn (upon him be peace), and the second *rakʿa* is al-Ḥusayn (upon him be peace). The repetition of al-Ḥusayn the First's virtues shall be with al-Ḥusayn the Last. There is no place for al-Ḥasan in the era of manifestation (*kashf*), for there shall be no place for the hidden succession (*istiqāla*, concealment) in the era of manifestation. Al-Ḥasan and al-Ḥusayn shall merge, and the two shall become one station together.

| First Rakʿa | Second Rakʿa |
| --- | --- |
| Al-Ḥusayn the First | Al-Ḥusayn the Last |
| Al-Ḥusayn (upon him be peace) | The station of Salmān |

## The Takbīrs and Qunūt of the Two ʿĪd Prayers

In the first *rakʿa*, the *qunut* (supplication) is recited five times with six *takbīrs* (magnifications: "Allāhu Akbar—Allah is Greatest"), and in the second *rakʿa*, *qunut* is recited four times with five *takbīrs*. The *qunut* supplication is recited nine times total, and the *takbīr* (recited after the intention to begin prayer) is pronounced twelve times.

Reciting the *qunut* nine times is an argument for the ninth station of our Lord the Mahdi. The twelve *takbīrs* recited in the two *rakʿāt* refer to the Imams—from our Lord ʿAlī ibn Aḥmad (the 36th Imam) to our Lord ʿAlī ibn al-Ḥasan (the 47th Imam). That is, there are eleven Imams from ʿAlī to ʿAlī. The twelfth *takbīr* of the *takbīr* of consecration is the 42nd Imam, ʿAlī ibn Aḥmad, who gathers the six Imams before him and the five Imams who shall come after him.

The recitation of *qunut* between two *takbīrs* is the supplication of the twelve Imams directed toward the Qa'im—upon whose name blessings descend—and their Great Argument, the Mahdi of the End of Time. *Qunut* and *takbīr* are recited after the sūras—that is, our Lord ʿAbdullāh and our Lord Abū Ṭālib are explaining the stations of the Mahdi and the Qa'im to all these Imams.

The twelve *takbīrs* in the higher circle correspond to the twelve days of Rabīʿ al-Awwal after which the Prophet was born. That is, the Messenger of Allah—or the Mahdi at the End of Time—shall gather with the help of Fāṭima a group of twelve people, and they shall be merged with the Qa'im. For this reason, the birth and death of the Messenger of Allah took place on the twelfth of Rabīʿ al-Awwal, indicating that he received from the Twelfth and bestowed the Twelfth.[^cite-8]

## Why Prayer Is Not Valid Without Five (the Symbolic Five)

*Ṣalāt* al-ʿĪdayn (ʿĪd prayers) or *Ṣalāt* al-Yawm al-Jumʿa (the Friday prayer) cannot be performed unless at least five people stand behind the Imam in a city.

### Five People on Friday

**Mawlānā Fāṭima**

In a certain sense, the day of Friday (*Yawm al-Jumʿa*, the Day of Assembly) signifies the station of Fāṭima—the Imām—and the Five Executors of the Will (*Khamsat al-Awṣiyāʾ*), who are adjacent to her congregation. The five letters of Fāṭima refer to the five trusts (*amanāt*) she received from her father.

Mawlānā Fāṭima, the gathering of the Executors of the Will, is constituted only when these five forms of persons are present. All other Executors of the Will are inferior to these five.

**Muḥammad the Arabian**

Similarly, the Prophet could not attain Prophethood until he had received five points from the First Argument and was called a Prophet.

### Five People on Eid al-Fiṭr

Similarly, the Second Argument (the Mahdi of the End of Time) shall also be constituted from five points of supreme light. He shall possess a gathering of the veils of ʿAbdullāh, the veil of Muḥammad, the veil of Fāṭima, the veil of al-Ḥasan, and the veil of Imam al-Mahdī Billāh (the 11th Imam). Apart from this, the three hidden ones (our Lord ʿAlī, our Lord Abū Ṭālib, our Lord ʿAlī) and the three veiled ones of the *nuṭq* period of each prophetic cycle shall also be gathered with the Mahdi of the Last Time.

### Five People on Eid al-Aḍḥā

The First Eye (*ʿAyn al-Awla*; the First Eye of Might, *ʿAyn al-ʿAẓīma*) gathered five Established Ones (*Muqīmīn*) from the *Nuṭaqāʾ* (the Speakers, the Prophets of each cycle)—Hūd, Ṣāliḥ, ʿĀd, Khuzayma—and other stations. The Last Eye (*ʿAyn al-Ākhira*; the Qa'im the Awaited) shall gather the three Secrets (*al-Asrār*, the hidden ones), the *Mīm*, the First Eye, and all the remaining lights.

When the Qa'im—upon whose name blessings descend—comes to Zikr al-Salām, the ʿĪd shall fall upon the day of Friday. That is, he shall merge the contents of both Friday and ʿĪd into his Great Temple, and their ferments shall be gathered in the same manner.

## The Traveller (Rules of Ṣalāt al-Musāfir)

When the Imam manifests, the Friday prayer shall be shortened from four *rakʿāt* to two *rakʿāt*, and the ʿĪd prayer shall be performed without the sermon. At that time, the traveller shall not be able to offer the Friday and ʿĪd prayers. That is, if a person is distant from the Imam's presence, he cannot offer these prayers independently at home. For this reason, the ʿĪd prayer is offered at the time of pilgrimage without the formal leadership, because on that day the Imam is present in the Mosque of Khayf.

In reality, a "traveller" refers to a person who has not yet been purified through retribution, and who has not been able to approach the temple of the Messenger of Allah because of his sins—whether the Mahdi of the End of Time (the exemplar of Eid al-Fiṭr) or the Qa'im al-Qiyāma (the exemplar of Eid al-Aḍḥā). That is, he is still upon the journey and has not reached his destination. He is given such services as clear his retribution and establish his affiliation with the successor of the Messenger of Allah or the Mahdi. That is, when figures like the Messenger of Allah or the Mahdi appear in the coming periods, he shall be able to join him.

## ʿĪd as a Return — The Spiritual Significance of 'Coming Back'

In a higher sense, Eid al-Fiṭr and Eid al-Aḍḥā refer to two mighty stations—the Mahdi of the End of Time and the Qa'im—upon whose name blessings descend:

At the end of each cosmic cycle (*dawr*, a period) and each prophetic utterance (*nuṭq*, the speaking), there shall also appear a Mahdi and a Qa'im. These prayers signify them. Their virtues shall be formed from the virtues of all the cosmic principles (*memīnāt*, the *Mīm* principles) and principles (*memīnāt*) that have been created throughout the universe.

ʿĪd literally means "to return" or "to come back." These days are called ʿĪd because the virtues of Muḥammad al-Muṣṭafā are returning to the Mahdi of the Last Time, and the virtues of ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib are returning to the Qa'im—upon whose name blessings descend.[^cite-9]

Sayyidnā ʿAlī ibn Muḥammad al-Walīd says: The virtues of the children of Isḥāq were stored in the body of the Holy Prophet. For this reason, the descendants of Isḥāq deserved to be connected with their sons. The Prophet called all the people to the exoteric Sharīʿa, and all of them performed only the exoteric acts of the Law. The spirit of life (the essence of truth) was not breathed into the multitude of these devotees. Their condition was such that they possessed only knowledge of the Law. When they come to the Imam (the Mahdi), they shall all be given the truth simultaneously in the period of manifestation, and the spirit of life shall be breathed into them. They shall acquire and preserve this knowledge through attentive listening, and they shall know their felicity through this knowledge. The Mahdi shall take these souls and hand them over to the son of the Qa'im, who shall gather both the exoteric and the esoteric, just as Imam al-Ḥusayn ibn ʿAlī possessed both the *Mustawdaʿ* (the trusteeship of prophecy and the will) and the *Muṣṭafā* (the station of the Imam and the will).

The Messenger of Allah came previously for the sake of the exoteric, but now he shall come in the form of the Mahdi. You shall satisfy those who possess exoteric knowledge by bestowing upon them knowledge of the inward realities. After conveying the knowledge of truth, you shall hand over these satisfied souls to the son of the Qa'im (the station of al-Ḥusayn—upon him be peace), and you shall return to your Lord (the Qa'im). After the emergence of the Qa'im, all the Imams shall invite only to the inward.

In reality, ʿĪd belongs to the gathering of those who have not yet been given knowledge. This reversal is called ʿĪd. That is: the gathering of the Messenger of Allah is returning to the Mahdi, or the gathering of the Law is returning to the Truth, or the virtues of the Messenger of Allah are returning in the form of the virtues of the Mahdi.

It is called an eruption, which occurs in singularity. For example, when milk boils, it does not bubble slowly, but erupts in an instant. This is the example of the Mahdi imparting knowledge to this gathering in a single way. After learning these sciences from the Mahdi, they shall return to Imam al-Ḥusayn to listen to the truth. And they shall be integrated into his congregation.

The virtue of Muḥammad and the virtue of the Mahdi (the 11th Imam) shall be the nobility (*Sharīf*) and excellence (*Fāḍil*) of the Mahdi shall be repeated. The Mighty Eye is ʿAlī's hidden one, and ʿAlī is his ultimate veil. In the same way, the virtues of the first shall appear in the form of the virtues of the last.

**The Realities**

The one who illuminates the Prophet is the Mighty Eye or the First Eye, and the one whose illumination shall be upon the Mahdi of the End of Time. The glory of the Qa'im shall be that he shall integrate all of them, even unto the very end.

**The Son of the Qa'im (the Caliph of the Qa'im)**

The Mahdi shall merge his entire congregation with the son of the Qa'im. The son of the Qa'im actually refers to the entire son-ship of the established period (the founder of the first period of the 400th cycle) who shall be established in the second period of the 400th cycle. The congregation of the Mahdi shall gather with them, and they shall return to the first Qa'im (their father) with this gathering.

In the same way, the Mahdis who appear at the end of the second period shall gather with the Qa'im of the third period, and the Qa'im of the third period shall also return to the Qa'im of the first period. Of the four-hundred-thousand periods of this entire 400th cycle, the highest and most established are from the first period.

As the successors of each era gather around the successors of the era before, their nobility increases. Even the entire cycle (400,000 rounds) shall gather around them. Then at the end of the cycle, a Mahdi shall come who shall be the proof of the Qa'im of the first period, and he shall also return to the Qa'im of the first period, and you shall be established in the 400th cycle. In this way, the formation of the 400th cycle shall be completed. When the 401st cycle arrives, it shall assemble the previous 400th cycle.
