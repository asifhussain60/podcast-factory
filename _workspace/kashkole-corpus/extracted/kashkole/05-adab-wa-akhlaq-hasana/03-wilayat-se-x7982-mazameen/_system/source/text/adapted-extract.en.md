## The Noble Oath-Letter (ʿAhd Sharīf Nāma)

In the Name of Allah, the Merciful, the Compassionate. May Allah's pleasure rest upon him—peace upon Muhammad and the Family of our Master Muhammad.

It is incumbent upon you to enter into obedience to God through the covenant (ʿahd) that has been enjoined upon you and to which you must hold fast with steadfastness. Through this covenant, you may enter the sanctuary of the Imam and his circle, and thereby grasp the rope of Allah—for there exists no path to salvation save through him. Your entrance into this age must proceed from your willing consent and genuine pleasure, never from temptation, intimidation, flattery, or fear. Affirm this with conviction![^cite-1]

The Imams—the Imam of the Age, the Family of the Prophet, and the descendants of ʿAlī and Fāṭima—have never been without successors. You shall obey his commands and those whom he has appointed as his deputies and representatives in his absence: the dāʿī (Summoner) and others appointed by textual designation, who continue to guide the way with sincerity and truth. Affirm this with conviction!

Pledge that you shall perform the five daily prayers, both obligatory and supererogatory, and that you shall fast in the month of Ramaḍān, pay the zakāh (alms-tax), and render obedience to the Imam of the Age or his deputy. Affirm this with conviction!

Pledge further that you shall perform the Ḥajj to the Sacred House and visit the grave of the Prophet—peace and blessings upon him—and that when the Imam of the Age or the dāʿī of the Age calls you to jihād (spiritual striving) in support of the faith, you shall stand ready to assist him with your life and your wealth. You shall love the Imam of the Age and his deputy with sincerity and protect his daʿwa (the summons) against its enemies. Affirm this with conviction!

In your dealings and transactions with whomever you encounter, you shall speak with excellence, truthfulness, loyalty, goodness, piety, and reverence. You shall distance yourself from the breaking of trusts, deception, falsehood, fraud, and all manner of wickedness. You shall hold fast to reconciliation and mutual accord, and you shall sustain yourself through lawful means. These are all the attributes of piety (taqwā), and they are the finest treasures of the Hereafter. Affirm this with conviction![^cite-2]

This covenant is the trust of God—do not betray it! Fulfill the conditions you have sworn to fulfill, and do not abandon them. Through obedience and loyalty to the Imam, you shall attain great success and an exalted station in the sight of Allah the Almighty. In the Qur'ān, Allah declares that those who honor their covenant and remain steadfast are the truthful and the pious. Affirm this with conviction!

This covenant—which you have undertaken—is the trust of Allah, His Messenger, and the Imam of the Age. Whoever betrays it shall suffer grievous harm in both religion and worldly life. He shall go astray; the wrath of Allah shall descend upon him, and he shall be cursed. His companion shall be the Devil, and what an evil companion he is![^cite-3] Allah, the Messenger of Allah, the Imams of the faith, the angels, and all the righteous of the faith shall sever themselves from him, and it shall be right and just to break all covenant with such a one. Affirm this with conviction!

Allah shall be angered with him, shall curse him, and shall prepare for him a severe punishment. Affirm this with conviction!

Hell shall be the dwelling-place of the covenant-breaker—a most painful abode. There he shall find no helper against Allah, nor shall he discover any refuge. Affirm this with conviction!

You have heard these things and agreed to them, accepting them fully and binding yourself through this noble covenant. Yet know this: unless all these things are enacted in your life, the trust shall not be truly fulfilled. Affirm this with conviction!

Now receive the glad tidings, for this covenant is a stepping-stone to tremendous success. Whoever fulfills the conditions of this noble covenant, Allah the Almighty shall grant him a mighty reward. May Allah Almighty preserve you in the observance of this covenant's conditions, and may it not become merely a fleeting thing. Remember: one may know the truth but fail to believe in it.

All praise and gratitude belong to Allah, whose grace and mercy attend us at every moment, and peace and blessings upon Ḥaḍrat Muḥammad al-Muṣṭafā—peace and blessings be upon him—the seal of all the Prophets. And peace and blessings upon Ḥaḍrat ʿAlī al-Murtaḍā, who is truly the rope of Allah's extension (ḥabl Allāh), the Basin of Kawthar to which all must come. And peace and blessings upon your Family—the People of the Mantle—who have been made witnesses over the deeds of all creation. And peace and blessings upon all the proofs and authorities of the Prophet—peace and blessings be upon him.

Those who are bound by covenant with Allah shall not be able to fulfill the conditions of the Prophet's covenant save in the name of Allah and His Messenger. Al-Fātiḥa.

## ʿAbdullāh ibn Masʿūd's Message to the People of Kūfa on Leaving the City

O people of Kūfa! Know that within every human being dwell two tribes, and behind each tribe stands an army of Satan. Guard yourselves! Do not allow your untamed inner nature to lead you into war. Understand your station: the wall of your dwelling adjoins the wall of prophecy itself. Know moreover that you draw breath beneath the sky through which rain the stars of the Messenger; that you walk upon the earth over which the angels glorify the Lord; that you speak a language through which Allah has established His dominion over creation. Commit no deed that would cause future generations to deem the Arabs unworthy of such extraordinary grace. Beware of discord and division, for faith shall vanish from your hearts should you succumb to them. Love one another instead of harboring hatred. Admonish one another gently rather than threatening. Our kinsmen—both near and distant—were slain so that we might neither die nor be killed. Forgive me for all that you have heard. If anyone has a right against me, let him come and claim it here. If I have wronged anyone, let him come to this place and take recompense. Let no one who loves ⟪ar:مالک اشتر نقعی⟫ be parted from me. This man's speech resembles that of ʿAlī. So long as you remain united wholeheartedly, you shall be safe from Walīd and Waleed.

## The State of an Unbridled Wayfarer (a parable)

To attempt praise of the Almighty is to attempt the impossible. What power has a servant to know his Master? How can creatures in such a condition comprehend their Creator? The saints have bent their knees in humility before this mystery, acknowledging their own nothingness. And this very confession of humility and servitude constitutes true monotheism. As is written: ⟪ar-quote:اَلعِجزُ عَن اِدرَاکِہِ اِدرَاکُ⟫—to admit that we are unable to grasp Him is the true knowledge and witness to His oneness. Yet there exists a praise that belongs to those who truly understand.

Today we shall recount the story of the praise offered by Rand al-ʿAubali—a man proud of his humility, taking pride in his destitution, glorying in his shortcomings despite his inconsistencies. He who trusts so profoundly in the forgiveness of the Pardoner (Ghafūr) and the Merciful (al-Raḥīm) that he grows indifferent to his transgressions and sins. This man appears in the court of Allah—the court of majesty, the Sovereign of sovereigns. On one side, the angels are engaged in tasbīḥ (glorification) and taʿzīm (magnification) in their ranks; on the other, the cherubim are lost in praise and exaltation in prostration. Yet this man—crude in speech, though reverent in heart—comes forward and prostrates himself in worship. Rising, he addresses his Lord: "O Lord of earth and heaven, Creator of all that dwells herein, it is the custom of the court that when one appears before the king, one comes bearing offerings. According to the custom of my household, I too have come to this court with gifts. But O Creator of earth, what could ever be lacking in Your treasure? Who could ever reach the measure of Your riches?

Yet, Possessor of Dominion, what use have You for things already present in Your treasury? And what value would such things bear, even if presented before You? Therefore, O Lord of the Worlds, I have sought out from Your creation and brought four things that are not in Your treasury."

Allah! A weak and feeble human—this claim, this audacity! A handful of dust, yet such courage! The angels near to the Throne begin to tremble in fear of Allah's wrath; the cherubim start in their prostration; the Angel of Death himself fixes upon the man with terrible eyes.

Rand al-ʿAubali perceives the angels' alarm, astonishment, and anger. Rather than being terrified, he smiles. In the court of the Divine, he says: "O God, the angels of the highest majesty are looking on with eyes full of wonder. Yet since permission has been granted to approach this court, should not permission also be given to perform the ritual of presentation?" And they say to him: "Present your offerings." He reaches into the vessel and draws forth the first treasure of Rand al-ʿAubali's gift, saying: "O Ever-Living, Self-Subsisting One! His name is Need (Ḥāja), and this thing is not in Your treasury." He places his hand in the cup again and brings forth another, saying: "O All-Mighty, All-Knowing One! This is Helplessness (ʿAjz), and this thing lies hidden in Your treasury." A third time he reaches in and brings forth another thing, presenting it: "O Almighty! This is Shame (Ḥayāʾ), and Your treasury stands empty of it." Then for the fourth and final time he draws forth something. He wipes the perspiration of shame from his brow and says: "O Pardoner and Most Merciful! This is Sin (Dhanb), and one cannot even find its address in Your treasury."

The angels stood astounded at this audacity. Yet this courage and daring of the servant pleased the court of the Possessor of Majesty. The Merciful One became gladdened and said: "The offering is accepted! But O servants, take these things with you, for they were not in Our treasury before and are not now. However, in addition to these four things, you shall receive four garments of honor. Because you have sought Our eternity by acknowledging your infirmity, We bestow upon you the garment of eternal life. Because you have sought Our pleasure by expressing your helplessness, We grant you wealth that shall never diminish. Because you have called upon Our transcendence by mentioning your shame, We give you the garment of obedience, in which you have always taken pride. And because you have taken refuge in Our forgiveness and pardon by expressing your sorrow and remorse for your sin, not for your sins, We have blessed you with the garment of forgiveness."[^cite-4]

Can a person receive humility and the forgiveness of sins? But can he possess eternal life—wealth without limits—and one who, having been found, remains forever free from the bondage of need? If you do not believe, then remember the words of the Almighty in the Gospel: ⟪ar-quote:یاَ اِبنِ آدَمَ اَطَعْنِی اَجْعَلُکَ مِثْلِی حَیًّ لاَ تَمُوْتُ وَ غَنِیًّ لاَ تَفْتَقِر⟫—"O son of Adam, obey Me and I shall make you like Myself: living, never to die, and rich, never to be poor."

The third garment—the one given to the servant—the poet describes thus:

*Stretch forth your hand when you stand in need of nothing,*
*Why should not the poor take pride in such humility?*

Iqbāl describes the fourth garment, granted as reward for a remorseful servant:

*The pearls understood when the hand of Generosity picked them—*
*Those drops that were once the tears of the dead and passive.*

Rand al-ʿAubali falls once more into prostration. He rises from it and gazes upon the angels with knowing eyes. Placing his gifts back into the vessel and donning the garments of honor upon his head, he walks toward his place, humming: ⟪ar-quote:سَبُّوْحٌ قُدُّوْس، رَبُّناَ وَ رَبُّ الْمَلاَءِکَۃِ وَالْرُّوحِ، رَبُّناَ الْحَیُّ الْقَیُّومُ اَلَّذِینَ لاَ یَمُوتُ اَبداً اَبدَا⟫

*—Compiled by the late Ḥāj Mullā Aḥmad Khurshīd Ḥasan Ṣāḥib*

## Confirmation of the Prophet's Revelation

⟪ar:ناطق اعظم، محمد مصطفی صلی اللہ علیہ وسلم⟫ When Gabriel descended with revelation, the Prophet fell overcome by the magnificence of that light. The disbelievers used to say: "The jinn has overwhelmed him, and this apparition is truly an epileptic seizure⟪ar:نعوذ باللّٰہ⟫." ⟪ar:مولاتنا خدیجہ⟫ He mentioned this matter to the Prophet and wished to know how he might verify whether ⟪ar:جبرئیل علیہ السلام⟫ truly came to the Messenger of Allah or whether obsession had seized him. ⟪ar:ورقۃ بن نوفل⟫ advised him thus: the next time the Messenger of Allah said, "Gabriel has come to me," the Prophet should take the hand of the Messenger and place it upon her naked thigh and ask: "Do you still see Gabriel?" He would reply: "Yes, I see Gabriel." Then she should place his hand upon her breast and ask again: "Do you still see Gabriel?" He would answer: "Yes!" Then she should undo the hair of her head and repeat the same question. If he were truly mad, he would say: "Yes, I still see Gabriel." But if he were truly a prophet of Allah, he would say: "No, I do not see Gabriel anymore."[^cite-5]

The hair on a woman's head is included within her private parts; therefore, it is permissible to cover it during prayer. Should even the slightest hair become visible, the angels not only refrain from calling out blessings upon one in that state of prayer, but they turn away entirely from those who pray around them. In this incident, the prophet was not approaching with revelation, but rather ⟪ar:جبرئیل علیہ السلام⟫ was coming to the Messenger. Yet when ⟪ar:خدیجہ ؑ⟫ uncovered her hair, the angel departed from the Messenger of Allah.

## Complete Certainty (Yaqīn Kāmil)

Allah declares in the Qur'ān:

"So glorify the praise of your Lord, and be of those who prostrate yourselves, and worship your Lord until there comes to you certainty." In this verse, Allah instructs us first to glorify the praise of our Lord, then to prostrate, and thereafter to worship. After performing these acts, there arrives a station where belief itself comes to the worshipper—he no longer needs to go in search of belief.

What we recite during the prayer is called tasbīḥ (glorification), whether it be the verses of the Qur'ān or the tasbihs of bowing and prostration. The perfection of our prayer lies in its prostration. When we raise our head from the final prostration, our worship is complete and we arrive at certainty (yaqīn). For this reason, when Imām Ḥusayn—peace be upon him—performed his final prostration, his words were: "O Allah, I have fulfilled my covenant; O Allah, fulfill Your promise." His martyrdom was the perfection of his worship, which brought him to certainty. In the Holy Qur'ān, certainty (yaqīn) is mentioned seven times, and in the seventh day of ʿĀshūrāʾ, Imām Ḥusayn was martyred. It is said that he had reached the seventh station of certainty, for which reason Allah granted him martyrdom and bestowed upon him the status of the truly certain.

No matter how much a person labors throughout the day, no matter how much sweat he sheds, if he does not arrive home convinced of the success and acceptance of his day's affairs, that day shall be wasted. And if the day is wasted, the night too shall be lost. But if he arrives with certainty, he shall be at peace, and his mind shall find rest. Thus there can be no true satisfaction without certainty.[^cite-6]

**An Argument for Certainty**

A pilgrim who descends from Mount ʿArafāt in the Ḥajj should believe with absolute conviction that all his sins have been forgiven and that he has become like a newborn child. The pilgrim must be certain of his forgiveness; the very reason for this certainty is that one who forgives does not demand forgiveness again for that same sin. This is the pinnacle of belief.

When one progresses toward the stations of certainty, there arrives a moment when the unseen merges with the visible—when the unseen becomes a witness. For one who is certain of death, for example, there is no difference between life now and the coming of death; they are one. The saints of Allah have given another name to death: they call it certainty. It is as though the verse means: "Worship your Lord until certainty comes to you."

If a saint of Allah attains faith in his lifetime, we shall see that he has died while yet alive. Though he still walks before us and breathes, in reality he has already tasted death and lives as one who is dead.

**On Backbiting**

When a pilgrim enters through the Gate of Banū Shayba during Ḥajj, he mingles the unseen with the seen and brings forth what is absent, calling upon Allah in supplication. The apparent meaning of this invocation is: "O Allah, I have forbidden every backbiting against myself, which is known to all the Muslims of the community." Yet the Imāms have unveiled a profound spiritual meaning in this supplication. They say: "Answer this supplication in such a way that I have made every backbiting forbidden for Myself, and I have made it obligatory for me to speak of the attributes of the Unseen (Allah)—attributes which are known to be absent from all the outward manifestations of the people of Islam." In other words, when we enter through this gate, we must engage in the remembrance and glorification of Allah alone, and mention nothing else but Him.

## The Hypocrite (Munāfiq) and the Apostate (Murtadd)

In religion there is the Muslim, the believer, and the hypocrite. A hypocrite is one who dwells within the realm of Islam yet considers himself within the realm of faith, though in truth he is not of the faithful. He appears to rise like a believer, and thus is difficult to discern. In Arabic, the hypocrite is one who digs—one who attempts to undermine faith itself, making him the greatest enemy of the believers. The wilāyat (spiritual guardianship) of Amīr al-Muʾminīn has been made the measure of his true identity. Whoever accords with this measure is a believer; whoever does not is a hypocrite. All three of the first caliphs were among the hypocrites, for they dwelt within Islam yet did not acknowledge the realm of true faith. Allah says concerning the hypocrites:

**The Execution of Hypocrites**

It is recorded in one of his books, *Ulu al-Akhbār*, that Idrīs ibn Ḥasan reports that the Messenger of Allah tested the limits of obedience in many ways. At the conquest of Mecca, where he proclaimed general amnesty, he nonetheless named certain hypocrites and ordered that they be killed wherever found. The three caliphs had relations or old friendships with some of these hypocrites. Yet the three dared not obey the command of the Messenger of Allah, and not only did they release these hypocrites but some even sheltered them, claiming ignorance of the Prophet's decree.[^cite-8]

One of them was ʿAbdullāh ibn Saʿd, a hypocrite whose crime was most grave and whom the Prophet most desired to see executed. The Prophet commanded: "Kill him even if he clings to the curtains of the Kaʿba." The reason was this: when this man had embraced Islam, the Messenger of Allah appointed him as a scribe of the Qur'ānic revelation. Once, when revelation came to the Prophet, he recited a verse wherein he mentioned the attributes of the Pardoner and Merciful One. ʿAbdullāh said to himself: "Besides this, there is nothing more appropriate." Later he committed far graver crimes that made him an unbeliever and polytheist. He began to claim that the Messenger of Allah—God forbid—had erred in the revelation, saying that where the Prophet had recited "All-Mighty, All-Wise" he had heard "Pardoner, Merciful." Thus he insisted on his error, declaring that the Qur'ān had indeed been revealed to him. ʿUthmān gave this man shelter.

Another of these hypocrites was Huwayrath ibn Nafīl, with whom Mawlānā ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib had once been acquainted. When this man came before Mawlānā ʿAlī, the Imām said to him: "O enemy of Allah, you are here!" The man said: "O my brother ʿAlī, do not kill me; let me go." ʿAlī replied: "May Allah not preserve you over me, and I shall not preserve you over me." Speaking thus, he slew him immediately.[^cite-9]

The hypocrisy of the hypocrite remains upon him until death. Abū Sufyān was the greatest of hypocrites, yet the Prophet granted him refuge. Believers have been commanded to wage jihād against such ones. Those who perform jihād have been given superiority over those who remain at home.

**Apostates**

Apostates are those who have embraced Islam or faith and then reverted to their former religion. ## The Human Is Ashraf al-Makhlūqāt — The Noblest of Creatures

Before Islam, Greek philosophical thought conceived of the universe as static—a state of stagnation and silence, not dynamic movement. This conception bred only abandonment and decay. The Islamic principle of jihād (spiritual striving) shattered this ancient worldview and revealed that all things in this universe are dynamic and in motion, moving according to their nature.

If we contemplate this universe deeply, we find that its essence is dynamic; this movement is inherent in its nature, which is why it possesses life and endurance. From the river to the sun, from the Divine Throne to the heavens, all things are active and shall remain so until their final moment. This is because motion is the sign of life, and stillness the mirror of death. Every motion, moreover, requires an impetus. The prayer that manifests this action is the prayer of jihād.

It has always been Allah's intention that every low point should rise and transcend stagnation. A drop of water becomes a pearl; a seed becomes a tree; a sperm becomes a human being. The salt water of the sea flows forth, yet when it falls as rain, its taste transforms and becomes sweet. When cold winds strike the mountains, they erode them; the mountains block the sun's rays, and diamonds and jewels are born. When humble blood enters the sanctuary, it begins to emit the fragrance of musk. All this is because nature wills evolution; it desires to ascend! Life in inanimate matter was a nightmare until the splash of water awakened it. With the force of this life, plants pulled their heads from the ground. When life reached the animal realm, it began to sense and move; and when it appeared in the human realm—what can we say of that state which Allah describes: "Truly We have created humankind in the finest form"?

Within this human being, the essence of the Eternal and the Contingent encompasses the entire universe—what Amīr al-Muʾminīn has called the microcosm. Amīr al-Muʾminīn said:[^cite-12]

Everything that exists in this universe has left its traces within the human being. The sun's heat is the warmth of his nature; the winds are his breath; the sea is his heart, from which blood flows. Rivers are his great veins; lesser veins are his smaller channels. Mountains are his bones; forests are his hair. Virtue resides in the soul's state. The volcano is his liver. The layers of the earth are his stomach. The two luminaries are his two eyes. Earthquakes are his tremors, and pores are the channels of his body. Death and life, sickness and health—all are his.

The secrets hidden in the composition of human life are no less wondrous in themselves! This man—composed of but a few elements—shines with water and lightning, grows and strengthens with heat and light. His senses are armed as warriors; he is enriched with feeling and emotion and adorned with intellect. Conscience, intuition, and instinct illuminate the depths of his soul. At times diving deep into the ocean of thought, at times soaring beyond the limits of comprehension. Truly human![^cite-13]

There is an entire world contained within his body. Security forces maintain watch at every turn. On one side, soldiers are deployed to control traffic upon the highways of the body; on the other, groups of workers struggle with the movement of heavy equipment. Factories throughout his body produce various chemical compounds, and a red fluid—blood—circulates to keep body and soul active. The heart, sovereign of the entire body, though safely enclosed within the chest, never ceases its beating. The brain is like a power station from which innumerable electrical wires extend to every part of the body, maintaining its nervous system. Though the brain is responsible for all bodily movements and possesses power over intention, it performs all its work by permission and will of the heart. The liver, through its selfless service, is the guardian of health. The kidneys are charged with purification, and the stomach is the body's ever-burning furnace, transforming food into vital forces.

To run and manage this mighty enterprise of the body, various departments exist in every corner, all working in perfect harmony, simultaneously discharging their individual and collective duties. There is no noise from these mechanisms, no clamor of instruments, no knocking, no commotion anywhere. All labor constantly, yet man in his negligence is so unaware that he does not even know what transpires in his inner world. Glory be to Him who made man the quintessence of the universe and granted him dominion over the three kingdoms—mineral, plant, and animal—as the noblest of all creatures.

Apparently, every human being resembles others in the parts of their body and its functions, yet in form and feature, each human differs from another. Similarly, from a spiritual perspective, no two humans are equal, and not every human can fully absorb the perfections of the spiritual realm. Each receives what his nature is given to receive. Yet among humans there are also those servants who are stripped of all material qualities and endowed with all the virtues of the soul. These are the purposes of Adam's creation, and concerning them the heavens said: "O Prophet, were it not for you, We would not have created these heavens."[^cite-14]

The famous philosopher Aristotle stated that whatever emanates from the First Source attains a state of perfection. The Necessary Being, in Its perfect self, cannot tolerate defect in Its creatures, for It is free from all deficiency. In this case, there can be no equality between Creator and creature. The perfection of the Necessary Essence is of one kind; the perfection of the contingent is of another. His Essence is necessary perfection, which is why He loves perfection even in the realm of possibility. The first luminous beings He created were the perfect beings. After them, creation gradually descended through many degrees of loss.

All creatures in the world are achieving perfection in their sphere. From their beginning, they ascend through elevated and evolutionary stages until they reach the degree that perfects them. All intermediate stations are called degrees of deficiency; they are needed only to reach perfection. The grain sown in the earth does not reach its perfection until it bears grain. Similarly, every creature cannot reach its perfection without producing a creature like itself. Every creature strives and labors to achieve this perfection, and after perfecting itself at each level, it advances to a higher level.

Bodies manifest in plants; plants in animals; animals in human beings. There is a lack of sensation in minerals, lack of sense in plants, lack of intellect in animals, and lack of ownership in humans. But the first creature can have no deficiency. It must be perfect in every way—alive but not merely alive; knowledge itself, not merely knowing; light itself, not merely illuminating. It is the intermediary between Allah and His servants, the guide and the guided, the interpreter of the Divine Word.

## The Basin, the Honey, and the Hair (a parable)

One day the Prophet—peace and blessings be upon him—came to the home of Ḥaḍrat Abū Bakr, ʿUmar, and ʿUthmān. Mawlānā ʿAlī immediately prepared a table and served the finest and most exquisite honey in a radiant basin. Incidentally, a hair lay in the honey. The Prophet saw it and addressed the Companions, saying: "This basin and the honey it contains, with a hair visible in it, wish to convey certain truths and knowledge. I desire that each of you speak of it in your own way."

Hearing this, Abū Bakr said: "O Messenger of Allah! A pious man is brighter than this basin; faith is sweeter in his heart than this honey; and his constancy in faith until death is finer than this hair."

Then ʿUmar said: "O Messenger of Allah! The kingdom is brighter than this basin; governance is sweeter than honey; and justice in rule is more delicate than this hair."

Then ʿUthmān said: "O Messenger of Allah! Religion is brighter than this basin; knowledge of religion is sweeter than honey; and its practice is more subtle than this hair."

Then Mawlānā ʿAlī said: "O Messenger of Allah! Hospitality to guests is brighter than this basin; the service of guests is sweeter than honey; yet winning the guest's comfort and affection is more delicate than this hair."[^cite-15]

Fāṭima al-Zahrāʾ—peace be upon her—was also present in the house. The Prophet said: "Sayyida, speak as well!" The Sayyida replied: "O Messenger of Allah, modesty in women is brighter than this basin; the veil upon women's faces is sweeter than honey; and guarding oneself from unlawful gazes is more delicate than this hair."

Then the Holy Prophet—peace and blessings be upon him—said: "I too wish to speak." He declared: "Divine knowledge is brighter than this basin; awareness of knowledge is sweeter than honey; yet preserving it in one's heart is more delicate than this hair."

The conversation had not yet ended when an Arab came to the door, calling out: "I seek audience with the Messenger of Allah." This was Ḥaḍrat Gabriel. He said: "O Messenger of Allah! The path of Allah is brighter than this basin; to walk upon it is sweeter than honey; yet to remain steadfast upon it until the end is finer than this hair."

Then revelation came to the Prophet, and the Almighty declared: "O Muḥammad! Paradise is brighter than this basin; the blessings of Paradise are sweeter than honey; yet the Bridge of Sirāṭ is more delicate than this hair."

*—Compiled by the late Ḥāj Mullā Aḥmad Khurshīd Ḥasan*

## The Greatness of the Tongue and Guarding It

The Messenger of Allah—peace and blessings be upon him—said: "Sometimes a person utters a word that pleases Allah, yet he does not even consider its weight, and because of it Allah raises him many degrees. And sometimes a person utters a word of disobedience to Allah, which he does not even ponder, and because of it he falls into Hell."

The tongue is a great blessing of Allah. Though it is but a small part of the human body, its miracles are immense. It possesses many virtues and many vices. Through it, a person can accumulate good deeds for the Hereafter if he wills; through it, he can destroy his Hereafter if he wills. If one uses the tongue correctly and speaks good words, Allah knows not how many degrees He will raise one because of it. When a person transforms from disbelief to faith, it is through the tongue. He recites the word of testimony with his tongue—and from that single word, Paradise opens before him. On the other hand, if the tongue is misused, it drags a person toward Hell. For this reason, excessive speech is forbidden.[^cite-16]

## The Signs of the Manifestation of Imam al-Mahdī, Peace Be Upon Him

**ʿAlī's Statement on the Appearance of the Mahdī**

Once, one of the Companions of Mawlānā ʿAlī—peace be upon him—Saʿsa ʿa ibn Suhān, asked him: "O Master! Tell me, when shall the Dajjāl emerge and when shall Imām al-Mahdī—peace be upon him—appear?" Amīr al-Muʾminīn replied: "There are signs which must be fulfilled before the Dajjāl emerges or the Imām al-Mahdī appears." Saʿsaʿa asked: "Master, what are the signs after which Imām al-Mahdī will appear?"[^cite-18]

Amīr al-Muʾminīn said: "There will come a time when prayer becomes a burden to most people, and they will begin to neglect it easily; very few will be those who pray. When people come to regard betrayal of trust and lying as trivial matters. When people begin to construct buildings, tall and lofty. When the selling of religion for worldly gain becomes common—when people prefer the world over the faith. When adultery becomes widespread and people follow every wicked desire. When people commonly break ties with their kin instead of showing them kindness. When the affairs of governance are entrusted to the unfit. When human blood is regarded as a simple and negligible matter. When patience departs from the people and they take pride in wrongdoing. When even the wealthy and privileged of the nation tremble in fear. When people openly commit adultery, slander, sin, and immorality without shame. When people wear the Qur'ān as an amulet rather than reciting it. When people take pride in adorning mosques with gold. When men imitate women in dress and women imitate men, and men begin to work as women work. When people abandon religious knowledge and pursue worldly learning. When people take pride in wearing clothes made from animal skins. When those who call themselves believers are drawn to worldly education rather than religious teaching, becoming lazy and negligent in imparting religious knowledge."

After saying this, he declared: "Remember: On the Day of Judgment, I shall have no connection with such people, nor shall they have any with me." The Prophet said of such a time: "The bellies of the people shall be their god, and women shall be their qibla. Their religion shall be their wealth, and the world shall be their glory. At that time, Islam and faith shall remain in name only. They will indeed recite the Qur'ān, but they shall have no understanding of its meaning. Mosques shall be inhabited by them, yet they shall be far from guidance."

Hearing this, many were astonished and asked: "Are these people idolaters?" He replied: "Yes—the dirham is their idol on the floor; they are Muslims and will not worship idols, yet they will value the dirham more than the divine commands."

At that time, those who remain patient in their faith shall be like one holding a burning coal in his hand. The faces of such people shall be like men's faces, but their hearts shall be like the hearts of devils. They shall shed blood as easily as flies bite, arrogantly refusing to turn back. Whoever follows them shall doubt; whoever opposes them shall be denied; whoever separates from them shall be slandered. The Sunna of the Prophet shall be deemed innovation, and innovation shall be considered Sunna. They shall honor the wicked and vile while considering the believer humiliated. Their wives shall be cunning, their sons ungrateful, and their elders shall be those who abandon the known good and forbid the evil. Whatever they say shall be for worldly gain."

Then he said: "Know that when such a time arrives, the first thing Allah shall do is to deprive them of the blessings of rain—there shall be rain, but scanty and untimely. Second, the worst of them shall be appointed as rulers over them, subjecting them to severe punishment. Not only their enemies but even their friends shall harm them. Those among them who are righteous shall supplicate for relief, yet their prayers shall not be answered. When such a time comes, you shall see great red storms, earthquakes, landslides, and similar signs! At that time, people shall flee from religious knowledge as a goat flees from a wolf! When the time of Ḥaḍrat al-Mahdī's appearance draws near, you shall see that the Chinese have taken over Tibet and are then planning to attack India. The monarchy in Iran shall come to an end."[^cite-19]

**The Prophet's Statement on the Appearance of the Mahdī**

The Prophet—peace be upon him—said: "In the last days, people shall wear clothes of so many varieties that it would be difficult to even imagine them today. And the vehicles of the people shall be of flesh and devoid of life, yet so swift that they shall cut the earth like scissors. ## ʿĪd and the Person of Imam al-Mahdī, Peace Be Upon Him

The term ʿīd (festival) carries a dual sense: it denotes both return and renewal—a joy that recurs again and again. By contrast, warnings that arrive are meant to endure permanently, leaving their imprint upon all who are worthy of receiving them. These two concepts frame the eschatological expectation that lies at the heart of the Ismaili faith.

History attests that the advent of the Messenger of Allah occurred at a time when the Arabs had reached a nadir of moral degradation. Intoxication was rampant, fornication commonplace, and the basest practices pervaded their societies. It was at this dark hour that the Almighty sent His Messenger as a mercy to all the worlds. Yet in our own era, humanity has returned to a condition of profound spiritual darkness. Ignorance has again descended like a veil, and the age of *jāhiliyyah* (pre-Islamic ignorance) has, in its essential character, returned. The time approaches, therefore, for the return of the Messenger of Allah—no longer in his initial dispensation, but in the form of Imam al-Mahdī (the Guided One) of the latter days.[^cite-10] He shall draw humanity from the depths of ignorance into the radiance of knowledge. This reappearance constitutes our true ʿīd, wherein the Messenger returns as al-Mahdī.

### The Promise of Inner Knowledge

All the prophets brought forth their scriptures, yet none was granted permission to unveil the mysteries contained within them. When al-Mahdī arrives, he shall bestow upon all of them—and upon humanity—a unified knowledge of these concealed truths, and this is the authentic ʿīd. The Messenger of Allah brought the outer, the *zāhir* (manifest); when he returns in the form of al-Mahdī, he shall reveal to all humanity the *bātin* (inner, esoteric) dimension of the manifest word.[^cite-11] This bestowal of inner knowledge (*ʿilm al-bātin*) constitutes the true festival. The Books which the Almighty has revealed to His Prophets shall be opened to esoteric understanding in their totality through al-Mahdī.

### The Personality of Imam al-Mahdī

The Messenger of Allah declared: "Though the mothers of all the Prophets differ, they are brothers to one another, and their religion is one. Jesus and I stand in particular closeness; no other prophet came between him and me. Soon will ʿIsā ibn Maryam appear among you as my caliph—to govern and to judge my Community. When you behold him, you shall know him by these signs."[^cite-12]

His stature shall be of medium height, with a most handsome countenance and a complexion fair with a hint of ruddy hue. The hair upon his head shall flow long and unbound. Water shall drip from his head, yet his body shall remain untouched by moisture. His chest shall be broad; his eyes shall bear a smile, and upon his face shall rest a gentle expression—yet he shall embody perfect dignity and awe. His neck shall be inclined before God, like a fair pillow. His hands shall be wide-palmed and his gaze penetrating. The earth shall tremble beneath his feet as he walks. When he addresses any person, he shall not merely turn his face; rather, he shall direct his whole being toward them. His gaze shall descend from the heavens to the earth with profound depth. He shall be foremost in greeting all persons, stepping forward to offer salutation. He shall be absorbed in the sorrows and griefs of the world. The person shall be immersed in silence and contemplation, uttering no word save out of necessity. When he gestures, it shall be with his palm; when he expresses amazement, he shall reverse his hand. When boundaries are set and he converses with you, he shall speak while holding his right hand to the thumb of his left hand.

His word and wisdom shall rest upon four pillars:

He shall shatter the cross into fragments—that sign which the followers of Jesus take as their emblem. He shall vacillate between the two (traditions of the Sharīʿa). He shall slay swine, abrogate the poll-tax (*jizya*), and disperse wealth as waters are dispersed. He shall purify the sanctuary and sanctify God from the place of Arcana, then depart for the greater pilgrimage (*ʿUmra*). He shall wage war against enemies until no religion remains upon this earth save Islam. At that time he shall raise the *Takbīr* (proclamation of divine magnification). False speech shall be driven away, the Antichrist expelled, and all false claimants destroyed. Thus shall ʿIsā rule this world for forty years. He shall marry, beget children, and then pass away. The Muslims shall offer his funeral prayer, and he shall be interred in Medina.
