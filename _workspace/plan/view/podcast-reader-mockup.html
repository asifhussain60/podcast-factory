The Architecture of Creation: Will, Command, and the Seven

Where this chapter picks up

The boy has just been bound. When the author had recited the covenant of God to him, the boy wept until his tears poured down, he understood that he had become part of the Party of God and of the company of His friends, and he fell silent in his composure. The author began to expound and explain. This is the first long expounding. Where an earlier teaching walked the boy through the outer of the call — who may receive it, how it is given, under what conditions — this exposition walks him through the architecture of creation itself. The bond has been struck; now the architecture inside the bond is shown.

The exposition is, in form, a sustained master-and-boy dialogue, but the proportion has shifted. In the earlier teaching the boy spoke half the lines; here the master speaks most of them. The boy is a prompter rather than an interlocutor — he asks short, structurally placed questions, and the master replies in long architectural answers. The substance is what later Ismaili philosophical literature will call the cosmology of the call: the doctrine of will, command, and saying; the doctrine of the seven principles and the twelve constellations; the doctrine of the parallel between the seven heavens above and the seven speaker-prophets below; the doctrine of the apparent and the inward; and the doctrine of the three layers of knowledge. The doctrines are not separate teachings; they are one architecture, and the chapter's force is in the way the master walks the boy down through each layer in order, never skipping a step.

The chapter begins immediately after the disciple's binding to the covenant. The author shifts from explaining the outer mechanics of initiation to walking the disciple through the cosmological architecture behind it — the doctrines of will/command/saying, the parallel between the seven heavens and the seven prophets, and the three layers of knowledge — all presented as a single ordered structure rather than separate teachings.

The three words: will, command, saying

The master begins exactly where the call's literature insists every teaching must begin — at the origin. God is the Creator of things and the One who composes them. He is not the first of every first created thing, the first of a series that begins with Him as the first member. He is the First of all firsts and the Inheritor of every thing — outside the series altogether, the One who inherits every thing because everything returns to Him.

The Commander of the Faithful, in the canonical compilation The Path of Eloquence, opens his great sermon on creation with the same negation:

Praise be to God whose worth no describer can attain, whose blessings no counter can number, whose due no exertion can discharge — whom heights of intellectual courage cannot reach, and depths of sagacity cannot fathom. For His description there is no limit fixed, no eulogy existing, no time appointed, no duration set.

— The Path of Eloquence, Sermon 1, trans. Reza (Tehran: WOFIS, 1980), p. 35.

Then the structural claim. He began the creation of what was created out of light, from which three words branched. The doctrine of the primordial light is the doctrine the rest of the architecture rests on. Before the seven, before the heavens, before the earth, there is light — and from that light three words branch. The order is precise:

The first of them is the will; from the will branched the command; and the command is divided into "saying" — that He says "Be" to what He wills, and it is. So the first creation is the will, then the command, then the saying.

The three words are will, command, saying. The will is the first; from the will the command branches; from the command the saying is divided. The saying is the divine fiat — Be, and it shall be — the Quranic formula by which God creates whatever He wishes.

The seven principles and the twelve constellations

From the three words, the architecture cascades. From the saying branched seven principles, and from each principle branched twelve. The seven and the twelve are the structural numbers the rest of the cosmology runs on — seven heavens above, seven earths below, twelve constellations above, twelve arguments below. The macrocosm and the microcosm are held in strict parallel; the figures above are likenesses of the figures below, and the meaning of each is the same.
